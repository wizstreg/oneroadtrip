East Anglia Road Trip in 10 Days: Secret England

Updated 2026 - 12 min read

Photo: Peter Mason / Unsplash

When people think of an English road trip, they picture the Cotswolds, Cornwall or the Lake District. But there is a region most tourists overlook, just two hours from London, where you will find endless pebble beaches, medieval cathedrals, fairytale villages and some of the most peaceful landscapes in the country. That region is East Anglia: the counties of Cambridgeshire, Norfolk and Suffolk.

In this article, we share a complete 10-day driving itinerary to discover the best of East Anglia, with practical tips, must-see places and everything you need to plan your trip.

Why choose East Anglia for a road trip?

East Anglia is one of England's most underrated regions. While the Cotswolds welcome millions of visitors every year, here you will find medieval villages just as beautiful but without the crowds. The region offers a unique mix of deep history and exceptional natural landscapes: salt marshes, cliffs, vast beaches and the famous Norfolk Broads, Britain's largest protected wetland.

East Anglia is also the perfect destination for slow travel lovers: quiet roads winding between windmills, fishing villages where time seems to have stopped, and a local food scene built on fresh fish, craft beer and farm produce.

Complete itinerary: 10 days in East Anglia

This itinerary covers East Anglia's three counties starting in Cambridge and ending in London, with short distances between each stop.

Day 1

Cambridge: the university city

Your road trip begins in Cambridge, one of England's most beautiful cities. Wander among the majestic colleges, take a punting tour on the River Cam past "The Backs", and explore the Fitzwilliam Museum (free). Nearby, the Imperial War Museum at Duxford houses an impressive aviation collection.

Tip

Book your punting early morning or at sunset to avoid crowds and enjoy the best light.

Day 2

Bury St Edmunds: history and craft beer

Head southeast to Bury St Edmunds, steeped in medieval history. Its crown jewel is the Benedictine abbey, founded in the 11th century. Don't miss The Nutshell, Britain's smallest pub (barely 10 sq m), or the Greene King brewery. The historic centre with its market square and Georgian streets deserves a leisurely stroll.

Day 3

Lavenham: England's finest medieval village

Just 20 minutes from Bury St Edmunds, Lavenham is one of the best-preserved medieval villages in all of England. In the 15th century it was one of the wealthiest towns thanks to the wool trade. Harry Potter fans will recognise its streets: Lavenham was used as Godric's Hollow. Visit the Church of St Peter and St Paul and have lunch at one of its gastropubs.

Day 4

Southwold: the gem of the Suffolk coast

Head to the coast to discover Southwold, with its white lighthouse in the centre of town, traditional Adnams brewery, and retro pier with eccentric mechanical toys. Continue to Aldeburgh, where fishing boats rest on the shingle beach and the Scallop sculpture pays tribute to Benjamin Britten.

Days 5-6

Wells-next-the-Sea: heart of the Norfolk coast

Drive north to the Norfolk coast. Wells-next-the-Sea welcomes you with its picturesque harbour, vast sandy beach and famous colourful beach huts. Walk to Holkham Beach, one of the most spectacular in the country. Take a boat to Blakeney Point, home to England's largest grey seal colony.

Day 7

Holt: Georgian elegance in Norfolk

Holt is a small market town destroyed by fire in 1708 and rebuilt in Georgian style. Its streets are full of independent shops, art galleries and charming cafes. It's the starting point for the North Norfolk Railway, a heritage steam train to Sheringham.

Day 8

Norwich: East Anglia's cultural capital

Norwich surprises with its cultural richness. Its Norman cathedral with one of England's largest cloisters, medieval castle turned museum and lively market deserve a morning. The city has more medieval churches than any other English city north of London. Tombland has excellent restaurants.

Tip

In Norwich, book a table at the restaurants in Tombland. The food scene has grown enormously in recent years.

Day 9

Wroxham and the Norfolk Broads / Ely

In the morning, hire a boat at Wroxham to cruise the Norfolk Broads - silent waterways through reedbeds, rare birds and waterside pubs. In the afternoon, head to Ely and its stunning cathedral, the "Ship of the Fens", with its unique octagonal tower and medieval stained glass.

Day 10

London: end of the journey

Your road trip ends with the drive to London, about two hours south. If you leave Ely early, stop at Duxford for the Imperial War Museum. A perfect ending to 10 days discovering secret England.

Top 5 highlights

1. Cambridge and its colleges

Beyond the university, Cambridge is a vibrant city with exceptional museums, outdoor markets and a unique atmosphere.

2. Lavenham

With over 300 listed heritage buildings, Lavenham is an open-air museum of medieval English architecture.

3. The Norfolk coast

From Holkham to Blakeney, vast beaches, endless skies, marine wildlife and villages with more charm than you can imagine.

4. The Norfolk Broads

Cruising from Wroxham through silent waterways, spotting rare birds and finding pubs only reachable by boat.

5. Ely Cathedral

Its octagonal tower, built after the collapse of the original in 1322, is an architectural marvel that still amazes eight centuries later.

Practical information

When to go?

May to September is best. May-June for long days and fewer tourists. September for spectacular light. Winter offers austere coastal beauty.

How to get there?

Nearest airport: London Stansted (45 min from Cambridge). From London: 2h by car. Eurostar to St Pancras, then direct train to Cambridge (under 1h).

Driving in England

Drive on the left. East Anglia has quiet roads. Roundabouts go clockwise. An automatic car helps if you are not used to it.

Budget guide

70-100 GBP per night in a B and B, 12-20 GBP per meal at a pub, 25-40 GBP per day for car hire. Many attractions are free.

Money-saving tip

If you plan to visit several National Trust sites, a pass pays for itself after 3-4 visits.

Frequently asked questions

Can you do it in less than 10 days?

Yes, in 7 days by combining stops. But East Anglia is best enjoyed at a relaxed pace.

Is it suitable for families?

Excellent for families. Safe beaches, Norfolk Broads, steam railway at Holt and museums like the Imperial War Museum at Duxford.

Do I need to book accommodation in advance?

In July-August yes, especially in Wells-next-the-Sea, Southwold and Lavenham. Book 2-3 months ahead.

What is the difference with the Cotswolds?

The Cotswolds are rolling hills and golden stone villages. East Anglia combines coast, marshes, medieval history and university cities, with far less tourism.

Can you get there by public transport?

Cambridge, Norwich and London are well connected by train. But a car is essential for the coast and villages.
