The Romantic Road in 7 Days: A Fairytale Road Trip

Updated in 2026 · Reading time: 14 min

Photo: Steven Wei / Unsplash

350 kilometres of half-timbered houses, medieval ramparts and castles perched in the Alps. The Romantische Straße, laid out in the 1950s between Würzburg and Füssen, is Germany's most iconic road trip — and probably one of the most beautiful in Europe. It winds through Franconia, Swabia and Upper Bavaria, threading together villages where time seems to have stopped in the 15th century.

This 7-day itinerary takes you from Frankfurt (the international gateway) to Füssen at the foot of the Alps, passing through all the must-see stops and a few hidden gems. Franconian vineyards, fortified towns, beer and Schneeballen: welcome to the world's most romantic road.

🗓️ When to Go

May–June and September–October are the ideal periods: mild weather, reasonable crowds, Franconian vineyards at their peak. Summer (July–August) offers the longest days but Rothenburg gets very busy. December is magical for Christmas markets — Rothenburg and Dinkelsbühl rank among Germany's finest.

Day 1 — Frankfurt: Skyline and Apfelwein

Frankfurt is not officially part of the Romantic Road, but it's the logical starting point if you arrive by plane. The city surprises with its striking contrast: glass skyscrapers frame the reconstructed old quarter of Römerberg, with its half-timbered facades and Gothic town hall that seems straight out of another century.

Not to be missed

Römerberg → Eiserner Steg → Sachsenhausen

Cross the Eiserner Steg (iron bridge) at sunset for the iconic skyline view. On the south bank, the Museumsufer lines up a dozen museums along the Main. For dinner, head to the Sachsenhausen neighbourhood to taste Apfelwein (apple cider) in a traditional tavern like Wagner or Dauth-Schneider — order it in the small blue-grey stoneware jug, it's a ritual.

🚗 Practical Tip

Pick up your rental car the next morning rather than the evening — no point paying an extra day, and parking in Frankfurt is expensive. FRA airport has all the major agencies. Allow 1h15 by road to Würzburg via the A3.

Days 2–3 — Würzburg: Vineyards and Baroque on the Main

Würzburg is the true kilometre zero of the Romantische Straße. The city is dominated by the Festung Marienberg, a fortress perched above vineyards that tumble down to the Main. But it's the Würzburg Residence (UNESCO) that steals the show: the ceiling painted by Tiepolo in the grand staircase is the world's largest fresco — 600 m² of baroque sky that leaves you breathless.

Day 2

Residence → Alte Mainbrücke → Vineyards

Start with the Residence (opens at 9am, arrive early). Then head down to the Alte Mainbrücke, the bridge lined with twelve baroque statues reminiscent of Prague's Charles Bridge. At each end, kiosks sell Franconian wine in the distinctive flask-shaped bottles (Bocksbeutel). A glass of Silvaner at sunset, feet dangling above the Main: it's the perfect beginning to the Romantic Road.

Day 3

Winterhausen → Tauber Valley → Rothenburg

Leave Würzburg via the small roads that follow the Main. Stop at Winterhausen, a sleepy wine village with cobblestone lanes, for a tasting at a family estate. Continue through the Tauber Valley: Weikersheim and its Renaissance château with baroque gardens, then Creglingen and the Herrgottskirche with Tilman Riemenschneider's altarpiece, a masterpiece of 15th-century wood carving. Arrive in Rothenburg in the late afternoon.

🍷 Did You Know?

Franconia is the only German wine region to use the Bocksbeutel, that flat round bottle protected by a designation of origin. The king grape here is Silvaner, a dry and mineral white that pairs perfectly with Franconian cuisine.

Days 3–4 — Rothenburg ob der Tauber: The Medieval Jewel

Rothenburg ob der Tauber is THE star of the Romantic Road — and for good reason. This fortified medieval city is one of only four German towns to have preserved its entire ramparts intact. The name means "red fortress above the Tauber", and when the setting sun sets the tiled rooftops ablaze, you'll understand why.

In the Middle Ages, when Berlin and Munich were merely villages, Rothenburg was Germany's second largest city with 6,000 inhabitants. Impoverished by the Thirty Years' War, it never grew again — accidentally preserving its 17th-century state like a time capsule.

Must-Sees

Plönlein → Ramparts → Nachtwächter

The Plönlein, that fork in the road framed by two towers and a yellow half-timbered house, is Germany's most photographed image. But Rothenburg rewards exploration beyond the clichés: walk the ramparts (4 km of free walkway with views over the Tauber Valley), visit the Kriminalmuseum (medieval crime museum — torture instruments and witch trials) and taste a Schneeball, that ball of fried pastry coated in icing sugar or chocolate.

In the evening, follow the Nachtwächter (night watchman) at 8pm on the Marktplatz for a theatrical torchlit guided tour (€9, in German or English). It's when the tourist coaches have left that Rothenburg reveals its true magic: deserted lanes, flickering lanterns, and the sound of the watchman calling out the hour from the Rathaus tower.

💡 Key Tip

Stay two nights in Rothenburg. The first evening for the Nachtwächter night tour, the second to explore peacefully in the morning before 9am, when the city is all yours. Gasthöfe (inns) within the ramparts offer incredible charm for €80–110 per night. Try the Hotel Gasthof zur Linde — free parking, which is rare in Germany.

Day 5 — Dinkelsbühl and Nördlingen: The Secret Sisters

Heading south from Rothenburg, two lesser-known but equally charming fortified towns await. Dinkelsbühl miraculously survived World War II bombing: it's one of the only German towns with its walls 100% intact. Its pastel-coloured Renaissance houses line cobblestone streets where you encounter more cats than tourists. It's Rothenburg without the crowds — and many travellers prefer it.

Morning: Dinkelsbühl

Ramparts → Münster St. Georg → Marktplatz

Park outside the town (free) and enter through the Wörnitztor. The Münster St. Georg, a 15th-century Gothic hall church, impresses with its proportions and interior light. Wander the lanes — every half-timbered house is more photogenic than the last. At the Marktplatz, sit down at a Gasthaus terrace for a coffee and a fresh Schneeball.

Afternoon: Nördlingen

Tower Daniel → Meteorite Crater → Ramparts

Nördlingen hides a fascinating geological secret: the entire town is built inside a meteorite crater 15 million years old and 25 km wide. Apollo 14 and 17 astronauts came to train here before their lunar missions. Climb the 350 steps of the Daniel tower (90 metres) to see the crater's perfect circle. Remarkably, the cathedral's stones contain micro-diamonds from the impact. Nördlingen is the only German town where you can walk the entire circuit of the ramparts (4 km).

Day 6 — Augsburg and the Road to the Alps

Founded by the Romans in 15 BC, Augsburg is one of Germany's oldest cities and once a major trading hub. It was here that the Fugger dynasty, Europe's most powerful bankers of the Renaissance, created the Fuggerei in 1521 — the world's oldest social housing estate, still inhabited today (annual rent: €0.88 + three daily prayers for the soul of Jakob Fugger).

Morning

Fuggerei → Rathaus → Maximilianstraße

Visit the Fuggerei (entry €8, with a reconstructed museum apartment). Then admire the Goldener Saal (golden hall) of the Town Hall, one of the most sumptuous in Germany. The Maximilianstraße, a grand boulevard lined with Renaissance palaces and fountains, leads to the Romanesque-Gothic cathedral.

Afternoon: Towards the Alps

Landsberg am Lech → Wieskirche → Schwangau

The road south of Augsburg grows increasingly spectacular. Stop at Landsberg am Lech, a pretty town with intact ramparts on the Lech river. Then a mandatory detour to the Wieskirche (UNESCO), a masterpiece of Bavarian Rococo nestled in alpine meadows. Arrive in the evening at Schwangau or Füssen, with the Alps silhouetted against the horizon — the contrast with the Franconian plains of earlier days is breathtaking.

⛪ The Wieskirche

This pilgrimage church isolated in the meadows is an absolute gem. The exterior is modest, but the interior bursts with white and gold stucco, trompe-l'œil frescoes and light. Free entry. Allow 30 minutes. It's one of the finest baroque interiors in Europe.

Day 7 — Neuschwanstein and Füssen: The Alpine Grand Finale

The grand finale. The Romantic Road ends where fairytales take shape: at the foot of Neuschwanstein Castle, the architectural dream of King Ludwig II of Bavaria and the direct inspiration for Disney's Sleeping Beauty Castle. Perched at 965 metres on a rocky spur facing the Alps, it is simply unreal.

Morning

Neuschwanstein → Hohenschwangau → Marienbrücke

Book your tickets in advance on the official website (€15, timed entry). Walk up (30 min) or take a horse-drawn carriage. The castle blends a Byzantine throne room, an artificial cave and Wagnerian frescoes in a total Romantic fantasy. Cross the Marienbrücke, a suspension bridge over a 90-metre gorge, for the iconic photograph of the castle. Just below, Hohenschwangau Castle, older and more intimate, is where Ludwig II grew up.

Afternoon

Füssen: Journey's End on the Lech

Füssen is a charming small town on the Lech river, with an old town featuring facades painted with trompe-l'œil frescoes and a castle (Hohes Schloss) overlooking the centre. It's the official end point of the Romantic Road — a sign confirms it proudly. Stroll along the Lech, an incredible alpine turquoise, and treat yourself to a final Bavarian dinner: Schweinebraten (roast pork), Knödel (dumplings) and a well-chilled Weißbier.

🚗 What's Next?

From Füssen, Munich is 1h40 by car. You can stop there for a night before returning the car, or continue to Austria (Innsbruck 1h30, Salzburg 2h45). If heading back to Frankfurt, allow 4 hours by motorway.

Bonus — Munich: Beer, BMW and Biergarten

If you have an extra day, Munich is the natural extension. Bavaria's capital combines a compact old town (Marienplatz, Viktualienmarkt), legendary Biergarten (Englischer Garten, Augustiner Keller), and world-class museums (Pinakothek, BMW Welt). It's also the best place to return your rental car and catch a flight home.

Practical Information

Driving in Germany: a car is essential to make the most of the route. Hire from Frankfurt (FRA) or Munich (MUC) airport. Motorways are free, but the Romantic Road follows secondary routes signposted with brown "Romantische Straße" signs. Parking is easy in small towns (€1–5/day). Some motorway sections have no speed limit, but secondary roads are limited to 100 km/h.

Budget: allow €80–120/day for two (Gasthof accommodation + meals + petrol). Beer costs €3–4 a pint, a Schneeball in Rothenburg around €2.50. Major attraction entry fees: Würzburg Residence €9, Neuschwanstein €15, Fuggerei €8.

Car-free alternative: the Romantic Road Coach (tourist bus) connects Frankfurt to Munich via the main stops, from April to October, a few times per week. The Deutsche Bahn Bayern-Ticket also allows travel on regional trains at reduced prices.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many days for the Romantic Road?

The minimum is 3 days (Würzburg–Rothenburg–Füssen in the quick version). We recommend 5 to 7 days to enjoy it at a relaxed pace and include detours (Dinkelsbühl, Nördlingen, Wieskirche). With Munich and Frankfurt, allow 7 to 9 days.

Can you do it in winter?

Yes, and it's magical in December for the Christmas markets (Rothenburg, Dinkelsbühl, Nördlingen). January–February is quieter and colder, but the snowy landscapes with the Alps as a backdrop are spectacular. Watch out for possible icy roads.

Romantic Road or Castle Road?

The Romantic Road (Würzburg–Füssen, north–south) passes through medieval towns and ends at the Alps. The Castle Road (Mannheim–Prague, west–east) goes through Heidelberg, Nuremberg and more than 70 fortresses. They cross at Rothenburg — you can combine both.

Should you book accommodation in advance?

In July–August and during Christmas markets, absolutely yes — especially in Rothenburg and Füssen. In spring and autumn, you can often book the day before. Gasthöfe (family inns) offer the best value for money.

Is the road trip suitable for families?

Perfectly. Ramparts to climb, the medieval crime museum, the meteorite crater in Nördlingen, fairytale castles — children love it. Stages are short (30–60 min drive between each town) so no long journeys.
