North York Moors Coast Road Trip: Cliffs and Villages

📅 4 days📍 4 stops🚗 145 km⏱ 3h33

Trace England's northern coast between harbours and chalk cliffs. Explore Hull and its sculpture-aquarium on the estuary, climb Scarborough Castle on its 91-metre headland, wander the smugglers' lanes of Robin Hood's Bay, ascend the 199 steps to Whitby's Gothic abbey where Bram Stoker conceived Dracula, and finish on Saltburn's Victorian beach with its 1884 water-powered funicular.

✨ Highlights
  • Bempton Cliffs: puffins, gannets and guillemots on the chalk cliffs from April to July
  • Whitby Abbey and the Dracula connection: the Gothic ruin that inspired Bram Stoker in 1890
  • Robin Hood's Bay: smugglers' village with hidden tunnels and 45-cm-wide lanes
  • Saltburn Cliff Tramway: the water-balanced funicular opened in 1884, still in service
📆 Best period

April, May, June, July, September

Day 1

Kingston Upon Hull

Where 800 years of maritime history meets the wild Humber estuary

★★★☆☆
1

The Deep, perched at Sammy's Point where the River Hull meets the Humber Estuary, is one of the UK's largest aquariums—5,000 marine species across 50 zones in an iceberg-shaped building designed by Terry Farrell. It opened in March 2002 with £52 million in Millennium Commission funding. The highlight: a 9-metre-deep underwater tunnel through a 2.5-million-litre tank holding 87 tonnes of salt water—several species of sharks glide overhead. Divers hand-feed the sharks every afternoon. Allow at least 2 hours.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Ferens Art Gallery, on Queen Victoria Square, has been open since 1927—funded personally by local MP Thomas Robinson Ferens, who'd spent 22 years giving the city money to buy paintings. The permanent collection is one of the finest in the regions: 17th-century Flemish masters, Hull marine paintings, and a portrait of a young woman by Frans Hals. In 2017, the gallery hosted the Turner Prize as part of Hull's year as UK City of Culture. Free admission.

⏱ 75 min
3

Wilberforce House, on High Street in the Old Town, is the birthplace of William Wilberforce (1759–1833), the MP who devoted his life to abolishing slavery across the British Empire. The museum, opened as early as 1906, traces the reality of the transatlantic slave trade and Wilberforce's decades-long battle leading to the Abolition Act of 1807. Personal journals and belongings are on display. Don't miss the garden out front, named after Nelson Mandela. Free entry.

⏱ 60 min
4

Hull Minster—formerly Holy Trinity Church—is one of England's largest parish churches by floor area. Its brick tower dates from the 14th century: Hull built in brick because the city imported materials from the Netherlands via the estuary, lacking local stone. It was in this church that the city gathered in 1642 to decide to close its gates to King Charles I—an act that directly helped trigger the English Civil War. Allow 30 to 45 minutes.

⏱ 45 min
5

The Streetlife Museum of Transport, free to enter, traces 200 years of transport history with a genuine collection of carriages, trams and period bicycles. The place is less touristy than The Deep and often quieter—ideal mid-afternoon when children start flagging. Allow an hour.

⏱ 60 min
6

Humber Street Gallery occupies a converted warehouse in the Fruit Market, Hull's creative quarter along the estuary. Since Hull City of Culture 2017, this contemporary art space has become the heart of the local arts scene—rotating exhibitions, regional artists' work and international shows. Check the online program before visiting.

⏱ 45 min
7

The Paragon Arcade, in the city centre, is a Victorian glass-roofed shopping gallery dating from 1887. You'll find second-hand bookshops, independent record stores and small cafés—the kind of place that's disappeared from modern shopping centres. Half an hour is enough to browse.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Hull's Old Town concentrates its historic lanes between High Street and the River Hull. Look for Ye Olde White Harte, a Grade II* listed pub built in 1550—tradition has it that the Governor of Hull refused to hand over the city keys to Charles I here in 1642, precipitating the start of the English Civil War. The carved oak interior is worth seeing.

⏱ 60 min

Come evening, head to the Fruit Market for dinner: the area around Humber Street has transformed since 2017 into a hub of independent restaurants. Fresh fish from the Humber, local craft beers, terraces facing the estuary. A good way to end the day waterside rather than in the city centre.

⏱ 90 min

Day 2

Scarborough

A Victorian seaside resort crowned by a 12th-century royal fortress

★★☆☆☆
1

The RSPB reserve at Bempton Cliffs, about 30 kilometres south of Scarborough, is one of England's great wildlife experiences. The chalk cliffs rise 120 metres above the North Sea, and between April and July they host over 200,000 nesting seabirds—the only mainland gannet colony in all of Great Britain (more than 11,000 pairs), plus puffins, guillemots, razorbills and kittiwakes. Gannets have a 2-metre wingspan and sometimes glide eye-level past the viewing platforms. Bring binoculars—puffins blend in among the guillemots. Best period: May to July.

⏱ 120 min
2

Flamborough Head, the chalk promontory jutting into the North Sea, has two lighthouses: the old one (1674, in white chalk, England's oldest intact lighthouse) and the new one (1806, still in service). From the coastal paths around the headland, you look straight down sheer cliffs to sea caves below—keep an eye out for seals sometimes basking on the rocks.

⏱ 75 min
3

Scarborough Castle dominates a 91-metre-high rocky headland with cliffs on three sides, between the town's two bays. The site has been occupied for 3,000 years: Roman signal station in the 4th century, Anglo-Saxon chapel, then royal fortress from 1159 when Henry II seized it and built the great stone keep. It was here that Edward II's favourite, Piers Gaveston, took refuge in 1312 before being captured. In December 1914, German naval bombardment (SMS Derfflinger and SMS Von der Tann) damaged the keep—the shell impacts are still visible. Managed by English Heritage, it's a vast 16-acre site with panoramic views over both bays. Allow 1h30.

⏱ 90 min
4

South Bay, the great sandy beach sheltered below the castle, is Scarborough's historic beach—the town claims to be England's first seaside resort, having attracted spa visitors to its mineral springs as early as the 1620s. In season, the colourful bathing huts and the Victorian Spa are an almost intact period backdrop. For you, the essentials: a seaside walk and an ice cream.

⏱ 45 min
5

The Scarborough Maritime Heritage Centre, near the old harbour, traces the town's history of deep-sea fishing and maritime trade. Collections of model boats, fishing gear and fishermen's family testimonies. Small but well documented, the place is often quiet—an hour suffices.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The coastal path around the castle headland links South Bay to North Bay via the ramparts—a 45-minute walk with plunging views over both bays and the fishing harbour below. Start from the castle entrance and descend to the old harbour on the far side to see the fishing boats.

⏱ 60 min

North Bay, quieter and less touristy than South Bay, is Scarborough's surf spot. If you don't have a board, local operators offer lessons and rentals on the beach in season. The water's cold even in summer—wetsuit essential.

⏱ 120 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 35 km · 1h10
Day 3

Whitby

Where Gothic spires rise above harbour mists and Dracula was born

★★★★☆
1

Robin Hood's Bay, 8 kilometres south of Whitby, is one of England's most beautiful coastal villages—and the most notorious. In the 18th century, it was the Yorkshire coast's most active smuggling hub: gin, brandy, tobacco and silk arrived from the Netherlands and France, and legend has it that a bale of silk could cross the village from harbour to clifftop without ever leaving the houses, thanks to underground tunnels linking the cottages. Customs officers attempting searches sometimes had buckets of boiling water thrown at them from first-floor windows. Locals call the village 'Bay Town'. Leave your car at the top—only residents can drive down to the harbour—and plunge into the lanes on foot.

⏱ 90 min
2

Robin Hood's Bay Museum, in an old house near the harbour, displays fossils found on local beaches, fishing gear and documents on smuggling and shipwrecks. Small and free, it gives context to a walk through the village. Half an hour suffices.

⏱ 30 min
3

Whitby Abbey, perched on the east cliff 70 metres above the harbour, is the most photogenic Gothic ruin on England's north coast. The site has been inhabited for over 3,000 years: in 657, Hilda—an abbess of royal blood, great-great-niece of Northumbria's first Christian king—founded the first monastery here, which hosted the famous Synod of Whitby in 664—the meeting that decided the English Church would follow the Roman Easter calendar rather than the Celtic one. The current Benedictine abbey dates from the 12th century, its limestone Gothic arches from the 13th. In 1890, Bram Stoker holidayed in Whitby and found in the town library the name of a 15th-century Wallachian prince nicknamed 'Dracula'. The rest is literary history: published in 1897, Dracula remains the defining Gothic novel and has never been out of print. Managed by English Heritage. Allow 1h30.

⏱ 90 min
4

St Mary's Church, Whitby, right next to the abbey, is one of England's most singular churches: founded in the 12th century, it was expanded over the centuries by local seafaring families who claimed the interior with their own private box pews, creating an utterly unique over-stuffed wooden interior. It was in the surrounding graveyard that Stoker noted names on the tombstones—including 'Swales', the name of Dracula's first victim in the novel.

⏱ 45 min
5

The 199 steps leading from the old town at the foot of the cliff up to the abbey have been carved into the sandstone since the 17th century. In Stoker's novel, it's by bounding up these steps in the form of a great black dog that Dracula reaches the graveyard after his ship wrecks in the harbour. The climb takes 10 minutes and comes with progressively beautiful views over Whitby's red rooftops and the Esk estuary.

⏱ 30 min
6

The Captain Cook Memorial Museum, in Grape Lane, occupies the house of shipowner John Walker where the young James Cook lived and studied navigation from 1746 to 1749, before joining the Royal Navy. It was here, late at night by candlelight, that he learned algebra, trigonometry and astronomy. The house has remained almost as it was in the 18th century—the rooms, the attic where Cook slept, original engravings from his voyages. Allow an hour.

⏱ 60 min
7

The Dracula Experience, on Marine Parade, takes you through a dramatised tour of Stoker's novel via sound-scapes and special effects in the cellars of an old building. The address itself supposedly has history: one of the earliest known deeds to the property reportedly bears Isaac Newton's signature—a local anecdote that Whitby guides repeat, though it can't quite be verified. Allow 45 minutes for the tour.

⏱ 45 min
8

Hayburn Wyke waterfall, 10 kilometres south of Whitby, cascades directly onto a pebble beach surrounded by ancient woodland—one of the very few coastal waterfalls in England. The access path starts from the Hayburn Wyke Inn car park and descends through the woods in 25 minutes. The beach pebbles sometimes yield ammonite fossils.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

The descent into the heart of Robin Hood's Bay is worth experiencing properly: lanes so narrow your shoulders brush the walls, cottages built one on top of another into the cliff, the tiny harbour with its cobles (traditional fishing boats). If the tide's out, the exposed rocks on the beach reveal ammonites and belemnites—with good shoes, it's free fossil hunting.

⏱ 60 min

Woodland coastal walk from Hayburn Wyke Inn car park to the waterfall and beach. A 5-kilometre round trip through ancient woods with, as reward, an almost always deserted pebble beach. Bring waterproof footwear.

⏱ 120 min

Whitby harbour at day's end, when the fishing boats return and gulls wheel above the quays, is worth every photo of the trip. Look for pubs and fish & chip shops on both banks of the Esk—queues outside the good places will guide you better than any guidebook.

⏱ 60 min

Day 4

Saltburn By The

★★★★★
1

Sandsend, 5 kilometres north of Whitby, is a sand-and-shingle beach on the Jurassic coast—one of the richest on the English coast for marine fossils. Ammonites, belemnites and marine reptile teeth from the Upper Jurassic regularly emerge from the cliff after storms. No special equipment needed: the shoreline at low tide is enough. The beach is uncrowded outside summer.

⏱ 75 min
2

Staithes, wedged between two headlands (Cowbar Nab and Penny Nab) at the mouth of a ravine, is one of the most authentic fishing villages on the north coast. Its lanes are so narrow—the narrowest, Dog Loup, is 45 cm wide—that the sun only enters at midday. Leave your car at the clifftop car park and walk down. In 1745, the young James Cook, aged 16, came here to work as a grocer's apprentice under William Sanderson. He didn't last 18 months: fascinated by the boats in the harbour, he left for Whitby to join the merchant navy. Sanderson's shop was destroyed by a storm but its materials were reused to build a cottage you'll still find on Church Street.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Captain Cook and Staithes Heritage Centre, in the former 1880 Methodist chapel at the top of the village, holds over 200 books, 61 original engravings from Cook's voyages and dozens of objects related to his life. The upper floor recreates William Sanderson's shop as it was in 1745—the only such recreation in the world. Free entry, donations welcome.

⏱ 45 min
4

The Saltburn Cliff Tramway is the oldest water-balanced cliff lift still operating in the UK. Opened in June 1884, it connects the Victorian town atop the cliff to the pier and beach 37 metres below, on 63 metres of track at a 71% gradient. Its operating principle has barely changed in 140 years: a 1,100-litre water tank is filled in the upper car until its weight exceeds that of the lower car—the journey from one level to the other takes 55 seconds, controlled by a brakesman in person. Allow 15 minutes for the round trip.

⏱ 20 min
5

Saltburn's Victorian pier, opened in May 1869 and Grade II* listed, is the only major pleasure pier surviving on the entire North-East coast of England. It extends 200 metres over the North Sea. Walk to the end to see the coast recede in both directions—on clear days, the Boulby cliffs to the north (England's highest, at 203 metres) are visible.

⏱ 30 min
6

Saltburn-by-the-Sea beach, a long strip of sand and shingle at the foot of the cliffs, is a recognised surf spot on the north coast. Beyond the water, the Victorian seafront—colourful bathing huts, esplanades, valley gardens in the background—is intact and particularly photogenic at sunset when the light takes on an orange tint.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Fossil hunting on Sandsend beach and cliffs: armed with a sharp eye and patience, you'll find ammonites on the surface among the pebbles after storms. Operators like Yorkshire Coast Rock run guided outings on this coastal stretch if you want to stack the odds—book ahead, groups are small.

⏱ 90 min

Walk down into Staithes to the harbour: allow 15 minutes downhill (very steep for the last 200 metres) and 20 minutes back up. At the bottom, the quay is tiny, the cobles (small flat-bottomed boats) are often hauled onto the bank. Take time to follow Staithes Beck to the sea. Don't attempt to drive down—the lanes are too narrow for any non-resident vehicle.

⏱ 60 min

Surfing or stand-up paddleboarding lesson on Saltburn beach: hire operators work on the main beach in season. The Saltburn surf break is one of the most reputed in northern England—consistent swell, sandy bottom, cold water but viable in a wetsuit. If you'd rather watch, stay atop the cliff and watch the surfers battle the tide.

⏱ 120 min

Ride the Saltburn Cliff Tramway: take the car both ways if you want to see the mechanism at work, and watch the brakesman operate at the top. The view over the beach and pier from the tracks, mid-slope, is one of the finest of the leg.

⏱ 20 min
