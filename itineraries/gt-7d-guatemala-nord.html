Northern Guatemala Road Trip: Mayan Jungle, Ancient Temples & Hidden Rivers

📅 7 days📍 7 stops🚗 648 km⏱ 14h40

In northern Guatemala, the Petén jungle conceals the pyramids of Tikal — a Mayan capital that dominated the region politically and militarily for over 1,000 years — and the lesser-known ruins of Yaxhá, perched on the edge of a lagoon. Further south, in the misty mountains of Alta Verapaz, Semuc Champey awaits: a chain of turquoise natural pools formed above a river that vanishes beneath 300 meters of limestone. Seven days of ancient temples, underground caves and tropical forest.

✨ Highlights
  • Tikal: 3,000 structures across 16 km², dual UNESCO World Heritage Site (cultural and natural) since 1979
  • Yaxhá: sunset from Temple 216 (30 m) over the lagoon and canopy, with almost no crowds
  • Semuc Champey: a 300 m natural limestone bridge where the Cahabón River disappears underground
  • K'anba Caves: underground canyoning by candlelight in a subterranean river — a unique experience in Central America
📆 Best period

November, December, January, February, March

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 1 and 5 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Dry Season (November-April): Perfect weather, minimal rainfall, ideal temperatures, clear skies, peak tourist season, excellent hiking conditions, advance booking recommended.

Shoulder Season (May & October): Good weather, fewer crowds, moderate temperatures, reasonable prices, occasional showers, excellent value, transitional seasons.

Wet Season (June-September): Heavy afternoon rains, lush landscapes, fewer tourists, lowest prices, muddy roads, challenging travel conditions, morning activities best.

Highland Climate: Year-round spring temperatures, cool evenings, dry season ideal, comfortable conditions, minimal seasonal variation, pleasant climate.

Maya heritage with colorful markets, volcanic landscapes, and authentic indigenous culture. Experience ancient ruins, traditional textiles, and incredible biodiversity. The country offers cultural authenticity with natural beauty and archaeological treasures. Guatemala provides deep cultural immersion with historical significance and natural wonders.

📋 Important notes

This is a demanding itinerary in terms of transport: the roads to Lanquín are partly unpaved. The final stretch from Lanquín to Semuc Champey is done by pickup truck (40 min on a dirt track). Bring enough cash before reaching Lanquín — ATMs there are unreliable.

Day 1

Flores

A colonial island on Lake Petén Itzá — the last Mayan stronghold to resist the Spanish, holding out until 1697

★★★★☆
1

The island of Flores, former capital of the last independent Mayan state, held out against the conquistadors until 1697 — a full 150 years after the fall of the rest of the empire. Landing at Santa Elena airport (FRS), you cross the causeway connecting the mainland to the island and find yourself in a maze of cobbled lanes and colorful facades perched on the shores of Lake Petén Itzá. Stroll along the malecón, the waterfront promenade that circles the island, in the late afternoon — the light on the lake is remarkable at that hour.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Parque Central is the heart of Flores, overlooked by the white cathedral Nuestra Señora de los Remedios. It was on this very spot that the Spanish built their town in 1697 using stones from the main Mayan temple they had just destroyed. Take a moment to sit in the square and watch daily life unfold — it's a world apart from Santa Elena, the more bustling mainland district across the causeway.

⏱ 45 min
3

From the island's dock, hop on a lancha (a 5- to 10-minute crossing) to the village of San Miguel on the north shore of the lake. The Mirador del Rey Canek, with its brightly painted viewing platform, offers the best panoramic view of Flores island and Lake Petén Itzá. From up here, you begin to understand why the Itzá people chose this island setting for their capital — a natural defensive position on the water.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Flight from Guatemala City (GUA) to Flores/Santa Elena (FRS): about 40 minutes, best booked in advance with local airlines. It's by far the most practical option — the road from the capital takes a grueling 8 to 10 hours. Collect your bags at Mundo Maya International Airport in Santa Elena and catch a tuk-tuk to the island (just a few minutes from the airport to the causeway).

⏱ 120 min

Dinner from the street food stands that set up along the lakefront in the evening, near the bridge at the entrance to Flores. Empanadas, Guatemalan tacos, burritos — everything is eaten standing up, facing the lake as night falls. The vibe is thoroughly local and much cheaper than the touristy terraces on the island itself.

⏱ 60 min
Day 2

Flores

A colonial island on Lake Petén Itzá — the last Mayan stronghold to resist the Spanish, holding out until 1697

★★★★☆
1

Tikal is worth an early start — ideally before 6 AM to be among the first in. The city, inhabited from 600 BC to AD 900, was home to one of the most powerful Mayan kingdoms in Central America. Its monumental core stretches across more than 16 km² with nearly 3,000 recorded structures. On the Gran Plaza, Temple I (Temple of the Great Jaguar, 47 m tall) holds the tomb of King Jasaw Chan K'awiil and faces Temple II (Temple of the Masks, 38 m), built in honor of his wife. Get there early and wait for the howler monkeys to launch their dawn chorus from the treetops — the sound is genuinely staggering.

⏱ 90 min
2

Temple IV, standing 65 m tall, is the largest pre-Columbian structure in the Americas. From its summit platform, the tops of other pyramids poke through the canopy like islands in a sea of green — this is exactly the setting that inspired the Yavin scene in Star Wars (Episode IV, 1977). In 2021, LiDAR technology revealed an entire buried district beneath the jungle that replicates the architecture of Teotihuacan (Mexico), over 900 km away — proof that the city was far more cosmopolitan than previously thought.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Mundo Perdido (Lost World) complex, in the southwestern part of the site, takes its name from the archaeologists who christened it after Arthur Conan Doyle's novel — the surrounding jungle reminded them that much of the fictional setting. Its main pyramid (30 m) is the oldest structure at Tikal, adorned with stucco masks depicting the Sun God. From its summit, you can take in Temples I, II, III and IV in a single sweeping view — one of the best vantage points on the entire site.

⏱ 45 min
4

In the afternoon, head to Yaxhá, about 30 km southeast of Tikal. Yaxhá means "blue-green water" in Mayan — a name the city has carried since the Classic period without ever changing it. With around 500 identified structures (including 9 temple-pyramids, 2 ball courts and 40 carved stelae), it's the third-largest archaeological site in Guatemala. Unlike Tikal, you can still freely climb all of the pyramids here.

⏱ 60 min
5

The North Acropolis of Yaxhá groups three nearly intact pyramids, two of which are climbable. From their summits, the unobstructed view over the canopy and the two lagoons is breathtaking. These are the best-preserved structures on the site, left in an almost untouched state — fascinating to see what these buildings looked like before any restoration work.

⏱ 45 min
6

Temple 216, the highest point at Yaxhá (over 30 m), is the unmissable spot for sunset. Archaeologists found red handprints painted on its walls during excavations — hence its nickname, the Temple of the Red Hands. Head up in the late afternoon: from the platform, you can see the jungle stretching to the horizon, the two lagoons flanking the city, and on a clear day, the pyramids of Tikal rising above the forest in the distance. Far fewer visitors than at Tikal to share the moment.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Wildlife spotting in Tikal National Park: howler and spider monkeys in the trees, toucans, macaws, coatis, and for the lucky few, an ocelot or puma on the trails. Bring binoculars — the park harbors an exceptional density of species. Local guides are excellent at spotting elusive animals, and hiring one for the day is well worth it.

⏱ 60 min

Return to Flores in the evening. Organized shuttles from Flores cover both Tikal and Yaxhá in a single day — the most practical way to hit both sites without your own vehicle. Fair warning: it's a packed day (4 to 5 hours at Tikal in the morning, 1.5 hours on the road, 2 to 3 hours at Yaxhá in the afternoon). If the pace feels too intense, save Yaxhá for a separate half-day and stay an extra night in Flores. Entry fees for both parks are payable on site.

⏱ 90 min

Day 3

Lanquin

A Q'eqchi' village at the foot of jungle caves and the Cahabón River, gateway to Semuc Champey

★★☆☆☆
1

The Flores-to-Lanquín journey is one of the most spectacular crossings in Guatemala: you leave the lowland jungle of Petén and climb into the misty mountains of Alta Verapaz. Count on roughly 7 to 8 hours on the road, usually in a shared shuttle. The last 18 kilometers before Lanquín are on a bone-shaking dirt track — hold on tight. The village of Lanquín itself is a quiet Q'eqchi' settlement with a lovely colonial church and hillsides carpeted with coffee and cardamom plantations.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Lanquín Caves open right at the edge of the village — you can walk there from the center. The Cahabón River springs from here and plunges underground for several kilometers before resurfacing in the open air. Inside, a network of impressive stalactites and stalagmites fills successive chambers, all bustling with bats. The marked path runs roughly 500 to 700 m. A perfect warm-up before the more challenging caves the following day.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Flores-to-Lanquín shared shuttle: a full day on the road that seasoned travelers consider a journey in itself. The climb into the Alta Verapaz mountains, the hair-raising switchbacks and the panoramas over the jungle make it a ride you won't forget. Plan to leave early in the morning from Flores to arrive before dark.

⏱ 480 min

Arrive in Lanquín in the evening and settle into one of the lodges on the banks of the Cahabón. Most places offer hammocks and terraces right over the river — enjoy a quiet dinner after the long day on the road. Sort out your next-day excursion to Semuc Champey tonight (all lodges arrange departures).

⏱ 60 min
Day 4

Lanquin

A Q'eqchi' village at the foot of jungle caves and the Cahabón River, gateway to Semuc Champey

★★☆☆☆
1

Semuc Champey, 11 km south of Lanquín, is what happens when the Cahabón River — a 196-km waterway — literally vanishes beneath a massive 300-meter limestone bridge. Above it, a series of tiered natural pools have formed, their water shifting from turquoise to emerald green depending on the light and depth. The name comes from Q'eqchi', a local Mayan language, and means "where the water hides in the stone." The site is sacred to the surrounding Mayan communities. Arrive as early as possible — before 9 AM if you're visiting independently — to enjoy the pools before the organized groups roll in.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Semuc Champey viewpoint is reached after a 20- to 30-minute hike through the jungle from the site entrance. The climb is steep — wear proper shoes, not flip-flops. From the top, the bird's-eye view over the turquoise pools framed by tropical forest is Guatemala's most iconic photograph. Head back down to the pools for a swim — the water is cool but wonderfully refreshing, especially after the effort.

⏱ 90 min
3

The K'anba Caves (Cueva de K'anba), near Semuc Champey, offer the most intense experience on the itinerary: you enter in your swimsuit, candle in hand. The visit is roughly 20% swimming and 80% wading, with sections where you climb ropes, slide down natural stone chutes and scramble past waterfalls. At one point, the guide extinguishes his own lamp so you're left in absolute darkness with nothing but your candle — a genuinely gripping moment. Let them know if you're claustrophobic.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

River tubing on the Cahabón: perched in a car-tire inner tube, you float downstream for anywhere between 30 minutes and 2 hours depending on the water level. Locals sometimes sell refreshments along the banks. It's the ideal late-afternoon activity after Semuc Champey and the caves — the perfect wind-down to an adventure-packed day.

⏱ 90 min

Free swimming in the natural pools of Semuc Champey: each pool has its own depth and color. Groups tend to cluster around the first few pools — if you walk a little further downstream, you'll find small cascades tumbling over the limestone, often with barely anyone around. The site is home to over 100 bird species and 34 mammal species in its immediate surroundings.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 65 km · 1h40
Day 5

Coban

Misty highland capital of Alta Verapaz at 1,300 m, founded in 1543 by Dominican friars who chose peace over conquest

★★★★☆
1

Cobán, perched at over 1,300 meters in the misty mountains of Alta Verapaz, was founded in 1543 by Dominican monks — the first missionaries to evangelize the region without resorting to military force, hence the name "True Peace" (Verapaz) given to the area. The town has a cool, provincial feel, a world away from the tropical humidity of Lanquín. The Q'eqchi' people remain very present here, and you'll often hear their language spoken in the markets.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Vivero Verapaz, a former coffee plantation converted into an orchid conservation center, shelters some 700 different orchid species, including the Monja Blanca — Guatemala's national flower. If you visit Cobán between November and January, you'll hit peak blooming season: the grounds are blanketed in a riot of multicolored flowers that even the most lukewarm botany enthusiasts can't ignore.

⏱ 60 min
3

El Calvario, the neoclassical chapel that crowns a hilltop above Cobán, offers panoramic views over the town and surrounding mountains from its rooftop. It's also an active place of worship: locals climb up to light candles and leave offerings that blend Catholic traditions with Mayan rites — a living religious syncretism you'll encounter throughout Alta Verapaz.

⏱ 45 min
4

The Museo El Príncipe Maya, a privately run and modest-sized museum, houses a collection of pre-Columbian Q'eqchi' art: costumes, jewelry, ceramics and ritual objects that illuminate the region's history far better than any textbook. It's the right way to close out a Maya-focused itinerary — understanding the link between these ancient civilizations and the communities living here today.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Lanquín-to-Cobán drive: roughly 2 to 3 hours through the rolling hills of Alta Verapaz, blanketed with coffee and cardamom plantations. The region is one of the world's leading cardamom producers — you'll see sacks drying by the roadside. Arrive in Cobán by late morning, leaving the afternoon free for sightseeing.

⏱ 180 min

Coffee tasting in Cobán at one of the cafés in the town center. The Alta Verapaz region produces a high-altitude arabica prized for its bright, fruity profile — the chipi-chipi, a near-permanent drizzle that blankets Cobán, creates ideal growing conditions. It's one of the most sought-after varieties among Guatemalan baristas.

⏱ 45 min

Cobán's central market: if your visit falls on a busy market day, take a wander through the aisles where spices, fresh coffee beans, embroidered Q'eqchi' textiles and tropical fruits all jostle for space. The atmosphere is far more local and genuine than the tourist markets of Antigua or Chichicastenango.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 220 km · 5h
Day 6

Guatemala

Guatemala's capital, founded in 1776 at 1,500 m after earthquakes shattered Antigua

★★★★☆
1

Guatemala City, founded in 1776 after the earthquakes that destroyed the former capital Santiago de los Caballeros (today's Antigua), sits at 1,500 meters above sea level. Zona 1, the historic center, is worth an hour's walk despite its unflattering reputation: the Plaza de la Constitución is flanked by the Palacio Nacional (1943, neoclassical facade in green stone), the Catedral Metropolitana and the old city gateway — key landmarks in the country's colonial history.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Palacio Nacional de la Cultura, on Guatemala City's Plaza Mayor, houses a collection of murals tracing the country's history from pre-Columbian civilizations through the colonial era. It served as the presidential seat until 1996. The guided tour (free, in Spanish) is one of the best introductions to the country's political history.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Museo Nacional de Arqueología y Etnología, in Zona 13, holds one of the most important collections of Mayan art and artifacts in Central America: stelae, ceramics, jade funerary masks, and scale models of sites like Tikal and Quiriguá. It's the logical conclusion to a Maya-centered itinerary — the pieces on display here put everything you've seen in the field into context.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Cobán-to-Guatemala City drive: roughly 4 to 5 hours via the CA-14 and then the CA-9. Direct buses from Cobán's terminal run regularly and are a practical option. The route winds through lush mountain scenery before descending toward the capital.

⏱ 300 min

Evening in the Zona Viva (Zona 10) of Guatemala City: it's the liveliest district in the capital, packed with restaurants, bars and late-night cafés. After a week in villages and jungle, it's a welcome transition before tomorrow's departure. Safety here is significantly better than in the historic center — the Zona Viva is the best bet for your final night's hotel.

⏱ 120 min
Day 7

Guatemala

Guatemala's capital, founded in 1776 at 1,500 m after earthquakes shattered Antigua

★★★★☆
1

Final morning in Guatemala City before your flight home. If you're departing in the afternoon, the Mercado Central in Zona 1, right behind the cathedral, is the place to pick up local crafts without the tourist-shop markups of Antigua: embroidered textiles, ceramics, jade pieces, and above all Alta Verapaz coffee sold in whole beans or ground on the spot.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Transfer to La Aurora International Airport (GUA), located in Zona 13, roughly 15 to 30 minutes from the Zona Viva depending on traffic (and Guatemala City traffic can be brutal during rush hour — leave plenty of time). End of the Northern Guatemala itinerary.

⏱ 90 min
