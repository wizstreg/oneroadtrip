Surrey to Kent: Castles and Gardens in 10 Days

📅 10 days📍 14 stops🚗 188 km⏱ 4h35

This 10-day circuit traverses the Weald from west to east, from Guildford and the Surrey Hills to Canterbury's doorstep. It passes through the film-set village of Shere, the Surrey Hills at Box Hill, East Grinstead's Jacobean almshouse, Hever Castle where Anne Boleyn grew up, the Pantiles of Tunbridge Wells, Leeds Castle on its lake, Sissinghurst's gardens, and the medieval brewing town of Faversham.

Guildford

★★☆☆☆
1

Paved with golden sandstone and steeply descending toward the Wey, Guildford's High Street follows the exact Saxon route from the year 900—the town received its royal charter from Henry III in 1257. The famous projecting Guildhall clock from 1683 has hung over the street for three centuries. The ensemble is regularly used as a filming location for its visual coherence. Allow 45 minutes to walk down and back up.

⏱ 45 min
2

Guildford Castle's Norman keep is modest, but it's the only royal castle ever built in Surrey. Erected after the Conquest of 1066, it was sold to the Guildford Corporation in 1885. The garden surrounding the keep is one of the town's most floriferous. Climb to the top for a view across the Surrey Hills on the horizon. Entry ticket around £5 (check on site).

⏱ 60 min
3

The scent of polished wood and old ceramics at Guildford Museum heralds a richer collection than expected: the medieval merchant town, Lewis Carroll who stayed here regularly in his final years, the Arts & Crafts movement in Surrey. The life-size statue of Alice in Wonderland in the Castle Grounds commemorates Carroll, who lived with his sister in Guildford and died here in 1898. Free entry.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Walk up the High Street to the Guildhall with its clock jutting over the road, then continue to Quarry Street to see the castle ruins and the Tunsgate Arch. Guildford offers guided walking tours departing from the Museum—ideal for catching the medieval lanes running perpendicular to High Street.

⏱ 60 min

If you're passing through on a Friday or Saturday morning, Guildford's market sets up on High Street and North Street with local producers from the Surrey Hills. Cheeses, artisan breads, seasonal vegetables. Perfect for assembling a picnic for Shere the next day.

⏱ 45 min

Shere

★★★☆☆
1

Film set and real village: Shere plays both roles effortlessly. The Holiday was shot here in 2006 with artificial snow—the village is already so photogenic that producers dared not alter it. The Tillingbourne flows under a low stone bridge through the main street, ducks linger there, and the 16th-17th century half-timbered houses lean slightly toward it. Fewer than 1,000 residents, no traffic lights, a handful of shops.

⏱ 30 min
2

Mentioned in the Domesday Book, Shere's St James's Church dates in its current form from the 12th century. Inside is a tiny cell walled up in which Christine Carpenter, a religious anchoress, voluntarily enclosed herself in the 14th century. The window through which food was passed to her is still visible. Free entry, generally open during the day.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Walk from the village toward Newlands Corner, a viewpoint about 3 km into the Surrey Hills where on clear days the vista extends to the South Downs. It's near here that Agatha Christie's car was found in 1926 when the novelist staged her own disappearance. Well-marked path, roughly 90 minutes return.

⏱ 90 min

Dorking

★★★☆☆
1

The view from the summit of Box Hill drops suddenly onto the Weald, 172 metres below, with the meanders of the Mole on the right. The white chalk slopes host wild orchids in spring and some thirty species of butterfly in summer. The zigzag road on the north face was already a promenade spot in the 17th century—and the 2012 Olympic cycling circuit passed through. National Trust property, paid parking at the top.

⏱ 90 min
2

Dorking locals call the zigzag path 'the Zig-Zag'—it's mainly where they gather to eat ice cream after a walk. The eccentric Major Peter Labilliere, who died in 1800, asked to be buried upside down at the top of Box Hill, reckoning that the world was so upside down that he wanted 'to be the right way up at the end'. His grave is still there.

⏱ 15 min

Activities

Denbies Wine Estate, England's largest single-estate vineyard with its south-facing chalk hills, offers guided tours with tastings. Commercial exploitation on this Dorking site dates from the 1980s. Booking recommended for tours during summer.

⏱ 90 min
$$$10Little Brook Glamping

East Grinstead

★★★☆☆
1

East Grinstead's medieval High Street is stranger than it appears: it claims one of the longest continuous runs of 14th-century timber-framed houses in England, yet the town was also the site of pioneering reconstructive surgery—the Queen Victoria Hospital treated RAF pilots burned during the Battle of Britain here. A plaque on the south side of the street commemorates three Protestants burned at the stake in 1556. It all coexists along 300 metres of pavement.

⏱ 45 min
2

Sackville College, founded by the will of Robert Sackville 2nd Earl of Dorset in 1609 and built in the following years, conceals behind its local sandstone facade a Great Hall with original hammerbeam roof and minstrels' gallery. The Reverend John Mason Neale, warden of the college from 1846 to 1866, composed Good King Wenceslas here—his study is preserved intact. Guided tour only, Friday-Saturday from mid-June to mid-September (£7.50).

⏱ 60 min
3

Linseed oil and old oak welcome you at East Grinstead Museum with a richer story than anticipated: Weald iron works, the plastic surgeons of Queen Victoria Hospital, and the 'Guinea Pig Club'—the circle of pilots reconstructed by Sir Archibald McIndoe. Free entry; his statue stands outside Sackville College.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Take the steam train on the Bluebell Railway from East Grinstead Station for a return trip to Sheffield Park—11 miles through Sussex on the UK's first preserved standard-gauge heritage railway, reopened in 1960 less than three years after the line closed. Allow 3 hours for the round trip with a stop at Sheffield Park.

⏱ 180 min

If you're visiting on a Thursday morning, East Grinstead's farmers' market sets up on High Street—the market charter dates from 1247. Sussex cheeses, artisan breads, Weald producers. Perfect for stocking up before the drive to Hever.

⏱ 45 min

East Grinstead

★★★☆☆
1

Arts and Crafts preached simplicity and craftsmanship against industry, then built houses like Standen—Grade I listed, designed by Philip Webb between 1891 and 1894, with William Morris tapestries in every room and the original electric light fittings still in service. Webb integrated the barns of an earlier farm into his new construction, creating the impression of a place that had always been there. The paradox is delightful. Timed entry tickets on weekdays.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Explore the paths of the Standen estate that link the house to Kingscote station on the Bluebell Railway—a two-hour woodland loop through ancient Weald woods with semi-wild Exmoor ponies. Map available for free download from the National Trust website before departure.

⏱ 120 min
$$$9.8The Cozy Stopover 3 bedroom house with free Parking
🚗Drive to next stop: 12 km · 20min

Hever

★★★☆☆
1

Hever Castle dates to 1383 in its original form, enlarged by the Boleyns after their acquisition in 1462—this is where Anne Boleyn grew up before becoming Henry VIII's second wife, for exactly 1,000 days. The double-moated castle greets you with a drawbridge and two portcullises in sequence. Inside: period Tudor portraits, two prayer books that belonged to Anne, and a collection reputed among the finest of Tudor portraits after the National Portrait Gallery.

⏱ 75 min
2

Hever's interior is not authentic—William Waldorf Astor, the fabulously wealthy American owner, redid the whole thing in the early 20th century with period tapestries and furniture brought from Europe. The result is meticulous and the distinction between original and restoration well documented in the included audio guide. What is authentic, however: the moats, the drawbridge, and the 14th-century gatehouse.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Climbing roses, shimmering moats, pleached lime trees—Hever's gardens spread over 125 acres with an Italian Garden of columns brought from Italy by Astor, a plant maze, and an artificial lake dug in 1904. Allow at least two hours for the garden alone. The rhododendrons in May are particularly spectacular.

⏱ 120 min
$$$9The George & Dragon
🚗Drive to next stop: 8 km · 15min

Chiddingstone

★★★☆☆
1

Hard to believe an entire village can belong to a single organization—yet the National Trust bought all of Chiddingstone in 1939 to freeze it in its Tudor state. Houses, inn, and shop have been preserved ever since. Over two-thirds of the buildings are more than two hundred years old. The village shop is one of the oldest houses on the street. There's only one street; you can walk it in ten minutes.

⏱ 30 min
2

Denys Eyre Bower had a problem: he couldn't stop collecting. When he moved into Chiddingstone Castle in 1955, he filled this neo-Gothic manor with a Tudor core (remodeled in the early 19th century by architect Henry Kendall) with samurai armor, ancient Egyptian treasures, Stuart paintings, and Jacobite manuscripts. Everything has remained intact since his death. Open April to October.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Local beer, shaded garden, Tudor houses opposite—The Castle Inn at Chiddingstone has been serving travelers since the 15th century. Lunch on the terrace overlooking the village's only street. No bookings for outside tables; check opening hours midweek off-season.

⏱ 60 min

Penshurst

★★★☆☆
1

Penshurst Place was granted in 1552 by Edward VI to Sir William Sidney, his tutor and steward—the castle had previously served as a hunting lodge for Henry VIII during his courtship of Anne Boleyn (Hever Castle is six kilometers away). The Sidney family still owns it, making it one of England's oldest family homes. The 14th-century Baron's Hall is one of the rare examples of a medieval domestic great hall still intact.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Sidneys extinguished their male line in the 18th century but their heirs took up the name—today the De L'Isles—and continue to manage Penshurst. Queen Elizabeth I stayed here on several occasions and the Queen Elizabeth Room bears her name. There's also a Toy Museum with toys from successive generations of Sidneys, including a doll's house and 19th-century lead soldiers.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Explore the 11 acres of walled gardens at Penshurst, divided into 'rooms' by more than a mile of yew hedges—a structure resembling what Vita Sackville-West would create at Sissinghurst a generation later. The rose beds in June and tulips in April-May are highlights. The heritage apple orchard is also worth a detour in autumn.

⏱ 75 min
$$$10Finest Retreats - Chartwell Luxury Dome
🚗Drive to next stop: 10 km · 20min

Royal Tunbridge Wells

★★★☆☆
1

Dudley Lord North fell ill in 1606, drank from a rusty-red iron spring in the Weald, felt better, and accidentally triggered the creation of an entire town. The Pantiles, a Georgian colonnade built through the 18th century when royalty and aristocracy came to take the waters, remains the heart of the spa. The chalybeate spring is still there at the north end of the promenade. The town received the title 'Royal' in 1909 from Edward VII.

⏱ 60 min
2

Walk up to Calverley Park, the work of architect Decimus Burton in the 1820s—the same Burton who worked on Hyde Park Corner in London. Neoclassical villas around a preserved public park, far from the shopping center. The adjacent Calverley Grounds remain freely accessible.

⏱ 45 min
3

The iron flavor of the chalybeate spring at the Pantiles is served by costumed attendants—the water is lukewarm and metallic, which isn't unpleasant if you're expecting it. The Amelia Scott, a museum and library merged and opened in 2022, exhibits the spa town's history with original objects including a period pantile. Free, open 7 days a week except public holidays.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Chic but unpretentious: the Pantiles shops mix independent art galleries, antique dealers, and terrace cafes. On Thursday evenings in summer, a free Jazz Festival sets up there. A craft and farmers' market runs fortnightly. Allow time to browse rather than buy.

⏱ 60 min

The Amelia Scott deserves better than its reputation as a merged library-museum: the 'Story of the Wells' exhibition traces the spa's origins with original objects. The Fine Grind cafe on the ground floor overlooks a sculpture garden with works commissioned for the building. Allow 45 minutes.

⏱ 45 min
$$$10Finest Retreats - Chartwell Luxury Dome
🚗Drive to next stop: 11 km · 15min

Tonbridge

★★★☆☆
1

Built after 1066 to control the Medway crossing on the route from Hastings to London, Tonbridge Castle is the work of Richard FitzGilbert, cousin of William the Conqueror. The 13th-century golden sandstone gatehouse is one of the finest examples of its kind in England. The included audio guide covers nine hundred years of history from the first timber motte-and-bailey.

⏱ 60 min
2

If the monthly market is on that day—usually the second Saturday of the month—settle by the Medway with Kent cheeses and artisan bread. The redeveloped riverfront facing the castle offers tables with views of the medieval gatehouse. On weekdays, the artisan bakers on High Street do just as well.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Embark with Tonbridge River Trips aboard the Enid May, an electric cruiser with the skipper's commentary on the town's medieval history. Kingfishers, cormorants, and dragonflies animate the banks between Georgian houses and the first reaches of the nature park. Available in summer; durations from 45 minutes to 2 hours depending on the chosen excursion.

⏱ 90 min

Leeds Castle

★★★★★
1

The castle floats. Set on two islands in a natural lake amid 500 acres of Kent parkland, Leeds Castle is one of England's most photographed medieval sites. The first stone fortification dates to 1119; Edward I turned it into a royal palace in 1278 for Queen Eleanor of Castile. Six queens of England lived here. Lady Baillie, the last private owner, bequeathed everything to a foundation on her death in 1974.

⏱ 90 min
2

You'd expect an armory, you find a dog collar museum in the old stables. The collection—the world's largest of its type—assembles over 130 pieces from the 16th to the 19th century, donated to the foundation in 1977 by Gertrude Hunt. The earliest iron-spiked examples protected hunting dogs' throats against wolves and bears that ran through England's forests. Included in the ticket.

⏱ 30 min
3

A dog collar museum in a medieval castle, and also: a birds of prey aviary, black swans on the lake, daily falconry, the Gloriette—a 13th-century keep with reconstructed royal apartments. Henry VIII undertook major works here in the 16th century. The multimedia audio guide included in the ticket covers nine centuries of history.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Get lost in the maze of 2,400 yews planted in 1988—designed to truly lose you: the classic 'hand on the wall' rule doesn't work here, the walls aren't all connected. At the center, an underground grotto with mythological deities carved into the walls. Allow 30 to 45 minutes.

⏱ 45 min

Attend Leeds Castle's daily falconry—buzzards, hawks, and eagles in free flight over the park with commentary from the falconers. The estate also houses an exotic bird aviary and the famous black swans on the lake. Displays usually run twice daily depending on season; times posted at the entrance.

⏱ 45 min
$$$9.1Leeds Castle Stable Courtyard Bed and Breakfast

Sissinghurst

★★★★★
1

In 1930, Vita Sackville-West paid £12,375 for these ruins—no running water, no electricity, no heating. She saw in them a substitute for Knole, the ancestral house from which she'd been excluded from inheriting. What she made of it in thirty years remains one of England's most influential gardens: seven walled plant 'rooms', each with its theme. The geometric structure came from her husband Harold Nicolson; the romantic chaos of the plantings, from her.

⏱ 30 min
2

White, silver-grey, pale green—the White Garden explodes in June with its white roses, silvery foliage, and clematis climbing the central framework. The mulliganii rose cascading over the central arbor in mid-June has been photographed for decades. Arrive before 10am on a Friday or Monday to visit without crowds.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Elizabethan tower is small, the 78 wooden stairs steep and worn—but the view from the top over the seven gardens and the 450-acre estate is worth the effort. Vita's study on the first floor is preserved as she left it. This is where she wrote her garden columns for the Observer until her death in 1962. Queue in high season.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Explore the 450 acres of farmland beyond the formal gardens—a working farm with marked paths through Weald woods and meadows. The main loop takes 90 minutes and passes the original barns and oast houses. Maps at reception. The estate is accessible even without a ticket for the formal garden.

⏱ 90 min

In June, come early: the Rose Garden reaches its peak in the third week of the month, when Vita's old roses are in simultaneous bloom. She preferred heritage roses to modern hybrids—an almost militant stance for the time. Sissinghurst closes Wednesdays and Thursdays.

⏱ 60 min

Faversham

★★☆☆☆
1

Faversham's Abbey Street is one of England's finest medieval streets: 15th-16th century timber-framed houses, exposed beams, vaulted passages—all in a state of preservation owing as much to the town's geographic isolation as to its heritage protections. Faversham has over 500 listed buildings. The Fleur de Lis Heritage Centre, at the entrance, traces the town's history from prehistory.

⏱ 60 min
2

Shepherd Neame, officially founded in 1698, has been brewing on its Court Street site since at least 1573 according to local records. It's Britain's oldest brewery in continuous commercial operation. Guided tours show the copper vessels, the brewhouse, and the collection of period delivery vehicles. The tasting at the end is included.

⏱ 90 min
3

Locals simply call the brewery 'Shep's'—and the sweet scent of boiling hops sometimes fills all of Court Street on brewing days. Faversham was also one of the centers of the gunpowder industry; Chart Mills, the powder mills on the creek north of town, is the world's oldest gunpowder works still standing. Free access to the outdoor site.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Wander Faversham's medieval market—three times a week (Tuesday, Friday, Saturday) in the market square surrounded by Tudor and Georgian houses. It's Kent's oldest market. The Creek east of town, a former seaport, can be walked along a towpath with views of restored barges.

⏱ 60 min

Useful contradiction: Chart Mills, the world's oldest gunpowder works still standing, sits in a bird nature reserve. The Oare Gunpowder Works site supplied powder for Trafalgar and Waterloo. Today 81 acres of marshland with snipe, plovers, and ducks. Wheelchair-accessible paths, free entry.

⏱ 60 min
$$$10The Nest at the Tree House
🚗Drive to next stop: 12 km · 15min

Chilham

★★★☆☆
1

Chilham's square smells of old oak and damp stone after rain. Surrounded on three sides by 17th-century Jacobean timber-framed houses, it opens on the fourth side toward the 13th-century St Mary's Church and the castle gates. The ensemble forms one of the region's most photographed architectural sets. The White Horse Inn dates from the 15th century. The village is 7 miles from Canterbury.

⏱ 30 min
2

Chilham's Norman keep dates to 1174 and is still inhabited, making it one of Britain's oldest residential buildings. The adjacent Jacobean manor was built in 1616 by Sir Dudley Digges, close associate of Shakespeare and merchant adventurer. The gardens, whose design is attributed to John Tradescant the Elder, are open Tuesdays and Thursdays from May to September.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

If you arrive on a Tuesday or Thursday between May and September, Chilham Castle gardens open from 10am to 4pm (£6, cash only). Three terraces descend to a lake with views over the Stour valley. Outside these hours, the village square, church, and marked paths around the estate are freely accessible.

⏱ 90 min
