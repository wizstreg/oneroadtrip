Hungary Road Trip: Thermal Baths, Lake Balaton and Budapest

📅 8 days📍 9 stops🚗 742 km⏱ 14h49

Sopron → Heviz → Sumeg → Balatonfured → Budapest → Szentendre → Holloko → Eger → Tokaj

Sopron → Heviz → Sumeg → Balatonfured → Budapest → Szentendre → Holloko → Eger → Tokaj

From Sopron, the civitas fidelissima clinging to the Austrian border, to the UNESCO-listed vineyards of Tokaj, this circuit crosses Hungary from west to east following the country's two great passions: thermal waters and medieval history. A castle never taken by the Ottomans, a thermal lake with pink water lilies, a Benedictine abbey founded in 1055, a baroque capital and a timeless Palóc village — every stop has its own chapter of history.

Stop 1

Sopron

A medieval border town that voted to stay Hungarian in 1921, earning the title civitas fidelissima

★★★☆☆
📍 Tűztorony Sopron📍 Fő tér Sopron📍 Kecske-templom Sopron📍 Középkori zsinagóga Sopron📍 Storno-ház Sopron
1

Start by climbing the Fire Tower (Tűztorony) — its Romanesque foundations date from the 13th century, but the baroque silhouette you see today was rebuilt after a major fire in 1676. More than 100 spiral steps take you 60 metres up, where the view over the old town rooftops and the Austrian hills is striking. The Fire Tower owes its name to its role as a fire-watch post — an ironic function, given that it burned down itself.

⏱ 45 min
2

Fő tér is Sopron's heart: the Holy Trinity Column stands at the centre, surrounded by immaculate baroque façades. Look closely at the gate at the foot of the tower — it's called the Gate of Loyalty, erected to commemorate the December 1921 referendum in which the townspeople voted 65% to remain in Hungary rather than be annexed to Austria after the Treaty of Trianon. This small border town has borne the title civitas fidelissima ever since.

⏱ 30 min
3

Step inside the Goat Church (Kecske-templom), a 13th-century Gothic building whose name comes from a local legend: a goat supposedly unearthed a treasure that funded its construction. In the 18th century, this baroque church served as a coronation venue and meeting place for the Hungarian Parliament — making it one of the most historically significant churches in the country. The 18th-century rococo wooden pulpit deserves a closer look.

⏱ 30 min
4

Detour into Új utca (New Street) to see the medieval synagogue built in the 14th century by Sopron's Jewish community — one of the oldest in Hungary. The inner courtyard contains a restored mikveh (ritual bath). After the expulsion of 1526 the building was converted into a dwelling, then rehabilitated from the 1960s onward.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Take your time strolling the Várkörút, the circular boulevard that traces the old medieval ramparts of Sopron. Walking along these partially preserved walls, you'll understand why the town held out against the Ottomans while Buda and Pécs fell — it was never occupied. The Lővérek forest, accessible on foot from the centre, offers shaded trails and a few lookout points over the Hungarian plain.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 2

Heviz

The world's largest natural thermal lake — 4.4 hectares of warm water renewed every 40 hours

★★★☆☆
📍 Hévízi-tó termáltó📍 Hévíz Tófürdő📍 Festetics-kastély Keszthely📍 Hévíz lombkorona sétány
1

The thermal lake at Hévíz is simply one of a kind: at 4.4 hectares, it's the world's largest biologically active natural hot-water lake. The water surges from a spring 40 metres below at a rate of 410 litres per second and a temperature of 40°C — which is why the entire lake renews itself every 28 to 40 hours. In winter the water rarely drops below 24°C, and in summer it climbs to 36°C. You'll swim surrounded by pink water lilies imported in the late 19th century, while steam rising off the surface gives the place an almost otherworldly feel. Consider renting a float ring at the entrance.

⏱ 30 min
2

Five kilometres away, Festetics Castle in Keszthely is the third-largest castle in Hungary. Construction began in 1745 under Kristóf Festetics, and the baroque building was expanded several times through the 18th and 19th centuries to reach 101 rooms. It now houses the largest intact aristocratic library in Europe: 86,000 volumes that survived the looting of the Second World War. The grounds and the former tropical glasshouse are worth a look.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

The real Hévíz experience is the swim: walk down the few steps from the thermal complex terraces and let yourself float in the mildly sulphurous water, rich in calcium, magnesium and radon. The sulphur smell is there but subtle. Allow at least two hours to make the most of it — regulars spend the entire morning reading newspapers propped on their floats. Avoid summer weekends, when the site gets very crowded.

⏱ 120 min

Stop 3

Sumeg

A 13th-century hilltop fortress the Turks never conquered, commanding the Transdanubian plain

★★☆☆☆
📍 Sümegi vár📍 Püspöki palota Sümeg📍 Sümegi plébániatemplom📍 Tapolcai-tavasbarlang
1

Sümeg Castle perches on an isolated hilltop commanding the plain — and that's no accident: King Béla IV had it built in the 1260s precisely for this reason, in the wake of the Mongol invasion of 1241. The fortress, 125 metres long by 80 metres wide, was never taken by the Turks, even though they occupied the town below on several occasions. Permanent exhibitions recreate the medieval dungeons, the chapel and the blacksmith's workshop. In summer, jousting tournaments are staged in the courtyard.

⏱ 75 min
2

At the foot of the castle hill, the 18th-century baroque Episcopal Palace is a monument often overlooked by visitors. Built by Bishop Padányi Bíró Márton in the mid-18th century — when the Veszprém diocese had retreated to Sümeg in the face of the Turks — it illustrates the town's baroque heyday. Its fresco-decorated halls rank among the finest north of Lake Balaton.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

From the top of the castle, the panoramic view over the Transdanubian plain, the ancient volcanic cones and the distant Bakony hills is one of the finest in the region. The trail circling the ramparts takes about 30 minutes. From Sümeg, a 20-kilometre detour to Tapolca lets you visit the underground cave lake — a natural curiosity unique in Europe, explored by boat.

⏱ 90 min

Stop 4

Balatonfured

Lake Balaton's oldest spa resort and the gateway to Tihany's 1055 Benedictine abbey

★★★☆☆
📍 Tagore sétány Balatonfüred📍 Balatonfüred kikötő📍 Tihanyi apátság📍 Visszhang-domb Tihany📍 Badacsonyi szőlők
1

Balatonfüred is the oldest spa resort on Lake Balaton. Walk along the Tagore sétány, the lakeside promenade — named after the Indian poet Rabindranath Tagore, who came here for a cardiac cure. The historic spa centre was already renowned in the 19th century for treating heart conditions, and a cardiac hospital still operates here. The marina, neoclassical villas and lakeside terraces make this the most elegant stop on the northern shore.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Tihany peninsula juts into Lake Balaton like a finger from the northern shore. At the top of an 80-metre hill, the Benedictine abbey was founded in 1055 by King Andrew I — he is buried here, in the Romanesque crypt that is one of the oldest in Hungary. The present baroque church, built between 1719 and 1754, dominates the village with its twin towers. From the forecourt, the view over the lake is the best on the entire Balaton. In June, the surrounding hills turn into a carpet of purple as the lavender fields bloom — lavender ice cream in the village is a must.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Echo Hill (Visszhang-domb), just below the abbey, has been known for centuries for its acoustics: sounds bounce off the church walls and return amplified. The Tihany peninsula is also Hungary's first protected nature reserve (designated in 1952) — marked trails cross its two crater lakes, the Belső-tó and Külső-tó, which sit at a higher elevation than Lake Balaton itself.

⏱ 60 min

If you have time on the road between Sümeg and Balatonfüred, make a stop at Herend, 45 kilometres north, to visit the porcelain manufactory founded in 1826 — its hand-painting workshops are open to visitors. Alternatively, Ajka (crystal) is in the same direction. It's not on the direct route, but this is one of the only manufactories of its kind still operating in Central Europe.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 5

Budapest

A Danube capital of thermal waters, where the world's third-largest parliament meets the 1913 Széchenyi Baths

★★★★★
📍 Halászbástya Budapest📍 Mátyás-templom Budapest📍 Országház Budapest📍 Széchenyi gyógyfürdő Budapest📍 Dohány utcai zsinagóga Budapest📍 Széchenyi lánchíd Budapest
1

The Fisherman's Bastion (Halászbástya) may be Budapest's most photographed monument — and yet it's much younger than its medieval appearance suggests: built between 1895 and 1902 by architect Frigyes Schulek to mark the millennium of the Hungarian state, it never served any defensive purpose whatsoever. Its seven conical turrets represent the seven Magyar tribes who founded Hungary in 895. Access to the terraces is free before 9 AM and after 9 PM — arrive early to beat the crowds and enjoy the view of the illuminated Parliament. Right next door, the 14th-century Gothic Matthias Church, where Hungarian kings were crowned, deserves 30 minutes inside for its neo-Gothic frescoes.

⏱ 75 min
2

On the Pest bank of the Danube, the Hungarian Parliament is the third-largest parliament building in the world — 268 metres long, 691 rooms and 16 tonnes of gold in its decoration. Built between 1884 and 1902 in a spectacular neo-Gothic style, it now houses the Crown of Saint Stephen behind bulletproof glass. Book your guided tour well in advance — tickets sell out fast in high season. The Danube embankment walk around the building, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, is free.

⏱ 90 min
3

In Pest's Jewish quarter, the Great Synagogue on Dohány Street is the largest in Europe and the second-largest in the world. Inaugurated in 1859, it seats 3,000 worshippers. In its garden stands the Tree of Life, a metal sculpture in the shape of a weeping willow whose every leaf bears the name of a Hungarian Jew murdered during the Holocaust. Don't miss the surrounding lanes of the Jewish quarter with their hidden courtyards and ruin bars tucked into former tenement buildings.

⏱ 60 min
4

The Széchenyi Baths were the first thermal baths built on the Pest side, inaugurated in 1913 in a striking neo-Baroque style. The water rises at 76°C from an artesian well 1,246 metres deep — it's cooled before feeding the 15 indoor pools and 3 outdoor pools open year-round. The classic scene: elderly gentlemen playing chess on floating boards in the 36°C outdoor pool. Book online to skip the queue.

⏱ 150 min

Activities

Board a twilight cruise on the Danube — this is where the city reveals its true grandeur, especially once night falls and the monuments light up. Operators offer one- to two-hour cruises from the central Pest embankment. The simultaneous view of the Parliament, Buda Castle, the seven bridges and the Fisherman's Bastion is worth the ticket alone.

⏱ 90 min

Explore Andrássy Avenue and its 19th-century covered passages — the finest is the Párisi Udvar (Paris Court), built in 1909 in a blend of Art Nouveau and Gothic styles. This UNESCO-listed avenue leads all the way to Heroes' Square and Városliget park. In the evening, the ruin bars of the 7th District — abandoned tenement courtyards turned into bars — offer a deep dive into Budapest's nightlife.

⏱ 90 min

Stop 6

Szentendre

A baroque artists' colony founded by Serbian refugees, with over 30 galleries for 25,000 inhabitants

★★★☆☆
📍 Szentendre Fő tér📍 Blagoveštenska szerb ortodox templom Szentendre📍 Kovács Margit Múzeum Szentendre📍 Szabadtéri Néprajzi Múzeum Skanzen Szentendre📍 Visegrádi fellegvár
1

Szentendre, the 'artists' town,' sits 25 kilometres north of Budapest on the Danube. Its historic heart, Fő tér, is dominated by a marble Plague Cross erected in the 18th century by Serbian merchants to celebrate the town being spared by the epidemic. The colourful baroque houses date from the arrival of Serbian settlers in the 17th and 18th centuries — families fleeing the Ottoman occupation of Serbia, which explains the many Orthodox churches you'll come across in the lanes.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Margit Kovács Museum showcases the work of Hungary's most famous 20th-century ceramicist, whose naïve, poetic style blends peasant influences with Art Deco. It's one of the most visited museums in Hungary outside Budapest — allow 45 minutes. Szentendre has more than 30 galleries and museums for a population of roughly 25,000, a concentration of art that is probably unique in Europe.

⏱ 45 min
3

On the outskirts of town, the open-air museum (Skanzen) recreates Hungarian rural life from every region of the country across several hectares. Entire houses have been dismantled, moved and rebuilt, complete with period furniture, tools and farm animals — including the grey Hungarian cattle and the woolly Mangalica pig, two breeds typical of the puszta. Allow at least two hours to see it all.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

From Szentendre, a stop at Visegrád (13 km north) is highly recommended if you have time: the hilltop fortress commands one of the most spectacular views of the Great Danube Bend (Dunakanyar) — where the river makes a sharp 90° turn between forested hills. Once a royal residence, Visegrád's upper castle served as headquarters for Hungarian kings in the 14th century.

Stop 7

Holloko

Hungary's only UNESCO village, frozen in its 1911 state with 55 whitewashed Palóc houses

★★★☆☆
📍 Hollókő Ófalu UNESCO📍 Hollókői vár📍 Hollókő falumúzeum📍 Cserhát-hegység Hollókő
1

Hollókő is the only village in Hungary inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage list, since 1987 — and the designation is well deserved. The name means 'Raven Stone' in Hungarian, from a legend in which ravens dismantled a castle stone by stone to free a captive woman. The old village, spread over 145 hectares, contains 55 protected buildings in the Palóc architectural style of the 17th and 18th centuries: whitewashed walls, wooden porches, tiled roofs dating from the reconstruction after the last major fire in 1909. Around 400 people still live here and maintain the traditions — at Easter, they dress in their colourful folk costumes.

⏱ 75 min
2

The ruins of Hollókő Castle stand at 365 metres on Mount Szár, above the village. Built in the 13th century by the noble Kacsics clan after the Mongol invasion, it played a defensive role during the Ottoman wars before being finally abandoned in 1683 at the end of the Turkish occupation. The castle has been partially restored, and its 360° panorama over the Cserhát hills is the reward for the 15-minute climb from the village.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hollókő's single-street village is best explored slowly, on foot, stopping in the small artisan workshops — leatherworkers, woodturners, potters — that occupy the historic houses. The village museum faithfully recreates an early 20th-century Palóc interior with its everyday and decorative objects. A short hike into the surrounding Cserhát hills offers perspectives over a landscape of orchards, vineyards and meadows that has barely changed in two centuries.

⏱ 90 min

Stop 8

Eger

A baroque town famed for its heroic 1552 stand against the Ottomans and its cellars of Bull's Blood wine

★★☆☆☆
📍 Egri vár📍 Egri bazilika📍 Egri minaret📍 Dobó István tér Eger📍 Szépasszony-völgy Eger📍 Egri termálfürdő
1

Eger's Basilica, built in a neoclassical style between 1831 and 1836 by architect József Hild, is the second-largest church in Hungary after the one in Esztergom. Its six 17-metre Corinthian columns and sculpted tympanum dominate the central square. The interior seats 6,000 and its remarkable acoustics explain the regular organ concerts held here. Beneath the church lies a network of cellars — 'the city under the city' — visitable with a separate ticket.

⏱ 60 min
2

Eger Castle (Egri Vár) is one of the defining sites of Hungarian resistance. In September 1552, Captain István Dobó and 2,000 soldiers held out for 38 days against a besieging Ottoman army of 40,000 — a victory that passed into national legend. The heroic defence was famously attributed to the local red wine, which the warriors smeared across their beards to appear more ferocious, giving rise to the name Egri Bikavér, 'Bull's Blood.' The castle fell nonetheless in 1596, ushering in 91 years of Turkish occupation. The castle museums and underground passages are not to be missed.

⏱ 90 min
3

Don't miss Eger's Turkish minaret, the only intact minaret in Hungary. Standing 40 metres tall and built in the early 17th century, it rises in the heart of the old town as a remnant of the 91 years of Ottoman rule (1596–1687). Its 97 steps lead to a balcony with a distinctive view over baroque Eger. Open from April to October only.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The Valley of the Beautiful Women (Szépasszony-völgy) lies one kilometre from central Eger: more than 200 wine cellars carved into the volcanic tuff line up over a few hundred metres. This is where you'll taste Egri Bikavér, Eger's robust red and one of Hungary's most famous wines. A network of 96 kilometres of underground tunnels runs beneath the town, connecting some of these cellars. Allow at least an hour for a proper tasting.

⏱ 60 min

Eger's thermal baths combine indoor and outdoor pools fed by waters ranging from 28 to 68°C. The Turkish baths (Török fürdő) date from the Ottoman occupation — Arnaut Pasha built the first hammam here in the 16th century. A perfect end-of-day wellness stop after the castle.

⏱ 90 min

Stop 9

Tokaj

Capital of a UNESCO sweet wine proclaimed the wine of kings by Louis XIV

★★☆☆☆
📍 Tisza-Bodrog összefolyás Tokaj📍 Tokaji Múzeum📍 Rákóczi-pince Tokaj📍 Tokaj-hegy Kopasz-hegy📍 Tokaji kilátó📍 Tokaj-Hegyalja szőlők
1

Tokaj sits at the confluence of two rivers — the Tisza and the Bodrog — and it's precisely this confluence that creates the autumn mists essential for the development of noble rot (Botrytis cinerea) on the Furmint and Hárslevelű grapes. That's why Tokaj wine, whose documented production dates back to 1571, can only come from here and nowhere else. Louis XIV, who adored it, proclaimed it 'vinum regum, rex vinorum' — the wine of kings, the king of wines — and Tsar Peter the Great kept a permanent Russian garrison in Tokaj charged with supplying the imperial court. UNESCO inscribed the Tokaj-Hegyalja cultural landscape in 2002.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Tokaj Wine Museum holds autographs from Louis XIV and Tsar Peter the Great attesting to their fascination with the wine, along with a room dedicated to the 1772 vineyard classification system — one of the earliest in the world. It also gives access to a 16th-century cellar where Hungarian oak barrels (the 136-litre gönci, the standard unit of measure for Tokaji) still age. The guided tour lasts about 45 minutes.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Climb Mount Tokaj (Kopaszhegy, 512 metres) — a moderate 45-minute hike through UNESCO-listed terraced vineyards. At the summit, a 25-metre wooden observation tower inaugurated in 2017 offers a 360° panorama over the Tisza–Bodrog confluence, the patchwork of vines and the Zemplén hills. This is the vantage point from which you'll understand why the region earned its UNESCO listing.

⏱ 120 min

Operators offer tastings in the region's historic cellars — some date back to the 15th century, hewn into volcanic rock. Book ahead in September–October (harvest season), when the estates are at their busiest. The region has more than 3,000 wine cellars in total. The speciality to ask for first: Tokaji Aszú, a luscious sweet wine made from individually hand-picked overripe berries.
