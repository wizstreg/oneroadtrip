9-Day Swiss Circuit: Geneva, Zermatt, Bernese Oberland, Lucerne & Zurich

📅 9 days📍 9 stops🚗 549 km⏱ 8h23

From Geneva and its Jet d'Eau facing Mont Blanc to Zurich and its Romanesque churches beside the Limmat, this nine-day circuit crosses Valais at the foot of the Matterhorn (two days in Zermatt), the Bernese Oberland valleys with 72 waterfalls (two days between Lauterbrunnen, Wengen, Mürren, and Schilthorn, one day in Grindelwald), and the medieval city of Lucerne (two days among covered bridges, lake cruises, and medieval ramparts).

✨ Highlights
  • Geneva and the Jet d'Eau facing Mont Blanc
  • The Matterhorn from Gornergrat at 3,089 m
  • Lauterbrunnen Valley with 72 waterfalls
  • Schilthorn and James Bond's Piz Gloria restaurant
  • Lucerne's Kapellbrücke rebuilt after the 1993 fire
  • Zurich between Romanesque Grossmünster and Dada movement
📆 Best period

June, July, August, September

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 12 and 16 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Summer (June-August): Perfect weather for hiking, warmest temperatures, peak tourist season, highest prices, long daylight hours, festival season.

Winter (December-March): World-class skiing, Christmas markets, winter sports paradise, expensive ski resorts, shorter daylight, magical snowy landscapes.

Spring (April-May): Mild weather, fewer crowds, moderate prices, wildflower blooms, excellent for cultural sites, unpredictable mountain weather.

Fall (September-October): Pleasant temperatures, autumn colors, harvest season, good weather, fewer tourists, excellent value before ski season.

🌍 What to discover in CH?

Alpine perfection with pristine mountains, luxury watches, and world-famous chocolate culture. Experience efficient trains, medieval towns, and outdoor adventures in Europe's playground. The country offers incredible hiking, skiing, and luxury experiences with stunning mountain scenery. Switzerland provides reliability, beauty, and luxury in compact Alpine paradise.

Day 1

Geneva

Where the world's tallest fountain soars above Europe's diplomatic heart

★★★★★
1

Geneva's Jet d'Eau launches 500 liters of water per second 140 meters skyward from the harbor jetty. First installed in 1891 as a simple pressure-release valve for the Coulouvrenière hydraulic plant, it became a monument in its own right and was relocated to the end of the jetty in 1951. Water exits at 200 km/h; at any moment, 7 tons of water hang suspended in midair.

⏱ 20 min
2

St. Pierre Cathedral, built between 1160 and 1232 in Romanesque and Gothic styles, crowns the old town. In 1536, John Calvin turned it into the temple of Protestant Reformation—his wooden chair still sits in the nave. The archaeological site beneath the cathedral, opened in 1986, reveals 2,000 years of religious history, from 4th-century early Christian mosaics to the foundations of the present structure. Climb 157 steps to the north tower's summit for panoramic views over the lake, city, and Mont Blanc on clear days.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Palais des Nations, European headquarters of the UN since 1946, is the second-largest UN complex after New York. Constructed between 1929 and 1938 for the League of Nations, it hosts around 10,000 meetings annually. On Place des Nations, the 12-meter-tall Broken Chair—a sculpture by Daniel Berset created in 1997—stands as a powerful symbol against anti-personnel mines.

⏱ 75 min
4

The Reformation Wall in Parc des Bastions stretches 100 meters and was erected between 1909 and 1917 to mark the 400th anniversary of Calvin's birth. Four central statues, each 5 meters tall, represent Calvin, Farel, Beza, and Knox.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Stroll through Geneva's upper old town: La Treille, the city's oldest promenade, shelters the "world's longest bench" (120 meters, 180 slats). Place du Bourg-de-Four, Geneva's oldest square, was once a Roman forum.

⏱ 60 min

Mini-cruise on Lake Geneva from Quai du Mont-Blanc: the yellow mouettes (water shuttles) link both shores in 10 minutes—public transport that doubles as a scenic boat ride.

⏱ 30 min

Day 2

Zermatt

A timeless car-free village cradled by the iconic 4,478-meter Matterhorn

★★★★★
1

Zermatt, a car-free village for decades—only electric vehicles circulate—nestles at the foot of the Viège valley, facing the Matterhorn (4,478 m), whose pyramidal silhouette looms from virtually every street. The name "Matterhorn" first appeared in 1682, derived from "Matte" (the grassy plain at the valley floor, "zur Matt") and "Horn" (peak, summit). Locals simply call it "ds'Horu."

⏱ 60 min
2

The Matterhorn Museum - Zermatlantis, designed underground like an archaeological site, recreates the 19th-century village of Zermatt and tells the story of the Matterhorn's conquest. Centerpiece: the broken rope from the first ascent on July 14, 1865, displayed in the "Matterhorn" hall. That day, the seven-man rope team led by Englishman Edward Whymper reached the summit at 1:40 PM via the Hörnli Ridge, beating the Italian rope team of Jean-Antoine Carrel by three days. But on the descent, the rope snapped: four climbers (Croz, Hadow, Hudson, and Douglas) plummeted down the north face. Lord Douglas's body was never found.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Mountaineers' Cemetery, adjacent to St. Peter's Anglican Church, gathers the graves of those who lost their lives on the peaks around Zermatt. Since 1865, nearly 600 people have died on the Matterhorn alone—an average of four per year. Headstones often bear simple engravings of rope and ice axe. The first woman to climb the Matterhorn was Englishwoman Lucy Walker in 1871, at age 34, wearing a flannel skirt and accompanied by guide Melchior Anderegg. Zermatt guide Ulrich Inderbinen, who lived to 103, summited the Matterhorn over 370 times and made his last ascent at age 90.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Explore the Gorner Gorge (Gornerschlucht): a 200-meter trail of walkways and staircases winds above the tumultuous waters of the Gornervispa. The gorge, carved into rock by meltwater from the Gorner glacier, is especially dramatic after spring snowmelt.

⏱ 45 min

Stroll along Zermatt's Bahnhofstrasse and beside the Vispa: ancient mazots (larch grain stores on stilts) and raccards (barns) from the old village contrast with modern shops and hotels. The Kirchbrücke bridge offers one of the most photographed framings of the Matterhorn.

⏱ 60 min
Day 3

Zermatt

A timeless car-free village cradled by the iconic 4,478-meter Matterhorn

★★★★★
1

The Gornergrat cogwheel train, inaugurated in 1898, climbs in 33 minutes from Zermatt (1,604 m) to the Gornergrat platform (3,089 m). It's Switzerland's first open-air electric rack railway. At the summit, panoramas embrace 29 peaks over 4,000 meters, including Monte Rosa (4,634 m, Switzerland's highest point), the Matterhorn, and the Gorner glacier stretching through the valley for roughly 12 km.

⏱ 90 min
2

Matterhorn Glacier Paradise, accessible via the tri-cable Matterhorn Glacier Ride I and II gondolas, reaches 3,883 m—Europe's highest cable car station. The observation platform overlooks 38 peaks above 4,000 m and 14 glaciers, with views spanning three countries (Switzerland, Italy, France). The Glacier Palace, carved 15 meters beneath the ice surface, showcases sculptures and crystals in chambers held at a constant -5°C.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Descend on foot from Gornergrat via the Panoramaweg trail: photo stops at Rotenboden (views over Riffelsee where the Matterhorn reflects) and Riffelberg, before descending to Zermatt. This 9-km trail with 1,500 m descent takes 3 to 4 hours and delivers ever-changing alpine vistas.

⏱ 210 min

Five Lakes hike from Sunnegga (reachable via 3-minute underground funicular): the circuit passes Stellisee, Grindjisee, Grünsee, Moosjisee, and Leisee, each offering a different Matterhorn reflection depending on time and light.

⏱ 180 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 117 km · 2h25
Day 4

Wengen

A Belle Époque hamlet perched high above the land of 72 waterfalls

★★★★★
1

Lauterbrunnen Valley, a deep glacial U-valley bordered by near-vertical 300-meter cliffs, counts 72 waterfalls over 8 kilometers. In 1911, 19-year-old J.R.R. Tolkien walked this valley during a trip to Switzerland—he drew inspiration here for Middle-earth's landscapes, notably Rivendell, in The Lord of the Rings.

⏱ 30 min
2

Staubbach Falls, at the village entrance, plunges 297 meters in free fall—Switzerland's tallest single-drop waterfall. Its name ("dust stream") comes from the thermal updraft that disperses water into mist before it hits the ground. In 1779, Goethe, mesmerized by the spectacle, composed his poem "Song of the Spirits over the Waters." In summer, a gallery allows visitors to walk behind the fall.

⏱ 45 min
3

Trümmelbach Falls, 3 km from the village, form Europe's largest subterranean waterfalls. Ten cascades fed by meltwater from the Eiger, Mönch, and Jungfrau glaciers thunder inside the mountain, accessible since 1877 via an elevator (100 m ascent), then a network of 600 meters of galleries, tunnels, and platforms across 140 meters of vertical drop. Flow can reach 20,000 liters per second, carrying over 20,000 tons of gravel annually.

⏱ 90 min
4

Mürrenbachfall, toward Stechelberg, plunges 417 meters: Switzerland's tallest waterfall (in multiple tiers). Less famous than Staubbach because it's partially hidden among rocks, it's most spectacular in spring after snowmelt.

⏱ 30 min
5

Wengen, first mentioned in 1268, perches on a plateau at 1,274 m above Lauterbrunnen Valley. The village is car-free and accessible only by the Wengernalpbahn (WAB) cogwheel railway, in service since 1893. The first inn opened in 1835 at the Wengernalp pass, but true tourism boom came with the train. Belle Époque hotels in the center recall the golden age of English tourism.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Valley floor hike from Lauterbrunnen to Stechelberg (8 km, nearly flat) along the Weisse Lütschine: this family-friendly trail passes several major waterfalls and offers full immersion in the glacial valley.

⏱ 150 min

Wengen-Lauterbrunnen descent trail (6 km, -500 m elevation, 1h30): the path crosses forests and alpine meadows with plunging views over the valley and the Eiger-Mönch-Jungfrau trio. The WAB train climbs back up in 14 minutes.

⏱ 90 min
Day 5

Wengen

A Belle Époque hamlet perched high above the land of 72 waterfalls

★★★★★
1

Mürren, a car-free village at 1,638 m, occupies a natural terrace balcony above Lauterbrunnen Valley, separated from the valley floor by a nearly 800-meter cliff. This is where Sir Arnold Lunn invented modern slalom on January 21, 1922—the first "turning race around flags" timed solely by stopwatch, without style judges. The Kandahar Ski Club, which he founded at Mürren in 1924, organized the first Inferno race on January 29, 1928: 17 skiers launched from the Schilthorn summit (2,970 m) toward Lauterbrunnen (800 m)—14.9 km and 2,170 m of vertical drop, the world's longest amateur descent, still raced every January.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Schilthorn (2,970 m), accessible by cable car from Stechelberg or Mürren, owes its worldwide fame to the James Bond film On Her Majesty's Secret Service (1969) starring George Lazenby. The Piz Gloria revolving restaurant at the summit, built specially for filming as villain Blofeld's lair, completes one rotation in 45 minutes facing over 200 Alpine peaks, including the Eiger, Mönch, and Jungfrau. The interactive Bond World exhibition chronicles the film's production.

⏱ 75 min
3

At the Birg intermediate station (2,677 m), the Thrill Walk is a 200-meter walkway bolted to the vertical wall: glass floor, crawl tunnel, and metal grates suspended above the void offer a dizzying route facing Lauterbrunnen Valley.

⏱ 30 min
4

Kleine Scheidegg (2,061 m), the pass between Eiger and Lauberhorn, is reachable by train from Wengen (25 minutes). It's the departure point for the Jungfraubahn to Jungfraujoch (3,454 m). The panorama facing the Eiger's north face is among the most famous in the Bernese Oberland.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Ascend to Mürren (1,638 m) by cable car from Stechelberg, then continue to Schilthorn (2,970 m). The full journey takes about 24 minutes. The Piz Gloria revolving restaurant at the summit delivers 360° panoramas over the Eiger, Mönch, and Jungfrau.

⏱ 180 min

Männlichen-Kleine Scheidegg hike (4.5 km, 1h15, nearly flat): considered one of the most beautiful panoramic walks in the Alps. The trail follows the ridge facing the Eiger-Mönch-Jungfrau trio, with no technical difficulty. Access Männlichen (2,230 m) via cable car from Wengen.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 22 km · 30min
Day 6

Grindelwald

Alpine adventure beneath the legendary north face of the Eiger

★★★★☆
1

Grindelwald village, mentioned as early as 1146, occupies a verdant hollow ringed by the Eiger (3,967 m), Mönch, and Wetterhorn. Nicknamed "glacier village," it welcomed its first tourists—mostly English—from the late 18th century, drawn by glaciers that then descended all the way to the valley floor. The first hotel, the Schwarzer Adler, opened in 1820, and the first carriage road followed in 1860.

⏱ 60 min
2

Take the gondola up to First (2,168 m) then walk the First Cliff Walk, a metal walkway suspended along the cliff face, extended by a single-cable bridge and a platform jutting 45 meters above the void. Opened in 2015, this installation offers direct views of the Eiger and Wetterhorn. The gondola ascent takes 25 minutes from the village, with two intermediate stations (Bort and Schreckfeld).

⏱ 90 min
3

From First, the trail leads in 1h30 to Bachalpsee (2,265 m), an alpine lake that, in calm weather, mirrors the Schreckhorn and Wetterhorn peaks. This lake sits within a nationally important wetland zone. The trail is moderately difficult, with no technical passages.

⏱ 75 min
4

The Gletscherschlucht (glacier gorge) opens between 300-meter-high rock walls, carved over 250 million years. A network of walkways extends 870 meters into the gorge, where the Lütschine river roars below. The 170-m² Spiderweb net stretched above the canyon allows crossing above the void. The lower glacier that sculpted it has now vanished, but meltwater continues to shape the rock.

⏱ 75 min
5

The Eiger's north face, 1,800 meters of nearly vertical wall, was long nicknamed "Mordwand" (death wall). Early attempts in 1935 and 1936 cost the lives of all engaged climbers. In 1936, the drama of the Kurz-Hinterstoisser rope team marked memories: stuck 15 meters from rescuers, a knot jammed in his carabiner, Toni Kurz spoke his last words: "Ich kann nicht mehr" (I can do no more). The first successful ascent came on July 24, 1938 by the Austro-German team of Heckmair, Vörg, Harrer, and Kasparek.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The First Flyer, an 800-meter-long zipline, propels participants at 84 km/h above alpine meadows from First station. The First Glider, an eagle-shaped structure, flies four passengers in horizontal position above the landscape.

⏱ 30 min

Mountain cart descent from Schreckfeld station: these three-wheeled carts hurtle down alpine slopes on a 3-km track to Bort. Accessible from age 10, no license required.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 120 km · 1h45
Day 7

Lucerne

Where Europe's oldest covered bridge spans centuries of Swiss history

★★★★☆
1

The Kapellbrücke (Chapel Bridge), built around 1333, crosses the Reuss diagonally for 205 meters. Originally conceived as part of the city's fortifications, it bore 158 triangular painted panels from 1614 onward, commissioned by chronicler Renward Cysat and illustrating Swiss history and the lives of patron saints Leodegar and Maurice. The fire of August 18, 1993—likely caused by a discarded cigarette on a moored boat—destroyed two-thirds of the structure and 86 of the 111 remaining panels. Rebuilt in eight months for 3.4 million francs, the bridge reopened on April 14, 1994. Around 13,800 people cross it daily.

⏱ 45 min
2

The octagonal Wasserturm (Water Tower) predates the bridge: built after 1262, likely under Abbot Berthold von Steinbrunn of Murbach, it served variously over its 700 years as prison, torture chamber, treasury, and municipal archives.

⏱ 20 min
3

The Löwendenkmal (Lion Monument), carved into the wall of a former sandstone quarry, measures 10 meters long by 6 meters high. Designed by Danish sculptor Bertel Thorvaldsen and executed by stonemason Lukas Ahorn between 1820 and 1821, it commemorates the roughly 760 Swiss Guards killed on August 10, 1792 defending the Tuileries in Paris during the French Revolution. The Latin inscription "Helvetiorum Fidei ac Virtuti" pays tribute to their loyalty. In 1880, Mark Twain described it as "the most mournful and moving piece of stone in the world." Around 1.4 million visitors come annually to contemplate the dying lion.

⏱ 30 min
4

The Spreuerbrücke (Mill Bridge), Lucerne's second covered bridge, built in 1408, houses a cycle of 67 "Dance of Death" paintings created by Kaspar Meglinger between 1626 and 1635. Unlike the Kapellbrücke, these panels survived intact.

⏱ 30 min
5

The Jesuitenkirche (Jesuit Church), consecrated in 1677, is the first major Baroque sacred building erected in Switzerland. Its single nave in Austrian Baroque style, ornamented with stucco and gilding, contrasts with the austere exterior. Jesuit brothers taught here until the order's dissolution in 1773.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Wander the old town's (Altstadt) alleyways: fresco-painted facades tell the stories of guilds and merchant families. The Weinmarkt, Hirschenplatz, and Kornmarkt squares preserve Renaissance fountains and oriel-windowed houses with carved detail.

⏱ 75 min

Stroll along the quays from Seebrücke to the Hofkirche (St. Leodegar Church), a Renaissance basilica built in 1633-1639 on the foundations of an 8th-century Benedictine monastery. Its organ of 4,950 pipes, installed in 1648, is one of Switzerland's oldest still in operation.

⏱ 60 min
Day 8

Lucerne

Where Europe's oldest covered bridge spans centuries of Swiss history

★★★★☆
1

The Museggmauer, a 13th-century medieval rampart, preserves nine of the original thirty towers. Four are open to the public. The Zytturm (Clock Tower) holds the privilege of running one minute ahead of all other city clocks—a right granted since the 15th century.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Glacier Garden (Gletschergarten), discovered in 1872 during cellar construction, displays glacial potholes carved roughly 20 million years ago, plus a fossilized palm-leaf relief proving the region enjoyed a subtropical climate. An 1896 mirror maze, inspired by the Alhambra, completes the visit.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Cruise on Lake Lucerne (Vierwaldstättersee) aboard paddle-wheel steamboats, some dating from the early 20th century. The journey between Lucerne and Flüelen (southern end of the lake) takes roughly 3 hours and skirts the Rütli Meadow, symbolic site of the 1291 Oath of Confederation.

⏱ 180 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 52 km · 45min
Day 9

Zurich

A vibrant metropolis where Reformation towers meet radical Dada art

★★★★★
1

The Grossmünster, whose construction began in the 12th century, dominates the right bank of the Limmat with its two distinctive towers. Legend has it that Charlemagne discovered the tombs of martyred saints Felix and Regula at this spot. From this church, Ulrich Zwingli launched the Protestant Reformation in Zurich in 1519, profoundly transforming the city's religious and political life. You can climb the south tower (187 steps) for panoramic views over the old town and lake.

⏱ 45 min
2

Cross the Limmat to reach the Fraumünster, a former abbey church founded in 853 by King Louis the German for his daughter Hildegard. The abbey held the right to mint coins, collect market taxes, and raise troops, making it Zurich's dominant power for centuries. Today's main attraction: the five choir windows created by Marc Chagall in 1970, each dedicated to a biblical theme in shades of blue, green, red, yellow, and blue-pink.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Lindenhof, a linden-shaded hill between the two quarters of the old town, occupies the site of a former Roman castrum (Turicum). Excavations have uncovered the fort's foundations. Today, this elevated, tranquil square offers one of the best viewpoints over the Limmat, Niederdorf, and the Grossmünster towers.

⏱ 30 min
4

Bahnhofstrasse, 1.4 km long, links the central station (Hauptbahnhof) to the lake. Laid out in 1864 on the route of the old city moats, it became one of the world's most expensive shopping streets. Beneath the street, bank vaults allegedly hold a significant portion of the world's gold, according to urban legend.

⏱ 45 min
5

The Kunsthaus, founded in 1787, holds Switzerland's most important art collection: works from medieval times to contemporary, with major ensembles by Munch, Giacometti, Monet, and Picasso. The David Chipperfield-designed extension, opened in 2021, doubled the exhibition space.

⏱ 90 min
6

The Niederdorf, nicknamed "Dörfli" by Zurichers, is the pedestrian heart of the right bank: narrow medieval lanes, half-timbered houses, wrought-iron signs, and a concentration of restaurants, cafés, and theaters. Here, the Dada movement was born at Cabaret Voltaire in 1916, founded by Hugo Ball and Emmy Hennings during World War I.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Stroll along the Lake Zurich quays from Bürkliplatz, where the flea market sets up on Saturdays. On clear days, the Glarus Alps chain stands out on the horizon. In summer, public baths (Seebad Enge, Frauenbadi) installed directly on the lake or Limmat have been a Zurich tradition since the 19th century.

⏱ 60 min

Fondue tasting in an old town restaurant or in the Zürichberg hills: Zurich tradition dictates dipping bread clockwise, and whoever loses their piece buys a bottle.

⏱ 90 min
