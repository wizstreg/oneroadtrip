📅 10 days📍 9 stops🚗 960 km⏱ 11h15

This 8-day drive follows the spine of America's civil rights history from Atlanta's Sweet Auburn neighborhood through Tuskegee and Selma, then west along the Tombigbee River to Vicksburg, Natchez, and finally New Orleans. Every night stop carries a distinct weight: the Tuskegee Airmen trained and flew from these fields, 600 marchers crossed the Edmund Pettus Bridge on a March Sunday in 1965, and 200,000 people were sold at the Forks of the Road before Union troops tore the pens down. The architecture - Greek Revival mansions, a 450-foot Art Deco skyscraper, an unfinished octagonal folly - tells the same history from the other side.

Stop 1

Atlanta

Birthplace of Martin Luther King Jr. and the modern civil rights movement, anchored by the Sweet Auburn neighborhood.

★★★★☆
📍 Martin Luther King Jr National Historical Park Atlanta📍 Sweet Auburn Historic District Atlanta📍 Georgia State Capitol Atlanta📍 Ebenezer Baptist Church Atlanta
1

The Sweet Auburn neighborhood holds the first block of the civil rights story. Walk to the Martin Luther King Jr. National Historical Park and start at the Visitor Center - it's free, and a ranger will orient you before you step outside. From there, Ebenezer Baptist Church is just across the promenade: the building where King was baptized and where he and his father co-pastored is still standing, still holding services on Sundays. Stop at the reflecting pool in front of Freedom Hall and read the crypt of King and Coretta Scott King, set flush into the water. Note that the birth home at 501 Auburn Avenue is currently closed for rehabilitation. Arrive by 9 a.m.; birth home tours, when available, fill on a first-come first-served basis with just 10 spots.

⏱ 120 min
2

Down the hill from Sweet Auburn, the Georgia State Capitol stands on the site of Atlanta's original City Hall - Federal troops camped here in 1864, three years before the building began rising. Completed in 1889, its gold dome is plated with gold from Dahlonega, Georgia. On the fourth floor, the Capitol Museum holds Confederate-era flags, portraits, and a famously beloved two-headed calf. Outside, look for the bronze statue of the Original 33 - the thirty-three Black legislators expelled from the Georgia General Assembly in 1868, a story the building rarely volunteers. Free admission; open Monday through Friday 8 a.m. to 5 p.m.

⏱ 75 min
3

Before or after the Capitol, walk Royal Street and the Sweet Auburn Curb Market on Auburn Avenue - operating since 1918 and still doing business with produce vendors and a rotating roster of small food stalls. The neighborhood's double history - as the cradle of Atlanta's Black business class and the birthplace of King - is visible in the blend of churches, old storefronts, and park infrastructure. If you're here on a weekday, swing by Fire Station No. 6 on the park grounds: it was among the first desegregated firehouses in the Deep South, staffed by NPS volunteers.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Civil Rights Walk of Fame runs along Auburn Avenue within the King Historic District. Walk it slowly: bronze footprints are embedded in the sidewalk honoring inductees including Rosa Parks and Coretta Scott King. The whole stretch from the Visitor Center to the King Center and back is about half a mile. Plan for late afternoon when the light is lower and foot traffic thins.

⏱ 45 min

Dinner in Atlanta before tomorrow's early departure east on I-85. The Ponce City Market on North Highland Avenue brings together local restaurants in a restored 1920s Sears building - a comfortable place to decompress after a day heavy with history. Alternatively, the small restaurants on Edgewood Avenue are a ten-minute walk from the park.

⏱ 90 min

Stop 2

Tuskegee

Home of the Tuskegee Airmen, Booker T. Washington's university, and the site of one of American medicine's darkest chapters.

★★★★★
📍 Tuskegee Airmen National Historic Site Moton Field📍 Tuskegee Institute National Historic Site📍 George Washington Carver Museum Tuskegee📍 The Oaks Booker T Washington Tuskegee
1

Moton Field sits just off I-85 and the airport runway is still active - you may hear a small plane overhead as you walk into Hangar 1. Inside, a canary-yellow PT-17 Stearman biplane is the first thing you see: that is where it began, primary flight training for men the Army had decided would probably fail. Between 1941 and 1946, 992 Black pilots were trained here and at nearby Tuskegee Army Air Field. The park is free and open Monday through Saturday. Walk through both hangars: Hangar 1 covers the training, the Double V campaign - victory abroad against fascism, victory at home against racism - and includes a WWII-era flight simulator. Hangar 2 centers a restored P-51 Mustang. Climb the three flights to the original control tower for a 360-degree view of the airfield.

⏱ 90 min
2

A ten-minute drive brings you to the Tuskegee University campus and the George Washington Carver Museum. The building was the school's laundry until 1938; Carver himself funded much of its conversion into a museum, dedicated in 1941 by Henry Ford. Inside: his laboratory equipment - he started with a single microscope - alongside the more than 300 industrial products he developed from peanuts, sweet potatoes, and native clays. Also on display, the paintings he cared about as much as his chemistry. The museum is open Thursday and Friday, 9 a.m. to 4:30 p.m. Call ahead to confirm hours.

⏱ 75 min
3

A few hundred meters from the Carver Museum on the same campus, The Oaks - Booker T. Washington's home - is open for NPS ranger-led tours. Washington founded the school in 1881 with nothing, building it literally brick by brick using student labor, then recruited Carver from Iowa State in 1896. The small campus cemetery nearby holds the graves of both Washington and Carver. Walk it: it takes ten minutes and it is quietly affecting. The campus itself - the brick buildings, the live oaks - was built largely by students learning trades while they earned their education.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Tuskegee Human and Civil Rights Multicultural Center, on Franklin Road in downtown Tuskegee, documents the 40-year Tuskegee Syphilis Study - in which the U.S. Public Health Service monitored the progression of untreated syphilis in 399 Black men who were told they had 'bad blood.' The study ran from 1932 to 1972, long after penicillin was available and known to be effective. President Clinton formally apologized in 1997. Check hours before visiting.

⏱ 60 min

If you have energy in the evening, the Tuskegee University campus invites a quiet walk after hours. The 1922 Booker T. Washington Monument - sculptor Charles Keck's bronze depicting Washington lifting the veil of ignorance - stands near the main entrance. The campus has been a National Historic Landmark since 1966, the first HBCU to receive that designation.

⏱ 40 min

Stop 3

Selma

Where Bloody Sunday and the Selma-to-Montgomery marches broke open the national debate over voting rights in 1965.

★★☆☆☆
📍 Edmund Pettus Bridge Selma Alabama📍 Selma Interpretive Center NPS📍 Brown Chapel AME Church Selma📍 National Voting Rights Museum Selma
1

Start at the Selma Interpretive Center at 2 Broad Street - the NPS site at the foot of the Edmund Pettus Bridge - and watch the short film before stepping outside. The bridge was built in 1940 and named for a Confederate general and Ku Klux Klan Grand Dragon. On March 7, 1965, 600 marchers led by John Lewis and Hosea Williams crossed it on their way to Montgomery. State troopers met them with clubs and tear gas. The televised beatings - Bloody Sunday - shifted national opinion enough that the Voting Rights Act of 1965 passed five months later. Walk the bridge. It is the same span, the same steel, the same view of the Alabama River below.

⏱ 60 min
2

Brown Chapel AME Church, six blocks north on Martin Luther King Street, was where the marchers assembled on March 7 and again on March 21 for the successful Selma to Montgomery march. King gave several of his key Selma speeches here. The George Washington Carver public housing development next door - named, with painful irony, for the Tuskegee scientist - served as a staging ground and sleeping quarters for out-of-town participants. Tours of the church by appointment; call Joyce O'Neal at (334) 875-3112. The exterior and the adjacent marker are worth the short walk even without a tour.

⏱ 60 min
3

The National Voting Rights Museum and Institute at 6 Hwy 80 East covers the full arc of the struggle for the franchise. Near the entrance, a registrar's desk uses a jar of jelly beans as a prop: one of the actual tests required applicants to count the number of jelly beans in a jar before they could even take the 68-question literacy exam. The museum tracks this history from Reconstruction through the passage of the 1965 Act and into the present. The Old Depot Museum a few blocks away keeps a Civil Rights Room with hospital logs from Bloody Sunday listing each person injured and how.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Sturdivant Hall, on Mabry Street in downtown Selma, was built in 1856 in Greek Revival style and is now a house museum with guided tours. It represents the antebellum prosperity that Selma's cotton economy produced - prosperity built entirely on enslaved labor. The contrast with the civil rights sites a few blocks away is not incidental: they are the same city, the same history, separated by a century. The collection includes period furniture, china, and artwork. Admission charged.

⏱ 60 min

In the late afternoon, drive part of the Selma to Montgomery National Historic Trail east on U.S. 80 - even 15-20 miles gives you the flat, open farmland the marchers crossed in March 1965. The Lowndes County Interpretive Center (about 35 miles east in White Hall) has a 25-minute film and exhibits on the campsites used during the march. A manageable detour if you want to understand the scale of the 54-mile walk.

⏱ 90 min

Stop 4

Demopolis

A small Alabama river town with two of the Deep South's finest antebellum houses and the Tombigbee running past.

★★★★★
📍 Gaineswood National Historic Landmark Demopolis Alabama📍 Bluff Hall Demopolis Alabama📍 Demopolis City Landing Tombigbee River📍 Lyon Hall Demopolis Alabama
1

Gaineswood at 805 South Cedar Avenue is one of the more unusual buildings you will see on this trip. General Nathan Bryan Whitfield started with a two-room dogtrot cabin in 1843 and spent the next 18 years redesigning and expanding it into a Greek Revival mansion - acting as his own architect, drawing from architectural treatises and his travels north. He was also a musician and inventor: the barrel organ he designed, which plays from large cylinders with pins, is still functional. The two front rooms have matching domed ceilings with cupolas; the mirrors in the ballroom are angled to reflect each other to infinity. By 1860, Whitfield owned 235 enslaved people and the plantation produced 600 bales of cotton a year. The house cannot be separated from that labor. Tours run with local docents who know every detail. Admission charged.

⏱ 90 min
2

Bluff Hall, a ten-minute walk from Gaineswood, sits on a white limestone cliff above the Tombigbee River - that is where the name comes from. The Federal-style house was built in 1832 by merchant Allan Glover as a wedding gift for his daughter and her husband, cotton plantation owner Francis Strother Lyon. The Marengo County Historical Society has restored roughly 75 percent of the authentic furnishings. The textile and clothing collection is one of the stronger holdings. On the grounds, a log cabin saved from a demolition site was found to date to the original French expatriate Vine and Olive Colony settlement of 1817.

⏱ 60 min
3

Lyon Hall on Main Street was built between 1850 and 1853 by enslaved workers for George Strother Gaines - the same Indian trader who originally owned the land Gaineswood was built on. The connection between these three properties maps a dense network of land ownership, political power, and forced labor in a small corner of the Alabama Black Belt. Check local hours before visiting.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Demopolis City Landing on the Tombigbee River gives you a different angle on this town. The river was the economic artery that carried Demopolis cotton to Mobile and from there to global markets. A fifteen-minute walk along the riverfront connects the antebellum architectural legacy of the town to the water that made it possible. Fishing boats still use the landing; it's a quiet place in late afternoon.

⏱ 45 min

Foscue Creek Park, just west of town on U.S. 80, offers a short walk down to the water - a good way to clear your head after a day of house museums. The park is operated by the Army Corps of Engineers and has a boat ramp and picnic area along the river. It's a place where the landscape reasserts itself.

⏱ 40 min

Stop 5

Vicksburg

Site of the 47-day Civil War siege that split the Confederacy and gave the Union control of the Mississippi River.

★★☆☆☆
📍 Vicksburg National Military Park Mississippi📍 USS Cairo Museum Vicksburg📍 Old Court House Museum Vicksburg📍 Lower Mississippi River Museum Vicksburg
1

Vicksburg National Military Park covers 1,800 acres along the ridge line the Confederates held in 1863. Start at the Visitor Center, then take the 16-mile one-way tour road - it follows the Union siege lines and passes 1,325 monuments, 144 emplaced cannons, and 20 miles of reconstructed earthworks. Plan at least two hours with stops. The Illinois Monument has 47 steps - one for each day of the siege, which ran May 18 to July 4, 1863. The Kentucky Monument at the end stages a bronze meeting between Abraham Lincoln and Jefferson Davis, both born in Kentucky: they stand at ground level, approachable.

⏱ 150 min
2

The USS Cairo is the park's most visceral artifact. On December 12, 1862, the Union ironclad hit a Confederate electrically detonated mine and sank in 12 minutes in the Yazoo River - the first U.S. warship lost to an electrically detonated mine. It sat in the river mud for 102 years. Raised in 1964 and brought to the park in 1977, the ship sits in an open pavilion near the National Cemetery. Walk alongside it and look into the explosion hole. The adjacent museum holds 3,000 recovered artifacts: sailors' clothing, personal effects, a bottle of pepper sauce reportedly still edible when opened.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Old Court House Museum on Cherry Street holds the chair Jefferson Davis sat in when he addressed Vicksburg citizens before the war, alongside one of the better Civil War document collections in Mississippi. The 1858 building survived the siege - Grant raised the U.S. flag from its cupola when the city surrendered on July 4. The Lower Mississippi River Museum down on the riverfront is a different kind of story: the working history of the river - commerce, floods, dredging - told through a decommissioned survey vessel and a full-scale Corps of Engineers model of the river's flow patterns.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

The Vicksburg National Cemetery, within the park, contains 17,000 Union soldiers - 12,954 of them unidentified. Walk it in the late afternoon when the light goes low across the white headstones. The cemetery was established in 1866, the same year Congress passed the first civil rights legislation after the war. Both things happening simultaneously.

⏱ 45 min

The Mississippi is wide and brown from the levee road below the bluffs, and the hairpin curve in the river is visible from ground level. The Confederate guns were positioned up here precisely for that bend: any Union ship had to slow through the curve, directly under the artillery. Lincoln called Vicksburg 'the key to the war.' The geography makes that legible without needing a map.

⏱ 30 min

Stop 6

Natchez

The wealthiest city in the antebellum South, built on cotton and slavery, now home to the Forks of the Road slave market and Longwood.

★★☆☆☆
📍 Melrose Estate Natchez National Historical Park📍 Longwood Natchez Mississippi📍 Forks of the Road Slave Market Natchez📍 Natchez Trace Parkway southern terminus📍 Under-the-Hill Saloon Natchez
1

The Forks of the Road site, at the intersection of Liberty Road and D'Evereux Drive, is now a city park with concrete-embedded slave chains and information panels. By the 1840s this was the second-largest domestic slave market in the United States. An estimated 200,000 men, women, and children were sold here, transported from the Upper South. In 1863, soldiers of the 58th United States Colored Troops - many of whom had been enslaved themselves - tore down the slave pens and used the timber as barracks. The site is open from sunup to sundown, free.

⏱ 45 min
2

Melrose Estate, managed by the National Park Service, is the most intact antebellum property in Natchez for understanding the full picture of plantation life. The 80-acre grounds include the main Greek Revival house, two slave cabins, a kitchen, dairy, smokehouse, and carriage house. Ranger-guided tours of the mansion are offered daily at set times - reserve on Recreation.gov. Grounds and outbuildings are free. After the owner's death in 1883, two formerly enslaved women - Jane Johnson and Alice Sims - maintained the house for nearly twenty years. Alice Sims died in the 1930s at 96. Jane Johnson at 103 in the 1940s. Their names are in the record.

⏱ 90 min
3

Longwood, at 140 Lower Woodville Road, is the largest octagonal house in the United States - and it was never finished. Philadelphia architect Samuel Sloan designed it in 1859 for cotton planter Haller Nutt; the million bricks were made on site, most of them handmade by enslaved workers. When the Civil War started in 1861, the Philadelphia craftsmen went home. Only nine of the planned 32 rooms were ever completed. The Nutt family lived in that basement level for the rest of their lives. Tour guides let you walk through the finished rooms and up onto the upper floors, where framing and tools sit exactly as they were left in 1861.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

The southern terminus of the Natchez Trace Parkway begins just northeast of town. Even a ten or fifteen-mile drive north gives you the character of the road: a two-lane parkway through second-growth forest, no commercial traffic, no billboards, following the ancient trading path that connected Natchez to Nashville. Mount Locust, about 15 miles up, is one of the oldest structures on the Trace, originally an inn for Kaintock flatboatmen walking home after floating cargo down to Natchez.

⏱ 60 min

The Under-the-Hill Saloon on Silver Street is one of the oldest bars on the Mississippi, in what remains of the old river port district known as Natchez Under-the-Hill. The neighborhood was largely destroyed by the river changing course. What is left - a single block of buildings above the water - gives you a sense of how much of Natchez's wealth arrived and departed by water. The saloon has been serving since 1876. Have a drink and watch the current.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 7

St Francisville

The Louisiana parish where Audubon painted 32 Birds of America in 1821, on grounds that now include restored slave cabins.

★★★★☆
📍 Audubon State Historic Site Oakley Plantation St Francisville Louisiana📍 Rosedown Plantation State Historic Site St Francisville
1

Pull off U.S. 61 onto Highway 965 south of St. Francisville. Oakley Plantation house was built around 1806 with West Indies influence - jalousied galleries to catch river breezes, simple Federal-period interiors. In 1821, John James Audubon arrived to teach drawing to Eliza Pirrie, daughter of the plantation's owners. He lasted four months before a dispute ended the arrangement. In those four months, he painted or began 32 of the bird illustrations that became Birds of America. The surrounding forest still holds most of the species he painted. Two slave cabins and a restored plantation kitchen are open on the grounds. Open daily 9 a.m. to 5 p.m.; guided house tours on the hour from 10 a.m.

Stop 8

Baton Rouge

Louisiana's capital, dominated by Huey Long's 450-foot Art Deco skyscraper and the corridor where he was shot in 1935.

★★★☆☆
📍 Louisiana State Capitol Baton Rouge📍 Old State Capitol Baton Rouge📍 USS Kidd Veterans Museum Baton Rouge📍 Capitol Park Museum Baton Rouge
1

At 450 feet tall with 34 floors, the Louisiana State Capitol is the tallest state capitol in the United States - and it has no dome. Huey Long insisted on a skyscraper, drawing inspiration from Nebraska's 1920s tower. The Art Deco building was completed in 1932, with floors cut from Italian volcanic stone and 26 types of marble throughout. Long is buried in the garden outside, marked by a 12-foot bronze by Charles Keck. Inside, ask to see the assassination hallway: a display case marks the ground-floor corridor where Long was shot on September 8, 1935, by Dr. Carl Weiss, whose father-in-law Long had just gerrymandered out of a judgeship. Bullet holes in a granite column are still visible. Take the elevator to the 27th-floor observation deck for views of Baton Rouge and the Mississippi.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Old State Capitol, half a mile toward the river on North Boulevard, is a Gothic Revival castle built in 1847 and rebuilt in 1882 after an accidental fire. The second version added the famous spiral staircase and a stained glass dome. Mark Twain called it a 'monstrosity.' It now houses the Museum of Political History. The contrast with Long's skyscraper a few blocks away - one a Victorian fantasy, the other a Depression-era tower of ambition - tells you something about Louisiana's political imagination.

⏱ 75 min
3

The USS Kidd, a World War II Fletcher-class destroyer, is moored permanently on the Mississippi riverfront. The ship fought at Leyte Gulf and survived a kamikaze strike in April 1945 that killed 38 men. You can walk nearly the entire vessel - engine room, combat information center, officers' quarters - and the adjacent Veterans Museum holds aircraft and personal accounts from multiple conflicts. The Louisiana State Capitol up the hill, the Old State Capitol behind you, and the destroyer on the water: three different scales of Louisiana history within a fifteen-minute walk.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

The Capitol Park Museum on River Road covers Louisiana history from pre-Columbian cultures through Hurricane Katrina. The 'Experiencing Louisiana' gallery has a full-scale oil platform section, a French Quarter streetscape, and one of the stronger Mardi Gras artifact collections in the state. Free admission.

⏱ 60 min

Dinner in Baton Rouge before the final leg to New Orleans tomorrow. The stretch of Government Street between downtown and Mid-City has a concentration of local restaurants that aren't aimed at tourists. Book ahead - Baton Rouge is a university city and good tables fill quickly on weekday evenings.

⏱ 90 min

Stop 9

New Orleans

Where the trip ends: Congo Square, Jackson Square, Frenchmen Street, and the city that absorbed every current this journey traced.

★★★★★
📍 Jackson Square New Orleans French Quarter📍 Garden District New Orleans Magazine Street📍 Frenchmen Street New Orleans📍 Congo Square New Orleans Louis Armstrong Park
1

Jackson Square is the geometry of New Orleans: St. Louis Cathedral at the far end, the Pontalba buildings running along the sides, the Mississippi one block south behind the levee. Artist stalls line the iron fence around the central park. The Cabildo - where the Louisiana Purchase was signed in 1803 - is on the left as you face the Cathedral; the Presbytère on the right. Walk through the square slowly. The sound design is extraordinary: a brass band at one end, a solo trumpeter somewhere else, the calliope of the Steamboat Natchez carrying from the water.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Garden District, accessible by the St. Charles Streetcar from Canal Street, is two miles of oak-lined streets and 19th-century mansions - built by American merchants who arrived after the Louisiana Purchase in a different architectural vocabulary from the French Quarter. Magazine Street runs parallel to St. Charles with six miles of local restaurants, vintage stores, art galleries, and coffee shops. Walk between the two streets, weaving between them: it takes the better part of an afternoon.

⏱ 120 min
3

Congo Square, in Louis Armstrong Park just north of the French Quarter, is where enslaved people gathered on Sunday afternoons from the 1740s onward - their one permitted period of relative freedom each week. By 1819 as many as 600 people were gathering to drum, sing, and dance, bringing musical traditions from West Africa, Haiti, and the Caribbean. Musicologists trace the origins of jazz, blues, and ultimately rock and roll to those Sunday afternoons. The square is a small park now, marked by a plaque. It deserves more than ten minutes.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Café du Monde on Decatur Street has been serving beignets and café au lait since 1862. The outdoor seating faces Jackson Square across the street. Order the beignets with a café au lait and accept that powdered sugar will go everywhere. The line in the morning is long; mid-afternoon is faster. Cash is preferred.

⏱ 45 min

Frenchmen Street, on the edge of the Marigny neighborhood just east of the French Quarter, is where New Orleans musicians actually play on weekday nights. The Spotted Cat, Blue Nile, and Snug Harbor all book working jazz bands. Unlike Bourbon Street, the sound here comes from musicians - not sound systems - and the crowd came to listen. Walk the three-block stretch around 9 p.m. and let the music settle the trip.

⏱ 120 min
