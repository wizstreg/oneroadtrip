Horn Bad Meinberg

1

Five towering rock pillars surge from the forest floor to 40 meters—nothing but stone and sky. The Externsteine have no archaeological proof of Saxon worship, yet their Romanesque Relief of the Descent from the Cross (circa 1160) remains the oldest large medieval rock carving north of the Alps. Look closely at the tilted column beneath Christ's feet: some scholars read it as a defeated Irminsul—the Saxon sacred pillar—bowing to Christianity. Climbing the rocks costs around €4 (April to October). Allow 90 minutes.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Teutoburg Forest unfolds along the Hermannsweg, a 156-kilometer marked trail. From the Externsteine, a 90-minute loop through beech woods shows why Roman legions lost their way here in 9 AD. No technical scrambling, easy terrain. Take the path climbing behind the rock formation and loop back via Bruchsee pond—weekday silence guaranteed.

⏱ 90 min

Hameln

★★☆☆☆
1

On June 26, 1284, 130 children left Hamelin and never returned. The town chronicle records the event, and the inscription on the Rattenfängerhaus beam repeats the exact date in medieval Low German. The house, built in 1602, owes its name to this inscription—not to the piper himself. Just beside it, the Bungelosenstrasse: according to tradition, no musician plays there—a memorial to the lost children. Respect for mourning or tourist staging? Locals don't settle the question.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

If you're here on a Sunday from May to September, the Rattenfänger-Freilichtspiel open-air play performs at noon on the Hochzeitshaus terrace—free, 30 minutes, medieval costumes. Otherwise, the mechanical figures on the Hochzeitshaus facade animate at 1:05pm and 3:35pm. The municipal museum, housed in two Renaissance houses on Osterstrasse, completes the visit for those curious about the historical thesis (Ostsiedlung migration to Pomerania).

⏱ 60 min

Hildesheim

★★★★☆
1

A rose bush clings to the apse wall, rooted for centuries—burned under bombardment in March 1945, it sent up 25 new shoots eight weeks later. The Thousand-year Rose Bush (Tausendjähriger Rosenstock) has been documented for over 400 years, linked to the diocese's founding around 815. The cathedral became a UNESCO site in 1985. Inside: the Bernward Column (1020), New Testament scenes spiraling up a 4-meter bronze shaft. Blooms in May, visit through the Domfoyer, 10am-5pm.

2

Its 13th-century painted Romanesque ceiling is one of the few large painted Romanesque ceilings preserved in Europe. Free entry, best light in the morning. Allow 30 minutes. After the visit, return to Hanover: 40 km, 35 minutes via A7.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Goslar

★★★★☆
1

A thousand years of ore without interruption: the Rammelsberg is, according to UNESCO, the only deposit in the world exploited continuously for over 1,000 years (until 1988), listed along with the old town in 1992. The guided tour descends into the galleries aboard the old miners' train—allow 2.5 hours, around €18. Book ahead in high season. The Feuergezähe Gewölbe from the 13th century, still visible on site, is presented by UNESCO sources as the oldest vaulted mining chamber in Europe.

⏱ 150 min
2

At night, Goslar's alleys smell of damp timber and ancient stone—roughly 1,500 half-timbered houses compressed into one square kilometer. The Kaiserpfalz (Imperial Palace), founded by the Ottonians around 1005 and brought to its apex by the Salians in the 11th century, is the largest preserved secular building of that century in Germany. The Marktplatz with its bronze imperial eagle, and the Frankenberg church with its legends of miner ghosts. The old town is compact: everything walkable in two hours.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Neuwerkkirche, hidden behind the market square, is one of Goslar's most austere Romanesque structures—no guided tour, no ticket. You push the door and enter. A walled garden behind the apse, stone benches, silence. A useful counterpoint after the Rammelsberg. Generally open mornings until noon.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Discover the Upper Harz Water Management System, an ingenious network of dams, reservoirs and channels listed by UNESCO, built to power the historic mines. This 800-year-old feat of hydraulic engineering stretches across the Harz mountains, demonstrating how medieval miners harnessed water power with remarkable sophistication.

⏱ 60 min

Schierke

1

Schierke is the last village before the summit—a handful of inns, a church, fir trees. Ideal for observing the atmosphere on Walpurgis Night eve (April 30th) before the Brocken ascent the next morning. Outside of April 30th, it's simply quiet and well positioned.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Goethe climbed on December 10, 1777 through snow and fog—today you can reach the Brocken (1,141 m) from Schierke via the Goetheweg in 3 hours, or take the Brockenbahn steam train from Wernigerode (round-trip around €49). At the summit: optical phenomenon of the Brocken specter (your shadow projected with a halo on the mist), museum on alpine flora and the history of the East-West German border—the Brocken was a closed Soviet military zone until December 3, 1989. Check weather at brocken.de before departing.

Thale

★★☆☆☆
1

Touristy to the bone—and yet the site is real. The Hexentanzplatz (454 m) was according to tradition a Saxon gathering place, forbidden after Frankish Christianization and renamed "Witches' Dance Floor." The cable car ascends from the Bode Valley. Below: a gorge with 280-meter cliffs, the roaring Bode River. Off-season and on weekdays, the plateau recovers a strange atmosphere. On April 30th, thousands in costume gather there for Walpurgis Night.

⏱ 75 min
2

The giant hoofprint in the granite: geology or legend? The Rosstrappe (403 m), facing the Hexentanzplatz across the Bode Valley, bears a horse-hoof-shaped cavity. The legend of Princess Brunhilde fleeing the giant Bodo (who drowns in the river, giving his name to the Bode) explains it. Access by chairlift or on foot. Less crowded than the Hexentanzplatz, the gorge view from here is superior.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hike along the Bode River on the Hexenstieg trail through the wild gorge

Quedlinburg

★★★★☆
1

In 1936, Himmler seized this church to turn it into an SS temple. He commemorated every July 2nd the millennial anniversary of Henry I's death—a king he presented himself as the spiritual successor to. The Collegiate Church of St. Servatius, a UNESCO site since 1994, had been confiscated from the Protestant community in 1938: the cross taken down, SS flags on the tower. Returned in 1945. The crypt houses Henry I's tomb, founder of the Ottonian dynasty. Free visit, Ottonian liturgical treasure on site.

⏱ 60 min
2

Quedlinburg locals still call the Münzenberg "the crooked hill"—its alleys tumble down following no plan. Over 1,300 half-timbered houses, one of the largest intact medieval ensembles in Germany. Stroll the Schlossberg, descend via Wordgasse (the house from 1310 bears number 3) and reach the Marktplatz for a beer at Brauhaus Lüdde (Blasiistr. 14), which has brewed on site since 1807.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Climb to the Schlossberg for views over Quedlinburg's rooftops and the Harz plain, then descend through cobbled alleys. About 15 minutes from the market square.

⏱ 60 min
