Jaca

★★☆☆☆
1

Jaca was the first capital of the Kingdom of Aragón in the 11th century and a major stopping point on the Camino Aragonés to Santiago de Compostela. Begin with the Cathedral of San Pedro, the first Romanesque cathedral built in Spain - construction began in 1077, almost simultaneously with Santiago de Compostela. The tympanum of the western portal features a Chi-Rho flanked by two lions, one protecting a repentant sinner, the other crushing a serpent. Inside, the pendentive dome above the transept crossing is the oldest in Spain. Note the checkerboard pattern (ajedrezado jaqués), created here and later adopted throughout Romanesque churches along the Way of Saint James.

⏱ 60 min
2

Don't miss the Diocesan Museum of Jaca, housed in the cathedral's cloister - it's an underrated gem. The museum preserves Romanesque frescoes recovered from small churches throughout the Aragonese Pyrenees - some were detached from walls to save them from abandonment and looting. The Romanesque painting gallery, with its figures sporting enormous eyes and intense blue backgrounds, is worth the visit alone. Take the guided tour (included in the ticket price), which lasts about an hour and reveals details invisible to the naked eye.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Citadel of Jaca is the only entirely preserved pentagonal fortress in Spain - one of only two in Europe, along with Liège. Designed by Italian engineer Tiburzio Spannocchi in 1592 under orders from Philip II to defend the border with France, it features a perfect star-shaped plan with five bastions connected by walkways, a deep moat (now home to deer), and a drawbridge. Walk around the ramparts to appreciate the perfect geometric precision of the design. Inside, the chapel and powder magazine are accessible, and the Museum of Military Miniatures recreates battles with 32,000 lead soldiers.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

If you have time at the end of the day, drive 20 minutes southwest to reach the Monastery of San Juan de la Peña, one of Aragón's most spectacular monuments. This 11th-century monastery is semi-troglodytic, wedged beneath an enormous rocky overhang that serves as its natural roof. It was the pantheon of the early kings of Aragón and one of the kingdom's cultural centers. The Romanesque cloister, carved directly beneath the rock, features historiated capitals of extraordinary fineness.

⏱ 120 min

Anso

★★☆☆☆
1

The valleys of Ansó and Hecho are the hidden jewels of the western Aragonese Pyrenees. Ansó, at 860 m altitude, is a museum-village of stone and slate, listed among Spain's Most Beautiful Villages. Its traditional ashlar stone houses, with their cylindrical chimneys typical of Pyrenean architecture, have remained virtually unchanged for centuries. The parish church of San Pedro (16th century), in late Gothic style, dominates the village. Wander through the sloping streets and don't miss the Museo del Traje Ansotano, which displays the valley's traditional costumes - worn until the mid-20th century.

⏱ 75 min
2

Then head to the Hecho Valley (30 min drive), parallel to Ansó. The village of Hecho is another magnificent example of Pyrenean stone architecture, with narrow streets and a Romanesque church. Three kilometers north, the Monastery of San Pedro de Siresa (9th-11th centuries), one of Aragón's oldest, was founded in the Carolingian era and is said to have housed the Holy Grail before its transfer to San Juan de la Peña. The church, massive and austere, is a gem of Aragonese pre-Romanesque art.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Drive up the Hecho Valley to the Selva de Oza, a beech and fir forest occupying the floor of an ancient glacial valley - one of the most pristine landscapes in the Pyrenees. The Camino de la Boca del Infierno (Mouth of Hell) follows a spectacular trail along limestone gorges. Allow 2 hours round trip for this easy walk that follows the Aragón Subordán river between 200-meter vertical walls.

⏱ 150 min

Torla

★★★☆☆
1

Full day dedicated to Ordesa and Monte Perdido National Park, UNESCO World Heritage since 1977 and Spain's second oldest national park (established 1918). The Monte Perdido massif (3,355 m) is Europe's highest limestone massif. Stay the night before in Torla, a Pyrenean village of stone and slate guarding the entrance to the Ordesa Valley. In the morning, reach Pradera de Ordesa (by car off-season, by shuttle from Torla in summer) and set off on the Cola de Caballo trail - 17.5 km round trip, 500 m elevation gain, 5 to 6 hours of hiking.

⏱ 60 min
2

The trail ascends the Ordesa Valley along the Arazas River. You'll first pass through dense forests of beech and fir, then by the Arripas waterfall, the Estrecho waterfall (water plunges between two narrow walls into turquoise pools), and the Gradas de Soaso - stepped waterfalls of surreal beauty. At the end, the glacial cirque of Soaso opens onto Cola de Caballo (Horsetail), a waterfall over 50 meters high cascading in a fan from the cirque walls, with the snow-capped Monte Perdido as backdrop. Marmots accompany you for the final kilometers. This is one of the most beautiful hikes in the Pyrenees.

⏱ 300 min

Activities

Back in Torla at day's end, enjoy the village. Stone streets converge on the church of San Salvador (16th century) and the viewpoint over the valley. In the evening, restaurants serve Upper Aragón specialties: migas de pastor (fried breadcrumbs with garlic and chorizo), roast lamb, setas (wild mushrooms) in season. It's a well-earned meal after the hike.

⏱ 60 min

Alquezar

★★★☆☆
1

In the morning, stop at Aínsa, former capital of the Kingdom of Sobrarbe and one of Spain's Most Beautiful Villages. Perched at the confluence of the Ara and Cinca rivers, beneath the gaze of Peña Montañesa, the medieval village seems frozen in the 12th century. The Plaza Mayor, trapezoidal and bordered by Romanesque arcades, is one of the world's best-preserved medieval squares. Climb the square tower of Santa María church (11th-12th centuries) for a panoramic view of the Pyrenees. The restored castle houses an ecomuseum and hosts a music festival in its courtyard each July.

⏱ 90 min
2

In the afternoon, drive to Alquézar (1h from Aínsa). The name comes from Arabic al-qasr (the fortress) - it was a 9th-century Muslim stronghold conquered by Sancho Ramírez in 1064. The village, clinging to a promontory above the Río Vero canyon, is classified as a Historic Site and ranks among Spain's Most Beautiful Villages. Climb up to the Collegiate Church of Santa María la Mayor, which preserves a Romanesque cloister with carved capitals and a major altarpiece. From the square, the view plunges into the limestone gorges - a dizzying spectacle.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Don't miss the Pasarelas del Vero, Aragón's most popular aerial trail. This 3 km route (approximately 2h loop) descends into the Río Vero canyon via metal walkways attached to limestone walls above turquoise water. The trail is paid and well-secured - no technical difficulty, but spectacular views over the gorges and natural pools. In warm weather, bring a swimsuit for a dip.

⏱ 120 min

Near Alquézar, the Río Vero Cultural Park contains over 70 shelters with rock paintings listed as UNESCO World Heritage (1998). The most accessible are the Quizans shelters (schematic art, 5000-1500 BC) and Chimiachas (a deer in Levantine art of striking realism, 6000-3000 BC). Allow 45 minutes' walk from Alquézar to reach the shelters. Access is free but a guide significantly enriches the visit.

⏱ 120 min

Sos Del Rey Catolico

★★★☆☆
1

Head west (2h30) to reach Sos del Rey Católico, in the Cinco Villas comarca. Perched on a rocky promontory on the border between the ancient kingdoms of Aragón and Navarre, this fortified city witnessed the birth on March 10, 1452 of one of the most important figures in Spanish history: Ferdinand II of Aragón, the Catholic King, who, together with Isabella of Castile, unified Spain and financed Christopher Columbus's voyage. The village, classified as a Historic Site since 1968, is one of Aragón's best preserved - seven fortified gates still pierce the Gothic ramparts.

⏱ 30 min
2

Begin at the Palacio de Sada, Ferdinand's birthplace, recognizable by its austere facade with coats of arms and crenellations. It now houses the tourist office and an interpretation center about the monarch. The church of San Esteban, the village's Romanesque jewel, preserves the baptismal font where Ferdinand was christened. But the real treasure is its 11th-century crypt, the oldest structure in the village, adorned with Gothic murals depicting scenes from Christ's life.

⏱ 60 min
3

Lose yourself in the cobbled streets of the casco antiguo. The Jewish quarter (Judería), around the Plaza de la Sartén, retains its medieval atmosphere - about thirty families lived here in the 12th century before their expulsion in 1492 by the Catholic Monarchs. Climb up to Peña Feliciana castle (12th century), whose keep offers a panorama over the comarca's five villages and, on clear days, the Pyrenees. The Plaza de la Villa, with its Renaissance town hall and medieval market hall (Lonja), completes the visit.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Enjoy Sos's tranquil atmosphere at day's end. The Parador, housed in a new building integrated into the ramparts, offers a terrace overlooking the village rooftops. For dinner, try Cinco Villas specialties: ternasco de Aragón (roast suckling lamb), migas, and artisanal sheep's cheese. Before leaving the village, stop by the Delfín Puente pastry shop for its handmade chocolates - a local institution.

⏱ 90 min

Daroca

★★☆☆☆
1

Long driving day with a morning stop in Zaragoza (1h20 from Sos). If you haven't yet visited the Aragonese capital, make at least a brief stop at the Aljafería Palace, the most beautiful Muslim palace outside Andalusia. Built in the 11th century for the kings of the Taifa of Saraqusta, it later served as a residence for the kings of Aragón and headquarters of the Inquisition. The Patio de Santa Isabel, with its polylobed arches and gardens, is a masterpiece of Taifa art. Allow 1h30 for the visit. If you already know Zaragoza, proceed directly to Daroca.

⏱ 90 min
2

Daroca (1h south of Zaragoza) is a complete surprise. This town of 2,000 inhabitants possesses one of Spain's longest fortified walls: 4 km of ramparts bristling with 114 towers, built from the 9th century by the Arabs and expanded after the Christian conquest in 1120. Enter through the Puerta Baja (14th century) or Puerta Alta (15th century) and climb Calle Mayor, the town's backbone, between brick Romanesque churches (San Miguel, San Juan) and noble palaces. The Basilica of Santa María de los Sagrados Corporales, housing the relics of a 1239 Eucharistic miracle, is one of Aragón's most remarkable.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Climb up to Castillo Mayor, on the plateau of San Cristóbal hill. The path follows the ramparts and offers plunging views over the town nestled between two ridges. The walkway allows you to link several towers - bring good shoes and water. Allow 1h30 for the ascent and return. From the castle, the panorama embraces the Jiloca valley, grain fields, and red clay hills.

⏱ 90 min

Valderrobres

★★★☆☆
1

Drive east to enter Lower Aragón. First stop at Alcañiz (1h40 from Daroca), the second city of Teruel province. Climb to the Calatrava castle, a fortress of the military Order of Calatrava that has dominated the town since the 12th century - it now houses a Parador. The Gothic Lonja (market hall) from the 15th century, one of Aragón's finest, stands out with its three pointed arches opening onto the Plaza de España. The collegiate church of Santa María la Mayor and the medieval underground passages beneath the square complete the visit.

⏱ 75 min
2

Continue 30 minutes east to reach Valderrobres, capital of the Matarraña comarca, nicknamed 'Aragonese Tuscany.' The arrival is spectacular: the medieval stone bridge spans the Río Matarraña and leads straight to the San Roque gate, one of seven gates in the old wall. At the village summit, the archbishop's castle-palace (14th-15th centuries) connects to the church of Santa María la Mayor, one of Aragón's finest examples of Levantine Gothic. Its sculpted facade and rose window are remarkably delicate.

⏱ 75 min
3

If time permits, drive 15 minutes south to Calaceite, the other jewel of Matarraña. Classified as a Historic Site, this stone village with sloping streets and seigneurial houses in wrought iron has kept its Mediterranean character intact. The Plaza de España, the Renaissance town hall (1609), and the church of the Asunción form a harmonious ensemble. The Catalan influence - the border is just kilometers away - is felt in the architecture and gastronomy.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In the evening, dine in Valderrobres. Matarraña's gastronomy is Mediterranean countryside cuisine: PDO Bajo Aragón olive oil, roast lamb, black truffle in season (Lower Aragón is one of Europe's top producers), goat cheeses, and Calanda peaches. Accompany your meal with a wine from the DO Bajo Aragón - powerful reds based on Garnacha.

Rubielos De Mora

★★★☆☆
1

Climb into the Maestrazgo mountains. Cantavieja, the comarca's capital, perches on a limestone rock at 1,300 m altitude. This triangular-plan village was a Templar commandery from 1169, then passed to the Hospitallers after the dissolution of the Order of the Temple. The medieval streets converge on the Plaza Porticada, one of the province's most beautiful arcaded squares, dominated by the imposing Baroque church of the Asunción and the town hall. The Carlist War Museum traces the battles of the 'Tiger of Maestrazgo,' General Ramón Cabrera, who made Cantavieja his headquarters in 1836.

⏱ 75 min
2

Descend south (1h mountain drive) to Rubielos de Mora, in the Gúdar-Javalambre comarca. Nicknamed the 'Court of the Sierra' for the number of nobles who settled here in the 16th-17th centuries, this town at 1,000 m altitude possesses a remarkable collection of Renaissance mansions and palaces. The Excolegiata Santa María la Mayor (16th century) houses a large 15th-century International Gothic altarpiece dedicated to the life of the Virgin. The Casa Consistorial (town hall) is a fine Renaissance building. Rubielos is part of the Cittaslow (slow cities) network - a label that suits it perfectly.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Enjoy Rubielos de Mora's peaceful atmosphere at day's end. Stroll beneath the arches of the Portal del Carmen and Portal de San Antonio, the two medieval gates still standing. The convents of the Discalced Carmelites and Augustinians are open for visits. In the evening, the Plaza de la Villa gently comes alive with its terraces - this is deep Spain, without tourists, where time seems to slow down.

Teruel

★★★★☆
1

Arrival in Teruel to conclude the itinerary. This small provincial capital (36,000 inhabitants) possesses Aragón's finest Mudéjar ensemble - four towers and the cathedral's timber ceiling are UNESCO World Heritage listed since 1986. Begin with the Torre de El Salvador (14th century), which you can climb for a panoramic view. Its structure is typical of Aragonese Mudéjar: an outer tower in brick decorated with green and white ceramics enveloping an older inner tower, with a vaulted passage between the two.

⏱ 45 min
2

Step inside the Cathedral of Santa María de Mediavilla - you'll be amazed by its painted Mudéjar timber ceiling (13th century), nicknamed the 'Sistine Chapel of Mudéjar.' Over a surface 32 meters long, painted wood panels depict scenes from medieval daily life: craftsmen, animals, knights, as well as Islamic geometric motifs and human figures in a rare style. Look up at the cimborrio (octagonal lantern) - it's a masterpiece of fusion between Christian and Muslim traditions.

⏱ 45 min
3

Stop at the Mausoleum of the Lovers of Teruel - it tells one of Spain's most famous love stories, the Spanish Romeo and Juliet, but three centuries earlier. Diego de Marcilla and Isabel de Segura, childhood sweethearts, were separated by Isabel's father who refused a suitor without fortune. Diego left to make his fortune and returned five years later, on the very day of Isabel's wedding to another. He died of grief asking for a kiss she refused; she in turn died the next day on his coffin. Look at the two alabaster tomb effigies - their hands reaching toward each other without touching convey rare emotion.

⏱ 30 min
4

Finish at the Plaza del Torico, Teruel's living room, with its column topped by a tiny bull (the torico) - the city's symbol. The surrounding Modernist facades (including Casa El Torico from 1912) testify to an era of prosperity. The Escalinata, a monumental neo-Mudéjar staircase built in 1921 by architect José Torán, links the train station to the old town - its ceramics and ornamental bricks encapsulate Teruel's entire aesthetic.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

For a final Aragonese meal, Teruel is the city of jamón. Jamón de Teruel PDO was the first Spanish ham to receive protected designation of origin status (1984). The pigs are raised at altitude in the surrounding sierras, producing firm, aromatic meat. Order a ración of jamón with pan con tomate and a glass of Garnacha from Bajo Aragón - it's the perfect conclusion to this journey through Aragón.

⏱ 60 min
