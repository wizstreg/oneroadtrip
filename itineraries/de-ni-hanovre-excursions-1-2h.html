Celle

★★☆☆☆
1

Celle's old town (Altstadt) boasts over 500 half-timbered houses designated as historical monuments, the oldest dating back to the 15th century. The town's name derives from the Latin cella—a small monastic chamber—recalling an ancient monastery established on the site. First mentioned in 985 as Kellu, Celle served as the residence of the Dukes of Brunswick-Lüneburg from 1378 to 1705. The carved beams above doorways once indicated the occupants' trades.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Hoppener Haus, built in 1532, stands among Germany's most lavishly decorated half-timbered houses. Its six-level facade is covered with carvings depicting devils, jesters, planetary gods, mythical creatures, and reptiles. Across the street, two lampposts equipped with speakers recount local anecdotes in both standard German and Low Saxon dialect—they activate automatically as you pass.

⏱ 30 min
3

Celle Castle (Schloss Celle) dominates the banks of the Aller River from a gentle hill surrounded by moats and a sixteen-acre park. Built in the 13th century and serving as the residence of the Dukes of Brunswick-Lüneburg for over three centuries, it houses Baroque state apartments adorned with Italian stuccowork and a Renaissance chapel. The Schlosstheater, founded in 1675, is considered Germany's oldest baroque theater still in operation. Queen Caroline Mathilde of Denmark was exiled here from 1772 until her death in 1775.

⏱ 90 min
4

The Stadtkirche St. Marien ranks among the town's oldest buildings. Its tower offers views across the old town's rooftops and the castle. Access to the tower depends on opening hours posted on the facade.

⏱ 45 min
5

Celle's synagogue, built in 1740, is the oldest synagogue in northern Germany. This baroque half-timbered building, located at Im Kreise 24, survived Kristallnacht in 1938 because authorities feared fire would spread to neighboring houses. Inside, a collection of objects that belonged to Holocaust survivors.

⏱ 45 min
6

The French Garden (Französischer Garten) was designed in the 17th century by French landscape architects, though over time it has taken on a more English appearance. Linden tree avenue, flower beds, and a pond—freely accessible.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Walk a loop along the Aller and the historic bridges—the best angle to see the castle reflected in the water.

⏱ 60 min

Wolfsburg

★★★☆☆
1

Autostadt is a 69-acre theme park opened in 2000, built along the Mittelland Canal for the Hanover World Expo. It showcases the Volkswagen Group's brands in carefully designed pavilions. Its most spectacular attraction: two 48-meter glass towers, fully automated, storing up to 800 new vehicles connected to the factory by a 700-meter tunnel. Nearly 175,000 customers come here each year to collect their cars. The ZeitHaus museum traces automotive history from the Ford Model T to the Beetle.

⏱ 180 min
2

The Kunstmuseum Wolfsburg, inaugurated in 1994, is one of northern Germany's most active contemporary art museums, featuring major temporary exhibitions in a building designed by Peter Schweger.

⏱ 75 min
3

Wolfsburg Castle is first mentioned in 1302—a Wasserburg, or moated castle, former residence of the noble Bartensleben family. When the British renamed the town in May 1945, they chose this Weser Renaissance castle to erase the name "Stadt des KdF-Wagens" imposed by Hitler in 1938.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walk along the Mittelland Canal with an overview of the Volkswagen facilities: the chimneys of the factory founded in 1938 and the two glass delivery towers form a recognizable industrial skyline.

Hildesheim

★★★★☆
1

St. Michael's Church in Hildesheim was built between 1010 and 1022 by Bishop Bernward. Its symmetrical double-apse plan, characteristic of Ottonian Romanesque art, earned it the nickname "heavenly castle." The painted wooden ceiling of the central nave, created in the 13th century on 1,300 oak planks, depicts the Tree of Jesse and measures 27.6 × 8.7 meters—one of the extremely rare monumental paintings from the High Middle Ages to have survived, along with those in Zillis, Switzerland, and Dädesjö, Sweden. UNESCO-listed since 1985.

⏱ 60 min
2

St. Mary's Cathedral (Hildesheimer Dom) dates back to 872. Its current form as a three-aisled basilica was completed in 1061. It houses the Bernward Doors, cast in bronze in 1015, and the Christ Column from 1020—the first major bronze castings since antiquity. In the cloister grows the thousand-year-old rose: planted according to tradition in the 9th century, destroyed during the 1945 bombings, it has since regrown from its roots. UNESCO-listed since 1985.

⏱ 75 min
3

Hildesheim's Marktplatz presents a curiosity: its gabled houses were completely destroyed in 1945 and faithfully reconstructed between 1984 and 1989 from precise architectural records. Renaissance and Baroque facades that appear medieval but are less than forty years old.

⏱ 30 min
4

The Roemer and Pelizaeus Museum houses one of Germany's most important Egyptian art collections, assembled in the 19th century by merchant Wilhelm Pelizaeus from Cairo. Funerary statues, masks, and papyri spanning over 3,000 years.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Urban walk between Michaelisplatz and Domhof—the two UNESCO sites connected on foot in about twenty minutes through medieval alleyways.

⏱ 60 min

Hameln

★★★☆☆
1

The Fagus Factory is a shoe-last factory built starting in 1911 by Walter Gropius—at 27, his first independent commission—in collaboration with Adolf Meyer. Gropius eliminated load-bearing corner pillars and placed the structure inside the building, creating fully glazed corners—a radical innovation for 1911. This curtain wall principle in glass, which he would perfect at the Bauhaus in Dessau, was used here for the first time. The factory still operates today and continues to produce shoe lasts. UNESCO-listed since 2011—the only World Heritage site still in production.

⏱ 90 min
2

Hameln is built along the Weser, with an old town whose half-timbered houses and 16th- and 17th-century sandstone facades illustrate the Weser Renaissance style. In the cobblestones, thousands of small bronze rat-shaped plaques guide visitors to the main sites—a nod to the legend whose earliest written traces date back to a chronicle from 1384.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Rattenfängerhaus (Pied Piper House), built in 1602, bears on its facade an inscription recalling the event of June 26, 1284: "130 children born in Hamelin were led away by a piper dressed in many colors to Calvary near the Koppen and disappeared there." Some historians see in this legend an allegory of eastward colonization migrations (Ostsiedlung) in the 13th century—the rats, incidentally, only appear in 16th-century versions.

⏱ 30 min
4

The Hochzeitshaus (Wedding House), built between 1610 and 1617, is the most imposing Renaissance building on the central square. Its mechanical carillon plays the Pied Piper melody three times daily with moving figures—crowds gather to watch.

⏱ 20 min
5

St. Boniface's Minster (Münster St. Bonifatius) is Hameln's oldest religious building, its origins tracing back to a Benedictine monastery founded in 851. The current church preserves Romanesque and Gothic elements.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Walk along the Weser from the historic center—the river borders the old town along its entire west side.

⏱ 45 min

Bremen

★★★★☆
1

Bremen's Marktplatz is one of the best-preserved medieval ensembles in northern Germany. The town hall (Rathaus), built in the 15th century in Gothic style then remodeled in Weser Renaissance in the 17th century, and the Roland statue erected in 1404—symbol of municipal freedoms—have been UNESCO-listed since 2004. Bremen had joined the Hanseatic League in 1358; much of its prosperity came from the 17th century onward through coffee and cotton trade.

⏱ 45 min
2

St. Peter's Cathedral (Dom St. Petri) was founded in 789 by Willehad, Bremen's first bishop—who died that same year. The current sandstone edifice was built starting in 1042 and remodeled several times. Its two green-roofed towers dominate the Marktplatz. In the crypt, eight naturally preserved mummies from the 17th and 18th centuries, maintained by particularly dry conditions.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Schnoor quarter is the old fishermen's district, whose 15th- and 16th-century half-timbered houses have remained virtually intact. Its alleyways so narrow you can touch both facades by stretching your arms now house galleries, artisans, and cafes.

⏱ 45 min
4

Böttcherstrasse is a 100-meter street rebuilt in the 1920s in expressionist style, mixing carved red bricks and decorative art—commissioned by a coffee merchant enriched in the Brazilian trade. At the entrance, a gold relief depicting the archangel Michael slaying a dragon.

⏱ 30 min
5

The Kunsthalle Bremen, founded in 1849, is one of Germany's oldest art museums. Its collection covers Flemish and Dutch painting, German Romanticism, French Impressionism—Monet, Pissarro—and modern art. It holds over 200,000 drawings and prints, one of the most important collections of this type in Europe.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Stroll along the Schlachte, the riverside promenade along the Weser that runs the entire length of the old town. Terraces and moored boats—very lively on summer evenings.

⏱ 45 min

Hamburg

★★★★★
1

HafenCity is Europe's largest urban renewal project: 388 acres of former port docks transformed since 2000 into a new residential and cultural district, expanding Hamburg's city center by 40%. At its heart, the Speicherstadt—a city of neo-Gothic red brick warehouses built between 1883 and 1927—has been UNESCO-listed since 2015. Originally a free zone, tobacco, coffee, cocoa, and oriental carpets were stored there in warehouses on stilts.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Elbphilharmonie was inaugurated on January 11, 2017, after ten years of construction and 865 million euros. Herzog & de Meuron placed atop the Kaispeicher A—a cocoa warehouse from 1963-1966—a 110-meter glass wave composed of 1,100 curved facade elements. The Grand Hall, designed with acoustician Yasuhisa Toyota, seats 2,100 people in terraces around the orchestra. The Plaza, at 37 meters height, is freely accessible—panoramic view over the port. Book access in advance during high season.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Hamburger Kunsthalle is one of Germany's largest art museums, spread across three buildings constructed between 1869 and 2001. Its collection spans seven centuries: Master Bertram's 1379 polyptych, an entire room devoted to Caspar David Friedrich, works by Edvard Munch, Picasso, and Max Beckmann, up to the Galerie der Gegenwart for art since the 1960s.

⏱ 90 min
4

St. Michaelis Church—"der Michel"—is the third church built on this site: the first burned after a lightning strike in 1750, the second caught fire during construction work in 1906. Completed in 1786, it measures 132 meters. The crypt held 2,500 burials between 1762 and 1817, including Carl Philipp Emanuel Bach, son of Johann Sebastian. Tower accessible by elevator or 453 steps.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Walk along the Elbe quays from the Landungsbrücken—the historic landing piers built between 1907 and 1909 offer the best angle on the industrial skyline and the Elbphilharmonie.

⏱ 60 min

Gottingen

★★★☆☆
1

The Georg-August University was founded in 1737 by George II, Elector of Hanover and King of Great Britain, to spread the ideals of the Enlightenment. Napoleon reportedly declared: "Göttingen belongs neither to a state nor to Germany—it is the university of Europe." Since its founding, 47 Nobel Prize laureates have studied or taught here, including Max Born, Werner Heisenberg, and Otto Hahn. One in four residents is a student. The Aula, the main classical-style building on Wilhelmsplatz, is the visible heart of the historic campus.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Gänseliesel—Little Goose Girl Lison—is the Art Nouveau fountain installed in front of the Altes Rathaus (13th century). Every new doctoral graduate must climb the fountain, offer the statue a bouquet, and plant a kiss on her cheek. The tradition was officially forbidden but always tolerated. On some summer days, the scene repeats almost hourly.

⏱ 20 min
3

The Old Botanical Garden, created in 1736, houses over 10,000 plant species and 19th-century historic greenhouses—one of Germany's oldest university botanical gardens, still active for research.

⏱ 45 min
4

The Junkernschänke is a late Gothic 15th-century house with a richly carved facade, one of the most beautiful in the old town. The Stadtwall, a former medieval rampart transformed into a green promenade, circles the historic center.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Walk a loop on the Stadtwall, the green ring that follows the route of the old medieval ramparts around Göttingen's center—about 4 km, flat and shaded path.

Quedlinburg

★★★★☆
1

Quedlinburg counts approximately 2,000 half-timbered houses spanning six centuries of construction—from the 14th to the 19th century. UNESCO-listed in 1994, the town owes this distinction to its imperial origins: first mentioned in 922 in a charter by Henry I the Fowler, it became the favorite residence of the first Kings of Germany. Between 922 and 1207, Germanic rulers stayed here 69 times.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Collegiate Church of St. Servatius dominates the town from the Schlossberg. Built from 1070 to 1129 on 9th-century foundations, it is UNESCO-classified as a masterpiece of Romanesque art. In its crypt lie Henry I and his wife Mathilde—Germany's first rulers. Under the Third Reich, Heinrich Himmler transformed the crypt into a neo-pagan sanctuary dedicated to Henry I, whom he considered "the most German of German leaders."

⏱ 75 min
3

The Lyonel Feininger Gallery presents works by the American-German master who joined the Bauhaus in 1919 and taught there until the Nazis forced its closure. He maintained a personal connection with Quedlinburg and deposited here a series of works that form this permanent collection.

⏱ 60 min
4

The Marktplatz is framed by the town hall mentioned as early as 1310 and its Roland statue, a medieval symbol of municipal freedoms. The inner courtyards hidden behind the facades are worth exploring.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Climb to the Schlossberg for views over Quedlinburg's rooftops and the Harz plain, then descend through cobbled alleyways. About 15 minutes from the market square.

⏱ 60 min

Berlin

★★★★★
1

The Reichstag was built between 1884 and 1894 by Paul Wallot in neo-Renaissance style. The 1933 fire—which the Nazis used as a pretext to suspend freedoms—followed by the 1945 battles left it in ruins. Norman Foster's reconstruction, completed in 1999, produced the 23-meter glass dome: a central mirror reflects natural light into the Bundestag chamber below. Free visit but advance booking required.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Brandenburg Gate was built between 1788 and 1791 by Carl Gotthard Langhans, inspired by the Propylaea of the Acropolis: 26 meters high, 65.5 meters wide, five passages. In 1806, Napoleon had the bronze quadriga—the goddess of Peace on a four-horse chariot—dismantled and shipped to Paris. It returned in 1814 after Napoleon's defeat. In 1961, the gate found itself in the middle of a no man's land for 28 years. On December 22, 1989, over 100,000 people gathered here for its official opening.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Holocaust Memorial—a field of 2,711 concrete stelae of varying heights designed by Peter Eisenman—is freely accessible at all hours. The underground Information Center traces the history of the Shoah. Its "Room of Names" continuously broadcasts the names and biographies of victims—complete reading would take over six years.

⏱ 60 min
4

Museum Island, a Spree island UNESCO-listed in 1999, groups five museums built between 1830 and 1930. The Pergamon Museum houses the reconstruction of the 2nd-century BC Pergamon Altar and the Ishtar Gate of Babylon (575 BC). The Neues Museum holds the bust of Nefertiti. Allow at least two hours for a single museum.

⏱ 120 min
5

The television tower (Fernsehturm) at Alexanderplatz was built by the GDR between 1965 and 1969—368 meters, Germany's tallest structure. Intended as a symbol of the regime's technical prowess, visible from the West, it produced an unintended effect: when the sun strikes its steel sphere, the light forms a cross that East Berliners ironically nicknamed "the pope's revenge."

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Historic walking route between the Reichstag, Brandenburg Gate, Holocaust Memorial, Unter den Linden, and Museum Island—about 4 km on an almost linear axis.

⏱ 90 min
