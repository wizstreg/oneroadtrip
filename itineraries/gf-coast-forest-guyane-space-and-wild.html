✨ Highlights
  • Guiana Space Centre—Europe's only launch base, free guided tours
  • Salvation Islands—French Guiana's most visited penal ruins (60,000 visitors/year)
  • Kaw Marshes—nighttime black caiman observation by pirogue
  • Camp de la Transportation—historic monument and penal memory site
  • Hmong village of Cacao—Sunday market with Southeast Asian flavors in the heart of Amazonia
📆 Best period

July, August, September, October, November

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 3 and 7 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Dry Season (August-November): Best weather conditions, minimal rainfall, ideal for outdoor activities, peak tourist season, comfortable temperatures.

Short Dry Season (February-March): Pleasant weather break, fewer crowds, good for cultural activities, moderate conditions.

Wet Season (December-July): Heavy rainfall, high humidity, challenging conditions, lush rainforest, fewer tourists, lower prices.

Space Launch Season: Year-round launches, advance booking essential, unique experiences, international visitors.

🌍 What to discover in GF?

South American wilderness with European standards, space center, and incredible biodiversity. Experience rainforest adventures, traditional cultures, and unique colonial heritage. This territory offers authentic South American experiences with French infrastructure and amenities. French Guiana provides wilderness adventures with modern conveniences and cultural diversity.

📋 Important notes

From Cayenne's markets to Kourou's space heritage and the Maroni and Kaw wetlands, discover forest edges, river villages, and Atlantic shores.

French Guiana Journey: Kourou, Amazon Rainforest & Coast

📅 8 days📍 10 stops🚗 541 km⏱ 8h30

Cayenne → Kourou → Saint Laurent → Roura → Kaw

Cayenne → Kourou → Saint Laurent → Roura → Kaw

Between Cayenne's Place des Palmistes and the wild marshes of Kaw, French Guiana unveils a territory where European space history meets penal colony vestiges and Amazonian biodiversity. This 10-day itinerary connects Kourou's Ariane rockets to the Salvation Islands' cells, Cacao's Hmong village to Maroni pirogues, the marshes' black caimans to the estuary's scarlet ibises.

On Île Saint-Joseph, nicknamed "the man-eater," nature has reclaimed its territory. Ruins with pink walls of solitary confinement cells vanish beneath vines and roots of giant trees.

⏱ 60 min

Île Royale's capuchin monkeys, true stars of the place, approach visitors with curiosity. Agoutis, iguanas, and sea turtles complete the scene.

Camp de la Transportation, classified as a historic monument in 1995, opened in 1858. Past the grand arch, the cells, inner courtyard, and former guillotine trace a century of penal history. Some 70,000 convicts passed through here.

3

Roura church — Heritage or natural stop with interpretation signs.

4

Cell 47, restored for the film "Papillon," is where Henri Charrière was imprisoned. Graffiti carved into stone by inmates remain visible on the walls of other cells.

⏱ 30 min
5

River bend lookout — Scenic point for photos; golden hours recommended.

⏱ 75 min
6

The "Petit Paris" quarter, the former administrative district of the penal town, displays colonial buildings including the former penal director's residence, now the sub-prefecture.

⏱ 40 min
7

Saint-Laurent's colorful wooden Creole houses testify to a unique urban planning in France: the entire town was built by and for the penal administration from 1880 onward.

⏱ 30 min
8

From the Maroni quays, the view carries to Albina, the small Surinamese town on the opposite bank. The 611-km river has never been a border for the Bushinengé who live on both sides.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The guided tour of Camp de la Transportation lasts about 75 minutes. Nighttime torch-lit tours are scheduled once a month from June to October, featuring convict songs and testimonies.

⏱ 60 min

The market at Place Flore Lithaw gathers Chinese, Creole, Bushinengé, and Hmong cuisines. Couac, pickles, tropical fruits, and local crafts exchange hands in a babel of languages.

The village of Cacao, created in 1977 by Hmong refugees from Laos, belongs to the commune of Roura. Its wooden stilt houses, flowered gardens, and market crops lend the hamlet a Southeast Asian air in the heart of Amazonia.

Le Planeur Bleu museum houses entomology collections (butterflies, tarantulas, centipedes), Amerindian archaeology, and Guianese ethnology. A concentrate of local biodiversity in a small building beside the hall.

⏱ 40 min
5

Historic plaques — Scenic point for photos; golden hours recommended.

⏱ 75 min
6

The church of Roura and its surroundings, on the right bank of the Oyak, retain a marked Creole character. The town center offers a striking contrast with Cacao's Hmong village.

⏱ 30 min
7

On the Cacao road, a viewpoint at kilometer marker 6 before the village overlooks the Amazon canopy. In season, flowering trees punctuate the green immensity.

⏱ 20 min

Push open the door to Cacao's potter's workshop for an introduction to the wheel. This colorful character welcomes visitors with humor and transmits his artisan know-how.

⏱ 60 min

From Cacao's landing, board a pirogue on the Comté River for a two-hour journey upstream through dense forest. Swimming breaks in creeks are possible.

Kaw

Where black caimans glide through 94,700 hectares of protected wetlands

★☆☆☆☆
📍 Marais de Kaw réserve naturelle📍 Village de Kaw📍 Rivière de Kaw📍 Dégrad de Kaw Roura📍 Montagne de Kaw
1

Botanical garden — Landmark with colonial/Amerindian context.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Kaw-Roura Marshes Nature Reserve covers 94,700 hectares, France's third-largest nature reserve. It harbors 535 bird species, 98 mammal species, and four caiman species.

⏱ 30 min
3

Creole wooden homes — Heritage or natural stop with interpretation signs.

⏱ 75 min
4

The village of Kaw, about thirty inhabitants, sits on a former sand ridge. Accessible only by pirogue, it served as a backdrop for the television series "Guyane."

⏱ 40 min
5

Coastal promenade — Scenic point for photos; golden hours recommended.

⏱ 75 min
6

On the forest road toward the landing, the Camp-Caïman inn marks the halfway point. Amerindian vestiges on Montagne Favard (engraved rocks, pottery) testify to ancient occupation.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Board a pirogue on the marshes at nightfall. The eyes of black caimans—the world's second-largest population of the species—shine in headlamp beams.

⏱ 60 min

By day, the marshes reveal scarlet ibises, cocoi herons, wattled jacanas, and hoatzins. Zebu cattle feed in water up to their chests, impassive.

⏱ 60 min

Gather at dawn on a floating ecolodge to watch the ballet of fishing birds in the morning mist. Far from any light pollution, the nocturnal starry sky is exceptional.
