Cayenne

Where Caribbean royal palms tower over Creole markets and colonial ramparts command the estuary

★★☆☆☆
1

Place des Palmistes — Landmark with colonial/Amerindian context.

⏱ 75 min
2

Place des Palmistes stretches across three hectares in the heart of Cayenne. Its towering Caribbean royal palms reach 25 meters skyward, while a statue of Félix Éboué stands watch over the esplanade.

⏱ 45 min
3

Fort Cépérou (ext) — Heritage or natural stop with interpretation signs.

⏱ 75 min
4

Climb the stepped street to Fort Cépérou, built in the 17th century on a hill purchased from the Amerindian chief Cépérou. A few walls, a vaulted gateway, and sweeping views over the city and estuary remain.

⏱ 50 min
5

Cayenne market — Scenic point for photos; golden hours recommended.

⏱ 75 min
6

Saint-Sauveur Cathedral, constructed in the 19th century, ranks among the city's oldest religious buildings. Its modest facade conceals an interior rich with tropical woodwork.

⏱ 30 min
7

The Museum of Guianese Cultures occupies two renovated Creole houses. Collections span Amerindian, Bushinengé, Creole, and Hmong traditions through artifacts and textiles.

⏱ 50 min
8

Wander the old town streets to admire colorful wooden Creole houses with their balconies and shutters. Murals by local artists punctuate the climb toward the fort.

⏱ 40 min
9

The Botanical Garden, established in 1879, covers three hectares in the city center. A shaded path showcases tropical species with signs explaining their origins and uses.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Cayenne's covered market occupies a metal hall attributed to Eiffel's workshop. Tropical fruits, Laotian soups, dried shrimp, and ti-punch appear from early morning.

⏱ 60 min

Come evening, Place des Palmistes transforms: food carts roll in with grilled meats, accras, and fritters. The Creole atmosphere thrives beneath illuminated palms.

⏱ 60 min

Kourou

Europe's spaceport between Atlantic beaches and Amazon wilderness

★★☆☆☆
1

Kourou riverfront — Landmark with colonial/Amerindian context.

⏱ 75 min
2

Guiana Space Centre sprawls across 750 km² between Kourou and Sinnamary. The three-hour guided bus tour passes through control centers and the Ariane 6 and Vega-C launch zones. Booking 48 hours ahead is mandatory.

⏱ 180 min
3

Beach spit — Heritage or natural stop with interpretation signs.

⏱ 75 min
4

Guyaspace Expérience, the CNES museum reopened in 2024, offers an immersive visit with digital installations chronicling European space history since the first Véronique rocket launch in 1968.

⏱ 60 min
5

Space murals — Scenic point for photos; golden hours recommended.

⏱ 75 min
6

Head to Pointe des Roches to discover Tour Dreyfus, a 19th-century telegraph tower that once communicated by signal with the Salvation Islands. Unobstructed views stretch over the river mouth.

⏱ 40 min
7

Kourou's beaches line Avenue des Roches. Plage de la Cocoteraie provides a pleasant setting for a break, while Plage Pim-Poum, bordered by coconut palms, suits families with its shallow waters.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

The bike path along Avenue des Roches follows the coastline. Walkers, runners, and cyclists gather here in late afternoon facing the Atlantic.

⏱ 60 min

Witness a rocket launch if the schedule allows. Viewing sites sit between 7 and 20 km from the launch pads. Access is free by invitation.

⏱ 60 min

Kourou

Europe's spaceport between Atlantic beaches and Amazon wilderness

★★☆☆☆
1

Îles du Salut viewpoints — Landmark with colonial/Amerindian context.

⏱ 75 min
2

Île Royale, the largest of the Salvation Islands at 28 hectares, concentrates the penal colony's administrative remnants: cells from the convicts' quarter, hospital, restored chapel, and solar-powered lighthouse. A marked trail circles the island in 90 minutes.

⏱ 120 min
3

Old hospital ruins — Heritage or natural stop with interpretation signs.

⏱ 75 min
4

A small free museum on Île Royale presents convicts' letters, including those of Dreyfus. Cell 47, where Henri Charrière—known as "Papillon"—was imprisoned, can be identified by its graffiti.

⏱ 40 min
5

Clifftop belvederes — Scenic point for photos; golden hours recommended.

⏱ 75 min
6

From the mango tree esplanade, the view embraces the guards' quarter, chapel, hospital, and lighthouse. Below, Île du Diable—Dreyfus's place of exile—remains off-limits.

⏱ 30 min
7

On Île Saint-Joseph, nicknamed "the man-eater," nature has reclaimed its territory. Ruins with pink walls of solitary confinement cells vanish beneath vines and roots of giant trees.

⏱ 60 min
8

The convicts' pool, a saltwater basin carved into Île Royale's rock, allowed prisoners to wash sheltered from sharks. At high tide, swimming remains possible.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Board a catamaran from Kourou marina for a full day at the Salvation Islands. The crossing takes about an hour.

⏱ 60 min

On Île Saint-Joseph, the crushed shell beach offers the archipelago's finest swimming. At low tide, small natural pools form behind the rock bar.

⏱ 60 min

Île Royale's capuchin monkeys, true stars of the place, approach visitors with curiosity. Agoutis, iguanas, and sea turtles complete the scene.

⏱ 60 min

Saint Laurent

A multicultural town born from penal history on the banks of the Maroni

★★☆☆☆
1

Cacao Hmong market — Landmark with colonial/Amerindian context.

⏱ 75 min
2

Camp de la Transportation, classified as a historic monument in 1995, opened in 1858. Past the grand arch, the cells, inner courtyard, and former guillotine trace a century of penal history. Some 70,000 convicts passed through here.

⏱ 90 min
3

Roura church — Heritage or natural stop with interpretation signs.

⏱ 75 min
4

Cell 47, restored for the film "Papillon," is where Henri Charrière was imprisoned. Graffiti carved into stone by inmates remain visible on the walls of other cells.

⏱ 30 min
5

River bend lookout — Scenic point for photos; golden hours recommended.

⏱ 75 min
6

The "Petit Paris" quarter, the former administrative district of the penal town, displays colonial buildings including the former penal director's residence, now the sub-prefecture.

⏱ 40 min
7

Saint-Laurent's colorful wooden Creole houses testify to a unique urban planning in France: the entire town was built by and for the penal administration from 1880 onward.

⏱ 30 min
8

From the Maroni quays, the view carries to Albina, the small Surinamese town on the opposite bank. The 611-km river has never been a border for the Bushinengé who live on both sides.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The guided tour of Camp de la Transportation lasts about 75 minutes. Nighttime torch-lit tours are scheduled once a month from June to October, featuring convict songs and testimonies.

⏱ 60 min

The market at Place Flore Lithaw gathers Chinese, Creole, Bushinengé, and Hmong cuisines. Couac, pickles, tropical fruits, and local crafts exchange hands in a babel of languages.

⏱ 60 min

Saint Laurent

A multicultural town born from penal history on the banks of the Maroni

★★☆☆☆
1

Kaw marshes levee — Landmark with colonial/Amerindian context.

⏱ 75 min
2

The House of Cultures and Community in Saint-Laurent hosts art exhibitions blending Bushinengé, Amerindian, and Creole traditions.

⏱ 40 min
3

Bird observation posts — Heritage or natural stop with interpretation signs.

⏱ 75 min
4

Along the Maroni, pirogues painted with patterns number in the hundreds. Their vivid colors and streamlined shapes tell the identity of each riverside community.

⏱ 30 min
5

Wetland boardwalk — Scenic point for photos; golden hours recommended.

⏱ 75 min
6

Île de la Quarantaine and Île aux Lépreux, visible from the banks, sheltered sick prisoners. Their ruins emerge from tropical vegetation in the middle of the river.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Board a pirogue on the Maroni to journey upstream toward Bushinengé villages. The Bushinengé, descendants of enslaved people who fled Suriname's plantations, have preserved ancestral music, basketry, and rituals.

⏱ 60 min

Stroll along the quays in late afternoon to watch the sunset over the Maroni. The back-and-forth of pirogues between both banks creates an unbroken spectacle.

⏱ 60 min

Bushinengé basketry workshops offer two-hour introductions to wana weaving. Local artisans create baskets, jewelry from wapa seeds, and carved objects.

⏱ 60 min

Kourou

Europe's spaceport between Atlantic beaches and Amazon wilderness

★★☆☆☆
1

Camp de la Transportation (ext) — Landmark with colonial/Amerindian context.

⏱ 75 min
2

Montagne des Singes, a hill on CSG grounds, offers two trails: the botanical path (700 m, 30 min) and the mountain trail (3.3 km, 2.5 h). Tamarin monkeys, sloths, and toucans inhabit the site.

⏱ 120 min
3

Maroni quay — Heritage or natural stop with interpretation signs.

⏱ 75 min
4

From Montagne des Singes summit, the panorama embraces Kourou, the offshore Salvation Islands, and the Space Centre's launch pads. An ideal spot for picnicking.

⏱ 30 min
5

Creole houses — Scenic point for photos; golden hours recommended.

⏱ 75 min
6

The Amerindian Archaeology Centre of Kourou (CAAK) presents the daily life of French Guiana's earliest Amerindian communities. Over 200 rock carvings (figures, triangular heads) have been documented in the region.

⏱ 45 min
7

The CSG savanna trails, freely accessible, reveal the space center's biodiversity from a naturalist angle. ONF-guided visits are organized monthly.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Descend the Kourou River by pirogue or canoe through Amazon rainforest. Swimming breaks in creeks, archery, and zip-lining punctuate some excursions.

⏱ 60 min

The mangroves downstream shelter shrimp, crabs, egrets, and scarlet ibises. A late afternoon outing offers the best observation chances.

⏱ 60 min

Kaw

Where black caimans glide through 94,700 hectares of protected wetlands

★★☆☆☆
1

Botanical garden — Landmark with colonial/Amerindian context.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Kaw-Roura Marshes Nature Reserve covers 94,700 hectares, France's third-largest nature reserve. It harbors 535 bird species, 98 mammal species, and four caiman species.

⏱ 30 min
3

Creole wooden homes — Heritage or natural stop with interpretation signs.

⏱ 75 min
4

The village of Kaw, about thirty inhabitants, sits on a former sand ridge. Accessible only by pirogue, it served as a backdrop for the television series "Guyane."

⏱ 40 min
5

Coastal promenade — Scenic point for photos; golden hours recommended.

⏱ 75 min
6

On the forest road toward the landing, the Camp-Caïman inn marks the halfway point. Amerindian vestiges on Montagne Favard (engraved rocks, pottery) testify to ancient occupation.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Board a pirogue on the marshes at nightfall. The eyes of black caimans—the world's second-largest population of the species—shine in headlamp beams.

⏱ 60 min

By day, the marshes reveal scarlet ibises, cocoi herons, wattled jacanas, and hoatzins. Zebu cattle feed in water up to their chests, impassive.

⏱ 60 min

Gather at dawn on a floating ecolodge to watch the ballet of fishing birds in the morning mist. Far from any light pollution, the nocturnal starry sky is exceptional.

⏱ 60 min

Carbet areas — Landmark with colonial/Amerindian context.

2

3

Mangrove edge — Heritage or natural stop with interpretation signs.

4

5

Sunset lookouts — Scenic point for photos; golden hours recommended.

6

Maison Thémire, a 19th-century Creole dwelling, embodies traditional local architecture with its woodwork and shutters. It now hosts a dining space.

⏱ 25 min
7

Craving greenery? The Botanical Garden of Cayenne, created in the 18th century, offers three hectares of shaded promenade among rare tropical species, with explanatory signs for each variety.

⏱ 45 min
8

The Joséphine Horth school, visible from Place des Palmistes, was Cayenne's first public school. Its classified facade testifies to the city's educational history.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Montjoly beach, minutes from the center, invites late afternoon swimming. The brown water results from river sediments, not pollution.

⏱ 60 min

Montabo beach, closer to downtown, is favored by Cayenne residents for jogging and relaxation. Unobstructed view over the Atlantic Ocean.
