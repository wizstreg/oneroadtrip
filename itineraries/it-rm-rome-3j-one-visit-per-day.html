Piazza Di Spagna

★★★★☆
1

The Scalinata di Trinità dei Monti (1723-1726) ranks among Europe's grandest monumental staircases: 135 travertine steps divided into 11 ramps that serpentine and converge, designed by Francesco De Sanctis with funds bequeathed by French diplomat Étienne Gueffier. The original plan called for a statue of Louis XIV at the summit; Pope Innocent XIII refused, and a garden was planted instead. At the base, the Barcaccia fountain (1629) by Pietro Bernini—father of the more famous Gian Lorenzo—depicts a half-submerged boat commemorating the 1598 Tiber flood that allegedly beached a vessel at this very spot. Each April, the entire staircase vanishes beneath azaleas during the annual exhibition celebrating Rome's birthday.

⏱ 45 min
2

The church of Trinità dei Monti, built between 1502 and 1585 at the request of Charles VIII of France, stands as one of the rare examples of late French Gothic style visible in Rome. Its Renaissance façade with twin bell towers—the work of Giacomo della Porta and Carlo Maderno—remains under French state care to this day. Inside, six chapels house remarkable artworks. In front of the entrance, the Sallustiano obelisk (13.91 m) is a Roman creation imitating Egyptian hieroglyphs, gifted to Pope Clement XII by the Ludovisi family in 1783.

⏱ 25 min

Activities

Climb the scalinata early in the morning, before the tourist rush, to watch Rome stretch out beneath Via dei Condotti: the street's vanishing perspective at dawn is one of the city's most photographed vistas.

⏱ 30 min

Coffee at Caffè Greco (Via dei Condotti 86), open since 1760—Rome's oldest café and Italy's second oldest. Stendhal, Goethe, Byron, Liszt, and Keats all took their coffee here. The walls are covered with portraits and dedications from artists who frequented the place.

Via Dei Condotti

★★★☆☆
1

Via dei Condotti takes its name from the condotti—the conduits of the Aqua Virgo aqueduct, built in 19 BC by Agrippa to supply the thermal baths in the area. Transformed into a fashion artery in the 18th century following the construction of the nearby scalinata, it became one of the three 'Trident streets' converging on Piazza di Spagna. In 1905, Bulgari opened its first workshop at number 10; in 1921, Guccio Gucci inaugurated his first boutique at number 8. The apartment where Guglielmo Marconi, inventor of the radio, lived for several years sits on this same street.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Stroll through the Trident: beyond Via dei Condotti, the parallel streets Via Borgognona and Via Frattina extend the neighborhood experience toward Via del Corso with its 18th-century palaces and balustrade façades.

San Luigi Dei Francesi

★★★☆☆
1

The church of Saint Louis of the French (built 1518-1589), constructed by Domenico Fontana following plans by Giacomo della Porta, has served as France's national church in Rome since its consecration in 1589. Its Renaissance travertine façade bears the coat of arms of France and statues of Francis I (with the royal salamander) and Charlemagne. Inside, the fifth chapel on the left—the Contarelli Chapel—houses Caravaggio's three canvases painted between 1599 and 1602: the Calling of Saint Matthew, the Martyrdom of Saint Matthew, and Saint Matthew and the Angel. This was the painter's first major public commission in Rome and the official starting point of the Baroque. The initial version of Saint Matthew and the Angel was rejected by the patrons: the saint was depicted as an old peasant with dirty feet, deemed unworthy of an evangelist. The bearded figure at the back of the Martyrdom is likely a self-portrait of Caravaggio.

⏱ 50 min

Activities

Spot the French royal symbols on the façade: fleur-de-lis from the coat of arms, Francis I's salamander. The church also holds the tomb of Frédéric Ozanam, founder of the Society of Saint Vincent de Paul, and that of Blessed Pauline de Beaumont, erected by her lover Chateaubriand.

Pantheon

★★★★★
1

The Pantheon is the best-preserved building from ancient Rome. The first temple was erected by Agrippa in 27 BC and burned in 80 AD. The current structure is the one Hadrian rebuilt between 118 and 125 AD—yet he kept Agrippa's inscription on the pediment, erasing his own name. Its hemispherical unreinforced concrete dome (43.30 m in diameter) remains the world's largest in this category. The interior height exactly equals the diameter, inscribing a perfect sphere within the rotunda. The central oculus (8.92 m diameter) is the only source of light: rain enters directly and drains through 22 nearly invisible outlets in the original marble floor. Each year at Pentecost, a shower of rose petals descends through the oculus. Raphael is buried here, along with Italian kings Victor Emmanuel II and Umberto I. Michelangelo called this building an 'angelic and not human design.'

⏱ 60 min
2

Santa Maria sopra Minerva, steps from the Pantheon, is Rome's only Gothic church. Built in the 13th century atop the ruins of a temple to Minerva, it contains Michelangelo's Christ Bearing the Cross (1521), the Caraffa Chapel decorated by Filippino Lippi, and Fra Angelico's tomb. In front of the façade, Bernini's marble elephant bearing the obelisk (1667) is one of Rome's most whimsical monuments.

⏱ 35 min

Activities

Watch the Pantheon's oculus in the rain: water falls directly onto the ancient marble floor and vanishes into Roman drains that still function perfectly. The effect is mesmerizing.

Piazza Navona

★★★★☆
1

Piazza Navona occupies the exact footprint of the Stadium of Domitian, built in 86 AD for Greek-style games that could accommodate 30,000 spectators. Its elongated shape (roughly 275 m by 55 m) is the direct imprint of that structure. At the center, the Fountain of the Four Rivers (1648-1651) is Gian Lorenzo Bernini's masterwork, commissioned by Pope Innocent X Pamphilj: four white marble giants represent the Nile (Africa, veiled because its source was unknown), the Ganges (Asia, holding an oar), the Danube (Europe, touching the pope's coat of arms), and the Rio de la Plata (Americas, holding a bag of gold). A 16-meter obelisk from the Circus of Maxentius crowns them. The legend of the Bernini-Borromini rivalry is apocryphal: the fountain was completed before Borromini began the façade of neighboring Sant'Agnese. The heavy statues rest on a hollow travertine base—visually impossible, a deliberate demonstration of Bernini's genius.

⏱ 45 min
2

The church of Sant'Agnese in Agone (begun 1652, Borromini's façade) marks the spot where, according to tradition, the young Agnes was exposed naked before her martyrdom under Diocletian, miraculously protected by her hair which grew to cover her.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Eat or drink in the alleys behind the square (Via del Governo Vecchio) rather than on the piazza's terraces: authentic Roman atmosphere, prices cut in half.

Piazza Venezia

★★★★☆
1

The Vittoriano—or Altare della Patria—is the neoclassical monument erected between 1885 and 1935 in homage to Victor Emmanuel II, first king of unified Italy. Designed by Giuseppe Sacconi after his victory in the 1882 competition, it's built from white Botticino marble (Brescia)—a choice that outraged Romans accustomed to local travertine. The structure measures 135 m wide and 70 m high. Romans nicknamed it the 'typewriter' or 'wedding cake.' Its construction required demolishing a medieval quarter and revealed, during excavations, a section of the Servian Wall (6th century BC)—both walls are still visible in the foundations. The Tomb of the Unknown Soldier, installed in 1921, is permanently guarded. At the inauguration, a banquet was held inside the bronze equestrian statue of Victor Emmanuel—guests entered through the horse's mouth.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Ascend to the panoramic terrace (paid elevator access or free stairs to the intermediate level): stunning views of the Imperial Forums, the Colosseum, and Rome's hills.

Fontana Di Trevi

★★★★☆
1

The Trevi Fountain (26 m high, 49 m wide) is Rome's largest Baroque fountain and the terminal point of the Acqua Vergine aqueduct, built in 19 BC by Agrippa. Nicola Salvi won the competition organized by Clement XII in 1730—he beat Alessandro Galilei, initially favored but rejected by the public because he was Florentine, not Roman. Salvi died in 1751 without seeing his work completed; Giuseppe Pannini finished it in 1762. The central figure is not Neptune but Oceanus, god of the primordial seas. The two hippocampi symbolize the sea's changing moods—one agitated, the other calm. Salvi placed a large travertine vase shaped like an ace of cups in front of a barber's shop whose owner harassed him with criticism, permanently blocking his view of the construction site. The fountain collects about 1.5 million euros in coins per year, donated to Caritas.

⏱ 35 min
2

The Vicus Caprarius—City of Water—is an underground archaeological site discovered during construction work in 1999, right next to the Trevi Fountain. You can see remnants of a 2nd-century Roman insula and the conduits of the Acqua Vergine that still feed the fountain today. A direct window into Rome's underground, visited by few tourists.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Toss a coin over your left shoulder with your back to the fountain: the tradition, popularized by the film La Dolce Vita (1960), ensures a return to Rome. Come at dawn to avoid crowds—the fountain is accessible outside peak hours.

Colosseo

★★★★★
1

The Flavian Amphitheatre—called the Colosseum since the Middle Ages, probably in reference to Nero's nearby colossal statue—was built on the site of the Domus Aurea's artificial lake. Vespasian launched construction around 70-72 AD with spoils from the sack of Jerusalem in 70; his son Titus inaugurated the arena in 80 with a hundred days of games during which 9,000 animals perished according to Dio Cassius. The structure measures 189 m by 156 m and 48 m high, with a circumference of 527 m. Its travertine façade is organized across four levels with 80 arches framed by half-columns in ascending orders (Doric, Ionic, Corinthian). Access for spectators was through 76 of the 80 numbered entrances—the other four were reserved for dignitaries. The east gate was the 'gate of life' (gladiators' entrance), the west gate the 'gate of death' (corpse exit). Below the arena lies the hypogeum, a network of corridors and trap-door elevators allowing spectacular appearances of wild beasts and sets. Estimated capacity: 50,000 to 70,000 spectators, divided by social rank.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Arch of Constantine (315 AD), just outside the Colosseum, is Rome's largest triumphal arch (21 m high, 25.7 m wide). It celebrates Constantine's victory over Maxentius at the Milvian Bridge. Architectural quirk: it's largely built from spolia of earlier monuments—friezes from the emperors Trajan, Hadrian, and Marcus Aurelius were reused, the heads recarved in Constantine's likeness.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Tour the hypogeum and upper levels of the Colosseum (special tickets required): the underground corridors reveal the lift mechanisms and animal cages, invisible to the ancient public.

Foro Palatino

★★★★☆
1

The Roman Forum is the nerve center of ancient Rome, formerly a marshy zone drained by the Cloaca Maxima of the Tarquins in the 6th century BC. For nearly a thousand years, it was the site of all political, judicial, and religious decisions of the Empire. The Via Sacra (600 m) crosses it from the Colosseum to the Capitol: this is where triumphant generals paraded laden with spoils. Among the still-visible monuments: the Temple of Saturn (5th century BC, one of Rome's oldest), the Curia of Julius Caesar (rebuilt 29 BC, Senate seat), the Arch of Septimius Severus (203 AD), the Temple of Vesta where the Vestals tended the sacred fire, and the Basilica of Maxentius whose three coffered vaults reached 35 m high.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Palatine Hill—included in the same ticket as the Forum—is the hill where, according to tradition, Romulus founded Rome in 753 BC. Augustus was born here and built his apartments, then his successors settled here with increasingly sumptuous palaces. From the summit, the view embraces the Forum below, the Circus Maximus on the other side, and on clear days, the hills of the Roman countryside.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Look on the floor of the Basilica Julia (south side of the Forum) for game boards carved by Roman soldiers who passed the time between judicial hearings—some are still perfectly legible.

Trastevere

★★★★☆
1

Trastevere ('beyond the Tiber') is one of Rome's oldest neighborhoods: from the Republican era, it welcomed foreigners, merchants, sailors, and the city's first Jews. Julius Caesar owned a villa here with his famous gardens (horti Caesaris). Its medieval urban fabric—cobbled alleys, ochre and brick façades, arches between houses—formed between the 10th and 13th centuries. The inhabitants, the Trasteverini, still spoke a dialect close to Corsican as late as the 19th century, a legacy of the Corsican and Sardinian presence in the quarter. Piazza di Santa Maria in Trastevere is the neighborhood's heart: in the evening, musicians, neighbors, and travelers occupy the church steps.

⏱ 60 min
2

The basilica of Santa Maria in Trastevere is one of Rome's oldest churches dedicated to Mary, founded in the 3rd century. Its 12th-century reconstruction gave it its golden mosaic façade—visible from the square—and its Romanesque bell tower. The interior preserves granite columns salvaged from Roman baths and extraordinary apse mosaics by Pietro Cavallini (13th century).

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Dine in the alleys behind the piazza rather than facing it: Via della Paglia, Via dei Vascellari. Neighborhood trattorias serve classic Roman cuisine—cacio e pepe, coda alla vaccinara—in a persistent village atmosphere.

Santa Cecilia In Trastevere

★★★☆☆
1

The basilica of Santa Cecilia in Trastevere is built on the house where, according to tradition, Cecilia—a 3rd-century Christian Roman noblewoman—suffered her martyrdom: she was locked for three days in the calidarium (steam bath) without perishing, then beheaded. In 1599, during the church's renovation, Cardinal Sfondrati had the saint's sarcophagus opened: the body was found intact, dressed in white, wounds to the neck visible. Stefano Maderno sculpted from Carrara marble an effigy reproducing the exact position of the body lying on its side—it's one of the most moving sculptures of the early Baroque. Pietro Cavallini's fresco of the Last Judgment (1293), hidden in the choir, was covered during a 1725 restoration and rediscovered only in 1900. Cavallini introduced a new realism in the faces and drapery that prefigured Giotto. In the crypt, excavations uncovered a 2nd-century domus and remnants of the baths of the ancient house.

⏱ 50 min

Activities

Access Cavallini's frescoes in the nuns' choir (paid entry, restricted morning hours): one of Rome's least known and most powerful medieval works.

Musei Vaticani

1

The Vatican Museums form one of the world's largest museum complexes, with approximately 1,400 rooms. The core of the collections was founded by Julius II at the beginning of the 16th century with the installation of the Laocoön and other ancient sculptures in the Belvedere courtyard. The Sistine Chapel (built 1477-1481, 40.93 m by 13.41 m, height 20.7 m) was decorated by Michelangelo between 1508 and 1512 for Pope Julius II: 500 m² of fresco, 350 individual figures painted almost single-handedly, working lying down on scaffolding 20 meters high. The vault depicts nine scenes from Genesis; the Creation of Adam is its most famous central panel. In 1534, Clement VII commissioned Michelangelo to paint the Last Judgment on the altar wall, completed in 1541. This is where cardinals gather in conclave to elect the pope: white smoke rises from the chimney on the chapel's roof.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Raphael Rooms (papal chambers on the 3rd floor) were decorated between 1509 and 1520 by Raphael and his pupils at Julius II's request. The School of Athens (Stanza della Segnatura) depicts ancient Greece's greatest philosophers around Plato and Aristotle—Plato has Leonardo da Vinci's face, and Raphael included himself in a discreet self-portrait on the right. The Pinacoteca gathers paintings by Raphael, Leonardo da Vinci, Giotto, Caravaggio, and Titian.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Book Vatican Museums tickets several weeks in advance during high season: queues without reservations can exceed 2 to 3 hours. A complete visit (Museums + Sistine Chapel) requires a minimum of 3 to 4 hours.

San Pietro

1

St. Peter's Basilica is the world's largest Christian church: 218.7 m long, 154.8 m transept, 22,067 m² surface area. Its construction, begun in 1506 by Julius II following Bramante's plans, wasn't completed until 1626 under Urban VIII—120 years and at least seven successive architects (Bramante, Raphael, Sangallo, Michelangelo, della Porta, Maderno, Bernini). Michelangelo, appointed chief architect at age 72 in 1546, designed the double-shelled dome inspired by Brunelleschi's dome in Florence—its interior diameter of 42 m and height of 136.57 m to the top of the cross make it the world's tallest dome. He died in 1564 without seeing it completed; Giacomo della Porta and Domenico Fontana finished it in 22 months with 800 workers laboring day and night, in 1588-1590. Inside, Michelangelo's Pietà (1499) was sculpted at age 24 from a single block of Carrara marble—it's the only work he signed, his name carved on Mary's sash after a visitor mistakenly attributed it to another master. Bernini's bronze baldachin (1623-1634), 29 m high, is placed directly above St. Peter's tomb.

⏱ 90 min
2

St. Peter's Square (1657-1667) is Bernini's elliptical colonnade: 284 columns and 88 pilasters in 4 rows, crowned by 140 statues of saints. On the ground, two marble discs mark the ellipse's focal points—from these points, the four rows of columns merge into a single line, creating the illusion of a simple colonnade. The central obelisk (25.31 m) was erected in Egypt under Caligula then transported to Rome.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Descend to the Vatican Grottoes (free entry through the basilica) where 91 popes are buried. The deeper scavi necropolis reveals excavations conducted under Paul VI starting in 1939 that uncovered what's identified as St. Peter's tomb—visit by reservation with the excavations office.

Cupola Di San Pietro

1

The ascent of the dome (Cupolone) offers one of Rome's most breathtaking viewpoints: first the basilica terrace (elevator access), then 320 spiral steps between the dome's two shells to the lantern. Midway, an interior walkway lets you contemplate the basilica's interior from 53 m high. At the summit, at 120 m, the 360° view embraces St. Peter's Square, the Vatican Gardens, Castel Sant'Angelo, the Tiber, and Rome's seven hills.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Stop at Castel Sant'Angelo to complete the day: Hadrian's Mausoleum (built 135-139 AD) is the only monumental building from ancient Rome still standing in its entirety, not ruined. After serving as a tomb for emperors until Caracalla (217), then as a fortress, prison (Giordano Bruno, Benvenuto Cellini, and Beatrice Cenci were detained here), and papal residence connected to the Vatican by the Passetto di Borgo, it's now a museum. In 590, Pope Gregory I allegedly had a vision of the archangel Michael sheathing his sword at the summit, announcing the end of a plague epidemic—hence the current name.

⏱ 60 min

Ponte Sant'Angelo: the ten marble angels lining it were designed by Bernini between 1667 and 1669, each carrying an instrument of Christ's Passion. The two statues sculpted by Bernini himself are preserved at Sant'Andrea delle Fratte—the angels currently on the bridge are copies. Remarkable view of the castle at sunset.
