Thrace Road Trip: Kavala, Thasos & Philippi

📅 7 days📍 6 stops🚗 299 km⏱ 6h27

Start in Kavala, where a 270-metre Ottoman aqueduct has straddled the city since 1530, then cross to Philippi, where Brutus and Cassius lost Rome in 42 BC and Saint Paul founded Europe's first Christian community. Board a ferry to Thasos, the white-marble island of emerald waters, before diving into the colourful alleyways of Xanthi — once the world capital of basma tobacco — and finishing in Alexandroupoli, gateway to the Evros Delta and its 300 bird species.

✨ Highlights
  • The Kamares Aqueduct in Kavala, built by Ibrahim Pasha between 1520 and 1536
  • The UNESCO site of Philippi, where Saint Paul founded Europe's first Christian community in AD 49
  • Thasos and its Archaeological Museum with the 3.5-metre Kouros from the 6th century BC
  • The Evros Delta, an international Ramsar wetland home to 300 bird species
📆 Best period

May, June, September, October

Day 1

Kavala

A Macedonian port where an Ottoman aqueduct soars above Venetian rooftops and the ghost of Mehmet Ali lingers

★★★☆☆
1

The fortress of Kavala commands the entire peninsula from the summit of Panagia hill. Its current form dates from the early 15th century, though it rests on Byzantine foundations subsequently reworked by the Venetians and the Ottomans. Climb up to the outer walls for the view: at your feet, the red-tiled roofs of the old town, the turquoise harbour, and in the distance the silhouette of Thasos on the horizon. The open-air theatre nestled within the walls hosts performances in summer — check the programme if you're here in July or August.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Kamares Aqueduct is the most photographed monument in the city, and it deserves every shot. This double-tiered structure — 11 large arches below, narrower arches above — stretches 270 metres long and stands 25 metres high. It was built between 1520 and 1536 on the orders of Ibrahim Pasha, Grand Vizier to Suleiman the Magnificent, after the city was destroyed in 1391. Made of local granite with Ottoman brick detailing, it supplied Kavala with water from Mount Pangaion until 1911. The best angle for a photo: from Lahana Street in the late afternoon, when the golden light brings out the stone.

⏱ 30 min
3

In the old quarter of Panagia, look for the house of Mehmet Ali (1769–1849) — the founder of the last dynasty to rule Egypt, born right here in this modest cantilevered stone building. The house is preserved as a museum and gives a good sense of Ottoman domestic architecture of the era. Across from it, the square bearing his name features an equestrian statue of the pasha — one of the rare depictions of a human figure tolerated within Islamic tradition.

⏱ 40 min
4

The Panagia quarter, perched on the peninsula between the fortress and the sea, is the historic heart of Kavala. Its cobbled alleyways, colourful houses with cantilevered balconies (sachnisia), and hidden courtyards make it one of the best-preserved Ottoman neighbourhoods in northern Greece. Don't miss the Halil Bey Mosque, built on the foundations of an early Christian basilica — during the population exchange of 1922–1923, it served as temporary shelter for Greek refugee families.

⏱ 45 min
5

The Imaret, on the western flank of the Panagia peninsula, is one of the largest Ottoman-era Islamic complexes still standing in Europe. Built in the early 19th century by Mehmet Ali to serve as a soup kitchen, madrasa and mosque, it blends Ottoman arches with slender columns around a remarkable inner courtyard. The interior has been privatised, but the exterior and gardens can be explored freely from the lane.

⏱ 25 min

Activities

Stroll along the Kavala waterfront down to the fishing harbour: the blue-and-white boats, nets drying on the quay, and fish tavernas lined up along the water give the city the feel of an authentic Greek port, well away from the mass-tourism circuit. This is where Saint Paul landed on his first journey to Europe — a commemorative plaque near the harbour marks the event.

⏱ 45 min

Sample sardeles pastes: salted, dried and pressed sardines are Kavala's signature culinary speciality, going back centuries. They're eaten on toasted bread, simply drizzled with lemon juice, alongside a glass of Macedonian tsipouro without anise — the local spirit served chilled with olives and feta. The tavernas along the harbour serve both without ceremony.

⏱ 50 min

In the evening, order a saganaki: a slab of graviera cheese, breaded and pan-fried in olive oil, served still crackling with a wedge of lemon. Simple, no-nonsense, and far better than it sounds — it's the go-to appetiser across the entire region.

⏱ 30 min

Day 2

Kavala

A Macedonian port where an Ottoman aqueduct soars above Venetian rooftops and the ghost of Mehmet Ali lingers

★★★☆☆
1

The site of Philippi has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2016 — the 18th Greek site to join the list. Founded in 360 BC by colonists from Thasos under the name Krenides, the city was conquered in 356 BC by Philip II of Macedon, father of Alexander the Great, who gave it his name. Allow at least two hours to cross the entire site — it covers 67 hectares, slightly larger than Pompeii.

⏱ 30 min
2

The theatre of Philippi was built by Philip II in the mid-4th century BC and remodelled under the Romans in the 2nd century AD into a theatre with a monumental three-storey stage building. In the 3rd century it was converted into an arena for gladiatorial combat: the wild animal pit beneath the orchestra is still visible. Since 1957 it has hosted the Philippi Festival every summer — one of the oldest ancient-theatre festivals in Greece.

⏱ 40 min
3

The Roman forum of Philippi is one of the best-documented in the eastern provinces of the Empire: a vast square surrounded by temples, shops, the orators' tribune, and a meeting hall for the decurions. A monumental inscription at least 20 metres long, with metal letters set into the pavement at a height of 62 cm, honoured the flamen of the divine Augustus. Look for the ruts left by chariots on the Via Egnatia — they're still legible in the stone.

⏱ 45 min
4

The early Christian basilicas of Philippi are among the most impressive in Greece. Basilica A (late 5th century) measured 130 by 50 metres, with a transept, galleries and atrium. The Octagon, an episcopal complex built on the presumed site of Saint Paul's house of prayer, went through three construction phases from the 4th to the 6th century. It was here, in AD 49–50, that the Apostle Paul founded Europe's first Christian community — and was briefly imprisoned in a Roman cistern traditionally identified as the 'Prison of Paul'.

⏱ 50 min
5

The Archaeological Museum of Philippi, spread over two floors, presents the site's collections chronologically: sculptures, vases, coins and gold jewellery from prehistoric through Roman times on the ground floor, Christian heritage and the city's decline upstairs. Allow 45 minutes to an hour — the Macedonian jewellery and coins from the era of Philip II are particularly worth your time.

⏱ 50 min

Activities

Side trip to the Baptistery of Saint Lydia, a few hundred metres from the archaeological site, on the bank of the Zygaktis River. Lydia, a wealthy purple-cloth merchant from Thyatira, was the first European baptised by Paul in AD 49–50 in this river. The modern octagonal baptistery dates from 1974, but an open-air baptism ceremony takes place every 20 May — if you happen to be here on that date, you can attend.

⏱ 30 min

Try kavourmas: pork or veal slow-cooked in its own fat with spices — a traditional Macedonian winter preservation method you'll find in tavernas around the Kavala area, served as a hot or cold meze. Pair it with an assyrtiko from Drama: this mineral white wine, produced in the highland vineyards to the west, is dry, bright and sharply acidic.

⏱ 45 min

The evening drive back to Kavala follows the Philippi plain, dominated by Mount Pangaion to the south-west — it was from its gold and silver mines that Philip II bankrolled his conquests. If you have time, stop at Krinides, the small modern village on the site of ancient Krenides, to see the remains of the Hellenistic city walls on the outskirts of the UNESCO site.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 49 km · 1h35
Day 3

Thasos

An emerald island of white marble, colonised by Parian Greeks in the 7th century BC and barely touched since

★★★★☆
1

To reach Thasos, take the ferry from Keramoti, 35 km south-east of Kavala — the crossing takes just 35 minutes and departures run every 30 minutes in season. It's by far the quickest and cheapest option. Once in Limenas, the capital, head down to the ancient agora: it sits right in the heart of the modern town, surrounded by the ruins of sanctuaries dedicated to Heracles, Dionysus, Artemis and Poseidon, with a medieval tower occupying one corner. Information panels at the entrance show a reconstruction of what the square once looked like — helpful for grasping the scale.

⏱ 40 min
2

The ancient theatre of Thasos, built into a natural hollow in the hillside north-east of town, dates in its earliest phases from the late 4th century BC. In the 1st century AD it was converted into an arena for gladiatorial combat. It has been fully restored in recent years using white marble from Thasos — the same stone used in antiquity, quarried on the island. The view from the upper tiers over the harbour of Limenas and the Aegean is one of the finest on the island.

⏱ 40 min
3

The Archaeological Museum of Thasos, founded in 1935 and renovated between 1989 and 2000, is the oldest museum in the Eastern Macedonia and Thrace region. Its centrepiece is the Kouros of Thasos: an archaic statue standing 3.5 metres tall, depicting a young man carrying a ram — a masterpiece of the 6th century BC discovered in 1911 in five pieces inside a Byzantine wall, where it had been reused as building material. The museum also holds a fine head of Alexander the Great and an Aphrodite with a Dolphin regularly lent to major international exhibitions.

⏱ 60 min
4

The acropolis of Thasos dominates the town from its rocky spur. Climb up from the theatre along the signposted path: you'll pass the sanctuaries of Apollo and Athena, then the Sanctuary of Pan carved directly into the rock face. Along the way, look for the Cyclopean marble ramparts — some blocks are fitted so precisely that you still can't slide a credit card between them. The view from the top sweeps across the entire northern coastline of the island.

⏱ 55 min

Activities

Swim at Makryammos, the closest beach to Limenas, just 2 km to the east: a crescent-shaped cove with fine sand, emerald-green water and maritime pines reaching right down to the sea. It's the postcard image of Thasos — the blend of dense vegetation and crystal-clear water that earned the island its nickname of 'the Emerald Isle'.

⏱ 90 min

In the evening, settle into a café on the harbour at Limenas and order a local retsina — the pine-resinated white wine produced on the island, slightly bitter, best drunk chilled with olives and goat's cheese. It's here that you begin to understand why ancient Greece regulated wine production by law: Thasian amphorae stamped with quality-control seals have been found across the entire Mediterranean basin.

⏱ 60 min
Day 4

Thasos

An emerald island of white marble, colonised by Parian Greeks in the 7th century BC and barely touched since

★★★★☆
1

The village of Panagia, perched in the mountains 10 km south of Limenas, is one of the most beautiful on the island: stone fountains, centuries-old plane trees, tile-roofed houses and cobbled lanes winding up the hillside. It's the birthplace of the local dance tradition and one of the best-preserved villages in Eastern Macedonia. Arrive on a Sunday morning to see the locals in traditional dress after church.

⏱ 45 min
2

The village of Theologos, 25 km south-west of Limenas, was the island's medieval capital during Genoese and Turkish rule. It was where the population retreated to escape pirates. Its narrow lanes, stone houses with walled gardens and old fountains set it apart from the modernised coastal villages. Allow 45 minutes for a full circuit.

⏱ 45 min
3

The marble quarries of Alyki, on the cape of the same name 32 km south-east of Limenas, are an exceptional archaeological site: the extraction of Thasian white marble — one of the most prized in antiquity — is documented here from the Archaic period through to Byzantine times. Half-cut blocks have been waiting in the rock for twenty centuries. This white marble with its glittering crystals was used, among other things, to build the Arch of Galerius in Thessaloniki. From the beach at Alyki, submerged blocks are visible beneath the water.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Drive around the island: the coastal road covers 95 km and can be comfortably done in a day with stops. Don't miss Golden Beach (Chrisi Amoudia) on the east coast — a long stretch of golden sand lapped by deep-blue water, widely considered one of the finest beaches in the northern Aegean. Behind the beach, archaeologists uncovered a well dating from the 3rd century BC, and on the headland above, the ruins of a 4th-century BC lighthouse.

⏱ 360 min

Tuck into spit-roasted goat with mountain herbs at a taverna in Theologos or Panagia: it's the island's traditional feast dish, oven-baked or spit-roasted with wild herbs gathered from the slopes of Mount Ipsarion. The mountain-village tavernas are cheaper and more authentic than those on the beaches.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 94 km · 1h45
Day 5

Xanthi

Where 1,200 pastel façades tell the story of basma tobacco, Pomak minarets and a Greek town they once called Little Paris

★★☆☆☆
1

The old town of Xanthi (Palia Poli) is one of the largest preserved traditional settlements in Greece: 380,000 m² declared a protected zone in 1976, with over 1,200 listed buildings. It was rebuilt after two devastating earthquakes in 1829 and reached its zenith between 1880 and 1925 thanks to basma tobacco — an exceptionally aromatic variety that supplied imperial courts and European markets. The Epirote and Macedonian craftsmen who built it blended Baroque, Neoclassical, Romanesque and Ottoman elements into a result the Greeks nicknamed 'Little Paris'.

⏱ 75 min
2

Walk up the main street to Demokratias Square to see the clock tower, erected in 1870 by the Pomak notable Hadji Emin Aga. The Greek authorities wanted to demolish it in 1972 — protests by Muslim residents forced a reversal. It stands as a symbol of Xanthi's particular brand of coexistence, where Greek Orthodox Christians, Turks and Pomaks have shared the same streets for centuries.

⏱ 20 min
3

The History and Folklore Museum of Xanthi occupies the Kougioumtzoglou House, a former double residence built in the 1860s by the Kougioumtzoglou brothers, major tobacco merchants. It's a rare example of a three-storey twin house in the local style. The collections feature traditional costumes, embroidered textiles, craft tools and archives from the tobacco era. Allow an hour to do it justice.

⏱ 55 min
4

Near the train station, the former tobacco warehouses (the Mandra) bear witness to Xanthi's economic might at the turn of the 20th century. The most notable, shaped like the Greek letter Pi (Π), housed the Ottoman tobacco monopoly's operations until 1912. It's currently being converted into a cultural venue. If you drive or bus through the neighbourhood, its imposing red-brick outline is visible from the road.

⏱ 25 min

Activities

Wander through Xanthi's traditional bazaar, in the lanes around Antika Square: this is where you'll find the open-air cafés, spice-scented groceries, leather workshops and Turkish-coffee shopfronts. The atmosphere in the morning is particularly authentic — locals gather here before heading to work. A thick Greek coffee, served with a glass of cool water, is the ritual opener.

⏱ 60 min

Day trip into the Rhodope Mountains to the Pomak villages, about an hour's drive north: Sminthi with its white mosque and stone lanes, Echinos set amid deep forest, Ottoman bridges over mountain torrents. These villages, tucked into the folds of the Rhodopes, have preserved an architecture and way of life virtually unchanged since the 19th century. The road itself is spectacular — forested gorges, pristine rivers, not a tourist in sight.

⏱ 180 min

Try soutzouk loukoum, Xanthi's signature sweet: Turkish delight flavoured with walnuts or rose, sometimes coated in a layer of toasted hazelnuts. It's the Thracian version of a widespread Ottoman confection, but Xanthi's sweet shops have a well-established regional reputation. Pair it with a lightly anise-flavoured Thracian tsipouro — milder than ouzo.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 115 km · 2h18
Day 6

Alexandroupoli

A frontier city whose 1880 lighthouse still guides ships across the Aegean to the flamingo-filled Evros Delta

★★★☆☆
1

The lighthouse of Alexandroupoli has been the city's emblem since 1880: 27 metres tall, six flights of stone steps, and its beam visible from 44 kilometres out. It's still listed under the city's former name, Dede-Agats, on nautical charts — the town was only renamed Alexandroupoli in 1920 during a visit by King Alexander of Greece, and definitively incorporated into Greece by the Treaty of Lausanne in 1923. Grab a terrace seat facing the lighthouse for your morning coffee — it's the best introduction to the city.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Ethnological Museum of Thrace occupies a Neoclassical house dating from 1899. Its collections of embroidered costumes, jewellery, craft tools and photographic archives offer a dense snapshot of Thracian culture — a region that was successively Bulgarian, Greek and Turkish in the 20th century before reaching its present configuration. Allow an hour: it's small but thoroughly documented.

⏱ 55 min
3

Some 28 km west of Alexandroupoli, in the village of Feres, the Monastery of Panagia Kosmosotira is worth the detour. Founded in 1152 by Isaac Comnenus, son of Emperor Alexios III, it's one of the oldest churches still standing in northern Greece. Its layout echoes Hagia Sophia in miniature, with 12th-century frescoes from the Constantinopolitan school. It became a mosque in 1357 and was returned to Orthodox worship in 1920.

⏱ 50 min

Activities

Excursion to the Evros Delta, designated a national park in 2007 and a Ramsar wetland since 1974: 188 km² of wetlands on the Turkish border where 300 of Greece's 400 bird species pass through or nest. Dalmatian pelicans, white storks, purple herons and greater flamingos are regularly spotted. Start at the Traianoupoli Information Centre (village of Loutra) to obtain your military-zone entry permits and book a boat ride on the Drana Lagoon. The trip lasts about 1 h 30.

⏱ 240 min

Evening walk along the Alexandroupoli seafront: the fish tavernas line up facing the Aegean and the illuminated lighthouse provides the backdrop. Order shrimp saganaki — baked in a tomato sauce with melted feta and ouzo — it's the unmissable local speciality, far better here than in Athens because the prawns come straight from the gulf.

⏱ 75 min

For your last meal, try kagianas: scrambled eggs with fresh tomato and feta, served on village bread. Technically a breakfast dish or an evening meze, though the tavernas in this region serve it at any hour. Simple, filling, and deeply rooted in everyday Thracian cooking.

⏱ 40 min
