Stop 1

British Museum

Where two million years of human history gather under one roof

★★★★★
📍 British Museum London📍 Rosetta Stone British Museum📍 Parthenon Marbles British Museum📍 Great Court British Museum
1

The British Museum, founded in 1753 from the 71,000-object collection bequeathed by physician Hans Sloane, is the world's first national public museum. Its collections now comprise over 8 million objects spanning two million years of human history—of which only 1% is on permanent display, roughly 80,000 pieces. Plan at least 2.5 hours for the highlights, though enthusiastic history lovers can easily spend an entire day here. Start with the Rosetta Stone (196 BC), which unlocked the decipherment of Egyptian hieroglyphs—it's the museum's most-viewed object. Then head upstairs to the Parthenon Marbles, acquired in the early 19th century by Lord Elgin and claimed by Greece for decades. Don't miss the Sutton Hoo treasure, a 7th-century Anglo-Saxon warrior's helmet, and the Lewis Chessmen carved from walrus ivory in the 12th century. The Queen Elizabeth II Great Court, designed by Norman Foster and unveiled in 2000, is Europe's largest covered public square—look up to admire the canopy of 3,000 glass panels.

⏱ 150 min

Activities

The museum offers a three-hour guided tour covering the masterpieces—an efficient way to avoid getting lost among the 60 galleries. If you prefer exploring independently, download the official app or grab a map at the entrance.

British Library

From medieval gospels to Beatles lyrics—Britain's literary treasures in one room

★★★★☆
📍 British Library London📍 British Library Treasures Gallery📍 King's Cross British Library
1

The British Library, opened in its current building near King's Cross in 1998 after 15 years of construction, is the largest public building erected in the UK in the 20th century. It holds over 170 million items on 625 km of shelving and receives a copy of every publication produced in the UK and Ireland—roughly 3 million additions per year. For visitors, one room is essential: the Treasures Gallery (free admission), where you'll find two of the four original copies of the 1215 Magna Carta, a 1455 Gutenberg Bible (one of 49 surviving copies), the 8th-century Lindisfarne Gospels, the Diamond Sutra from 868 AD (the oldest known printed book), the 4th-century Codex Sinaiticus, Shakespeare's First Folio from 1623, and the lyrics to 'Yesterday' scribbled by Paul McCartney on an envelope. The room is kept in low light to protect these treasures—allow 1.5 to 2 hours to properly view everything.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

If you're interested in research, request a reader's pass to access the reading rooms. Otherwise, the museum shop offers high-quality reproductions of certain manuscripts.

Madame Tussauds is an attraction founded in London in 1835 by Marie Tussaud, whose origins trace back to the French Revolution—she had created the death masks of Marie Antoinette and Louis XVI. If you're visiting with children or celebrity enthusiasts, book your time slot in advance as queues get long during peak season.

Design Museum

From the first iPhone to the Olympic torch—design shaping our world

★★★★☆
📍 Design Museum Kensington High Street London📍 Designer Maker User Design Museum London
1

The Design Museum on Kensington High Street was founded in 1989 and relocated in 2016 to a Grade II* listed 1960s building, formerly the Commonwealth Institute, renovated for £83 million by architect John Pawson. It's the only London museum entirely devoted to contemporary design in all its forms—architecture, fashion, graphic design, industrial and digital design. The permanent collection Designer Maker User (free admission) gathers around 1,000 objects from the 20th and 21st centuries: you'll find the London 2012 Olympic torch, a Sony Walkman, the first Apple iPhone, British road signage, and an AK-47. The museum won the European Museum of the Year Award in 2018. Temporary exhibitions are ticketed and often very popular—book ahead.

Natural History Museum

Where Hope the blue whale soars above a Victorian cathedral of nature

★★★★★
📍 Natural History Museum London📍 Hintze Hall Natural History Museum📍 Dinosaur Gallery Natural History Museum London
1

The Natural History Museum in South Kensington, designed by Alfred Waterhouse and opened in 1881, is sometimes called the 'cathedral of nature'—its terracotta facades adorned with sculpted animals fully justify this nickname. Upon entering the Hintze Hall, look up: since July 2017, you'll find Hope, the 25.2-metre blue whale skeleton suspended from the ceiling, weighing 4.5 tonnes and comprising 221 bones. She replaced Dippy the Diplodocus, who had greeted visitors for 112 years since 1905—a cast gifted by industrialist Andrew Carnegie for £2,000 at the time. Hope represents the largest animal ever to exist on Earth, and her story is poignant: this 15-year-old female beached in Wexford harbour in Ireland in March 1891 after being wounded by whalers. For dinosaurs, the dedicated gallery houses an animatronic T-Rex and skeletons of Stegosaurus and Triceratops. Allow at least 2 hours to see the highlights.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

On weekends during school holidays, the museum gets mobbed. Arrive at opening time (10am) to avoid long queues. Admission is free but temporary exhibitions are ticketed.

Science Museum

Home to the only Apollo capsule outside America and Britain's industrial legacy

★★★★☆
📍 Science Museum London📍 Apollo 10 Science Museum London📍 Space Gallery Science Museum London
1

The Science Museum in South Kensington holds over 300,000 objects in its collections and houses one of the most striking pieces in all of London: the Apollo 10 capsule nicknamed Charlie Brown, the only Apollo spacecraft displayed outside the United States. In May 1969, Thomas Stafford, John Young and Eugene Cernan conducted a dress rehearsal for the first Moon landing in this capsule, approaching within 14.5 km of the lunar surface without touching down. The capsule arrived at the Science Museum in 1976 and has resided in the new Space gallery inaugurated in 2025, alongside the Soyuz TMA-19M capsule that brought British astronaut Tim Peake back to Earth. You can still see the hand-scribbled calculations by astronauts on the interior walls. Nearby in the Making the Modern World gallery, don't miss Stephenson's Rocket from 1829 and the first commercial steam engine.

Victoria Albert Museum

Where decorative arts span five millennia and Victoria demanded modesty

★★★★★
📍 Victoria and Albert Museum London📍 Cast Courts V&A London📍 Fashion Gallery V&A London
1

The Victoria & Albert Museum, founded in 1852 from the profits of the 1851 Great Exhibition, is the world's largest museum of decorative arts and design. Its 145 galleries across 12.5 acres house 2.3 million objects covering 5,000 years of human creativity. Its first director Henry Cole declared he wanted to make the museum 'a schoolroom for everyone'—thus inventing the concept of the museum café to encourage visits. Queen Victoria laid the foundation stone of the current building in 1899 during her last public appearance. Don't miss the Cast Courts on the ground floor: two immense halls filled with life-size plaster casts of the world's greatest artworks, including Michelangelo's David—Queen Victoria, shocked by David's anatomy, demanded that a modesty fig leaf be placed on the statue during official visits. Also seek out the Great Bed of Ware, a 16th-century bed three metres wide mentioned by Shakespeare, and the 1937 evening coat designed by Elsa Schiaparelli with a collar in the form of a bouquet created by Jean Cocteau. Allow at least 2.5 hours.

⏱ 150 min

Activities

On Friday evenings, the V&A stays open until 10pm with special events—a pleasant way to visit the galleries without daytime crowds.

Stop 8

Wallace Collection

Where Rococo masterpieces hide in plain sight off Oxford Street

★★★★★
📍 Wallace Collection London Manchester Square📍 The Swing Fragonard Wallace Collection📍 Laughing Cavalier Wallace Collection
1

The Wallace Collection is one of London's best-kept secrets: a collection of 5,500 artworks housed in Hertford House, a Georgian townhouse on Manchester Square, five minutes from Oxford Street. Admission is free. The collection, assembled by four generations of Marquesses of Hertford and by Richard Wallace in the 19th century, was bequeathed to the nation by Lady Wallace in 1897 with one condition: nothing can be added or removed. The centrepiece is Fragonard's The Swing (c.1768), considered the absolute masterpiece of Rococo. A courtier had commissioned the painting from a religious painter, who refused the overly licentious subject and suggested Fragonard—the scene shows a woman on a swing kicking off her shoe, allowing her lover to admire beneath her skirts while her husband remains in shadow. This painting relaunched Fragonard's career. Right next to it, Frans Hals' The Laughing Cavalier (1624): this portrait of an unknown 26-year-old man painted with brushstrokes of stunning modernity was purchased in 1865 by the 4th Marquess of Hertford outbidding a Rothschild. The gallery also includes works by Rembrandt, Velázquez, Rubens, Poussin and a remarkable armoury.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Free guided tours of the Great Gallery are offered at 3pm Friday through Sunday. It's the most efficient way to understand the major works in 30 minutes.

Royal Academy Arts

Hosting the world's longest-running exhibition series since 1769

★★★☆☆
📍 Royal Academy of Arts London📍 Burlington House Piccadilly London
1

The Royal Academy of Arts has occupied Burlington House on Piccadilly since 1868. Founded in 1768 with Joshua Reynolds as first president, it's the UK's oldest institution dedicated to the fine arts. Its temporary exhibitions are among London's most sought-after—the annual Summer Exhibition, running since 1769 without interruption, is the world's longest-running exhibition series. Auguste Rodin exhibited here in 1914 during his first major exhibition in his lifetime, before bequeathing his sculptures to the institution as a symbol of Franco-British friendship. Check the programme before visiting: the permanent collections are less spectacular than the major temporary exhibitions.

National Gallery

Seven centuries of European masterpieces facing Trafalgar Square—entirely free

★★★★☆
📍 National Gallery London Trafalgar Square📍 Van Gogh Sunflowers National Gallery📍 Arnolfini Portrait National Gallery
1

The National Gallery, facing Trafalgar Square, opened in 1824 with just 38 paintings in a townhouse—today its collections hold over 2,300 paintings spanning 700 years of European art. Admission is free, which was revolutionary at the time. Van Gogh's Sunflowers are in Room 43: painted in August 1888 in Arles over one week to decorate his friend Gauguin's bedroom, this painting is one of seven versions in the series—Van Gogh himself declared 'The sunflower is me.' It was acquired by the National Gallery in 1924. Don't miss Leonardo da Vinci's The Virgin of the Rocks, Jan van Eyck's Arnolfini Portrait (1434) and Holbein's The Ambassadors with its famous distorted skull in trompe-l'oeil. The Impressionist room is always packed—arrive at 10am or during lunch. Allow 2 to 3 hours minimum.

⏱ 150 min

Activities

Free audio guides are available via the National Gallery app—an excellent way to delve deeper into the most important paintings without following a group.

National Portrait Gallery

British history told through faces, from Tudor monarchs to modern icons

★★★☆☆
📍 National Portrait Gallery London📍 National Portrait Gallery Trafalgar Square
1

The National Portrait Gallery, steps from the National Gallery, reopened in 2023 after a major renovation, presents historical and contemporary portraits of figures who shaped British history—from Queen Elizabeth I to current celebrities. The collection is organized chronologically: start on the top level and descend through the centuries. If you only have limited time, focus on the Tudor and Stuart galleries where you'll find the most striking portraits, including the only painted portrait of Shakespeare from his lifetime.

Tate Britain

Where Turner's legacy meets the controversial Turner Prize

★★★★☆
📍 Tate Britain London Millbank📍 Turner Collection Tate Britain📍 Clore Gallery Tate Britain
1

Tate Britain, on the banks of the Thames at Millbank, is the gallery dedicated to British art from 1500 to the present day. If your time is limited, head straight to the Clore Gallery: it houses the world's largest collection of J.M.W. Turner's works, bequeathed by the artist himself to the nation at his death in 1851—hundreds of paintings and thousands of sketches covering his entire career. The Turner Prize, the UK's most famous and most controversial contemporary art prize, is awarded here annually. Don't miss William Blake, John Constable and the Pre-Raphaelites either. Admission is free.

National Maritime Museum

Where Nelson's blood-stained coat tells the story of Trafalgar

★★★☆☆
📍 National Maritime Museum Greenwich📍 Nelson Trafalgar coat National Maritime Museum📍 Royal Observatory Greenwich📍 Cutty Sark Greenwich
1

The National Maritime Museum in Greenwich is the world's largest maritime museum, with nearly 2.5 million objects in its collections. Established by Act of Parliament in 1934 and opened in 1937, it occupies the buildings of the former Royal Hospital School, including the 17th-century Queen's House designed by Inigo Jones. The museum's most moving object—and one of the most poignant in all of England—is the admiral's coat Nelson wore at the Battle of Trafalgar on 21 October 1805. You can still see the bullet hole in the left shoulder of the uniform, at the precise spot where the French marksman from the Redoutable shot him at 1:15pm. The right sleeve is pinned—a reminder that Nelson had lost that arm at the Battle of Santa Cruz de Tenerife in 1795. He died a few hours later knowing that the Franco-Spanish fleet had been defeated. Don't miss the gilded 19-metre state barge built in 1732 for the Prince of Wales, and Turner's largest painting, The Battle of Trafalgar (1824). Make the trip to Greenwich: the area deserves a full half-day, between the museum, the Royal Observatory and the Greenwich meridian.

⏱ 150 min

Activities

Take advantage of your Greenwich visit to climb to the Royal Observatory—15 minutes on foot uphill from the museum. You can photograph yourself straddling the prime meridian and see John Harrison's H4 marine chronometer, which solved the longitude problem in the 18th century.

Tower Of London

Where Norman stone guards a thousand years of intrigue and the Crown Jewels

★★★★★
📍 Tower of London📍 Crown Jewels Tower of London📍 White Tower London📍 Yeoman Warders Tower of London
1

The Tower of London, a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1988, is one of Europe's most remarkable Norman fortresses. The White Tower—its central keep standing 27 metres tall with walls 4.5 metres thick—was built from 1078 on the orders of William the Conqueror using Caen stone imported from Normandy. It has successively served as a royal palace, armoury, prison for high-ranking prisoners and royal menagerie. Anne Boleyn was executed here, King Edward IV's two sons mysteriously disappeared here, and Guy Fawkes was tortured here after the Gunpowder Plot of 1605. Today, the Tower houses the Crown Jewels in the Jewel House: over 23,000 gemstones including the 530-carat Cullinan I diamond (the world's largest cut diamond) set in the royal sceptre, and the 105-carat Koh-i-Noor diamond in the Queen Mother's crown. The Crown Jewels have been displayed to the public since 1661—one of Britain's most visited attractions. Book tickets online in advance.

⏱ 150 min

Activities

Don't miss the guided tour by the Yeoman Warders (the Beefeaters): these guards in Tudor uniforms bring history to life with quintessentially British verve. Tours depart every 30 minutes from the main entrance—they're included in admission.

Tate Modern

An industrial power station reborn as a cathedral of contemporary art

★★★★★
📍 Tate Modern London Bankside📍 Turbine Hall Tate Modern📍 Millennium Bridge Tate Modern
1

Tate Modern has occupied the former Bankside Power Station since 2000, built by Sir Giles Gilbert Scott—the same architect who designed the red British telephone boxes. The museum conversion cost £134 million and was carried out by Swiss architects Herzog & de Meuron, who preserved the industrial skeleton while creating a 155-metre-long, 35-metre-high turbine hall. This hall has hosted the world's most ambitious art commissions since 2000: Olafur Eliasson's artificial sun in 2003 attracted two million visitors, Ai Weiwei's 100 million porcelain sunflower seeds in 2010, and Carsten Höller's giant slides in 2006. The opening night brought together Yoko Ono, Tony Blair and Kylie Minogue. Permanent collections are free, temporary exhibitions ticketed. Allow at least 2 hours for the permanent galleries and turbine hall.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Head to the top floor of the Blavatnik Building for panoramic views over the Thames, St Paul's and the City rooftops—one of London's best free views.

Stop 17

Courtauld Gallery

Where Manet's final masterpiece challenges perception itself

★★★★★
📍 Courtauld Gallery London Somerset House📍 Bar Folies-Bergere Manet Courtauld📍 Somerset House London
1

The Courtauld Gallery, housed in Somerset House on the Strand since its 2021 reopening after restoration, holds one of the world's finest Impressionist and Post-Impressionist collections. Samuel Courtauld, a British industrialist, began buying French paintings in 1922 at a time when they were widely scorned in Britain—he wanted to 'transform the representation of modern French art in the UK.' His most famous acquisition is Édouard Manet's A Bar at the Folies-Bergère (1882), the painter's last major work, completed a year before his death while already gravely ill. Manet had installed a fake bar in his studio and had a real Folies-Bergère barmaid named Suzon pose. The composition is deliberately troubling: the reflections in the mirror behind the barmaid don't correspond to normal perspective, and it's unclear whether the woman in the background talking to a man is her or her reflection. Also seek out Renoir's La Loge, Cézanne's The Card Players, and Toulouse-Lautrec's Jane Avril at the Moulin Rouge.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Somerset House café in the interior courtyard is pleasant between visits. In winter, the courtyard transforms into an ice rink.

Sir John Soanes Museum

An architect's eccentric treasure maze frozen in time since 1837

★★★★★
📍 Sir John Soane Museum Lincoln's Inn Fields London📍 Soane Museum Picture Room London
1

Sir John Soane's Museum at Lincoln's Inn Fields is one of London's strangest and most fascinating places—and it's free. John Soane (1753–1837), architect of the Bank of England, transformed three adjoining townhouses into a labyrinth of rooms where he crammed his collections according to personal logic: Egyptian sarcophagi, Roman columns, Hogarth paintings, architectural models, Greco-Roman antiquities. The house is preserved exactly as it was at his death in 1837, by an Act of Parliament he had passed during his lifetime. The Picture Room is a marvel of ingenuity: Soane installed hinged panels that unfold to reveal 118 paintings hidden behind each other—including Hogarth's eight-painting series A Rake's Progress (1733). In the Sepulchral Chamber rests the alabaster sarcophagus of Pharaoh Seti I, discovered in Egypt in 1812 and considered one of the most important objects ever found in Egypt. Arrive early—the museum is very popular and space is limited.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Picture Room panels are opened at fixed times (11am, 2pm, 3pm, 4pm)—plan your visit to witness the opening of the panels, it's a special moment.
