Waikabubak

★★☆☆☆
1

Welcome to Sumba — one of the last islands in the Indonesian archipelago where a living megalithic civilization still buries its dead beneath multi-ton stone slabs. Lede Kalumbang Airport (formerly Tambolaka, IATA: TMC) was built in 1945 by the Japanese military as a defensive base; its terminal, renovated in 2015 and inaugurated by President Jokowi, sports a pointed roofline replicating the silhouette of the traditional uma mbatangu houses. From the airport, a 90-minute drive takes you to Waikabubak, the capital of West Sumba, perched at 400 meters on a green plateau where water buffalo occasionally wander through town.

⏱ 60 min
2

Kampung Tarung and Kampung Waitabar are two adjoining traditional villages perched right on the edge of downtown Waikabubak — a ten-minute walk from the market. Their tall conical-roofed thatched houses (uma mbatangu) flank carved stone megalithic tombs where kings and clan chiefs rest alongside their sacrificed horses and buffalo. The Rato (animist priests) still officiate here for Marapu ceremonies: harvest festivals, funerals and purification rituals. Announce your presence at the entrance and leave a donation in the guest book — these villages are inhabited, not museums.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Wander through Waikabubak market (Pasar Inpres, Ahmad Yani Street) for a first taste of local flavors and crafts. You'll find ikat cloth — the ceremonial hand-dyed and hand-woven textile that can take months to produce for a single piece — Sumbanese machetes (parang) with carved handles, and bracelets braided from banana leaves. Come early in the morning to cross paths with farmers descending from surrounding villages with baskets of vegetables and betel nuts.

Waikabubak

★★☆☆☆
1

Praijing is the most accessible and best-preserved traditional village in West Sumba, about an hour's drive from Waikabubak. Its 38 houses (originally 42, four destroyed by fire) with conical thatched roofs reaching 10 to 15 meters cluster on a hilltop with panoramic views over the rice paddies below. Arrive in the morning: you'll see women weaving ikat on their doorsteps, dyeing the threads with natural indigo and stretching them on stakes to dry. The height of the roof is not an aesthetic choice — it's a marker of social status: the higher the roof points skyward, the nobler the lineage.

⏱ 90 min
2

Twenty kilometers east of Waikabubak, the village of Pasunga (Anakalang district) holds one of the largest concentrations of megalithic tombs on Sumba. The main tomb — a multi-ton slab finely carved in bas-relief — is reputed to be the most elaborate on the entire island. The motifs depict ancestors, sandalwood horses (a symbol of wealth in Sumba) and sacred crocodiles. The extraction and transport of these blocks is still done by human traction during collective ceremonies called wulla poddu, gathering hundreds of villagers.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Horseback ride through the hills around Waikabubak — Sumba's sandalwood horses, small and sturdy, have been bred on the island for centuries. They serve as bride-price currency at weddings, ritual companions at funerals, and living weapons during the Pasola. Local operators offer one- to two-hour sunset rides through the hills, with views of longhouses perched on ridges and terraced rice paddies.

Tambolaka

★★☆☆☆
1

Ratenggaro is arguably the most spectacular traditional village on Sumba: its uma mbatangu houses with thatched roofs soaring 15 to 20 meters dominate the beach at the mouth of the Waiha River, on the island's southwest corner. The name comes from the Sumbanese rate (tomb) and nggaro (the founding people) — according to legend, this village was captured in a tribal war and the defeated were buried here. It counts 304 officially catalogued megalithic tombs, some dating back 4,500 years. Three of them have a unique shape and still serve as sacred territorial markers. Arrive early — the morning light on the thatched roofs and white sand is the best of the day.

⏱ 120 min
2

Weekuri Lagoon is one of the most striking natural wonders on Sumba: a turquoise saltwater pool 130 meters long and 40 meters wide, separated from the Indian Ocean by 10- to 15-meter coral cliffs. Seawater seeps through fissures in the reef, creating a calm, crystal-clear lagoon while waves crash on the other side of the rocks. At low tide you can see starfish on the white bottom; at high tide the water level rises to 5 meters in places — check tide times before diving in. A wooden bridge crosses the pool so you can admire both sides at once.

⏱ 90 min
3

Mandorak is a small white-sand cove framed by two tall rock cliffs that form a natural amphitheater facing the Indian Ocean. The deep-turquoise water here is clear and sheltered from the waves by the flanking rocks — ideal for swimming. No warung, no infrastructure: just the beach, the surf and a few local fishermen. Enjoy this solitude — it's becoming rarer on Sumba by the year.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Horseback ride on Ratenggaro beach — the sandalwood horse owners from the village spontaneously offer beach rides along the megalithic tombs. Galloping over white sand in front of the pointed rooflines and millennia-old stone blocks is one of the most unforgettable moments on Sumba. Negotiate directly on the spot.

⏱ 60 min

Tambolaka

★★☆☆☆
1

The Wanokaka coastline is one of the wildest stretches of shore in all of Indonesia — a succession of bays separated by sheer cliffs plunging into the Indian Ocean. Nihiwatu Beach, in the Wanokaka district, is home to the NIHI Sumba resort (voted best hotel in the world multiple times) and the surf break known as "Occy's Left" — a perfect left-hander over reef, one of the finest in the region, accessible exclusively to resort guests. But the beach itself — white sand, clear water, coconut palms and green hills behind — is open to all from the coastal road. It's also on the Wanokaka plain that the March Pasola is held each year.

⏱ 90 min
2

Marosi Beach, in the Lamboya district, is a long stretch of near-deserted white sand backed by green hills. The Indian Ocean hits hard here — swimming is inadvisable when the swell is up, but the spectacle of the waves and the absolute solitude make it a rare find. A 15-minute walk away, the village of Lamboya is one of the four official Pasola grounds (along with Kodi, Wanokaka and Gaura). During the festival (Feb–Mar, timed to the nyale worm calendar), hundreds of riders in traditional dress charge at each other with wooden lances.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

⚠️ If you're visiting Sumba in February or March: the Pasola is a ritual mounted combat where two clans face off hurling blunted wooden lances at high speed. The word pasola comes from the Sumbanese sola (wooden lance), itself derived from the Sanskrit sula. The ritual begins when the Rato priests observe the arrival of nyale worms on the shore — a sign that the planting season can begin. The blood spilled (both human and animal) is considered an offering that fertilizes the earth. Dates are announced only 1 to 2 weeks in advance. Four sessions take place: Kodi and Lamboya in February, Gaura and Wanokaka in March. Arrive early (6 AM) — the atmosphere of the preparations in the villages is as powerful as the battle itself.

⏱ 240 min

Outside Pasola season, the Wanokaka district is worth visiting for its traditional villages perched on the hills overlooking the coast. Waigalli, Prai Goli and Waru Wora are accessible Marapu villages reachable on foot from the coastal road. Their inhabitants still practice ancestor ceremonies and will welcome you with betel nut and tuak if you come with respect and a local guide.

⏱ 120 min

Waikabubak

★★☆☆☆
1

The Kodi district, 2 hours from Tambolaka heading east, is home to the most isolated and most traditional villages in West Sumba. Bondo Kodi and Wainyapu are known for their uma mbatangu houses — among the tallest on the island, reaching up to 20 meters — and for the local Pasola ground where two sessions are held in February. This is where the ritual is at its rawest and least touristy. The people of Kodi have raised horses for generations, and their ikat motifs — bold rearing horses on red and black backgrounds — are distinct from the textiles produced elsewhere on the island.

⏱ 120 min
2

The village of Pero, on the Kodi coast, is a Bugis fishing settlement established in this sheltered bay for generations. The beach is beautiful and calm — a lunch of fish grilled directly on the sand, amid nets drying between the palms, ranks among the best meals of the trip. Pero is also the departure point for ferry connections to Sape (Sumbawa) for travelers looking to reach Komodo from the west.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Return to Waikabubak via the northern coastal road — longer but dotted with sea views and villages where women lay out their indigo-dyed ikats to dry. Stop at the Rumah Budaya Sumba (Sumba Cultural House) in Waitabula if you pass through: it gathers a sample of ritual costumes, Marapu objects, mamuli (gold pendants shaped like a uterus, the central feminine symbol and key item of the bride-price dowry) and ceremonial lances.

Waingapu

★★☆☆☆
1

The trans-Sumba road from Waikabubak to Waingapu (137 km, 4 hours) cuts through the heart of the island and reveals its most arresting landscape: golden savannas stretching to the horizon, dotted with red termite mounds and herds of semi-wild sandalwood horses. Pull over at Bukit Tanarara, a panoramic viewpoint over the central hills where horses graze freely — the kind of Sumba image that earns the island its nickname as "the Africa of Indonesia." Take advantage of the drive to buy ikat directly in the small villages along the way — prices are lower than at the tourist shops.

⏱ 60 min
2

Wairinding Hill (Bukit Wairinding) is the most photographed landscape in East Sumba: a succession of gentle hills blanketed in savanna grass that shifts color with the seasons — tender green from November to June, gold and ochre from July to October. The rounded ridgelines evoke a sleeping giant (raksasa tidur in Indonesian), and part of the Indonesian film "Pendekar Tongkat Emas" by Mira Lesmana (2014) was shot here. Children offer traditional costumes to rent for photos; local riders pose their horses on the crests for a few rupiah. Go at sunrise (5:30 AM) or sunset (4 PM) for the light.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Puru Kambera savanna, east of Waingapu, is a vast grassland where herds of wild and semi-wild horses emerge in a scene reminiscent of the great African plains. The park is also home to deer, buffalo and numerous endemic bird species. If you visit in the dry season (July–October), the tall yellowed grasses bend in the wind like an ocean of straw — a spectacle that is as much about sound as it is about sight.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Arrival in Waingapu in the late afternoon — the capital of East Sumba is an easygoing port town on the bay of Waingapu. Its historic harbor, where Makassarese and Arab traders came to load sandalwood and horses in the 16th and 17th centuries, is still active. Take an evening stroll along the waterfront (Siring Piere Tendean) and eat at the grilled-fish warungs that set up on the roadside after dark.

⏱ 90 min

Waingapu

★★☆☆☆
1

Prailiu is the "royal village" of Waingapu — a traditional enclave on the immediate outskirts of town, regarded as the cultural heart of the Kambera royalty. Its large bas-relief megalithic tombstones mark the burials of former rajas. Most importantly, it's the main ikat weaving center of East Sumba, where women weave at home producing hinggi (large ceremonial mantles for men) and lau (sarongs for women) using ancestral techniques: cotton threads are tied in bundles with palm leaves to reserve the patterns, dipped in baths of natural dye (indigo, kombu root for red), dried, then hand-woven. A single high-quality cloth can take 6 to 12 months of work.

⏱ 120 min
2

Walakiri Beach, 24 km from Waingapu, is world-famous for its "dancing trees" — dwarf mangroves whose trunks, twisted by decades of monsoon winds and salt spray, form expressive silhouettes at sunset. When the tide drops around 4–5 PM, the roots emerge from the shallow water and their reflection on the calm surface creates remarkable photographic compositions. Come 45 minutes before sunset knowing the low-tide schedule — without it, the experience is ordinary; with it, it's unforgettable. Note: don't climb the trees — some have already died under the weight of selfie-takers.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Ikat weaving workshop at Prailiu: the artisans of the Prailiu Weaver's Collective welcome visitors to observe and understand every step of the process — from raw cotton hand-spun into thread to the finished cloth dyed in natural colors. Each motif — horses, crocodiles, deer, fish — tells a clan or ceremonial story. An authentic naturally dyed piece can be recognized by its slightly asymmetric colors and its indigo scent; chemically dyed pieces shine with a uniform red. Buy directly from the weavers to support their cooperative.

Waingapu

★★☆☆☆
1

Day trip to the eastern weaving villages: from Melolo (60 km from Waingapu, 1 hour 15 minutes), fan out to the villages of Pau, Umbara and Tambahak in the fertile delta of the Melolo River. Pau village is the most celebrated for its high-quality ikat — featuring traditional East Sumba motifs including horsemen, dolphins, crocodiles and ancestral spirits in red-brown and indigo blue on an off-white background. In eastern Sumba, the motifs are figurative (village scenes, mythical animals), while in the west they're geometric — two distinct visual languages on the same island.

⏱ 120 min
2

Tanggedu Waterfall, in the Kanatang region east of Waingapu, is often nicknamed the "Grand Canyon of East Sumba": the river plunges 8 meters off a limestone cliff into a deep blue-green pool, framed by layered rock walls in red and ochre strata. Rapids channeled between stone walls create a natural body-rafting course. The water is clear and the bottom visible. Count on 300 steps down from the parking area — and remember you'll need to climb back up.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Body-rafting on the Tanggedu River — the natural currents between the limestone walls carry you through gentle rapids interspersed with swimming pools. The water is clean and cool. The activity is family-friendly as long as the children can swim — locals do it naturally. Bring water sandals and avoid weekends, when it gets busier.

Waingapu

★★☆☆☆
1

Tarimbang Beach, 80 km south of Waingapu, is one of the finest in East Sumba — a long crescent of white sand framed by limestone cliffs dropping into the Indian Ocean. It's also an intermediate-to-advanced surf spot, with a right-hander over reef that works on the dry-season swells (May–October). Campers and local surfers set up here for days at a stretch. The road in is partly unpaved — bring a 4x4 or a trail scooter.

⏱ 90 min
2

Waimarang Waterfall, alongside rice paddies in the central East Sumba region, is a succession of three natural rock tiers forming stone pools of green, translucent water. Moss-covered limestone cliffs frame the site with a rare coolness in this otherwise dry landscape. Wild orchids grow in the crevices of the rocks — watch your footing. It's a good meditation and rest stop on the road to Tarimbang.

Waingapu's harbor, around the old sandalwood warehouses, is still active with PELNI boats linking Sumba to Bali, Lombok, Flores and Timor. An early-morning walk along the docks before your departure will show you fishermen coming in with their catch, colorful wooden boats and a handful of old Chinese trading houses from the colonial era. The town is emptier and more photogenic at 7 AM than at any other hour.
