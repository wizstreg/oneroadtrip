November, December, January, February, March

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 21 and 25 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Summer (December-February): Peak season with warmest weather (18-25°C), long daylight hours, and best conditions for hiking, beaches, and outdoor activities. Highest prices and crowds.

Autumn (March-May): Excellent weather with comfortable temperatures (12-22°C), stunning fall colors, and fewer tourists. Great value season with stable weather conditions.

Winter (June-August): Cool temperatures (5-15°C) perfect for skiing, fewer crowds, and lower accommodation rates. Best time for Southern Alps snow sports and cozy indoor experiences.

Spring (September-November): Mild weather (10-20°C) with blooming landscapes, moderate crowds, and good value. Ideal for hiking and outdoor activities before summer crowds arrive.

🌍 What to discover in NZ?

Adventure paradise with stunning landscapes, Maori culture, and outdoor activities. Experience Lord of the Rings scenery, traditional culture, and incredible natural beauty. The country offers adventure activities with cultural authenticity and natural preservation. New Zealand provides life-changing experiences with natural wonders and cultural richness.

📋 Important notes

The Wellington-Picton ferry is mandatory between D13 and D14 (approx. 3h). Book ahead in high season. The East Cape road (SH35) is fully sealed but winding; allow plenty of time. The Forgotten World Highway (SH43) includes 12 km of gravel. East Cape lighthouse requires a 20 km return trip on gravel from Te Araroa. Franz Josef glaciers are no longer accessible on foot to the terminal face; helicopter flights available on-site.

Stop 1

Auckland

Where volcanic peaks breach a city of sails and twin harbours

★★★★★
📍 Mount Eden Auckland📍 Viaduct Harbour Auckland📍 Sky Tower Auckland📍 Auckland Waterfront
1

Auckland is built across 53 volcanic cones - no other city on earth can claim that. Climb to the crater rim of Mount Eden (Maungawhau) at 196 m elevation: the panorama over the isthmus, both harbours and the skyline is worth every step. At the bottom of the perfectly preserved crater, ancient Māori cultivation terraces are still visible from the rim.

⏱ 60 min
2

Head down to Viaduct Harbour, a former fishing basin redeveloped in 2000 for the America's Cup. Today it's 30 restaurants and bars lining the marina, with direct views of superyachts. The New Zealand Maritime Museum, set on the waterfront, traces the history from early Polynesian explorers through to the great sailing regattas - Auckland genuinely deserves its nickname 'City of Sails'.

⏱ 90 min
3

Wander through the Ponsonby neighbourhood, ten minutes from the CBD: restored Victorian villas, art galleries, independent cafés and local boutiques. It's Auckland's liveliest quarter for understanding contemporary Kiwi culture.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Head up the Sky Tower for the 360° view - the tower rises to 328 m and remains the tallest structure in the southern hemisphere. The brave can sign up for SkyJump (guided freefall from the top). For everyone else, the glass-walled restaurant on the 52nd floor is more than enough.

⏱ 60 min

In the evening, explore the waterfront from Viaduct to Wynyard Quarter: oyster bars, seafood restaurants and lively terraces facing the marina. It's one of the city's best spots for dinner before an early start tomorrow.

Stop 2

Paihia

Gateway to the birthplace of a nation, where chiefs and Crown forged history

★★★☆☆
📍 Waitangi Treaty Grounds Paihia📍 Russell Bay of Islands📍 Paihia Waterfront📍 Haruru Falls Waitangi
1

Waitangi Treaty Grounds, 2 km from Paihia, is where it all began: on 6 February 1840, 46 Māori chiefs and representatives of the British Crown signed the Treaty of Waitangi here, the founding document of the New Zealand nation. The Treaty House, delivered as a kit from Sydney in 1834, was the residence of British Resident James Busby. Inside Te Whare Rūnanga, the carved whare built for the 1940 centenary, the ornaments represent tribes from across New Zealand. The ceremonial waka Ngātokimatawhaorua measures 35 metres and is launched every Waitangi Day.

⏱ 120 min
2

Take the ferry from Paihia wharf to Russell (15 minutes), New Zealand's unofficial first capital. This is where whalers, traders and missionaries disembarked from the 1830s onwards. Christ Church (1836) is the country's oldest church - its walls still bear bullet marks from the 1845 wars. Pompallier Mission House, built in 1842, was the first Catholic establishment in the entire Asia-Pacific region.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Join the Māori cultural evening at Waitangi Treaty Grounds: pōwhiri (welcome ceremony), haka, poi and a traditional hāngī meal - vegetables and meat slow-cooked in an earth oven. Available October to April, booking essential.

⏱ 150 min

Bay of Islands sunset cruise: the 144 islands scattered across the bay are accessible by catamaran from Paihia. It's one of New Zealand's most prized sailing spots, and the late afternoon light on the forested islands is particularly beautiful.

⏱ 120 min

Stop 3

Kaitaia

Springboard to the sacred northern cape where oceans collide

★★★☆☆
📍 Cape Reinga Lighthouse📍 Ninety Mile Beach Te Paki📍 Te Paki Sand Dunes📍 Spirits Bay Cape Reinga
1

Cape Reinga - Te Rerenga Wairua in Māori, 'the leaping place of spirits' - is the most sacred site in all of New Zealand. According to Māori tradition, the souls of the departed dive from the 800-year-old pōhutukawa clinging to the rocks to return to Hawaiki, the ancestral homeland. A one-kilometre path from the car park leads to the lighthouse and the famous headland where the Tasman Sea and Pacific Ocean collide in a visible swirling current. This isn't the northernmost point - that honour belongs to Surville Cliffs, inaccessible to the right on the horizon.

⏱ 90 min
2

Ninety Mile Beach is actually only 88 km (55 miles) long - its name comes from European horsemen who estimated their horses covered 30 miles per day, forgetting that sand slows the pace. The beach is officially a national highway: 4x4 vehicles can drive on it, and tour buses regularly venture out between tides. Stop at Spirits Bay, a sheltered beach east of the cape, quieter and often deserted.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Sandboarding on the Te Paki dunes: north of Ninety Mile Beach, immense sand dunes rise like desert mountains - a completely unexpected landscape in New Zealand. Local operators offer board hire. Allow about 20 minutes to climb for memorable descents.

⏱ 90 min

The road to Cape Reinga follows the Aupōuri Peninsula, a narrow tongue of land with the Tasman Sea to the west and the Pacific to the east. Make photo stops at Rarawa Beach, reputed to have the whitest sand in all of New Zealand, and at Tapotupotu Bay beach, sheltered and perfect for a swim.

Stop 4

Hahei

Where golden sands meet cathedral arches carved by Pacific waves

★★★☆☆
📍 Cathedral Cove Hahei Coromandel📍 Hot Water Beach Coromandel📍 Hahei Beach Coromandel📍 Cathedral Cove Marine Reserve
1

Cathedral Cove - Mautohe - owes its English name to James Cook who, sailing through Mercury Bay in November 1769, thought the natural arch resembled a cathedral roof. Access is on foot (45 min from Grange Road, fully sealed marked track) or by water taxi from Hahei Beach. The giant arch connects two separate coves, and Te Hoho Rock, the stone pinnacle in the middle of the bay, is visible from inside. The 9 km² marine reserve surrounding the site is the country's sixth. Warning: there is a real rockfall risk under the arch - don't linger beneath it.

⏱ 120 min
2

Hot Water Beach is a geological curiosity: buried ancient lava flow heats underground water that rises through the sand at temperatures reaching 64°C. The active zone is accessible only two hours either side of low tide. You dig your own natural spa with a spade (hire available at nearby cafés), then cool off in the ocean waves. The beach receives over 150,000 visitors per year - arrive early.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Sea kayaking from Hahei Beach to Cathedral Cove: this is the most magical way to approach the cove, gliding under the arch at low tide. Local operators offer 3-hour guided trips including snorkelling stops in the marine reserve.

⏱ 180 min

In the evening, stroll along Hahei Beach itself, a long golden sand beach with views to the Mercury Islands. The Ngāti Hei people inhabited these shores from 1350 AD and numerous pā (forts) are still visible on the headlands.

Rotorua

Earth breathes fire beneath a lake, while culture pulses through steam

★★★★☆
📍 Wai-O-Tapu Thermal Wonderland Rotorua📍 Lady Knox Geyser Rotorua📍 Champagne Pool Wai-O-Tapu📍 Whakarewarewa Forest Redwoods Rotorua
1

Wai-O-Tapu - 'sacred waters' - lies 27 km south of Rotorua and covers 18 km² of active geothermal zone in the Reporoa caldera. Lady Knox Geyser erupts every morning at precisely 10:15 am - arrive by 9:45 am for a good spot. After the geyser, three walking circuits (45 min to 90 min) lead to Champagne Pool (57°C, ringed with orange sulphur deposits), the Artist's Palette, bubbling mud pools and Devil's Bath, a toxic chartreuse-green pool from sulphur and ferrous salt deposits. Allow at least 2 hours minimum.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Redwoods Whakarewarewa Forest, on the edge of Rotorua, is a California redwood forest planted from 1901 as forestry trials. The trees have reached impressive heights - some exceed 60 m - and the understory is carpeted with silver ferns. Cycling and walking tracks traverse the entire forest. The Redwood Treewalk (suspended walkway between the canopy) is a separate paid activity.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Māori cultural evening at Tamaki village: warriors collect visitors by bus, divide them into 'tribes' and welcome them with a full pōwhiri including the wero (challenge) ceremony. You learn poi handling, haka, traditional games, before a hāngī served in the hall. Tamaki is renowned for its passionate guides and authenticity.

⏱ 180 min

Relax in the Polynesian Spa pools on the shore of Lake Rotorua: 26 pools at different temperatures fed by natural geothermal springs. The waters have distinct mineral properties depending on the source - some high in sulphur, others alkaline.

Tolaga Bay

A pier stretching into forever on a coast time forgot

★★☆☆☆
📍 Tolaga Bay Wharf New Zealand📍 Opotiki East Cape📍 Cook's Cove Walkway Tolaga Bay📍 SH35 East Cape Road
1

The East Cape coast begins at Ōpōtiki, where SH35 splits from the main highway to follow the Bay of Plenty shore for 330 km of winding road. This is where the black sand beaches appear - the dark sand comes from eroded volcanic rock from the hinterland. Slow down at every viewpoint: the coast alternates between cliffs, river mouths and isolated bays where nobody stops.

⏱ 60 min
2

Tolaga Bay Wharf is New Zealand's longest pier at 660 metres. Built in 1929 to allow steamships to load wool and meat without running aground on the shallows, it was the economic artery of the entire coast. Walk to the end: the waters are crystal clear, you can see fish swimming between the piles, and the view of the white cliffs is striking. Some locals jump from the end at high tide.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hike to Cook's Cove: from the car park at Tolaga Bay entrance, this 2.5 km one-way trail leads to a secluded cove with historical panels recounting James Cook's 1769 anchorage. The captain needed fresh water and timber after five weeks at sea - he stayed six days. Spectacular cliff views from the headland.

⏱ 120 min

Sunset over the bay from the wharf or Tolaga Bay beach: the black sand beaches ignite at sunset, and the sound of Pacific waves echoes in a silence that contrasts completely with the rest of the circuit. This is the Kiwi Far East - one of the remotest and quietest corners of the country.

Gisborne

First light touches earth here, before anywhere else on the planet

★★★☆☆
📍 East Cape Lighthouse Te Araroa📍 Raukokore Anglican Church East Cape📍 Te Araroa Pohutukawa Tree📍 Gisborne Tairawhiti Museum
1

Christ Church Raukokore is an isolated Anglican church built in 1894 on a small rocky headland battered by the waves of Papatea Bay. It's so small and exposed it seems to rise from the sea. The New Zealand Automobile Association ranked it among the region's 101 must-see sites - penguins sometimes nest under the floorboards. It's one of the most iconic images of the East Cape road.

⏱ 30 min
2

At Te Araroa, on the roadside, stands New Zealand's oldest pōhutukawa - 600 years old, with tentacular branches covering over 20 metres in diameter. The pōhutukawa is New Zealand's Christmas tree: it blazes with crimson red flowers in December. From Te Araroa, a 22 km track leads to East Cape lighthouse, relocated here in 1922 from the small offshore island (the original island was too unstable). You must climb 700 steps from the car park to reach the lighthouse - the Pacific view from the top is worth every step.

⏱ 150 min
3

Gisborne is the first city in the world to greet the sun each morning. The Tairawhiti Museum, by the river, displays Māori and colonial collections including the helm from the Star of Canada, wrecked here in 1912. The Hawke's Bay vineyards begin on the city outskirts - Gisborne produces notably reputed chardonnay.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Swimming and surfing on Gisborne's beaches: Wainui Beach, 5 km north of town, is one of the North Island's most renowned surf beaches. Locals gather there in late afternoon. The Pacific view from the dunes at sunset is particularly beautiful.

Stop 8

Napier

Phoenix risen in Art Deco glory from the ashes of catastrophe

★★★☆☆
📍 Napier Art Deco precinct Emerson Street📍 National Tobacco Company Building Napier📍 Marine Parade Napier📍 Pania of the Reef statue Napier
1

On 3 February 1931 at 10:47 am, a magnitude 7.8 earthquake levelled central Napier in two and a half minutes, killing 256 people. The fires that followed finished destroying the commercial districts. The reconstruction, conducted during the depths of the Great Depression in less than two years, naturally adopted the Art Deco style then in vogue, especially since its reinforced concrete buildings better withstood earthquakes. Napier now has the world's greatest concentration of Art Deco buildings - 144 buildings constructed between 1931 and 1937 out of 160 total remain intact. The National Tobacco Company Building (1933), with its arched entrance, dome and stained glass adorned with raupo, is the most photographed structure.

⏱ 120 min
2

Marine Parade runs along the seafront for 2 km, lined with palms and Norfolk Island pines planted on land raised by the earthquake - the tremor literally created 4,000 hectares of new land by lifting the bay floor 2.7 m. The statue of Pania of the Reef, a local legendary figure, has stood at the water's edge since 1954 - she is to Napier what the Little Mermaid is to Copenhagen.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Guided Art Deco walking tour with the Art Deco Trust: guides dressed in 1930s costume take you through shopping streets and interior lanes to decode the facades, chevron patterns and mosaics. The tour lasts about 1h30. If you pass through in February, the Tremains Art Deco Festival transforms Napier into a film set with vintage cars and residents in period costume.

⏱ 90 min

Excursion to Hawke's Bay vineyards: 15 minutes from Napier, the region is New Zealand's second-largest wine area by surface. It specialises in chardonnay, pinot gris and red blends. Several estates offer on-site tastings in the afternoon.

⏱ 120 min

Whangamomona

Where a republic was born from a joke and a goat became president

★★☆☆☆
📍 Whangamomona Hotel Forgotten World Highway📍 Taumarunui Forgotten World Highway📍 Moki Tunnel Forgotten World Highway📍 Tangarakau Gorge Highway 43
1

The Forgotten World Highway (SH43) is New Zealand's oldest touring route - 150 km between Taumarunui and Stratford, built over more than 50 years. The road crosses four passes, traverses the Tangarakau Gorge and plunges into a single-lane tunnel - Moki Tunnel (183 m) - before arriving at Whangamomona. There are no petrol stations along these 150 km. Fill up at Taumarunui. The 12 km of gravel are easily drivable in a normal car.

⏱ 90 min
2

Whangamomona, founded in 1895, declared itself an independent Republic in November 1989 after an administrative redistricting split the village between two regions without consulting residents. What started as a joke in the local hotel bar became a biennial celebration attracting up to 5,000 people. Successive presidents have included a goat, a poodle and a human. Buy your Republic of Whangamomona passport at the Whangamomona Hotel bar to ensure trouble-free transit.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Detour to Mount Damper Falls: midway between Taumarunui and Whangamomona, a 3 km track leads to the North Island's tallest falls (88 m by some sources). The path passes through the former community of Aotuhia, abandoned in the 1940s after total isolation from floods.

⏱ 90 min

Rail cart on Forgotten World Adventures: self-guided electric rail carts allow you to travel the historic railway line between Taumarunui and the remote Tokirima bush. A quirky way to see the landscape.

⏱ 120 min

New Plymouth

Where a perfect volcanic cone commands the Tasman horizon

★★★☆☆
📍 Mount Taranaki New Plymouth📍 Coastal Walkway New Plymouth📍 Govett-Brewster Art Gallery New Plymouth📍 Puke Ariki Museum New Plymouth
1

Mount Taranaki (2,518 m) is one of the world's most symmetrical volcanoes - its silhouette is so perfect it doubled for Fuji in the film The Last Samurai. It stands alone in a dairy plain, with no mountain range, making it visible from great distances. The Pouakai Circuit (2 days) and Around the Mountain Track (4 days) are the major hikes in Egmont National Park. From New Plymouth, Egmont Road leads to the Visitor Centre at 900 m elevation where several shorter trails begin.

⏱ 90 min
2

The New Plymouth Coastal Walkway follows 13 km of coast between black lava rocks and the Tasman Sea. Free, accessible by bike or foot, with views of Mount Taranaki on clear days. The Len Lye Centre, a kinetic art installation along the walkway, is the work of an internationally renowned New Zealand artist.

⏱ 75 min
3

Puke Ariki, New Plymouth's museum, sits on the site of the region's first pā fort. It houses Māori and colonial collections relating to the 19th-century Taranaki Wars, a period when lands were confiscated en masse from local iwi.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hike to Pouakai Tarn from the North Egmont Visitor Centre: this small mountain lake, 2 hours' walk one way from the car park, offers a perfect reflection of Taranaki in calm weather - one of New Zealand's most famous photographs. Leave early in the morning to find still waters.

Wellington

Wind-whipped capital where creativity thrives between harbour and hills

★★★★☆
📍 Te Papa Museum Wellington Waterfront📍 Mount Victoria Lookout Wellington📍 Cuba Street Wellington📍 Wellington Cable Car
1

Te Papa Tongarewa - 'the place of treasures of this land' - is New Zealand's national museum, opened in 1998 on the Wellington waterfront. The 36,000 m² building, set on lead-rubber seismic isolators, houses over two million objects: Māori taonga, natural fauna including the famous colossal squid, art collections and the Gallipoli exhibition created by Weta Workshop with figures 2.4 times life-size. Allow at least 3 hours minimum.

⏱ 180 min
2

The Mount Victoria lookout (196 m) offers the best view over Wellington, its harbour and the entrance to Cook Strait. This is where Peter Jackson filmed several scenes from the Lord of the Rings trilogy. You can drive up or walk in 30 minutes from the centre. Cuba Street below, with its Bucket Fountain and alternative shops, is the capital's liveliest artery.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Ride the cable car from Lambton Quay up to the Botanic Garden: Wellington's botanical gardens (26 hectares) descend in terraces to the city centre with panoramic harbour views. The cable car has operated since 1902. At the top, the Carter Observatory completes the visit.

⏱ 90 min

Dinner on Cuba Street: Wellington's bohemian artery concentrates the capital's best independent restaurants, cocktail bars and cafés. Wellington's culinary scene is renowned throughout the country - proportionally to its size, the city has more cafés per capita than any other city in New Zealand.

Wellington

Wind-whipped capital where creativity thrives between harbour and hills

★★★★☆
📍 Zealandia Ecosanctuary Wellington Karori📍 Old St Paul's Cathedral Wellington📍 New Zealand Parliament Wellington Beehive📍 Wellington Waterfront
1

Zealandia Te Māra a Tāne is the world's first fully fenced, predator-free urban sanctuary. Its 225 hectares, 4 km from the CBD, shelter 40 endemic bird species including kiwi, tuatara (prehistoric reptile unchanged for 200 million years), kākā and takahē - believed extinct until its rediscovery in 1948. Since the sanctuary's opening in 1999, 18 species have been reintroduced. The night tour allows observation of kiwi in their natural element.

⏱ 180 min
2

Old St Paul's Cathedral (1866) is a Gothic cathedral built entirely of local pine timber with kauri reinforcements - a world-unique technique dictated by the absence of available building stone during colonisation. The interior with its coloured stained glass and exposed timber frame has remarkable acoustics. It's one of New Zealand's most precious heritage buildings.

⏱ 45 min
3

The New Zealand Parliament with its cylindrical tower nicknamed 'the Beehive' (1977, designed by architect Basil Spence) is one of the country's most recognisable buildings. Free guided tours run several times daily.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Zealandia night tour with guide: guides equipped with red-light torches lead groups searching for kiwi, tuatara and glow-worms roaming free in the forest. The sanctuary's transformation after nightfall is spectacular - nocturnal birds take over the pathways.

⏱ 120 min

Picton

Where ferries thread through drowned valleys of impossible beauty

★★☆☆☆
📍 Interislander Ferry Wellington Picton📍 Queen Charlotte Sound Picton📍 Picton Harbour Marlborough📍 Queen Charlotte Drive
1

The Interislander ferry (3h Wellington to Picton) is a spectacle in itself: the Cook Strait crossing opens onto the Marlborough Sounds, a labyrinth of 1,500 km of submerged coastline created by rising waters 14,000 years ago at the end of the last ice age. Mountains plunge directly into the water from heights of several hundred metres, and successive sea arms reveal themselves at each turn. Settle on the outer deck.

⏱ 180 min
2

From Picton, head out on the Queen Charlotte Drive, a 35 km scenic road winding between forested ridges and the coves of Queen Charlotte Sound to Havelock. Several lookouts open onto views that photos don't do justice. Havelock is nicknamed 'the green-lipped mussel capital of the world' - a seafood meal is mandatory here.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Cruise in Queen Charlotte Sound from Picton: water taxis and excursion boats offer half-day trips into the most remote arms of the Sound, with dolphin, seal and seabird watching. James Cook anchored at Ship Cove - accessible only by sea - five times between 1770 and 1777; his memorial is still visible there.

Nelson

Sunshine capital where artisans craft and hops flourish in golden light

★★★☆☆
📍 Nelson City Centre New Zealand📍 Tahunanui Beach Nelson📍 Centre of New Zealand Monument Nelson📍 Nelson Market
1

Nelson is New Zealand's sunniest city - Blenheim sometimes competes, but Nelson usually wins over the year. The city centre with its art galleries, Saturday morning market (one of the country's liveliest) and independent cafés is particularly pleasant to explore. Christ Church Cathedral dominates the central hill.

⏱ 90 min
2

Climb to the Centre of New Zealand Monument, the official geodetic point from which all topographic maps of the country were drawn. The climb from Queens Gardens takes 20 minutes on foot through bush. The view over Tasman Bay and surrounding ranges is remarkable.

⏱ 60 min
3

Tahunanui Beach is Nelson's main beach, with 3 km of golden sand on Tasman Bay. Its waters are among the warmest on the South Island in summer. The sunset over the forested hills of the hinterland reflects in the shallow water of the bay.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Visit artisan workshops in Nelson: the city is known for its strong artisan community - potters, glassblowers, jewellers and painters have established their studios here. Nelson Arts Gallery and Suter Art Gallery showcase the best of local creation. Bridge Street concentrates many of these studios.

⏱ 90 min

Craft beer tastings: the Nelson region is New Zealand's hop capital - much of the hop used throughout the country is grown here. Several craft breweries offer tastings from the city outskirts.

Stop 15

Marahau

Portal to golden coves and turquoise waters edged by native forest

★★☆☆☆
📍 Abel Tasman National Park Kayak📍 Anchorage Bay Abel Tasman📍 Marahau Abel Tasman📍 Kaiteriteri Beach Abel Tasman
1

Abel Tasman National Park is New Zealand's smallest national park (225 km²) and the sunniest. Its golden sand beaches ringed by native forest and turquoise waters are accessible on foot (Coastal Track, one of the country's 10 Great Walks) or from the sea. The park takes its name from Dutch explorer Abel Janszoon Tasman who sailed along the coast in 1642 - he didn't land as his men were killed during first contact with Māori.

⏱ 60 min
2

Anchorage Bay is considered one of Abel Tasman's most beautiful beaches: a sheltered bay of white sand surrounded by dense forest, accessible only on foot (2-3h from Marahau) or by water taxi. Observation Beach cove, accessible at low tide from Anchorage, is even more secluded.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Sea kayaking in Abel Tasman: this is the park's signature activity. Operators offer day trips or multi-day trips with lodge accommodation. The kayak + water taxi formula allows paddling one way and returning by boat. Fur seals and oyster catchers are common in the coves.

⏱ 240 min

Section of the Coastal Track between Marahau and Anchorage: this roughly 13 km segment follows the coast with constant sea views, crosses estuaries and reaches several almost-deserted intermediate bays. Water taxis pick up walkers at the agreed time.

Takaka

Bohemian outpost guarding the clearest springs in the southern hemisphere

★★★★☆
📍 Farewell Spit Collingwood Golden Bay📍 Wharariki Beach Cape Farewell📍 Te Waikoropupu Springs Takaka📍 Pupu Springs Golden Bay
1

Pupu Springs - Te Waikoropupu - are New Zealand's largest freshwater springs and the southern hemisphere's largest cold-water springs. Visibility measurements by NIWA in 1993 recorded 63 m of transparency, approaching optically pure water - only the Weddell Sea in Antarctica does better. For local Māori these waters are wahi tapu (sacred) - swimming, drinking and smoking are strictly forbidden within the precinct. A short loop walk (1 km) follows the river to Dancing Sands spring, which appears to 'dance' from upwellings from the bottom.

⏱ 60 min
2

Farewell Spit is a 35 km sand bar extending into the Tasman Sea from Cape Farewell, the northernmost point of the South Island. It's New Zealand's most strictly protected nature reserve - only the first 4 km are accessible to the public, the rest only via authorised guided tours. The spit hosts several thousand gannets each spring in the country's only sea-level colony, over 90 bird species, and regular sightings of pilot whale strandings.

⏱ 90 min
3

Wharariki Beach, west of Cape Farewell, is perhaps New Zealand's most spectacular beach - not for swimming (dangerous currents) but for its wind-sculpted rock arches and giant dunes crossed on foot from the car park (20 minutes). Fur seals lounge on the rocks at the beach entrance.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Guided 4x4 tour of Farewell Spit: authorised operators depart daily from Collingwood to cross the dunes, reach the 1869 lighthouse and observe birds at the spit's feeding stations. It's the only legal way to explore the closed portion of the reserve.

⏱ 180 min

Karamea

Where limestone arches tower above rivers older than memory

★☆☆☆☆
📍 Oparara Arch Karamea📍 Moria Gate Arch Oparara Basin📍 Honeycomb Hill Caves Karamea📍 Kahurangi National Park Karamea
1

The Ōpārara Basin, 20 km north of Karamea in Kahurangi National Park, is one of New Zealand's least-known curiosities. The Ōpārara Arch, the southern hemisphere's largest natural limestone arch, measures 219 m long, 79 m wide and 43 m high - the river carved it through 35-million-year-old limestone over more than a million years. Inside, you walk on fine sand banks while the tea-coloured river (stained by humic acids from the forest) flows silently below. The trail from the car park takes 25 minutes.

⏱ 90 min
2

Moria Gate Arch, 20 minutes' walk from the same car park, is smaller (19 m high, 43 m wide) but its ceiling is completely carpeted with stalactites and hanging roots - the name comes straight from Lord of the Rings (as do several neighbouring streams: Galadriel Creek, Nimrodel). The adjacent Honeycomb Hill caves, accessible only on guided tours, have yielded some of the world's best-preserved moa bones - some dating back 20,000 years.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Whitebait (galaxiidae) tasting: Karamea is one of New Zealand's last regions where whitebait fishing is still abundant (season September to October). These tiny transparent fish are a national delicacy - whitebait fritters (light egg patties) are available at local cafés like the Karamea Village Hotel.

⏱ 60 min

Hike in the Ōpārara valley: beyond the arches, a 4.5 km one-way trail follows the river through primeval rimu and kahikatea forest - the same trees that populated forests in the dinosaur era. Blue ducks (whio), one of the country's rarest species, breed in the rapids.

⏱ 120 min

Stop 18

Punakaiki

Where the sea stacks stones like pancakes and erupts through blowholes

★★☆☆☆
📍 Pancake Rocks Blowholes Punakaiki📍 Paparoa National Park📍 Truman Track Punakaiki📍 Dolomite Point Punakaiki
1

Punakaiki's Pancake Rocks are New Zealand's most photographed geological formation. These 30-million-year-old limestone pillars - built layer by layer from marine shell debris - were uplifted by tectonics and carved into distinct slices by alternating hard and soft layers. At high tide with a southwesterly swell, the sea surges into underground galleries and shoots through vertical vents in columns of water and spray. The boardwalk circuit is 1.1 km - allow 45 minutes.

⏱ 60 min
2

Truman Track, 3 km north of the Pancake Rocks, is a 30-minute trail through nīkau palm forest (New Zealand's only native palms) emerging onto a spectacular pebble beach with cliffs, caves and marine arches. Less crowded than Pancake Rocks, it's well worth the detour.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hike through Pororari Gorge: the Pororari River Track climbs 5 km through limestone gorges overrun with tropical vegetation and tree ferns. The deep green river contrasts with the light rock - one of the West Coast's most remarkable landscapes.

⏱ 180 min

Seal and Hector's dolphin watching from Punakaiki cliffs: Punakaiki Marine Reserve (created 2014, 3,520 ha) protects this exposed coast. Hector's dolphins - the world's smallest and rarest - frequent the area, and fur seals regularly rest on rocks at the cliff base.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 19

Franz Josef

Where glaciers plunge into rainforest at impossible altitudes

★★☆☆☆
📍 Franz Josef Glacier terminal face walk📍 Lake Matheson Fox Glacier📍 Franz Josef village West Coast📍 Callery Gorge Walk Franz Josef
1

Franz Josef Glacier (Kā Roimata o Hine Hukatere) descends from the Southern Alps to below 300 m elevation - one of the few glaciers worldwide to plunge to this level into temperate rainforest. The valley trail (5.4 km return) follows the glacial river bed to the terminal face with its blue ice towers. The final section is on unmarked terrain since glacier modifications - stop at safety barriers. Helicopter flights with glacier landing are the most impressive way to explore it.

⏱ 150 min
2

Lake Matheson, 5 km from Fox Glacier village, is a kettle lake formed 14,000 years ago by Fox Glacier's retreat. The water is stained dark brown by tannins from the surrounding forest - this perfect mirror reflects Aoraki/Mount Cook (3,724 m, New Zealand's highest peak) and Mount Tasman (3,497 m) in optimal conditions. The complete circuit (1h30) passes Reflection Island, the best viewpoint. Sunrise and sunset are prime times.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Helicopter flight with glacier landing: several operators offer 20 to 45-minute flights over both glaciers with névé landing - the aerial view over the Southern Alps and both ice caps is one of New Zealand's most spectacular.

⏱ 60 min

Soak in Franz Josef hot pools: geothermal pools heated to 36-38°C surrounded by outdoor forest on the village outskirts allow well-deserved relaxation after the day's walking. Private pool option available.

⏱ 90 min

Stop 20

Wanaka

Quieter sister to Queenstown, where a lone tree captures every photographer's heart

★★★☆☆
📍 Wanaka Lake Central Otago📍 Blue Pools Makarora Haast Pass📍 Thunder Creek Falls Haast Pass📍 Roy's Peak Wanaka
1

Haast Pass (Tioripatea, 562 m) is one of three roads crossing the Southern Alps. Ngāi Tahu used it to reach the West Coast and its pounamu (jade/greenstone) deposits. Geologist Julius von Haast officially explored it in 1863 - he returned to Wanaka 'virtually shoeless' after six weeks' expedition. Along the road, several stops are essential: Blue Pools (glacial turquoise water on suspension bridges, 3 km return), Thunder Creek Falls (96 m drop), and Fantail Falls (23 m) - all accessible in less than 15 minutes' walk from the road.

⏱ 90 min
2

Wanaka sits on the shore of its namesake lake, ringed by mountains at 900 m elevation. It's Queenstown's quieter, more authentic cousin - same access to ski fields (Cardrona, Treble Cone), same mountains, but without the crowds. The 192 km² lake offers direct views of snow-capped peaks. The solitary tree growing at the lake edge is Wanaka's most photographed object.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Roy's Peak hike: from central Wanaka, this 16 km return trail climbs to 1,578 m with panoramic views over the lake and Alps. The ascent takes about 3h. Leave before dawn for sunrise - it's New Zealand's most shared photograph.

⏱ 360 min

Central Otago wine tasting from Wanaka: the region is the world's southernmost wine zone and produces the country's most renowned pinot noir. Several wine estates are accessible within 20-30 minutes from Wanaka towards Cromwell.

⏱ 120 min
Kauri House Apartment9.9

Stop 21

Queenstown

Adventure capital where bungy was born and adrenaline flows eternal

★★★★☆
📍 Queenstown Skyline Gondola Wakatipu📍 Arrowtown Gold Rush Village📍 Queenstown Waterfront Lake Wakatipu📍 Queenstown Gardens
1

Queenstown stretches along the shore of Lake Wakatipu, whose Māori name means 'the place of the goblin absorbed' - according to legend, a giant creature sleeps beneath the waters and its breathing makes the lake rise and fall 6 cm every five minutes (a real phenomenon, actually due to hydrodynamic oscillations). The Skyline Gondola climbs 450 m above town - the gradient is the steepest of any cable-supported line in the southern hemisphere. From the summit, the view over the lake, the Remarkables and Cecil Peak is exceptional.

⏱ 90 min
2

Arrowtown, 20 minutes from Queenstown, is a gold rush village preserved intact. Hundreds of miners flocked here after gold was discovered in the Arrow River in 1862. The Lakes District Museum displays relics from this period, and the Arrow River banks remain scattered with traces of old gold-panning channels. In autumn (April-May), the poplars and plane trees turn the Arrow gorge into an unreal landscape of reds and golds.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Bungy jump from Kawarau Bridge (43 m): this is where the world's first commercial bungy jump took place in 1988. The historic 1880 bridge is now entirely dedicated to the activity with a free spectator platform overlooking. Queenstown also has the Nevis Bungy (134 m, the country's highest) and the Ledge (400 m height on the Skyline).

⏱ 60 min

Jet boat on the Shotover or Kawarau River: flat-bottomed boats powered by jet turbines skim the gorge walls at 85 km/h with 360° turns in a few centimetres of water. Invented in New Zealand by Sir William Hamilton in the 1950s for navigating shallow rivers - Queenstown is its world capital.

⏱ 60 min

Te Anau

Gateway to fjords where glow-worms illuminate cathedral caves

★★★☆☆
📍 Te Anau Glowworm Caves Fiordland📍 Lake Te Anau Fiordland📍 Te Anau Glowworm Caves Tour📍 Fiordland National Park Te Anau
1

Te Anau Glowworm Caves are on the west shore of the lake, inaccessible by road - a catamaran crosses the lake from town centre (25 min). The caves, only 12,000 years old, are still forming: an active underground river constantly carves the limestone corridors. You descend on foot through sculpted passages, pass waterfalls and whirlpools, then board a small boat in complete darkness. The ceiling then illuminates with thousands of Arachnocampa luminosa larvae - bioluminescent midges that spin sticky threads to trap prey. Photography forbidden - flash disturbs the organisms.

⏱ 150 min
2

Te Anau is the main base for exploring Fiordland National Park (12,500 km², UNESCO World Heritage listed). Lake Te Anau is the South Island's largest lake and New Zealand's second-largest. The town itself is quiet and friendly - the right place to stock up and review weather forecasts before Milford.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hike a section of the Kepler Track from Te Anau: the Great Walk leaves the lake shore 5 km past town. The segment to the ridge lookouts (3-5h return) offers aerial views over the lake and Fiordland mountains without committing to the full 4-day circuit.

⏱ 240 min

Te Anau

Gateway to fjords where glow-worms illuminate cathedral caves

★★★☆☆
📍 Milford Sound Cruise Fiordland📍 Mitre Peak Milford Sound📍 Milford Road Homer Tunnel📍 Milford Sound Waterfall
1

The Milford Road from Te Anau (120 km) is itself spectacular: Mirror Lakes, Fiordland's alpine landscapes, and Homer Tunnel (1,270 m, hand-carved between 1935 and 1954 under Homer Saddle at 945 m elevation) before the switchback descent to the fiord. Leave early to avoid the buses.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Milford Sound cruise crosses the fiord for 22 km from the wharf to the Tasman Sea mouth. Mitre Peak (1,692 m) rises almost vertically from the water less than a kilometre from the wharf. The captain approaches Stirling Falls (155 m) and Bowen Falls (162 m) close enough to get drenched in spray. Bottlenose dolphins, fur seals and sometimes Fiordland crested penguins appear regularly. Milford Sound receives 7 m of rain per year - the light after a shower on the cliffs is unforgettable.

⏱ 150 min

Activities

Kayaking in Milford Sound: operators offer 3-hour morning outings at water level, beneath the vertical cliffs. You perceive the wall heights on a human scale, and seals let you approach within a few metres. The morning calm contrasts with the cruise boat bustle mid-day.

Stop 24

Mount Cook

Where New Zealand's highest peak pierces clouds above milky glacial lakes

★★★☆☆
📍 Hooker Valley Track Aoraki Mount Cook📍 Lake Pukaki Mount Cook Road📍 Tasman Glacier Aoraki Mount Cook National Park📍 Aoraki Mount Cook National Park
1

Lake Pukaki, on the road to Mount Cook, is one of the world's most spectacular glacial lakes. Its intense turquoise hue is caused by 'glacial flour' - microscopic rock particles suspended in meltwater from Tasman Glacier. The road follows the east shore for 55 km to Mount Cook village, with Aoraki (3,724 m, New Zealand's highest peak) growing with every kilometre.

⏱ 60 min
2

Hooker Valley Track is the most popular hike in Aoraki/Mount Cook National Park. This 10 km return trail (3-4h, easy) crosses three swing bridges over the Hooker River and terminates at Hooker Lake, where icebergs float before the Hooker Glacier terminal face. It's the closest accessible point to the Mount Cook summit. The Tasman Glacier base, the country's longest (29 km), is visible to the side. In spring, the Mount Cook buttercup - the world's largest - blooms along the trail edges.

⏱ 240 min

Activities

Glacier Explorers on Lake Tasman: motorised zodiacs navigate between icebergs in the glacial lake at the foot of Tasman Glacier. The guide explains how the glacier's terminal face has retreated several kilometres since the early 20th century - the lake didn't exist yet in 1973.

⏱ 120 min

Stargazing from Mount Cook village: at 900 m elevation, far from any light pollution, the Mackenzie sky is rated level 2 on the Bortle scale (one of the world's darkest skies). The Milky Way is visible to the naked eye most clear nights.

⏱ 90 min

Stop 25

Lake Tekapo

Where a stone church frames infinity and the Milky Way casts shadows

★★★★★
📍 Church of the Good Shepherd Lake Tekapo📍 Mount John Observatory Lake Tekapo📍 Aoraki Mackenzie Dark Sky Reserve📍 Lake Tekapo Lupins
1

Church of the Good Shepherd, built in 1935 from local stone to honour Mackenzie Basin pioneers, is one of New Zealand's most reproduced photographs. Its small window behind the altar frames the turquoise lake and mountains directly - by day. But at night this church becomes iconic: it's photographed with the Milky Way backdrop from the neighbouring sheepdog memorial. It's an active church - respect services in progress.

⏱ 45 min
2

Aoraki Mackenzie International Dark Sky Reserve covers 4,367 km² including Aoraki/Mount Cook National Park and the Mackenzie Basin. Designated by the International Dark-Sky Association in 2012, it's the world's largest dark sky reserve and the southern hemisphere's only Gold-tier reserve. Level 2 on the Bortle scale means the Milky Way casts shadows in clear weather.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Guided tour of Mount John Observatory: authorised operators bus visitors (private access closes after 5pm to preserve darkness) to the summit of Mount John (1,029 m) where the University of Canterbury operates New Zealand's only professional research observatory. Guides show southern constellations, visible planets and explain Māori astronomy (tātai aroraki). In cloudy conditions, a VR replacement tour is offered.

⏱ 120 min

Soak in Tekapo Springs hot pools and stargaze lying in the pools: the 'star pool' formula combines guided astronomical observation with outdoor hot bathing at 37°C. It's one of New Zealand's most memorable night experiences.

Stop 26

Kaikoura

Where mountains meet ocean and giants surface from the deep

★★☆☆☆
📍 Whale Watch Kaikoura📍 Kaikoura Peninsula Walkway📍 Point Kean Seal Colony Kaikoura📍 Ohau Point Seal Colony Kaikoura
1

Kaikoura - 'eat crayfish' in Māori - sits at the intersection of the Kaikōura Range and an undersea canyon plunging over 1,000 m just kilometres offshore. It's this convergence of cold and warm currents, combined with upwelling from the depths, that attracts a concentration of marine life unique worldwide. The Point Kean fur seal colony is accessible on foot from the car park - some individuals regularly come to inspect parked cars.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Peninsula Walkway circles the Kaikōura peninsula for 11 km (3h, free). The lookouts overlook the undersea canyon where sperm whales regularly surface. It's one of the South Island's most beautiful coastal walks, with views of the Seaward Kaikōura Ranges (the mountains plunge directly into the sea).

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Whale watching cruise with Whale Watch Kaikoura: giant sperm whales are permanent residents at Kaikōura year-round, feeding on giant squid in the canyon depths. Whale Watch Kaikoura, the only boat company of its kind in New Zealand, posts a 95% success rate and refunds 80% of the fare if no whales are sighted. You may also encounter dusky dolphins, orcas and albatrosses depending on season. Book several days ahead in high season.

⏱ 150 min

Swimming with dusky dolphins: morning trips allow plunging with pods of dusky dolphins (groups of 100 to 1,000 individuals). These dolphins are particularly playful and acrobatic - Kaikōura is one of the world's best places for this experience.

⏱ 150 min

Stop 27

Christchurch

Garden city rebuilding itself with cardboard cathedrals and boundless resilience

★★★★☆
📍 Akaroa Harbour Banks Peninsula Hector dolphins📍 Banks Peninsula New Zealand📍 Christchurch Botanic Gardens📍 Akaroa French Settlement
1

Akaroa, 85 km from Christchurch, is the only town founded by French settlers in New Zealand. In August 1840, 63 French and Germans arrived to colonise Banks Peninsula in France's name - but the British frigate HMS Britomart had preceded them by a few days with the Treaty of Waitangi in hand. The settlers finally established themselves under the British flag. The French memory is everywhere: Rue Jolie, Rue Lavaud, bistros serving bouillabaisse, and even the Tricolour flying from certain windows. Banks Peninsula is the eroded remnant of a volcano extinct 9 million years ago - the harbour is a drowned crater.

⏱ 90 min
2

Akaroa's Hector's dolphins: these dolphins are the world's smallest (up to 1.4 m long) and one of the most endangered species - the South Island subspecies numbers fewer than 15,000 individuals. Akaroa harbour is one of the world's best places to see them. Operators offer nature cruises with over 98% detection rate, and swimming trips with dolphins are also available.

⏱ 120 min
3

Return to Christchurch: arrival late afternoon. If time permits, a pass through the Botanic Gardens (75 ha) and a visit to the Cardboard Cathedral (2013), designed by Shigeru Ban after the 2011 earthquake, show how the city has reinvented itself. Christchurch has the South Island's most convenient international airport for departures.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Scenic drive on Banks Peninsula coastal roads: from Akaroa, several country roads lead onto the ridges with 360° views over the harbours below. The peninsula is scattered with nearly-deserted bays accessible on foot from the road.

⏱ 90 min
