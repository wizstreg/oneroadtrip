Aidipsos

★★★☆☆
1

Edipsos' thermal springs have risen from the depths of the earth for 20,000 years, welling up from 3,000 meters below. More than 80 springs dot the municipality—the largest concentration anywhere in Greece. The waters reach 70 to 84°C at the Damaria source, cooling to 40 to 50°C at the beach springs. The ancients knew these waters well: Plutarch, Aristotle, and Strabo all documented them as the "Springs of Heracles." Legend has it that after each of his Twelve Labors, Heracles himself soaked in these waters to recover. The Roman general Sulla, plagued by a mysterious skin condition, came here and departed healed—a victory that cemented Edipsos' reputation among the empire's elite.

⏱ 60 min
2

Along the central beach, Roman bathing pools carved directly from the rock still feed from natural 38°C springs. You can wade in for free—search the rocky outcrops north of the waterfront for telltale steam rising from the stones. It's an extraordinary sensation: sulfur-tinged air, steam rising, the Aegean Sea just meters away. History hovers here too: Aristotle, whose family hailed from Chalcis, reportedly visited to study the region. Later arrivals included Winston Churchill, Greta Garbo, Maria Callas, and Omar Sharif, all seeking the waters' restorative magic.

⏱ 45 min
3

Modern thermal facilities offer medically supervised treatments: thermal baths, hydrotherapy, mineral mud wraps, and programs for rheumatoid conditions. Recognized therapeutic applications include arthritis, sciatica, and circulatory disorders. If you're not taking a formal cure, you can access outdoor thermal pools on a day-visit basis—though advance booking is wise during peak season, as capacity is limited.

⏱ 90 min
4

Edipsos' waterfront stretches several hundred meters along the North Eubean gulf. Stroll it at late afternoon when the light rakes across the continental mountains across the strait—the panorama is particularly stunning at that golden hour. Tavernas, cafés, and small shops selling local produce line the promenade, where the pace slows to that of a quiet Greek seaside town.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Thermal bathing or a structured cure: both are available. A medical cure lasting several days is recommended for rheumatism, arthritis, or sciatica—it requires a local doctor's prescription. For a shorter visit, stick to the outdoor pools or natural beach basins. Water temperatures of 28–34°C are considered optimal for therapeutic benefit.

⏱ 120 min

Half-day excursion to northern Eubean villages: Istiaia and the small port of Oreoi, 20 km to the east. Istiaia is an animated market town; Oreoi has a picturesque jetty and waterfront tavernas beloved by locals. Plan roughly an hour's drive from Edipsos along the coast.

⏱ 180 min

Taste kolokythokeftedes—herb-stuffed zucchini fritters fried golden in olive oil. It's the emblematic vegetarian mezze of the Greek summer, served everywhere along the waterfront. Pair them with local Savatiano, a crisp dry white produced on the island, which cuts beautifully through the richness of fried vegetables and grilled fish.

⏱ 60 min

In the evening, order galaktoboureko: creamed semolina wrapped in crispy phyllo leaves, drenched in warm lemon syrup. It's the ultimate comfort dessert of this region—more tender than baklava, less aggressively sweet, often served still warm.

⏱ 30 min

Chalkida

★★★☆☆
1

Stop at the historic sliding bridge in the city's heart: you're standing at the narrowest point of the Euripus Strait, where just 38 meters separate island from mainland. Watch the water for a few minutes—the current reverses approximately every six hours, building to speeds of 12 km/h, then achieving eight minutes of eerie stillness before reversing again. This phenomenon, governed by lunar cycles, has mystified scientists since antiquity. Aristotle, whose ancestors hailed from Chalcis, came here to study what seemed to defy the laws of nature. A wooden bridge already spanned this strait in 411 BCE; the current structure dates to 1962.

⏱ 45 min
2

Karababa Castle towers over the city from the opposite hillside. Constructed by the Ottomans in 1686—just years after they conquered Chalcis from the Venetians—it commands a spectacular vantage over the strait, the bridge, and the terracotta rooftops below. The climb from the center takes about 20 minutes on foot. It's the finest spot for photographing the current phenomenon from above.

⏱ 60 min
3

Agia Paraskevi Church in the old town is among Greece's rarest surviving examples of Gothic Dominican architecture. Begun around 1250, it housed one of the earliest two Dominican priories approved in the Greek province. The central vault and carved stone sculptures in the southern chapel rank among the finest examples of Italian Gothic in Greece. Don't miss the central arch above the iconostasis.

⏱ 30 min
4

Chalcis Archaeological Museum holds regional finds: geometric and archaic ceramics, Hellenistic sculpture, and domestic objects from local excavations. The Eubean pottery collection is especially rewarding for understanding Chalcis' pivotal role in Greek colonization. In the 8th and 7th centuries BCE, Chalcidian colonists founded roughly thirty cities on the Chalcidice peninsula and several in Magna Graecia, including Cumae in Italy. Museum labels appear in both Greek and French.

⏱ 60 min
5

Chalcis' old quarter interweaves Ottoman alleyways, Venetian traces, and a historic Jewish district. The Etz Hayyim Synagogue, one of Greece's oldest surviving Jewish places of worship, testifies to a Jewish presence reaching back to antiquity. Wander the narrow streets behind the waterfront to discover its most characteristic facades.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Claim a terrace table facing the old bridge and observe the current shift. Locals do the same—it's free theater and genuinely hypnotic. On "chaotic" days—about 6–7 per lunar month—the current can reverse up to 14 times instead of the usual four. If you hit one of those days, you'll understand why the ancients spoke of Chalcis' "wild waters."

⏱ 45 min

Taste syglino, Eubean smoked pork cured in fat—a highland specialty. Served on local bread with olives, it's the quintessential savory mezze of the region. Pair it with Eubean retsina, that resinous white wine of ancient tradition, here produced locally and superb alongside charcuterie and cheese.

⏱ 45 min

As evening descends, Chalcis' waterfront comes alive. Residents gather at waterfront cafés and tavernas overlooking the strait. Order loukoumades—small fried pastries drizzled with honey and dusted with cinnamon—and watch pleasure boats navigate against the current.

⏱ 60 min

Eretria

★★★★☆
1

Eretria's archaeological site spreads beneath the modern town—you'll circulate among open-air ruins with a free map distributed at the museum. At the site's heart stands the Temple of Apollo Daphnephoros: its earliest phases date to the late 8th century BCE, with the Doric version of 520–490 BCE destroyed by the Persians during the 490 BCE sack. Foundation stones and sculptured pediment fragments remain. Bring water and a hat in summer—the site offers no shade.

⏱ 60 min
2

The House of the Mosaics, dated to the 4th century BCE, stands among Greece's finest preserved Hellenistic dwellings. Its floors showcase pebble mosaics depicting mythological scenes—Nereids astride sea creatures, griffins, panthers. Check before visiting: travelers report it's occasionally closed early despite posted hours.

⏱ 40 min
3

Eretria Archaeological Museum, founded in 1960 and expanded by the Swiss School of Archaeology in Greece (excavating since 1964), displays remarkable finds. Don't miss the sculptured group of Theseus and Antiope, attributed to the Athenian sculptor Antenor (circa 510 BCE), and a black-figure funerary amphora dated 560 BCE depicting Heracles battling Centaurs. Labels appear in Greek and French.

⏱ 60 min
4

Eretria's ancient theater, with earliest phases from the 5th and 4th centuries BCE, is remarkable for an underground tunnel still visible—actors vanished through it for stage effects, a rarity of ancient theatrical machinery. Climb to the acropolis in the north for a commanding overview of the site and the coastline.

⏱ 45 min
5

The Persians destroyed Eretria in 490 BCE—Darius sought vengeance for the city's aid to the Ionian Revolt. The male population was slaughtered; women and children were enslaved and deported to Persia. The city rebuilt swiftly, sent 600 hoplites to the Battle of Plataea in 479 BCE, flourished in the 4th century, then fell to the Romans in 198 BCE and was finally abandoned after 87 BCE. Marshland reclaimed it until 1824, when refugees from Psara resettled the site.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Swimming along Eretria and Amarynthos beaches—a neighboring village to the east celebrated for seafood tavernas and grilled shellfish spreads. Waters here are calm and shallow, ideal for children. If you're motoring about, Amarynthos merits a detour for dinner: locals prefer its fish freshness to Eretria.

⏱ 120 min

Sample fava: a creamy yellow-pea purée dressed with excellent olive oil, capers, and lemon juice. It's among Greece's finest yet most underappreciated mezzes. Pair it with local Savatiano white—a dry, fruity Eubean varietal that harmonizes beautifully with legumes.

⏱ 45 min

Pasteli—sesame bars bound with thick honey—sells in waterfront shops and groceries. It's one of Greece's oldest candies, energy-dense and nourishing, perfect to carry for tomorrow's hiking.

⏱ 20 min

Kymi

★★★☆☆
1

Kymi perches 200 meters above the Aegean Sea, hidden behind forested hills—locals call it "the Balcony of the Aegean." Shipowner mansions from the 19th century, when Kymi commanded 400 sailing vessels and ranked second only to Chalcis in island prominence, lend the center a rare architectural character. Climb to the main square for vistas across the open sea. A statue in the square commemorates the town's most celebrated son.

⏱ 45 min
2

Kymi's folk museum gathers embroidered costumes, maritime documents, traditional tools, and daily-life artifacts chronicling this sailors' town. It also houses archives connected to Dr. George Papanicolaou, born here May 13, 1883. This Greek physician, who emigrated to the United States in 1913, developed the cytological test known as the "Pap smear"—a screening method for early cervical cancer detection that has saved millions of lives worldwide. His birthhouse is visible nearby.

⏱ 50 min
3

Descend the serpentine 5-km road to Paralia Kymis at the clifftop's base. This is where ferries depart for Skyros, Skopelos, and Alonissos. The atmosphere is that of an animated fishing harbor: seaside tavernas line the quay, blue-and-white boats bob at anchor, nets dry in the sun. If timing allows, dine here rather than in the upper town.

⏱ 60 min
4

The Monastery of the Transfiguration (Metamorphosis), founded in the 16th century by monks from Mount Athos, nestles in greenery along the route between Kymi and the sea. It's a peaceful sanctuary with characteristic monastic architecture and small-batch honey production sold on-site. The nuns who inhabit it today welcome visitors outside service hours—dress appropriately.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Kymi's dried figs are among Eubean products most prized: fumigated with sulfur by the local cooperative, they acquire a pale hue and slightly tart edge distinguishing them from standard dried figs. Search for them in central-town groceries—they're the edible souvenir worth bringing home.

⏱ 30 min

Hike to Maniakiatis Falls: the Maniakiatis River originates on Mount Kserovouni. A 50-minute trail leads to a 15-meter waterfall amid lush vegetation. Start early to enjoy the cool morning air.

⏱ 120 min

Savor Kymi's baklava: the local pastry craft is celebrated for its quality, crafted with Eubean walnuts and honey. Pair it with amygdalota—tender almond cakes found in every pastry shop in town.

⏱ 30 min

Karystos

★★★☆☆
1

Bourtzi Castle stands harborside in Karystos, steps from waterfront cafés—a hexagonal Venetian fortress from 1350 built partly with marble salvaged from a nearby Roman mausoleum. It's among southern Eubean's finest-preserved fortifications. In summer, the courtyard hosts concerts and exhibitions. Entry is free.

⏱ 30 min
2

From town, gaze toward the heights 4 km away: Castello Rosso (Kokkino Kastro) crowns a rocky spur. Early Byzantine fortifications date to 1030. The Lombard baron Ravano dalle Carceri rebuilt the castle between 1209 and 1216. It later passed to Franks, Venetians, and Ottomans before 1470. A ten-minute car journey from the center rewards you with vistas spanning the bay and surrounding mountains.

⏱ 50 min
3

Karystos Archaeological Museum, housed in the Yokaleio Cultural Foundation, presents local excavation finds including ceramic sherds recovered in 1959 during Professor Moutsopoulos' excavation of the Drakospito on Mount Ochi. Pieces related to ancient quarries and maritime history appear as well. Small but well-documented.

⏱ 40 min
4

About 20 kilometers northwest of Karystos, above the village of Myloi, lie the ancient "Kolones" quarries: monolithic cipollino columns (marmor Carystium) in green marble, some reaching 12 meters, abandoned in situ two thousand years before shipping. This green marble with characteristic veining was quarried from the 1st century BCE and exported to Rome for imperial forums, temples, and bathhouses. Hadrian's Library in Athens still displays Karystian columns. The site is accessible on foot from Myloi.

⏱ 90 min
5

The Drakospita—Dragon Houses—rank among Eubean's most enigmatic monuments. Twenty-three exist across southern Eubean; the largest and best-preserved crowns Mount Ochi's 1,389-meter summit. These megalithic structures, raised without mortar from slabs weighing up to 10 tons, likely date to the Classical or Proto-Classical period (5th–4th centuries BCE). Their purpose remains baffling: temple to Zeus and Hera? Watchtower? Quarryman's lodge? Astronomical observatory? One doorway slab measures 4 meters long, 2 meters wide, yet weighs 10 tons. Their first scholarly mention came October 21, 1797, when British geologist John Hawkins ascended Mount Ochi and documented what he found.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Mount Ochi hike to reach the summit Dragon House: this is the day's grand excursion. Depart from the parking area above Myloi village (via the forest road ascending from the main highway). Pass the refuge at 1,050 meters and reach the Profitis Ilias chapel. The Dragon House stands meters from the summit. Plan 4–5 hours round-trip. Leave early, carry water, bring a windbreaker—wind can be brisk at the summit even in summer. The trail traverses the Kastanologos chestnut forest, a rare ecological sanctuary.

⏱ 300 min

Visit the Myloi quarries (alternative to the Ochi hike or complementary short excursion): chisel marks in living rock, abandoned columns, and ancient transport systems remain perfectly legible. The open-air, unfenced site commands spectacular vistas over Karystos Bay and the Aegean.

⏱ 120 min

Taste matsata—homemade Karystos pasta served in sauce with braised cock and tomato: this is the town's festive specialty, rustic and generous. Seek it in central tavernas, preferably at dinner. Wash it down with Karistia Gi white, a local blend of Savatiano, Malagouzia, and Assyrtiko.

⏱ 60 min

Before departing toward Rafina or Athens via ferry from Marmari (10 km from Karystos, regular service to Rafina in 1h15), take one last café on the waterfront facing Bourtzi. This ranks among the island's most tranquil waterfronts—fishermen, a handful of pleasure boats, waves lapping stone beaches. The whistled language of nearby Antia village (10 km away) is one of the world's last remaining systems of long-distance vocal communication: a detour there is delightfully offbeat if time permits.
