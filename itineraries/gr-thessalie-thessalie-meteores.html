Thessaly & Meteora — 8 Days

📅 9 days📍 8 stops🚗 196 km⏱ 4h05

This 8-day circuit through Thessaly starts in Volos, a port city at the foot of Mount Pelion, climbs through the peninsula's stone villages to the turquoise beaches of the east coast, then cuts across the plain to the Meteora. The Byzantine monasteries suspended on their 400 m rock pillars, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, rank among the most striking sights on the Greek mainland. The trip ends around Lake Plastiras, an artificial lake ringed by fir forests and hilltop villages untouched by mass tourism.

✨ Highlights
  • Meteora: 6 active Byzantine monasteries perched on 400 m rock pillars, UNESCO-listed since 1988
  • Pelion: 24 traditional stone villages with carved balconies, mythical home of the Centaurs, turquoise beaches accessible on foot
  • Volos: archaeological museum with the painted funerary stelae of Demetrias, unique in Greece
  • Lake Plastiras: 25 km² artificial lake nestled among the Agrafa mountains, villages perched at 850–1,000 m
📆 Best period

April, May, June, September, October

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 4 and 8 days.

📋 Important notes

This itinerary requires a car — Pelion is poorly served by public transport. Expect winding roads through the Pelion massif and around Lake Plastiras. Strict dress code at the Meteora monasteries: shoulders and knees must be covered.

Day 1

Volos

A Thessalian port at the foot of Mount Pelion, where the Argonauts are said to have set sail.

★★★★★
1

Volos stretches between the sea and the first slopes of Mount Pelion — a setting that gives it a character unlike anywhere else in Greece. Start with the Athanasakeion Archaeological Museum, built in 1909 thanks to the fortune of Alexios Athanasakis, a Pelion merchant who made his wealth in Egypt. The collection will surprise you: the painted funerary stelae from ancient Demetrias, their colours still vivid, are an absolute rarity in Greece — the pigments survived because the stelae were buried. You'll also see Neolithic pieces from the sites of Dimini and Sesklo, the two oldest organized settlements in Europe (6800 BC), located less than 15 km from the city.

⏱ 90 min
2

The old Palia quarter, a former Byzantine fortress fortified in the 4th century, grew around the port from the 19th century onwards. The Ottomans held it from 1423 until the liberation of Volos in 1881 — nearly 460 years. Behind the train station, the Volos City Museum occupies the former Papantou tobacco warehouse (1920), restored in 2014: the glass floor on the first level reveals 6th–7th-century water channels beneath your feet. The building alone is worth a visit for its industrial architecture.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

In the evening, Volos reveals its true self through its tsipouradika — tsipouro bars, named after the local grape spirit, where you eat mezze while sipping slowly. It's an institution here: Volos is widely regarded as the Greek capital of tsipouro. The waterfront (Argonauts' promenade) is packed with these places, at their best around aperitif hour. The city owes its mythological name to Iolcos, from where Jason and the Argonauts are said to have set sail in search of the Golden Fleece — a myth that looms over the entire region.

⏱ 90 min

Day 2

Makrinitsa

A stone village perched 650 m above the Pagasetic Gulf, known as the balcony of Thessaly.

★★★★★
1

Portaria, 13 km from Volos, is the first village on western Pelion — its nickname "lady of welcome" is well earned: centuries-old plane trees, fountains, stone houses with carved wooden balconies. During Ottoman rule (15th–19th c.), Pelion was a refuge for Greek intellectuals fleeing Constantinople and for merchants who traded freely with Europe. That era left behind exceptional architecture, libraries, and secret schools in every village.

⏱ 45 min
2

Makrinitsa — "the balcony of Pelion" — lives up to its reputation. At 650 m altitude, its cobbled lanes open onto a central square with views stretching across Volos, the Pagasetic Gulf and, on clear days, the Sporades islands. The 18th-century houses in local stone, with their carved wooden overhangs, are among the best preserved in Greece. On the square, the church of Agios Ioannis Prodromos (Saint John the Baptist) holds frescoes by the folk painter Theophilos, who lived and worked in the Pelion villages in the early 20th century. The four-lion-head fountain of 1809, known as the "Spring of Immortal Water", still flows. Allow at least 2 hours to wander.

⏱ 120 min
3

The Tapalis House Museum, built in 1844 and now the Folk Museum, is a window into Pelion's bourgeois past: furniture, jewellery, costumes, weapons, and mountain utensils. While you're there, visit the church of Agia Magdalini (1737) with its icons and wall paintings — the interior is unexpectedly rich for such a small village.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The shops in Makrinitsa sell local Pelion produce — mountain herbs, honey, fruit preserves (apple, chestnut), liqueurs, handmade pasta. Try the spetsiotiko (spiced Pelion sausage) and mountain tea (tsai tou vounou) in a taverna overlooking the bay. It's a distinctly Thessalian food experience — a world away from the tourist spots on the islands.

⏱ 90 min
Day 3

Makrinitsa

A stone village perched 650 m above the Pagasetic Gulf, known as the balcony of Thessaly.

★★★★★
1

Tsagarada, on Pelion's east side facing the Aegean, is one of the most beautiful villages on the peninsula. Its central square is dominated by a plane tree that locals say is over 1,000 years old — its circumference exceeds 14 m and its shade covers half the square. It was under this tree that villagers gathered for centuries to hold the agora, settle village affairs, and debate politics. Have your morning coffee here, facing the Aegean Sea glimpsed between the trees — it's one of the finest terraces in Greece.

⏱ 60 min
2

Damouchari, below Tsagarada, is one of those tiny Pelion harbours that look like a postcard of the ideal Mediterranean: a bay split in two by a headland topped with the ruins of a medieval castle, fish tavernas right on the jetty, water so clear you can see the bottom at 5 metres. Several scenes from the film "Mamma Mia" were shot here — the producers didn't have to look far.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Mylopotamos beach, reached from Tsagarada via 7 km of side road followed by a 15–20 minute walk along a forest trail, is one of the most beautiful on the Greek mainland: white sand, rocks splitting the beach into two coves, turquoise water, and pine trees reaching down to the shore. The walk through Pelion's dense forest is as good as the beach itself. Plan on spending the afternoon — the descent is easy, the climb back takes a good 30 minutes of effort.

⏱ 180 min

The inter-village trails of Pelion (monopatia) are paved with grey schist slabs and date largely from the Ottoman period — they connected villages when there were no roads. The Damouchari–Tsagarada section (roughly 45 minutes one way) is one of the finest: dense forest, waterfalls, and sea views. The trail is waymarked and manageable for everyone.

⏱ 90 min

Day 4

Kalambaka

A town dwarfed by the rock pillars of Meteora, gateway to the UNESCO Byzantine monasteries.

★★★★★
1

Arriving in Kalambaka from the Thessalian plain, the Meteora pillars appear without warning — conglomerate columns rising 400 m above the town, as if the ground had been punched upward from below. These geological formations took shape during the Tertiary period: a river flowing into the ancient Thessalian sea deposited pebbles cemented by sand over millions of years, creating these "pudding stones" later sculpted by erosion. The first hermits took refuge on them in the 10th century. The Great Meteoron, the first monastery built in 1382 by the monk Athanasios, is also the largest: it has never been empty since its founding. Before going in, look at the pulley systems still in place — until the early 20th century, monks were hauled up in nets by hand. Allow 30 minutes to climb the 200 steps carved into the rock.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Monastery of Varlaam, founded in 1517, is often the favourite of returning visitors: more intimate than the Great Meteoron, it offers spectacular valley views from its terraces and houses an impressive capstan system with a still-visible storage net. Note the 16th-century frescoes in the main church — the martyrdom scenes display a level of anatomical precision that's unsettling for the era.

⏱ 75 min
3

Roussanou is the female monastery of Meteora, perched on a 60 m pillar and connected to the cliff by a walkway. Founded in the 16th century, it was restored and reoccupied by nuns in 1988. The interior is strikingly beautiful: 16th-century frescoes in the main church, a flower garden suspended at the edge of the void. If you only visit three, make it the Great Meteoron, Varlaam, and Roussanou.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Stay in Kastraki rather than Kalambaka for the night — this tiny village sits 2 km from the monasteries, right at the foot of the pillars. Sunset from the viewpoints above Kastraki paints the rocks orange and pink — it's one of the most photographed sights in Greece, and it's free. Stay until nightfall if you can.

⏱ 90 min
Day 5

Kalambaka

A town dwarfed by the rock pillars of Meteora, gateway to the UNESCO Byzantine monasteries.

★★★★★
1

The Monastery of the Holy Trinity (Agias Triados) is the most dramatically positioned of all: it rises on a completely isolated pillar, reachable only by 140 steps carved through a tunnel in the rock. This is where the final scene of "For Your Eyes Only" (James Bond, 1981) was filmed — the producers needed a location impossible to access from below, and they found it. The view from the terrace spans the entire Pinios valley.

⏱ 60 min
2

Agios Stefanos is the most accessible monastery — no steps, just a walkway. It's also the only female monastery with an entirely different architectural style, featuring a 14th-century early-Christian basilica. For this reason, it often welcomes visitors with limited mobility. Its museum holds a remarkably well-preserved collection of liturgical embroidery and vestments from the 14th to the 19th century.

⏱ 60 min
3

Agios Nikolaos Anapafsas is the least known monastery and the furthest from the main road — which means it draws far fewer visitors. Founded in the 15th century, it holds exceptional frescoes by Theophanis the Cretan (1527), one of the great masters of late Byzantine painting. If you can only visit one monastery for its frescoes, come here.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

A day on foot is the other way to experience Meteora. The Kalambaka–Great Meteoron–Varlaam circuit takes about 3 hours of walking and passes through forest at the base of the cliffs, with constantly shifting views of the pillars. The trails (monopatia) are waymarked from the village of Kastraki — far less busy than the paved roads between the monasteries. Bring plenty of water in summer.

⏱ 180 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 20 km · 25min
Day 6

Trikala

A Thessalian riverside town, mythical birthplace of Asclepius the god of medicine.

★★★★★
1

Trikala is little known to foreign travellers — which makes it one of the best "real" towns in Thessaly. The Byzantine fortress on the hill above the city has ancient origins: this is the site of the Sanctuary of Asclepius, the Greek god of medicine whose mythical birthplace was Trikala. The current fortress dates from the 4th century, with later Byzantine and Ottoman modifications. The Ottoman clock tower, still standing at the eastern corner, has become the symbol of the city.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Varousi quarter, at the foot of the fortress, is the old Ottoman neighbourhood with its narrow lanes, overhanging houses, and old churches. The Asclepieion of Trikala — the remains of the ancient healing sanctuary dedicated to Asclepius — is visible in the park below the fortress. The Koursoum Mosque (16th c.), one of the best preserved in Greece, stands in the lower town — it's no longer active but its Ottoman architecture is remarkable.

⏱ 75 min
3

On the banks of the Litheos river, the Matsopoulos Mill (19th c.) is a singular piece of industrial heritage: it was the first flour and pasta factory in Greece, built in imposing stone and still standing. The pedestrian bridge crossing the Litheos opposite the mill is where locals gather in the evening — with views over the water and the lights of the old town.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Trikala is known for its spiced sausage (loukaniko Trikalinon) seasoned with herbs from nearby Mount Koziakas, its mountain feta cheese, and its tsipouro. The tavernas in the centre are affordable and packed with locals — a welcome break after the tourist prices of Kalambaka. The Tuesday morning market in the lower town draws producers from across the region.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 45 km · 55min
Day 7

Karditsa

A Thessalian market town, launching point for Lake Plastiras and the villages of Agrafa.

★★★★☆
1

Lake Plastiras — a 25 km² artificial lake created in the 1950s by damming the Tavropos river — has every appearance of a natural Alpine lake: clear blue water, fir and oak forests along the banks, the Agrafa mountains as a backdrop. The 70 km loop road around the lake connects villages perched between 850 and 1,000 m altitude. Neochori, the largest (500 inhabitants), offers a spectacular lake view from its belvedere — and a few decent tavernas.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Monastery of Panagia Pelekiti is one of the strangest attractions in the area: it's literally carved into the vertical cliff face overlooking the lake. Founded in the 19th century, its vertiginous position is comparable to the Meteora monasteries — only much smaller and far less visited. The access path winds along the rocks above the lake.

⏱ 60 min
3

Morfovouni, a hillside village built in an amphitheatre layout on the northern shore, houses a small museum dedicated to General Nikolaos Plastiras — the man who ordered the construction of the dam in the 1950s and after whom the lake is named. Plastiras was born in this village in 1883 and wanted to protect his home region from seasonal flooding. About 12 km away stands the Monastery of Agia Triada, a rare example of domed Byzantine architecture outside the monastic republic of Mount Athos.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The lake is ideal for kayaking and canoeing — calm waters and wooded banks. Local operators rent out boats from the lakeside villages, particularly Kalyvia Pezoula. The atmosphere is a world away from the coastal beaches: quiet, uncrowded, with nothing but wind in the fir trees for company. If you're here in spring, the seasonal waterfalls in the Megalo Rema gorge are worth a detour.

⏱ 120 min
Day 8

Karditsa

A Thessalian market town, launching point for Lake Plastiras and the villages of Agrafa.

★★★★☆
1

Messenikolas, nicknamed "the balcony of Thessaly", is built in an amphitheatre on a promontory with views in two directions: on one side Lake Plastiras, on the other the Thessalian plain. With fewer than 500 inhabitants, the village has kept its traditional stone houses and an end-of-the-world atmosphere. It's also the starting point for the Megalo Rema gorge, a moderate-level hike through a narrow ravine.

⏱ 60 min
2

Fylakti, a viewpoint village on the southern shore, offers one of the finest panoramas on the circuit. From its square, the winding dam road is visible below — a 1950s structure that's still impressive, passable by car in a single lane. The Tavropos Dam is the engineering feat that created this entire landscape: 82 m high, 275 m long.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Drive the full 70 km loop around the lake at your own pace, stopping at viewpoints and villages that catch your eye. Morning is the best time — the low-angle light on the lake is perfect and the roads are empty. Finish in Karditsa for lunch: the town is a lively agricultural market, and its tavernas serve game from the Agrafa mountains — wild boar, roe deer — depending on the season. From Karditsa, the road to Volos (1h30) and Larissa (45 min) offers a convenient return to the airports.

⏱ 240 min
