Sofia

A capital where a Roman rotunda, a golden Orthodox cathedral and an Ottoman mosque stand within a stone's throw of each other

★★★★★
1

Alexander Nevsky Cathedral has dominated Sofia's skyline since 1912. Its gilded domes cover 3,170 square metres, the central nave soars to 45 metres, and the interior walls are clad in marble from Italy, Brazil and Morocco. The crypt houses one of the largest collections of Orthodox icons in the Balkans, spanning the 15th to the 20th century.

⏱ 60 min
2

Tucked behind the Presidency building, the St. George Rotunda was built in the 4th century under the Roman Empire — making it Sofia's oldest standing structure. Three overlapping layers of frescoes bear witness to its uninterrupted use across the centuries.

⏱ 25 min
3

Built in 1576, the Banya Bashi Mosque is the only active mosque in Sofia, attributed to the legendary Ottoman architect Mimar Sinan. Outside its walls, fountains continuously pour the city's natural mineral water at 37°C — locals stop by every morning to fill their bottles.

⏱ 20 min
4

The National Archaeological Museum occupies a 15th-century Ottoman konak. Six millennia of Bulgarian history are on display: Neolithic tools, Thracian ceramics, Roman statues, and the star exhibit — the Panagyurishte Treasure, a solid gold ceremonial set from the 4th century BC.

⏱ 60 min
5

Beneath Nezavisimost Square, the ancient Serdica Complex reveals the cobbled lanes of the former Roman capital: the decumanus maximus, house foundations, basilicas and kilns from the 2nd to 4th centuries. Visitors can walk freely among the ruins.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Join a free walking tour of the historic centre to connect the dots in a single morning — from the Roman Largo to the cathedral, taking in the famous 'square of tolerance' where a mosque, a synagogue and a Catholic cathedral stand within a hundred metres of each other.

⏱ 180 min

Every weekend, an antiques and icon market sets up around Alexander Nevsky Square. Folk embroideries, old coins, silver jewellery and Soviet-era paintings all find a home here — and prices are negotiable.

⏱ 60 min

Plovdiv

One of Europe's oldest cities, with a Roman theatre still used for concerts and a vivid old town perched across three hills

★★★★☆
1

Plovdiv is one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in Europe. Its old town climbs across three hills: cobbled streets, brightly painted 19th-century bourgeois houses with jutting upper storeys, artists' studios, and house-museums. The city was the European Capital of Culture in 2019.

⏱ 90 min
2

Wedged between two old-town houses, the Roman Theatre of Philippopolis dates to the 1st century AD and once seated 6,000 spectators. The view from the upper tiers over Plovdiv's colourful rooftops is breathtaking — and the theatre still hosts concerts and opera performances in summer.

⏱ 40 min
3

Kapana — the old craftsmen's quarter — has reinvented itself as one of the most vibrant creative hubs in the Balkans. Designer boutiques, galleries, cafés tucked into converted garages, giant murals. On weekends, the entire pedestrian zone fills with outdoor tables and live music.

⏱ 60 min
4

Along the main pedestrian street, the ruins of a 2nd-century Roman stadium (capacity: 30,000) surface between modern buildings. A short walk away, the 15th-century Dzhumaya Mosque raises its nine domes and minaret above the city centre.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Climb to Nebet Tepe hill in the late afternoon. The oldest of Plovdiv's seven hills, it holds the remains of a Thracian fortress founded 6,000 years ago. The panorama over the city and the Thracian Plain is at its most beautiful around sunset.

⏱ 60 min

The Ethnographic Museum occupies the magnificent Argir Kuyumdzhioglu house (1847), the finest example of Bulgarian National Revival architecture in Plovdiv. Six rooms recreate everyday life in the 19th century.

⏱ 50 min

Ardino

A quiet Rhodope town keeping watch over the Devil's Bridge — a 1515 Ottoman arch spanning a wild gorge of the Arda River

★★☆☆☆
1

The Devil's Bridge (Dyavolski Most) spans the Arda River in a narrow gorge 10 km from Ardino. Built between 1515 and 1518 on the foundations of a Roman crossing, it stretches 56 metres long and rises 11.5 metres at its central arch. Three arches, a 12-centimetre stone parapet — and the legend of the mason who supposedly walled up his wife's shadow to make the bridge hold forever.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Arda gorges around Ardino rank among the wildest landscapes in the Rhodopes: limestone cliffs, dense riverine woodland and tight river meanders carved deep into the rock. The international Sultan's Trail (Vienna to Istanbul, 2,400 km) passes by the bridge and follows the Arda toward Kardzhali.

⏱ 60 min
3

Ardino is a small town in the Kardzhali region, predominantly Bulgarian-Turkish, draped across the bends of the Arda. Its central fountain is a miniature replica of the Devil's Bridge.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Park 4 km from the bridge and walk the eco-trail along the Arda to reach it on foot. The ruins of old houses on the left-hand slope are worth pausing at even before you reach the bridge itself.

⏱ 90 min

The Arda is well known for its populations of barbel, chub and brown trout. Local anglers take up their spots on the riverbanks below the bridge from dawn onwards.

⏱ 120 min

Devin

The thermal heart of the Rhodopes, where mineral springs meet the most spectacular underground waterfall in the Balkans

★★☆☆☆
1

The Trigrad Gorge, 26 km from Devin, cuts 6 kilometres through vertical limestone walls rising 100 to 300 metres. The road was blasted directly from the rock. At the far end, the Trigrad River vanishes underground into the Devil's Throat Cave (Dyavolsko Garlo): a 42-metre underground waterfall — the highest in the Balkans — thundering through a chamber 40 metres wide. Guided tours only, 45 minutes, constant interior temperature of 6°C.

⏱ 90 min
2

Yagodinska Cave, 10 km from Trigrad, is the longest and most ornate cave in the Rhodopes: 10 kilometres of passages across five levels, curtain stalactites, giant stalagmites. Neolithic human remains were found inside — the cave was inhabited 7,000 years ago.

⏱ 75 min
3

Devin is the thermal capital of the Rhodopes. Its springs emerge at temperatures ranging from 16 to 76°C. Several establishments welcome day visitors for half-day access to thermal pools and spa treatments.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Bring a jacket into the Devil's Throat Cave — it stays at 6°C inside regardless of the season.

⏱ 60 min

End the day with a soak in the thermal baths. Devin's waters have been prized since antiquity for their exceptionally low mineral content and purity.

⏱ 90 min

From the village of Trigrad, a marked trail climbs up along the mid-cliff face of the gorge with dramatic views down to the river. A round trip of 6 km with moderate elevation gain.

⏱ 180 min

Batak Reservoir

A pine-fringed mountain lake at 1,108 metres, where the silence is broken only by paddles and the occasional cast of a fishing line

★☆☆☆☆
1

Batak Reservoir is a mountain dam lake built between 1954 and 1959 in the Western Rhodopes at 1,108 metres altitude. At 22 square kilometres, it is one of the largest reservoirs in Bulgaria. Its pine-forested shores, sheltered coves and fish-rich waters make it a favourite retreat for Bulgarians seeking mountain tranquillity.

⏱ 60 min
2

The resort of Tsigov Chark on the south-western shore is the main gateway to the lake. Its waterfront establishments, jetties and boat rentals give it the relaxed feel of a Bulgarian mountain holiday village.

⏱ 45 min
3

The town of Batak, 4 km east of the reservoir, deserves a short stop: its old houses, church and museum recall one of the most painful episodes in Bulgarian history — the massacre of 1876.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The full loop around the lake by bicycle covers 35 km on quiet roads. Bike rentals are available by the day in Tsigov Chark.

⏱ 180 min

Head out by rowing boat or kayak early in the morning, when mist still hangs over the conifers. Boat hire and equipment are available from the Tsigov Chark operators.

⏱ 120 min

The reservoir holds pike, carp, wels catfish and trout. Fishing is permitted from the banks and from boats — local anglers know the best spots below the dam.

⏱ 180 min

★★☆☆☆

The Kleptouzka gorge trail starts from Kleptuza park and follows the wooded canyon for 4 km. Along the way, springs burst directly from the limestone walls.

⏱ 120 min

Walk across Velingrad to compare its three historic districts — Kamenitsa, Ladzhene and Chepino — each with its own thermal spring and its own church.

⏱ 90 min

Bansko

Bulgaria's premier ski resort by day, a cobblestoned National Revival town by night, in the shadow of UNESCO-listed Pirin

★★★☆☆
1

Bansko's old town has held its own against the ski resort sprawl surrounding it. Its kuli — thick-walled stone fortress-houses from the 18th century — recall a time when wealthy families built their homes as citadels against raiders. The Holy Trinity Church (1835), sunk partly below street level as Ottoman regulations required, shelters vivid frescoes and a 12-metre carved wooden iconostasis.

⏱ 75 min
2

The birthplace of Neofit Rilski (1793–1881) — the monk who published the first modern Bulgarian grammar in 1835 — is now a museum of the Bulgarian National Revival. Its rooms, restored with period furniture and manuscripts, tell the story of intellectual life in Bansko under Ottoman rule.

⏱ 40 min
3

Sixteen kilometres from Bansko, Vihren Chalet (1,980 m) is the gateway to Pirin National Park, a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Five hundred metres from the chalet, a 1,300-year-old Macedonian pine — the oldest tree in Bulgaria — stands at the forest edge. The jagged limestone summit of Vihren (2,914 m) dominates the skyline above.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hike to the summit of Vihren (2,914 m), Bulgaria's second highest peak. From Vihren Chalet (1,980 m), allow 3 hours up and 2 hours down for 934 metres of ascent. Rocky terrain, views over glacial lakes, and on clear days the eye reaches as far as Greece.

⏱ 300 min

In winter, Bansko is Bulgaria's largest ski resort: 80 km of runs, 13 lifts and 1,150 metres of vertical drop. Day passes are available at the resort.

⏱ 360 min

Come evening, push open the door of a mehana — one of Bansko's traditional taverns — for dinner to the sound of gaida (bagpipe) and gadoulka (folk fiddle). Think slow-roasted lamb, banski starets cheese and grilled breaded kashkaval.

⏱ 120 min

Rila Monastery

The greatest monastery in the Balkans — a spiritual fortress born in the 10th century, nestled deep in Rila's forests and listed by UNESCO

★★★☆☆
1

Rila Monastery is Bulgaria's most visited monument and one of the finest Orthodox monastic complexes in the world. Founded in the 10th century by the hermit John of Rila, rebuilt after a fire between 1833 and 1862, and inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1983. Its inner courtyard stretches 3,200 square metres, ringed by four tiers of polychrome arched galleries.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Church of the Nativity of the Virgin (1837) is entirely covered in frescoes by the painter Zahari Zograf, depicting over 1,200 biblical scenes. The gold-leaf carved wooden iconostasis measures 10 metres across — ten years' work by craftsmen of the Samokov school. The Hrelyo Tower (1335), standing 23 metres, is the only surviving structure from before the great fire.

⏱ 60 min
3

The monastery museum houses the Cross of Raphael (1802): an 81-centimetre crucifix in which every square centimetre is carved with 650 figures and 104 miniature scenes. The monk who created it spent 12 years on the work and lost his sight in the process.

⏱ 40 min
4

Four kilometres from the monastery along a marked forest trail, the cave where the hermit John of Rila lived and died in the 10th century has been an active pilgrimage site for over a thousand years. A narrow tunnel cuts through the rock: tradition holds that those who pass through without getting stuck are absolved of their sins.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

The monastery bakery, just outside the walls, sells its mekitsi (honey-drizzled fried doughnuts), rye bread and sheep's milk yoghurt every morning. Have lunch at the adjacent restaurant looking out over the Rila forests.

⏱ 60 min

From the monastery, a trail follows the Rilska River downstream through the gorge for 7 km to the village of Rila, descending 650 metres through waterfalls and jade-green plunge pools. Arrange a taxi back from the village in advance.

⏱ 180 min

Samokov

A mountain town at the foot of Rila — the ideal base for the Seven Lakes and the slopes of Borovets

★★★★☆
1

Seven glacial lakes stepped between 2,095 and 2,535 metres in a Rila cirque, formed 10,000 years ago. Each bears a name: Dolnoto (The Lower), Ribnoto (The Fish), Trilistnika (The Trefoil), Bliznaka (The Twin, 480 m long), Babreka (The Kidney, 37 m deep), Okoto (The Eye) and Salzata (The Teardrop, 2,535 m). Their blue-green waters shift colour with the light.

⏱ 240 min
2

The Panichishte chairlift rises in 20 minutes from 1,450 to 2,050 metres, dropping you at the foot of the lowest lake. From the upper station, the full loop of the seven lakes covers 7 to 9 km and takes 4 to 6 hours.

⏱ 30 min
3

From Lake Okoto (2,535 m), the view sweeps across the entire Rila massif. On a clear day, Mount Musala (2,925 m, the highest point in the Balkans) is visible to the north-east.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Ride the chairlift from Panichishte and complete the seven-lakes loop in an anti-clockwise direction to catch the best morning light at each lake.

⏱ 360 min

Experienced hikers can push on to the Damga summit (2,669 m), an hour beyond Lake Okoto, for a 360° panorama over the Rila — but only venture there in clear skies, as afternoon storms can build rapidly.

⏱ 120 min

The Rilski Ezera mountain hut, midway around the circuit, serves hot soup, tea and biscuits — an essential rest stop for weary legs at 2,300 metres.

⏱ 30 min
