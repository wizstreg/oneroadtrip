Stop 1

St Louis

Gateway Arch and the Old Courthouse where Dred Scott sued for freedom in 1846.

★★★★☆
📍 Gateway Arch St. Louis📍 Old Courthouse St. Louis📍 Camp Dubois Lewis Clark Hartford Illinois
1

Six hundred and thirty feet of stainless steel curves over the Mississippi, and you feel it before you see it — the Gateway Arch pulls the eye straight up from the moment you step into Gateway Arch National Park. Designed by Eero Saarinen in 1948 and built between 1963 and 1965, it stands as the tallest arch in the world, equal in height and width. Below it, the Museum of Westward Expansion connects the Arch directly to the Lewis and Clark Expedition, which launched from this region in 1804. Book tram tickets to the observation deck well in advance — the tiny capsule ride is part of the experience, and views stretch 30 miles on a clear day.

⏱ 120 min
2

The green dome of the Old Courthouse is impossible to miss from the Arch, and the building earns a stop on its own terms. On April 6, 1846, Dred and Harriet Scott filed for their freedom in this building — one of more than 300 freedom suits filed here before 1860. Their case reached the Supreme Court in 1857, where Chief Justice Roger Taney ruled that enslaved people were not citizens; a decision many scholars believe accelerated the Civil War. The courthouse, completed in its current form in 1864, is open daily 9 to 5 with free admission. The mock trial program run by rangers brings the Scotts' case back to life.

⏱ 75 min
3

Cross the Mississippi to Hartford, Illinois, and you're standing at the spot where the Corps of Discovery spent the winter of December 1803 to May 14, 1804. The Lewis & Clark State Historic Site holds a full-scale replica of the 55-foot keelboat, cut away on one side so you can see exactly how 200-plus bundles, barrels, and boxes were packed for the journey. Six galleries in the 14,000-square-foot interpretive center cover the preparation phase in detail that the Arch museum doesn't reach. Plan 45 minutes minimum — the confluence viewing area outside, where the Missouri meets the Mississippi, is worth lingering over.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Walk the levee along the Mississippi south of the Arch after dark and you get the full scale of Saarinen's curve lit against the sky. During the day, Gateway Arch Riverboats run one-hour narrated cruises on replica paddlewheelers — a gentle way to see St. Louis from the water and understand why this bend in the river made the city. Boats depart from the levee just north of the Arch; check the schedule as departures vary by season.

⏱ 75 min

The City Museum is not a museum — it's a multi-story playground built from salvaged architectural fragments, with caves, tunnels, rooftop Ferris wheel, and a ten-story slide. Built by artist Bob Cassilly in a former shoe factory, it opened in 1997 and has no real equivalent anywhere in the country. Two miles from the Arch; open until midnight on weekends. If you're travelling with kids or simply want to end Day 1 somewhere genuinely strange, allow two hours.

Stop 2

St Charles Mo

Where the Corps of Discovery launched May 21, 1804, after one last party on the levee.

★★★★★
📍 Lewis Clark Boat House Museum St. Charles Missouri📍 First Missouri State Capitol St. Charles📍 Historic Main Street St. Charles Missouri
1

Down at Bishop's Landing on the Missouri River, the Lewis & Clark Boat House and Museum keeps the full-scale operational replicas of the keelboat and both pirogues that carried the Corps out of this town on May 21, 1804. The keelboat was built by volunteers from the Discovery Expedition of St. Charles using period-accurate tools and methods reconstructed from the expedition's journal descriptions — the original construction plans were never preserved. Clark's journal entry that day is brief: the crew had partied until 5 a.m. the night before and could only manage a few miles upriver. Open Monday to Saturday 10 to 5, Sunday noon to 5; paid entry. The 15-foot bronze of Lewis, Clark, and Seaman just outside is the tallest Lewis and Clark monument on the entire trail.

⏱ 75 min
2

Federal-style brick buildings line Historic Main Street, and the one at 200 South Main deserves particular attention: the First Missouri State Capitol, where the new state's legislature met from 1821 to 1826 before Jefferson City was ready. A guided tour of the 11-room building covers the debates over slavery, states' rights, and taxation that shaped early Missouri — all while commerce hummed in the Peck Brothers dry goods store downstairs. Free admission; tours run throughout the day.

⏱ 60 min
3

South Main Street runs directly alongside the Missouri River, and the Katy Trail — 240 miles of converted rail corridor — begins right here. Even if you only walk half a mile north from Frontier Park, you're on the same riverbank the Corps navigated in 1804. The trail is packed dirt and crushed limestone, flat and easy, with cottonwoods overhead and the river in earshot. Bike rentals are available from local outfitters near the trailhead; a one-hour pedal in either direction rewards you with Missouri River bottomland scenery.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Historic Main Street has 25-plus restaurants, and the strip from South Main to Second Street covers everything from old-school Missouri bar food to riverside patios. Eat dinner outside if weather allows — this is one of the few spots on the trail where you can watch the Missouri River while you eat. Lewis and Clark were guests of honor at dinners here the night before departure; several restaurants stay open past 9 p.m. on weekends.

⏱ 90 min

Route 94 west of St. Charles follows the Missouri River into Defiance and Augusta through Missouri wine country, with hillsides planted in Norton, Vignoles, and Traminette. Lewis and Clark passed this exact landscape in 1804. Chandler Hill Winery in Defiance and Sugar Creek Winery in Augusta are the two closest tasting rooms; the round-trip scenic drive takes about 90 minutes with one stop. The road itself is as much the point as the wine.

Arrow Rock Mo

A 45-person town that is itself a National Historic Landmark, 12,000 years in the making.

★★☆☆☆
📍 Arrow Rock State Historic Site Missouri📍 J. Huston Tavern Arrow Rock📍 George Caleb Bingham Home Arrow Rock
1

Pull off I-70 at Exit 89 and drive 13 miles north to Arrow Rock — a National Historic Landmark whose entire town is the attraction. Lewis and Clark passed the limestone bluff here on June 9, 1804; Clark noted 'the Prairie of Arrows' in his journal. The bluff had been a flint-quarry site for 12,000 years of Indigenous cultures, and 'pierre à flèche' appeared on French maps as early as 1732. Today you'll find 45 permanent residents, wooden sidewalks, the 1834 J. Huston Tavern — Missouri's first state-preserved historic site — and the home of painter George Caleb Bingham, separately designated a National Historic Landmark in 1968. Thirty minutes is enough for a quick walk; the tavern serves lunch.

Stop 4

Kansas

★★★★☆
📍 National WWI Museum Memorial Kansas City📍 Arabia Steamboat Museum Kansas City📍 Nelson-Atkins Museum of Art Kansas City📍 Kaw Point Park Kansas City
1

Walk across the glass bridge at the National WWI Museum and look down — beneath your feet are 9,000 red poppies, each representing 1,000 combatant deaths. This is America's only federally designated WWI museum, built beneath the 217-foot Liberty Memorial tower that Kansas City erected after 83,000 citizens raised $2.5 million in just ten days in 1919. The 1921 site dedication was the only time the supreme commanders of all five Allied nations stood in the same place. From the tower's observation deck you get the best panorama in Kansas City. Budget two hours for the museum plus the tower. Paid entry; the Over There Café is open without a ticket.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Arabia Steamboat Museum in the City Market is running its final year — the Hawley family announced closing in November 2026 after 35 years, so this visit has a deadline. On September 5, 1856, the 171-foot steamboat Arabia hit a tree snag and sank 6 miles west of Kansas City, carrying 200 tons of cargo bound for 16 frontier towns. The Missouri River shifted course, and the Arabia lay buried 45 feet underground beneath a Kansas cornfield for 132 years until treasure hunters dug it up in 1988. The collection is the largest single assemblage of pre-Civil War artifacts in the world. Allow 90 minutes.

⏱ 90 min
3

Four badminton shuttlecocks, each 18 feet tall and weighing 5,500 pounds, sit on the lawn of the Nelson-Atkins Museum of Art as if giants had just abandoned a game. Artists Claes Oldenburg and Coosje van Bruggen installed them in July 1994, using the neoclassical building as a net and the 22-acre lawn as the court. The museum itself holds one of the country's great encyclopedic collections — standout rooms include the ancient Chinese galleries, a Caravaggio, and the photography wing. Free admission; free outdoor access at any hour.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Kaw Point Park sits at the confluence of the Kansas and Missouri Rivers on the east side of Kansas City, and a short interpretive loop tells the story of Lewis and Clark's camp here on June 26, 1804. Ten minutes from downtown, often overlooked, and worth the detour — standing at the exact meeting point of the two rivers connects Day 4 directly back to the Corps. The park is small, the trail is under a mile, and the industrial backdrop makes the historical fact feel even starker.

⏱ 45 min

The City Market district surrounding the Arabia Steamboat Museum has operated as a public market since 1857. On weekends, the outdoor farmers' market draws crowds to stalls selling Missouri produce, local charcuterie, and every kind of food. Kansas City smoked meat culture is distinct enough that a meal here qualifies as cultural research — the tradition of low-and-slow hickory-smoked brisket, burnt ends, and ribs runs through dozens of family-owned joints within a few miles of downtown.

⏱ 90 min

Stop 5

Omaha

Council Bluff, the first Otoe council, and the Missouri River frontier in one riverfront city.

★★★★☆
📍 Lewis Clark Landing Omaha Nebraska📍 Durham Museum Omaha Union Station📍 Fort Atkinson State Historic Park Nebraska
1

Lewis and Clark Landing is a riverfront park at the foot of Douglas Street where the Corps camped in 1804, and the bronze sculpture garden here depicts key moments from the expedition — including York, Clark's enslaved companion. The Durham Museum, a mile inland in the beautifully restored 1931 Art Deco Union Station, holds rotating exhibits on Omaha's railroad and immigration history alongside a solid permanent Lewis and Clark section. The restored station interior — terrazzo floors, vaulted ceiling, original rail clocks — is worth the admission even without the exhibits. Open Tuesday through Saturday.

⏱ 90 min
2

Drive 20 miles north of Omaha to Fort Calhoun and you reach Fort Atkinson State Historic Park — site of the first U.S. Army post west of the Missouri River, established in 1819. This is where Lewis and Clark held their first formal council with the Otoe-Missouri people on August 3, 1804, under a bluff they named Council Bluff. The reconstructed fort covers the infantry post as it stood in the 1820s, with living history interpreters on summer weekends. Free admission to the grounds.

⏱ 75 min
3

DeSoto National Wildlife Refuge straddles the Iowa-Nebraska border 25 miles north of Omaha and gives you two things: the DeSoto Bend oxbow lake — one of the best autumn migration stopovers for snow geese and bald eagles in the Great Plains — and the preserved cargo of the steamboat Bertrand, which sank here in 1865 carrying supplies to Montana Territory. The Bertrand collection of 250,000 artifacts excavated in 1969 is displayed in the refuge visitor center. Free with a federal lands pass or small per-vehicle fee.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Old Market neighborhood in downtown Omaha occupies a six-block district of 1880s brick warehouses now filled with independent restaurants, galleries, and bars. Cobblestone streets, iron bridges between buildings, and no chain restaurants — this is where Omaha actually eats. The best window is 6 to 9 p.m., when the market is at full energy. Several farm-to-table restaurants here specialize in Nebraska beef, and the strip of Howard Street has the densest concentration.

⏱ 120 min

The Riverfront Trail runs 7 miles along the Missouri from Lewis and Clark Landing north through Gene Leahy Mall, passing interpretive markers about the 1804 and 1806 passages of the Corps. The flat trail is paved and accessible for cyclists — bike rentals are available near the landing. Even a 30-minute walk toward Heartland of America Park rewards you with views of the Council Bluffs bank across the river, the same eastern horizon the Corps saw from the other side.

Stop 6

Sioux

★★★★★
📍 Sergeant Floyd Monument Sioux City Iowa📍 Lewis Clark Interpretive Center Sioux City📍 Sioux City Art Center
1

The Sergeant Floyd Monument stands on a bluff overlooking the Missouri — a 100-foot Egyptian-style obelisk of Kettle River sandstone completed in 1901 and among the first 92 sites designated as National Historic Landmarks in the United States in 1960. Floyd's remains are sealed at its base. He died on August 20, 1804, age 22, likely from a ruptured appendix — the only member of the Corps of Discovery to perish on the entire expedition. His last words to Clark were: 'I'm going away and I want you to write me a letter.' The grave was twice washed away by Missouri River erosion before the Floyd Monument Association secured permanent burial here. Free site; the overlook of the river valley is the reason to linger.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Lewis and Clark Interpretive Center on the riverfront levee goes deeper into the Great Plains section of the 1804 journey than almost any other stop on this trail. Interactive exhibits cover the Lakota tensions near present-day Pierre, Floyd's death, the natural history the Corps documented, and the Missouri as the highway of the interior. The center sits in a converted pavilion with direct views of the river bend the expedition navigated. Paid entry; open daily.

⏱ 75 min
3

Built in 1918, the Woodbury County Courthouse at 7th and Douglas is a National Historic Landmark — designed by William Gray Purcell and George Elmslie, two protégés of Louis Sullivan, in the organic Prairie style. The exterior terra cotta ornament and the central rotunda are among the most elaborate public interior spaces in the upper Midwest. Free to enter during business hours. The Sioux City Art Center, three blocks away, holds regional and contemporary works with free admission and is worth a 30-minute stop.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Stone State Park in the northwest corner of Sioux City has bluff trails reaching 1,000 feet of elevation with overlooks of the Missouri Valley and three states simultaneously — Iowa, Nebraska, and South Dakota. The Dorothy Pecaut Nature Center inside the park runs natural history programming and exhibits on the Missouri River ecosystem the Corps navigated. The Sergeant Floyd River Museum on the south end of town doubles as the city visitor center and has a 19th-century sternwheeler on display.

⏱ 90 min

The Historic Fourth Street District two blocks from the river is Sioux City's restaurant and entertainment corridor. The city has a strong Czech and German immigrant food culture that persists in family-owned bakeries and supper clubs. If you're arriving in the evening, Fourth Street is where you eat — several restaurants specialize in grilled Midwest beef and the craft beer scene on this block has grown considerably in recent years.

Yankton sits at the base of Gavins Point Dam, the smallest of the six Missouri River mainstem dams but the one that creates Lewis and Clark Lake — a 25-mile reservoir over what was open river in September 1804. Pierre

South Dakota's capital, Lewis and Clark's showdown with the Lakota, and a dam that drowned homelands.

★★★★☆
📍 South Dakota Cultural Heritage Center Pierre📍 Oahe Dam Pierre South Dakota📍 Farm Island Recreation Area Pierre📍 South Dakota State Capitol Pierre
1

The South Dakota Cultural Heritage Center on Governors Drive is built into a hillside in the shape of a Lakota winter camp structure, and the architecture signals that this museum takes Indigenous history seriously. Exhibits run from 12,000-year-old Clovis points through the Lakota Nation to the Lewis and Clark passage in September 1804 — when the Corps had a tense armed standoff with Lakota warriors over upriver passage near present-day Fort Pierre. Out-of-state paid entry; South Dakota residents free. Plan 90 minutes.

⏱ 90 min
2

Five miles north of Pierre on Route 1804 — literally named for the expedition — the Oahe Dam Visitor Center tells the story of the fourth-largest man-made reservoir in the United States. Construction ran from 1948 to 1962, when President Kennedy dedicated it; the dam displaced entire Lakota communities and submerged traditional homelands under 231 miles of reservoir stretching north to Bismarck. A small exhibit connects the dam back to Lewis and Clark's river passage. The center is free, open weekdays 9 to 4.

⏱ 60 min
3

Farm Island Recreation Area, 4 miles east of Pierre on the Missouri, has the Lewis and Clark Family Center — hands-on interpretive site open Memorial Day to Labor Day where you can examine dug-out canoe replicas, animal hides, and walk terrain the expedition camped on. Across the river, a monument on the Fort Pierre levee marks the September 1804 confrontation with Chief Black Buffalo — an encounter that almost ended the expedition before it reached the Mandan villages.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The South Dakota State Capitol grounds have the 1910 building at the center — 115 acres with an artesian-fed lake, the Fighting Stallions Memorial, and the Flaming Fountain veterans' memorial. The capitol interior has murals, tile work, and a rotunda that rewards a 30-minute self-guided walk. Everything on the grounds is free and accessible daily. Pierre is one of the least-visited state capitals in the country — the lack of crowds makes the building more accessible, not less interesting.

⏱ 45 min

Lake Oahe stretches 231 miles from Pierre north to Bismarck, and the Missouri River access points near Pierre offer some of the best walleye and northern pike fishing in the Great Plains. Shore fishing from Oahe Downstream Recreation Area is free. The 26-mile Lewis and Clark Bicentennial Trail runs from Pierre along the river through rolling prairie and cottonwood groves — a gravel and dirt multi-use path where you're more likely to see white-tailed deer than another person.

Mobridge Sd

Sitting Bull's grave on the Missouri bluff, sculpted by Korczak Ziolkowski in 1953.

Bismarck

Custer's last fort and a Mandan earthlodge village that predates the expedition by 200 years.

★★★★★
📍 North Dakota Heritage Center Bismarck📍 Fort Abraham Lincoln State Park Mandan North Dakota📍 On-A-Slant Mandan Village Fort Lincoln📍 Custer House Fort Lincoln
1

The North Dakota Heritage Center and State Museum on the capitol grounds in Bismarck is the largest museum in the state, with galleries spanning 600 million years of natural history and 13,000 years of human history on the Northern Plains. The Geologic Time spiral winds from Cretaceous marine fossils to a reconstructed T-rex, and the Adaptation galleries cover the Mandan, Hidatsa, and Arikara nations with depth. Lewis and Clark spent 164 days with these peoples at Fort Mandan during the winter of 1804-05 — those encounters are well documented here. Free admission; open daily.

⏱ 90 min
2

Drive 7 miles south to Fort Abraham Lincoln State Park in Mandan, where Lt. Col. George Armstrong Custer commanded the 7th Cavalry from 1873 until he rode out in May 1876 and died at Little Bighorn that June. The reconstructed Custer House, Central Barracks, and Granary sit on the original footprints; living history tours of the Custer House run daily in summer. Adjacent is On-A-Slant Village — a Mandan settlement occupied from about 1575 to 1781, with six reconstructed earthlodges that show how this culture thrived on the Missouri for two centuries before the expedition arrived. Park fee applies.

⏱ 120 min
3

The North Dakota State Capitol is the tallest in the country at 19 stories — a 1934 Art Deco skyscraper locals call 'the skyscraper on the prairie.' The observation floor offers a 360-degree view of the Missouri River bottomlands and the flat plains beyond. Lewis and Clark would have seen a very similar horizon from their winter camp at Fort Mandan about 60 miles north. Free tours run Monday through Friday.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The Lewis and Clark Riverfront at Bismarck has a paved trail and interpretive panels along the Missouri that connect the modern city to the 1804 and 1806 passages. Walk north along the river in the evening — the Capitol dome is lit against the sky, the opposite bank is dark and wild-looking, and the scale of the Great Plains journey suddenly makes sense. Local restaurant Pirogue Grille on East Broadway, named for the expedition's smaller boats, is where Bismarck's food scene takes itself seriously.

⏱ 60 min

The On-A-Slant Trail at Fort Lincoln State Park connects the cavalry post to the Mandan village and continues through native prairie and cottonwood groves along the Missouri and Heart rivers. The full loop is 2.5 miles at an easy pace. This is also the best spot in the Bismarck area to look for bald eagles along the river in fall and spring. Park rangers offer guided walks to On-A-Slant Village in summer — check the schedule at the visitor center.

Stop 13

Williston

Fort Union Trading Post, 39 years of fur trade, and where Sitting Bull surrendered in 1881.

★★★★★
📍 Fort Union Trading Post NHS Williston North Dakota📍 Fort Buford State Historic Site Williston📍 Missouri Yellowstone Confluence Williston
1

Twenty-five miles southwest of Williston, right on the North Dakota-Montana border, Fort Union Trading Post National Historic Site rises from the floodplain with its white palisade walls and red-roofed towers exactly as the original looked in 1851. John Jacob Astor's American Fur Company built Fort Union in 1828, and for 39 years it controlled the buffalo robe and fur trade across a vast Northern Plains territory — over 25,000 robes and $100,000 in merchandise changing hands annually. Assiniboine, Crow, Blackfeet, Mandan, and six other nations traded here. The reconstructed Bourgeois House holds museum exhibits; rangers in period dress staff the trade room. Free admission.

⏱ 90 min
2

Two miles downstream, Fort Buford State Historic Site marks where the U.S. Army dismantled Fort Union in 1867 — using its own timber and stone to build Buford — and where Sitting Bull surrendered on July 19, 1881, famously handing his rifle to his young son first before giving it to the commander. A small museum holds the original surrender document. The Missouri-Yellowstone Confluence Interpretive Center, a half-mile further, stands at the exact meeting point of the two rivers where Lewis arrived on April 26, 1805, calling it the most remarkable river junction in North America. Free state park entry.

⏱ 75 min
3

The drive back to Williston from Fort Union follows Route 1804 through Missouri River bottomland that looks much as Karl Bodmer painted it during his 1833 journey up the river with Prince Maximilian of Wied. The cottonwood galleries, oxbow ponds, and sandbar configurations remain relatively intact through this corridor. Williston itself was the epicenter of the 2010s Bakken oil boom; the Frontier Museum on 2nd Avenue West — an open-air village of restored homestead buildings from the 1800s to 1950s — and the James Memorial Art Center document the territory's earlier history.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The Missouri River bottomlands around the Fort Union-Fort Buford corridor are some of the least-disturbed riparian habitat remaining on the Upper Missouri — cottonwood galleries, oxbow ponds, and sandbars that look much as they did in 1805. Walk the park trails from Fort Union toward the river in the early morning and you'll likely see white-tailed deer, wild turkey, and migrating waterfowl. Bald eagles are common year-round in the cottonwood groves.

⏱ 60 min

The annual Fort Union Rendezvous in June draws hundreds of buckskinners and mountain man reenactors to the site for two days of 19th-century fur trade demonstrations, flintlock shooting, and period camp life. If your dates align, it's one of the most historically vivid events on the entire Lewis and Clark Trail. Outside of that, the Williston Saturday farmers' market at city park runs through summer and serves both the oil-field and ranching communities — unusually good produce for this latitude.

Wolf Point is the largest town on the Fort Peck Indian Reservation, home to Assiniboine and Sioux communities who have lived along the Missouri since before Lewis and Clark passed through in May 1805. Fort Peck Mt

The world's largest hydraulic earth dam and 1,520 miles of wild Missouri River shoreline.

★★★★★

Fort Peck Dam — the world's largest hydraulically filled earth dam, built by the Army Corps of Engineers between 1933 and 1940 — created a reservoir with 1,520 miles of shoreline through the Charles M. Russell National Wildlife Refuge. Lewis and Clark passed this stretch May 8-11, 1805, noting the Milk River confluence. The dam's visitor center covers both the engineering story and the boomtown of 10,000 workers that appeared and vanished in eight years during the Depression. Stop 45 minutes, then continue west toward Fort Benton.

Stop 16

Fort Benton

America's most remote 19th-century port, a hotel built in 1882, and the start of Montana's Wild and Scenic River.

★★★★★
📍 Fort Benton Montana historic levee📍 Grand Union Hotel Fort Benton📍 Museum of the Upper Missouri Fort Benton📍 Old Fort Benton remains
1

The Grand Union Hotel opened November 2, 1882, at a total cost of $200,000 — the finest hotel between St. Louis and Seattle, built at the absolute peak of the steamboat era, seven years before Montana became a state. US Army officers, Canadian Mounties, and road agents all signed the same register. The Northern Pacific Railroad arrived in Helena in June 1883 and killed the steamboat trade almost overnight; the hotel went bankrupt by 1884. Restored and reopened in 1999, it's now Montana's oldest continuously operating hotel — 26 rooms, many overlooking the river. Dinner at the Union Grille is the best meal on the entire trail.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Steamboat Levee stretches a mile along the Missouri through the center of Fort Benton, and this is where the trail becomes visceral. A full-size keelboat replica is moored at the levee; interpretive signs tell the story of the July 13, 1805 Lewis and Clark passage — the farthest navigable point of the Missouri and the last flat water before the Great Falls portage. The remains of the original adobe fort from 1847, renamed Fort Benton on Christmas 1850, stand at the levee's north end. The bridge across the Missouri, standing since 1888, closes the downstream view.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Museum of the Upper Missouri and the Museum of the Northern Great Plains share a campus two blocks from the levee and together cover Fort Benton's full arc — fur trade, steamboat era, the Whoop-Up Trail to Alberta, homestead boom, and ranching culture. The Old Fort Benton site next door preserves two original adobe blockhouses from the 1840s trading post — the oldest standing structures in Montana. Allow 75 minutes for both; combined paid admission.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Fort Benton is the put-in point for the Upper Missouri River Breaks National Monument — 149 miles of Wild and Scenic River corridor that the National Park Service describes as the most intact section of the Missouri River Lewis and Clark traveled. Float trips ranging from half-day to week-long expeditions launch here. Upper Missouri River Guides and Missouri River Outfitters both operate from Main Street. Even a half-day float downstream gives you white cliffs, eroded sandstone formations, and the same view Clark described on May 31, 1805.

⏱ 240 min

Walk Fort Benton end to end in the evening when the light hits the levee cottonwoods from the west. The story of Old Shep — the border collie who met every train for five and a half years waiting for his deceased owner, becoming national news in 1939 — is told in a bronze statue near the levee. The entire walkable town grid: three museums, two river outfitters, a saloon, and the Grand Union Hotel, all within six blocks.

Stop 17

Great Falls

Five waterfalls and a 33-day portage — the hardest mile of the Lewis and Clark Expedition.

★★☆☆☆
📍 Lewis Clark National Historic Trail Interpretive Center Great Falls Montana📍 Giant Springs State Park Great Falls📍 CM Russell Museum Great Falls Montana📍 River's Edge Trail Great Falls
1

The Lewis and Clark National Historic Trail Interpretive Center is built into a bluff overlooking the Missouri, and the Corps spent more time here than anywhere outside of winter camp. The portage of five successive waterfalls took 33 days in June and July 1805, covering 18 miles on improvised log-wheeled wagons through prickly pear cactus, grizzly bear territory, and flash-flooding coulees. The 25,000-square-foot center's centerpiece is a two-story diorama of the portage; you can test your strength on a simulated boat-hauling station. An actor-portrayed 45-minute audio walk reconstructs the scene in William Clark's voice. Paid entry ($8 adults); closed Mondays and Tuesdays outside peak season.

⏱ 120 min
2

Giant Springs Heritage State Park is a quarter-mile walk from the Interpretive Center along the River's Edge Trail, and it holds one of the largest freshwater springs in the world. Lewis described it on June 18, 1805 as 'a most beautiful fountain' — the spring discharges at 156 million gallons per day through limestone. The Roe River, flowing just 200 feet from the spring to the Missouri, long claimed the title of the world's shortest river — a distinction that has since been contested and removed from the Guinness records. The park has picnic areas, trout ponds, and a wildlife center. Free admission.

⏱ 60 min
3

The C.M. Russell Museum covers an entire city block and holds close to 1,000 original works by Charles Marion Russell — painter, sculptor, and self-described cowboy who arrived in Montana at age 16 in 1880 and spent the rest of his life documenting the West before it vanished. His log cabin studio, built in 1903 from western red cedar telephone poles, stands on the museum grounds alongside his family home — both National Historic Landmarks since 1965. More than 3,000 pieces of Western art fill 16 galleries. Paid entry; open daily in summer.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The River's Edge Trail runs 60 miles of paved non-motorized path through Great Falls along the Missouri, connecting the Interpretive Center to Giant Springs, the five waterfall overlooks, and the CMR Museum district. Rent a bike in town and ride the section from Giant Springs past Rainbow Falls and Black Eagle Dam — about 8 miles of riverside trail with the actual waterfalls that stopped the Corps for 33 days. The falls look modest now, controlled by power dams, but the side canyons and cottonwood banks still tell the story.

⏱ 120 min

First Peoples Buffalo Jump State Park, 10 miles south of Great Falls off I-15, is a mile-long sandstone cliff where Indigenous hunters drove bison for at least 2,000 years — the remains of millions of processed animals lie in the talus below. An interpretive trail and shelter explain the archaeology. It predates Lewis and Clark by millennia, but it gives the Great Falls stop a deeper Indigenous context that the expedition barely acknowledged during its grueling month-long portage. Free admission; 30-minute drive from downtown.
