Asuncion

★★★☆☆
1

Start at Plaza de Armas, Asunción's civic and historic heart, surrounded by its institutional landmarks: the Cabildo (now a cultural center), the Metropolitan Cathedral, and the National Congress. This square has been the stage for the country's major political moments since the city's founding on August 15, 1537 - hence the name Asunción, the feast of the Assumption. Take time to soak in the atmosphere, more relaxed than in many other South American capitals.

⏱ 30 min
2

Just steps from the Plaza, the Panteón Nacional de los Héroes is unlike anything else in South America: construction began in 1863 on Francisco Solano López's orders, inspired by Paris's Panthéon and Les Invalides, but remained unfinished for 73 years - two devastating wars interrupted the works. It was finally inaugurated on October 12, 1936, after the Chaco War. Inside rest the remains of Marshal Solano López, President Carlos Antonio López, Marshal José Félix Estigarribia - hero of the Chaco whose path you'll follow for ten days - and the Niños Mártires of Acosta Ñu. Note that the Panteón's portico serves as kilometer zero for all Paraguayan national roads, including Ruta 9 that you'll take tomorrow. The changing of the guard takes place daily at 5 PM - a solemn spectacle worth witnessing.

⏱ 45 min
3

Walk up to the Palacio de López - or Palacio de Gobierno - whose white facade stands out elegantly against the Bahía de Asunción in pure neoclassical style. Built from 1857 onwards on Carlos Antonio López's orders as a residence for his son Francisco Solano, materials came from all over Paraguay: stone from Emboscada quarries, bricks from Tacumbú, timber from Yaguarón, and ironwork cast at Ybycuí. Solano López never lived there: the War of the Triple Alliance broke out in 1865 before finishing touches were complete, and the Brazilian army occupied the palace for seven years, carrying off furniture and ornaments. Don't visit the interior (restricted government access), but walk around the esplanade on the río Paraguay side to admire the facade from the Costanera - when illuminated at night, it's breathtaking.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Before leaving Asunción at dawn tomorrow, your arrival day is the perfect time to prepare for your Chaco expedition: stock up on water (plan at minimum 10 liters per person for isolated stretches), non-perishable food, medicines, and extra fuel (jerry cans holding 20 to 40 additional liters are recommended beyond Filadelfia). The supermarkets in Villa Morra neighborhood are well-stocked. Also check your vehicle's condition - spare tires, oil, air filter - because garages become scarce very quickly once you're on Ruta 9.

⏱ 90 min

Late in the day, head down to Asunción's Costanera, the waterfront promenade along the Bahía on the río Paraguay. This is where Asuncenos gather to run, cycle, or simply watch the sun plunge behind the river's immensity - the Paraguay is so wide here you can barely make out the opposite shore. The sight of Palacio de López backlit is worth the trip alone. Take advantage of the moment to eat: waterfront restaurants serve surubí (giant Paraguay River catfish) grilled, a local specialty not to be missed before immersing yourself in the Mennonite cuisine of the coming days.

⏱ 90 min

Filadelfia

★★☆☆☆
1

Ruta 9, called the Trans-Chaco Highway, is one of South America's most singular roads: 835 km of asphalt stretching in a straight line from Asunción's outskirts to the Bolivian border. The first 50 km cross the Bajo Chaco (humid Chaco), an intensely green savanna dominated by caranday palm groves - the palms grow so dense and uniform they look like plantations. Watch the roadside at dawn: yacarés (caimans), capybaras, and dozens of aquatic bird species are common. After the town of Pozo Colorado (around km 270), the vegetation changes abruptly: palms disappear, replaced by the dry thorny forest of the Central Chaco - quebracho, palo santo, cacti. This is the invisible threshold between humid and dry Chaco, one of Paraguay's most striking landscape contrasts.

⏱ 120 min
2

Make the most of roadside stops to observe the Chaco's characteristic fauna. Along Ruta 9, especially in early morning hours, you have good chances of spotting armadillos (several species), rheas (South American ostriches reaching 1.2 m tall), and all sorts of raptors perched on power poles. Around km 350, the landscape of dense dry forest with quebracho colorado and paddle cacti gives the Chaco its subtropical wild-west look - you'll understand why some travelers compare it to the Australian bush.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Leave Asunción very early (ideally before 6 AM) for the 450 km to Filadelfia - count on 6 to 7 hours including stops. Ruta 9 is fully paved to Mariscal Estigarribia, but pavement condition deteriorates after rains and speed bumps are frequent in villages. Never take this road at night: wildlife constantly crosses and animal collisions are common. Fill up in Filadelfia as soon as you arrive - it's the last major gas station before the northern dirt tracks.

⏱ 420 min

Filadelfia

★★☆☆☆
1

Filadelfia rises from the middle of the Chaco like an improbable Germanic mirage: ruler-straight streets, orderly houses, German-language signs, gleaming tractors in front of cooperatives. Founded on August 17, 1931, as the administrative center of the Fernheim Colony, it bears the Greek name meaning "brotherly love." The 1,700 Mennonite refugees who arrived from the Soviet Union starting in 1930 - fleeing Stalinist collectivization - transformed in less than a century a thorny scrubland without potable water into a prosperous city of 18,000 inhabitants, capital of Boquerón department since 1993. Stroll along Avenida Hindenburg (named after the German president who facilitated emigration) and Avenida Trébol: you'll hear Plautdietsch (Low German) spoken as much as Spanish, and the local pastries in shop windows will remind you that you're not in the heart of traditional Paraguay.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Museo de la Colonia Fernheim (also called the Mennonite History Museum), located on Menno Simons Hof on Avenida Hindenburg, traces the incredible odyssey of these settlers: departing Russia under Communist repression, they transited through Germany before completing their journey by ox-cart in one of the continent's most inhospitable regions. The exhibition starts with the 16th-century Anabaptist movement and arrives at the present day with photographs, maps, objects, and life stories. Allow 1.5 hours for a thorough visit - the panels in Spanish and German are well documented.

⏱ 90 min
3

Just steps away, the Jakob Unger Museum is worth an hour's visit. Its name honors the first Mennonite settler to conduct systematic scientific studies on Chaco fauna, between 1950 and 1975. The museum divides into two buildings: the first presents the communal agricultural machinery from the colony's early days; the second houses an impressive collection of mounted animals - jaguar, giant armadillo, puma, anaconda - and an entire section devoted to the indigenous communities who inhabited the Chaco before the Mennonites' arrival. Don't miss the mural depicting the journey from Russia to Paraguay via several countries - it alone summarizes these settlers' epic.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Fernheim Cooperative manages practically everything in Filadelfia: the supermarket, hotel, hospital, and agro-industrial plants. Guided tours of facilities - cheese factory, slaughterhouse, sesame processing plant - are organized by the cooperative's tourism office (Avenida Hindenburg 131-S, corner of Avenida Trébol). These tours in Spanish, German, or English provide access to normally closed facilities and help understand the unique cooperative economic model: each Mennonite family holds a cooperative share and receives dividends, health insurance, and pension coverage. Book ahead.

⏱ 120 min

Visit the market and cooperative stalls to taste local products: European-style aged cheeses (the Trébol brand is known throughout Paraguay), smoked sausages, sesame bread, and Chaco fruit preserves. It's also a good place to buy indigenous crafts from Nivaclé and Enlhet communities - mats, pottery, embroidery - whose pieces are sometimes sold directly by community members working in connection with the cooperative.

⏱ 60 min

Filadelfia

★★☆☆☆
1

Loma Plata lies 20 km southeast of Filadelfia: about twenty minutes' drive to enter a slightly different world. This is the administrative center of Menno Colony, the oldest and most conservative of the Chaco's three Mennonite colonies, founded in 1926 by 1,743 settlers from Manitoba, Canada - who were refusing the new English-language schooling requirement imposed in 1917. With 6,000 inhabitants, the town is smaller and more rural than Filadelfia, dominated by the Chortitzer Cooperative and its dairy processing plants (Trébol brand, Paraguay's major cheese brand). German is even more prevalent on the streets than in Filadelfia.

⏱ 40 min
2

The Museo de la Fundación de la Colonia Menno, housed in the colony's former administrative building now converted, displays agricultural implements, ox-carts, and the first pioneers' tools. You'll learn among other things that 171 immigrants died waiting in transit camps for 18 months before reaching their lands, that the first problem to solve was water - the Chaco's water table being too saline for consumption - and that economic prosperity only really began in 1980 thanks to a World Bank loan that enabled investment in dairy processing. The adjoining tourism office (turismo@chortitzer.com.py) organizes tours of the Trébol factory and provides access to the lagoons at Laguna Capitán and Campo María.

⏱ 75 min
3

Neuland, the third Mennonite colony in the Chaco, is accessible from Filadelfia via a junction off Ruta 9 at km 413. Its name means "new land" in German - and for good reason: founded in 1947 by refugees fleeing the Soviet Union after World War II, it welcomed people that other countries refused to accept, often widows, elderly people, and families shattered by war. Striking fact: the majority of early settlers were women, the men having been sentenced to Siberian labor camps by the Stalinist regime. The town of Neu-Halbstadt, its center, is the most modest of the three colonies but has a colonization museum and a monument dedicated to the Chaco War. Allow about 1.5 hours for the round trip and visit.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The route between Filadelfia, Loma Plata, and Neuland crosses typical Central Chaco landscape: thorny bush cut with estancias (large extensive cattle ranches), herds of gray-coated Brahman zebu cattle, and occasionally the silhouette of a century-old quebracho whose blood-red trunk gives the region its name (quebracho comes from "quebrar hacha," axe-breaker - the wood is so hard it was once exploited for railroad ties). Keep binoculars handy: rheas, armadillos, and sometimes Chaco foxes frequently cross the road.

⏱ 90 min

In the Loma Plata area, several salt lagoons constitute excellent birding spots. The Chortitzer Cooperative's tourism office can guide you to the most accessible sites depending on season: bare-faced ibis, egrets, roseate spoonbills, jabiru storks, and Chilean flamingos regularly frequent the shores in dry season (May to September). Head out early morning - the light is better and birds more active.

⏱ 90 min

Filadelfia

★★☆☆☆
1

Fortín Boquerón is the Chaco's most moving site. Here, in August-September 1932, the first major battle of the Chaco War was fought - that deadly conflict that pitted Paraguay against Bolivia from 1932 to 1935 for sovereignty over a region both countries believed rich in oil (there was almost none). The Bolivians seized the fort on July 31, 1932, with 711 heavily equipped men; Paraguay's 1st Army Corps encircled them by September 9 and besieged them relentlessly for three weeks. The defenders surrendered on September 29, 1932: 320 Bolivian dead, 500 Paraguayans killed in the assault. This single battle officially claimed 3,738 casualties (killed, wounded, and missing) on both sides. The site today houses an imposing monument built from the original trenches and defenses. Look for the hollowed-out palo borracho ("drunken tree") that served as a Bolivian sniper's nest - more than 70 years after combat, the tree is still alive.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Museo de la Guerra del Chaco at Fortín Boquerón is one of the continent's best-documented military museums. It holds weapons seized from the enemy, uniforms from both armies, tactical maps, and photographs from the period, some strikingly real. What's striking is the belligerents' asymmetry: Bolivia had 3 million inhabitants versus Paraguay's 1 million, and fielded an army trained by German General Hans von Kundt; yet the Paraguayans, better acclimated to the terrain and remarkably led by General José Félix Estigarribia, ultimately won the war and gained 75% of the contested Chaco. Allow 45 minutes to an hour for a serious visit.

⏱ 60 min
3

The military cemetery and commemorative monuments at Fortín Boquerón rank among Paraguay's most contemplative places. Graves of Paraguayan and Bolivian soldiers stand side by side in respectful silence - a symbol of reconciliation in a war that claimed between 80,000 and 100,000 total deaths, roughly one combatant in four. Take time to read the epitaphs. Practical note: access is via a ~65 km dirt track from the junction on Ruta 9 (Cruce Los Pioneros). The site is accessible by ordinary car in dry season, but 4x4 is strongly recommended after rains.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The round-trip excursion to Fortín Boquerón from Filadelfia represents about 130 km of track round-trip (depending on the exact junction taken from Ruta 9) and a full half-day. Leave early in the morning to enjoy relative coolness and avoid afternoon heat that can exceed 40°C in summer. The track crosses dry forest with quebracho, candelabra cacti, and typical Chaco thorny vegetation - a near-desert landscape that helps understand the hell in which soldiers from both sides fought, without water, under torrid heat.

⏱ 300 min

Mariscal Estigarribia

★☆☆☆☆
1

Mariscal Estigarribia, 200 km north of Filadelfia via Ruta 9 (partly on dirt track), is not a Mennonite colony: it's an ordinary Paraguayan town, or almost. Founded in 1944 on the site of the former Bolivian fort Fortín Camacho - recaptured by Paraguay in 1933 during the Chaco War - it bears the name of the commander-in-chief of Paraguayan armies during that conflict. General José Félix Estigarribia later became president of the Republic in 1939, before dying in a plane crash in 1940. The town, with around 1,500 inhabitants, is the last serious resupply point before the north. Fill up with fuel without exception: beyond here, pumps become extremely rare.

⏱ 30 min
2

The airport at Mariscal Estigarribia, Dr Luis María Argaña International Airport (IATA code: ESG), deserves a look from outside: its 3,500 m runway is the country's longest, built between 1977 and 1986 by Paraguayan military engineers with the idea of making it a commercial free zone. The infrastructure's enormity - oversized for the Paraguayan Air Force's small fleet - has long fueled theories about a supposed American military base, which both countries' governments have always denied. A curiosity in the middle of the Chaco.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

The Filadelfia-Mariscal Estigarribia leg (~200 km) represents about 4 hours under normal conditions, but can lengthen considerably after rains. The road crosses the Chaco Boreal, a region even more arid than the Central Chaco: the dry forest of white quebracho and palo santo is more open here, scattered with thorny scrub and large expanses of red earth. Drive slowly and keep your eyes open: Chaco peccaries (tagua), rheas, and numerous raptors frequent the roadside. Parrots fly in flocks of dozens.

⏱ 240 min

Mariscal Estigarribia

★☆☆☆☆
1

Parque Nacional Defensores del Chaco is Paraguay's largest national park and one of South America's vastest protected areas: 780,000 hectares of xerophytic forest created on August 6, 1975. Think the size of Corsica - that's roughly it, and roughly as isolated. Vegetation is dominated by white quebracho, palo santo, samu'u, and dozens of cactus species. The fauna is exceptional: jaguars (one of the southernmost known populations), pumas, tapirs, giant armadillos, giant anteaters, and the tagua (Chaco peccary, Catagonus wagneri), a species considered extinct until its rediscovery in 1975. Count more than 460 animal species recorded in the park. Warning: access requires authorization from MADES (Ministerio del Ambiente y Desarrollo Sostenible) - contact them in Asunción before the trip.

⏱ 240 min
2

At the park's heart, Cerro León is a spectacular geological anomaly in the great Chaco plain: a massif of sandstone and quartzite of Silurian age (444 to 419 million years old) 40 km in diameter, culminating at 604 m altitude. In an almost entirely flat country, this isolated mountain is visible from afar and constitutes a unique natural curiosity. For the Ayoreo, an indigenous people partly still in voluntary isolation in this region, Cerro León is the center of the world - a sacred place. The ascent is possible but done only with a forest ranger (contact via Madrejón post, 220 km north of Filadelfia). The view from the summit embraces a 360-degree horizon of infinite forest.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

Hiking or driving in the park must be done in 4x4 and with prior MADES approval. The Madrejón ranger station is the entry point for the Cerro León area and biological station. On park tracks, leave very early in the morning (6-9 AM) to maximize wildlife observation chances - jaguars and pumas are extremely discreet but ground tracks are frequent. Giant anteaters move about more readily in early morning. Bring water in large quantities - temperatures can exceed 42°C in summer (November-March) and drop below 0°C in winter (June-August), with nocturnal frosts.

⏱ 300 min

Filadelfia

★★☆☆☆
1

On the return route toward Filadelfia, stop at Laguna Capitán, a private ornithological reserve in the transition zone between dry and humid Chaco. This site, classified as an "Important Bird Area" by BirdLife International, is one of Paraguay's best birding spots: roseate spoonbills, jabiru storks (South America's largest, with a wingspan of 1.8 m), Chilean flamingos, bare-faced ibis, and a great variety of ducks migrate here according to seasons. Walking trails let you explore the edge vegetation between dry forest and floodable zones - an ecotone particularly rich in biodiversity.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Central Chaco is also one of South America's most productive extensive cattle-raising regions. Along the return track, you'll cross several estancias with vast pastures of grasses imported from North America (buffelgrass, introduced in 1955, transformed the local economy). Herds of silver or coffee-colored Brahman and Nelore zebu cattle, supervised by gauchos on horseback, are a characteristic image of the deep Chaco. Also watch the ponds and water holes for yacarés (Paraguayan caimans) basking there during the day.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The return leg from Mariscal Estigarribia to Filadelfia represents ~200 km, or 4 to 5 hours depending on track conditions after recent rains. Organize your day to arrive at Laguna Capitán mid-morning - it's the best window for bird observation - then resume the road to reach Filadelfia before nightfall. Never drive at night in the Chaco: wildlife is omnipresent on tracks and collision risks are very high.

⏱ 270 min

As you approach Filadelfia late afternoon, stop at the trackside to admire the sunset over the dry Chaco. The low late-day light gives the thorny forests and red plains an absolutely striking golden-orange hue - one of Paraguay's most beautiful natural spectacles, which you won't see again in town.

⏱ 30 min

Asuncion

★★★☆☆
1

The return to Asunción via Ruta 9 offers a chance to reread the landscapes in reverse. After days of dry Chaco, the gradual return to humid Chaco - which you perceive in the vegetation change around Pozo Colorado - feels almost liberating: caranday palm groves reappear, flooded areas and ponds reflect the sky, and dry heat gives way to gentler humidity. Around km 100 before Asunción, you'll make out the first structures of the periphery and, approaching the Remanso bridge over the río Paraguay, the capital's northern suburbs sprawling over the hills.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Filadelfia-Asunción leg runs ~450 km, a full day's drive. Leave at dawn to arrive in the city before nightfall. If you have time, make a lunch stop in the village of Pirahu or at a roadside bodega (snack-restaurant) to taste empanadas chaqueñas - filled with ground beef, olive, and hard-boiled egg, they're considered among the country's best. Final practical tip: if you enjoyed the Chaco, note that the next Trans-Chaco rally (Trans-Chaco Rally) takes place every year in late September - a legendary off-road race of more than 800 km on Chaco tracks.

⏱ 420 min

Asuncion

★★★☆☆
1

The Manzana de la Rivera is Asunción's most endearing cultural site: a complex of nine restored colonial houses, facing the Palacio de López, that form a unique municipal cultural center. The oldest, Casa Viola, dates from 1750-1758 - its adobe walls and tile roof survived wars and demolitions. This is where you'll find the Museo Memoria de la Ciudad, inaugurated in 1996, which traces Asunción's history from its founding in 1537 with old maps, photographs, and models. In 1989, architecture students launched the "Save the Manzana" campaign to prevent its demolition and replacement with a parking lot - they won, and the city is grateful. Entrance is free; the café terrace on the second floor offers one of the best views of the Palacio de López.

⏱ 75 min
2

If you have time before transferring to the airport, the Jardín Botánico in Asunción, north of downtown, makes a lovely 2 km² walk with some fifty species of indigenous Paraguayan trees, including specimens of quebracho and lapacho (the national tree with spectacular pink blooms). The Museo de Historia Natural y Antropológica, housed in Carlos Antonio López's former presidential residence within the garden grounds, displays collections of mounted Chaco animals - an unexpected transition between the nature you've just crossed and the return to the city.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Use this final morning to buy souvenirs: Guaraní handicrafts (ñandutí embroidery, ceramics from the Guaraní ethnic group), Chaco leather goods (belts, wallets, artisan-tanned zebu leather bags), and Fernheim cooperative preserves if you haven't consumed everything on the road. The Mercado 4, Asunción's popular market, is the most authentic - you'll find everything from chimarrão mate to hammocks in caraguatá fiber. Allow an hour.

⏱ 60 min

Transfer to Silvio Pettirossi International Airport (ASU), located about 15 km northeast of downtown Asunción. Count on 30 to 45 minutes outside rush hours, and up to 1.5 hours during peak traffic. The official taxi from downtown costs less than rideshare apps depending on timing - check with your hotel.

⏱ 45 min
