Rhodes

Where medieval knights ruled for two centuries behind Europe's finest Gothic ramparts

★★★★★
1

Rhodes Old Town has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1988, and it thoroughly deserves that recognition. The Knights of St. John occupied it from 1309 to 1522 and set about transforming it into an impregnable fortress—the 4 kilometers of ramparts you'll walk along bear witness to this. What makes the site exceptional is the layering: behind Gothic walls hide Ottoman mosques, Turkish baths, and Byzantine alleyways. Enter through the d'Amboise Gate late morning, before tour buses flood the narrow streets.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Palace of the Grand Masters dominates the old town from the highest point of the citadel. Built in the 14th century, it contains 150 rooms, of which 24 are open to the public. What you absolutely can't miss inside: the mosaic floors, brought from Kos by the Italians during their controversial 1930s restoration. Worth knowing: in 1856, a gunpowder magazine explosion nearly destroyed the entire palace—what you see today is largely a Fascist reconstruction, intended to welcome Mussolini, who ultimately never visited. Look for the commemorative stele bearing his name near the ticket office.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Street of the Knights (Odós Ippotón) is one of the best-preserved Gothic monuments in Europe. The Knights of the Order were organized into seven "tongues"—France, Italy, Spain, Provence, Auvergne, England, Germany—and each had its inn along this main thoroughfare. You'll see their facades at every turn. Take time to read the coats of arms carved above the doors: the Auvergne inn bears those of Guy de Blanchefort, Grand Master from 1512 to 1513.

⏱ 45 min
4

The Archaeological Museum of Rhodes is housed in the former Great Hospital of the Knights, built between 1440 and 1489. The star of the collection? The Crouching Aphrodite, a Hellenistic marble statue of remarkable finesse. Allow 45 minutes to an hour for a complete tour. The combined ticket (valid 3 days) includes this museum, the Palace of the Grand Masters, and the church of Panagia tou Kastrou—a smart way to optimize your visits.

⏱ 60 min
5

The Suleiman the Magnificent Mosque, built in 1522 just after the Ottoman conquest of Rhodes, visually marks the transition between two civilizations. Its pink minaret is visible from afar within the old town. It's closed to regular visits, but the exterior and surrounding square are worth seeing in the evening, when the light is soft and crowds thinner.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Wander through the lanes of the lower town (Chora) following your instincts: craft shops, painted ceramic workshops, small tavernas tucked into courtyards. If you're there on a weekday morning, the covered market on Socratous Street offers a much more local atmosphere than the tourist boutiques along the waterfront.

⏱ 60 min

Stroll along Mandraki harbor late in the day. At the harbor entrance stand two columns topped with bronze stag and doe—tradition claims the Colossus of Rhodes, one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World, stood at this spot (nothing proves it, but visitors love the story). Three Ottoman windmills and Fort Saint Nicholas complete the panorama. A café terrace facing the harbor at sunset—that's the best moment of the day.

⏱ 60 min

Try pitaroudia, chickpea fritters typical of Rhodes, fried in olive oil with onion and dill. You'll find them in all the old town's tavernas, often served hot as an appetizer. Pair them with a glass of Athiri, the local white varietal: dry, light, slightly citrusy, perfect with mezze and fresh fish.

⏱ 45 min

If you have energy after dinner, climb onto the ramparts from the Palace of the Grand Masters entrance (different hours from the palace, check before going). The walk offers plunging views over the old town and harbor you won't find anywhere else. Bring a flashlight—lighting is minimal.

⏱ 60 min

Rhodes

Where medieval knights ruled for two centuries behind Europe's finest Gothic ramparts

★★★★★
1

The Acropolis of Lindos perches on a cliff 116 meters above the sea—it's the third most-visited archaeological site in Greece, right after Athens and Delphi. The Temple of Athena Lindia, in its current form, dates from around 300 BC (replacing an earlier temple destroyed by fire in 392 BC). At the entrance, a rare detail: a warship's prow carved directly into the rock in the 2nd century BC, which served as a pedestal for a now-vanished bronze statue. Arrive before 9 AM—after that hour, the climb becomes a procession.

⏱ 90 min
2

The village of Lindos, entirely pedestrian, is built in an amphitheater on a hillside. Its lanes paved with black-and-white pebble mosaics lead to the "captains' houses" from the 16th century, built by shipowners who had made fortunes in maritime trade. Push open the door of the Church of the Virgin (Panagia), founded in the 14th century and restored in 1489 by Grand Master d'Aubusson: its interior frescoes date from 1779 and are among the best-preserved on the island. Skip lunch on a terrace here—prices are noticeably higher than elsewhere on Rhodes.

⏱ 60 min
3

St. Paul's Bay (Paralia Agios Pavlos), below Lindos, takes its name from the apostle Paul, who supposedly landed here during his journey to Rhodes. The water is a striking turquoise, enclosed on three sides by cliffs and the acropolis. It's also a popular wedding venue—if you're there on a weekday, you'll have better chances of finding the peace needed to enjoy it.

⏱ 45 min
4

On the island's west coast, Kamiros is nicknamed the "Greek Pompeii" of Rhodes: a Doric city founded in the 5th century BC, abandoned after an earthquake, and rediscovered in 1852. You'll see a sophisticated water system—cisterns, pipes, fountains—that supplied the entire city. The stoa, terraced houses, and Temple of Athena are well-preserved enough to give you a precise idea of ancient urban planning. Allow one hour for the visit.

⏱ 60 min
5

On the way back from Kamiros, the Valley of the Butterflies (Petaloudes) is worth a quick stop between June and September, when thousands of Panaxia quadripunctaria butterflies settle there in the coolness of plane trees and waterfalls. Off-season, the site remains pretty but the butterflies aren't there—check the timing before making the detour.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The climb to Lindos acropolis is on foot from the village—bring good shoes and water, the slope is steep under the sun. Mules offer rides up, but several local sources discourage this option out of concern for the animals. The reward at the top: a 360° view over Lindos' two bays, the Aegean Sea, and on clear days, the Turkish coast.

⏱ 60 min

After the acropolis, head down to swim in the main bay of Lindos (Main Beach). The water is clear, the beach organized with sunbeds and umbrellas. To avoid summer crowds, opt for neighboring St. Paul's Bay: smaller, less known, and just as beautiful.

⏱ 90 min

Try souma before leaving: it's a traditional Rhodes fig eau-de-vie, sweet and fragrant, found in small grocers in Lindos village. Pair it with takakia, these little bites of fried octopus with lemon served by most waterfront tavernas. For wine, the local Mandilaria—fruity and light red—is perfect with grilled meats.

⏱ 45 min

Symi

A harbor where pastel mansions cascade down hillsides like a neoclassical waterfall

★★☆☆☆
1

Entering Gialos harbor, the spectacle hits you all at once: hundreds of neoclassical houses in shades of ochre, pink, blue, and yellow rising in tiers up the hillside, reflected in the harbor water. One hour by ferry from Rhodes, Symi owes this architecture to its 19th-century prosperity, when the island was a major shipbuilding center producing up to 500 vessels a year. The village of Gialos is a classified historic monument—facades are protected in their original state. Give yourself time on the waterfront before climbing toward Chorio.

⏱ 60 min
2

Chorio, the upper village, is reached from Gialos via the Kali Strata—literally "the beautiful stairs"—500 steps lined with the island's finest manor houses. The climb is worth it as much for the sculpted facades as for the views over the harbor that open at each landing. At the top, the ruins of the 15th-century Knights' castle dominate everything, and Chorio's quiet lanes contrast with the bustle of Gialos below.

⏱ 60 min
3

Panormitis Monastery, in the island's south, is one of the Dodecanese's most important pilgrimage sites. Founded in the 18th century, it's dedicated to the Archangel Michael, patron saint of sailors. Its baroque bell tower rises above a sheltered bay accessible only by sea or by winding road. Inside, the walls are covered with ex-votos offered by grateful sailors—bells, silver boats, objects recovered after shipwrecks. Several boat excursions from Gialos stop here.

⏱ 60 min
4

The Symi Maritime Museum, housed in the old neoclassical shipyard on Kampos square, traces the history of this island of sailors and sponge divers. Diving suits, archival photos, 19th-century diving equipment: you understand here just how dangerous this fishing was—decompression accidents were frequent and left many men disabled. It's a short visit (30 to 45 minutes) but gives crucial context to what you see around you.

⏱ 45 min
5

Symi's beaches are mostly pebbles rather than sand, accessible mainly by taxi-boat from Gialos. Agios Georgios Dyssalonas, Nanou, and Agia Marina are among the island's most beautiful coves—turquoise waters, cliff backdrops, no roads leading to them. Check at the harbor in the morning for departures and the last return boat.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Position yourself on the ferry deck as you approach Symi—the entrance to Gialos harbor with its amphitheater of houses is one of the Dodecanese's most photographed moments. If you're spending the night on the island, enjoy the evening calm when day-trippers from Rhodes have caught their boat back: tavernas empty by half and lanes regain their silence.

⏱ 30 min

Take a taxi-boat from Gialos to reach one of the isolated coves on the island's south or east coast—Nanou or Agia Marina are best for swimming. It's the only way to access these bays unreachable by road. Ask at the harbor about the last return boat before departing.

⏱ 180 min

Symi's specialty is tiny local shrimp (garides Symis)—minuscule, caught in the deep waters around the island, crispy and delicious eaten whole. They're served fried with a squeeze of lemon and a glass of ouzo. Every harbor taverna offers them—quality varies, but it's the dish you can't miss. Finish with galaktoboureko, that pastry of semolina cream wrapped in phyllo, soaked in lemon syrup.

⏱ 60 min

Kos

Where Hippocrates founded Western medicine beneath plane trees and marble colonnades

★★★★☆
1

The Asclepeion of Kos sits 4 km southwest of town, and it's the island's most important archaeological site. Founded in the 4th century BC, it was simultaneously a religious sanctuary, healing center, and medical school—one of history's first hospitals, capable of receiving up to 4,000 patients according to some ancient sources. Hippocrates, a native of Kos, supposedly taught here. The sanctuary spreads across three terraces connected by marble staircases: Roman baths and consultation rooms on the first level, temples of Apollo and Asclepius on the second, grand Doric temple and porticoes on the third. From the top, the view sweeps across Kos town, the sea, and the Turkish coast opposite. Bring a hat and water—there's little shade on site.

⏱ 90 min
2

Opposite the Knights' castle, on Platanos square, stands the Hippocrates Plane Tree. The tree measures 4.7 meters in diameter and spreads more than 12 meters in radius—it's one of Europe's oldest and largest plane trees, even if botanists estimate its age at around 500 years rather than the legendary 2,400. No matter: the spot is pleasant, shaded, and tradition says Hippocrates taught medicine beneath its branches.

⏱ 20 min
3

The Castle of the Knights of Kos (Neratzia), built from 1315 directly atop the ruins of an ancient fort, controls the harbor entrance from its promontory. The Knights of St. John reused ancient marble blocks for its walls—you can still clearly spot them in the lower courses. The interior houses a collection of ancient and medieval sculptures. Until the early 20th century, the castle was separated from town by a moat filled with seawater.

⏱ 60 min
4

The ancient agora of Kos, south of the castle, is one of the region's best-preserved ancient urban centers. Founded in the 4th century BC on a geometric plan, it features a massive colonnaded stoa, ruins of a sanctuary to Aphrodite, a 2nd-century BC Temple of Hercules, and a 5th-century Christian basilica. The site is generally open and lightly monitored—ideal for peaceful exploration.

⏱ 45 min
5

The Archaeological Museum of Kos, on Eleftherias square, houses the collection of discoveries made on the island since antiquity. The centerpiece: a marble statue of Hippocrates, and several remarkable mosaics from the Hellenistic and Roman periods. Combined ticket with the Asclepeion available.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Kos is one of the Dodecanese's most bikeable Greek islands—flat, well-served by bike paths, and crossed end-to-end by a coastal road. Rent a bike in the morning to reach the Asclepeion (4 km), then head toward the beaches of Tigaki or Marmari on the north coast, with their fine sand and shallow waters. The total journey from Kos town represents about twenty kilometers round-trip—very accessible for any level.

⏱ 180 min

In the evening, explore the lively center of Kos town with its blend of Ottoman architecture (mosques, fountains) and Greek and Roman remains around street corners. Apellou street and the areas around Eleftherias square concentrate tavernas, bars, and restaurants for ending the day on a terrace.

⏱ 90 min

Try patatato, Kos's traditional dish: veal slowly braised with potatoes, white wine, and cumin. Simple, generous, rooted in the island's rural tradition. For dessert, sample the local baklava, more generous with walnuts and cinnamon than the neighboring Turkish version.

⏱ 60 min

Kalymnos

From the depths where sponge divers risked their lives to cliffs where climbers chase the sky

★★★☆☆
1

Pothia, Kalymnos's capital, welcomes you with its amphitheater of colorful houses as soon as you leave the ferry. Along the waterfront, stalls displaying natural sponges of all shapes and sizes immediately remind you which island you're on. Homer already mentioned this tradition in the Iliad—Kalymnos was long the world's leading exporter of natural sponges. Wander through Pothia's lanes to see the neoclassical buildings inherited from this prosperity, and spot the coats of arms of the Knights Templar engraved on certain facades.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Maritime Museum of Pothia (Vouvalio district) is the best introduction to the island's history. You'll see the archaic diving suits used by divers until the 1960s to descend to depths of several dozen meters, archival photos of seasonal expeditions to North Africa and the eastern Mediterranean, and sponge-processing equipment. Decompression accidents were so frequent that the island had an abnormally high number of disabled men—the museum doesn't hide this reality.

⏱ 45 min
3

Chrysocheria Castle dominates Pothia from a promontory above town. Byzantine in origin, it was reinforced by the Knights of St. John. The climb on foot from the center takes 20 to 30 minutes—the view over the harbor and sea is well worth the effort. Two small churches with post-Byzantine murals stand within the walls.

⏱ 45 min
4

Vathy, on the island's east side, is one of Kalymnos's most unusual sites: a narrow fjord of turquoise water cuts inland between limestone cliffs, opening onto a peaceful village surrounded by orange and mandarin groves. The road leading there is winding but passable. Avoid lunchtime when excursion boats from Kos and Bodrum discharge their groups—the village regains its calm by mid-afternoon.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Since the early 2000s, Kalymnos has become one of the world's premier sport climbing destinations, with over 3,500 equipped routes across roughly eighty sectors. The Grande Grotta sector, near Myrties, is the most photographed: its stalactites overhang the Aegean directly. Even without experience, you can try it out—several local centers offer guided sessions on beginner sectors like "Afternoon," with sea views. French-speaking instructors are active on the island. A Climbing Festival is organized every autumn, attracting climbers from around the world. Book the day before in high season.

⏱ 180 min

Take a taxi-boat from Myrties to reach the island of Telendos, directly opposite—10 minutes crossing. Since an earthquake in AD 554, Telendos has been separated from Kalymnos, and local legend has it that the vestiges of an ancient city still rest on the seabed between the two islands. On site: a few tavernas, a quiet beach, and rare peace. Ideal for sunset.

⏱ 120 min
