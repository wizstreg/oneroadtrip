Bali in 10 Days: From the Trendy South to the Hidden Rice Terraces of the East

📅 11 days📍 10 stops🚗 220 km⏱ 5h15

Seminyak → Uluwatu → Ubud → Munduk → Sidemen

Ten days to cross Bali end to end: Seminyak's beach clubs, the sacred cliffs of Uluwatu with its sunset Kecak, Ubud's temples and rice terraces, the misty crater lakes of Munduk in the north, and finally the quiet valley of Sidemen in the shadow of Mount Agung. Bali in all its forms, far from the clichés.

✨ Highlights
  • The Kecak at sunset in Uluwatu: 50 to 70 men in a circle narrating the Ramayana without instruments, facing the ocean
  • The Tegalalang Rice Terraces, UNESCO-listed as part of the 8th-century subak irrigation system
  • The twin lakes of Buyan and Tamblingan at Munduk: two crater lakes separated by a landslide in 1800
  • The Sidemen Valley with a direct view of Pura Besakih and Mount Agung (3,031 m), Bali's sacred volcano
📆 Best period

April, May, June, July, August, September, October

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 12 and 16 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Dry Season (May-September): Perfect weather, minimal rainfall, ideal temperatures, clear skies, peak tourist season, best diving conditions, advance booking recommended.

Shoulder Season (April & October): Good weather, fewer crowds, moderate rainfall, reasonable prices, excellent conditions, transitional seasons, good value.

Wet Season (November-March): Heavy rainfall, lush landscapes, tropical storms, humidity high, lowest prices, fewer tourists, challenging conditions.

🌍 What to discover in ID?

World's largest archipelago with incredible diversity, pristine beaches, and rich cultural heritage. Experience Bali's temples, Java's volcanoes, and authentic Indonesian culture. 📋 Important notes

Land-based Bali itinerary only. No sea transfers required. Return from Sidemen to Denpasar Airport (DPS) on Day 10 — allow about 2 hours of driving. The Ubud–Munduk road crosses the northern highlands (Bedugul): winding route, best avoided at night.

Day 1

Seminyak

Trendy seaside strip where surf and beach clubs set the pace at sundown

★★★★★
📍 Pura Petitenget Temple Seminyak📍 Double Six Beach Seminyak Bali📍 Legian Beach Bali📍 Kuta Beach Bali📍 Eat Street Jalan Kayu Aya Seminyak
1

Welcome to Bali — your first stop is Seminyak, the trendiest stretch of the island's south coast. From Ngurah Rai Airport (DPS), it's about a 30-minute drive into a long coastal strip where five-star hotels rub shoulders with designer boutiques. Start with a stroll along the beach: the sand is golden grey, the waves moderate — the sea here works well for beginner surfers, though watch out for currents at low tide. As the sun drops, beach clubs come alive and terraces fill up — this is the Seminyak show in full swing.

⏱ 90 min
2

Just steps from the beach, don't walk past Pura Petitenget without stopping. This 16th-century temple is one of six sacred sea temples forming a 'spiritual chain' of protection around the island, established by the great Hindu priest Dang Hyang Nirartha during his pilgrimage from Java. Its name means 'magic box' — according to legend, it contained the priest's powers. The red brick and sandstone architecture with its dragon-carved stairway is worth a look. Bring a sarong to enter (mandatory; available at the entrance). Arrive early in the morning to avoid the weekend crowds.

⏱ 30 min
3

Double Six Beach (Pantai Berawa) is the quintessential sunset spot in Seminyak. Colourful loungers line the waterfront, beach bars start turning up the volume around 5 pm and locals join tourists to watch the orange disc sink into the ocean. It's the most social stretch of the area — arrive about an hour before sunset to grab a spot.

⏱ 60 min
4

Legian, squeezed between Kuta and Seminyak, is often overlooked even though it deserves a good 30 minutes of wandering. Its side streets running perpendicular to the beach are lined with Balinese painting galleries, sculpture shops and sarong tailors. The vibe sits somewhere between Kuta's bazaar energy and Seminyak's polish — and so do the prices. A solid stop for mid-range souvenir shopping.

⏱ 45 min
5

Kuta Beach, 3 km south of Seminyak, is the birthplace of Bali tourism. Since the 1930s, Australian and American surfers set down their boards here and gradually put the island on the global travel map. Today the beach stretches over 3 km, is wide, and the surf remains excellent for beginners thanks to the sandy bottom and moderate waves. The Kuta neighbourhood has lost in charm what it gained in hotels, but the beach itself is still very much there.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In the evening, head to Jalan Kayu Aya, dubbed 'Eat Street' by locals. This pedestrian road packs in many of Seminyak's best restaurants: traditional Balinese cuisine, fresh seafood, fusion spots. Grab a terrace table and order a nasi campur (rice with assorted sides) or a sate lilit (Balinese spiced fish skewer) for your first taste of the local food scene.

⏱ 90 min

Day 2

Seminyak

Trendy seaside strip where surf and beach clubs set the pace at sundown

★★★★★
📍 Pasar Badung market Denpasar Bali📍 Batu Bolong Beach Canggu Bali📍 Petitenget Beach Seminyak Bali📍 Seminyak Square shopping Bali📍 Kuta Art Market Bali
1

The beaches of Seminyak and Kuta are really one continuous stretch of sand running over 5 kilometres. Since the 1930s, American and Australian surfers have been riding the waves at Kuta Beach — historically one of the cradles of surfing in Asia. Today, the Seminyak section concentrates the beach clubs and luxury hotels, while Kuta remains more popular and affordable. The dry season (April–October) is ideal for surfing: offshore winds from the northeast give the waves their shape. In the wet season, onshore winds degrade conditions and debris can wash up on the beach.

⏱ 120 min
2

If shopping appeals, Seminyak Square and the surrounding craft galleries are worth an hour of browsing. You'll find Balinese sarongs, ceramics, wood carvings and silver jewellery from Celuk. Watch out for listed prices: bargaining is expected in small shops, though not in branded stores.

⏱ 60 min
3

In Denpasar, Pasar Badung is Bali's largest traditional market. Four floors overflow with spices, flowers for offerings, exotic fruits, sarongs and kitchen utensils. This is where Balinese cooks stock up every morning from 4 am. The market is loud, dense and colourful — nothing like a tourist-oriented market. Go early in the morning and wander without a set agenda.

⏱ 60 min
4

Canggu, a few kilometres north of Seminyak, is the neighbourhood that overtook Kuta as Bali's hip epicentre since the pandemic. Batu Bolong Beach, with its black volcanic sand and consistent waves, has become one of the most popular surf spots in the south of the island. Cafés, vegan restaurants and street-art galleries line the lanes — the atmosphere is a world apart from Seminyak: more bohemian, less flashy.

⏱ 60 min
5

Petitenget Beach, directly in front of the temple of the same name, is the quietest stretch in the Seminyak area. Fewer beach clubs, more Balinese families strolling at the end of the day, a handful of fishermen pulling in their nets. A useful contrast to step outside the hotel bubble and see how locals live along their own coastline.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Use your second day to try a surf lesson. Seminyak's conditions — sandy bottom, short and shallow waves — make it one of the best beginner spots in Bali. Operators offer 90-minute group lessons on the beach with a foam board and instruction. Allow 2 hours on site so you don't miss the incoming-tide session, which offers the best conditions.

⏱ 120 min

In the late afternoon, treat yourself to a traditional Balinese massage: an age-old technique combining thumb pressure, stretching and application of herb-infused coconut oil. Seminyak has one of the highest concentrations of spas on the island. Allow 1 to 1.5 hours for a full session.

⏱ 90 min

Day 3

Uluwatu

An 11th-century sea temple perched on a 70-metre cliff with nightly Kecak performances

★★★★☆
📍 Pura Luhur Uluwatu Temple Bali📍 Padang Padang Beach Uluwatu📍 Bingin Beach Uluwatu Bali📍 Jimbaran Bay seafood restaurants Muaya Beach
1

Pura Luhur Uluwatu sits perched on a 70-metre cliff above the Indian Ocean — one of the most spectacular views in all of Bali. This sea temple is one of the six 'Sad Kahyangan', the sacred directional temples that protect the island from negative forces according to Balinese Hindu tradition. Its history dates back at least to the 11th century, when Javanese monk Mpu Kuturan laid its earliest foundations. It was later expanded by Danghyang Nirartha in 1489, who, legend has it, reached spiritual enlightenment (moksha) here and vanished in a flash of light. The name Uluwatu comes from Balinese: ulu (edge) and watu (rock). Arrive at least an hour before sunset to walk the clifftop path along the bluffs. Hold on to your sunglasses: the long-tailed macaques — considered sacred guardians — have a nasty habit of snatching them.

⏱ 90 min
2

On the way down to the coast, stop at Padang Padang Beach. This small cove tucked between limestone cliffs was made famous by the film Eat Pray Love. Access is through a narrow passage cut into the rock. The water is clear and the waves fairly powerful — it's more of an advanced surf break than a family swimming beach, but the views from above are worth the detour on their own.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Kecak is the most striking cultural performance in Bali, and Uluwatu is its ideal stage. Every evening at 6 pm, 50 to 70 bare-chested men form concentric circles in the cliff-edge amphitheatre and begin a rhythmic 'cak-cak-cak' chant — with no musical instruments at all. The piece tells the Ramayana: Prince Rama, his wife Shinta abducted by demon king Rahwana, and Hanuman the white monkey warrior who burns Rahwana's palace to free her. The fiery finale against the orange sunset sky is unforgettable. Book your seats in advance during peak season and arrive before 5 pm to get a good spot. Note: the amphitheatre is exposed to the sun until 6 pm — bring sunscreen.

⏱ 75 min

After the show, head down to Jimbaran Bay for dinner with your feet in the sand. Before becoming a dining hotspot, Jimbaran was a simple fishing village. Today, around thirty seafood restaurants line the white sand of Pantai Muaya, their wooden tables just metres from the waves. The speciality: fresh fish grilled over coconut-husk charcoal, served with rice, sautéed water spinach and two varieties of sambal. Choose your fish at the counter by the entrance. The crowds arrive around 7 pm — try to get there earlier.

⏱ 120 min

Day 4

Ubud

Bali's cultural heartbeat — UNESCO rice terraces and Hindu temples deep in the jungle

★★★★★
📍 Tegalalang Rice Terraces Ubud Bali📍 Sacred Monkey Forest Sanctuary Ubud📍 Ubud Art Market Bali📍 Campuhan Ridge Walk Ubud
1

The Tegalalang Rice Terraces, located 10 km north of central Ubud, are one of Bali's most iconic sights. Listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site as part of the subak irrigation system — whose origins trace back to the 8th century and the holy man Rsi Markandeya — they cascade in tiers down the slopes of a lush valley. Come early in the morning, before 9 am: tour buses arrive mid-morning and the place gets crowded fast. March and September offer the most intense green (just before harvest). Don't settle for the view from the road: head down into the valley on the footpaths to see the farmers at work.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Ubud Monkey Forest, officially the Sacred Monkey Forest Sanctuary, is home to over 700 long-tailed macaques spread across a tropical woodland in the heart of town. The site includes three Hindu temples dating from the 14th century. The monkeys are used to visitors but remain wild — hold tight to your glasses, cameras and bags. A banana in your pocket will make you their best friend.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Ubud Art Market (Pasar Seni) is the best place to pick up authentic souvenirs: wood carvings, Balinese paintings, endek and songket cloth (a ceremonial weave once reserved for Balinese nobility), jewellery and ritual objects. The market is open daily from dawn. Arrive early for the lowest prices and the thinnest crowds. The Ubud Royal Palace (Puri Saren Agung) sits right next to the market and is worth a stop.

⏱ 60 min
4

In the late afternoon, treat yourself to the Campuhan Ridge Walk: a 2-to-3-km trail running along the ridge between two streams, crossing hills covered in grass and banana trees with wide-open views over the Balinese countryside. It's one of the few walks in Ubud where you don't have to pay an entrance fee. Set off around 5 pm for the golden light of late afternoon.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

In the evening, catch a traditional dance performance at Puri Saren Agung (Ubud Royal Palace). Several styles of Balinese dance are presented nightly in the palace courtyard: the Legong (a female dance with ultra-precise eye and finger movements), the Barong (a dramatisation of the battle between good and evil) and the Topeng (masked dance). Operators run sessions from 7:30 pm. Book on the spot or through your accommodation.

⏱ 75 min

Day 5

Ubud

Bali's cultural heartbeat — UNESCO rice terraces and Hindu temples deep in the jungle

★★★★★
📍 Goa Gajah Elephant Cave Temple Ubud📍 Tirta Empul Temple Tampaksiring Bali📍 Tegenungan Waterfall Ubud Bali📍 Pura Gunung Kawi Temple Bali
1

Goa Gajah, the 'Elephant Cave', is one of Bali's most intriguing archaeological sites. The cave entrance is carved with a monstrous mouth — most likely a kala, a guardian demon from the Hindu-Buddhist tradition — which archaeologists date to the 11th century. Inside, a small chamber holds statues of Ganesh and Shiva lingams. In front of the cave, two 11th-century stone pools were rediscovered during excavations in the 1950s; the fountains are still used for ritual purification. Bring a sarong (provided at the entrance).

⏱ 60 min
2

Tirta Empul, in Tampaksiring, is one of Bali's most sacred temples. It was founded in 962 according to Balinese chronicles, around a natural spring gushing from the ground — the Balinese believe its waters were created by the god Indra to resurrect his poisoned warriors. Today, hundreds of worshippers dressed in white immerse themselves daily in the purification pools. The sight is striking. Tourists can take part in the melukat (purification ritual) in appropriate clothing — if you want to bathe, bring a change of clothes. Arrive before 9 am to beat the groups.

⏱ 75 min
3

Tegenungan is the most easily accessible waterfall from Ubud, about a 15-minute drive away. The 15-metre drop pours into a pool of black stones where swimming is possible. Tropical vegetation frames the site. Bring a change of clothes and walking shoes — the path down is slippery.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Ubud is the Balinese capital of kopi luwak and arabica plantations. Operators around Ubud offer plantation tours where you discover how coffee, cocoa and vanilla are grown in volcanic soil — the ash from Mount Batur and Mount Agung gives the beans a unique mineral richness. You'll inevitably be introduced to the famous kopi luwak (made from beans digested by the civet cat): interesting to know about, but you're under no obligation to buy it. The tasting of the other varieties — ginger, lemongrass, cocoa — is included and worth the trip on its own.

⏱ 90 min

In the evening, join a Balinese cooking class — one of the best ways to get to grips with the local culture. The class usually starts with a visit to the morning market to choose ingredients, then under the guidance of a local cook you prepare a series of traditional dishes: sate lilit (spiced fish skewers), sayur urab (coconut vegetable salad), lawar (spiced meat and vegetable mix) and of course nasi goreng. Operators run 3-to-4-hour sessions from central Ubud.

⏱ 180 min

Day 6

Ubud

Bali's cultural heartbeat — UNESCO rice terraces and Hindu temples deep in the jungle

★★★★★
📍 Mount Batur sunrise trek Kintamani Bali📍 Pura Ulun Danu Batur lake temple Bali📍 Toya Bungkah hot springs Lake Batur Bali📍 Penelokan viewpoint Kintamani caldera Bali📍 Toya Devasya hot springs Batur Bali
1

Mount Batur is an active volcano standing 1,717 metres above a crater lake — one of the most striking panoramas in Bali. The sunrise trek is one of the island's most popular experiences, and with good reason: from the summit, you watch the sun emerge behind Mount Agung (Bali's highest peak at 3,031 m) as its rays light up the lake and the rice terraces below. Departure is around 3 am from the villages in the caldera. The climb takes roughly 2 hours on a volcanic ash trail. A local guide is mandatory (enforced by the village guide associations). Moderate fitness level required.

⏱ 300 min
2

After the descent, the natural hot springs of the Batur caldera are the perfect reward. The thermal pools at Toya Bungkah (a lakeside village) or nearby developed sites offer geothermal pools at 30–40 °C, with direct views of the lake and the volcano. A breakfast overlooking Lake Batur in one of the village warungs rounds off the morning nicely.

⏱ 90 min
3

Pura Ulun Danu Batur, on the edge of the crater lake at Toya Bungkah, is one of Bali's most sacred temples — dedicated to Dewi Danu, goddess of the waters, and governing the entire subak irrigation system on the island. The original complex was engulfed by a lava flow from Mount Batur in 1926 and entirely rebuilt in 1929 on the crater rim. Allow 45 minutes to walk around it.

⏱ 45 min
4

The ridgetop road at Penelokan offers a breathtaking panorama of the Batur caldera: a 600-metre drop between the crater rim and the lake, with the cone of the active volcano in the centre — its last major eruption was in 2000. Numerous viewpoints allow you to pull over and take in the volcanic landscape of Bali. Morning is ideal, before the clouds build up.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In the afternoon, drive through Kintamani to admire the panorama from the ridgetop road overlooking the caldera: 600 metres of elevation separate the crater rim from the lake, and on a clear day you can see all the way to Lombok to the east. The many panoramic restaurants lining the road serve Balinese buffet lunches with views of Mount Batur — it's touristy, but the view is the real deal.

⏱ 60 min

Day 7

Munduk

A colonial mountain village ringed by volcanic lakes and coffee plantations

★★★☆☆
📍 Twin Lakes viewpoint Buyan Tamblingan Wanagiri Bali📍 Lake Buyan Bali📍 Lake Tamblingan Bali📍 Munduk village Bali📍 Pura Ulun Danu Tamblingan
1

The road from Ubud to Munduk crosses the Bedugul highlands — as you climb, the scenery shifts: rice paddies give way to coffee, clove and banana plantations, and the temperature drops several degrees. Munduk is a village perched in the volcanic hills of northern Bali, at roughly 900 metres above sea level — a striking contrast with the heat of the south. It was once a plantation centre developed during the Dutch colonial era. Bring a layer: evenings get cool.

⏱ 60 min
2

Your first stop: the viewpoint over the twin lakes of Buyan and Tamblingan, from the Wanagiri ridgetop road. These two crater lakes sit within the same volcanic caldera and were originally a single body of water before 1800, when a landslide split them apart. Lake Buyan covers roughly 490 hectares; Tamblingan is smaller but more mystical, ringed by dense virgin forest. From the viewpoint, Mount Agung appears in the background on clear days. Arrive at sunrise for the best visibility.

⏱ 60 min
3

Pura Ulun Danu Tamblingan, partly swallowed by forest on the lake shore, is one of Bali's most hidden temples. Reachable by traditional dugout canoe or via a jungle trail, it is draped in moss and ancient fig trees. Locals come here for regular ceremonies and pilgrimages. The contrast between the temple's meditative atmosphere and the wild canopy surrounding it is unforgettable.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Hike through the forest between the two lakes with a crossing by traditional dugout canoe on Lake Tamblingan. The trail passes through dense tropical forest, skirts half-swallowed temple ruins and emerges on the shore of Lake Buyan. The flat-bottomed canoe crossing is offered by local village guides. Allow 3 to 4 hours for the whole experience; start early in the morning.

⏱ 240 min

Day 8

Munduk

A colonial mountain village ringed by volcanic lakes and coffee plantations

★★★☆☆
📍 Munduk village Bali colonial📍 Banyumala Twin Waterfall Munduk Bali📍 Munduk Waterfall trekking Bali📍 Golden Valley Waterfall Munduk Bali📍 coffee plantation Munduk Bali
1

Start the day by exploring Munduk village itself. This highland settlement still has several Dutch colonial wooden houses dating from the plantation era (late 19th – early 20th century) — a rare sight on Bali. The village produces cloves, arabica coffee, vanilla and strawberries. Munduk's small and authentic morning market is the ideal place to buy local spices directly from the growers.

⏱ 60 min
2

The waterfalls around Munduk are among the least visited and most beautiful on the entire island. The most accessible is Munduk Waterfall, about a 2-km walk from the village. For a more committed loop, the Golden Valley waterfall trail strings together 4 waterfalls over a 2-hour hike through a forest of clove trees, coffee plants and tree ferns. The most spectacular is Banyumala Twin Waterfall, two parallel jets plunging into a swimmable pool. Wear closed shoes — the trails can be muddy.

⏱ 150 min
3

Stop at one of the coffee plantations in the Munduk area. The volcanic soil and altitude provide ideal conditions for Balinese arabica. Some family-run plantations welcome visitors for a walkthrough of the production stages — from coffee cherry to cup — followed by a tasting. It's also a chance to try cold-brew iced coffee, a refreshing local speciality after a morning of walking.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

In the late afternoon, take time to wander through Munduk village itself. Its Dutch colonial wooden houses, quiet warungs and flower markets (a legacy of the colonial era) make a pleasant contrast with the hustle of the south. This is the authentic Bali of the highlands: locals tending their plots, landscapes that haven't been curated for tourists. Walk through the lanes towards the end of the day to catch a glimpse of everyday life.

⏱ 60 min

Day 9

Sidemen

A hidden eastern valley of rice terraces, artisan weaving and views of Mount Agung

★★★☆☆
📍 Sidemen rice terraces walk Bali📍 Pura Besakih Mother Temple Mount Agung Bali📍 Gembleng Waterfall Sidemen Bali📍 ikat weaving workshop Sidemen Bali
1

Sidemen is one of Bali's best-kept secrets. This village is tucked into the eastern valley of Karangasem, about 35 km northeast of Ubud — an Ubud without the tour buses, with terraced rice paddies thrown in and Mount Agung (3,031 m) as a permanent backdrop. The valley is an intense green, criss-crossed with paths between plots, bamboo bridges and small neighbourhood temples (sanggah). Here, farmers still work their fields by hand using the ancestral subak system. Set out on a walk from 7 am to see Agung before the clouds roll in.

⏱ 120 min
2

Sidemen has been one of Bali's great textile weaving centres for centuries. Local artisans craft songket (a ceremonial cloth woven with gold and silver threads, once reserved for Balinese nobility) and gringsing, a complex double-ikat weave found only in the nearby village of Tenganan. Workshops let you watch the looms in action and try your hand at weaving for a few hours. Allow some time: certain fabrics take weeks to complete.

⏱ 75 min
3

Pura Besakih, a 30-to-40-minute drive from Sidemen, is the largest and most sacred Hindu temple complex in Bali — the Balinese call it the 'Mother Temple' (Pura Agung). It comprises 86 individual temples terraced across the slopes of Mount Agung. Some foundation elements of the site may date back to the 8th century. The main complex is built on six stacked terraces, and the view over the valley from the top is vertiginous. Arrive early in the morning to beat the clouds around the volcano and the tour groups. A sarong is mandatory and respectful attire required.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

For waterfall lovers, Gembleng Waterfall, 7 km from Sidemen, is one of the most authentic in eastern Bali. Locals also use it for purification ceremonies (melukat). The access path runs alongside rice fields and gardens. Check the day before whether it's open: the waterfall is occasionally closed temporarily during religious ceremonies.

⏱ 90 min

Day 10

Sidemen

A hidden eastern valley of rice terraces, artisan weaving and views of Mount Agung

★★★☆☆
📍 Lempuyang Temple Gates of Heaven Bali📍 Tirta Gangga Water Palace Bali📍 Virgin Beach White Sand Beach Candidasa Bali📍 Sidemen Valley rice fields walk
1

If your flight is in the evening, the day allows you to explore the Sidemen area before heading back to Denpasar. Start with the 'Gates of Heaven' (Candi Bentar) at Lempuyang Temple, about an hour's drive away. This mountain temple perched on the lower slopes of Mount Lempuyang frames Bali's most photographed image: the split gateway perfectly framing the cone of Mount Agung. Arrive before 7 am to avoid the queue of photographers and the heat haze — the wait can exceed 2 hours in peak season.

⏱ 120 min
2

Nearby, the Tirta Gangga Water Palace in Amlapura was built in 1946 by the Raja of Karangasem. Its cascading pools with deity-shaped fountains, giant koi and statues from Balinese mythology create a timeless setting. A deep calm reigns here, the polar opposite of the busy tourist sites down south. You can swim in the sacred pools in appropriate attire.

⏱ 75 min
3

On the road back to Denpasar for your flight, stop at Virgin Beach (Pantai Pasir Putih) near Candidasa — one of the rare white-sand beaches in eastern Bali. Jungle-covered cliffs, shallow turquoise water, a handful of cold-drink vendors and barely any tourists: this is the Bali of 20 years ago. The drive from here to Denpasar Airport takes roughly 1.5 to 2 hours depending on traffic.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

One last possible morning in the Sidemen rice terraces before hitting the road. Walk up the ridgeline path overlooking the valley on its eastern side for a full view of Mount Agung — on a clear day, the panorama stretches across the terraces, down the valley and out to the Lombok ocean on the horizon. A fine farewell to end the trip on.

⏱ 60 min
