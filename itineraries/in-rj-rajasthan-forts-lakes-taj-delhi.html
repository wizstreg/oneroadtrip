Udaipur

★★★★☆
1

Lose yourself in the winding lanes of Udaipur's old city, descending toward the ghats of Lake Pichola. Textile shops, silver jewelry vendors and spice stalls stretch endlessly. The city was founded in 1559 by Maharana Udai Singh II after the fall of Chittorgarh to the Mughals—he chose this location, protected by the Aravalli hills. Circle around via Gangaur Ghat for the finest view of the Lake Palace at twilight.

⏱ 90 min
2

Just 150 meters from the City Palace stands the unmissable Jagdish Temple—24 meters tall, dedicated to Vishnu. Built in 1651 by Maharana Jagat Singh I, you'll climb 32 marble steps to reach the entrance, guarded by a brass statue of Garuda (half-man, half-eagle). Inside sits a black stone idol of four-armed Vishnu. If you're there in the evening, the aarti (lamp prayer ceremony) is worth catching: bells, chants and incense in the golden light.

⏱ 45 min
3

The City Palace is Rajasthan's largest palace complex: a facade stretching 244 meters long and 30 meters high, dominating Lake Pichola. Construction began in 1559 and continued for 400 years under 22 generations of Sisodia rulers. Don't miss the Mor Chowk (Peacock Courtyard) with its relief mosaics representing the seasons, and the mirror-lined Sheesh Mahal. The palace served as a backdrop for the 1983 James Bond film Octopussy. Allow at least 2 hours—an audio guide is available.

⏱ 120 min
4

Jag Mandir, on an island in Lake Pichola, is accessible only by boat from Bansi Ghat—and the crossing is part of the experience. The palace was built between 1551 and 1652 by three Maharanas. In 1623, Prince Khurram, in rebellion against his father Jahangir, took refuge here with his family. Later crowned Emperor Shah Jahan, he's said to have drawn inspiration from Jag Mandir when designing the Taj Mahal. Stroll through gardens of bougainvillea and jasmine, flanked by white marble elephant statues.

⏱ 90 min
5

Step through the doors of Bagore ki Haveli, an 18th-century mansion on the shores of Lake Pichola, near Gangaur Ghat. Once the residence of the Mewar Prime Minister, it now displays royal costumes, jewelry and musical instruments. In the evening, the courtyard hosts a Rajasthani dance performance—a more intimate alternative to the big tourist shows.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Take a boat ride on Lake Pichola in late afternoon. The lake was created in the 15th century by a Banjara tribal chief, then expanded by Maharana Udai Singh II in 1560. From the water, you'll get the finest overview: the illuminated City Palace, the seemingly floating Lake Palace, and the Aravalli as backdrop. Choose the 5pm departure for the best light.

⏱ 60 min

Attend the Dharohar show at Bagore ki Haveli. Each evening in the open-air courtyard of Neem Chowk, Rajasthani folk dances and kathputli puppets perform for about an hour. Arrive 20 minutes early—seating is limited and fills quickly.

⏱ 75 min

Chittorgarh

★★★★☆
1

Chittorgarh Fort is India's largest fort: 280 hectares enclosed by 4.5 km of ramparts, perched 180 meters above the plains. A UNESCO site since 2013, it has seven successive gates—Padan Pol, Bhairon Pol, Hanuman Pol, Ganesh Pol, Jorla Pol, Laxman Pol and Ram Pol—which you'll pass through driving up. The site contains 65 structures including 4 palaces, 19 temples and 2 towers. Around 3,000 people still live within the walls.

⏱ 60 min
2

Rana Kumbha Palace, the most massive of the fort's ruins, dates in its earliest foundations to 734 under Bappa Rawal. Rebuilt by Maharana Kumbha in the 15th century, this is where Maharana Udai Singh was born—saved as a baby by his nursemaid Panna Dai, who reportedly hid him in a fruit basket to protect him from a usurper. The remains still show stables, water reservoirs and women's quarters (zenana). It's also the site of the terrible jauhars—the collective self-immolations of Rajput women facing invading armies.

⏱ 75 min
3

You'll spot the Vijay Stambha (Tower of Victory) from afar—37 meters of red sandstone and white marble, 9 stories, 157 steps. Built between 1442 and 1449 by Rana Kumbha to celebrate his victory over the Sultan of Malwa. The interior is carved floor to ceiling: Hindu deities, seasonal allegories, weapons, musical instruments. On the fifth floor, look for portraits of architect Jaita and his three sons—a rare signature for the era. The view from the top embraces all of Chittorgarh.

⏱ 60 min
4

Smaller at 22 meters but older, the Kirti Stambha (Tower of Fame) dates from the 12th century. Dedicated to Adinath, the first Jain master, it's adorned with Jain sculptures across its entire surface. The 54 steps lead to the top via a very narrow staircase—claustrophobics beware. It testifies to the religious coexistence that prevailed in the fort.

⏱ 40 min
5

Make a detour to the Mirabai Temple, built in classic north Indian style. It commemorates the famous 16th-century mystic poet, a Krishna devotee whose fervor scandalized the royal court. The small sanctuary surrounded by an open porch with four pavilions radiates a soothing atmosphere after the intensity of the warrior ruins.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Take a tuk-tuk or private vehicle to complete the full circuit of the fort—at 280 hectares, covering it on foot in a day is impossible. Allow 3 to 4 hours to link the palaces, towers and temples, photo stops included.

⏱ 210 min

If you stay into evening, the sound and light show recounts the battles and jauhars of the fort under the stars. Timings vary by season—check on-site.

⏱ 60 min
$$$9Sukh Sagar Homestay

Bundi

★★★☆☆
1

Raniji ki Baori is the largest of Bundi's 50 stepwells—and one of Rajasthan's most impressive. Built in 1699 by Queen Nathavati Ji Solanki, it plunges 46 meters deep. You enter on the second level through an arched portal flanked by marble elephant statues. Each level houses a place of worship, and the pillars are carved with figures of Vishnu—look for his incarnations as Varaha (boar), Matsya (fish) and Narasimha (man-lion). The entrance is small and easy to miss on the main street.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Garh Palace seems to emerge from the cliff face—seen from below, the effect is striking. Built by Rao Raja Ratan Singh between 1607 and 1631, it's renowned for its turquoise and gold frescoes of the Bundi miniature painting school, created with natural pigments (minerals, vegetables, precious stones, silver and gold). Enter through the Hathi Pol (Elephant Gate), climb to the Diwan-e-Aam, then push on to the Chhatra Mahal and Badal Mahal. Warning: many rooms are closed and left to the bats—a flashlight is useful.

⏱ 90 min
3

Taragarh Fort (Star Fort) crowns the hill above Garh Palace. Built in the 14th century, it offers a panoramic view over Bundi, its blue houses and the Aravalli. The climb is steep—bring good shoes and water. At the top: labyrinthine passages, crumbling bastions and rock-cut reservoirs. No infrastructure, no crowds—raw Rajasthan.

⏱ 75 min
4

Bundi resembles a miniature Jodhpur, minus the tourists. Blue houses, narrow lanes and vintage shops evoke another century. Wander through the fruit and vegetable market in the town center, where merchants wear imposing colorful turbans. This is also where Rudyard Kipling reportedly found inspiration for Kim—he stayed at Sukh Mahal, a small palace on the edge of Jait Sagar lake.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Set out to discover Bundi's hidden baoris—the town once had about a hundred. Dhabhai Kund, not far from Raniji ki Baori, is a massive 17th-century symmetrical well—possibly India's second largest. Also seek out Nagar Sagar Kund, twin wells near Chougan Gate, often overlooked amid the bustle of the shops.

⏱ 60 min
$$$9Classic Sapphire Ananta Bundi

Sawai Madhopur

★★★☆☆
1

Ranthambore Fort perches 213 meters above the forest, at the heart of the national park—the only major Indian fort located entirely within a tiger reserve. Founded around 944 by Chauhan king Sapaldaksha, a UNESCO site since 2013, it changed hands about ten times over the centuries. Climb the 273 steps to the summit to discover the Trinetra Ganesh temple—the world's only one where Ganesh is depicted with three eyes. Langurs populate the ruins, and occasionally a tiger prowls below. The view over the park from the ramparts is spectacular.

⏱ 120 min
2

Outside safari hours, stroll around Padam Talao and the Raj Bagh ruins at the park entrance. The lakes harbor crocodiles basking in the sun, sambar deer and langur hordes. The area is rich in birdlife—purple herons, crested serpent eagles, kingfishers. Bring binoculars.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Dawn jeep safari in Ranthambore National Park. The 392 km² park shelters around 70 Bengal tigers—a rare trait: they're diurnal, which increases the odds of spotting them in broad daylight. The 6-seater jeep safari lasts 3 hours. Book online several weeks ahead—zones 2, 3 and 4 offer the best chances. Bring warm layers for the winter morning, and keep your eyes on the langurs: their alarm call often betrays a nearby cat.

⏱ 180 min
$$$9.6Khem Villas

Jaipur

★★★★★
1

See the Hawa Mahal (Palace of Winds) early when the sun strikes the facade—that's the magic hour. This 15-meter pink sandstone wall, built in 1799 by Maharaja Sawai Pratap Singh, contains 953 honeycomb windows. Architect Lal Chand Ustad designed it in the shape of Krishna's crown. The jharokhas allowed royal women to observe street processions without being seen. Surprising detail: the building has no foundation. Entry is from the rear, not the facade—search carefully.

⏱ 45 min
2

Jaipur has been a trading city since its founding in 1727 by Maharaja Sawai Jai Singh II—one of India's first grid-plan cities. Johari Bazaar for gemstones, Tripolia Bazaar for textiles and lac bangles, Bapu Bazaar for souvenirs. Stop for roadside chai—often offered by shopkeepers hoping you'll linger.

⏱ 75 min
3

The City Palace of Jaipur is still partially inhabited by the royal family. The Mubarak Mahal (Palace of Welcome), built in the late 19th century, houses a royal textile museum. Don't miss the two giant silver urns in the Diwan-i-Khas—standing 1.60 m tall, they're the world's largest solid silver objects (Guinness record). They carried Ganges water on royal trips to England.

⏱ 90 min
4

Right next to the City Palace, Jantar Mantar is an astronomical observatory built in 1734 by Sawai Jai Singh II. Its 19 monumental stone instruments are a UNESCO site since 2010. The most imposing: the Samrat Yantra, the world's largest sundial—27 meters tall, accurate to 2 seconds. Hire a guide on-site, or the instruments remain incomprehensible.

⏱ 60 min
5

If you have energy late in the day, head to Galtaji (Monkey Temple) 10 km east. Nestled in a rocky gorge, this 18th-century temple complex is overrun by macaques. Natural pools fed by springs are sacred. The main temple dedicated to Hanuman is decorated with murals. Guard your belongings from the monkeys—they're quick and opportunistic.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Take a guided bicycle tour of Jaipur at dawn, before the heat and traffic. Several operators offer 2-3 hour circuits through the old city, with stops at the flower and vegetable markets, and breakfast with a local family.

⏱ 150 min
$$$9.3Pearl Palace Heritage Boutique Hotel

Jaipur

★★★★★
1

Start with Nahargarh Fort at dawn—the view over Jaipur from the ramparts is the city's finest. Built in 1734 by Sawai Jai Singh II then expanded in 1868, the fort dominates the Aravalli ridge. Inside lies Madhavendra Bhawan, with its symmetrical suites built for the maharaja's nine queens. If you're athletic, cycle up from the city—the road is steep but spectacular.

⏱ 75 min
2

Descending, stop at Jal Mahal (Water Palace) for the photo. This 18th-century palace seems to float in the middle of Man Sagar Lake—in reality, four of its five floors are submerged. You can't enter, but the view from the shore, especially with the Aravalli backdrop, is one of Rajasthan's classic postcards.

⏱ 20 min
3

The royal cenotaphs of Gaitore, nestled in a valley between Nahargarh and the city, are often ignored by tourists. These are the tombs of Jaipur's maharajas—the most elaborate, in carved white marble, are those of Sawai Jai Singh II and Sawai Pratap Singh. The place is peaceful and photogenic, especially in the morning.

⏱ 30 min
4

Amber Fort is the most spectacular monument in the Jaipur region. Built from 1592 by Raja Man Singh I on a hill overlooking Lake Maota, it blends Rajput and Mughal architecture. The Sheesh Mahal (Mirror Palace) inside is dazzling—thousands of convex mirror fragments line walls and ceilings. The Diwan-i-Aam courtyard and the Ganesh Pol (with its colored tile mosaic) are unmissable. The fort is part of the six Rajasthani forts inscribed as UNESCO in 2013. Allow at least 2 hours.

⏱ 120 min
5

Just below Amber Fort, don't miss Panna Meena ka Kund—a 16th-century geometric stepwell whose zigzagging staircases create a hypnotic pattern. Highly photogenic, and far less crowded than Abhaneri's Chand Baori.

⏱ 20 min
6

If Indian textiles interest you, head to the Anokhi Museum in Amber, housed in a restored haveli. It traces the history of block printing (hand-stamping on fabric), an ancestral Rajasthani technique. Artisan demonstrations are often offered.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Late in the day, settle on the ramparts of Amber for sunset. The golden light on the Aravalli hills and Lake Maota below composes a sumptuous tableau. Bring water—there are no vendors at the top.

⏱ 45 min
$$$9.3Pearl Palace Heritage Boutique Hotel

Pushkar

★★★★☆
1

Pushkar Lake is one of Hinduism's few sacred lakes—according to legend, it was created when Brahma dropped a lotus petal to earth. Its 52 ghats (sacred steps) descend to the water where pilgrims bathe and make flower offerings. Remove your shoes when approaching the ghats and be discreet—this is a place of prayer, not a photo backdrop. Beware fake priests who insist on tying a red thread to your wrist then demand a 'donation.'

⏱ 60 min
2

Pushkar's Brahma Temple is one of the very rare temples worldwide dedicated to Brahma, the creator god. The entrance is marked by a marble gate and the floor is paved with silver tiles. The temple is small but the fervor of devotees is intense. Opens at 6am—go early to avoid the queues.

⏱ 30 min
3

Climb to the Savitri Temple, perched on the hill overlooking Pushkar. The walk up takes 30-40 minutes (a cable car also exists). From the summit, the view over the lake, temples and surrounding dunes is Pushkar's finest, especially at sunrise or sunset.

⏱ 75 min
4

Wander through the lanes of Pushkar bazaar—a surprising mix of hippie souvenir shops, incense stalls and street food stands. Pushkar is a vegetarian town (no alcohol or meat in the town center restaurants) due to its sacred character. Try malpua, a local sweet fried pancake, the town's specialty.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

If your trip coincides with Holi (March), Pushkar is one of India's premier locations for the festival of colors. Celebrations around the lake are organized and highly festive—bring clothes you can sacrifice and protect your cameras with a waterproof bag.

⏱ 120 min
$$$9.1Camp Lands End

Jodhpur

★★★★☆
1

Mehrangarh dominates Jodhpur from a 125-meter rocky spur—it's one of India's most massive forts. Built from 1459 by Rao Jodha, it houses a remarkable museum with an excellent audio guide available in French. The collections of palanquins, armor and royal cradles are exceptional. Pass through Loha Pol gate—you'll see the handprints of queens who committed sati (immolation on their husband's funeral pyre). Allow 2 to 3 hours for the complete visit.

⏱ 150 min
2

From Mehrangarh's ramparts, you'll understand why Jodhpur is the 'Blue City'—the indigo blue of the Brahmin quarter's houses stretches endlessly below. Descend into the lanes to lose yourself in this labyrinth of bright blue facades, carved doors and nonchalant cows. The best viewpoints are from the guesthouse terraces in the Navchokiya quarter.

⏱ 75 min
3

Jaswant Thada, a 5-minute walk from Mehrangarh, is a white marble cenotaph built in 1899 in memory of Maharaja Jaswant Singh II. The marble is so thin it's translucent in sunlight. The garden and small artificial lake surrounding it make this a haven of peace after the fort's intensity.

⏱ 30 min
4

Below the Clock Tower, Sardar Market is Jodhpur's beating heart—spices (Rajasthani red chili, turmeric), lac bangles, tie-dye textiles and miniatures. If you're seeking the city's best lassi, follow the locals—the most popular stalls are always the most crowded.

⏱ 60 min
5

Push on to Toorji Ka Jhalra, an 18th-century stepwell recently restored, hidden among the old city houses. Less monumental than Bundi's, but the turquoise water and geometric stairs make it an unexpected photo spot.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Operators offer zipline descents above Mehrangarh's ramparts and lakes—6 lines spanning 1.2 km with plunging views over the blue city and fort. Thrills guaranteed. Book the day before.

⏱ 90 min
$$$9.4The Stepwell Hotel

Jodhpur

★★★★☆
1

Mandore Gardens, 9 km north of Jodhpur, shelter the cenotaphs of the Marwar rulers in a shaded park. The dewals (funerary temples) in red sandstone are carved with Hindu figures. The place is frequented by local families in the morning—a good glimpse of daily life away from tourist circuits. Watch for langurs, which are numerous and bold.

⏱ 60 min
2

Rao Jodha Desert Rock Park, at the foot of Mehrangarh, is a 72-hectare ecological reserve created to restore desert vegetation. Marked trails traverse a landscape of volcanic rocks and endemic plants—euphorbias, commiphoras, acacias. If you enjoy walking, it's the perfect complement to the fort visit, with different views of the ramparts.

⏱ 75 min
3

Umaid Bhawan Palace, part luxury hotel and part royal family residence, merits at least an outside glance. Built between 1929 and 1943, it's one of the last great palaces constructed in India—347 rooms, in sandstone and marble. The interior museum displays Art Deco collections and palace models.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Set out on an excursion to the Bishnoi villages, 30 km from Jodhpur. The Bishnoi are a community renowned for their nature protection since the 15th century—they live in harmony with local wildlife (antelopes, gazelles, demoiselle cranes). Several operators offer half-day circuits that also include visits to potter and weaver artisans.

⏱ 180 min
$$$9.4The Stepwell Hotel

Jaisalmer

★★★★☆
1

Jaisalmer Fort is one of the world's few 'living forts'—around 3,000 people still inhabit its walls. Built in 1156 by Rawal Jaisal on Trikuta Hill, its yellow sandstone ramparts turn golden at sunset, merging with the Thar Desert. The fort has been a UNESCO site since 2013. Inside: merchant lanes, Jain temples from the 12th to 15th centuries with sculptures of extraordinary delicacy, the Raj Mahal (royal palace) and desert panoramas from the bastions. Jaisalmer is twinned with Carcassonne—the other great inhabited fortified city.

⏱ 150 min
2

Jaisalmer's havelis are among Rajasthan's most ornate. Patwon ki Haveli—a cluster of five mansions built in the 19th century by Bâniyâ merchants—is the most spectacular, with facades carved like stone lace. Salim Singh ki Haveli is distinguished by its peacock-shaped roof. Nathmal ki Haveli was built by two architect brothers who each worked on one half, without a common plan—the result is harmonious but asymmetrical.

⏱ 75 min
3

Gadisar Lake, at the south gate of the city, was dug in 1367 by Maharawal Gadsi Singh and long served as Jaisalmer's only source of drinking water. The entrance passes under a sandstone arch adorned with sculptures. In winter, migratory birds settle here. Boats are available for a tranquil late-afternoon ride.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In late afternoon, head toward the Sam dunes, 42 km from Jaisalmer, for a camel ride through the Thar Desert. Sunset over the dunes is the Rajasthan highlight. Desert camps offer dinner under the stars and Manganiyar folk music—the hereditary desert musicians. Book ahead in high season.

⏱ 180 min
$$$9.51st Gate Home- Fusion

Agra

★★★★★
1

Transfer day Jaisalmer → Agra on domestic flight via Jaipur or Delhi (allow 4 to 5 hours door-to-door with the layover). No direct flight exists—plan an early morning departure. On arrival, visit the 'Baby Taj'—the tomb of Itimad-ud-Daulah, built between 1622 and 1628 by Empress Nur Jahan. It's the first Mughal monument entirely in white marble inlaid with semi-precious stones (pietra dura), a technique later used at the Taj Mahal. Far less crowded than its bigger sibling, it's remarkably refined.

⏱ 60 min
2

Agra Fort, built in red sandstone by Emperor Akbar from 1565, is a 38-hectare fortified city on the banks of the Yamuna. This is where Shah Jahan, builder of the Taj Mahal, was imprisoned by his son Aurangzeb for 8 years—he could glimpse his wife's mausoleum from his cell window. Don't miss the Diwan-i-Khas and the Musamman Burj (the octagonal tower from which Shah Jahan gazed at the Taj).

⏱ 90 min
3

Late in the day, cross the Yamuna to Mehtab Bagh (Moonlight Garden), right across from the Taj Mahal. This is the finest vantage point for photographing the Taj at sunset—the mausoleum's symmetry with its river reflection is perfect from here. The place is also much calmer than the Taj's esplanade.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Guided bicycle tour through Agra's countryside in late afternoon, with viewpoints of the Taj from the Yamuna banks. Several operators offer 2-hour circuits passing through villages, fields and potters' quarters—a striking contrast to the monumentality of the Mughal sites.

⏱ 120 min
$$$9.5Inara House

Agra

★★★★★
1

The Taj Mahal at dawn—wake up early, it's worth it. The mausoleum opens at sunrise, when the light shifts from pink to white on the marble. Built between 1632 and 1653 by Shah Jahan for his wife Mumtaz Mahal (who died giving birth to their 14th child), it engaged 20,000 artisans. The white marble from Rajasthan is inlaid with 28 types of semi-precious stones from across the ancient world—carnelian from Arabia, lapis lazuli from Afghanistan, turquoise from Tibet. The Mughal gardens in four parts (charbagh) are a model of the genre. Closed Fridays. Allow 2 hours.

⏱ 120 min
2

After the Taj, lose yourself in Kinari Bazaar, Agra's liveliest market—a stone's throw from Jama Masjid. It's an organized chaos of embroideries, mojari shoes, petha (local pumpkin candy), and spices. The streets are narrow and packed—leave valuables at the hotel.

⏱ 60 min
3

If you have time, make the detour to Fatehpur Sikri, 37 km from Agra. This Mughal ghost town, built by Akbar between 1571 and 1585 as his new capital, was abandoned after just 14 years—probably due to water shortage. The five-story Panch Mahal, Jama Masjid mosque and Salim Chishti's tomb (in pierced white marble) justify the trip. UNESCO listed.

⏱ 150 min

Activities

Attend a marble inlay workshop (pietra dura) at one of Agra's artisan studios. The technique used to decorate the Taj Mahal is still practiced—they'll show you how semi-precious stones are cut and inlaid into marble. Beware commercial scams, however—stick to workshops recommended by your accommodation.

⏱ 60 min
$$$9.5Inara House
🚗Drive to next stop: 230 km · 4h30

New Delhi

★★★★★
1

The Red Fort of Delhi (Lal Qila), built between 1638 and 1648 by Shah Jahan when he transferred his capital from Agra to Delhi, is a vast UNESCO red sandstone complex. Its 2.5 km walls rise to 33 meters. Inside: the Diwan-i-Am (public audience hall), the Diwan-i-Khas (private hall with silver ceiling, now vanished), and Mughal gardens. It's from these ramparts that the Prime Minister delivers the annual Independence Day speech.

⏱ 90 min
2

Chandni Chowk is Old Delhi's main artery, laid out in the 17th century by Shah Jahan's daughter. It's a deluge of rickshaws, tangled electrical cables, frying smells and merchant shouts. Khari Baoli, at the street's end, is Asia's largest spice market—the air is saturated with turmeric, chili and cardamom. Expect to cough. Explore the small side lanes (galis) too, each specializing in one product: paper, fabrics, jewelry, books.

⏱ 90 min
3

Jama Masjid, a stone's throw from Chandni Chowk, is India's largest mosque—built by Shah Jahan between 1644 and 1656. Its courtyard can hold 25,000 worshippers. Climb the south minaret for a 360° view over Old Delhi—the steps are steep and narrow. Remove your shoes and cover your shoulders before entering.

⏱ 45 min
4

Gurudwara Bangla Sahib, Delhi's main Sikh temple, is open to all. Cover your head (scarves are lent at the entrance) and remove your shoes. The sacred pool (sarovar), the prayer hall with continuous chanting, and especially the langar—the free canteen serving thousands of meals daily to anyone who shows up, regardless of religion—are a moving experience.

⏱ 60 min
5

If you have time before leaving Delhi, head to Humayun's Tomb—built in 1570, it's the first great Mughal garden-tomb, and the direct model for the Taj Mahal. A UNESCO site since 1993. The white marble and red sandstone dome, surrounded by symmetrical charbagh gardens, is superb in late afternoon.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Take a cycle-rickshaw to cross Chandni Chowk—it's the only way to navigate the chaos without losing your mind. The ride is an adventure in itself: negotiate the price before boarding and enjoy the spectacle. Operators also offer guided food walks through Old Delhi, with stops at legendary street food stalls—paranthas, jalebis, kebabs.
