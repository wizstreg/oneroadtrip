Adelaide

★★★★☆
1

Adelaide is a grid city - literally. In 1836, Colonel William Light designed it as a perfect square of streets surrounded by 7 km² of park lands on all sides, a green belt that still separates the city from its suburbs today. It was the only Australian capital founded by free settlers, no convicts, and the German Lutheran identity stuck: schnitzel, bratwurst, church spires. Start on North Terrace, the cultural mile. The Art Gallery of South Australia has a solid colonial and contemporary collection - and it's free. So is the South Australian Museum next door, which holds one of the world's major Aboriginal ethnographic collections. Allow 2h for both.

⏱ 120 min
2

Adelaide Central Market has been running since 1869 and still fits 80 stalls under one roof - one of the largest covered markets in the southern hemisphere. Oysters from Coffin Bay, local cheese, German mettwurst and bratwurst from the old Barossa food tradition, honey, pastries. This is where Adelaide shops, not just tourists. Best mid-morning when the stalls are full and the coffee queues are human.

⏱ 90 min
3

Peel Street and Leigh Street are the two laneways where Adelaide actually goes out. Both are narrow, low-key, and lined with bars and restaurants that change constantly. Come here at dusk to see which tables are filling up - that's your dinner shortlist. Press Food and Wine on Waymouth Street does an all-Australian wine list by the glass: good way to calibrate your Riesling baseline before the Barossa.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Spend the market visit eating - Coffin Bay oysters, a slice of Schuler's mettwurst, a flat white. This is not a detour, it's the point. Adelaide Central Market is one of the genuine pleasures of South Australian food culture and worth 90 minutes of slow grazing.

⏱ 90 min

Order a glass of Eden Valley Riesling at dinner - dry, mineral, nothing like the German sweet version. This is the wine that will surprise you most in South Australia and it costs almost nothing in its young form. Consider it a palate warm-up for the week ahead.

⏱ 60 min

Adelaide

★★★★☆
1

Thirty minutes east of the city, the road climbs into the Adelaide Hills and the temperature drops 5-7°C. You've left Mediterranean heat behind for something closer to Burgundy: morning fog, steep slopes, cool-climate Chardonnay and Pinot Noir. Shaw + Smith was founded in 1989 by cousins Martin Shaw and Michael Hill Smith - the first Australian Master of Wine. Their M3 Chardonnay is a single-site wine that makes a strong case for the Hills as a serious cool-climate zone. Tasting runs 25-50 AUD. The architecture is sharp and minimal, no heritage kitsch.

⏱ 90 min
2

Hahndorf is the oldest continuously operating German village in Australia, founded in 1839 by Prussian Lutheran settlers. The main street is lined with linden trees, half-timbered cottages, and a pub - The German Arms Hotel - that has been serving beer since the same year. During both World Wars, anti-German feeling was so intense that most German place names in South Australia were anglicised. Hahndorf was briefly renamed Ambleside. It went back.

⏱ 60 min
3

Penfolds Magill Estate is the company's founding property - planted by Dr Christopher Penfold himself in 1844, before the Barossa operation existed. The Make Your Own Blend experience lets you try assembling a Shiraz at the tasting bench, which is more educational than it sounds: you start to feel the difference between warmth, tannin, and fruit balance in a glass rather than reading about it. Budget 100-200 AUD for the experience.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Lunch in Hahndorf at The German Arms Hotel - schnitzel, bratwurst, German beer. This pub opened in 1839 and is still the social centre of the village. Book a table or eat at the bar. The food is straightforward, the atmosphere is genuine, and the courtyard is shaded.

⏱ 75 min

At Bird in Hand, skip the standard tasting and head to the garden if the weather holds. The winery sits on the site of a 19th-century gold mine - the name comes from the old shaft. Order the Mr Bird (their flagship blend) and sit outside with a cheese plate. It's the most relaxed cellar door in the Hills.

⏱ 60 min

Tanunda

★★★★☆
1

The Penfolds Barossa Cellar holds something that exists nowhere else in Australian wine: a complete run of every Grange since the first experimental 1951 vintage. Max Schubert made that first batch in secret - the Penfolds board had ordered him to stop after tasting the 1951, calling it 'unpalatable'. He kept going, hidden in the underground cellars at Magill. By 1962, the 1955 Grange had won its first gold medal. Today the 1951 changes hands at auction for over AU$140,000 a bottle. The standard Cellar Door tour (90-120 AUD) covers the history and includes premium pours. The Grange Tasting (around 600 AUD) is the experience of the week - book 2 months ahead.

⏱ 150 min
2

Henschke is a 6th-generation family winery, established in 1868, now run by Stephen and Prue Henschke at Keyneton in the Eden Valley sub-zone. Their Hill of Grace Shiraz comes from a single block planted around 1860 - roughly 8,000 bottles per year, priced around 600-1,200 AUD at the cellar door. It is, alongside Grange, one of two wines that defined the idea of Australian fine wine for international collectors. The Mount Edelstone Shiraz (also single-site, 1912 planting) is more accessible and still extraordinary.

⏱ 120 min
3

Tanunda itself is the heart of the Barossa - a tight grid of 19th-century stone buildings, Lutheran churches, and the Barossa Farmers Market (Saturday mornings, buy local produce, charcuterie, breads). Walk the main street slowly: the historical texture is intact and the scale is human.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Lunch at Hentley Farm Restaurant, 10 minutes from Tanunda - one of the best kitchens in the Barossa, using produce from the estate and surrounds. The menu changes with the season. Booking essential.

⏱ 90 min

Evening stroll through Tanunda's Lutheran churches. There are four within walking distance of the main street, all 19th century, all built by German settlers who had fled religious persecution in Prussia. The largest, St John's, was built in 1868. The architectural consistency of the village - stone, timber, brick - is unlike anywhere else in Australia.

⏱ 40 min

Tanunda

★★★★☆
1

One acre of gnarly old vines at the heart of the Langmeil property - that's the Freedom Vineyard, planted in 1843 by Christian Auricht, a Prussian blacksmith who fled religious persecution and settled in the Barossa. The vines are classified as 'Ancestor Vines' under the Barossa Old Vine Charter, a category requiring 125+ years of age. They were almost lost - when the Lindner family took over the derelict property in 1996, the vineyard was tangled and untended. It took several vintages of careful pruning to recover. The Freedom 1843 Shiraz made from these vines is around 200 AUD a bottle. The tour lets you stand in the actual vineyard and hear the whole story.

⏱ 90 min
2

Seppeltsfield was founded in 1851 by Joseph Seppelt, a Silesian tobacco farmer who switched to grapes after arriving in the Barossa. His son Benno built the bluestone maturation cellar in 1878 and made a decision that still runs today: one barrel of vintage Tawny would be set aside every year, untouched for 100 years. The Centennial Cellar now holds an unbroken lineage of every vintage from 1878 to the present - the only collection of its kind on earth. The current release is the 1926, and the 100ml flask costs around AUD 1,650. Come here if you're celebrating a birthday - you can taste a Tawny from the year you were born, direct from the barrel.

⏱ 120 min
3

Eden Valley sits above the Barossa at 400-500m altitude, 5-10°C cooler, and it produces the best Riesling in Australia - dry, mineral, with natural acidity that lets it age 20 years in bottle. Yalumba, founded in 1849 and still family-owned (the Hill-Smith family), is the benchmark producer. Their Eden Valley Riesling is a reference point. Pewsey Vale, also in the Hills, is the alternative if you prefer a walk through the vineyard.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

The Centennial Collection tasting at Seppeltsfield is unlike any other wine experience in Australia. You don't compare vintages of the same young wine - you compare 100-year-old Tawnies, each shaped by the particular season of its harvest. The 3% annual evaporation (the 'angel's share') means the oldest wines are intensely concentrated. The chief winemaker Fiona Donald says she limits herself to 10 at a sitting before the intensity becomes overwhelming.

⏱ 90 min

Lunch at the Seppeltsfield property - the 1919 Appellation restaurant or a picnic on the grounds. The estate is large enough to walk: palm-lined alleys, heritage buildings, gardens. It's one of the most photogenic wine properties in the country.

⏱ 75 min

Mclaren Vale

★★★★☆
1

d'Arenberg Cube is the most talked-about building in McLaren Vale - a five-storey cube where each face is rotated at a different angle, designed by winery owner Chester Osborn and opened in 2017. Inside: a maze, immersive art installations, and a restaurant at the top with views over the vines to Gulf St Vincent. The wines are serious: The Dead Arm Shiraz (old dry-grown vines, named after a vine disease) and The Coppermine Road Cabernet Sauvignon have been benchmarks for McLaren Vale for decades. Touristique, but well done.

⏱ 90 min
2

Wirra Wirra is the quiet alternative to d'Arenberg - more relaxed, genuine garden, petanque court on-site (yes, really). Founded in 1894, derelict for decades, revived in 1969 by Greg Trott. The Church Block (Shiraz-Cabernet-Merlot blend) has been made since the 1970s and is probably McLaren Vale's most familiar label internationally. The Angelus Cabernet Sauvignon is the prestige cuvée.

⏱ 75 min
3

Yangarra Estate works in biodynamics and focuses on varieties that love the McLaren Vale heat: Grenache on old vines (some planted 1946), Mourvèdre, Cinsault. The Old Vine Grenache is one of the most honest expressions of the variety in Australia - no sugar, no overextraction, just old-vine intensity. S.C. Pannell nearby is worth a stop if time allows.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Lunch at The Star of Greece in Port Willunga, 10 minutes west - a restaurant perched on a cliff above the beach, open since the 1970s, serving local seafood and Gulf St Vincent fish. Order the oysters if they're on. The view is the reason you're here. Book ahead on weekends.

⏱ 90 min

Late afternoon at Aldinga Beach or Sellicks Beach - long, uncrowded, Southern Ocean water. McLaren Vale is 15km from the sea, which is part of why the Shiraz is slightly fresher here than in the Barossa. Swim or walk the cliff path north from Sellicks.

⏱ 75 min

Mclaren Vale

★★★★☆
1

Coriole has been making Shiraz in McLaren Vale since 1967, but it's the Italian varieties that set it apart: Sangiovese, Fiano, Vermentino - planted by the Lloyd family in the 1980s when this was genuinely eccentric. Today they're the reference point for Italian varieties in South Australia. The original stone buildings and shaded courtyard make it one of the most pleasant cellar doors in the region.

⏱ 75 min
2

Hugh Hamilton produces some of the best-value wines in McLaren Vale - the Black Ops Shiraz and The Mongrel (red blend) are both under 30 AUD and overdeliver for the price. Family-run, no pretension. The tasting room staff are knowledgeable without being formal.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Fleurieu Peninsula coast between Maslin Beach and Sellicks Beach is a continuous stretch of cliff and sand that most visitors skip because they're focused on wineries. Wrong instinct: the walks above the cliff give views over the Gulf St Vincent on one side and olive groves on the other. Maslin Beach is South Australia's first official clothing-optional beach - noted for context, not as a recommendation.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Morning cooking or wine blending class at a McLaren Vale producer - several offer this. It's the bridge between visiting a cellar door and understanding what you're actually tasting. Coriole and Kay Brothers both run versions. Two hours, roughly 100 AUD.

⏱ 120 min

Dinner at Salopian Inn - the best kitchen in McLaren Vale, in a 19th-century stone building. The wine list is almost entirely the region, and the kitchen uses local producers seriously. Book two days ahead minimum.

⏱ 90 min
