Paraguay: Jesuit Missions, Waterfalls & Red Earth in 11 Days

In 11 days, this circuit crosses Paraguay from its capital founded in 1537 to Iguazú Falls viewed from three countries, lingering at the two UNESCO Guaraní Jesuit missions—Trinidad and Jesús de Tavarangüé—and at South America's only colonial astronomical observatory. Asuncion

★★★☆☆
1

Asunción's historic center unfolds around Plaza de Armas, surrounded by the Cabildo (now a cultural center and museum), the Metropolitan Cathedral, and the former National Congress building. Beneath this square, archaeologists have uncovered the foundations of the first colonial governors' houses—walk slowly and imagine that everything began here when the city was founded in 1537. The Cabildo houses the National Museum with collections tracing Paraguayan history; allow 45 to 60 minutes to explore it thoroughly.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Panteón Nacional de los Héroes is Asunción's most symbolic building. Francisco Solano López ordered its construction in 1863 as an oratory dedicated to the Virgin, but the War of the Triple Alliance (1864-1870) halted work for more than seventy years—the building sat on its scaffolding while Paraguay recovered from a war that cost roughly 70% of its population. Inspired by the Panthéon in Paris, it was finally inaugurated on October 12, 1936, to house the remains of Marshal Francisco Solano López, unknown soldiers, and other national figures. The building marks kilometer zero for all of Paraguay's national highways. Admission is free—don't miss the flag-raising ceremony at 5:45 a.m. if you're an early riser.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Palacio de López, the presidential residence overlooking the Río Paraguay, deserves a stroll around the Costanera esplanade. Construction began in 1857 under English architect Alonso Taylor, commissioned by Francisco Solano López as a private residence—he never slept there: the war forced him to leave Asunción before its completion, and the Brazilian navy bombarded the building in 1868. Elected first among the seven treasures of Asunción's cultural heritage in 2009, it impresses with its Italianate neoclassical style, columned façades, and river-facing front. Security restrictions prevent entry, but the view from the Costanera at day's end, with the building illuminated, is sumptuous.

⏱ 25 min

Activities

The Loma San Jerónimo neighborhood has been Asunción's first official "tourist neighborhood" since 2013—and quite possibly its most photogenic. Perched on a hill from which Jeronimite monks once watched over the bay entrance during colonial times, this maze of colorful lanes, brightly painted houses, and street murals feels like a blend of Cinque Terre and a Guaraní village. The 45-step mosaic staircase (Escalinata San Jerónimo), decorated by artist Claudia Godí, leads to the neighborhood's lookout point—the view over Asunción and the Río Paraguay is exceptional. Avoid weekday mid-afternoons: everything shuts down in the heat. In the evening, bars and restaurants come alive.

⏱ 90 min

Asunción's Costanera, a long waterfront promenade along the Río Paraguay, is the perfect place to end the day. The setting sun tints the Palacio de López façade orange, joggers share the path with families pushing strollers, and a few vendors selling chipa (the Guaraní manioc bread) hawk their trays. Allow 45 minutes for an easy stroll with views across the river.

⏱ 45 min

Asuncion

★★★☆☆
1

The Museo del Barro—officially the Centro de Artes Visuales—is one of the most singular museums in Latin America. Founded in 1979 by Carlos Colombino, Ysanne Gayet, and Osvaldo Salerno during the Stroessner dictatorship, it established itself as both a cultural counterweight to the regime and an exhibition space. Its collection brings together more than 10,000 pieces across three museums under one roof: the Museo del Barro (mestizo popular art from the 17th century onward, ceramics from pueblos like Itá and Tobatí), the Museo de Arte Indígena (some 2,500 pieces of baskets, masks, feathered ornaments, and pottery from Guaraní ethnic groups), and the Museo Paraguayo de Arte Contemporáneo (Paraguay's only permanent collection of contemporary painting, engraving, and sculpture). Admission is free on Friday and Saturday; other days cost a few guaraníes. Allow at least two hours.

⏱ 90 min
2

In Areguá, a town founded in 1538 just 28 km from Asunción on the shores of Lake Ypacaraí, the historic center deserves an hour of wandering. Avenida del Lago, declared National Heritage in 1997, slopes down from the Church of the Virgen de la Candelaria (built in 1862, colonial baroque style) to the municipal beach on the lake—from its atrium, the view over Lake Ypacaraí alone justifies the trip. Along the way, you'll pass old colonial houses with wide verandas that once belonged to Asunción families who spent summers by the lake. Since 2019, Areguá has been recognized as a UNESCO Creative City in the handicrafts and folk art category.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The excursion to Areguá (round-trip from Asunción, about 30 km) is also worth it for its pottery workshops. The pottery tradition reportedly dates back to a craftsman named Ricardo Pérez who introduced it; today, dozens of workshops line the main avenue—you can step inside, watch potters spin their black clay, and leave with a piece wrapped in newspaper. The Centro Artesanal de la Cuenca brings together the region's best artisans. In August, the town hosts the Feria de la Frutilla: since the 1920s, strawberries from the Compañía Estanzuela gardens have spread throughout the region and become the local gastronomic specialty—they're eaten in tarts, jams, licor, lakeside.

⏱ 120 min

Encarnacion

★★★☆☆
1

Make a stop in Yaguarón, 48 km south of Asunción, to visit the Franciscan church of San Buenaventura—one of the most beautiful on the continent and probably Paraguay's least-known jewel. Construction of this temple began in 1755 and was completed in 1772 under the direction of Portuguese sculptor José de Souza Cavadas, assisted by Guaraní artisans trained at the Franciscan mission. The exterior in white adobe displays Franciscan simplicity. Push open the door and brace yourself for the shock: the 14-meter-high, 6-meter-wide altarpiece explodes with polychrome baroque wood carvings in peterebi, covered in vegetal gold and silver—the black comes from ñandypa, the red from urucú, the green from yryburetyma. Every figure tells an episode in the story of Christian redemption. The ensemble has been designated National Cultural Heritage and is a candidate for UNESCO inscription.

⏱ 45 min
2

Paraguarí is a brief roadside stop on RN1: a quiet central square, kampala (dried meat) shops, and the rural atmosphere of deep Paraguay. Nothing extraordinary to see, but it's a chance to feel the countryside rhythm and buy cold tereré from a street vendor. Twenty minutes is enough.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

The RN1 highway from Asunción to Encarnación (about 370 km, 5.5 hours' drive) cuts through characteristic Paraguayan landscapes: brick-red earth, intensely green pastures, estancias with wooden fences, and soybean fields stretching to the horizon. Take the road early in the morning to see lapacho trees in bloom (September-October) or cotton fields in spring. Arrive in Encarnación—the "Pearl of the South"—in late afternoon for a first stroll along the Costanera beside the Río Paraná.

⏱ 330 min
$$$10Guest House 3

Encarnacion

★★★☆☆
1

The ruins of Santísima Trinidad del Paraná are the largest and best-preserved of Paraguay's Jesuit missions—allow at least two and a half hours. Founded in 1706 by Jesuit Juan Bautista Prímoli (a Milanese architect), the mission had 3,000 Guaraníes by 1728. Inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List in 1993, it preserves the footprint of a true city-state: plaza mayor, grand church whose nave with its planned dome would have reached three times the current height, school, workshops, indigenous houses, and cemetery. Look for the frieze of musical angels—a carved panel depicting clavichords, organs, trumpets, and Paraguayan harps, the only known visual testimony of Jesuit music before the discovery of the Moxos scores (Bolivia). The archaeological museum installed in the former sacristy houses Guaraní sculptures and a model of the mission at its height.

⏱ 150 min
2

Jesús de Tavarangüé, about ten kilometers from Trinidad, is the other mission inscribed on UNESCO's list in 1993 and the only one whose architecture features Mudéjar-style trefoil arches—an Arab-Christian influence found nowhere else in the region's Jesuit missions. Founded in 1685, it was under construction when the Jesuits were expelled from Spanish America in 1767: the church remained unfinished, allowing you to see the stone scaffolding just as it was left abandoned. In the evening (Friday through Sunday, schedule varies by season), a 3D video-mapping show is projected onto the ruins—if you're there at the right moment, it's a striking experience.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Interpretation Center at Trinidad explains the political and religious context of the 30 Guaraní Jesuit missions that stretched across present-day Paraguay, Argentina, Brazil, and Uruguay between 1609 and 1768. The panels recount how the Jesuits protected the Guaraníes from the encomienda slavery system by creating self-sufficient societies with their own agriculture, music, printing press, and even astronomy. Allow 30 minutes.

⏱ 30 min
$$$10Guest House 3

Encarnacion

★★★☆☆
1

San Cosme y Damián merits a 90-km detour from Encarnación for two unique reasons. First, its former Jesuit college—the only two-story building preserved among all 30 Jesuit missions in South America, with original ceiling paintings still visible. Second, and above all, its astronomical observatory: Father Buenaventura Suárez arrived at this mission in 1703 and built, with the help of Guaraní artisans, a telescope and solar quadrant carved from quartz extracted from the Río Paraná's basalt quarries. His observations, published in the "Lunario de un siglo" (more than 200 pages), astonished European universities with their precision. He was the southern hemisphere's first astronomer to conduct systematic measurements of the celestial vault. Today, the Astronomical Interpretation Center inaugurated in 2010 bears his name and offers nighttime telescope sessions—book in advance if you're staying overnight.

⏱ 120 min
2

Encarnación's Costanera stretches along the Río Paraná facing the Argentine city of Posadas. In summer (December-March), its developed urban beaches become one of Paraguay's most popular attractions. In any season, the late-afternoon promenade is pleasant: the river is wide at this point, the sunsets are spectacular, and the city comes alive with snack vendors and families.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Encarnación's downtown explores easily on foot. Stop by the municipal market to taste local specialties—vorí-vorí (corn dumpling soup), Paraguayan asado, and tereré (cold mate with yerba mate) are unmissable. The city was largely rebuilt after the Yacyretá Dam was filled in the 1990s, which flooded part of the old historic center.

⏱ 90 min

Ciudad Del Este

★★★☆☆
1

The Cataratas del Monday are the Triple Frontier region's big surprise. These falls on the Río Monday—a tributary of the Paraná—reach 40 to 45 meters in height across 120 meters of width, split into three main cascades, and are framed by dense tropical vegetation. Since TripAdvisor confirmed their reputation in 2017 as Paraguay's most popular attraction, the park has developed serious infrastructure: four lookout points, a 40-meter panoramic elevator (inaugurated in 2015, the tallest in Mercosur), forest trails, and a zip line. The Guaraní name "Monday" means "water that falls." Allow 2 hours on site and bring a waterproof jacket: you'll come out wet.

⏱ 120 min
2

Lago de la República, a small urban park in Ciudad del Este's center, is the calmest spot in an otherwise frenetic city. A 20-minute loop around the lake is enough to grasp the border town pulsing to the rhythm of international trade.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Ruta 6 from Encarnación to Ciudad del Este (about 300 km, 4.5 hours) crosses eastern Paraguay's agricultural heartland: soybean plantations to the horizon, glossy-leaved yerba mate groves, and a few estancias behind rusted gates. Ciudad del Este itself merits a brief evening exploration: it's one of Latin America's most important free-trade zones, a chaotic and fascinating border city where Paraguayans, Brazilians, Chinese, and Lebanese mingle.

⏱ 270 min

Ciudad Del Este

★★★☆☆
1

Iguazú National Park on the Argentine side (founded in 1934) is, by general consensus, the more immersive side of the falls. The Upper Circuit (1,750 m, flat) offers plunging views from atop the cascades: you look down, the water falls, and birds—toucans and swifts—wheel at eye level. Start here early in the morning for the light and the absence of crowds. Arrive at the park at opening—organized tour groups pour in from 9 a.m. onward.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Lower Circuit (1,400 m) descends to the riverbank and brings you close to the falls from below—you'll emerge soaked but happy. From the dock, a free boat takes you to Isla San Martín, from which the trail yields the closest views of several falls. Don't forget a change of clothes.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Garganta del Diablo (Devil's Throat) is the visit's apotheosis: a 1,100-meter walkway crosses the Río Iguazú above calm water to emerge at a balcony overlooking the 80-meter-high main cascade. The water drops in one continuous white wall, the noise is deafening, and the rising mist soaks everyone within seconds. The Tren Ecológico de la Selva (included in the ticket) runs to the Garganta station—save this circuit for late afternoon when the light softens. The walkway was entirely rebuilt after the historic October 2023 flood that swept away 90% of its length.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Crossing the Paraguay-Argentina border via the International Bridge takes place by car or taxi; customs formalities usually last 30 to 60 minutes. Keep passports handy and avoid Paraguayan and Argentine public holidays, when queues lengthen. The park day requires a minimum of 5 to 6 hours on site: don't leave before 2 p.m. if you want to see the Garganta.

⏱ 60 min
$$$9.6Elegante, sofisticado, acogedor, moderno, turismo

Ciudad Del Este

★★★☆☆
1

Iguaçu National Park on the Brazilian side offers a totally different angle from yesterday's: here, the panoramic walkway runs along the base of the falls for about 1,200 meters, placing you facing the amphitheater of 275 cascades deployed in an arc. If Argentina immerses you in the falls, Brazil gives you the overview—this is the viewpoint that appears on all the postcards. Allow a minimum of two and a half hours, including the walkway, viewpoints, and, if available, the boat that approaches the foot of the Garganta do Diabo.

⏱ 150 min

Activities

Crossing the Paraguay-Brazil border via the Friendship Bridge (Puente de la Amistad) is the Triple Frontier's liveliest border experience. This 552-meter bridge over the Río Paraná connects Ciudad del Este to Foz do Iguaçu and supports one of South America's busiest road border crossings. Formalities can take anywhere from 30 minutes to several hours depending on the day—avoid weekends. Returning to Paraguay at day's end, soak in Ciudad del Este's atmosphere: shops open late, a bustling covered market, and gastronomy blending Paraguayan, Brazilian, and Asian cuisines.

Asuncion

★★★☆☆
1

Coronel Oviedo, the strategic crossroads of eastern Paraguay's highways, merits a 20- to 30-minute stop to refuel and quickly visit the cathedral and central square. It's a lively agricultural town—market, motorcycles, mango vendors—and a window onto rural Paraguay, far from tourist circuits.

⏱ 20 min
2

Caacupé, 54 km from Asunción, is Paraguay's spiritual capital and home to the Basilica of Our Lady of Miracles. Devotion to the Virgen de los Milagros—nicknamed "la Virgencita Azul"—dates back to a 17th-century legend: a Guaraní artisan reportedly hid behind a tree trunk to escape Mbayá warriors and promised to carve the Virgin from that same trunk if he survived. The wooden statue, about fifty centimeters tall, is preserved in the basilica, whose current construction began in 1945 and was consecrated in 2006. Every December 8, up to two million pilgrims converge on Caacupé on foot from across Paraguay. The basilica is open for visits year-round—30 to 40 minutes is enough.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

The drive from Ciudad del Este to Asunción (about 330 km, 5 hours) via Coronel Oviedo and Caacupé crosses the geographic heart of eastern Paraguay. Leave early in the morning to enjoy both stops without rushing and arrive in Asunción in late afternoon with energy to spare for a final stroll through the historic center.

Asuncion

★★★☆☆
1

Manzana de la Rivera, directly across from the Palacio de López, is one of Asunción's few restored colonial ensembles. These are nine houses from the 18th and 19th centuries—the oldest, Casa Viola, dates to 1750-1758—gathered around interior courtyards. Saved from demolition in 1989 by architecture students, they now house the Museo Memoria de la Ciudad (an old colonial gate, a map of Asunción drawn by Félix de Azara in 1787, ceramics, and archives), the Museo del Arpa, and several temporary exhibition halls. The terrace of bar Casa Clari offers an unbeatable view of the Palacio de López—one of the capital's best photo spots. Free admission.

⏱ 60 min
2

Cerro Lambaré, a few kilometers from downtown, is the hill from which you'll get the most complete panorama over Asunción and the course of the Río Paraguay. The site also has historical value: the Carios, a Guaraní people, had established a hamlet here before the Spanish conquistadors arrived in 1537. Allow 30 minutes for the climb and the views.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

For last-minute shopping, head to Mercado La Recova (handicrafts: ñandutí, the extraordinary spiderweb-like lace originating in Itauguá, ao po'i embroidered fabric, Paraguayan leather goods) or the shops along Avenida Mariscal López. Bagged yerba mate, tropical fruit liqueurs, and Guaraní wooden figurines make excellent souvenirs. After shopping, head to Silvio Pettirossi International Airport, about fifteen kilometers from downtown—allow 30 to 45 minutes depending on traffic.

⏱ 90 min
