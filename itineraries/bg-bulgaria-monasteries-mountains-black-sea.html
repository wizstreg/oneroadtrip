Sofia

★★★★☆
1

The Alexander Nevsky Cathedral presides over its namesake square with golden and turquoise domes that dominate the Sofia skyline. Built in neo-Byzantine style to honour Russian soldiers who fell in the 1877–1878 Liberation War, it ranks among the largest Orthodox cathedrals in Europe and serves as the seat of the Bulgarian Patriarch.

⏱ 60 min
2

Saint George's Rotunda — a squat red-brick drum dating from the 4th century — is Sofia's oldest standing building. Roman engineers raised it during the imperial era, and its walls carry medieval frescoes layered in three successive campaigns, spanning twelve centuries of religious painting. The rotunda stands at the heart of the excavated ruins of ancient Ulpia Serdica.

⏱ 50 min
3

The Boyana Church, on Sofia's southern outskirts, comprises three attached buildings from the 10th to the 19th century. Its 1259 frescoes — 89 scenes, 240 figures — are counted among the masterpieces of medieval European art. The donors' portraits of Kaloyan and Desislava, strikingly naturalistic for their time, anticipate the Italian Renaissance by a full century. UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1979.

⏱ 60 min
4

The National History Museum occupies the former official residence of the Communist Party Central Committee — which gives the building a surreal grandeur of its own. Inside, the star exhibit is the Rogozen Treasure, the largest Thracian silver hoard ever found. Collections span 7,000 years of Bulgarian history, from Thracian gold to Soviet tanks parked incongruously in the garden.

⏱ 75 min
5

Unearthed in 2004 beneath the city centre, the Serdica Archaeological Complex exposes the foundations of a 3rd-century amphitheatre capable of seating 25,000 spectators. Parts of it are visible from the metro station below; ongoing excavations continue to uncover forums, bathhouses and paved thoroughfares of the old Roman capital.

⏱ 40 min
6

Stroll down Vitosha Boulevard, Sofia's main pedestrian artery, which runs from the city centre towards the mountain of the same name. Belle Époque facades and neoclassical buildings from the reign of Tsar Ferdinand rub shoulders with Soviet-era apartment blocks — a compressed summary of Bulgarian history in a few hundred metres.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Head up to Vitosha (2,290 m) for a bird's-eye view over Sofia's rooftops. The Simeonovo cable car serves the ski runs in winter; in summer, marked trails from the Dragalevtsi neighbourhood allow a 3–4 hour return hike up to the alpine meadows.

⏱ 240 min

Wander through Kapana — 'the trap' in Bulgarian — Sofia's creative quarter, where galleries, ceramics workshops, cocktail bars and sun-drenched terraces spill through a maze of back lanes behind the central market.

⏱ 60 min

The Museum of Socialist Art lines its garden with the Lenins and Georgi Dimitrovs evicted from public squares after 1989. The outdoor section is free to enter — a quirky but illuminating detour through fifty years of communist iconography.

⏱ 45 min

Rila Monastery

★★★★☆
1

Rila Monastery (Sveti Ivan Rilski), founded in the 10th century by the hermit John of Rila, is the largest and most revered Orthodox monastery in Bulgaria. Cradled at 1,147 metres in the valley of the Rilska River, it served as guardian of Bulgarian culture and language through four centuries of Ottoman rule. UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1983.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Church of the Nativity, rebuilt between 1834 and 1837, is covered in frescoes completed in 1846 by masters Zahari Zograf and Dimitar Zograf. Every centimetre of the exterior arcades is painted. Inside, a gilded iconostasis carved by four craftsmen over five years represents the pinnacle of Bulgarian National Revival art. Photography is not permitted inside.

⏱ 60 min
3

Hrelyu's Tower, standing 23 metres tall, is the oldest surviving part of the complex — built around 1334–1335. Its top floor houses a chapel with fragments of 14th-century frescoes. Open in summer only (June–September).

⏱ 30 min
4

The Monastery Museum holds 600 objects: medieval manuscripts, icons and vestments — but the undisputed centrepiece is Rafail's Cross, a wooden cross measuring 81 by 43 cm on which a monk carved 104 biblical scenes and 650 miniature figures over twelve years. When he completed it in 1802, he had lost his sight.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The cave of Saint John of Rila, an hour's walk from the monastery, is the narrow rocky refuge where the hermit spent the last twenty years of his life. Pilgrims who manage to squeeze their entire body through the natural fissure believe they receive absolution. A 4 km return hike through pine forest.

⏱ 120 min

The monastery bakery sells breads and pastries prepared by the monks each morning. A simple and authentic way to get a sense of everyday monastic life.

⏱ 20 min

Borovets

★★★☆☆
1

The Seven Rila Lakes are a chain of glacial lakes stacked between 2,100 and 2,500 metres in the Rila massif, each with a poetic name: The Tear (the clearest), The Eye (the deepest at 37.5 m), The Kidney (shaped accordingly), The Twins, The Fish Lake, The Trefoil and The Lower Lake. Connected by cascades and streams, they form one of the most iconic alpine circuits in Bulgaria.

⏱ 90 min
2

Mount Musala (2,925 m), the highest peak in the Balkans, is visible from the surrounding ridges. For experienced hikers, the ascent from the lakes is possible in a long day.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Take the chairlift from Panichishte station — a 20-minute ride that drops you right at the start of the Seven Lakes trail. The full loop covers around 9 km with 583 metres of elevation gain. Allow 4 to 5 hours in total.

⏱ 300 min

In mid-August, hikers may witness Paneurhythmy — a meditative circular dance performed on the shores of the Kidney Lake for decades by Bulgarian practitioners of a spiritual movement.

⏱ 60 min

Borovets, a family-friendly resort at the foot of the Rila, offers mountain bike rentals and marked trails for exploring the high-altitude spruce forests.

⏱ 120 min

Bansko

★★★☆☆
1

Bansko's old town is a labyrinth of cobbled lanes lined with thick black-stone houses — the signature architecture of the 18th-century Bulgarian National Revival. Behind their austere facades, inner courtyards bloom with flowers, and traditional taverns (mehanas) hide in vaulted cellars.

⏱ 60 min
2

Sveta Troitsa Church (Holy Trinity), built in 1835, is one of the largest pseudo-basilicas in Bulgaria. Its freestanding bell tower is a monument of national importance. The interior frescoes are the work of the celebrated Samokov School of painting.

⏱ 40 min
3

Pirin National Park, inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List, stretches across 107,000 hectares between 800 and 2,914 metres. It shelters more than 180 glacial lakes, ancient Bosnian pine forests, wolves, bears and chamois. Vihren peak (2,914 m) is Bulgaria's second highest summit.

⏱ 30 min
4

The Baikushev Pine, in the Vihren section of the park, is the oldest and largest tree in Bulgaria. This Bosnian pine, estimated at 1,300 years old and standing 26 metres tall, was discovered by forester Kostadin Baikushev in 1897. Accessible from the road leading to Banderitsa hut.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Hike to Vihren Hut (2,000 m) from the Banderitsa car park, then push on to the summit of Vihren itself. The full ascent is demanding (6 hours return), but the panorama across the Balkans and the Rhodopes is extraordinary. Trails are well-marked in summer.

⏱ 360 min

The Banderishki Lakes Loop (3 glacial lakes, 9 km, 450 m elevation gain) is a more accessible alternative to Vihren. Allow 4 hours and stop at the mountain hut for a bowl of hot soup.

⏱ 240 min

Melnik

★★★★☆
1

Melnik is Bulgaria's smallest town — a few hundred souls — wedged in a canyon of yellow-ochre sandstone. Its National Revival houses, with stone ground floors and overhanging timber upper storeys, form one of the best-preserved architectural ensembles in the country.

⏱ 50 min
2

The Melnik Pyramids rise up to 100 metres above the valley floor. These eroded Miocene sandstone formations — shaped into obelisks, mushrooms and towers — cover 17 km² around the town. Declared a natural monument as far back as 1960, they shelter 113 recorded bird species and a flora with a distinctly Mediterranean character.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Kordopulov House is the largest National Revival mansion in the Balkans, built in the 18th century for a wealthy wine-trading family. Its stained-glass windows, Venetian-style reception hall and wine cellars carved directly into the sandstone are a testament to the thriving trade between the Ottoman Empire and Western Europe.

⏱ 50 min
4

The Boyar's House, in the eastern part of town, is one of the best-preserved medieval buildings in the Balkans. Dating from the 13th century, it formed part of the residence of Despot Alexis Slav. Remnants of mosaic floors and frescoes speak to its former opulence.

⏱ 30 min
5

Rozhen Monastery, 7 km from Melnik, is the largest monastery in the Pirin. Founded in the 13th century under the Second Bulgarian Empire, it preserves medieval frescoes, a carved iconostasis and the atmosphere of a still-living monastic community. A 3-hour hiking trail through the pyramids connects it to Melnik.

⏱ 50 min

Activities

The Melnik–Rozhen Monastery trail (3 hours one way; return by road or taxi is possible) passes through the finest of the sandstone pyramids. The path follows the Melnishka River before climbing between the rock formations up to the monastery.

⏱ 180 min

The Melnik area produces the Shiroka Melnishka Loza grape — found nowhere else on earth. Wine cellars carved into the sandstone offer tastings of bold reds with notes of cherry, tobacco and leather. Villa Melnik, a few kilometres outside town, is among the most acclaimed producers.

⏱ 90 min

Velingrad

★★★☆☆
1

Velingrad has earned its title as the 'Queen of Balkan Spa Towns'. More than 90 mineral springs feed its treatment centres, outdoor pools and spa hotels, with water temperatures ranging from 27 to 91°C depending on the source.

⏱ 30 min
2

Lake Kleptuza is Velingrad's emblem — a natural karst spring that feeds a pool of crystal-clear green water in the heart of the municipal park. Ducks and swans share the water with locals out for an afternoon stroll.

⏱ 40 min
3

Mineral Beach, established in 1937, features two open-air pools fed by thermal springs: a 50-metre cold pool and a 33-metre heated pool. It remains one of the largest public mineral bathing complexes in Bulgaria.

⏱ 90 min
4

Tsepina Fortress, half an hour's drive towards the village of Dorkovo, sits perched on a wooded Rhodope hillside. Nearby, the Pliocene Museum presents the reconstruction of a mastodon whose bones were unearthed in the area — an offbeat and genuinely memorable stop.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Sink into the outdoor thermal pools for a balneotherapy session. Velingrad's spa facilities welcome day visitors — some offer mineral mud treatments and massages using herbs gathered from the surrounding Rhodopes.

⏱ 150 min

The Kleptouzka Gorge trail sets off from Kleptuza Park and follows the wooded canyon for 4 km. Along the way, springs burst straight from the limestone walls.

⏱ 120 min

The Rhodope narrow-gauge railway connects Velingrad to Septemvri on a spectacular 125 km route with 32 tunnels, 97 bridges and breathtaking gradients. A partial trip to Avramovo — the highest inhabited railway station in the Balkans at 1,267 m — takes about 3 hours.

⏱ 180 min

Batak Reservoir

★★☆☆☆
1

Batak Reservoir is a highland lake created between 1954 and 1959 in the western Rhodopes at an altitude of 1,108 m. Covering 22 km², it is one of Bulgaria's largest reservoirs. Its conifer-fringed shores, quiet coves and fish-rich waters make it a favourite retreat for Bulgarians seeking mountain calm.

⏱ 60 min
2

The resort of Tsigov Chark on the south-western shore is the main gateway to the lake. Waterfront guesthouses, jetties and boat rentals set a relaxed tone — Bulgarian mountain holidays at their most unpretentious.

⏱ 45 min
3

The town of Batak, 4 km east of the reservoir, carries the memory of one of the darkest episodes in Bulgarian history. Its exceptional acoustics have made it a venue for classical concerts. The name comes from the brothers Sae and Seyu, who took shelter here during an Ottoman invasion.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Iskar Gorge cuts through the mountain for 70 km — the only Bulgarian river to breach the Balkan range from south to north. The road threading beside the torrent passes Cherepish Monastery (14th century), clinging to the limestone cliff directly above the water.

⏱ 60 min
3

Prohodna Cave, near the village of Karlukovo, is a natural tunnel 262 metres long bored through a limestone hill. The smaller entrance rises 35 metres; the larger reaches 45 metres — the highest accessible cave vault in Bulgaria.

⏱ 45 min
4

At the midpoint of the tunnel, look up: two oval openings pierce the ceiling — the celebrated 'Eyes of God'. On clear days, the shafts of light streaming into the darkness create a contrast so dramatic that this has become one of the most photographed images in Bulgaria.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The guided tour of Saeva Dupka takes around 45 minutes. The cave maintains a constant temperature of 6–8°C — bring a layer even in summer.

⏱ 45 min

Walk straight through Prohodna Cave from one end to the other — the full traverse takes under 15 minutes, the floor is flat and entry is free. Suitable for all ages, including children.

⏱ 30 min

Hike through the Iskar Gorge along the river. The limestone cliffs shelter falcons and vultures, and the Mediterranean-influenced vegetation creates a striking contrast with the beech forests higher up.

⏱ 120 min

Veliko Tarnovo

★★★☆☆
1

Tsarevets Fortress (12th–14th century) was the citadel of the Second Bulgarian Empire, whose capital Veliko Tarnovo was from 1185 to 1393. Excavations have uncovered 400 houses, 22 churches and 4 monasteries on the peninsula-like hill encircled by the Yantra River. The 3,000 m² royal palace, the Patriarchal Cathedral at the summit and the Execution Rock — from which traitors were hurled into the river — are the highlights of the visit. The fortress held out three months under Ottoman siege before falling on 17 July 1393.

⏱ 120 min
2

Veliko Tarnovo's old town cascades down near-vertical slopes above the Yantra gorge (240 m). National Revival houses — colourful overhanging facades, red-tiled roofs — appear stacked on top of each other. Gurko Street, with its artisan shops and stone staircases, is the most photographed. The 'Monkey House', built in 1849 by architect Kolyu Ficheto, takes its name from the primate carved above the front door lintel.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Church of the Forty Martyrs (1230), in the medieval Asenov quarter by the Yantra, was built by Tsar Ivan Asen II to mark his victory over the Byzantines at Klokotnitsa. Two marble columns carry inscriptions of major historical importance: one recounting Asen's campaigns, the other a 10th-century Bulgarian stone tablet. It was here that Bulgarian independence was proclaimed in 1908.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The Tsarevets Sound and Light Show, staged on the fortress during national holidays and selected summer weekends, deploys 2,400 coloured projectors, lasers and six bells rung live to tell the story of Tarnovo's fall. From the terraces of the old town, the spectacle is visible without a ticket.

⏱ 45 min

The village of Arbanasi, 4 km north, is a remarkable ensemble of 16th–17th century Ottoman fortified houses. Their blind stone facades conceal richly decorated interiors. The Church of the Nativity (16th century) contains the most comprehensive frescoes in Bulgaria: over 3,500 figures covering every wall and ceiling.

⏱ 90 min

Belogradchik

★★★★☆
1

The Belogradchik Rocks are formations of red sandstone and conglomerate covering 50 km² on the western slopes of the Balkan range. Shaped 230 million years ago by erosion and iron oxidation (haematite), some reach 200 metres in height. Their fantastic silhouettes all carry names: the Madonna, the Monk, the Schoolgirl, the Bear, Adam and Eve. Nominated for the 'New 7 Wonders of Nature' in 2007, they are on UNESCO's tentative list.

⏱ 90 min
2

Belogradchik Fortress (Kaleto) is built directly among the rocks, which serve as its most formidable natural walls. The Romans laid the first works in the 1st century AD; Bulgarians and Ottomans extended the complex between 1805 and 1837. The final structure covers 10,210 m² with three fortified courtyards, 2-metre-thick walls rising 12 metres high, and the rocks themselves soaring like natural towers above the ramparts.

⏱ 90 min
3

Magura Cave, 25 km from Belogradchik, is one of Bulgaria's largest (2.5 km of galleries). Its walls carry rock paintings made with bat guano — hunting scenes, ritual dances and solar figures dating from between 8,000 and 3,000 BC. Excavations have also revealed traces of Neolithic winemaking, among the earliest evidence of the practice in Europe.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

The panoramic rocks trail, marked around the main massif, lets you follow the formations for 2–3 hours (easy, minimal elevation) and study the silhouettes from ever-changing angles. In the golden hour before sunset, the ochre sandstone deepens to a rich, burning red.

⏱ 120 min

Ivanovo

★★★☆☆
1

The Ivanovo Rock Churches are carved into the cliffs of the Roussenski Lom gorge, 32 metres above the river. A UNESCO World Heritage Site, they were founded in the 1220s by the monk Yakim — later Patriarch of Bulgaria. At the complex's peak it numbered 40 churches and 300 cells.

⏱ 90 min
2

The 14th-century frescoes belong to the Tarnovo School of painting: expressive postures, detailed landscapes, gently Hellenistic faces. The Last Supper depicted here predates Leonardo's by 150 years. These paintings rank among the masterpieces of medieval Christian art in South-East Europe.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Sveshtari Tomb (3rd century BC), 50 km to the south-west, is the region's second UNESCO site. Discovered in 1982, its burial chamber features ten polychrome caryatids — half-woman, half-plant — the only figurines of this type ever found on Thracian territory.

⏱ 60 min
4

Cherven Fortress, 30 km south of Ruse, commands the Roussenski Lom gorge from a rocky spur. A stronghold of the Second Bulgarian Empire (12th–14th centuries), its 17 towers and 7 ruined monasteries survey a wild, deeply cut valley.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The trail to the Rock Churches follows the gorge from Ivanovo village. Rust-red chalk cliffs overhang the river — one of the most photogenic landscapes in north-eastern Bulgaria.

⏱ 60 min

Ruse, 20 km to the north, is worth a stop: the 'Little Vienna of the Danube' lines up Austro-Hungarian neoclassical facades found nowhere else in Bulgaria. The riverside promenade looks across to Romania over the widest river in Europe.

⏱ 90 min

Madara

★★★☆☆
1

The Madara Horseman is the only medieval rock relief in Europe. Cut into a vertical 100-metre cliff in the early 8th century, it depicts a warrior on horseback thrusting a lance into a lion, with a dog at his heels. A UNESCO World Heritage Site, it appears on Bulgarian euro coins.

⏱ 60 min
2

Three ancient Greek inscriptions carved in the rock record the reigns of Khans Tervel, Krum and Omurtag between 705 and 831. Madara was the principal sacred site of the First Bulgarian Empire before the country's conversion to Christianity.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Madara Reserve gathers remains from every era: Thracian sanctuaries, Roman ruins, a 4th-century Bulgarian fortress on the plateau, 150 14th-century monastic cave-cells. The on-site archaeological museum presents finds spanning the Neolithic to the Ottoman period.

⏱ 60 min
4

Pliska, 20 km to the north-east, was Bulgaria's first capital (681–893). Its ruins spread across 23 km² — the largest medieval fortified complex in Europe at the time. The foundations of the Khan's palace and the Great Basilica are still visible in the open plain.

⏱ 75 min
5

Veliki Preslav, Bulgaria's second medieval capital (893–971), 18 km south-west of Shumen, is famous for its glazed decorative tiles with floral motifs — unique in the Byzantine world. The Golden Round Church, a circular Byzantine-inspired edifice, is its architectural emblem.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Climb to the Madara plateau via the trail that passes beneath the horseman. The view from the ridge over the Shumen plain offers a striking contrast with the cliff face seen from below.

⏱ 60 min

Combine Madara, Pliska and Preslav in a single driving day: these three sites form the founding triangle of the Bulgarian state, all reachable on paved roads.

⏱ 30 min

Varna

★★★★☆
1

The Varna Archaeological Museum holds the most precious treasure of the prehistoric world: the Varna Gold, dated 4,600–4,200 BC — the oldest worked gold ever found on Earth, predating Sumer and the pyramid-age Egypt by centuries. Discovered in 1972 by an excavator operator digging a trench, the hoard comprises more than 6 kg of jewellery, sceptres and ornaments in pure gold from 300 graves. Tomb No. 43, belonging to a high-ranking individual, contained more gold than all other Chalcolithic burials in South-East Europe combined.

⏱ 120 min
2

Varna's Roman Thermae, built in the 2nd century AD in the ancient city of Odessos, are the largest in Bulgaria and the fourth largest in Europe. The ruins — frigidarium, tepidarium, caldarium and palaestra — reach 22 metres in height in places. The complex is freely accessible and forms a fascinating open-air backdrop in the heart of the modern city.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Sea Garden of Varna, founded in 1878, stretches 8 km along the shore and is one of the largest coastal parks on the Black Sea. A shaded promenade links the city centre to the beaches, with sea views throughout and several museums within its grounds (Naval Museum, Aquarium, Planetarium).

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Varna Beach — a 10-minute walk from the Sea Garden — is the city's main beach with fine sand and full facilities. For wilder surroundings, Perla Beach (15 km north) or Gradina Beach (20 km south) offer calmer, less crowded waters.

⏱ 180 min

The Varna Chalcolithic Necropolis, 4 km from the centre, is the archaeological site where the treasure was found. Excavations remain active. Information panels on-site let you visualise the extent of the necropolis and the locations of excavated graves.

⏱ 30 min

Kaliakra

★★★☆☆
1

Cape Kaliakra ('Beautiful Headland' in Byzantine Greek) is a 2 km rocky peninsula thrusting into the Black Sea, with reddish cliffs plunging 60 to 70 metres to the water. The Thracian Tirizi tribe settled here in the 4th century BC under the name Tirisis. In the 14th century it served as capital of the Despotate of Dobruja — the last Bulgarian territory to fall to the Ottomans. The nature reserve protects 417 plant species and is a key stop on the Via Pontica, Europe's second most important bird migration route.

⏱ 90 min
2

Kaliakra Fortress runs the entire length of the rocky spur. Two successive enclosures, Roman baths, the ruins of a 14th-century Bulgarian church and a museum carved into a cave present artefacts found on the headland. At the tip of the promontory stands the small white chapel of Saint Nicholas, patron saint of sailors.

⏱ 60 min
3

The legend of the Forty Maidens is the most celebrated story of the cape: in the 15th century, forty young Bulgarian women, refusing to be taken captive by Ottoman soldiers, braided their hair together and leapt from the cliffs into the sea. An obelisk at the entrance — 'The Gate of the Forty Maidens' — commemorates their sacrifice. In 1791, the cape was the scene of the greatest naval battle ever fought on the Black Sea, between the Russian and Ottoman fleets: Admiral Ushakov's victory here later inspired Nelson's manoeuvres at Trafalgar.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Bolata Beach, 5 km north of Kaliakra, is considered one of the finest stretches of coast in Bulgaria: a cove tucked between red clay cliffs, reached by a path from the road (20 minutes on foot). Clear water, rarely crowded outside July and August.

⏱ 120 min

Birdwatching from the Kaliakra plateau (March–May and August–October) offers sightings of rare migrating species along the Via Pontica: Eleonora's falcon, osprey, imperial eagle, Dalmatian pelican. The cape is one of the five best raptor watchpoints in all of Europe.

⏱ 90 min
