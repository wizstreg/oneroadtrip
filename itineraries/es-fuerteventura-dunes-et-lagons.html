Puerto Del Rosario

Where streets become galleries with 150 sculptures scattered along the waterfront

★★☆☆☆
1

The city has transformed into an open-air museum with over 150 sculptures scattered throughout its streets. Juan Bordes' whimsical snail-shaped works line the coastal promenade, bringing contemporary art to the waterfront.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Iglesia Nuestra Señora del Rosario, a 19th-century neoclassical edifice, houses a sculpture of the Virgin of the Rosary and remarkable red stone stained glass windows. The square bell tower commands the historic quarter.

⏱ 45 min
3

Visit the Casa Museo Miguel de Unamuno, the former hotel where Spain's great philosopher lived in exile in 1924. Period furniture, photographs, and his writings chronicle his island wanderings and philosophical reflections.

⏱ 50 min
4

The lime kilns (Hornos de Cal) stand as monuments to the city's economic past. In the 18th century, Puerto del Rosario became the "Port of Lime," exporting burned limestone to England for construction.

⏱ 35 min
5

Playa Chica, recently expanded, offers an urban beach right in the heart of the capital. Showers and facilities make it perfect for a quick swim between city explorations.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Stroll along Calle Primero de Mayo, the main pedestrian street lined with boutiques and terraces. This is where locals and visitors converge for evening paseos.

⏱ 50 min

Browse the weekly artisan market for PDO Majorero cheese, aloe vera products, and traditional crafts. It's your chance to taste the island's renowned goat cheese at its source.

⏱ 60 min

Sample vieja (local parrotfish) grilled or fried, roasted goat, and papas arrugadas with mojo sauce. The island's goat cheese carries a protected designation of origin and appears on every menu.

⏱ 75 min

Corralejo

Where endless white dunes meet turquoise waters and volcanic horizons

★★★☆☆
1

The Corralejo Dunes Natural Park stretches across 2,600 hectares of pristine wilderness. These white dunes, formed from disintegrated seashells and marine organisms, create a Saharan landscape that contrasts dramatically with the dark volcanic rock.

⏱ 90 min
2

Climb Montaña Roja, a 314-meter volcano at the park's southern edge. The summit rewards you with 360-degree panoramas over the dunes, the turquoise Atlantic, and the volcanic silhouette of Lobos Island.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Grandes Playas stretch for 10 kilometers of pure beachfront bliss. Immaculate white sand, turquoise waters, and unobstructed views toward Lobos and Lanzarote. Stone wind shelters called corralitos dot the beach, offering refuge from the Atlantic breezes.

⏱ 120 min
4

Flag Beach and El Médano attract surfers and kitesurfers from around the world. The constant Atlantic winds create ideal conditions for riding the waves and catching air.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Take the ferry to Lobos Island, a 6-square-kilometer protected nature reserve. Once home to a colony of monk seals, the island now shelters the Punta Martiño lighthouse and preserved volcanic landscapes where time stands still.

⏱ 180 min

Swim in the shallow lagoons sheltered by natural reefs. Watch for strong currents in certain areas—always check the flag warnings before entering the water.

⏱ 90 min

The Kite Festival in November draws participants from across the globe. Multicolored kites dance across the natural park's skies throughout an entire weekend of celebration.

⏱ 120 min

El Cotillo

Where fishing boats still bob in the harbor and lagoons glow like gemstones

★★☆☆☆
1

El Cotillo's lagoons (Playa de los Lagos) stretch from the village to the lighthouse. These natural swimming pools, protected by coral reefs, shimmer with immaculate white sand and crystalline waters that glow emerald in the sunlight.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Torre del Tostón, an 18th-century circular fortress, once defended the port from pirate raids. Thick local stone walls, a drawbridge, and quarters designed for 12 soldiers tell tales of maritime vigilance.

⏱ 40 min
3

The Faro del Tostón lighthouse complex includes three structures built in 1897, 1955, and 1986. The site houses the Museum of Traditional Fishing, chronicling the village's timeless bond with the sea.

⏱ 50 min
4

Playa de La Concha curves like a crescent moon of white sand. Shallow emerald waters make it perfect for families. Despite its proximity to the village, the beach retains a wild, untouched character.

⏱ 75 min
5

Explore the old fishing harbor with its colorful boats bobbing in the water. Fishermen continue their ancestral trade, delivering fresh catch daily to the village's seafood restaurants.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Feast on grilled fish and seafood at the harbor restaurants. Fresh squid, vieja parrotfish, and traditional fish stew are the house specialties, caught just hours before reaching your plate.

⏱ 90 min

The surf spots south of the village draw surfers, windsurfers, and kitesurfers seeking Atlantic power. Consistent waves and steady winds create world-class conditions.

⏱ 120 min

Watch the sunset from the old harbor or lighthouse. El Cotillo faces due west, offering spectacular views as the sun sinks into the Atlantic horizon in a blaze of orange and gold.

⏱ 60 min

Betancuria

Where mountain valleys cradle the island's colonial soul in cobbled streets

★★★☆☆
1

The Iglesia Santa María de Betancuria, founded in 1410 by Jean de Béthencourt in French Gothic style, was Fuerteventura's first cathedral. Rebuilt after pirates destroyed it in 1593, it now blends Gothic, Renaissance, and Baroque elements into a harmonious whole.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Sacred Art Museum displays paintings, sculptures, and religious artifacts from the 15th and 16th centuries. Ornate chalices, ancient missals, and Béthencourt's banner carried during the conquest reveal the island's spiritual heritage.

⏱ 50 min
3

The Archaeological Museum showcases pottery, bone tools, and fishing equipment of the Mahos, the indigenous people. Discover how they survived with limited resources before Norman arrival, adapting to one of the Canaries' harshest environments.

⏱ 60 min
4

Wander among the ruins of the San Buenaventura Convent, founded in 1414 by Franciscan monks. As the island's oldest convent, it bears witness to Betancuria's central religious role in the archipelago.

⏱ 40 min
5

The Mirador de Morro Velosa offers sweeping panoramas across the Betancuria massif. Two bronze statues represent Guise and Ayose, the legendary Maho kings who ruled before conquest.

⏱ 45 min
6

Lose yourself in the cobbled streets of the historic center. Traditional whitewashed houses, carved wooden doors, and flower-filled balconies create an atmosphere where time seems to have stopped centuries ago.

⏱ 50 min

Activities

Hike through the Betancuria Rural Park, which covers 90% of the municipality. Ravines, hills, and a small pine forest—Fuerteventura's only one—shelter endemic flora and fauna found nowhere else on Earth.

⏱ 120 min

Descend to Vega del Río Palmas to visit the Ermita de Nuestra Señora de la Peña. This mountain chapel houses the Virgin of the Peña, the island's patron saint, honored during a colorful procession each September.

⏱ 75 min

Savor authentic Canarian cuisine in the village restaurants. PDO Majorero cheese, roasted goat, and traditional Canarian stew feature on every menu, paired with local wines.

⏱ 90 min

Peninsula De Jandia

Where wind-sculpted beaches stretch wild toward Africa's distant shores

★★☆☆☆
1

Playa de Sotavento stretches for kilometers, famous for its lagoon that forms at low tide. Shallow turquoise waters, ever-shifting sandbanks, and spectacular vistas make this beach a photographer's dream come true.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Faro del Matorral lighthouse, standing 60 meters tall, visually divides Playa del Matorral into two distinct zones. It's a prime observation point for watching sunsets paint the Atlantic in brilliant hues.

⏱ 50 min
3

The Saladar de Jandía, a protected wetland, harbors the Canaries' largest salt flats. This area of scientific interest preserves a rare salt-resistant plant species found almost nowhere else.

⏱ 60 min
4

Journey to the Punta Jandía lighthouse at the island's southwestern tip, built in the 19th century to guide ships toward Africa. The dramatic route cuts through Jandía Natural Park, revealing desert landscapes and wave-battered cliffs.

⏱ 90 min
5

Stroll through Morro Jable village, once a humble fishing port. The marina now welcomes up to 300 yachts, while narrow streets shelter boutiques and restaurants serving the day's fresh catch.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Relax on Playa del Matorral's 4 kilometers of golden sand, equipped with beach bars, restrooms, sun loungers, and lifeguards. Perfect for families seeking comfort and safety without sacrificing natural beauty.

⏱ 150 min

Kitesurf and windsurf at Playa de la Barca. Constant Atlantic winds make Sotavento one of the world's premier spots, hosting international competitions that draw elite riders.

⏱ 120 min

Join a boat excursion to observe dolphins and whales. Success rates run 50-70% depending on conditions. Curious and playful dolphins often approach boats, delighting passengers with their acrobatics.

⏱ 180 min

Hike to Pico de la Zarza (807m), Fuerteventura's highest peak. The summit reveals panoramic views over the wild Cofete beach and the entire Jandía Peninsula stretching toward the horizon.

⏱ 180 min
