Erzurum

★★★★☆
1

The Çifte Minareli Medrese thrusts twin minarets skyward right in the city center. Built at the end of the 13th century, it's the largest madrasa in Seljuk Anatolia. Its entrance portal concentrates carved stone tree-of-life and floral motifs. Allow 45 minutes, and step inside the octagonal mausoleum at the back—it's often empty.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Yakutiye Medresesi is worth visiting for its portal adorned with lions and eagles, carved in 1310 under the Ilkhanids. Today it houses the Turkish-Islamic ethnography museum. Entry around 100 TL, budget 30 minutes. Display cases present costumes, carpets, and local calligraphy.

⏱ 45 min
3

Erzurum Kalesi dominates the old town from its perch, dating back to the 12th-century Saltukids. From the top of Tepsi Minare, the view stretches all the way to the Palandöken mountains. Free access, plan on 30 minutes. Avoid visiting in high wind: Erzurum sits at 1,890 m above sea level.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Try cağ kebabı in a downtown lokanta. It's lamb skewered horizontally and sliced to order, a regional specialty. The Erzurum Evleri restaurant serves the traditional version. Expect 200 to 300 TL for a full meal with ayran.

⏱ 75 min

The air is dry and biting: at 1,890 m elevation, this is one of Turkey's highest cities. Stroll at sunset on Freedom Square, between the Üç Kümbetler and the old Russian houses. The slanting light brings out the yellow tuff stone.

⏱ 60 min

Tortum

★★★★☆
1

Tortum Waterfall, 48 meters high: magnificent from May to June, nearly dry the rest of the year. An 18th-century landslide blocked the valley and created both the falls and the lake. Allow 30 minutes on the viewing terraces.

⏱ 45 min
2

Lake Tortum stretches its turquoise waters 8 km, wedged between red cliffs. The D950 hugs its eastern shore and offers several picnic areas with views. Make the most of it: you're crossing one of northeastern Turkey's most beautiful road stretches.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Pack a picnic by the lake: bread, Kars cheese, tomatoes, and cucumbers are available in Uzundere grocery stores. The waterfall runs dry outside May–June, so the lake is the better bet. A vendor often sells fresh cucumbers at the main parking area.

⏱ 60 min

Kars

★★★☆☆
1

In Kars, locals say you feel the Russian Empire on every street corner. The city was Russian from 1878 to 1918, and the pale yellow neoclassical buildings attest to it. Walk the grid layout around Atatürk Street—it's unique in Turkey.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Church of the Twelve Apostles dates to the 930s, built by Armenian king Abas I of the Bagratids. It has served as a mosque, a Russian Orthodox church, a warehouse, and a museum before becoming a mosque again in 1993. You can still make out bas-reliefs of the apostles on the drum.

⏱ 45 min
3

Kars Kalesi, perched on its basalt outcrop, was built in 1153 by the Saltukids then destroyed by Tamerlane. Rebuilt in 1579, it's free to visit today. The ascent takes 20 minutes; the view over the steppe and river makes it worthwhile.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Sample Kars cheese, inherited from Russian immigrants in the late 19th century. The gravyer (local gruyère) and çeçil stretch under the knife. Kars Bakkaliyesi, near Fethiye Mosque, offers tastings and retail sales. Expect 400 to 600 TL per kilo of aged gravyer.

⏱ 60 min

Taşköprü bridge has spanned the river since 1725. Three old hammams are visible just beside it, closed but photogenic. Walk from the citadel to the bridge at sunset: 15 minutes, the gold of the stone contrasts with the gray basalt of the fortress.

⏱ 45 min

Ani

★★★★★
1

Ani strikes you with its silence: an entire Armenian capital, abandoned on a plateau above the Arpaçay River. At its peak around the year 1000, it may have held 100,000 inhabitants. Mongols, earthquakes, and shifting trade routes emptied it from 1236 onward. Inscribed as a UNESCO site in 2016.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Church of St. Gregory of Tigran Honents, dated 1215 by the Armenian inscription on its south façade, preserves the site's best-kept frescoes. Lives of Christ and St. Gregory the Illuminator in still-vivid colors. You're now 50 meters from the closed border with Armenia.

⏱ 45 min
3

Ani Cathedral, completed around 1001 by architect Trdat (the same one who worked on Hagia Sophia after the 989 earthquake), measured 38 m high. The dome has collapsed, but the elevation of the three naves remains impressive. Converted to Fethiye Mosque under the Seljuks after 1064.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Plan 3 to 4 hours on site: the grounds span about 5 to 6 km on foot between major points. No refreshment stand inside—water and a hat mandatory in summer. Entry around 90 TL in 2025. Parking is at the entrance to the Seljuk ramparts on the west side.

⏱ 90 min

Dogubayazit

★★★☆☆
1

Built over 99 years, Ishak Pasha Palace was begun in 1685 and completed in 1784 by its namesake, a Kurdish sanjak-bey. 7,600 m² perched on a rocky spur facing Ararat. Mosque, harem, salamlık, and 366 rooms according to the portal inscription. An Ottoman, Persian, and Georgian blend.

⏱ 90 min
2

Doğubayazıt Fortress, more discreet, stands on a ridge east of the palace. Its foundations date back to the Urartian era, around 800 BC. Allow 30 minutes on foot from Ishak Pasha. You'll have the place to yourselves.

⏱ 60 min
3

Kurds call the poet Ehmedê Xanî their spiritual father. His mausoleum, right next to the palace, draws Kurdish pilgrims in silence. Xanî wrote Mem û Zîn in the 17th century, an epic in Kurmanji. Free visit, allow 20 minutes.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Park at dawn in front of the palace: morning light illuminates the east-facing façade over the plain. Tour buses arrive around 10 a.m. and the crowded site loses its atmosphere. The drive from downtown takes 15 minutes by taxi (around 100 TL round-trip with wait time).

⏱ 60 min

In the evening, from the terrace of Tehran Boutique Hotel or any downtown rooftop, Ararat cuts a mauve silhouette. Doğubayazıt sits at 1,600 m altitude; the air is dry and the late-day light turns pink. Clouds often cling to the summit in the afternoon.

⏱ 45 min

Mount Ararat

★★★★☆
1

Mount Ararat, 5,137 m, is Turkey's highest peak. It's a dormant stratovolcano whose last eruption dates to July 2, 1840, and destroyed the Armenian monastery of St. James. According to Genesis, Noah's Ark came to rest here. The massif is about 35 km wide at the base.

⏱ 60 min
2

Little Ararat, a regular cone of 3,896 m, accompanies Greater Ararat 11 km away. Both peaks emerge from the same volcanic base. The Iranian border passes 16 km to the south, the Armenian border 32 km to the north. You'll see both summits from the road between Doğubayazıt and Iğdır.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

If you want to summit, count on 4 to 5 days with an approved local guide, from Doğubayazıt. Permit required (military zone) to request at least a month in advance. Camp 1 at 3,200 m, Camp 2 at 4,200 m, summit push at 2 a.m., crampons and ice axe essential above 4,900 m.

⏱ 120 min

Van

★★☆☆☆
1

Van Kalesi stretches its rocky spur nearly 1.5 km long and 100 m high, above the lake's eastern shore. Built from 840 BC by Urartian king Sarduri I as the capital of the Kingdom of Urartu. Eight royal tombs carved into the rock on the south face, cuneiform inscriptions everywhere.

⏱ 120 min
2

Van Museum, reopened in 2020, is one of Turkey's best archaeological museums. Rich collection of Urartian objects, including 9 rooms devoted to Urartu. Model of the Ayanis temple, pottery, jewelry. Allow 90 minutes. Ticket at 10 TL at the time several recent guides were written.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Van cat, white with one blue eye and one green eye, is known for loving water. You can see some at the Van Kedi Evi at Yüzüncü Yıl University, west of the city. Free visit, allow 30 minutes. Photos allowed sparingly: these are protected animals.

⏱ 45 min
4

The old town destroyed in 1915 is no longer visible today, but the ruins at the foot of the castle are accessible. A modern mosque, some foundations, and funerary stelae mark the location. Stroll at sunset when the light grazes the salt lake.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Vanlı locals speak of the lake as a 'sea': 3,700 km², making it Turkey's largest lake. Its water is alkaline and saline because of Nemrut lavas that closed off the basin. Catch the sunset from Adır Iskelesi on the north shore, about 30 km northeast of downtown.

⏱ 90 min

Have breakfast in a local kahvaltı salonu: Van is known throughout Turkey for its breakfast. Sufi Kahvaltı Salonu or Bak Hele Bak offer about twenty plates: otlu peynir herbed cheese, kavut, honey, churned butter. Expect 200 to 300 TL per person.

⏱ 90 min

Cavustepe

★★★★☆
1

Mehmet Kuşman, guardian of Çavuştepe, can read and write the Urartian language—one of 25 people in the world able to do so, according to multiple sources. The site, built by King Sarduri II between 764 and 734 BC, includes an open-air temple to Haldi. Cuneiform stelae still in place on the enclosure.

⏱ 60 min
2

Hoşap Castle, perched on its rock in the village of Güzelsu, dates from 1643 and was built by a Kurdish governor of the Ottoman Empire. Massive round towers, well-preserved curtain walls. The site likely occupies an earlier Urartian fortress. Allow 45 minutes for your visit.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Buy a stele carved in Urartian on site—Mehmet Kuşman sculpts and sells them for a few euros. It's a chance to chat with one of the last speakers of this isolated language. The site has no refreshment stand; bring water and cash.

⏱ 45 min

Akdamar

★★★★☆
1

Built between 915 and 921, the Church of the Holy Cross at Akdamar is the work of architect Manuel for Armenian King Gagik I Artsruni. Hripsimé-type cross plan, 15 meters by 12. The exterior friezes in red tuff tell in bas-relief Adam and Eve, Jonah and the whale, David and Goliath, Daniel among the lions.

⏱ 90 min
2

The red tuff of the façade glows in the midday sun: quarried from distant sites, the stone seems to radiate. Inside, faded but legible frescoes, biblical scenes on the four absidioles. The church is open for Armenian worship once a year since 2010; the rest of the time it's a museum.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Buy your ticket at the Gevaş pier, 35 km southwest of Van. The crossing takes 20 minutes; the boat waits for you to return (often only 1 hour on the island—negotiate 2 hours if possible). Round-trip fare is around 200 TL in 2025. Sunglasses and water mandatory.

⏱ 75 min

Tatvan

★★☆☆☆
1

Nemrut's crater lake, at 2,247 m altitude, occupies a volcanic caldera of more than 12 km² north of Tatvan. It holds two distinct lakes, one cold and one hot (thermal springs at the edge). Blue water, sheer rock walls. Access only from mid-May to late October—under snow the rest of the year.

⏱ 90 min
2

Mount Nemrut sealed the Lake Van basin with its lava flows, transforming an open basin into a salt lake with no outlet. Eruptions date back to roughly 90,000 to 30,000 years ago. You'll spot obsidian and tuff at several crater points; collect sparingly (officially prohibited).

⏱ 60 min
3

Mount Süphan, 4,058 m, intimidates less than Ararat but remains a major summit. Visible from the north rim of Lake Van, its snow-capped cone dominates the Adilcevaz plain. It's one of Turkey's highest volcanoes, dormant. You can gaze at it from the Tatvan pier at dawn.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

If the crater road isn't iced over, drive up to the rim: 13 km from the main road, paved but potholed. Round-trip taxi with wait around 600 TL from Tatvan. Better to take your own car if you want to stay for sunset.

⏱ 120 min

Pitch a tent near Nemrut's hot springs if you're camping: one side of the crater has water above 60°C, the other freezes in winter. The site received the European Destination of Excellence label in 2010. Birch forest surrounds the shores; spectacular colors in September–October.

⏱ 90 min

Bitlis

★★★☆☆
1

The black stone of Ahlat colors all of Bitlis. This city at 1,545 m altitude is wedged in a gorge; two rivers cross it. The castle overlooks the town from a spur. According to local legend, it was built by an Alexander general named Badlis in 312 BC—information unverifiable but persistent.

⏱ 45 min
2

Şerefiye Mosque, dated 1529 by inscription, is a rare complex: mosque, madrasa, soup kitchen, and founder's tomb for Emir Şeref. The muqarnas portal evokes Seljuk style even though the Ottomans already dominated the region. Madrasa roof accessible for a panoramic view.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Eat büryan kebabı, lamb cooked in a pit for several hours then served on flatbread. It's Bitlis's specialty, to taste at lunchtime only (pits are lit in the morning and emptied around 2 p.m.). Expect 250 to 350 TL for a portion, served with raw onions and fresh tomatoes.

⏱ 75 min

Diyarbakir

★★★★☆
1

Diyarbakır's ramparts stretch 5.8 km all around the old city. Sixty-three inscriptions on towers and gates, dating from Roman to Ottoman according to the 2015 UNESCO dossier. Black basalt, which gives the city its nickname Kara Amid. Walk the exterior circuit, about 2 hours on foot; recent restorations have secured several sections.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Hevsel Gardens stretch toward the Tigris over about 4,000 decares. Cultivated for several millennia according to archaeological estimates, they're UNESCO-listed along with the ramparts. Rich birdlife, observed by local ornithologists. Go from Mardin Gate, descending path, allow 1 hour on site.

⏱ 90 min
3

Ulu Camii, one of Anatolia's oldest mosques, occupies the site of a 4th-century Byzantine cathedral converted after the Arab conquest in 639. Two-level arcaded courtyard, reused Roman columns. The form recalls Syrian mosques like Damascus. Free visit; headscarf for women.

⏱ 60 min
4

Hasan Paşa Hanı, a caravanserai from 1575, stands just opposite Ulu Camii. Built by the son of Grand Vizier Sokollu Mehmed Paşa. Restored, it now houses restaurants and cafés under two-level arcades. The kahvaltı breakfast here is legendary in town; expect 350 to 500 TL per person.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Have a drink in the courtyard of Sülüklü Han, a caravanserai from 1683 converted into a café-bar. Local wine of Assyrian production, sheep's cheese, and jam on the side. No credit card—cash only. Atmosphere blending Kurdish, Assyrian, Armenian, and Turkish; the portal inscription is in five languages.

⏱ 90 min

Diyarbakırlı locals boast of their giant watermelon, some exceeding 30 kilos. The Balıkçılarbaşı market, near Mardin Gate, offers the region's best from late July to September. Also try mırra, strong cardamom coffee served in a demi-tasse, an Arabic-speaking tribal tradition from the southeast.

⏱ 75 min

At dusk, the black walls take on a chocolate hue. Climb Keçi Burcu, the Ram's Bastion, accessible by stairs. The view plunges over the Hevsel Gardens and the Tigris. It's one of the old city's best viewpoints. Allow 30 minutes at the top; free access.

⏱ 45 min
