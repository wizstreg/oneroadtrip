Castleton

★★★☆☆
1

The village of Castleton deserves a proper walkabout as soon as you arrive. Mentioned in the Domesday Book of 1086 under the unlikely name 'Pechesers' (literally 'the Peak's backside'), it's remained remarkably intact since medieval times. Stone cottages, weathered pubs and the 13th-century St Edmund's church set the tone immediately: you're in the heart of the Peak District, far from any tourist crowds. Arrive early—car parks fill by 9am on weekends.

⏱ 45 min
2

Above the village, the ruins of Peveril Castle have commanded the Hope Valley since the 11th century. William Peveril, rumoured illegitimate son of William the Conquerer, built it around 1086—the square keep is one of the best-preserved Norman examples in England. The climb is steep but short, and the view from the ramparts across the limestone hills rewards every step. Allow 45 minutes return from the village centre.

⏱ 60 min
3

Peak Cavern—locally known as 'Devil's Arse' for the unsettling sounds it makes—has the largest natural cave entrance in Britain: 18 metres high, 30 metres wide. Until 1915, Britain's last troglodytes lived here: ropemakers who'd set up home in the cave mouth itself. The philosopher Thomas Hobbes declared the passages one of the Seven Wonders of the Peak in 1636. Book guided tours ahead in season.

⏱ 75 min
4

Speedwell Cavern is the only cavern in the world toured entirely by boat, gliding along an underground canal hand-cut by 18th-century lead miners. At the end waits the 'Bottomless Pit'—originally estimated at 150 metres deep. Miners dumped so much debris into it over the centuries that it now measures only 35 metres. An experience unlike any other.

⏱ 75 min
5

Blue John Cavern and Treak Cliff Cavern are the only two places on Earth where you can see Blue John stone in situ—a banded blue-and-yellow fluorite whose name likely comes from the French 'bleu et jaune'. The Romans discovered it 2,000 years ago: two Blue John vases were unearthed during the Pompeii excavations. Blue John Cavern contains 8 of the 15 known veins of this mineral. At Treak Cliff, about 500kg are still extracted each year. In both caverns, it's the miners themselves who serve as guides.

⏱ 90 min
6

Winnats Pass is a spectacular limestone gorge: a narrow road plunges between cliffs that rise more than 100 metres above the tarmac. It became the only passable route after the old road across Mam Tor's flank was abandoned in 1979, the hillside having slipped slowly but inexorably toward the valley for centuries. Even if you don't walk, simply driving slowly through the pass at dawn or dusk is arresting.

⏱ 30 min
7

The Mam Tor walk is one of the Peak District's most popular—plan to rise early to beat the crowds and catch the morning light. The summit reaches 517 metres and shelters the remains of a Bronze Age fort, one of the oldest in the UK. The 'Shivering Mountain' owes its nickname to constant landslips on its eastern flank, composed of unstable layers of shale and gritstone. Geologists estimate these slips won't stop for another 1,500 years. On clear days, you'll spot Manchester on the horizon. The classic loop from Castleton (9km, 460m ascent) passes through Winnats Pass and Cave Dale—allow 3.5 to 4 hours.

⏱ 210 min
8

The Dragon's Back ridge—Chrome Hill and Parkhouse Hill—is less famous than Mam Tor but equally striking. These two narrow limestone ridges in the Dove Valley, 30 minutes south of Castleton, resemble the dorsal scales of a sleeping dragon. The climbs are short (about 45 minutes return for each summit) but the views across valleys carved into white limestone rank among the Peak District's finest. Do it in late afternoon for the light.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

The Roaches ridge, 20 minutes from Castleton, is a 5km gritstone escarpment that juts southward. The bouldering makes it a climbers' reference point, but simply walking the ridgeline offers sweeping views across the Cheshire plain. A mob of Bennett's wallabies—descendants of animals escaped from a private park in the 1930s—still lives semi-wild here. Allow 2 hours for the ridge return.

⏱ 120 min

Curbar Edge is a viewpoint atop a gritstone cliff 20 minutes from Castleton. Dark rock blocks loom above the Hope Valley below—the view across the moorland and drystone walls is quintessentially Peak District. It's one of the most photographed sunset spots in the park. From the Curbar Gap car park, it's just 10 minutes to the edge.

⏱ 60 min

Bakewell

★★★★☆
1

Bakewell is the only proper town inside Peak District National Park. The Monday market is the town's oldest, active since 1254; if you're there on a weekday, the stalls of local produce and the cattle market across the river give a precise sense of what the Peak District still is: a living agricultural region. The medieval five-arched bridge over the Wye dates from the 13th century.

⏱ 60 min
2

Don't leave without trying a Bakewell Pudding—and no, it's not the same as the Bakewell Tart found everywhere else. The real local pudding is puff pastry filled with jam and an egg-and-almond custard. Legend says it was born from a cook's mistake at the Rutland Arms around 1860: the recipe got reversed and the result was adopted by the locals. Several shops still compete today for the title of 'original recipe'.

⏱ 30 min
3

Five kilometres from Bakewell, the village of Ashford-in-the-Water warrants 45 minutes of wandering. Sheepwash Bridge, a medieval single-arch span over the Wye, takes its name from the practice of washing sheep there before shearing. The water runs so clear you can see trout swimming below. The village itself, with its pale grey limestone houses, is the archetype of Derbyshire villages.

⏱ 45 min
4

Chatsworth House, 5km from Bakewell on the banks of the Derwent, is one of England's grandest stately homes. The Cavendish family has lived here since 1549. Mary, Queen of Scots was imprisoned here on Elizabeth I's orders in 1569. The interiors span 4,000 years of art collection, from Roman artefacts to Old Master drawings to portraits by Lucian Freud. The house inspired the Pemberley estate in the 2005 film 'Pride & Prejudice'. The 42-hectare garden, with its cascade and Emperor Fountain (powered by gravity alone), rivals the house itself. Allow at least half a day.

⏱ 180 min
5

Haddon Hall, 3km south of Bakewell, is one of England's best-preserved medieval manor houses. Unlike Chatsworth, it underwent no baroque or neoclassical renovation—you're visiting a 15th-century manor in near-original condition. The terraced gardens tumbling down toward the River Wye are particularly striking in June and July when in bloom.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Monsal Trail is a disused Victorian railway line converted into a cycling and walking trail that winds through the limestone gorges of the Wye Valley for 13km. Stone viaducts, tunnels and wooded gorges make it one of the Peak District's most spectacular non-motorised routes. Bike hire is available in Bakewell. Allow 3 hours for the return trip to Monsal Head viaduct.

⏱ 180 min

Shrewsbury

★★★☆☆
1

Ironbridge Gorge has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1986—and the title 'birthplace of the Industrial Revolution' is no exaggeration. This is where Abraham Darby I, in 1709, developed the technique of smelting iron with coke, making metal production infinitely cheaper and faster. His grandson, Abraham Darby III, erected the world's first iron bridge across the Severn in 1779: 30 metres span, 378 tonnes of cast iron from the Coalbrookdale foundries. The first tolls on this bridge weren't collected until 1 January 1781. You can still cross the bridge on foot—it's been closed to traffic since 1934.

⏱ 60 min
2

The ten museums of the Ironbridge Gorge site cover the entire industrial history of the valley—you needn't do them all, but three deserve your time. Start with the Coalbrookdale Museum of Iron, housed in the old blast furnaces: this is where it all began in 1709. Then push on to Blists Hill Victorian Town, a full-scale recreation of an 1900 industrial town with working shops and costumed actors—allow at least 2 hours. If you have a soft spot for decorative arts, the Jackfield Tile Museum is worth the detour: the faience tiles produced here once adorned London Underground stations and palaces worldwide. A combined Passport ticket covers all ten sites with no time limit.

⏱ 120 min
3

Shrewsbury, 30km northwest of Ironbridge, is a near-intact medieval town nestled in a meander of the Severn—the river encircles the old town on three sides. The historic centre contains more than 660 timber-framed buildings dating from the 15th and 16th centuries. The Old Market Hall from 1596 still presides over the main square. Wander the 'shuts'—covered passageways between houses dating from the Middle Ages—to lose yourself in the lower town.

⏱ 90 min
4

Shrewsbury Castle, in red sandstone, has overlooked the river since the 11th century. It now houses a military museum, but the view across the medieval rooftops of the old town from the castle gardens is worth the visit. The Quarry park, on the banks of the Severn, contains 'the Dingle'—a sunken garden maintained since the 19th century, one of England's most floral.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Severn at Ironbridge is a working and accessible river. Local operators offer kayak or canoe trips from the banks below the Iron Bridge—an original way to see the gorge from the water, where barges once carried cast iron downriver to Bristol. Allow at least 2 hours.

⏱ 120 min

Local companies run boat trips on the Severn from Shrewsbury, passing under the old stone bridges and along the wooded banks of the meander. Some evening cruises include a meal on board.

⏱ 60 min

Hereford

★★★☆☆
1

En route to Hereford, stop at the Malvern Hills—a ridge of Precambrian hills 680 million years old that surge abruptly from the Worcestershire plain. Great Malvern is a Victorian spa town that's kept its elegance: an 11th-century priory, terraced gardens. The walk from the town centre to the Worcestershire Beacon (425m) takes 1.5 hours return and offers, on clear days, views from the Welsh Black Mountains to the Cotswolds.

⏱ 120 min
2

Hereford Cathedral houses two treasures absolutely unique in the world. The first is the Mappa Mundi, the largest known medieval map still in existence: drawn around 1300 on calfskin (1.59m x 1.34m), Jerusalem sits at the centre, Paradise at the top, monsters and strange creatures at the edges. In 1988, the diocese nearly sold it to cover a deficit—a public fundraising campaign and a donation from Paul Getty kept it in Hereford. The second is the Chained Library: the world's largest library of chained books, with 1,500 volumes including 229 medieval manuscripts still attached to their shelves by chains.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Old House, a timber-framed building from 1621 that survived 19th-century demolitions, is the finest specimen of Jacobean architecture in the region. The banks of the Wye, two minutes' walk from the cathedral, make a pleasant pause at day's end.

⏱ 45 min
4

Hereford is the world capital of cider—the region produces more cider than anywhere else in Britain. The Cider Museum traces 400 years of production through antique presses, barrels and the reconstruction of a Herefordshire calvados distillery. In season, several cideries around town offer tours and tastings directly in the orchards.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The walk along the Wye in Hereford is one of the Midlands' most pleasant urban strolls: 5km between medieval bridges, willow-lined banks and the cathedral in the background. If you want to go further, the Wye Valley Walk departs from Hereford toward Wales—even 2 hours heading south are enough to see the river carve into increasingly wild landscape.

⏱ 90 min

Stratford Upon Avon

★★★★☆
1

Begin with Shakespeare's Birthplace on Henley Street: a 15th-century timber-framed house where William was born in 1564. His father John was a wool merchant and glover, and the front half of the house served as his workshop. Shakespeare inherited the property on his father's death in 1601 and rented it out as an inn. The bedroom where he was born is open to visitors, with a copy of the 1623 First Folio—the collection that saved 18 of his plays from oblivion.

⏱ 60 min
2

Anne Hathaway's Cottage, 1.5km from the centre in Shottery, is the farmhouse where Shakespeare's wife grew up. The beams and period furniture are largely authentic. William married Anne in 1582, aged 18 (she was 26). The garden, recreated from plants mentioned in Shakespeare's plays, is especially pleasant in spring. You can walk there from the centre in 20 minutes.

⏱ 60 min
3

Nash's House and the site of New Place occupy the location of Stratford's grandest house, purchased by Shakespeare in 1597. The house was demolished in 1759 by an owner exasperated by tourists coming to admire the mulberry tree planted by the playwright—he tore up the tree and razed the house, and the town never forgave him. Archaeological excavations have since uncovered the foundations.

⏱ 45 min
4

Hall's Croft is the Tudor house where Shakespeare's eldest daughter lived with her physician husband John Hall. The collection of 17th-century medical equipment—and herbal remedies—gives a striking sense of Elizabethan medicine. The adjoining garden is one of the most tranquil in the town centre.

⏱ 45 min
5

Holy Trinity Church, by the Avon, is the church where Shakespeare was baptised on 26 April 1564 and where he's buried before the chancel. The inscription on his tomb remains famous: 'Good friend for Jesus sake forbear, to dig the dust enclosed here'—a plea that his remains not be moved, as was customary for overcrowded churchyards. Among the chancel windows, some came from Antwerp and date from the 15th century.

⏱ 30 min
6

Mary Arden's Farm in Wilmcote (5km north) is the 16th-century farm where Shakespeare's mother grew up. The estate still operates as a working Tudor farm, with heritage breed animals and craft demonstrations. It's the most rural and most living of the five Shakespeare Birthplace Trust sites.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

A boat or canoe trip on the Avon from Stratford is one of the region's most pleasant activities. The river flows between meadows and wooded banks, and you'll glimpse Holy Trinity Church from the water along the way. Rowboat and canoe hire is available on the Avon banks two minutes' walk from the centre.

⏱ 90 min

The Royal Shakespeare Theatre programmes Shakespeare performances year-round. If you've planned your visit ahead, an evening at the RSC is an experience you won't find anywhere else. Tickets sell fast in summer—book several weeks in advance.

⏱ 180 min

Warwick

★★★★☆
1

Warwick Castle is one of England's best-preserved medieval castles. Æthelflæd, daughter of King Alfred the Great, founded a fort here in 914 to repel the Danes. William the Conqueror converted it to a stone castle in 1068. Caesar's Tower rises to 45 metres, Guy's Tower to 39 metres. The 'Kingmaker' Richard Neville, the Earl of Warwick who played English kings according to his interests in the 15th century, held court here. Canaletto painted the castle five times—making it the English building most depicted by the Venetian painter. Allow a full half-day.

⏱ 180 min
2

Warwick's historic centre is compact and pleasant to explore on foot from the castle. The Lord Leycester Hospital, founded in 1571 as a retirement home for wounded soldiers, is a splendid ensemble of timber-framed buildings that frames a medieval gate. The great church of St Mary contains the Beauchamp Chapel—one of England's richest perpendicular Gothic chapels.

⏱ 60 min
3

Ten kilometres from Warwick, Kenilworth Castle clearly merits the detour—it's England's largest castle ruin and one of the most romantic. Its great Norman keep dates from the 12th century; the artificial lake that once surrounded it, now drained, was medieval England's largest artificial lake. Picture the scene: Elizabeth I stayed here for 19 days in 1575, welcomed by her favourite Robert Dudley in festivities that cost the equivalent of several million modern pounds—fireworks, banquets, jousts. Walter Scott was so taken with it that he drew his novel 'Kenilworth' (1821) from the story. Allow 1.5 hours to tour it.

⏱ 90 min
4

Royal Leamington Spa, 5km from Warwick, is the Midlands' most elegant spa town. The 19th-century Pump Rooms are now an art gallery and cultural centre. Jephson Gardens, designed by Victorian gardener Edward Milner, are among the Midlands' best-maintained urban gardens. Regent Street and The Parade are lined with Regency facades.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The River Avon runs alongside Warwick Castle for several kilometres—local operators offer canoe or rowboat trips from the banks of the castle park. It's the ideal angle to see Caesar's and Guy's towers reflected in the water, from the same perspective as Canaletto's 18th-century paintings. Allow 1.5 hours for a leisurely outing.

⏱ 90 min

The River Leam path between Leamington Spa and Warwick (5km) follows the wooded banks between the two towns—a fine walk or cycle linking the two historic centres. Start from Jephson Gardens and follow the river up to the castle walls.

Birmingham

★★★★☆
1

Birmingham has 35 miles of canals—officially more than Venice. Gas Street Basin is the historic heart of the network: this is where the Birmingham Canal (1773) met the Worcester & Birmingham Canal, separated for decades by the famous 'Worcester Bar', a 2.2-metre physical barrier because each company refused to share its water with the other. Cargoes had to be transhipped by hand from one barge to another. Today the basin is ringed by bars and restaurants in old brick warehouses.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Jewellery Quarter is the historic goldsmiths' district—40% of jewellery made in the UK still comes from these workshops. At its 19th-century peak, the quarter employed more than 30,000 artisans. The Museum of the Jewellery Quarter is housed in a family factory closed in 1981 exactly as it was: machines, workbenches and semi-finished products still in place. The quarter's Assay Office still hallmarks 12 million pieces of jewellery a year.

⏱ 90 min
3

Birmingham Museum and Art Gallery holds one of the world's most important Pre-Raphaelite collections—notably works by Burne-Jones, a Birmingham native. Even if you're not a museum enthusiast, the grand Victorian hall on the ground floor is worth seeing for the architecture alone.

⏱ 90 min
4

Cadbury World at Bournville, 6km from the centre, is England's most-visited chocolate attraction. In 1879, brothers George and Richard Cadbury moved their factory here and built a model village around it for their workers—housing, sports grounds, swimming pool. The village of Bournville, with its red-brick cottages and gardens, is still inhabited today. Book ahead.

⏱ 120 min
5

Digbeth, southeast of the centre, is Birmingham's creative quarter: old factories converted to galleries, street art, makers' markets. The Custard Factory (a 19th-century Bird's Custard factory) is now an artists' studio hub. J.R.R. Tolkien grew up in the neighbouring Moseley district—the 'Shire' of The Lord of the Rings draws on the fields and mill at Sarehole, now swallowed by the suburbs.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

A canal boat cruise in Birmingham is the best introduction to the industrial city. Companies run 1- to 2-hour cruises from Gas Street Basin or Brindleyplace, passing under brick bridges, along old warehouses and through the locks of the Farmers Bridge Lock Flight. Some boats offer brunch or dinner on board.

Coventry

★★★☆☆
1

Coventry Cathedral is the most powerful symbol of the Blitz on British soil. On the night of 14–15 November 1940, nearly 500 Luftwaffe bombers targeted Coventry for 11 hours—Operation 'Moonlight Sonata'—568 dead, a third of the city razed. St Michael's Cathedral burned through the night. The next morning, stonemason Jock Forbes gathered two charred beams that had fallen in a cross and tied them together—this Charred Cross still stands in the ruins today. The decision not to rebuild identically but to preserve the ruins as a memorial and attach a new cathedral was taken the day after the bombing.

⏱ 60 min
2

The new cathedral built 1956–62 by Basil Spence is one of the major works of 20th-century British architecture. The great tapestry of Christ in Glory by Graham Sutherland (24 metres high) dominates the chancel. The west wall of glass, engraved by John Hutton with 66 angels and saints—it took him 10 years—looks out onto the ruins of the old cathedral through a transparent wall. Coventry has since adopted Dresden and Kiel as twin cities—a symbol of reconciliation.

⏱ 60 min
3

Coventry Transport Museum is the world's largest museum devoted to British automotive production—and it's free. Coventry was the heart of the English motor industry: Jaguar, Land Rover, Triumph, Hillman were all born here. Don't miss Thrust SSC—the car that broke the world land speed record in 1997 at 1,228 km/h.

⏱ 90 min
4

Spon Street is Coventry's best-preserved medieval street—a paradox in a city almost entirely destroyed in 1940. Several timber-framed buildings from the 15th and 16th centuries survive here, some moved stone by stone from other parts of the city during post-war reconstruction.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Coventry has established a 'Holy Trinity Trail' walking route linking the main historic sites that survived the Blitz—the cathedral, Spon Street, the 14th-century St Mary's Guildhall and the remains of the 12th-century Benedictine priory. Allow 1.5 hours on foot from the centre to tour the medieval survivors, which gives striking context to the destruction of 1940.

⏱ 90 min

Nottingham

★★★★☆
1

Nottingham Castle occupies a rocky bluff overlooking the city—the real medieval castle has gone, replaced by a 17th-century ducal mansion housing museum and gallery. It's the site itself that counts: the cliffs honeycombed with caves in red sandstone, the historic tunnels where Robin Hood was supposedly imprisoned by the Sheriff, and the bronze statue of the outlaw at the entrance. The castle underwent a £30 million renovation, with interactive exhibitions on the Robin Hood legend.

⏱ 90 min
2

Ye Olde Trip to Jerusalem, in the shadow of the castle cliffs, claims the title of England's oldest pub—founded in 1189, allegedly frequented by Crusaders en route to the Holy Land. Much of the pub is carved directly into the rock, with alcoves hewn from the sandstone. A drink here is compulsory.

⏱ 45 min
3

The City of Caves is a network of more than 500 caves carved into the sandstone beneath Nottingham's streets—occupied since Anglo-Saxon times. The guided tour descends into a medieval tannery, a Victorian slum and a Second World War air-raid shelter. Nottingham literally sits atop a sandstone Swiss cheese.

⏱ 75 min
4

The Lace Market is the Victorian lace-trade quarter, built around the magnificent Gothic church of St Mary. At its 19th-century peak, Nottingham produced more than half the world's lace. Old Market Square, next door, is England's largest public square after Trafalgar Square.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Sherwood Forest is 30km north of Nottingham. The Major Oak, estimated at 800 to 1,000 years old, is England's most famous tree: its trunk measures 10 metres around, its canopy spreads 28 metres. Since Victorian times, its branches have been supported by metal props. The forest contains more than 1,000 oaks older than 500 years—the highest concentration of ancient oaks in western Europe. The village of Edwinstowe is where, according to legend, Robin Hood married Maid Marian.

Leicester

★★★☆☆
1

In August 2012, archaeologists from the University of Leicester began digging in a city centre car park. On the first day, they unearthed human bones. On 4 February 2013, the university announced 'beyond reasonable doubt' that they'd found the remains of Richard III, last Plantagenet king, killed at Bosworth in 1485. The King Richard III Visitor Centre, built on the exact site of the car park and grave, tells the story of his life, death and 527 years of oblivion. His tomb is at Leicester Cathedral, less than 100 metres away—reinterred in 2015 during a televised ceremony in the presence of the Archbishop of Canterbury.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Jewry Wall, steps from the centre, is one of the largest sections of Roman wall still standing in England—9 metres high, dating from the 2nd century, remains of the public baths of the Roman city of Ratae Corieltauvorum. Don't miss the adjacent museum: the Roman mosaics displayed in situ in their original context are far more eloquent than a standard museum case, and entry is free. A brief stop but one that completely changes how you see the city.

⏱ 60 min
3

The National Space Centre is one of the UK's most impressive science museums—entry includes a session in the country's largest planetarium. The 42-metre tower houses rockets in vertical position. The University of Leicester coordinated the mission control for Beagle 2, the British Mars probe launched in 2003—the first ESA mission with public access to the control centre. Allow at least 2 hours.

⏱ 120 min
4

Leicester's outdoor market is Europe's largest covered and open-air market, active since the 12th century. The stalls reflect the city's diversity. The Belgrave Road district, nicknamed 'the Golden Mile', lines up South Asian restaurants and shops for 1.5km—some of England's finest curries outside Birmingham or London are found here.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Twenty-five kilometres southwest of Leicester, the site of the Battle of Bosworth (22 August 1485) is where Richard III met his death in the last major clash of the Wars of the Roses. The Bosworth Battlefield Heritage Centre offers a reconstruction of the terrain and an exhibition on the discovery of the king's remains in 2012. Medieval re-enactments take place in August.

Lincoln

★★★☆☆
1

Lincoln Cathedral was, for more than two centuries, the tallest building in the world. Built from 1072 on William the Conqueror's orders, a spire added in 1311 exceeded Egypt's pyramids—until it collapsed in a storm in 1549. Today the central tower peaks at 81 metres. The interior is one of England's great Gothic cathedrals: seek out the Lincoln Imp, a small sculpture of a grimacing demon in the Angel Choir—legend says he came to wreak havoc before being turned to stone by an angel. Book the Roof Top Tour ahead for panoramic views from the summit.

⏱ 90 min
2

Lincoln Castle, built by William the Conqueror in 1068, houses one of only four surviving copies of the 1215 Magna Carta. It's functioned as a courthouse for 900 years. The Wall Walk lets you circle the complete ramparts with views of the cathedral on one side and the Lincolnshire countryside on the other. The old Victorian prison, with its system of total isolation between prisoners, is one of England's most unsettling.

⏱ 90 min
3

Steep Hill is one of England's finest shopping streets. Independent shops, antique dealers, bookshops and cafés occupy 14th- and 15th-century timber-framed houses. At the top, the Jew's House is one of England's oldest inhabited houses still standing, dating from 1150. At the bottom, High Bridge is England's oldest bridge still in use to have houses built on it—12th century.

⏱ 60 min
4

Newport Arch is a Roman gate from the 2nd century still standing—the only one in Britain still used by motor traffic. The Romans founded Lincoln (Lindum Colonia) around 48 AD as a legionary fortress. Around it, Bailgate's streets preserve the original Roman orthogonal plan. The Medieval Bishop's Palace, just behind the cathedral, is one of England's most important medieval residences—its vineyard, a gift from the twin town of Neustadt in Germany, is still in production.

⏱ 30 min
5

Brayford Waterfront, at the foot of the hill, is one of England's oldest natural harbours—of Roman origin. Today it's Lincoln's lively quarter of bars and restaurants, with the university campus as backdrop. It's the ideal place to end the road trip: a waterside terrace, the cathedral silhouetted on the hill above.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The International Bomber Command Centre, 3km from the centre, is one of the most moving Second World War memorials in the UK. Bomber Command suffered the heaviest losses of all British units—55,573 men killed. The 31-metre central spire corresponds to a Lancaster's wingspan. The walls bear the 57,861 names of the dead. Lincolnshire was nicknamed 'Bomber County' during the war: more RAF bases than any other English county. Free entry.
