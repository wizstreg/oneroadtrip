Reykjavik

★★★★☆
1

Concrete erupting from the hillside like frozen lava: Hallgrímskirkja (Hallgrímstorg 1) looks like nothing else. State architect Guðjón Samúelsson drew inspiration from the basalt columns of Svartifoss to design this 74.5 m spire—construction ran from 1945 to 1986, consecrated for Reykjavík's 200th anniversary. In front of the church stands the statue of Leifur Eiríksson, a gift from the United States to Iceland in 1930, reminding visitors that this Viking explorer reached America five centuries before Columbus. Take the elevator to the tower for views over the colorful rooftops and Faxaflói bay. Tower entrance: around 1400 ISK adults.

⏱ 60 min
2

A ten-minute walk toward the harbor, Harpa (Austurbakki 2) rises like a giant crystal. The concert hall opened on May 4, 2011 after a saga-worthy adventure: begun in 2007, construction was halted by the 2008 financial crisis, then completed thanks to state intervention. The hexagonal glass facade is the work of Danish-Icelandic artist Ólafur Elíasson. The lobby is free to enter; head up to Kolabrautin restaurant for views over the fjord and mountains.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

In the afternoon, head down to Hafnarfjörður (10 km south). The town is the unofficial capital of the Huldufólk, the 'hidden people'—Iceland's elves. Hellisgerði park, created in 1922 on a lava field, is said to house the elven royal family. The Hidden Worlds walk (starts at Strandgata 6, alfar.is) is a 90-minute guided tour led by a storyteller who points out the inhabited rocks. Book ahead, around €30. Quirky but genuine: this isn't tourist marketing, it's a mythological bedrock preserved intact since the 13th-century Eddas.

Thingvellir

★★★★★
1

Walking through the Almannagjá canyon means having both feet on two continents at once: the North American plate on your left, the Eurasian on your right, drifting apart at about 2 cm per year. This national park was declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2004 for two inseparable reasons: its unique geology and its founding role in Icelandic history. Here, the Alþingi held open-air sessions from 930 CE to 1798—a legislative and judicial assembly, the world's oldest still in operation. In the year 1000, under pressure from the King of Norway, Icelanders voted here to adopt Christianity while quietly permitting private paganism for a few generations. Free admission, parking around €7. Allow 2 hours.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Geysir geothermal area (64.3148, -20.3022) gave its name to all geysers worldwide—the great original Geysir is now dormant. Its neighbor Strokkur erupts every 6 to 10 minutes, shooting 20-30 m high in a surge of boiling blue water before the explosion. Arrive early or late in the day to avoid the tour buses. Free access.

⏱ 60 min
3

Gullfoss, the 'Golden Falls', plunges in two tiers of 11 m and 21 m into a narrow, perpendicular gorge—32 m total drop, permanent mist, frequent rainbows. In the early 20th century, investors wanted to build a hydroelectric plant here. Sigríður Tómasdóttir (1871-1957), daughter of the landowner, fought fiercely: she spent her savings on a lawyer, walked on foot to Reykjavík and threatened to throw herself into the falls. The project ultimately collapsed when the operators defaulted on payments—but her struggle made her Iceland's first environmentalist. Her memorial stands near the site. Free access.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Lunch at Friðheimar (Reykholti, 64.1500, -20.4225): a farm that grows tomatoes in geothermal greenhouses year-round, serving unlimited tomato soup among the flowering plants. Original and delicious. Around €30, reservation essential.

Seljalandsfoss

★★★★★
1

You're going to get soaked. Seljalandsfoss (63.6156, -19.9886) drops 60 m and a narrow path runs behind the curtain of water—the only waterfall in Iceland where this is possible. A raincoat isn't enough: in summer the spray reaches 20 m around. Come early morning or after 6 PM to avoid the buses, and note that the path closes in winter due to ice. Parking around 1000 ISK, waterfall free.

⏱ 30 min
2

500 m to the north, Gljúfrabúi hides in a split gorge: you enter through the stream, feet in the water, and discover a waterfall enclosed in its chamber of basalt. Almost no one goes there. It's free and takes 20 minutes.

Vik

★★★★☆
1

The Viking Þrasi supposedly hid a chest of gold behind Skógafoss—nobody's found it. The waterfall itself is real: 60 m wide, one of Iceland's highest flow rates, and a side staircase that climbs to the edge for a plunging view. The Fimmvörðuháls trail starts here to reach Þórsmörk in 25 km. Parking around 1000 ISK.

⏱ 45 min
2

Reynisfjara (63.4053, -19.0444) is one of Iceland's most spectacular black sand beaches—basalt columns in the cliff (Hálsanefshellir cave), sea stacks offshore (Reynisdrangar). Legend says these stacks are two trolls and a longship petrified by the sun as they tried to drag a ship ashore in the night. The sea is genuinely dangerous year-round: sneaker waves surge without warning and have killed visitors. Never turn your back on the ocean, stay away from the wet zone. Parking around 1000 ISK.

⏱ 50 min

Activities

If the day stretches long in summer (the sun doesn't set until 11 PM in June), detour to the Sólheimasandur plane wreck. This US Navy DC-3 crashed on the black sand beach in 1973—the entire crew survived. The fuselage has lain there since, battered by wind. Allow 45 minutes of walking each way from the parking lot on a flat but windy trail.

⏱ 60 min

For hiking enthusiasts, a walk along the Reynisfjall ridge connects Reynisfjara beach to the village of Vík from above. The trail offers dizzying panoramas of the coast on both sides. Bring hiking boots and a windbreaker—the wind blows hard on the ridge.

Fjadrargljufur

★★★★★
1

A hundred meters of walls covered in acid-green moss, a river snaking below, and almost no one around—Fjaðrárgljúfur feels like a place you invented yourself. The canyon runs 2 km long and carved itself in 9000 years through the lava. The rim trail takes about an hour; the viewpoint at the end gives you the full picture. Free access; arrive early, crowds rise after 10 AM in season.

Jokulsarlon

★★★★★
1

Beneath your feet, black volcanic sand. Above, the tongue of Europe's largest glacier outside the Arctic—Vatnajökull, 7700 km², 8% of Iceland's territory. Skaftafell (64.0162, -16.9678) is the southern entry point to the national park. The hike to Svartifoss (45 minutes round trip) leads to a 20 m waterfall framed by black basalt columns: these same pillars inspired architect Guðjón Samúelsson for Hallgrímskirkja. The walk is easy—stay on marked trails, never walk on the glacier without a guide.

⏱ 90 min
2

There are places you recognize in photos you've never seen, that don't disappoint in person—Jökulsárlón (64.0784, -16.2306) is one of them. Icebergs calved from Breiðamerkurjökull glacier drift across the lagoon before melting into the sea, light plays through blue ice blocks streaked with volcanic ash, seals swim among them. A zodiac excursion (May-October, allow 75 min, rate around 15-17,000 ISK) lets you approach the icebergs and glacier wall within a few meters. Book ahead.

⏱ 120 min
3

Black sand, translucent ice, raking light: Diamond Beach (64.0431, -16.1789) is one of Iceland's most copied images, and it deserves its reputation. Melting icebergs from Jökulsárlón wash ashore here as irregular blocks, each one different. Free. Go at sunset if you can—light passes through the ice like crystal.

Egilsstadir

★★☆☆☆
1

Thirty-four giant granite eggs placed at the water's edge, each representing a nesting bird of the region—sculptor Sigurður Guðmundsson's installation Eggin í Gleðivík is absurd and beautiful at once. The fishing village behind is tiny. A 20-minute photo stop, free.

⏱ 25 min
2

Petra Sveinsdóttir spent 80 years collecting stones in her region. Result: a house and garden in Stöðvarfjörður (64.8311, -13.8569) overrun with tons of agates, jasper and opal—turned into a museum after her death in 2012. It's one of those improbable places that only exist because one person decided it was a good idea. Around €13, allow 1 hour.

⏱ 60 min
3

If you have energy for a 30 km detour, Seyðisfjörður (65.2622, -14.0089) is worth it: a deep fjord, an artists' village with colorful wooden houses, a blue church at the end of a rainbow-painted path from 2016. It's also the port where the Norröna ferry docks from Denmark—passengers arrive here with cars loaded up for the Ring Road. The winding road descending into the fjord is spectacular in itself.

Myvatn

★★★★☆
1

If you expected an ordinary rock forest, Dimmuborgir will disorient you. This lava field formed about 2300 years ago when a flow invaded a swamp: steam from water trapped beneath the magma blew hollow pillars and arches that erosion has sculpted into black castles. Here, according to Icelandic mythology, live Grýla the ogress and her 13 sons, the Yule Lads—each one descends on one of the 13 nights before Christmas to put a candy (or a rotten potato) in children's shoes. The Kirkjan cave, a natural arch shaped like a Gothic chapel, is the most photographed formation. Three marked trails from 15 to 60 min. Free access.

⏱ 90 min
2

Twenty minutes climbing and you stand on the rim of a perfectly circular crater—1 km in diameter, drawn with a compass. Hverfjall (65.6019, -16.8753) is a tephra cone formed during a volcanic eruption, 140 m above Lake Mývatn. The panorama from the rim embraces Dimmuborgir, the lake and the lava fields. Don't descend into the crater: it's forbidden to protect the vegetation.

⏱ 50 min
3

A crack in the lava, a few meters descent, and turquoise hot spring water glows in the dark—Grjótagjá (65.6261, -16.8775) looks like a manufactured set. This is where the Jon Snow-Ygritte scene was filmed in Game of Thrones. Bathing is forbidden: the water reaches 50°C and the temperature varies unstably with the region's volcanic activity. Free, 20 minutes.

⏱ 20 min
4

Námafjall / Hverir (65.6406, -16.8081): violent fumaroles, boiling mud pools in ochre, yellow sulfur on red background. The sulfur smell warns from afar. Marked trails—don't leave the designated paths. One of Iceland's most spectacular geothermal sites. Free.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The Mývatn Nature Baths (65.6308, -16.8472): thermal baths in milky blue-turquoise water maintained at 36-40°C. Less famous and cheaper than the Blue Lagoon, they sit at the heart of the volcanic region you just explored. Around €45 adults. Allow 2 hours. Reservation recommended in season.

Akureyri

★★★☆☆
1

If you're looking for a founding myth to attach to an image, here it is: in the year 1000, according to the saga, chieftain Þorgeir Ljósvetningagoði supposedly threw his statues of Norse gods into this waterfall after voting for conversion to Christianity—hence the name 'Waterfall of the Gods'. The original historical source (Íslendingabók) doesn't mention this episode; the story appears to be a 19th-century construction. The waterfall itself is indisputable: a 12 m arc across the Skjálfandafljót river, with trails on both sides. Free access.

⏱ 45 min
2

After days of roads without towns, Akureyri feels like a capital—it only has 19,000 inhabitants. Akureyrarkirkja (1940), designed by the same architect as Hallgrímskirkja, dominates from its hilltop at the top of a monumental staircase. Below, the botanical garden Lystigarðurinn is free and cultivates around 7000 species at this northern latitude. Stroll the pedestrian center of Hafnarstræti before hitting the road for Húsavík.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

An hour's drive northeast of Akureyri, and you board a traditional wooden boat to search for humpback whales in Skjálfandi bay. Húsavík has built a European reputation for whale watching: local operators report over 95% sighting success in season. North Sailing (pioneers for over 30 years) and Gentle Giants offer 3-hour trips, around 12-13,000 ISK adults, April-October. Book at least the day before—morning departures fill quickly. The whale museum (Hafnarstétt 1) is worth an extra hour for its 11 complete skeletons.

⏱ 240 min

Reykholt

★★★☆☆
1

Fifteen meters of basalt rising from the sea shaped like a drinking dragon—Hvítserkur (65.6064, -20.6386) is the North Iceland photo you see less often. Legend says a troll from Strandir wanted to destroy the nearby Þingeyrar monastery, but the sun petrified him before he arrived. The detour from Route 1 is about 25 km on paved track. Allow 30 min.

⏱ 30 min
2

A man was murdered in his cellar here on September 23, 1241—and the works he'd written at Reykholt (64.6661, -21.2961) are still read today. Snorri Sturluson (1179-1241) composed the Prose Edda there, which saved Norse mythology from oblivion, and the Heimskringla, history of the kings of Norway. Snorralaug, his medieval thermal bath fed by a natural spring, remains intact, complete with the underground tunnel that led from his house. Snorrastofa, the attached study center, tells his life. Around €10, allow 1.5 hours.

⏱ 90 min
3

5 km from Reykholt, Deildartunguhver (64.6633, -21.4006) is Europe's highest-flow geothermal spring: around 180 liters per second at 97°C. The steam plume is visible from afar. The Krauma baths next door let you end the day in hot water (around €45).

Snaefellsjokull

★★★★★
1

Impossible to miss: Kirkjufell (64.9419, -23.3066) is Iceland's most photographed mountain, a 463 m black pyramid at the fjord's edge. It served as 'the arrowhead' in Game of Thrones. Kirkjufellsfoss waterfall at its feet gives the classic composition. No summit hike without a guide—the slopes are technical. Allow 30 min for photos.

⏱ 35 min
2

Jules Verne never saw it—but he chose Snæfellsjökull (64.8083, -23.7758) as the entrance to the center of the Earth in his 1864 novel, after reading descriptions of Iceland. The volcano rises to 1446 m beneath a glacier, its last eruption dates to about 1800 years ago, it remains active and monitored. In clear weather, its silhouette is visible from Reykjavík 120 km away. The national park has surrounded the volcano since 2001; guided snowcat excursions reach the crater some summers.

⏱ 90 min
3

Four stones placed on the black pebble beach once determined who could board the fishing boats: Fullsterkur (154 kg, the strongman), Hálfsterkur (100 kg), Hálfdrættingur (54 kg), Amlóði (23 kg, the useless). If you lifted Hálfsterkur to belt height, you got taken aboard. Twisted debris from an English trawler wrecked in 1948 still litter Djúpalónssandur (64.7475, -23.9039)—Iceland leaves wrecks in place. Free.

⏱ 40 min
4

A three-meter statue welcomes Bárður Snæfellsás at the entrance to Arnarstapi (64.7700, -23.6314)—half-man, half-troll, legendary guardian of the glacier according to local saga. Behind him, the 2.5 km coastal trail to Hellnar follows basalt arches and bridges above the Atlantic. Easy, free, one of the peninsula's best short hikes.

⏱ 60 min
5

The small Búðakirkja (64.8253, -23.3886), a black church isolated in the middle of a lava field in a silent graveyard, is one of the peninsula's most powerful images. No extended interior visit, just a photo stop and a few minutes facing this landscape.

⏱ 20 min

Grindavik

★★☆☆☆
1

The Blue Lagoon (63.8804, -22.4495) isn't a natural phenomenon: it's wastewater from the Svartsengi geothermal plant, rich in silica and minerals, recovered since the 1980s in a lava basin. Not natural—but the milky blue water at 37-40°C, steam over black lava and the Atlantic horizon create a spectacle that lingers in memory. Comfort rate from around 73 USD (dynamic pricing, rises with time and season). Reservation mandatory, sometimes several weeks ahead. Allow 2 hours.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Before returning the car at KEF, the Reykjanes peninsula offers three quick stops in the UNESCO geopark: Brimketill, a natural pool carved by waves in the lava; Gunnuhver, fumaroles and mud pools on ochre and red background; Miðlína, the symbolic bridge between the North American and Eurasian continents. It's the same tectonic rift as Þingvellir, but here in the sea. Allow 1.5 hours for all three if you don't linger too long.

⏱ 90 min
