☀️ Weather

Dry Season (November-April): Peak tourist season with minimal rainfall, comfortable temperatures (20-28°C), and perfect beach weather. Highest prices but ideal conditions for all activities.

Shoulder Season (May, October): Good weather with brief rains, moderate prices, and fewer crowds. Excellent value while maintaining comfortable conditions for most regions.

Hurricane Season (June-September): Hot, humid weather with potential storms on Caribbean and Pacific coasts. Lowest prices and lush landscapes, but weather monitoring essential.

Regional Variations: Northern deserts hot year-round, central highlands mild, coastal areas tropical. Timing depends on specific destinations and preferred activities.

📋 Notes

Ciudad De Mexico

★★★★★
1

Roma Norte is the beating heart of Mexico City. Art Deco buildings from the 1920s-1940s, ivy-covered facades, cobblestone streets opening onto fountain-dotted plazas. Stroll down Álvaro Obregón Boulevard—it's the main drag, pedestrian in the center, lined with sidewalk terraces and galleries end to end.

⏱ 90 min
2

Neighboring Condesa was laid out in the 1920s over a former horse-racing track. Its streets curve in ellipses around two parks. Parque México, with shaded paths and sculptures, serves as Condesa's living room. This is where locals jog, walk their dogs, and read on Sunday mornings.

⏱ 60 min
3

Museo Soumaya offers free admission daily from 10:30 AM to 6:30 PM. The building itself is spectacular—16,000 polished aluminum hexagons designed by Fernando Romero, shifting appearance with the light. Inside: 66,000 works across six levels. The largest Rodin sculpture collection outside France, plus Dalís and Van Goghs—the only ones in Mexico.

⏱ 90 min
4

Mercado Ciudadela is Mexico City's finest handicraft market. Two levels of stalls sell Talavera ceramics, embroidered huipiles, wooden toys, and silver jewelry from Taxco. Bargaining is expected. Bring cash.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Roma/Condesa street art reveals itself best on aimless walks without a map. At Plaza Luis Cabrera in Roma Norte, walls transform into a permanent rotating gallery. Artist Curiot painted one of the neighborhood's most photographed works on Frontera Street—giant pre-Hispanic symbols covering an entire facade.

⏱ 60 min

Prep for your road trip: confirm your rental car, download offline Google Maps for areas without signal, fill the tank. The circuit follows well-maintained federal highways—no 4x4 necessary.

⏱ 45 min

Queretaro

★★★☆☆
1

Querétaro's aqueduct delivers the first visual punch upon arrival. Built between 1726 and 1738 by Marquis Juan Antonio de Urrutia y Arana—local legend claims he built it to bring water to a nun he loved. Seventy-four pink stone arches stretch 1,280 meters long and rise 23 meters high. It still feeds the downtown fountains today.

⏱ 45 min
2

Plaza de Armas anchors the UNESCO-listed historic center (inscribed 1996). Surrounding it: the 18th-century Palacio de Gobierno, arcades sheltering terrace restaurants, and at the center a fountain honoring the marquis who built the aqueduct. It's one of Mexico's best-preserved colonial plazas—not a fast-food joint in sight.

⏱ 60 min
3

Templo de la Cruz sits on a hill at the edge of the historic center. It's the first Franciscan convent founded in the Americas, established in 1531 when the city was founded. From here, the Mirador Los Arcos viewpoint offers the finest aqueduct views, especially late afternoon when the pink stone glows golden.

⏱ 60 min
4

Jardín Zenea is where Queretanos come to pause. A central kiosk, benches beneath giant ficus trees, cafés spilling onto sidewalks. Querétaro consistently ranks as Mexico's most livable city—streets are clean, crime is low, the atmosphere peaceful.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

After dark, the historic center transforms. Storytellers lead nocturnal walking tours through the alleys recounting city legends—Inquisition ghosts, Emperor Maximilian's execution at Cerro de las Campanas in 1867. Surprisingly well-done and hugely popular with Mexican families.

⏱ 90 min

Querétaro's gastronomy deserves a stop at Mercado de la Cruz, a few blocks behind the center. Ground-floor fondas serve enchiladas queretanas, pozole, and tamales for next to nothing. This is where locals eat.

⏱ 60 min

San Miguel De Allende

★★★☆☆
1

Parroquia de San Miguel Arcángel dominates the main plaza with its pink neo-Gothic spires. What most don't know: a self-taught 19th-century mason named Zeferino Gutiérrez redesigned the facade inspired by a postcard of Cologne Cathedral—without ever leaving Mexico. The result is magnificent oddity, improbable tropical Gothic.

⏱ 45 min
2

San Miguel counts over 150 art galleries—a rare concentration for a city of 175,000. Fábrica La Aurora, a former 1902 textile mill converted into a cultural center, gathers 40 studios and galleries under one roof. Original stone arches and machinery create an unlikely backdrop for contemporary sculptures and abstract paintings.

⏱ 90 min
3

The UNESCO-listed historic center is compact and entirely pedestrian. Cobblestone streets like Calle Cuna de Allende, with coral and ochre facades, frame direct views to the Parroquia's spires. The town hosts about 10% foreign expats—the vibe is cosmopolitan but not artificial: local markets and neighborhood festivals coexist with gourmet restaurants.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Climb to Mirador El Chorro late afternoon. The trail starts from Parque Juárez—you'll pass the 18th-century washbasins, still in use. From the lookout, the entire town unfolds backlit: the Parroquia's bell towers, red-tiled roofs, golden hills. It's the circuit's finest sunset.

⏱ 90 min

Wander San Antonio and Guadalupe streets for street art. Over 100 murals cover walls in the peripheral neighborhoods—local and international works, some enormous. The annual Muros en Blanco festival transforms the district every year.

⏱ 60 min

Guanajuato

★★★★☆
1

Guanajuato wedges itself into a ravine. Cars navigate underground tunnels carved from former dried riverbeds—it's a city with entirely subterranean downtown traffic. Houses stack up the slopes, each facade painted a different color. Take time to get lost upon arrival.

⏱ 60 min
2

Jardín de la Unión is the triangular plaza at the heart of everything. Terraces, musicians, and facing you Teatro Juárez—inaugurated in 1903 with Doric columns and statues of Greek muses perched on the pediment. The Belle Époque interior is equally sumptuous. Visit if it's open.

⏱ 75 min
3

Callejón del Beso is the city's narrowest alley—68 centimeters between the two facing balconies. The legend: Ana, a noble's daughter, and Carlos, a miner, met balcony-to-balcony at night. Her father caught them and stabbed his daughter in her lover's arms. Since then, kissing on the third step guarantees seven years of happiness.

⏱ 30 min
4

Universidad de Guanajuato, founded in the 17th century, is one of the Americas' oldest. Its white-and-ochre neo-Baroque neoclassical facade descends in monumental steps into the city. It's the icon—everywhere in travel photos.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Drive through the tunnels upon arrival—one of Mexico's most unusual experiences. Several kilometers of lit two-lane underground galleries, with subterranean roundabouts and exits opening into neighborhood hearts.

⏱ 30 min

Evening, catch a callejoneada starting from Jardín de la Unión. Students in period costume—the estudiantinas—guide groups through the alleys singing ballads and recounting city legends. Men receive clay jugs with drinks, women receive roses. It ends with dancing in the cobblestone streets.

⏱ 120 min

Guanajuato

★★★★☆
1

Alhóndiga de Granaditas was an 18th-century granary. In 1810, Spanish troops barricaded themselves inside against Miguel Hidalgo's 20,000-strong insurgent army. A miner nicknamed El Pípila strapped a stone slab to his back for bullet protection and set the doors ablaze. The city fell. It's the Independence War's first turning point. Today it's a regional museum, closed Mondays.

⏱ 75 min
2

Climb to Monumento al Pípila for the panoramic view. The pink statue depicts the miner holding his torch above the city. From here you see colored rooftops stacking up the hills, church domes, tunnels disappearing underground. This is THE Guanajuato photo. Climb via stairs or take the funicular from downtown.

⏱ 60 min
3

Mercado Hidalgo is a large industrial-style metal hall built in 1910—originally intended as a train station. Inside, two levels of fruit, spice, souvenir, and small restaurant stalls. Downstairs vegetables and local specialties, upstairs artisans. Enchiladas mineras, Guanajuato's signature chicken-and-red-sauce dish, are eaten here.

⏱ 60 min
4

Diego Rivera House-Museum marks the painter's 1886 birthplace. The first floors recreate the period bourgeois apartment with original furniture. The rest hosts temporary exhibitions and a selection of his works. Small but well-curated.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Explore the tunnels on foot—several pedestrian passages cross the city underground, accessing neighborhoods otherwise unreachable in a straight line. It's a labyrinth requiring some orientation but revealing interior courtyards and secret passages at the end.

⏱ 45 min

Wander the stepped callejones above downtown. Alleys climb to residential neighborhoods where houses are painted bright blue, orange, yellow. The higher you climb, the fewer tourists. Improvised miradors between houses offer plunging city views.

⏱ 90 min

Morelia

★★★☆☆
1

Morelia Cathedral began in 1660 and finished in the 18th century—a baroque-neoclassical blend built entirely in local pink stone. Two 64-meter towers dominate Plaza de Armas. The domes are covered in azulejos. Saturday evenings, a sound-and-light show projects onto the facade.

⏱ 60 min
2

Morelia's aqueduct has 253 arches spanning 1,700 meters, built 1705–1730 to channel water from nearby springs. It's superbly illuminated at night. Calzada Fray Antonio de San Miguel runs alongside—a tree-lined pedestrian promenade leading to the Sanctuary of Guadalupe, whose gilded, colorful interior is visually stunning.

⏱ 60 min
3

Palacio de Gobierno, facing the cathedral, merits a detour for Alfredo Zalce's interior murals. They trace Michoacán's history from the Purépecha through Independence. The Conservatory of Las Rosas, founded 1595, is one of the Americas' oldest music schools—its children's choir is renowned throughout Mexico.

⏱ 60 min
4

Palacio Clavijero is a former 17th-century Jesuit college with an impressive baroque octagonal dome. It now hosts quality temporary exhibitions and is free to visit. Just next door: the candy street—dozens of shops selling dulces de Morelia, goat-milk candies and candied fruits specific to Michoacán.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Evening stroll along the illuminated aqueduct, then drinks on a downtown bar terrace. Morelia is lively at night—studious, relaxed, and local. It's a city that often surprises travelers who weren't expecting it.

⏱ 90 min

Morelia

★★★☆☆
1

Pátzcuaro (1 hour from Morelia) is a pueblo mágico built on a lake at 2,000 meters elevation. Plaza Vasco de Quiroga is one of Mexico's largest colonial plazas—200 meters long, ringed by white arcades, centered by a statue of the 16th-century bishop who protected the Purépecha from slavery.

⏱ 60 min
2

The boat crossing to Janitzio (30 minutes) traverses Lago de Pátzcuaro. From the dock, you see the island crowned by the giant statue of Morelos, Independence hero, fist raised. The Purépecha consider this lake the gateway to the underworld—Día de Muertos ceremonies here on November 1st are among Mexico's most authentic.

⏱ 90 min
3

The famous butterfly nets of Purépecha fishermen—shaped like monarch butterfly wings—have become Janitzio and the lake's symbol. Today they're mostly used for tourist photos and demonstrations, commercial fishing having evolved. But the spectacle remains beautiful, especially early morning from the dock.

⏱ 45 min
4

In Pátzcuaro, Casa de los Once Patios is a former 18th-century Dominican construction with eleven interior courtyards. It now houses artisan workshops: golden lacquerware, ceramics, hammered copper from Santa Clara, Purépecha embroidery. It's Michoacán's best place to buy authentic handicrafts directly from artisans.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Excursion to Tzintzuntzan on the return (15 minutes from Pátzcuaro). It was the Purépecha empire capital before the conquest—the only civilization that resisted the Aztecs in the 15th century, notably through their copper mastery. Five terraced pyramids (the yacatas) overlook the lake. Paid admission.

⏱ 60 min

Pátzcuaro's artisan market around Plaza Gertrudis Bocanegra. The former church converted into a library houses at the back a monumental fresco on Michoacán's history—often overlooked by guides, very beautiful.

⏱ 60 min

Guadalajara

★★★☆☆
1

Hospicio Cabañas is the main reason to come to Guadalajara. Built in 1810 as an orphanage and hospital, this UNESCO-listed complex houses in its chapel 57 murals by José Clemente Orozco, painted 1938–1939. The showstopper: 'El Hombre de Fuego' (Man of Fire) beneath the dome—a figure consumed by flames, considered by Orozco himself his masterwork. Take time to lie on the benches and gaze at the ceiling.

⏱ 90 min
2

Guadalajara's Metropolitan Cathedral is recognizable from afar by its two blue azulejo towers. Construction began 1541, in hybrid baroque-neoclassical style. The interior holds canvases attributed to Murillo and Rubens. Plaza de la Liberación in front links four successive plazas all the way to the Hospicio.

⏱ 45 min
3

Tlaquepaque, technically an independent town absorbed by the metropolis, is Guadalajara's artisan quarter. Pedestrian Calle Independencia is lined with 19th-century mansions converted to galleries. Blown glass, talavera, and Huichol beadwork pieces (colored glass beads on wooden figures—work taking several weeks) are the signature purchases. El Parián, the round plaza ringed by restaurants, pulses with mariachi groups morning to night.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Evening in Chapultepec neighborhood, Colonia Americana. Avenida Chapultepec is Guadalajara's hippest corridor—independent cafés, cocktail bars, galleries spilling onto sidewalks. The vibe picks up from 7 PM when students and young professionals fill the terraces.

Guadalajara

★★★☆☆
1

Mercado San Juan de Dios is Latin America's largest covered market—3,000 stalls over three levels. Electronics, clothing, handicrafts, jewelry, and at every corner fondas serving torta ahogada, the sandwich drowned in spicy red sauce, Guadalajara's emblematic dish. Birria, goat stew, is also eaten here as quesabirrias covered in melted cheese.

⏱ 75 min
2

The town of Tequila sits 65 km from Guadalajara, at the foot of the volcano of the same name. It's a pueblo mágico surrounded by UNESCO-listed blue agave fields. Agaves used for tequila production take 7–12 years to mature—you see them stretching endlessly, their blue-green lance-shaped leaves under Jalisco sun.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Half-day excursion to Tequila. Visit a distillery—La Rojeña by José Cuervo is Mexico's largest and oldest, but smaller artisanal distilleries are often more instructive. Tasting a blanco, reposado, and añejo reveals the evolution through oak-barrel aging.

⏱ 240 min

Stroll through downtown Tequila after the distillery. The main plaza, giant multicolored 'TEQUILA' letters for photos, jimador (agave harvester) sculptures, and a cantarito—the local cocktail served in earthenware pottery with tequila and grapefruit juice. At 9 PM, the priest emerges from the temple and rings the three bells while all the townspeople rise wherever they are.

⏱ 90 min

Sayulita

★★★☆☆
1

Sayulita, a designated pueblo mágico of 5,000 residents, sits 40 km north of Puerto Vallarta. People who knew Puerto Vallarta before it became a major resort say Sayulita resembles what it was in the '70s. Colorful cobblestone alleys, Huichol art galleries, hammocks everywhere, dogs sleeping on grocery store thresholds.

⏱ 60 min
2

San Pancho (San Francisco) sits 7 km north of Sayulita—even quieter, even less touristy. A wide exposed beach, few restaurants, lots of locals. If Sayulita feels too busy, San Pancho is your refuge. The two connect easily by taxi or on foot along the coast.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

First Pacific immersion at Sayulita beach. The main beach splits in two around a rocky outcrop—one side for surfers and lessons, the other a calmer zone. Waves are gentle for beginners December through April. Surf schools offer daily lessons.

⏱ 180 min

Climb Cerro del Mono late afternoon—count on 1 hour up, 350 meters elevation. From the summit, 360° views across all of Banderas Bay, Punta Mita, offshore Islas Marietas, and sunset over the Pacific. The sky paints itself orange and violet with no obstacles on the horizon.

Free beach day in Sayulita if the Marietas excursion isn't possible. Surf lessons on the main beach—instructors are there from 8 AM. Grilled fish on the waterfront, shrimp ceviche, and marlin tacos are the reference dishes of the Nayarit coast.

Sunset from the main beach with a drink. The Pacific faces due west—Sayulita sunsets rank among the finest on the Mexican coast. Colors often last 30–40 minutes after the sun disappears.

Puerto Vallarta

★★★★☆
1

Puerto Vallarta's Malecón is a 1,300-meter promenade facing Banderas Bay. No cars—only pedestrians, palm trees, and every 100 meters a bronze sculpture. The collection brings together fifteen works by Mexican artists installed since 1960. Most famous: 'La Rotonda del Mar,' eight monumental thrones resembling a Tim Burton scene. At the end, the Voladores de Papantla perform several times daily—they descend spiraling from a 30-meter pole hanging by their feet.

⏱ 90 min
2

Zona Romántica, also called Old Town or Viejo Vallarta, is the historic district south of the Cuale River. Cobblestone streets, white houses with red-tiled roofs, wrought-iron balconies overflowing with bougainvillea. This is where locals eat, where neighborhood bars and the best street food concentrate—lime ceviche, shrimp tacos, churros.

⏱ 75 min
3

Iglesia Nuestra Señora de Guadalupe, with its distinctive crowned dome, is Puerto Vallarta's symbol visible from everywhere. Built early 20th century, the bell tower was damaged in a 1995 earthquake—the crown was remade in stone-covered concrete. Nighttime processions pass the church almost nightly during the Virgen de Guadalupe festivities in December.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Sunset from Playa Los Muertos. Puerto Vallarta's best-known beach faces directly onto the Pacific. The Los Muertos pier spirals out into the sea—it's the best spot to watch the sun disappear into the water with no obstacles. Beach bars serve margaritas and cold beer with feet in the sand.

⏱ 90 min

Dinner in Zona Romántica. It's the circuit's finest gastronomic evening. Pescado zarandeado (fish marinated and charcoal-grilled, served on banana leaves), raicilla (local mountain agave spirit, rustier than tequila), and lime-marinated seafood. Small street restaurants are worth as much as white-tablecloth establishments.

⏱ 120 min

Final waterfront evening on the Malecón. The sculptures shift appearance under streetlight glow. Promenade restaurants serve until midnight. The Pacific, at this hour, makes its waves heard beneath the conversations.
