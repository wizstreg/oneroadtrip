Orvieto

★★★☆☆
1

Duomo di Orvieto - Pope Nicholas IV laid the first stone of this Gothic cathedral on November 13, 1290, to house the corporal stained with blood from the miracle of Bolsena (1263). Its construction spanned three centuries, involving Fra Bevignate, Lorenzo Maitani (who arrived in 1308), Andrea Pisano, and Andrea Orcagna. The triptych façade—one of Italy's most spectacular—is divided into three superimposed registers of bas-reliefs, golden mosaics, and four pilasters carved by Maitani between 1325 and 1330. The central rose window, Orcagna's work, is framed by 52 sculpted heads symbolizing the weeks of the year. The flanks alternate white marble and black basalt, a legacy of the Sienese tradition. Inside, the Cappella San Brizio preserves Luca Signorelli's fresco cycle of the Last Judgment (1499-1502), considered one of the Italian Renaissance's major works. Sigmund Freud mentions this chapel in The Psychopathology of Everyday Life, recounting how he forgot Signorelli's name.

⏱ 90 min
2

Pozzo di San Patrizio - In 1527, after the Sack of Rome by Charles V's troops, Pope Clement VII took refuge in Orvieto and ordered this well's construction to guarantee water during a siege. Florentine architect Antonio da Sangallo the Younger dug between 1527 and 1537 a cylinder 13 meters in diameter and 54 meters deep into the volcanic tuff of the plateau. His technical solution is remarkable: two independent helical ramps of 248 steps each wind around the central well, illuminated by 72 large windows, allowing mules to descend and ascend with water jars without ever crossing paths. The well was never actually used for military purposes: Clement VII reconciled with Charles V before its completion. Its current name, given in the 19th century by monks from the neighboring Servite convent, was inspired by the legend of St. Patrick's Purgatory in Ireland.

⏱ 60 min
3

Cappella Nova (San Brizio) - Accessible from the cathedral, this chapel in the right transept (built from 1408) is the jewel of the visit. Fra Angelico began its vault in 1447 before being recalled to Rome by the pope. In 1499, Luca Signorelli took over and spent two years covering the walls with scenes from the Apocalypse and the Last Judgment of unprecedented violence and anatomical precision. Michelangelo drew direct inspiration from it for the Sistine Chapel.

⏱ 60 min
4

Orvieto Sotterranea - Beneath the upper town's streets, more than 1,200 artificial cavities carved into the tuff constitute a network whose origins go back 2,500 years. Local speleologists began exploring and documenting it in the late 1970s; about 440 cavities have since been mapped and studied. Guided tours traverse Etruscan cisterns from the 5th century BC, water collection tunnels, medieval olive presses, and waste pits that have yielded exceptional stratigraphy of daily life over two millennia. The temperature remains constant throughout the year.

⏱ 75 min
5

Necropolis of the Crucifix of Tuff - At the foot of the cliffs supporting the city, this Etruscan necropolis (6th-3rd century BC) aligns over 200 tuff tombs arranged in an orthogonal plan, like a miniature city of the dead. Each tomb bears the name of the deceased and their clan carved into the lintel. The Etruscan city that overlooked these tombs was called Velzna; the Romans destroyed it in 264 BC and deported its inhabitants to the site of present-day Bolsena.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Orvieto Classico DOC tasting in a tuff-carved cellar - Orvieto's vineyards have been producing wine since antiquity (one still has an Etruscan cellar). Wine cellars in the historic center offer tastings of the dry white Orvieto Classico DOC, a blend of Grechetto and Trebbiano, which Pope Paul III Farnese particularly appreciated.

⏱ 60 min

Panoramic views from the ramparts of Rocca Albornoz - The medieval fortress built by papal legate Albornoz in the 14th century offers circular views of the tuff plateau, the Paglia valley, and the Tuscan hills. The circuit from Porta Rocca to the northern ramparts takes about 40 minutes.

⏱ 45 min

Narni

★★★☆☆
1

Rocca Albornoz - This imposing fortress was built in the 14th century by Cardinal-Legate Egidio Albornoz to establish papal dominance over central Italy. Perched at the edge of the rocky promontory dominating the town and the Nera river, it presents a quadrilateral plan flanked by four corner towers. Its thick walls and ramparts offer panoramic views of the Nera valley and the Narni hills. It served for a long time as a prison; in the 16th century, condemned prisoners whose names remain carved into the walls were detained there.

⏱ 60 min
2

Centro storico di Narni - The medieval town, founded on the site of ancient Roman Narnia (from which C.S. Lewis took the name Narnia for his novel), is organized around Piazza dei Priori with its 13th-century Palazzo del Podestà and its loggia. Stone alleyways span vaulted passages, fountains, and small silent squares. Narni once controlled the Via Flaminia, the strategic road axis linking Rome to the Adriatic: whoever held Narni held the road to Rome.

⏱ 75 min
3

Narni Sotterranea - In May 1979, six young speleologists from Narni climbed a wall on the edge of San Bernardo park and discovered, behind a walled-up door indicated by an old gardener, a 12th-13th century church entirely covered with frescoes, forgotten beneath the San Domenico convent. Thirty years of research in the Vatican and Trinity College Dublin archives identified, in an adjacent cell, graffiti in Masonic-alchemical symbols left by an Inquisition prisoner named Giuseppe Lombardini—persecuted by the Dominican order that precisely managed this convent. The guided tour (1h30) also traverses a Roman cistern from the Republican era and the Hall of Torments, where the Holy Office Tribunal interrogated its suspects between the 16th and 19th centuries. Only one visit is possible at a time; reservation required.

⏱ 90 min
4

Ponte d'Augusto - At the entrance to the lower town, a single arch 30 meters in span, remnant of a 1st-century AD Roman bridge that once crossed the Nera in five arches, still stands in the vegetation. It's one of the best-preserved testimonies of Roman road engineering on the Via Flaminia.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Swimming at the Mole di Narni (Stifone) - 5 km from Narni, the Nera river forms remarkably transparent turquoise pools among white limestone rocks. This natural site, once a Roman shipyard area according to ancient sources, is accessible by a path descending from the village of Stifone. The water, fed by karst springs, remains cool even in summer.

⏱ 90 min

Castelluccio Di Norcia

★★★☆☆
1

Borgo di Castelluccio - This village of about 30 inhabitants perched at 1,452 meters altitude is Umbria's highest inhabited settlement. It dominates the Pian Grande, a 15 km² karst plateau formed by the drying of a lake about one million years ago. The October 2016 earthquake (magnitude 6.5) destroyed most of the village; its reconstruction spans several years but access to the plateau is restored. 360° panoramic views of the Sibillini Mountains, including the Monte Vettore summit (2,476 m), the watershed between Umbria and Marche.

⏱ 60 min
2

Piana di Castelluccio - This high karst plateau comprises three plains: Pian Grande, Pian Piccolo, and Pian Perduto. Between late May and mid-July, the simultaneous blooming of lentils (white flowers with bluish reflections), poppies, cornflowers, wild mustard, and Venus's looking-glass covers the 20 km² of cultivated land with a mosaic of colors changing week by week. The phenomenon is due to pesticide-free agriculture practiced since antiquity: farmers have preserved production methods unchanged for 3,000 years (Castelluccio lentil seeds have been found in Neolithic tombs). Each species blooms at a slightly different period, which modifies the dominant colors of the landscape throughout the season.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Hiking on the trails of Parco Nazionale dei Monti Sibillini - The national park covers over 70,000 hectares between Umbria and Marche. From Castelluccio, several marked trails reach the ridges overlooking the plateau in 2 to 4 hours. The trail to Rifugio Perugia offers views of Monte Vettore's fossil glacier.

⏱ 180 min

Tasting of lenticchie di Castelluccio IGP - Grown at 1,500 meters altitude on the karst plains, these small round lentils with skin so thin they cook without soaking in 20 minutes obtained IGP label in 1997 (some sources indicate 1999 for the EU version). They are rich in protein, iron, and fiber. Sold directly in the village's few grocery stores or to restaurants around Norcia, which make soups and side dishes with local olive oil and black truffles.

⏱ 45 min

Paragliding and hang gliding above Pian Grande - The plateau's configuration surrounded by reliefs over 2,000 meters generates thermals favorable for free flight. Local operators offer tandem flights allowing you to admire the flowering mosaic from the sky.

⏱ 90 min

Rasiglia

★★☆☆☆
1

Borgo dei Ruscelli - The first mentions of Rasiglia date back to the early 13th century under the name Curtis de Rasilia, in the archives of Sassovivo Abbey. The hamlet, a frazione of Foligno at 600 meters altitude, owes its identity to the Capovena spring, which gushes in the upper part of the village and divides into a series of channels, small waterfalls, and wooden bridges crossing the stone alleyways. These waters fed a flourishing textile industry from the Middle Ages: fulling mills, spinning mills, dyeworks, and wool factories operated until the mid-20th century. At its peak, the village had three wool factories with dyeworks, three grain mills, a post office, and a bank branch. The 1997 earthquake nearly finished it off; its gradual renaissance through cultural tourism is described locally as the "miracolo Rasiglia."

⏱ 60 min
2

Peschiera and ruins of the Trinci castle - After crossing the entire village, the various streams converge in a large artificial basin, the Peschiera, before joining the Menotre river. On the hill, the remains of the castle built by the Trinci lords (masters of Foligno from 1305 to 1439) today consist of a section of walls and two small defensive towers, partially restored in the 1990s. The castle controlled the Via della Spina, a commercial route linking the Adriatic to Rome. The entire center is pedestrian.

⏱ 45 min
3

Traditional weaving workshop - The village preserves and showcases the instruments of the medieval textile industry: carding machines, reels, wooden looms, and a mechanical Jacquard loom from the early 20th century. A small hydroelectric plant at the foot of the spring, built in the early 20th century to power the wool factories, is also visible.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Stroll through the alleyways along the channels - The pedestrian circuit (about 1 km) winds from the Capovena spring to the Peschiera along the two old 15th-century mills, the public washhouse, and stone houses with moss-covered walls. In winter, the hamlet hosts a living nativity scene in the alleyways, recreating traditional crafts in period costumes.

⏱ 60 min

Hike to the Menotre waterfalls (Altolina) - A few kilometers downstream from Rasiglia, the Menotre river forms a series of waterfalls in a wooded canyon accessible from the Oasi dell'Altolina in Belfiore di Foligno. Two entrances allow you to explore the canyon from upstream or downstream.

⏱ 120 min

Spello

★★★★★
1

Chiesa di Santa Maria Maggiore and Cappella Baglioni - This Romanesque collegiate church founded in the 11th-12th centuries houses Spello's major artistic treasure. In 1500, Prior Troilo Baglioni, future Bishop of Perugia, commissioned painter Bernardino di Betto known as Pinturicchio to decorate a side chapel completely. Pinturicchio had just finished decorating the Borgia Apartment in Rome for Pope Alexander VI. Between summer 1500 and spring 1501, he covered the three walls with the Annunciation, the Nativity, and the Dispute of Jesus with the Doctors, and the vault with the four Sibyls. This cycle, unanimously considered the pinnacle of his production, includes a self-portrait of the artist on the right wall, signed "BERNARDINVS PICTORICIVS PERVSINVS." The floor is paved with Deruta ceramics from 1566. This was Pinturicchio's last important commission in Umbria before his departure for Rome and Siena where he died in 1513.

⏱ 75 min
2

Roman gates and ramparts - Spello, called Hispellum in Roman times, preserves several sections of its Augustan ramparts from the 1st century BC. The Porta Venere, flanked by two polygonal towers from the 1st century, is one of the best preserved in Italy. The Porta Consolare, opening onto Corso Cavour, dates from the same century and still bears on its facade three Roman statues from the imperial era reused in the Middle Ages. Julius Caesar already described Hispellum as a haven of peace.

⏱ 45 min
3

Villa dei Mosaici di Spello - Discovered during construction work in 2012, this 4th-century AD Roman villa is one of Umbria's most important archaeological sites. Its polychrome mosaics depict mythological scenes and geometric patterns in exceptional preservation. The ensemble is sheltered under a modern museographic structure open to the public.

⏱ 60 min
4

Centro storico - The alleyways of Spello, with houses of pink and yellow stone quarried from Monte Subasio, are covered with climbing plants and flower pots. The annual competition among residents for the most beautiful floral decoration of the vicoli makes Spello one of Italy's most flowered towns.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Infiorate of Corpus Domini - Every year in June, Spello's residents spend Saturday night to Sunday morning covering the hamlet's streets with elaborate flower carpets depicting sacred scenes, prepared collectively by neighborhood. Petals, leaves, colored sands, and other natural plant materials form compositions several dozen meters long. The carpets are blessed in the morning during the Corpus Domini procession, then trampled. This tradition dates back to the 19th century.

⏱ 120 min

Hiking on Monte Subasio - From Spello, a trail climbs through garrigue of olive trees and broom toward the summit plateau of Monte Subasio (1,290 m), from which the panorama embraces the Umbrian valley, Assisi, Perugia, and the Sibillini Mountains. The round-trip route to the rifugio takes about 4 hours.

⏱ 240 min

Montefalco

★★★★☆
1

Complesso Museale di San Francesco - This Gothic church built between 1335 and 1338 by the Friars Minor was converted into a municipal museum in 1990. Its apse preserves Benozzo Gozzoli's most important fresco cycle in Umbria: in 1450-1452, this Florentine painter (student of Fra Angelico in Florence) covered the five sections of the apse with twenty episodes from the Life of Saint Francis in three ascending registers, symbolically rising from Birth to celestial glory. In the scene of the meal at the knight Celano's (episode 12), a carafe of red wine is visible on the table—likely an allusion to local Sagrantino. On the counter-façade, a 1503 Nativity by Perugino (Pietro Vannucci) completes the pictorial ensemble. The Franciscan cellars discovered during 2006 restoration yielded a pressing vat and period tools.

⏱ 90 min
2

Piazza del Comune - This semicircular square, also called Piazza del Certame, is the hamlet's heart. It's surrounded by the Palazzo del Comune with its bell tower, the Municipal Theater, and Palazzo Bernardino de Cuppis. All of Montefalco's streets converge here from the ramparts. A covered passage connects the palace to the vineyard sector below.

⏱ 30 min
3

Santuario di Santa Chiara della Croce - This sanctuary houses the chapel decorated with 14th-century frescoes of the Franciscan order, attributed to a student of Giotto. Adjacent, a cloistered Poor Clares monastery preserves a vegetable garden and a stone cloister. It's one of the most silent spiritual places in central Umbria.

⏱ 45 min
4

Ramparts and panorama over the "Ringhiera dell'Umbria" - Montefalco's nickname comes from the expression Ringhiera dell'Umbria (Umbria's balcony): from its medieval ramparts at 473 meters altitude, the 360° view covers Assisi, Spoleto, Foligno, the Sibillini Mountains and, on clear days, Perugia. The town itself owes its name to Frederick II of Swabia who, visiting in the 13th century, noted the abundance of falcons in the region and baptized the hamlet Montefalco in their honor.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Tasting of Sagrantino di Montefalco DOCG - Sagrantino is a black-berry grape variety native to this single micro-region of Umbria, one of the most tannic of all Italian grapes. Its red DOCG wine (Denominazione di Origine Controllata e Garantita) comes from long vinification and barrel aging. Wine estates surrounding the hamlet offer guided tastings with vineyard and cellar visits.

Bevagna

★★★☆☆
1

Piazza Silvestri - This asymmetrical square is one of Umbria's most architecturally coherent. It brings together face to face two 12th-century Romanesque churches built by masters Binello and Rodolfo: San Silvestro (1195, sober façade with elegant portal and three-nave interior with medieval crypt) and San Michele Arcangelo (flat façade with rose window, sculpted portal depicting Saint Michael slaying the dragon, three-nave interior). Between them stands the Palazzo dei Consoli (1270), former Gothic town hall with external staircase and mullioned windows, now home to Teatro Francesco Torti since 1886. A Roman column with Corinthian capital completes the ensemble. Bevagna occupied ancient Mevania, a Roman city on the Via Flaminia, founded at least since the Iron Age.

⏱ 75 min
2

2nd-century Roman baths - On Via di Porta Guelfa, these public baths rediscovered in 1891 preserve a remarkable black and white mosaic floor depicting fantastic sea creatures: tritons, seahorses, lobsters, squid. They testify to Mevania's prosperity in the 2nd-3rd centuries, when the city drew its wealth from the waterway and commerce.

⏱ 45 min
3

Medieval craft workshops - The Mercato delle Gaite cultural association has reconstructed medieval craft workshops in the hamlet accessible for visits year-round by reservation: coin-minting workshop, silk-weaving loom (with a wheel of complexity close to an industrial machine), parchment-making, and apothecary's workshop.

⏱ 60 min
4

Cinta muraria - Bevagna's medieval ramparts, erected on foundations of earlier Roman walls, are almost entirely preserved. Their circumference is punctuated by square, round, and polygonal towers and four Gaite (access gates). It's possible to walk along part of the outer ramparts.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Mercato delle Gaite (June) - For ten days in June, Bevagna's four historic quarters recreate medieval life in competition: crafts, cuisine, banners, and games. Residents wear period costumes and workshops operate in public. It's one of Umbria's most authentic medieval recreations.

Bettona

★★☆☆☆
1

Mura etrusche - Bettona, which was called Vettona in Umbrian-Etruscan times, preserves sections of 4th-century BC Etruscan walls in local sandstone around its perimeter, covered in the 13th-14th centuries by medieval ramparts that used them as foundations. From Porta Romana, several sections of this irregular polygonal apparatus characteristic of Etruscan construction are still visible. On the outskirts, on the Perugia road at the locality called Colle, an Etruscan tomb entirely built in local sandstone is accessible. In 1352, Perugia almost completely destroyed the town during a siege, leaving only the churches intact.

⏱ 60 min
2

Belvedere and panorama - From the highest point of the village perched at 353 meters, the view embraces the Umbrian plateau across its full width: Assisi with the Basilica of Saint Francis recognizable in the distance, Foligno, Spello and the foothills of Monte Subasio to the east, the Tiber valley to the northwest and the hills toward Perugia. Bettona was the outpost that controlled, beyond the Tiber, the border between Umbrian territory and Etruscan lands.

⏱ 40 min
3

Pinacoteca comunale - The 14th-century Palazzo dei Priori houses an art gallery assembling works from the Umbrian and Tuscan schools. Notable pieces include paintings from Giotto's school from the 14th century, a collection of 15th-16th century altarpieces, and detached frescoes from local religious buildings. The Oratory of Saint Andrew preserves a 13th-century carved wooden coffered ceiling with a cycle on the Passion of Christ attributed to Giotto's school (1394).

⏱ 45 min
4

Centro storico - Via di Mezzo, the former axis of the Roman town, crosses Bettona from Porta Vittorio Emanuele to Porta Romana. The perpendicular alleyways, accessible only on foot, are lined with stone houses and hanging gardens. Piazza Cavour brings together the main civic and religious monuments in a very compact space.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walk along the outer ramparts - The outer rampart path allows you to walk along the medieval fortifications over a large part of the perimeter. In autumn, the vines and olive trees on the slopes color the landscape rust and gold.

⏱ 45 min

Corciano

★★☆☆☆
1

Chiesa di Santa Maria Assunta - Corciano's parish church houses two major works. The first is the Pala di Corciano, an Assumption altarpiece painted in 1513 by Pietro Vannucci known as Perugino, native of Città della Pieve and Raphael's master: it's one of his last important commissions before his death (1523). The second is the Gonfalone della Madonna della Misericordia painted in 1472 by Benedetto Bonfigli, depicting the Virgin protecting Corciano's inhabitants under her cloak—a processional banner commissioned during an epidemic. These two works make this small church in a hamlet of 20,000 inhabitants a site of artistic pilgrimage.

⏱ 45 min
2

Torrione and medieval walls - The Torrione (1482), keep of the Santa Maria gate, is Corciano's symbol. Built to reinforce the castle's defenses against recurrent assaults of the late 15th century (in 1416, the condottiere Braccio Fortebraccio da Montone took Corciano after a long siege), it now houses a permanent exhibition center on medieval and Renaissance culture. The complete, concentric walls enclose the hamlet in a triple circle of well-preserved fortifications.

⏱ 45 min
3

Chiesa-Museo di San Francesco - This 13th-century Gothic church, founded according to tradition after Saint Francis's passage through Corciano in 1223, preserves frescoes datable between the late 13th and early 14th centuries. Its floor is covered with 21 engraved funerary slabs bearing the coats of arms of Corciano's noble families. It now hosts temporary exhibitions and concerts.

⏱ 40 min
4

Centro storico and Piazza Coragino - The hamlet's heart is organized around Piazza Coragino, dominated by a 15th-century circular well with engraved municipal coats of arms. Facing it are the Palazzo del Comune (16th century, former residence of the Della Corgna dukes) and the Palazzo del Capitano del Popolo (15th century). Corciano is classified among Italy's most beautiful hamlets.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Almond tree path and panorama over Lake Trasimeno - From the village, the almond tree path descends toward Monte Malbe through a dense wood. On clear days, the view westward extends to Lake Trasimeno, which Corciano historically regarded as the gateway to the Etruscan lands of Arezzo and Cortona.

⏱ 90 min

Castiglione Del Lago

★★★☆☆
1

Rocca del Leone - This fortress was built in 1247 according to plans by Frate Elia Coppi da Cortona on a limestone promontory that was once Lake Trasimeno's fourth island. Frederick II of Swabia had a first castle erected there—his emblem was the eagle, but the lion symbolized the Empire in Italian tradition, hence the name Castello del Leone. The Rocca presents an irregular pentagonal plan with five towers and a triangular keep about 30 meters high offering a 360° view. A covered passage on murder holes, similar to the one connecting the Vatican to Castel Sant'Angelo, connects it to the neighboring Palazzo della Corgna. Currently used as a natural amphitheater.

⏱ 60 min
2

Palazzo della Corgna - Built in 1560 by Ascanio della Corgna (nephew of Pope Julius III, Marquis of Castiglione and war captain who died at the Battle of Lepanto in 1571), according to plans attributed to either Vignola or Galeazzo Alessi, this Renaissance palace is one of the best examples of late Mannerism in Umbria and Tuscany. Its eight rooms on the piano nobile are entirely decorated with frescoes by Niccolò Circignani known as Il Pomarancio (1574-1590) and Salvio Savini: mythological scenes, Ascanio's military exploits, Aeneid cycle (allusion to the family's first name—Ascanius is Aeneas's son) and a representation of the Battle of Lake Trasimeno of 217 BC. The Hall of the Acts of Ascanio notably depicts the "duel of the century" during which Ascanio pierces his adversary Giannetto Taddei with a single blow.

⏱ 60 min
3

Chiesa di Santa Maria Maddalena - Built in 1836 on the ruins of an earlier medieval church, it houses a 1500 altarpiece depicting the Virgin with Child, Saint Anthony Abbot and Saint Mary Magdalene, attributed to Eusebio da San Giorgio, Perugino's student. The work was for some years at the center of an attribution controversy with Raphael.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Ferry crossing to Isola Polvese - From Castiglione del Lago's pier, the ferry serves the largest of Lake Trasimeno's three islands (about 70 hectares). The island is a regional nature reserve: a 13th-century medieval castle, the ruins of San Secondo's Olivetan monastery and a centuries-old olive grove coexist with preserved lake flora. Trails allow you to circle it in less than two hours.

⏱ 150 min

Lake cycling tour - A 60-kilometer cycling and pedestrian path allows you to make a complete tour of Lake Trasimeno alternating between beach, fields, and villages. The northern section passes through Tuoro sul Trasimeno, site of the battle of June 24, 217 BC where Hannibal ambushed Consul Caius Flaminius and massacred about 15,000 Roman soldiers in the morning fog—one of the greatest defeats in Roman history.

⏱ 240 min
