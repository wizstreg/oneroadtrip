Morella

★★★★☆
1

A hilltop village that earns its place among Spain's most beautiful. Morella clings to a rocky outcrop at nearly 1,000 meters, ringed by 13th-century ramparts that stretch for two kilometers. The castle at the summit offers 360-degree views across the Maestrazgo. Arrive before 10am or after 5pm to dodge the tour buses from Castellón.

⏱ 120 min
2

Built in the 13th century, the Gothic cathedral Santa María la Mayor conceals two carved portals of exceptional delicacy for the region. The spiral staircase in the triforium—suspended in mid-air—dates from the 1380s and has drawn photographers for decades. Paid entry, audioguide available in Spanish.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The scent of cold stone and wax along Cuesta de Liñán signals the town's finest viewpoint. Follow the inner ramparts on the north side: the view plunges across the valleys of Els Ports and, on clear days, reaches the peaks of Gúdar. Bring non-slip shoes—the cobblestones turn treacherous after rain.

⏱ 60 min

Culla

★★☆☆☆
1

Tiny and nearly forgotten, Culla nonetheless appears in Spain's national roster of most beautiful villages. Fewer than 50 permanent residents, limestone-white lanes, and the ruins of a 12th-century Templar castle. The visit takes 90 minutes—take your time finding the house with red geraniums, which is the bakery.

⏱ 90 min
2

If the sky is clear, the panorama from the old watchtower rewards the 10-minute climb. On fine days you can make out the Columbretes Islands to the east—a spectacle that locals reserve for curious visitors who ask the way. The tower isn't signposted: it's just behind the church.

⏱ 20 min

Vilafames

★★★★☆
1

Locals say Vilafamés is their best-kept secret from the Peñíscola crowds. A medieval village perched on a block of rose granite, zigzagging staircases, old houses draped in bougainvillea. The 14th-century castle commands the Castellón plain from 360 meters.

⏱ 60 min
2

The contemporary art museum here genuinely surprises: housed in a 15th-century palace, it holds more than 600 Spanish works from the 20th century. Many artists chose this village to work in during the 1960s and 70s—the museum preserves a trace of that era. Closed Mondays, modest entry fee.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The village's artistic cafés attract painters and sculptors. Settle onto a terrace to watch the ballet of visitors moving between galleries and workshops.

⏱ 60 min

Every lane offers a photogenic frame. The rose-stone houses, pots of geraniums, and perspectives across the countryside compose natural tableaux.

⏱ 60 min

Vilafamés produces wines of Castellón. Several village cellars offer tastings of Mediterranean grape varieties cultivated on the surrounding slopes.

⏱ 60 min

The Fuente de las Picas is a cascading fountain surrounded by centuries-old olive trees. The site combines coolness and panorama over the old town.

⏱ 60 min

Peniscola

★★★☆☆
1

Peñíscola's orange ramparts rise from the sea like a movie set—El Cid was indeed filmed here in 1961. The rock connects the old town to the coast by a narrow isthmus, with the Mediterranean on both sides. Take the lane behind the church to escape the souvenir shops.

⏱ 90 min
2

Antipope Benedict XIII took refuge here in 1415, during the Council of Constance that sought to depose him, refusing to abdicate from this Templar castle until his death in 1423. That detail of papal stubbornness is what makes the place singular. The castle is well preserved, the visit lasts 45 minutes, the audioguide is worthwhile.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Climb early in the morning before the Valencia tour buses to cross the old town without crowds. The viewpoint at the rock's tip, facing the open sea, is the only moment when Peñíscola resembles the guidebook photo rather than a theme park. Arrive before 9:30am in July-August.

⏱ 45 min

Vall D Uixo

★★★☆☆
1

On the surface, Vall d'Uixó has nothing remarkable. Beneath its feet, that's another story: the San José caves shelter the longest navigable underground river in Europe, according to official site data. The main gallery extends over 700 meters explored by boat.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The boat glides under calcite vaults where stalactites brush within centimeters of your face. The underground river remains at a constant 18°C year-round—bring a light jacket even in August. Book online in high season, morning slots fill quickly.

⏱ 60 min

Montanejos

★★★☆☆
1

Montanejos is this itinerary's pleasant surprise. This mountain village owes its reputation to the Fuente de los Baños, a natural thermal spring whose water emerges at 25°C at the foot of a spectacular gorge. The rocks polished by the Mijares River serve as natural loungers—just bring a towel.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

If the water is 25°C even in November, you might as well make the most of it without waiting for summer. Swimming in the Fuente de los Baños happens directly in the river—no infrastructure, no ticket. The gorge trail starts from the village car park and offers viewpoints over the cliffs in a 45-minute round trip.

⏱ 90 min

Sagunto

★★★☆☆
1

Hannibal besieged Sagunto for eight months in 219 BC—the episode that triggered the Second Punic War with Rome. The hill still bears the traces of that clash: Iberian enclosure, then Roman, then Arab, all superimposed in readable strata. Allow a good hour for the full circuit.

⏱ 75 min
2

From the castle summit, the plain spreads to the sea and the chimneys of Valencia port point southward. The 2nd-century Roman theatre, controversially restored in the 1990s, still hosts summer performances. Tickets available on the municipal website.

Chulilla

1

The road to Chulilla seems to lead nowhere—and that's exactly why you should go. The 400-inhabitant village perched on the Turia canyon is one of the region's most renowned climbing destinations, with routes opened on limestone cliffs over 200 meters high.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Turia canyon here is quite different from Valencia's: wild, steep-sided, populated by griffon vultures. The hanging bridges trail (pasarelas) connects two cliffs over a 3km easy walk. Start and finish from the Chulilla car park, total duration 2 hours with photo stops.

Valencia

★★★★★
1

The City of Arts and Sciences impresses by its sheer scale—the complex designed by Calatrava and Bofill stretches two kilometers along the old Turia riverbed. The Hemisfèric and Museu de les Ciències merit the entrance fee; the Oceanogràfic, Europe's largest aquarium according to its official site, deserves an entire half-day.

⏱ 180 min
2

The Lonja de la Seda dates from 1482 and was listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1996. This Gothic trading hall—built when Valencia was one of the Mediterranean's busiest commercial centres—gives a tangible idea of the city's mercantile power at that time. Quick visit, under 45 minutes.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Explore the Barrio del Carmen in the evening, when galleries and tapas bars open their doors. This medieval quarter between the city's two old walls concentrates street art, vermouth taverns, and lively terraces. No reservation needed before 9pm; after that, it's packed.

⏱ 120 min

Valencia

★★★★★
1

Valencia's central market is perhaps Spain's most beautiful covered market—an Art Deco nave of iron and ceramic inaugurated in 1928 that still houses over 900 stalls today. Come between 9am and 11am to see it in action. After 1pm, half the stalls close.

⏱ 60 min
2

The railway station's architecture astonishes: closer to a Belle Époque palace than a utilitarian station, the Estació del Nord was inaugurated in 1917 with Valencian ceramic frescoes on every wall. It still functions—travellers await their trains surrounded by orange-tree mosaics.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

The blue smoke and scent of toasted rice escaping from the large paëlleras signal the good addresses. The true paella valenciana contains no seafood—beans, chicken, rabbit, rosemary. Avoid restaurants that offer it all hours: a proper paella is prepared to order and takes 30 minutes.

⏱ 90 min

El Palmar

★★☆☆☆
1

The fishermen of El Palmar served this rice dish with duck and eels long before Valencia claimed it. The Albufera is a coastal lagoon of around 2,800 hectares separated from the sea by a strip of dunes—the ecological birthplace of Valencian rice cultivation. Arrive at sunset to watch the lake turn red-orange.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

If you rent a barco, skirt the reed beds to the north: ducks and grey herons are denser there and local guides avoid that corner. Boat trips from El Palmar or El Saler, duration 1 hour, guide not compulsory but useful for identifying birds.

Bunol

★★★☆☆
1

The Cueva Turche hides 15 minutes' drive from Buñol, in a valley most visitors ignore in favour of the Tomatina. Result: a near-deserted forest trail leading to a cave with an interior waterfall. The marked circuit is 3km return from the car park.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The water drops seven meters into a natural pool where swimming is possible from June to September. The water temperature stays cool even in midsummer—count on 18°C. Walking shoes essential on the trail, the ground is slippery after rain.

Xativa

★★★☆☆
1

Xàtiva is the town most travellers miss between Valencia and the Costa Blanca. That's a mistake: its double castle—one Iberian, the other Arab-medieval—commands the plain from a 120-meter ridge and can be visited in two hours. The lower town preserves several intact Gothic palaces.

⏱ 120 min
2

The portrait of Philip V hangs upside down in the municipal museum—symbolic revenge for the city's burning in 1707 ordered by that same king. This small detail of iconographic defiance sums up the city's pride better than any explanatory panel.

⏱ 30 min

Gandia

★★★★☆
1

The Palau Ducal dels Borja dates from the 15th century and was the Borgia family's residence before Francis Borgia became a Jesuit and was canonized. The palace is remarkably well preserved, with a gilded gallery and rooms decorated with Manises ceramics. 45-minute guided tour, available in Spanish and Valencian.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Gandia's beach stretches five kilometers of fine sand, separated from the town centre by a residential zone. Less dense than Benidorm, still managed on a human scale. The north section of the beach, near the fishing port, stays quieter and retains a few chiringuitos without amplified music.
