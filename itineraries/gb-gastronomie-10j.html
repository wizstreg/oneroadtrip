London

★★★★★
1

Borough Market condenses a thousand years of food history beneath Victorian iron arches a stone's throw from the Thames. There's been a market near London Bridge since 1014—the current iron-and-glass structures date from 1851, with an Art Deco entrance added in 1932. Arrive before 10am to beat the crowds: the market draws around 20 million visitors a year. Inside, seek out The Ginger Pig for sausage rolls made with heritage-breed pork, Wright Brothers for fresh oysters shucked to order, and Padella for fresh pasta that routinely queues down the street. Take your time—every stall has a story worth hearing.

⏱ 90 min
2

A short walk from Piccadilly Circus, Fortnum & Mason has held court at 181 Piccadilly since 1707—founded by William Fortnum, a footman to Queen Anne who funded his grocery venture by reselling the palace's half-burned candles. This is where the Scotch Egg was invented in 1738 and where the famous picnic hampers packed with Stilton, smoked salmon and champagne for Ascot were born. Head up to the Diamond Jubilee Tea Salon, opened by Queen Elizabeth II herself, for a ceremonial afternoon tea. Don't miss the monumental 4-tonne clock installed in 1964: every hour, figurines of Mr Fortnum and Mr Mason emerge and bow to each other.

⏱ 75 min
3

Maltby Street Market, tucked beneath railway arches in Bermondsey, is where Londoners in the know go. Less touristy than Borough Market, this is where you'll find the city's finest artisans: charcutiers, cheesemongers, sourdough bakers. If you're there on a Saturday morning, that's peak time—locals and restaurant chefs mingle in the narrow archways. It's also a brilliant spot for standing-room lunch eaten straight from the stall.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hunt down proper London fish and chips: look for a traditional chippy in the lanes around Borough Market and order with malt vinegar—that's the local way. The batter should be light and crispy, the fish white and flaky. A complete meal for under a tenner.

⏱ 60 min

Afternoon tea is an institution dating back to the Duchess of Bedford in 1840, who made a habit of filling the 5pm gap with tea and small cakes. Book one of the grand historic hotels in the centre for the full ceremonial experience: warm scones, cucumber-and-cream-cheese sandwiches, cakes and tea cakes served on a tiered stand. Reserve well ahead, especially midweek.

⏱ 90 min

Bath

★★★★☆
1

Sally Lunn's Eating House at 4 North Parade Passage is Bath's oldest house—the foundations date to Roman times and the current building to 1622. According to legend, a Huguenot refugee named Solange Luyon brought her brioche recipe here in 1680: a round, pillowy bun halfway between French brioche and English bread, now known as the Sally Lunn Bun. The recipe is a jealously guarded secret, passed down with the building's lease. Head down to the tiny kitchen museum in the cellars, then order a toasted bun with cinnamon butter—that's the classic way.

⏱ 75 min
2

Bath Farmers Market sets up in the city centre and gives you access to Somerset's finest: artisan cheeses, charcuterie, market-garden vegetables, local beers and ciders. It's one of the West Country's best producers' markets. If you're there on a Saturday morning, arrive early—the best stalls sell out before noon.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The great Bath question: jam first or cream first on your scone? In Somerset, it's cream first, then jam—the exact opposite of neighbouring Cornwall's tradition, and the debate is deadly serious on both sides of the border. Find a tea room near the Abbey to sample artisan Somerset cheeses: aged Cheddar, Bath Soft Cheese or Shropshire Blue would make a fine platter paired with local chutney.

⏱ 75 min

A baking workshop focused on Bath specialities is a wonderful way to spend a morning: scones, Bath Buns (rich with eggs, sugar and currants, with a lump of sugar baked into the bottom) and Sally Lunn Buns are regional classics you won't find anywhere else in England. Classes are offered by several culinary schools in town.

⏱ 90 min

Exeter

★★★☆☆
1

Devon is one of England's gastronomically richest regions, boasting more Michelin-starred restaurants than any county outside London. Exeter Farmers Market is a solid starting point for understanding this abundance: clotted cream with 64% butterfat, Devon cheeses like Sharpham (a raw-milk brie awarded Super Gold at the World Cheese Awards), meat from Ruby Red Devon Cattle, and crab from the Exe estuary. Note: in Devon, you put cream on the scone first, then jam—the exact opposite of neighbouring Cornwall.

⏱ 75 min
2

A few miles from Exeter, Quicke's Cheese at Newton St Cyres is a family cheesemaking farm producing their own aged Cheddars, offering guided tours with tastings. The farm even established a Cheese Academy where you can earn the title of 'Master of Cheese'. If you haven't time for the detour, look for their cheeses at Exeter Farmers Market—the Double Devonshire Truckle is a safe bet.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The cream tea is Exeter's obligatory ritual: warm scone, local clotted cream and strawberry jam. Find a tea room near the Cathedral—the setting is unbeatable. Respect the local order: cream first, jam second. The Cornish do it backwards and will never get over it.

⏱ 60 min

Devon is historically one of the world's finest cider apple terroirs. Trade records show cider was produced here on a large scale as early as 1285. Several cideries offer tours with tastings around Exeter, notably Sandford Orchards in Crediton, which blends traditional methods with a modern approach, complete with shop and tasting room.

⏱ 90 min

Padstow

★★★☆☆
1

Padstow is nicknamed 'Padstein' by Cornish locals, a nod to Rick Stein, who opened his first restaurant on the quay in 1975 and has steadily expanded his empire ever since: The Seafood Restaurant, St Petroc's Bistro, Rick Stein's Café, the fishmonger on the harbour, the cookery school, and even a pub in the neighbouring village of St Merryn. The Seafood Restaurant remains the heart of the operation—it received the AA Food Service Award 2024 for hospitality standards. The menu changes with the catch, but expect lobster thermidor, turbot hollandaise and seafood platters. Book well ahead in summer.

⏱ 90 min
2

Padstow harbour is steps from the restaurant: you'll see fishing boats unloading their catch directly on the quay, and Stein's fishmonger stocks the same species served in the restaurants—turbot from Trevose Head, lobsters and crabs from the Boscastle Belle. If you want ultra-fresh without fine dining, stop by the fishmonger one morning.

⏱ 45 min
3

About forty kilometres south, Newlyn fish market is one of Britain's busiest fishing ports. The daily auction happens early morning—early risers can watch the bidding for mackerel, sardines, sea bass and scallops. It's the true starting point of Cornwall's fish supply chain.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Stein's cookery school in Padstow offers one- or two-day courses focused on Cornish seafood: how to choose a whole fish, fillet it, prepare a court-bouillon or cook a lobster. The level is open to all. It's one of the best ways to understand why Rick Stein has been repeating for 50 years that 'nothing is more exhilarating than fresh fish simply cooked'.

⏱ 480 min

North Cornwall coast oysters are among the country's finest—order them fresh, plain or with a squeeze of lemon, at The Seafood Restaurant's oyster bar or from harbour stalls. It's the most direct way to taste the Cornish sea.

⏱ 45 min

Bristol

★★★★☆
1

St Nicholas Market, or St Nicks to Bristolians, has been open since 1743 and gathers the city's largest collection of independent traders beneath its covered halls. It's divided into three sections—the Glass Arcade, the Covered Market and the Exchange—with over 60 stalls. If you're there on a Friday, the open-air street food market on Wine Street is an unmissable appointment. Inside, look for Pieminister for artisan beef or veggie pies, and stalls selling local Somerset cheeses.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Clifton neighbourhood, just above the centre, is home to several quality delicatessens and cheese shops. Papadeli on Whiteladies Road is a reference point for cakes, salads and its cheese counter. If you're there on a Saturday morning, Whiteladies Farmers' Market is where the city's chefs stock up on local produce.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Bristol has several active gin distilleries in the city centre. Psychopomp, housed in a former grocer's on St Michael's Hill, is one of the best known and offers tasting workshops and 'create your own gin' masterclasses. Six O'Clock Gin, larger, has a distillery in Thornbury with guided tours, and a bar-shop on The Glassboat beside the Avon. Bristol's gin offering is serious—don't leave without a bottle.

⏱ 90 min

Several award-winning gastropubs occupy Bristol's streets. Bristol Lido, wrapped around its heated Victorian pool, is probably the city's most original restaurant: you can swim before dining in magnificent surroundings. The menu relies on local produce from Somerset and Devon. Book your dinner—and if you're tempted, the Swim & Dine package includes pool, spa and restaurant.

⏱ 120 min

Stow On The Wold

★★★☆☆
1

Stow-on-the-Wold is the Cotswolds' highest town, perched at 244 metres on the Roman Fosse Way. Its Market Square, which has hosted markets since 1107, once saw up to 20,000 sheep at a single fair—the 'tures', those narrow stone-walled lanes, were used to funnel flocks toward the square. Today, start at the Cotswold Cheese Company on Digbeth Street: over 120 artisan cheeses are stocked, with a strong focus on local producers. Try the Double Gloucester from Smart's Farm, whose tradition goes back to local cheese competitions.

⏱ 60 min
2

Six kilometres from Stow, Daylesford Organic Farm Shop is a national reference for organic and sustainable farming. You'll find pasture-raised meat, artisan bread, house-made cheeses and an exceptional deli. The farm also has a restaurant, creamery and cookery school. If the budget allows, lunch on site is a fine way to taste what the Cotswolds terroir can deliver.

⏱ 75 min
3

Don't miss Stow's farmers' market, held on the second Thursday of each month on Market Square from 9am to 1pm. Oils, jams, breads, meats and cheeses direct from local producers—this is where 'zero food miles' makes complete sense in the Cotswolds.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

An artisan cheese tasting at the Cotswold Cheese Company can be improvised easily with help from the cheesemongers—they'll guide you through the differences between young and aged Double Gloucester, Shropshire Blue and local goat's cheese. Then assemble a platter to take away for a picnic in the Cotswolds countryside.

⏱ 45 min

The gastropub meal is a Cotswolds institution. The Porch House on Digbeth Street claims the title of Britain's oldest inn, with beams carbon-dated to over 1,000 years. Cotswolds lamb regularly features on the menu—raised on local pastures, the meat is finer and more flavourful than average.

⏱ 90 min

Ludlow

★★★☆☆
1

Ludlow is a British gastronomic anomaly: a small town of 10,000 souls on the edge of Shropshire, tucked between the River Teme and the Welsh hills, that ends up with more Michelin Guide establishments than many large cities. Poet John Betjeman called it 'the loveliest town in England'. The annual Food Festival (in September) brings butchers, cheesemakers, brewers and starred chefs together in the castle moat, with a famous Sausage Trail where five local butchers compete with their house recipes. Even outside the festival, the twice-weekly market and bimonthly farmers' market supply restaurants committed to sourcing within a 30km radius.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Ludlow Food Centre, on the edge of town on the Earl of Plymouth's estate, is a rare gastronomic complex: butchery, charcuterie, creamery, bakery and deli, all made on site with 80% of raw materials from the four neighbouring counties. You'll find Shropshire terrine, pork pies and sausages made with Gloucester Old Spot pork—a local heritage breed. This is the address for understanding what 'local' truly means.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

The Welsh Marches produce perry (pear cider) and ciders among England's finest. Traditional perry, made from astringent perry pears, is found almost nowhere else. Several Ludlow pubs serve local productions on draught—ask for advice at the bar of the Charlton Arms, on the banks of the Teme.

⏱ 60 min

Forelles, right in Ludlow's centre, is one of the town's highest-rated Michelin Guide restaurants: French cuisine with local ingredients, regularly changing menu, worked flavour combinations (salt-and-vinegar beetroot, unexpected pairings). The Charlton Arms on the Teme, run by the Bosi family—Claude Bosi, the two-star chef who put Ludlow on the gastronomic map in the 2000s, is his brother—offers more accessible cooking in a characterful pub setting.

⏱ 120 min

York

★★★★☆
1

York is the world capital of British chocolate—this isn't an image: Terry's was founded here in 1767 near Bootham Bar, and Rowntree's opened in 1862, inventing KitKat, Smarties and Aero. York's Chocolate Story, steps from The Shambles, tells this history through guided tours: from cacao's ritual origins in Central America to Yorkshire's production lines. Allow 1.5 hours, with tasting included and a make-your-own chocolate lollipop workshop to take home.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Shambles, the medieval lane with timber-framed frontages, is mentioned in the Domesday Book of 1086. It was occupied by butchers who hung their meats on hooks still visible on the facades today. Just behind, Shambles Market is open 7 days a week with a permanent food court. Don't miss The York Roast Co and its viral creation: the YorkyPud wrap, a giant Yorkshire pudding stuffed with a complete roast with gravy and stuffing, viewed 19 million times on social media.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

York Cocoa Works, an artisan bean-to-bar chocolate factory in the city centre, offers chocolate-making workshops: you choose the beans, manage roasting and conching, and leave with your bar. It's a hands-on way to understand why York chocolate has a worldwide reputation.

⏱ 120 min

A gastropub crawl through York's centre makes for a brilliant evening: historic pubs serve traditional Yorkshire dishes—roast beef with crispy Yorkshire pudding, cold pork pie, Lancashire hotpot. Local breweries like Yorkshire Brewing Co and several city microbreweries supply amber ales and stouts that pair perfectly with these generous plates.

Cambridge

★★★★☆
1

Cambridge Market Square is one of England's oldest markets, in continuous operation for over 800 years. Fenland producers, cheesemongers, bakers and grocers hold stalls every day. This is the place to buy vegetables from the fen marshes, including the famous Cambridge potatoes or spring asparagus that grows in these flat, fertile lands.

⏱ 60 min
2

Cambridge Burnt Cream—or Trinity Cream—has been claimed by Trinity College since 1879: the story goes that a student proposed the recipe to the college cook, who refused it, before adopting it twenty years later when the student had become a fellow. The college even used a red-hot branding iron to stamp its coat of arms onto the caramelised sugar crust. Trinity College still contests priority with the French over the origin of crème brûlée—a dispute both sides maintain with great pleasure. If you want to taste the official version, some Cambridge restaurants still serve it on the menu.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Cambridge Gin Laboratory, in the city centre, is a microdistillery and gin bar with custom gin creation workshops. You choose your botanicals from dozens of options, distill your own recipe and leave with your labelled bottle. Book ahead—slots fill quickly.

⏱ 120 min

Fitzbillies on Trumpington Street has been a Cambridge institution since 1920, famous for its Chelsea Buns—rolled buns with currants and orange, glazed with sticky syrup. It's the unmissable address for students and visitors alike. Finish the day at a fine-dining restaurant in the centre for a quality dinner before returning to London the next day.

⏱ 90 min

London

★★★★★
1

Columbia Road Flower Market in London's East End only opens on Sunday mornings, from 8am to around 3pm. Around the flower market clusters a small community of independent cafés, delis and bakeries worth the trip alone. The market is packed between 10am and 1pm—arrive early to breathe, stay for brunch at one of the cafés on Ezra Street just behind.

⏱ 75 min
2

Smithfield Market, in the City of London, is Britain's largest covered meat market, operating on this site since the 10th century. The Victorian cast-iron and glass buildings date from 1868 and are listed. Wholesale trading happens overnight and early morning, but the halls remain visitable during the day. The surrounding neighbourhood, Farringdon, has become one of London's most dynamic for fine dining.

⏱ 60 min
3

Covent Garden and its pedestrian streets gather dozens of restaurants from around the world, but seek out British addresses instead: Rules, London's oldest dining room opened in 1798, still serves game, pies and traditional puddings in intact Victorian surroundings. It's the ideal closing note for an English gastronomic circuit.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

London's gin bars are one of the capital's great pleasures: London has several dozen active distilleries, and specialist bars offer lists of several hundred references with tonics and garnishes tailored to each gin. It's a pleasant way to close a circuit that has crossed several gin terroirs—Devon, Bristol, Cambridge—and compare styles.

⏱ 90 min

For a farewell dinner worthy of the circuit, several London restaurants offer gastronomic British cuisine that rivals the great French tables. Rules has been an institution since 1798. Berners Tavern in the London Edition hotel, or St John in Farringdon—rewarded over the years for its 'nose-to-tail' cooking that influenced an entire generation of chefs—are excellent choices depending on your budget. Book well ahead.

⏱ 150 min
