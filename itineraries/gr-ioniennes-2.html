Argostoli

A capital reborn after 1953, where a 700-metre stone bridge spans a lagoon full of sea turtles

★★★★☆
📍 De Bosset Bridge Argostoli📍 Koutavos Lagoon Argostoli📍 Archaeological Museum Argostoli📍 Korgialenios Museum Argostoli📍 Lighthouse Saint Theodore Fanari Argostoli📍 Katavothres Argostoli📍 Lithostroto Argostoli
1

Argostoli has been the capital of Kefalonia since 1757 — before that, the population sheltered inside the Venetian fortress of San Giorgio as protection against pirates. The town thrived as one of the busiest ports in the Greek islands, until the earthquake of 12 August 1953 levelled it entirely. What you see today is therefore a rebuilt city, but it has held on to an authentic Mediterranean character with its palm-lined waterfront and the lively Vallianos Square, which comes alive every evening.

⏱ 60 min
2

The De Bosset Bridge — also called the Drapano Bridge — is the longest stone bridge over the sea in the world, stretching 700 metres. Originally built in wood in 1813 by Swiss engineer Charles-Philippe de Bosset under British command, it was entirely reconstructed in stone a few decades later. Roughly halfway across, you'll come upon an obelisk erected by the British to commemorate their presence — the original plaque reading 'To the glory of the British Empire' was destroyed in 1865, one year after the island was united with Greece. The bridge marks the edge of the Koutavos Lagoon, where you can spot Caretta caretta turtles swimming freely.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Archaeological Museum of Argostoli, opened in 1960 (architect Patroklos Karantinos), houses finds from across the island spanning prehistory to the Roman era. The Mycenaean collection is especially impressive — highlights include geometric ceramics, gold jewellery and bronzes unearthed during excavations at the island's four ancient cities: Same, Kranioi, Pronnoi and Paleis. Allow about an hour if you want to see it properly.

⏱ 75 min
4

The Korgialenios Historical and Folklore Museum, founded in 1963 on the ground floor of the library bearing the same name, traces the history of Kefalonia from the Venetian period through to the 1953 earthquake. You'll find antique furniture, silverware, household tools, traditional costumes and close to 3,000 photographs — a tangible way to understand what the town looked like before it was completely destroyed. The entrance is through the library, which also holds 50,000 rare books and manuscripts.

⏱ 60 min
5

The Lighthouse of Saints Theodore — locally known as Fanari ('the lighthouse') — is a neoclassical circular structure supported by 20 Doric columns, built in 1829 during the British occupation to plans drawn by J.P. Kennedy. Its lantern sits 11 metres above the Bay of Argostoli. Destroyed in the 1953 earthquake, it was faithfully rebuilt by 1960. Come here in the late afternoon: the sun sinking behind the Paliki peninsula on the far side of the bay paints the sea orange — one of the finest sunsets on the island.

⏱ 40 min
6

The Katavothres are one of the strangest geological phenomena in the Mediterranean: seawater rushes into cracks in the limestone, vanishes underground, crosses the entire island — roughly 15 km — and resurfaces in the Bay of Sami, on the eastern coast, fourteen days later. In 1963, Austrian hydrogeologists Maurin and Zoetl poured 160 kg of fluorescent green dye (uranine) into the sinkholes — and detected it again in the underground lake at Melissani and the springs at Karavomilos. The process remains partly unexplained. As far back as 1835, a British tax collector had the idea of harnessing the current to power corn mills — the ruins of those mills are still visible on site.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Lithostroto, the main pedestrian street of Argostoli, is where locals gather every evening for the ritual stroll. Shops, cafés, the Catholic church, the clock tower rebuilt in 1985 — everything is packed into a few hundred metres. Keep walking to Vallianos Square, the large central plaza, for a Greek coffee under the palm trees. Quiet during the day, buzzing and full of life at night.

⏱ 60 min

The Koutavos Lagoon, once a mosquito-infested swamp and a hotbed of malaria, is now a bird sanctuary where migratory species nest. Caretta caretta turtles feed here regularly. You can walk the coastal path or rent an electric boat to get a closer look at the wildlife. Morning is the best time — calm water, soft light, and usually a few fishermen by the harbour.

⏱ 60 min

Kreatopita is the quintessential Kefallonian pie: flaky pastry stuffed with a mixture of meats (lamb, pork, beef), rice and spices, baked until golden. It's a festive dish that families still prepare for special occasions. Track it down in the bakeries and tavernas around the market — it's the best quick lunch in town.

⏱ 45 min

Robola of Kefalonia is one of Greece's most acclaimed white wines. This ancient grape variety grows on the Omala plateau at 700 metres elevation and carries a PDO label. Dry, mineral, taut, with crisp acidity — nothing like the soft, rounded whites from the rest of Greece. Ask for it at any taverna on the waterfront: it pairs perfectly with grilled fish and mezze.

⏱ 30 min

Stop 2

Sami

Ferry port and launchpad for the two most spectacular caves on Kefalonia

★★★☆☆
📍 Melissani Cave Karavomilos📍 Drogarati Cave Sami📍 Antisamos Beach Sami📍 Sami Port Kefalonia📍 Karavomylos Village Sami
1

Melissani Cave — also known as the 'Cave of the Nymphs' — is a limestone cenote whose ceiling collapsed during the 1953 earthquake, allowing sunlight to pour directly onto the underground lake. The lake stretches roughly 160 metres long and reaches depths of 30 to 40 metres; its surface sits one metre above sea level, fed by water that has travelled 15 km underground from the Katavothres in Argostoli. Archaeological excavations in 1962 uncovered a sanctuary dedicated to the god Pan on a small central islet, along with clay figurines and oil lamps dating from the 3rd–4th centuries BC. Plan your visit between 11 am and 1 pm: that's when the sun strikes the opening directly and transforms the water into a kaleidoscope of blues and violets. The tour is by rowboat and lasts 10 to 15 minutes.

⏱ 60 min
2

Drogarati Cave, 5 km from Sami, was discovered around 300 years ago when an earthquake caused its entrance to collapse inward. It measures 65 metres long, 45 metres wide and 20 metres high, with a steady temperature of 18°C — a welcome relief in summer. The stalactites and stalagmites, some of which resemble dolphins according to speleologists, have formed over roughly 100 million years. The main chamber, known as the 'Hall of Apotheosis,' has such remarkable acoustics that it has hosted concerts — including, in 2014, a performance by the Bavarian Philharmonic who sailed in on a cruise ship docked at Argostoli. Bring a jumper even in summer, and wear sturdy shoes for the slippery steps.

⏱ 60 min
3

Sami's harbour is Kefalonia's main ferry port, with connections to Patras and Ithaca. The town itself is modest but pleasant, with a handful of tavernas and cafés along the waterfront. Just next door lies Karavomylos, the village where the resurgent springs from the Katavothres emerge — the seawater that entered the ground in Argostoli reappears here, slightly brackish, after its 14-day underground journey.

⏱ 40 min
4

Antisamos Beach, 4 km from Sami, is one of the most beautiful on Kefalonia: a long sweep of white pebbles framed by pine forest that tumbles down to the shore, with deep turquoise water. Several scenes from the 2001 film 'Captain Corelli's Mandolin' were shot right here. The beach is lifeguarded in summer but never overcrowded — the winding access road from the main highway puts off the lazier crowd. Bring your snorkelling mask: the seabed is rich.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

A combined visit to both caves (Melissani + Drogarati) works best as a half-day outing. Start early in the morning with Drogarati to beat the cruise-ship coaches, then head to Melissani around 11:30 am to catch the peak sunlight. Allow about an hour at each cave, including queuing time in high season.

⏱ 180 min

After the caves, head to Antisamos for a swim. The water is crystal-clear and far less crowded than the beaches in the south. If you have a mask, dip your head below the surface and look around — the visibility is exceptional.

⏱ 120 min

Riganada is a rustic dried bread rubbed with fresh tomato, drizzled with olive oil, topped with feta and scattered with dried oregano — a summer classic across the Ionian Islands. Dead simple, dead good. Order one with a chilled glass of Robola at a taverna on the Sami waterfront at the end of the day.

⏱ 45 min

Stop 3

Ithaca

A 96 km² island forever tied to Odysseus's homecoming in Homer's epic

★★☆☆☆
📍 Vathy Port Ithaca📍 Archaeological Museum Vathy Ithaca📍 Kathara Monastery Ithaca📍 Cave of the Nymphs Vathy Ithaca📍 Aetos Hill Ithaca📍 Stavros Village Ithaca
1

Vathy, the capital of Ithaca, is a small town of 1,700 people tucked at the end of a horseshoe-shaped gulf — one of the best natural anchorages in the Ionian Sea. Most of the Venetian-style buildings were reconstructed faithfully after the 1953 earthquake; it is said that one of El Greco's earliest works hangs in the local cathedral. Stroll along the harbour, stop at one of the quayside tavernas — the atmosphere is gentle, worlds away from mass tourism.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Archaeological Museum of Vathy holds over 1,000 objects unearthed on the island, covering the Mycenaean through Roman periods. The star exhibit is a pottery fragment inscribed with the name 'Odysseus,' found during excavations at the Alalkomenes site. There is also a small bronze statuette that some scholars identify as a portrait of Odysseus himself, along with Mycenaean votive tripods. Admission is free. The museum is closed on Tuesdays.

⏱ 60 min
3

Aetos Hill, 4 km from Vathy, rises to 380 metres. From the church of Ai Giorgios, a steep trail climbs for 20 minutes to the ruins of the ancient city of Alalkomenes — this is where Heinrich Schliemann dug in 1868 and 1878, convinced he had found the palace of Odysseus. Modern archaeologists are more cautious: the fortifications date from the 7th century BC, well after the events described in the Odyssey. But the view from the top — spanning both coasts of the island and Kefalonia across the water — is worth the effort on its own.

⏱ 90 min
4

Kathara Monastery sits perched at roughly 600 metres elevation on the mountain that Homer called Neriton. Founded in the late 17th century, it offers a full 360° panorama over Vathy, the southern half of the island and Kefalonia beyond. Even if the monastery itself doesn't interest you, make the drive for the view — it ranks among the most beautiful in all the Ionian Islands.

⏱ 60 min
5

The Cave of the Nymphs, locally known as Marmarospilia, lies a few kilometres north of Vathy. According to the Odyssey, this is where Odysseus hid the gifts from the Phaeacians upon his return home. The cave contains stalactites and a small ancient altar, with a natural opening in the ceiling — the smoke from sacrificial offerings supposedly rose through it. It is not always open to visitors due to ongoing excavations — check before making the trip.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Ithaca is tailor-made for gentle hiking. Signposted trails connect the villages through olive groves and scrubland of kermes oak. The island is home to just 3,000 people, and the paths are often deserted — one of the most authentic experiences in the Ionians. Ask in Vathy for details on the marked routes.

⏱ 120 min

Revithokeftedes are chickpea fritters laced with fresh herbs, typical of the Ionian Islands. In Ithaca, they're usually served with yoghurt or skordalia (garlic mash). A simple, delicious vegetarian starter — one of the best things to order in a harbourside taverna in Vathy.

⏱ 45 min

The village of Stavros, in northern Ithaca, deserves a stop: the central square features a bust of Odysseus and a model of his 'idealised' palace, and a small archaeological museum displays finds from excavations led by the British School of Archaeology in the 1930s. Not to be confused with the museum in Vathy — this one focuses on the northern part of the island and its ties to the Odyssey.

⏱ 60 min

Zakynthos

Venetian grace, nesting sea turtles, the Navagio shipwreck and glowing Blue Caves

★★★★☆
📍 Solomos Square Zakynthos📍 Agios Dionysios Church Zakynthos📍 Byzantine Museum Zakynthos📍 Venetian Castle Bohali Zakynthos📍 National Marine Park Zakynthos📍 Saint Mark Square Zakynthos
1

Zakynthos Town — dubbed 'Il Fiore di Levante' (the Flower of the East) by the Venetians — was completely destroyed in the 1953 earthquake and rebuilt in a neo-Venetian style with arcaded streets and colonnaded façades. Solomos Square, the largest in town, opens directly onto the Ionian Sea. Here you'll find the Byzantine Museum, a statue of the poet Dionysios Solomos (who penned the Greek national anthem in 1823), and the town hall. This is where the action is in the morning; in the evening, head up to Bohali.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Church of Agios Dionysios is dedicated to the island's patron saint. Founded in 1708, it houses his relics in a silver reliquary and features a gilded carved iconostasis along with fine Byzantine frescoes restored after 1953. The bell tower overlooking the harbour is one of the defining landmarks of Zakynthos. Every year on 24 August and 17 December, thousands of worshippers gather here for the saint's feast days.

⏱ 40 min
3

The Byzantine Museum of Zakynthos, on Solomos Square, is one of the richest in the Ionians. It brings together carved wooden Byzantine icons, original frescoes from the 15th to 17th centuries rescued from churches destroyed in 1953, and 18th-century paintings by artists of the Ionian School such as Doxaras, Koutouzis and Kandounis. Some canvases depict the town before the earthquake — a striking contrast with what it looks like today. Allow about 1 hour 30 minutes.

⏱ 90 min
4

The Venetian Castle of Bohali, set on the hillside above the town, was built in the 15th century by the Venetians on even older foundations. The pine-shaded enclosure overlooks the harbour from 150 metres — from the crumbling ramparts you can take in the entire bay, the port and the mountains of the interior in a single sweep. Drive up or walk (25 minutes from Solomos Square). At sunset, locals from the village of Bohali settle on café terraces with a glass in hand — join them.

⏱ 60 min
5

The National Marine Park of Zakynthos (NMPZ) was established to protect the Caretta caretta sea turtles. It encompasses Laganas Bay and the nesting beaches on the island's south coast. Turtles lay their eggs between June and August on these shores — access is regulated, but glass-bottom boat excursions let you observe them in their natural habitat without disturbing them. If you're visiting in summer, don't miss a trip out on the water.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Roma Street, the main pedestrian thoroughfare leading off Saint Mark's Square, is lined with local shops where you can pick up mandolato nougat (almonds, honey, egg whites — the island's signature sweet), ladotyri (a hard cheese aged in olive oil, Zakynthos PDO) and olive-oil products. It's also the place for evening outings — the tavernas kick into gear around 8 pm.

⏱ 60 min

Facing Saint Mark's Square stands the Solomos and Kalvos Museum, housed in the island's former casino and open since 1968. On the ground floor lie the tombs of the two poets — Dionysios Solomos, author of the national anthem, and Andreas Kalvos. Upstairs: manuscripts, personal belongings and period documents. A must for anyone interested in Greek literature.

⏱ 45 min

Fritoura is the local evening tradition: small fish — mackerel, sardines, anchovies — fried in olive oil and served with a squeeze of lemon. Simple, no-nonsense, excellent. The Zakynthos waterfront is lined with tavernas serving fritoura as a starter alongside Verdea — the local white wine, dry and lively, made from grape varieties found nowhere else.

⏱ 60 min

Verdea is the PDO white wine of Zakynthos, made from indigenous varieties (Pavlos, Goustolidi) grown nowhere else in Greece. Dry, with bright acidity and a faint bitterness on the finish — a world apart from the soft whites produced across most of the islands. Order it at the taverna with the fritoura or local cheeses.

⏱ 30 min

Stop 5

Zakynthos

Venetian grace, nesting sea turtles, the Navagio shipwreck and glowing Blue Caves

★★★★☆
📍 Navagio Beach viewpoint Zakynthos📍 Cape Skinari lighthouse Zakynthos📍 Blue Caves Skinari Zakynthos📍 Xigia Beach sulphur springs Zakynthos📍 Volimes Village Zakynthos📍 Anafonitria Monastery Zakynthos
1

Navagio Bay — also known as 'Smuggler's Cove' — is the most photographed spot in all of Greece. It owes its name to the wreck of the cargo ship Panagiotis, which ran aground here on the night of 2 October 1980 after being chased by the Greek navy during a storm. The vessel had left Argostoli loaded with contraband goods. White limestone cliffs rise over 200 metres above the beach of white sand and turquoise water. Note: since 2022, landing on the beach has been banned due to rockfall hazards — but you can approach by boat and swim in the bay. For the view from above, take the road from Anafonitria or Volimes to the free clifftop viewing platform.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Blue Caves are a series of arches and tunnels carved by the sea into the white limestone cliffs on the island's north coast, between the port of Agios Nikolaos and Cape Skinari. Their name comes from the electric blue colour the water takes on inside, the result of sunlight reflecting off the submerged limestone — a phenomenon strikingly similar to the Blue Grotto on Capri. The rock walls and even your body underwater glow vivid blue. They are only accessible by small boat (50 passengers maximum to enter the arches). Depart from the port of Agios Nikolaos or from Cape Skinari.

⏱ 75 min
3

Cape Skinari is the northernmost tip of Zakynthos, facing Kefalonia — on a clear day, Mount Ainos on the neighbouring island is perfectly visible. The lighthouse dates from 1897 and stands 9.5 metres tall. This is the departure point for boat excursions to the Blue Caves and Navagio Bay. The traditional windmills on the cape have become one of the island's most recognisable symbols.

⏱ 30 min
4

Anafonitria Monastery, set inland in the northwest, dates from the 15th century. It is one of the oldest on the island and is famous for having sheltered Saint Dionysios as a monk before he became the patron saint of Zakynthos. It is surrounded by centuries-old olive groves and a medieval defence tower that still stands. The atmosphere here is peaceful, far removed from the beaches.

⏱ 40 min
5

Xigia Beach is a natural curiosity: sulphur springs rise directly into the sea from the rocks, giving the water a milky tint and a faint but distinctive smell. The therapeutic properties of these sulphurous waters have been known locally for a long time. The beach is small, wild and rarely crowded — a fine alternative to Navagio for anyone looking for a different kind of swim.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The ideal plan for this last day: leave early in the morning for Cape Skinari and board a small boat to the Blue Caves (allow 2 hours). Then head inland to Volimes and Anafonitria, winding through olive groves and mountain villages. In the afternoon, make the detour to the Navagio viewpoint for the photo, then finish at Xigia Beach for a dip in the sulphurous waters before driving back to Zakynthos Town.

⏱ 60 min

The village of Volimes, up in the hills, is known for its textile artisans — rugs, embroidery and lace are displayed in the small shops lining the main street. It's also where you'll find the best local produce: mountain honey, olive oil and handmade mandolato nougat. The vendors are often the producers themselves.

⏱ 45 min

The combined boat trip covering the Blue Caves and Navagio Bay (seen from the sea) takes 3 to 4 hours depending on the option chosen. Boats depart from the port of Agios Nikolaos (10 minutes from Navagio) or from the main port of Zakynthos Town (90 minutes). Book the evening before in summer — spots go fast. Small boats carrying 12 to 50 passengers can enter the caves; the larger vessels can only circle them from outside.

⏱ 240 min

Ladotyri is the PDO cheese of Zakynthos: a hard cheese made from sheep's and goat's milk, aged in olive oil — hence the name (lado = oil, tyri = cheese). It has a firm texture, a dark rind and a powerful, slightly fruity flavour. Eat it at the end of a meal with local honey, or diced as an appetiser.

⏱ 30 min
