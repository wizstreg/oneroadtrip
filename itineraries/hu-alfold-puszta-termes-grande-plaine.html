Budapest

★★★★★
1

Forty million bricks, dozens of kilos of gold according to period sources, and a dome rising 96 meters: the Parliament (Országház) doesn't do subtle. Construction began in 1885, inaugurated in 1896 for the Hungarian Millennium, it houses the Holy Crown in the central hall. The guided tour lasts 45 minutes—book online, as English slots sell out fast.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Fisherman's Bastion looks like a movie set—but what a view. Built between 1895 and 1902 to designs by Frigyes Schulek, its seven towers symbolize the seven Magyar tribes of 895. Climbing the upper terraces costs extra, but the panorama of the Parliament across the Pest side is worth every forint.

⏱ 60 min
3

The scent of wax and polished wood greets you at the threshold of Matthias Church (Mátyás-templom), attached to the Bastion. It served as an Ottoman mosque in the sixteenth century before returning to Catholic worship—Károly Lotz's Art Nouveau frescoes still bear traces of that history. Avoid service times if you want to wander freely.

⏱ 40 min
4

Cross the Danube on the Chain Bridge (Széchenyi Lánchíd) via the pedestrian walkway to catch all of Budapest in a single sweep. At 375 meters long, it was the city's first permanent bridge when it opened in 1849. The crossing takes ten minutes; the impression lasts much longer.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

If you arrive at Buda Hill before 9 a.m., the Castle Quarter is nearly empty. Tour groups show up after 10:30—take advantage of this quiet window to walk the ramparts and watch the rooftops of Pest emerge in the morning light.

⏱ 60 min

Touristy, yes, but the Great Market Hall of Budapest (Nagycsarnok) stays authentic upstairs. Ground floor: paprika, Pick salami, pálinka. First floor: embroidery and gyors bufé restaurants. Arrive before noon to beat the cruise-ship crowds.

⏱ 60 min

Slip into the golden-yellow pools of Széchenyi Baths—the city's largest thermal complex, opened in 1913, fed by springs that gush at 74–77°C. Three outdoor pools, fifteen indoors. Book online to skip the queue. Bring flip-flops.

⏱ 120 min

Kecskemet

★★★☆☆
1

A ceramic cake planted in the middle of the square: Kecskemét's Cifrapalota (1902, architect Géza Márkus) is coated in lilac, blue, and red Zsolnay majolica flowers and hearts. Originally a residence and casino, the building now houses the municipal art gallery. Stop even if you don't go inside—the façade alone justifies the detour.

⏱ 45 min
2

Locals call it 'the capital of Art Nouveau,' and the Town Hall (Városháza) backs up the claim. Built between 1893 and 1897 by Ödön Lechner and Gyula Pártos, it's covered in hand-painted pink, yellow, and turquoise Zsolnay tiles. The ceremonial hall with its frescoes by Bertalan Székely is open to visitors.

⏱ 40 min
3

The Town Hall's ceremonial chamber is worth the visit alone—but the Hungarian Museum of Photography next door, installed in the former Orthodox synagogue, is the country's only museum dedicated to photography as an art form. Small entrance fee, surprising collection.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Taste the local barackpálinka, Kecskemét's apricot brandy—the city is famous for its orchards, and this specialty is classified as a 'Hungarikum.' The Zwack Kecskeméti Pálinka Manufactory offers guided tastings with distillery tours.

⏱ 60 min

Founded in 1899, the Hungarian Museum of Naïve Artists in Kecskemét is the country's only museum dedicated to self-taught painters. Small but genuine, it reveals a lesser-known side of Hungarian art. Allow 30 minutes, modest entry fee.

⏱ 35 min

Bugac

★★★★☆
1

Kiskunság National Park at Bugac shelters the largest sandy steppe in central Europe. Cars aren't allowed on the puszta—you cross it by horse-drawn carriage for 1.5 km to the csikós arena. Along the way, grey long-horned cattle and racka sheep graze freely.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Shepherd Museum at Bugac is tiny but touching: it traces nomadic life on the puszta, with shepherds' tools, reed huts, and traditional cheese-making. Allow 20 minutes. Free with the horse-show ticket.

⏱ 25 min

Activities

Climb into the carriage for the 1.5 km ride to the csikós grounds: the puszta horsemen still crack whips from ten meters away, fall and remount at full gallop, and drive teams standing balanced on horseback. Shows run May through September, Wednesday/Friday/Saturday/Sunday at 10:30 a.m.

⏱ 90 min

Opusztaszer

★★★★☆
1

One hundred and twenty meters of canvas wrap around you all at once: the Feszty Panorama at Ópusztaszer is a monumental circular painting by Árpád Feszty from 1893–94, depicting the arrival of the Magyars in the Carpathian Basin. The rotunda is the centerpiece of this grand heritage park created in 1982. Allow at least an hour for the painting alone.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Ópusztaszer Heritage Park, created in 1982 on 55 hectares, combines an open-air village museum (skanzen), memorials to the Hungarian Millennium, and a nomadic park. According to the Gesta Hungarorum of Anonymus, this is where the Magyar tribal chiefs held their first council around 895.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Try archery at the Nomad Park before 11 a.m.—demonstrations with nomadic weapons (bow, lance, saber) start in the morning. Children can ride ponies. Open April through October, closed Monday.

⏱ 40 min

Szeged

★★★★☆
1

Red bricks, 91-meter bell towers, and a façade that dominates the entire Dóm tér: Szeged's Votive Church (Fogadalmi Templom) was inaugurated in 1930 after sixteen years of construction. It seems to crush the small octagonal tower beside it—the Dömötör Tower, a twelfth-century remnant and the oldest structure in the city.

⏱ 40 min
2

Szeged Synagogue, completed in 1903 by Lipót Baumhorn, is considered one of Hungary's most beautiful. Its dome blends Romanesque, Gothic, Art Nouveau, and Moorish styles. The stained-glass windows depicting Jewish holidays are by Miksa Róth, the same glazier who created those at Budapest's Gellért Baths.

⏱ 50 min
3

Light filters through the stained glass of the dome at Reök Palace—an Art Nouveau jewel with undulating lines and floral motifs, now home to an art center. The ground-floor café serves cakes flavored with Szeged paprika. Open Tuesday through Sunday.

⏱ 40 min
4

The Dömötör Tower is the junior among Szeged's monuments—and the only twelfth-century survivor. Octagonal, isolated at the foot of the Votive Church, it contrasts sharply with the neo-Romanesque giant that dwarfs it. A detail that says much about the city's turbulent history, flooded and rebuilt in 1879.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Stroll through Dóm tér in the evening: the arcades encircling the square shelter statues of Hungarian luminaries—an open-air museum you can walk in ten minutes, illuminated at dusk. The square is one of the largest in Hungary.

⏱ 30 min

If you're passing in summer, the Szeged Open-Air Festival stages operas and musicals outdoors on Dóm tér from July to August. Tickets sell out fast—book weeks ahead from abroad. The square's natural acoustics are excellent.

⏱ 150 min

Mako

★★☆☆☆
1

Twenty-nine grey domes undulate across Makó's skyline: the Hagymatikum is a thermal complex designed by Imre Makovecz and inaugurated in 2012. The dome shapes evoke the onion—the city's agricultural specialty, classified as a 'Hungarikum.' Thermal water rises at 41°C from a well 993 meters deep.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Hagymaház, Makovecz's organic house on Makó's main square, is another example of his living architecture: wood, curved forms, natural materials. The contrast with the neighboring socialist-era blocks is striking. Exterior viewing is free.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Soak in the healing waters of the Hagymatikum—medicinal mud from the Maros River, one of five registered natural therapeutic products in Hungary, is available for treatments. The complex opened an additional wing in 2024 with new slides and a terrace. Allow at least two hours.

⏱ 120 min

Gyula Castle is one of the few intact medieval brick castles on the Hungarian Great Plain—the Ottomans occupied the fortress from 1566 to 1695 without demolishing it. Now restored, it hosts a museum and medieval reenactments in summer. Climb the rampart walk for the view.

⏱ 60 min
2

Gyula's medicinal water gushes at 72°C from a depth of 2,174 meters—one of the hottest in the region. Its golden-brown color indicates a high concentration of minerals recommended for rheumatic conditions. Várfürdő sits next to the castle in a park of century-old trees: a rare pairing in Hungary.

⏱ 30 min
3

For a baroque castle on the Hungarian plain, Almásy Castle surprises: its 8.5-hectare park shelters old trees and borders the thermal baths to the north. The eighteenth-century building houses a museum devoted to the history of Békés County—sumptuous interior, collection of everyday objects from the nineteenth and twentieth centuries.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Taste Gyula sausage—declared rival to Békéscsaba's for decades. Local butchers sell it spiced, cold-smoked, sliced in front of you. A local pride eaten standing up, paper in hand, with a glass of Villány wine.

Activities

Activities

Hortobagy

★★★★☆
1

The horizon is so flat it shimmers: heat mirages on the Hortobágy puszta make herds float above the ground. Inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1999, this 80,000-hectare park is the largest grassy steppe in central Europe. Created in 1973, it also shelters Scythian burial mounds thousands of years old.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Nine-Arch Bridge, built in 1833, measures 167 meters and was long Hungary's longest stone bridge. Local legend says the mortar was mixed with sand wine. On the other side: the Hortobágy Csárda and the Shepherd Museum (Pásztormúzeum).

⏱ 30 min
3

The csikós simply call it 'the endless plain'—and Hortobágy's shepherds perpetuate techniques unchanged for centuries: herding grey long-horned cattle, racka sheep, and mangalica pigs. The park's Wildlife Zoo lets you observe them up close.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

If you're a birder, Hortobágy's fish ponds are an exceptional observation site: over 340 bird species including the great bustard, one of Europe's last populations, and thousands of common cranes migrating in March–April and October.

⏱ 90 min

Attend the csikós show and demonstration at the Hortobágy Equestrian Center: whip cracking, standing on a five-horse team, skill games. Program during tourist season—inquire at the Tourist Office on Petőfi tér at the village entrance.

⏱ 60 min

★★★☆☆
1

Calvinist and proud of it: the Great Reformed Church of Debrecen (Református Nagytemplom) is Hungary's largest Protestant church. Its yellow neoclassical façade dominates Kossuth Square. It was within these walls that Lajos Kossuth proclaimed the country's independence in April 1849.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Déri Museum deserves its reputation for one reason alone: Mihály Munkácsy's Christ Trilogy. These three monumental canvases—Ecce Homo, Christ Before Pilate, Golgotha—occupy an entire room. The museum also houses an ancient Egyptian collection and ethnographic pieces.

⏱ 60 min
3

Nagyerdő Park smells of linden in June. Hungary's first nature reserve (1939), this 1,000-hectare forest north of the center shelters Debrecen Zoo, Aquaticum Baths, and quiet trails. Perfect for decompressing after museums.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Inaugurated in 2006, MODEM is Debrecen's contemporary art center—a modern building that contrasts with the city's neoclassicism. Temporary exhibitions by Hungarian and international artists. On-site café. Closed Monday.

⏱ 50 min

Lake Tisza is the trip's surprise: 127 square kilometers of artificial lake created in the 1970s to regulate Tisza River floods, now a major bird reserve. From Tiszafüred, the largest lakeside town, you can access the beaches, reed beds, and nautical routes of the reserve.

⏱ 120 min

In the morning, herons lift silently from Lake Tisza's reeds at sunrise—the best time for birdwatching. Renting binoculars or a local birding guide from Tiszafüred lets you identify species nesting in the reed beds: grey herons, egrets, bitterns.

Activities
