Trabzon

★★★☆☆
1

The Panagia Sumela Monastery is built directly into a vertical cliff face at 1,200 meters altitude in Altındere National Park, 45 km from Trabzon. According to tradition, it was founded in 386 AD by two Athenian monks, Barnabas and Sophronios, who followed a vision of the Virgin Mary, and served as an Orthodox spiritual center for over fifteen centuries. The frescoes in the rock-cut church - scenes from the New Testament, Last Judgment, Pantocrator - date mostly from the 18th century. Wear sturdy shoes: the path from the parking lot climbs through forest for 600 meters of elevation gain.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Hagia Sophia of Trabzon is one of the few major Byzantine churches still standing in Anatolia - built between 1238 and 1263 under Manuel I Komnenos, sovereign of the Empire of Trebizond. The frescoes in the narthex, uncovered during a restoration led by the University of Edinburgh in the 1960s, notably depict a Last Judgment on the west wall and a Dormition of the Virgin in the apse. The building is now an active mosque: the frescoes are partially veiled during prayers. Check prayer times to plan your visit.

⏱ 60 min
3

Trabzon's citadel occupies a rocky spur above the old center. The ramparts date partly from the Byzantine era and were modified by the Ottomans after the conquest of 1461. The view from the walls sweeps across the harbor and the Black Sea. The interior houses a small mosque and traces of a Komnenian palace.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Arriving in Trabzon early in the day (flight from Istanbul or continuation of the Mesopotamia itinerary), start with Sumela in the morning when crowds are lighter. Shuttles depart from Trabzon bus station to Maçka, then dolmuş connect to the park entrance. By car, allow 1 hour from downtown.

⏱ 30 min

In the evening, head down to the harbor and look for a restaurant serving hamsi - the Black Sea anchovy. It's the quintessential local fish, fried in a cornmeal crust. Bakeries also offer Trabzon ekmek, a dense wood-oven bread.

⏱ 60 min

Uzungol

★★★☆☆
1

Uzungöl - literally "the long lake" - is a mountain lake formed by a landslide that dammed the Haldizen Stream valley, at 1,090 meters altitude. The village bordering it, populated by Laz and Pontic communities, long served as a summer yayla before transforming into a tourist destination. Around the lake: forests of spruce and beech, morning fog, and wooden hotels built on the shores - some directly on stilts over the water. The trail that circles the lake (about 5 km) is walkable in two hours.

⏱ 120 min
2

Above Uzungöl village, the yayla of Demirkapi and Şekersu open onto high-altitude pastures wrapped in mist. In summer, Laz families still bring their herds up to these plateaus. From the ascending road, the views over the steep-sided valley are among the most beautiful in Pontus.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Trabzon → Uzungöl road crosses the Haldizen valley and climbs in switchbacks through humid forests characteristic of eastern Pontus. Stop at the viewpoint at the village entrance for the classic view of the lake and mosque reflected in the water.

⏱ 30 min

The horon dance - a traditional Laz and Pontic circle dance performed to the sound of the kemençe (local bowed fiddle) - is still practiced at weddings and festivals. If your visit falls on a July or August weekend, the chances of witnessing a ceremony in the villages around Uzungöl are real.

⏱ 60 min

Rize

★★★☆☆
1

The tea plantations of Rize cascade in green terraces from the seashore up to 700 meters altitude - it's the landscape that defines the entire province. Turkey is the 6th largest tea producer worldwide, and Rize is the capital: more than 200,000 families cultivate Camellia sinensis here. The Ziraat Bahçesi botanical garden, in the city center, offers a panorama over the terraced plantations and the Black Sea. Free entry, free parking.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Fırtına ("storm") valley, 30 km from Rize in the Çamlıhemşin district, is classified among the world's 200 priority ecoregions by WWF. The Fırtına river, fast-flowing and jade-colored, is crossed by several Ottoman stone arch bridges from the 17th and 18th centuries still in use. The most famous is the Taş Köprü bridge at Çamlıhemşin. The road then climbs toward the Ayder plateau - the ultimate destination for hikers tackling Kaçkar.

⏱ 120 min
3

The fortress of Rize sits atop a rocky spur in the city center. It dates from the Byzantine era and was modified under the Ottomans. From the ramparts, views over the harbor, the tiered plantations, and the Kaçkar range in the background. Climb on foot from the center: 15 minutes.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

At any shop in Rize, ask to see how Turkish çay is prepared: double teapot (çaydanlık), boiling water in the large one, dry leaves steeping in the small. The result - an amber tea served in a tulip-shaped glass - is drunk sweet and without milk. It's a social ritual as much as a beverage.

⏱ 30 min

Rafting on the Fırtına is offered by several operators in Çamlıhemşin from April to September. Level accessible to beginners on downstream sections, more technical upstream. The river is icy cold even in summer - allow one hour of descent and mandatory change of clothes.

⏱ 90 min

Amasya

★★★★☆
1

The road between Rize and Amasya is this itinerary's transition leg: about 290 km and 6 hours of driving, leaving the coast to cross into interior Anatolia. Leave early. Midway, the city of Samsun on the Black Sea offers a lunch break before descending toward the Yeşilırmak valley. Amasya appears late afternoon: Ottoman wooden houses line both riverbanks, with cliffs and rock-cut tombs looming above. First view from the central bridge.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Yalıboyu houses - the riverside houses - are Amasya's most photographed buildings. These Ottoman residences from the 18th and 19th centuries, built on stone foundations with wooden overhangs above the Yeşilırmak, have mostly been restored as hotels and cafés. The promenade along the south bank takes 20 minutes and allows views of all the colorful facades. In the evening, the rock tombs are illuminated and reflect in the water.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Late arrival obliges, settle this evening for a riverside stroll and dinner in one of the restaurants set in old Ottoman houses. The nighttime view of the illuminated tombs from the bank is worth the journey's fatigue.

⏱ 90 min

Amasya

★★★★☆
1

The rock tombs of the Pontic kings are carved into the limestone of Mount Harşena, at a height of 8 to 15 meters above ground. The five principal tombs belonged to kings who ruled Amaseia between 281 and 155 BC: Mithridates I, Ariobarzanes, Mithridates II, Mithridates III, and Pharnaces I. Impossible to enter the burial chambers - the openings are too high - but the access path offers a plunging view over the city and the Yeşilırmak. Allow 20 minutes' climb from the ticket office.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Amasya Archaeological Museum is one of the region's most interesting - and not just for museum regulars. The centerpiece is a hall devoted to 14th-century Ilkhanid mummies: bodies naturally preserved without organ extraction, discovered beneath the Burmalı Minare mosque. The museum also houses a bronze statuette of the Hittite storm god Teshub, found locally. Information panels in English.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Sultan Beyazıt II Mosque (1486) is Amasya's largest religious complex: mosque, medrese, fountain, and imaret (charitable kitchen) form a cohesive ensemble on the south bank. The architecture - sober, well-proportioned, without the decorative excesses of certain imperial mosques - illustrates the transition between Seljuk and classical Ottoman styles.

⏱ 30 min
4

Amasya's citadel perches atop Mount Harşena, 272 meters above the city. The first fortifications date to the Bronze Age, and the citadel was modified under the Pontic Kingdom, Byzantines, and Ottomans. A 186-meter tunnel carved through rock connects different parts of the enceinte. The climb on foot from the tombs takes an additional 30 minutes but the view from the summit surpasses everything you've seen from the lower viewpoints.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Amasya has produced apples since antiquity and made them its symbol. The orchards surrounding the city in autumn (September-October) give rise to very lively farmers' markets in the center. If you're there outside apple season, local groceries sell jams, apple vinegar, and artisanal juice.

⏱ 30 min

Return to see the tombs at nightfall: the spotlights that illuminate them create a mirror effect on the river below. This is when Amasya is most beautiful - and least crowded.

⏱ 30 min

Bogazkale

★★★★☆
1

Hattusa was the capital of the Hittite Empire for over four centuries, from approximately 1650 to 1178 BC. Inscribed on UNESCO's World Heritage list since 1986, the site covers several square kilometers and should be visited by car - the ruins are too spread out to do everything on foot. Begin with the lower city: the Great Temple dedicated to the Storm God and the Sun Goddess of Arinna, with its storerooms for cuneiform tablets and cyclopean blocks. More than 30,000 cuneiform tablets were discovered here, including a copy of the peace treaty of Kadesh between Hattusili III and Ramesses II.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Lion Gate closes the southwestern entrance to the upper city. Two limestone lions - protruding heads, open muzzles - have guarded the passage since the 13th century BC. Right next door, the Yerkapı (Earth Gate) is pierced by a 70-meter tunnel under an artificial rampart. This corbelled tunnel - a rare technique in Anatolia at that time - probably served during religious rituals or defensive sorties. Climb onto the rampart from outside to see the two sphinxes that flanked the entrance on the steppe side (copies - the originals are in Berlin and Istanbul).

⏱ 60 min
3

The King's Gate (Kral Kapı), to the southeast, is decorated with a relief depicting a divine warrior - probably the Storm God himself. The original is in Ankara's Museum of Anatolian Civilizations; here, a faithful copy stands in place. The upper city rampart runs for 6 km and hints at the city's extent at its peak.

⏱ 45 min
4

Yazılıkaya, 2 km northeast of Hattusa, is an open-air rock sanctuary carved into two natural limestone chambers. The walls are covered with 63 reliefs of Hittite deities in procession - the largest ensemble of this type known. In chamber A, the Storm God Teshub faces the Sun Goddess Hepat, both standing on deified mountains. In chamber B, narrower, King Tudhaliya IV is depicted in the arms of the god Sharruma. The quality of the reliefs, carved around 1250 BC, rivals contemporary Egyptian bas-reliefs.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Hire a local guide in Boğazkale before visiting the site - the on-site panels are useful but insufficient for understanding Hittite cosmology and the function of the different gates. Guides speaking French are rare but English speakers are good. Allow 3 to 4 hours for Hattusa + Yazılıkaya.

⏱ 30 min

After the visit, the small village of Boğazkale offers a few simple guesthouses and a handful of restaurants serving central Anatolian dishes. Enjoy the evening calm - most visitors leave the same day for Ankara or Çorum.

⏱ 60 min

Safranbolu

★★★★☆
1

Safranbolu's old town is one of two Turkish cities inscribed on UNESCO's World Heritage list as early as 1994, along with Istanbul. Of the 2,000 Ottoman houses that compose the urban fabric, 800 are officially protected by the state. These three-level residences - stone basement, upper floors in timber frame with overhangs - were built primarily in the 18th century, when Safranbolu was a major stopover on the caravan route between Istanbul and the Black Sea coast. Begin the visit in the Çarşı (market) quarter to see the town at its usual rhythm.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Cinci Han is the most imposing caravanserai east of Istanbul, built in 1645 by Cinci Hoca, an influential figure at the Ottoman court. This two-level ashlar stone building housed merchants, horses, and goods on the Silk Road. Today converted into a hotel and café, it also houses a coffee museum on the second floor, with Ottoman preparation tools and archives on coffee's role in Safranbolu's urban life.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Cinci Hamam (Cinci Hamamı), built in the 17th century at the same time as the neighboring caravanserai, is one of the rare historic baths still functional in the region. The two entrances - men and women - open onto the same cobbled streets. The domes pierced with frosted glass pentagons let in filtered light characteristic of classical Ottoman hammams.

⏱ 90 min
4

The Köprülü Mehmet Paşa Mosque, built in the second half of the 17th century, is the other central monument of the Çarşı. Its painted three-dimensional coffered ceiling and colorful wall details contrast with the usual external sobriety of provincial mosques. Right across from the Cinci Hamam, the two buildings form a cohesive ensemble on the same square.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The arasta of the shoemakers (Yemeniciler Arastası) is Safranbolu's historic artisan market - a covered gallery where craftsmen still work making yemeni, heelless leather sandals using Ottoman technique. Only one active manufacturer remains in the entire city. The arasta also houses groceries selling local saffron and artisanal lokum.

⏱ 45 min

Safranbolu is explored on foot - the Çarşı's cobbled streets are not drivable. Park the vehicle at the old town entrance parking and set out on foot for the day. The climb toward the Bağlar quarter (summer houses with gardens and vines) offers a view over the entire valley.

⏱ 60 min

Bolu

★★★☆☆
1

Before leaving Safranbolu, a morning visit to the saffron fields is possible in October - the only month when the Crocus sativus crocuses are in bloom. Stigma harvesting is done by hand, at dawn, before the heat weakens the filaments. About 30 farmers still cultivate Safranbolu saffron (European geographical indication saffron). Outside October, drying rooms and artisan cooperatives remain visitable on request.

⏱ 60 min
2

Lake Abant, 34 km southwest of Bolu, is a natural lake at 1,310 meters altitude formed by a landslide in a cradle of pine and fir forests. A 7 km trail circles the entire lake - walkable in two hours, without significant elevation. The lake harbors an endemic trout subspecies, the Abant Alası (Salmo trutta abanticus), listed on the red list of vulnerable species.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

The Safranbolu → Bolu road descends westward through the forests of the Karabük region before joining the D-100 / TEM highway. Lake Abant is a 20 km detour from Bolu - don't miss it if the weather's fine. In autumn, the beech forests around the lake take on orange and red tones worth the trip alone.

⏱ 30 min

Bolu is known in Turkey for producing many great cooks - the Mengen region has been renowned for its chefs since Ottoman times. In the evening, look for a restaurant serving Mengen Pilavı or a local kebap: Bolu cuisine is simple, generous, and little known to foreign tourists.

Istanbul

★★★★★
1

Mudurnu, 50 km southwest of Bolu, is an Ottoman village classified as Cittaslow (international slow cities network) with 173 protected historic buildings, including wooden houses from the 17th to 19th centuries. The Yıldırım Beyazıt Mosque (1382) is one of the first examples of a large dome without interior columns in Ottoman architecture. The cobbled streets, covered market, and Ottoman fountains make Mudurnu a perfect rural interlude before tackling the highway to Istanbul.

⏱ 90 min
2

The final Bolu → Istanbul road via the TEM highway (D-100) crosses the wooded mountains of Bolu Dağı before descending toward the Thracian plain. Approaching Istanbul from the east, the city gradually reveals itself - minarets, Bosphorus, suspension bridges. Arrival late afternoon if leaving Bolu in the morning. Traffic entering Istanbul is unavoidable after 5pm.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Arriving in Istanbul in the evening, don't try to see everything tonight. A walk on the Galata Bridge or tea by the Bosphorus is enough to mark the end of the Turkey circuit. You've traveled from Trabzon, crossed the Pontic Alps, the Anatolian plains, Hittite ruins, Ottoman townhouses of Safranbolu - and you're back at the starting point of any journey in Turkey.

⏱ 60 min
