Torridon

★★★☆☆
1

The NC500 officially starts and ends at Inverness Castle, but the route makes more sense driven clockwise - west first, east last, saving the dramatic coast of Caithness and the east Highland passage for the final days. Head south-west on the A82 and A87 toward Kyle of Lochalsh. You'll pass through Eilean Donan Castle country - the most photographed castle in Scotland, perched on a tiny island where three lochs converge, built in the 13th century to guard against Viking raids, blown up in 1719 when Spanish troops supporting the Jacobites stored 343 barrels of gunpowder inside, then rebuilt stone by stone from 1919 to 1932. Stop for the view from the road bridge; the castle entrance itself is worth 45 minutes if you have time.

⏱ 45 min
2

From Kyle of Lochalsh, take the A896 north into Glen Torridon. This valley is one of the most geologically extraordinary places in Scotland: the mountains on either side are Torridonian sandstone, around 750 million years old, sitting directly on Lewisian gneiss that is 3 billion years old. Almost nowhere else in the world does rock of such different ages sit in direct contact. Beinn Eighe (1,010 m) and Liathach (1,055 m) loom over the road. The National Trust for Scotland manages a deer museum at Torridon village - worth a 20-minute stop to understand the landscape before walking in it.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The Torridon hills offer some of the finest - and most serious - ridge walking in Scotland. The approach to Liathach from the road up the glen is for experienced hillwalkers only; the summit ridge involves Grade 2 scrambling in places and the exposure is significant. For a more accessible but still dramatic walk, the path up the Coire Mhic Fhearchair on Beinn Eighe reaches a dramatic Triple Buttress cirque at 750 m - one of the grandest mountain corries in Scotland. Expect 4 to 5 hours return and proper boots.

Gairloch

★★★☆☆
1

The Bealach na Bà is the reason many people drive the NC500. The road was built in 1822, modelled on Alpine passes, and rises from sea level at Loch Kishorn to 626 metres in 9 kilometres - the greatest ascent of any road climb in the UK, and one of the highest mountain roads in Scotland. Hairpin bends approach gradients of 20%. The warning sign at the foot makes no pretence: 'This road rises to a height of 2053ft, with gradients of 1 in 5 and hairpin bends. Not advised for learner drivers, very large vehicles or caravans after first mile.' Take it seriously - local breakdown services advise nothing larger than a small van. Drive up from the east (Loch Kishorn side) for the better views on descent into Applecross. At the summit parking area, by clear days you can see Skye, the Outer Hebrides and the mainland mountains in all directions.

⏱ 60 min
2

Applecross village, once reachable only by the Bealach na Bà or by sea, sits on a sheltered bay facing Skye. The Applecross Inn on the shore has been feeding travellers since the 1970s and its seafood reputation is justified - langoustines from the bay, local crab, venison from the estate. Eat here if you can; the setting is as good as the food. From Applecross, take the coastal road north around the peninsula via Shieldaig to rejoin the A896 - a much gentler drive than the Bealach, but with constant sea views and the chance of otters along the shore.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Inverewe Garden at Poolewe, 12 km north of Gairloch on the A832, is a botanical anomaly: at latitude 57°N, warmed by the Gulf Stream, it grows Himalayan rhododendrons, South American plants and Australasian tree ferns in the open air. Osgood Mackenzie founded it in 1862 on a bare headland, importing soil by boat from the mainland. The National Trust for Scotland now manages it. Allow 2 hours and check whether it is open - seasonal closures apply.

Ullapool

★★★☆☆
1

The A832 north of Gairloch passes Gruinard Bay - a wide beach with sand that shifts between gold and rose-pink depending on the light. The island visible from the shore, Gruinard Island, carries a specific and grim history. In 1942, the British government tested anthrax bombs here under Operation Vegetarian, contaminating the island with the Vollum 14578 strain. It stayed under quarantine for 48 years. In 1986, 280 tonnes of diluted formaldehyde were sprayed across every acre. In 1990 it was declared safe and sold back to the original owner's heirs for £500 - the same price agreed in 1942.

⏱ 30 min
2

Twenty kilometres south of Ullapool on the A835, the Corrieshalloch Gorge is one of Scotland's most dramatic geological features: a box canyon 1.5 km long, 60 metres deep and in places only 10 metres wide, carved at the end of the last ice age 10,000 to 13,000 years ago. A Victorian suspension bridge designed by Sir John Fowler - one of the engineers of the Forth Rail Bridge - crosses 25 metres above the Falls of Measach, a 46-metre waterfall. The bridge sways in the wind. Plan 45 minutes.

⏱ 45 min
3

Ullapool was designed and built in 1788 by Thomas Telford on behalf of the British Fisheries Society as a planned herring port. Its grid of white houses around the Loch Broom pier still follows Telford's original layout. The town is the main ferry terminal for Stornoway on Lewis - you'll see the CalMac ferries in the harbour. Walk the waterfront at dusk when the light on the loch turns the mountains pink.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The Summer Isles, a scattering of uninhabited islands at the mouth of Loch Broom, are one of the best wildlife-watching areas on the NC500. Boat trips from Ullapool harbour run from spring to autumn and give access to grey seals, bottlenose dolphins, minke whales in summer, and gannet colonies. The largest island, Tanera Mòr, has its own post office issuing private stamps. Book in advance.

Lochinver

★★★☆☆
1

North of Ullapool, the road enters Assynt - a landscape unlike anything south of it. Here the mountains don't form ranges but stand alone, isolated on a flat moorland plain like pins in a map: Stac Pollaidh (613 m), Cùl Mòr (849 m), Suilven (731 m). Suilven is the most dramatic, a long whale-backed ridge that appears from the south as a single improbable sugarloaf. The first geological survey of this area in the 19th century triggered one of the most important revolutions in the history of geology: two local quarrymen - not professional scientists - correctly identified that the Moine Thrust Sheet had pushed ancient rock over younger rock, overturning the assumption that older rock always lay beneath younger. Archibald Geikie of the Geological Survey initially dismissed their findings, then was proved wrong.

⏱ 30 min
2

The ruined Ardvreck Castle, jutting from a promontory into Loch Assynt, is one of the most photogenic ruins on the NC500 - three storeys of 15th-century MacLeod tower reflected in still water. It is free to visit and the walk from the parking area takes 5 minutes. James Graham, 1st Marquess of Montrose, was held prisoner here in 1650 after his defeat at the Battle of Carbisdale, before being handed to the government and executed in Edinburgh.

⏱ 30 min
3

Lochinver is a real working fishing harbour - langoustines, haddock, monkfish - and the best place to eat on this section of the route. The Lochinver Larder, a bakery attached to a small restaurant, makes pies that have achieved cult status on the NC500: venison and cranberry, smoked haddock and cheddar, haggis, neeps and tatties. Arrive before noon if you want the best selection.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The ascent of Stac Pollaidh from the car park on the B869 is one of the best short hillwalks in Sutherland: 3 km return, 350 m of ascent, with scrambling near the top and a spectacular ridge view over the Assynt lochs and sea. Allow 3 hours including the scramble. The path is well marked. For those who don't want to scramble to the summit, the walk to the bealach (col) at the base of the pinnacles is itself worth 2 hours.

Durness

★★★☆☆
1

Kylesku Bridge, an elegant concrete span opened in 1984 to replace a cable ferry, carries the NC500 over Loch a' Chàirn Bhàin. Stop at the hotel car park below for the view back along the loch. From here, boat trips run to Eas a' Chual Aluinn, the highest waterfall in the UK at 200 metres - three times the height of Niagara - hidden in a remote gorge accessible only by boat or a long walk. The boats also pass grey seal colonies on the rocks.

⏱ 30 min
2

Sandwood Bay is the most remote and arguably most beautiful beach on the NC500 - 4 km of pink shell sand backed by dunes and an inland loch, with Am Buachaille, a 65-metre sea stack, standing sentinel at the south end. It is reached only by a 8 km return walk across open moorland from the car park at Blairmore, south of Kinlochbervie. The track is good and the walk takes about 2.5 to 3 hours return at a moderate pace. Carry food and water - there is nothing there. The beach is almost always deserted. A reported ghost - a bearded sailor - has been sighted here more times than anywhere else in Scotland; the story is rooted in a real shipwreck on this coast, and has been embellished by folklore ever since.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

The crossing to Handa Island, a mile offshore from Scourie, is one of the best wildlife experiences on the NC500. A small passenger ferry (10 minutes) takes you to a Scottish Wildlife Trust reserve where around 100,000 seabirds nest from April to August: guillemots, razorbills, puffins, fulmars, great skuas. The Great Stack - a 120-metre sandstone tower - is a landmark of Scottish climbing history; the first ascent in 1876 was made via a rope crossing. The walk around the island takes about 2.5 hours. No dogs, booking not required.

⏱ 180 min

Tongue

★★★☆☆
1

Cape Wrath is the most north-westerly point of the British mainland - and getting there requires a small ferry across the Kyle of Durness, followed by an 11-mile minibus ride over military training ground. The route is managed by a single operator and runs April to October, weather permitting. At the cape, the Stevenson lighthouse of 1828 stands above 280-metre cliffs of Torridonian sandstone, the same ancient rock as the Torridon mountains. On a clear day the Orkney islands are visible 45 miles north-east. The cliffs below the lighthouse hold one of the largest mainland gannet colonies in Britain. The minibus driver's commentary is part of the experience - book in advance.

⏱ 120 min
2

Smoo Cave, 2 km east of Durness on the A838, is the largest sea cave entrance in Britain - 40 metres wide and 15 metres high. What makes it unusual within the UK is that the first chamber was formed by sea action while the inner chambers are freshwater passages, carved by the Allt Smoo stream dissolving the dolomite limestone. They are entirely separate geological processes that happen to have joined up. A 20-metre waterfall drops through a hole in the inner chamber's ceiling. You can walk to the first chamber and waterfall platform for free at any time; guided boat tours of the inner chamber run April to September. The name is Old Norse - 'smjúga' or 'smuga', meaning a narrow hole or hiding place.

⏱ 60 min
3

Balnakeil beach, 2 km west of Durness, is a wide arc of white sand on a sheltered bay that is surprisingly tropical-looking when the sun is on the water. A ruined 17th-century church stands at the back of the beach; the grave of Donald McMurchow, a murderer who paid for the church's construction in exchange for a burial inside the walls rather than in unconsecrated ground, is still visible. The Balnakeil craft village - a collection of former Cold War early warning station buildings converted into artists' studios and workshops - is worth a wander.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Kyle of Tongue causeway, carrying the A838 across the tidal Kyle, is one of the most photographed spots on the north coast. Pull over at the parking area at the southern end and look north: the castle ruin of Castle Varrich on its hilltop, the causeway striping the blue water, the cone of Ben Loyal (764 m) - sometimes called the Queen of Scottish Mountains - in the background. Spend 20 minutes here.

Kirkwall

★★★☆☆
1

The east coast of Caithness is flat, agricultural and windswept - a dramatic change from the mountain scenery of Sutherland. Before catching the ferry at Scrabster, make time for Duncansby Head, 3 km east of John O'Groats along a track from the car park. The headland is the true north-east corner of the mainland, and the Duncansby Stacks - three tall sea stacks separated from the cliffs by narrow geos - are among the most striking rock formations on the north coast. Fulmars nest on every ledge. The 20-minute walk from the lighthouse takes you to the cliff edge above the largest stack.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Castle of Mey, 12 km west of John O'Groats on the A836, was a derelict 16th-century castle when Queen Elizabeth the Queen Mother bought it in 1952 as a personal retreat after the death of George VI. She renovated it herself and used it as a private holiday home until her death in 2002. It is now a charitable trust and open to the public from May to September. The walled garden is particularly well maintained. If you have time before the ferry, allow 90 minutes.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The NorthLink Ferries service from Scrabster to Stromness (Orkney) takes 90 minutes and runs twice daily. As the ferry clears the Pentland Firth - one of the most tidal stretches of water in Europe - look back for the Old Man of Hoy, a 137-metre sea stack visible from the upper deck. Then look forward: the low green coastline of Orkney Mainland emerging from the water with no mountains, no drama, just a flat ancient landscape - which makes the Neolithic density of what's waiting there all the more astonishing. Book well in advance in summer, particularly with a car.

⏱ 90 min

Kirkwall

★★★☆☆
1

Skara Brae, on the Bay of Skaill 8 km north of Stromness, was buried under sand for nearly 4,000 years until a severe storm in 1850 stripped the dunes back and revealed the rooftops of a Neolithic village. What was uncovered is the best-preserved prehistoric village in northern Europe: eight stone houses connected by covered passageways, with stone-built furniture - beds, dressers, hearths - still in place as left around 2500 BC. The residents kept cattle and sheep, grew grain, fished, and made pottery. Who they were, why they abandoned the village, and where they went remains unknown. A replica house beside the visitor centre shows the interiors at full scale. Book in advance in summer - visitor numbers are managed.

⏱ 90 min
2

Five km south-east of Skara Brae, the Ring of Brodgar and Stones of Stenness sit within a few hundred metres of each other on a narrow isthmus between two lochs - part of the UNESCO Heart of Neolithic Orkney World Heritage Site designated in 1999. The Ring of Brodgar is a true circle 130 metres in diameter, originally 60 stones, now 27 surviving, surrounded by a rock-cut ditch up to 3 metres deep. It was estimated to have required 80,000 man-hours to construct. The Stones of Stenness, erected around 3100 BC, are earlier and the remaining four megaliths stand up to 6 metres tall. Between them, the ongoing Ness of Brodgar excavation has revealed a massive ceremonial complex - what archaeologists are calling a Neolithic 'cathedral' - that rewrites the understanding of this landscape. Both stone circles are free to visit. Maeshowe, the chambered passage tomb whose entrance aligns with the midwinter sunset, is nearby - book a guided tour in advance; the runic inscriptions left by Norse raiders in the 12th century are extraordinary.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

St Magnus Cathedral in Kirkwall, begun in 1137 by Jarl Rögnvald in memory of his uncle Magnus who was martyred in 1117, is the most northerly medieval cathedral in Britain and has been in continuous use for nearly 900 years. It is built entirely from red and yellow Orkney sandstone with no imported stone - one of the purest expressions of local materials in Scottish ecclesiastical architecture. Entry is free. In the choir, human remains later identified as those of Magnus himself were found concealed in a pillar in 1919.

Kirkwall

★★★☆☆
1

The Churchill Barriers, four causeways connecting the southern islands of Orkney, were built between 1940 and 1945 to block the eastern approaches to Scapa Flow after a German U-boat torpedoed HMS Royal Oak in the anchorage in October 1939, killing 833 men. The 1,700 Italian prisoners of war who built the barriers also constructed the Italian Chapel on Lamb Holm - two Nissen huts converted into a remarkably ornate Roman Catholic chapel with trompe l'oeil stonework, a concrete rood screen and an altar fronted with painted iron. The main craftsman, Domenico Chiocchetti, returned in 1960 and 1964 to restore the work he had done as a prisoner. It is one of the most affecting places in Scotland.

⏱ 45 min
2

Scapa Flow, the natural harbour enclosed by the southern Orkney islands, was the main base of the British Grand Fleet in both World Wars. After the German fleet surrendered in 1918, 74 ships were interned here. In June 1919, the German admiral ordered all ships scuttled rather than allow them to be taken as prizes - 52 sank, some in shallow water. Seven warships still lie on the seabed, offering some of the best wreck diving in the world. The Orkney Museum in Kirkwall tells the Scapa Flow story well, with artefacts from both German and British ships.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The ferry from Houton to the island of Hoy takes 25 minutes and gives access to the Old Man of Hoy - a 137-metre sandstone sea stack first climbed in July 1966 by Tom Patey, Hamish MacInnes and Pete Crew. The following year, a BBC television broadcast of a second ascent involving Chris Bonington, Patey and MacInnes was watched by around 15 million people - one of the most-watched climbing broadcasts in history. The walk to the cliff edge above the stack from Rackwick Bay takes about 3 hours return across moorland. A longer 8-km circuit via the summit plateau is one of the best day walks in Orkney. Note that Houton is a 30-minute drive west of Kirkwall - factor that in when planning the day. Book the ferry in advance.

Wick

★★☆☆☆
1

Ferry back from Stromness to Scrabster, then south along the A99 toward Wick. John O'Groats is worth stopping at for the sign and the view across to the Orkney islands you've just come from - though be clear that it is not the most northerly point of mainland Scotland (that is Dunnet Head, 20 km to the west). The most interesting stop near here is the ruin of Castle Sinclair Girnigoe, on a cliff promontory 5 km north of Wick: a 15th-century fortress that partly collapsed into the sea, its walls split by geological fault lines. Walk from the car park and allow 45 minutes.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Whaligoe Steps, south of Wick on the A99, descend 365 hand-cut flagstone steps down a sheer cliff to a tiny natural harbour barely large enough for three boats. It was built in the 18th century for the herring fishery - fisherwomen carried full creels of fish up these steps to the road above. The views from the top and bottom are extraordinary. The steps are steep and uneven; allow 30 minutes.

⏱ 45 min
3

Wick was the herring capital of the world in the 19th century, processing millions of barrels a year for export to the Baltic states and the West Indies. The Wick Heritage Museum has one of the best collections of Victorian industrial photography in Scotland - nearly 70,000 glass plates taken by Johnston & Sons between 1863 and 1977 documenting the town, the fleet and the fishery. Entry is free and the museum is run entirely by volunteers. Allow 1 hour.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Flow Country, west of Wick and accessible via the A882 through Halkirk, is the largest blanket peat bog in Europe - 4,000 square kilometres of undulating peatland that stores more carbon per square metre than tropical rainforest. In 2023 it became a UNESCO World Heritage Site - the first peatland in the world to receive that designation, recognised not just as a landscape but as a critical carbon store whose protection matters to the entire planet. The Forsinard Flows visitor centre, operated by the RSPB, has a walkway over the bog, observation tower and guided tours in season. A surreal, silent landscape unlike anywhere else in Britain.

Dornoch

★★☆☆☆
1

Dunrobin Castle, a mile north of Golspie on the A9, is the largest house in the northern Highlands - 189 rooms - and the most northerly of Scotland's great Victorian country houses. Its origins are medieval, first mentioned as a stronghold in 1401, but most of what you see was built between 1835 and 1850 by Sir Charles Barry - the architect of the Houses of Parliament in Westminster. The result is something between a French château and a Scottish Baronial castle, with conical spires and formal gardens modelled on Versailles, sitting on a bluff above the Dornoch Firth. Queen Victoria described it in 1872 as 'a mixture of an old Scotch castle and a French chateau'. Daily falconry displays in the garden from spring to autumn. Open April to October.

⏱ 120 min
2

Dornoch, 15 km south of Dunrobin on the A9, is a small town of considerable refinement that most NC500 drivers pass straight through on the way to Inverness. Don't. The cathedral dates from the 13th century, was burned by clan rival Clan Murray in 1570 (destroying most of it), and rebuilt in 1835. Inside, a window commemorates the Carnegie family - Andrew Carnegie, the Scottish-American steel magnate, lived at nearby Skibo Castle. Dornoch has two other claims to fame: the last recorded execution for witchcraft in Scotland took place here in 1722, and in 2000 Madonna baptised her son Rocco in the cathedral. The beach north of town is one of the finest on the east coast.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Emigrants' Statue in Helmsdale, on the waterfront of this former herring town, is a quietly devastating monument to the Highland Clearances - the forced eviction of tens of thousands of crofters from Sutherland between 1811 and 1820 by the Countess of Sutherland's factor, Patrick Sellar, to make way for sheep farming. The bronze shows a man, woman and child looking out to sea. The wealth from those clearances partly funded the rebuilding of Dunrobin Castle you visited this morning. Worth 15 minutes and some reflection.

⏱ 20 min

Royal Dornoch Golf Club, founded in 1877 and consistently ranked in the world's top 10 courses, welcomes visitors. The links course plays along the Dornoch Firth with the cathedral in view. Green fees apply; book well in advance for peak season.

Inverness

The final leg of the NC500 cuts south across the Black Isle - actually a peninsula, not an island. Chanonry Point, a narrow spit of land on the Moray Firth south of Fortrose, is the most reliable place in Scotland to see bottlenose dolphins from the shore. The Moray Firth population is the most northerly in the world and one of the most studied. Dolphins come to fish the strong tidal current off the point, especially on an incoming tide - arrive 2 hours before high tide and stand on the beach. Year-round sightings, most reliable from May to September.

2

Cromarty, at the tip of the Black Isle, is one of the best-preserved 18th-century towns in Scotland - a planned merchants' town that prospered on trade with the Baltic states and American colonies before the railway bypassed it and locked it in amber. The birthplace of Hugh Miller, the stonemason turned geologist and writer who became one of the most important scientific voices of the Victorian era - his 1841 book The Old Red Sandstone transformed popular understanding of geology and fossils - is preserved as a museum. Miller died in 1856 at 54, having spent his life arguing that the rocks of northern Scotland held stories more ancient than anyone had imagined. The courthouse of 1782 is still standing. Walk the high street and down to the harbour.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Glenmorangie Distillery in Tain, on the south shore of the Dornoch Firth, is one of the most distinctive single malts in Scotland - distilled in the tallest pot stills in Scotland (5.14 metres, roughly the height of a giraffe, chosen to produce a lighter, more floral spirit). Founded in 1843, it is now owned by LVMH. Tours and tastings are available and the visitor experience is excellent. Allow 1.5 hours.

Inverness

★★★★☆

Back in Inverness, 8 km to the east lies Culloden Battlefield - the site of the last pitched battle fought on British soil, on 16 April 1746. The Jacobite army of Bonnie Prince Charlie was destroyed in under an hour by the Duke of Cumberland's government forces; around 1,500 Jacobites were killed against 50 government troops. The defeat ended the Stuart claim to the throne and triggered the systematic dismantling of Highland clan culture: the wearing of tartan was banned, clan chiefs stripped of their powers. The National Trust for Scotland's visitor centre (opened 2007) is one of the best battlefield interpretive centres in Britain - the immersive 360° film is genuinely affecting. Allow 1.5 hours.

⏱ 90 min
2

Cawdor Castle, 15 km east of Inverness on the B9090, is forever associated with Macbeth - though Shakespeare's play pre-dates the castle's thane of Cawdor title by several centuries and the historical Macbeth had nothing to do with this building. The castle itself, built around a 14th-century tower, is one of the most liveable-feeling historic houses in Scotland: the current family still lives here and the interiors feel inhabited rather than preserved. The gardens are exceptional. Open May to October.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Clava Cairns, 10 km east of Inverness near Culloden, are three Neolithic passage tombs and ring cairns built around 2000 BC. They are free to visit, almost always empty of tourists, and more immediately accessible than most prehistoric monuments - you can walk into the chambers. The bronze-age cairns are aligned with the winter solstice sunset. If you've seen Culloden and are travelling through Outlander country, these are the stones that inspired the Craig na Dun circle in Diana Gabaldon's fiction.
