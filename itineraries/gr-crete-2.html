Lasithi

★★★☆☆
1

Approaching from Heraklion—about 90 minutes by car—or from Agios Nikolaos (75 minutes), you emerge onto a sight utterly unexpected after the coastal scrubland: the Lasithi Plateau, a green basin 850 meters above sea level, 11 kilometers long, ringed by the Dikti Mountains that soar above 2,100 meters. What strikes you first are the cultivated fields and, dotted here and there, restored windmills. The Venetians once built up to 5,000 of them to irrigate the land through a network of canals called linies. Stop at Seli Ambelou at the plateau's entrance to see them lined up against the sky—it's the most photographed viewpoint in Lasithi.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Dikteon Cave, known as the Cave of Zeus, clings to the mountainside at 1,025 meters above the village of Psychro. According to Greek mythology, this is where Rhea hid her infant son Zeus from his father Cronos, who had a nasty habit of devouring his own children. Descending the 314 steps into the mountain's belly is a bit of a workout, but the payoff is real: a forest of stalactites and an underground lake in an atmosphere that feels genuinely mystical. Bring a sweater—it's cool inside year-round, no matter how scorching it is outside. The cave draws over 200,000 visitors annually; arrive early in the morning to explore without the crowds.

⏱ 90 min
3

On the road climbing toward Lasithi, stop at the Kera Kardiotissa Monastery, perched at 650 meters on the northern slopes of Mount Dikti. Its first written mention dates to 1333. What sets this place apart is the story of its miraculous icon: stolen three times by the Turks, it returned three times on its own. The third time, they chained it to a marble column to prevent another escape—it came back anyway, bringing the column with it. You'll see the column in the courtyard and the chain beside the icon. The original has been in Rome since 1498; the copy that replaced it in 1735 is also considered miraculous.

⏱ 45 min
4

Make a stop in Tzermiado, the plateau's 'capital.' Its folklore museum reconstructs two Cretan interiors: one traditional mountain dwelling, the other a neoclassical home from the post-Ottoman period. If you have time, head to the Kronion Cave at the village entrance—a Minoan cult site frequented since the Neolithic, far less known than the Dikteon Cave and often completely deserted. The plateau is one of the rare places in Crete where people live year-round, even under winter snow: take the time to chat with locals in the village cafés.

⏱ 45 min
5

Agios Georgios houses a museum dedicated to daily life on the plateau—better curated than its modest size might suggest. You'll find a carefully reconstructed Cretan mountain house and a room devoted to Eleftherios Venizelos, the great Greek statesman whose family hailed from the region. Between May and October, the museum is open Monday to Saturday from 10:30 AM to 5 PM. Nominal entrance fee.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

The drive up to the plateau from Heraklion or Agios Nikolaos is a pleasure in itself: the road winds through panoramic mountain passes and perched villages. From Agios Nikolaos (about 75 minutes), the approach from the south side of the Dikti massif is particularly beautiful. If you're coming from the north coast, stop at the Seli Ambelou viewpoint to see the windmills lined up with the valley below—and in the distance on clear days, the Aposelemis reservoir and the submerged village of Sfendyli.

⏱ 60 min

The Seli windmill loop is an easy 3-kilometer walk among the 19th-century stone windmills and orchards. The E4 trail also crosses the plateau for those who want to push on to the Minoan ruins at Mount Karfi, with spectacular views over the coast. Bring sturdy shoes and water.

⏱ 90 min

In the tavernas of Psychro or Tzermiado, ask for stakovoutyro: a fermented mountain butter from the Lasithi highlands, made by churning sheep's milk, that you eat spread on grilled bread. Rare—you'll find it almost nowhere else in Crete. If you're there in season, the taverna tables overflow with local products: fresh cheeses, wild thyme honey, mountain potatoes.

⏱ 45 min

For dinner, order ofto at a plateau taverna: a whole lamb slow-roasted in an earthen oven with potatoes, garlic, and wild herbs—meat so tender it falls apart, juices concentrated and rich. Lasithi tavernas serve it mostly on Sundays as a traditional dish. Pair it with a glass of Romeiko, the rustic and ancient red grape of eastern Crete, vinified naturally by local producers.

⏱ 60 min

Agios Nikolaos

★★★☆☆
1

Start at Lake Voulismeni, the beating heart of Agios Nikolaos. This 64-meter-deep lake of karst origin was long thought to be bottomless—hence its local nickname—and ancient legend held that Athena and Artemis bathed in its waters. Since 1870, a narrow canal has linked it to the harbor. The tavernas and cafés lining its shores make it the perfect spot for a first break. If you're there in July, don't miss the Cliff Diving Festival: divers from around the world leap from the cliffs overlooking the lake—quite an improbable spectacle.

⏱ 30 min
2

Don't skip the Archaeological Museum of Agios Nikolaos—it's the second most important in Crete after Heraklion. It traces the region's history from the Neolithic (around 5700 BC) through the Roman period. Its most famous treasure is the Goddess of Myrtos: a libation vessel shaped like a female figure with long outstretched arms, discovered at the Bronze Age settlement of Fournos Korifi and dating to approximately 2500 BC. Allow 60 to 90 minutes to see it properly.

⏱ 90 min
3

On the road to Kritsa, stop 1 kilometer before the village: the Church of Panagia Kera sits tucked on the right side, guarded by an ancient olive tree with a hollow trunk. It's the best-preserved Byzantine monument on the entire island, and the frescoes covering every wall are the reason why. The central nave dates to the 13th century; the two lateral naves were added in the 14th—one bears the exact date of 1348 painted on the wall, along with a portrait of the donor Georgios Mazizanis, his wife, and child. The Palaeologan style, with its expressive figures, clean contours, and vivid colors, is rare for religious art of the period. Photos without flash are permitted.

⏱ 60 min
4

Kritsa, perched at 300 meters elevation 9 kilometers from Agios Nikolaos, is one of Crete's largest villages and among its most picturesque, with stepped alleyways, stone houses, and weavers still working traditional looms. The local embroidery and textiles are renowned throughout the region—you'll find several artisans in the village center. In the evening, once the tour buses have left, the village becomes surprisingly quiet and authentic again.

⏱ 60 min
5

From Kritsa, push on to the site of Lato, 3 kilometers away: a Dorian city founded in the 7th century BC, perched on two hills overlooking Mirabello Gulf. You'll find paved streets, monumental staircases, an agora, and temple foundations—and a view of the sea and mountains worth the trip alone. Allow 45 minutes to an hour for a leisurely visit. Bring water: there's no shade on the site.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Stroll around Lake Voulismeni in the evening: that's when locals gather on the café terraces lining both shores, connected by a bridge. The buzz builds as night falls—there's real nightlife in the adjacent streets. If you're there early in the morning, the lake is strikingly calm and the reflections of the cliff in the water feel otherworldly.

⏱ 45 min

Explore Kritsa for its craft workshops: weaving, embroidery, pottery, and local products (honey, oil, herbs) are sold directly by artisans in the alleyways. The village also has several modest museums, including the House of Kritsotopoula, a folklore museum housed in a traditional old dwelling, and the Rodanthi museum dedicated to Cretan medicinal plants and herbs.

⏱ 60 min

If you're curious about the most ancient Cretan cuisine, order xinochondros at an Agios Nikolaos taverna: a fermented dried paste made from sheep's cheese and cracked wheat, a winter staple of the Cretan mountains, simmered into a soup with seasonal vegetables. It has a distinctly sour and salty taste—an acquired one, let's say. Pair it with a glass of Vilana white, the indigenous Cretan grape, light and fresh with citrus notes, ideal with fish from Mirabello Gulf.

⏱ 45 min

Swim at Almyros Beach, about 2 kilometers south of Agios Nikolaos: a long stretch of fine sand with calm waters, well-equipped and less crowded than the town center beaches. Several beaches then dot the coast toward Istro and Pachia Ammos along Mirabello Bay—ideal for a late afternoon if you're returning from Lato or Kritsa.

⏱ 60 min

Elounda

★★☆☆☆
1

Spinalonga is the most history-laden place in all of eastern Crete. The Venetians began fortifying this 34-hectare rocky islet in 1579 to protect Mirabello Gulf—the fortress was so solid that even after the Ottomans took Crete in 1669, it held out another 46 years before falling in 1715. Entry is through a long dark tunnel that the lepers deported here between 1903 and 1957 nicknamed 'Dante's Gate.' For over 50 years, between 300 and 400 patients lived here as a community—with a baker, a barber, a priest. The last inhabitant, a priest, didn't leave the island until 1962. Allow 90 minutes to explore it fully. From Plaka (5-minute crossing) or from Elounda (20 minutes), boats depart regularly in high season.

⏱ 120 min
2

Between Elounda and the peninsula, a causeway runs along the ancient city of Olous, submerged since antiquity. At low tide, you can see the mosaics of a 4th-century early Christian basilica with the naked eye—just beneath the surface, among the rocks. Olous was one of the great cities of Minoan and Classical Crete, mentioned as early as 3000 BC. With a mask and snorkel, the walls of the ancient acropolis are visible in shallow water. Swimming in this small lagoon sheltered from the wind is among the most pleasant in the region.

⏱ 45 min
3

Elounda is the most comfortable base for this day. The resort is upscale—international resorts have set up here since the 1970s—but the waterfront of the original fishing village, the old salt pans between the town and the Olous peninsula, and the fish tavernas at the harbor are worth a detour beyond the hotels. The view of Spinalonga from the lagoon beaches is one of the finest in the region, especially late afternoon when golden light falls on the fortress walls.

⏱ 45 min
4

Plaka is a fishing village 5 kilometers north of Elounda, facing Spinalonga. It's the closest departure point for the island (5-minute crossing) and also a remarkably preserved village with tavernas on the harbor, colorful boats, and nets drying in the sun. The grilled catch of the day here is impeccably fresh—Spinalonga's residents used to get their supplies here. Reserve a table for sunset over the island.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The boat trip to Spinalonga is unmissable—but organize it early in the morning. By 10 AM in summer, groups land in waves and the vaulted passages become packed. Leaving Plaka at 8:30 or 9 AM, you stand a good chance of exploring the fortress in relative calm. Bring water and a hat: there's virtually no shade on the island.

⏱ 180 min

After Spinalonga, swim in the lagoon between the Olous peninsula and Elounda: calm, shallow, and remarkably clear waters. With a mask and snorkel, you can search for the underwater ruins of the ancient city in shallow depths. The sandy beach on the way to the peninsula is one of the least crowded in the area.

⏱ 90 min

For breakfast or brunch, order sfougato at a harbor taverna in Elounda: a thick omelet of zucchini with sheep's cheese and Cretan herbs. Several places serve it in the morning. In season, also look for clusters of octopus drying in the sun outside the Plaka tavernas—order it grilled, it's one of the best things you'll eat on this coast. A white Cretan Assyrtiko, mineral and dry, pairs perfectly with both.

⏱ 60 min

Sitia

★★★☆☆
1

Sitia is the easternmost town in Crete, and the least touristy—it moves at its own rhythm. Built in an amphitheater facing the Aegean Sea, it was destroyed then abandoned in the 16th century before being repopulated from the mid-19th century onward, giving it a half-historic, half-living character. Climb to the Venetian fortress of Kazarma late in the afternoon for a panoramic view over the bay—the walk from the harbor takes barely 10 minutes and the coastal panorama is worth the effort. Below, the harbor hosts fishing boats and ferries to the Dodecanese.

⏱ 60 min
2

Toplou Monastery sits about ten kilometers east of Sitia, in the middle of an arid, lunar landscape. It's one of Crete's most important and most fortified monasteries—its Turkish name 'Toplou' means 'with a cannon,' which sums up its character nicely: pillaged by the Knights of Malta in 1530, destroyed by earthquake, rebuilt in the 17th century. Its icon museum houses the masterpiece of Ioannis Kornaros: the icon 'Megas ei Kyrie' (Great art Thou, O Lord), dated 1770, a composition in 57 zones with hundreds of faces illustrating the Great Blessing of the Waters. The monastery also produces renowned organic wine and quality olive oil, sold at the entrance. Open in summer 9 AM to 1 PM and 2 to 6 PM, entrance €3.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Vai palm forest, 24 kilometers east of Sitia, is Europe's largest natural palm forest, classified as a nature reserve. It shelters thousands of specimens of Phoenix theophrasti, the palm endemic to Crete and Turkey's southern coast—a species that grows nowhere else in Europe. The beach bordering the palm forest has earned the Blue Flag. According to legend, the palms grew from date pits tossed by Egyptian sailors, Saracen pirates, or Mithridates' army... Arrive early: in summer the beach is overrun by mid-morning.

⏱ 90 min
4

Three kilometers north of Vai, take time to climb up to the site of Itanos: an ancient Phoenician city whose ruins—temples, basilicas, cisterns—sprawl across a sandy hill that's almost deserted. The city was famous in antiquity for its trade in purple dye, the red color extracted from murex shells. You'll very likely have the site to yourself. Below lie two wild beaches in isolated coves—ideal for snorkeling in crystalline waters, far from any infrastructure.

⏱ 60 min
5

If you have a full day to devote to Minoan Crete, head down to Kato Zakros (45 kilometers from Sitia) for the Palace of Zakros—the fourth-largest Minoan palace in Crete after Knossos, Malia, and Phaistos. Built around 1900 BC, it faced eastward: excavations have yielded elephant tusks, ivory, stone vases, and exotic objects from Egypt and the Near East. You can reach it directly by car, or through the Gorge of the Dead (8-kilometer hike from the village of Zakros). Excavations, begun in the 1960s, still regularly reveal new rooms.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Explore Sitia's harbor in the evening: tavernas come alive around 8 PM, locals play cards in traditional cafés, and the promenade runs along the sea to Sitia Beach, a long strip of fine sand. Sitiots are renowned in Crete for their warmth and musical culture—you might stumble upon an impromptu terrace concert. Local raki (tsikoudia) automatically accompanies the welcome meze at authentic tavernas.

⏱ 90 min

To reach Zakros Palace on foot, take the Gorge of the Dead starting from the village of Zakros: 8 kilometers descending through a limestone canyon with vertiginous walls, dotted with caves that served as burial sites in the Minoan era—hence the name. It's one of the most beautiful hikes in eastern Crete, shaded and cool even in summer. Allow 3 hours, wear closed shoes, and arrange to have a car waiting at Kato Zakros below—otherwise you'll climb back up those 8 kilometers on foot.

⏱ 180 min

At the end of a meal in a Sitia taverna, order xerotigana: ribbons of dough fried in oil, drizzled with honey, and sprinkled with sesame or crushed walnuts. It's the traditional festive dessert of eastern Crete, served at weddings and baptisms—and at good tavernas in summer. Pair them with a red Liatiko, the indigenous grape of the region, sweet and fragrant with notes of dried grapes.

⏱ 45 min

Ierapetra

★★★☆☆
1

Ierapetra holds a title few cities can claim: Europe's southernmost, nicknamed 'the bride of the Libyan Sea.' Its position at the southern tip of Crete's isthmus has made it a strategic crossroads since antiquity—the ancient city of Hierapytna was already famous in the 3rd century BC for its piratical tendencies, before being destroyed by the Romans in 67 BC. Climb up to Kales Fortress, rebuilt in 1626, for a view over the fishing harbor—allow 30 minutes. And if you want a local oddity: Ierapetra is also one of Crete's wealthiest towns, thanks to the 13 square kilometers of greenhouses supplying all of Greece with off-season vegetables.

⏱ 45 min
2

Twelve kilometers offshore from Ierapetra, the island of Chrissi—'the golden island'—is a protected, uninhabited nature reserve, except for a few seasonal caretakers. It's famous for its forest of two-hundred-year-old Lebanon cedars, the only one in Europe, and for its beaches of white sand and intensely turquoise waters. Facing an influx of 200,000 visitors per year, the Greek government imposed access restrictions in summer starting in 2022—prefer May-June or September-October to find it nearly empty.

⏱ 60 min
3

Wander down into the alleyways of Kato Mera, Ierapetra's old town: a medieval quarter of narrow lanes and dead-ends leading to the fishing harbor. You'll find the Ottoman mosque, a former Christian church converted under Turkish rule, and 'Napoleon's House'—an 18th-century dwelling where, according to local legend passed down since 1798, Bonaparte spent one night during his Egyptian expedition. Undocumented, but locals will tell you the story with infectious pride.

⏱ 45 min
4

The Archaeological Museum of Ierapetra is small, calm, and often empty—the anti-Heraklion. You'll find collections spanning the Minoan era through the Greco-Roman period: amphorae, decorated sarcophagi, statuettes, and ceramics testifying to Hierapytna's importance in antiquity. Ideal for an hour mid-day, before the Chrissi excursion or after.

⏱ 45 min
5

If you have time late in the day, the coastal road between Ierapetra and Makrygialos offers several wild and uncrowded beaches—particularly around Xerokambos and Agia Fotia. This is also the route you'll take if heading to Sitia airport (52 kilometers, about 1 hour) or Heraklion for an international flight (minimum 1 hour 45 minutes—leave mid-morning so you're not pressed for time).

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The boat trip to Chrissi Island is the day's highlight in Ierapetra. Book the morning crossing (departure around 10 AM) to maximize your time on the island. Once ashore, follow the marked trails to the cedar forest—the scent of resin beneath the centuries-old trees, with the turquoise sea glittering between the trunks, is striking. Belegrina Beach, covered in a carpet of bleached fossil shells, is a unique natural curiosity. Bring your lunch: the island's only taverna is rudimentary.

⏱ 240 min

Walking exploration of the old town: start from Kales Fortress, then into the alleyways of Kato Mera, the Ottoman mosque, Napoleon's House, the 17th-century Venetian fountain, and the lanes leading to the waterfront. The waterfront promenade runs along a long beach of gray-gold sand to the modern neighborhoods—the perfect spot for a final Cretan swim late in the day.

⏱ 75 min

For dinner, settle into a waterfront taverna and order chtapodi krasato: octopus simmered slowly in red wine with onions and herbs until it's tender—the house specialty at most local establishments. Finish with loukoumades, fried doughnuts served hot and drizzled with honey, dusted with cinnamon. Tsikoudia (Cretan raki) usually arrives unbidden as an end-of-evening digestif.

⏱ 75 min

Return to the airport: if you're departing by flight from Sitia (small terminal with seasonal flights to Athens and some European destinations), count on 52 kilometers and about 1 hour from Ierapetra. For an international flight from Heraklion, allow minimum 1 hour 45 minutes—leave mid-morning so you're not pressed for time. The faster northern coastal road runs through Agios Nikolaos.

⏱ 60 min
