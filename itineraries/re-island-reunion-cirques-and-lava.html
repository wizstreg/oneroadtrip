Réunion Island Loop: Cirques, Volcanoes & Lagoons

📅 8 days📍 8 stops🚗 245 km⏱ 4h53

In 8 days, this clockwise loop from Saint-Denis crosses the three UNESCO World Heritage cirques — Salazie, Cilaos, and Mafate — before descending to Piton de la Fournaise, one of the planet's most active volcanoes with over twenty eruptions since 2015. Between the 400-bend road inaugurated in 1932, the Creole cases of Hell-Bourg labeled Plus Beau Village de France, and the turquoise lagoon of Saint-Gilles-les-Bains, Réunion strings together landscapes in a few hundred kilometers that would make any continent jealous.

✨ Highlights
  • Piton de la Fournaise: one of the world's most active volcanoes with over 20 eruptions since 2015, accessible on foot from Pas de Bellecombe (12 km round trip, 5h)
  • Three cirques listed as UNESCO World Heritage in 2010 under Pitons, Cirques and Remparts: Salazie, Cilaos, and Mafate (accessible only on foot or by helicopter)
  • Maïdo viewpoint at 2,190 m altitude, the island's 2nd-most-visited natural site with 400,000 visitors per year — arrive before 6am for clear views
  • Hell-Bourg labeled Plus Beau Village de France: former 19th-century spa town with Creole cases listed as Historic Monuments
📆 Best period

May, June, July, August, September, October

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 5 and 9 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Winter/Dry Season (May-November): Perfect weather conditions (18-26°C) with minimal rainfall, excellent hiking conditions, and ideal temperatures for outdoor activities. Peak tourist season with highest prices but most comfortable climate for exploring volcanic landscapes.

Summer/Wet Season (December-April): Hot, humid conditions (23-30°C) with heavy rainfall, cyclone risk, and challenging hiking conditions. Lower prices but weather limitations, lush green landscapes, and authentic tropical experience.

Shoulder Seasons (April-May, October-November): Excellent transition periods with moderate temperatures, decreasing rainfall, and good value pricing. Ideal for outdoor activities with fewer crowds and stable weather patterns.

Year-Round Diving: Consistent water temperatures (24-27°C) make diving and water activities possible throughout year. Marine life varies seasonally with whale watching optimal June through September.

🌍 What to discover in RE?

Indian Ocean paradise with volcanic landscapes, French culture, and tropical beauty. Experience Creole cuisine, adventure activities, and beautiful natural environments. The territory offers cultural diversity with natural beauty and French sophistication. Réunion provides French experiences with tropical paradise and cultural authenticity.

📋 Important notes

Clockwise loop from Saint-Denis (Roland Garros Airport). Best May–November (dry season). Check RN2 road status before D7: Piton de la Fournaise erupts regularly and may close the Route des Laves.

Saint Denis

Where weathered cannons point toward an empire that never came

Start at the Barachois — those cannons pointing toward the open sea are the city's iconic symbol. They were installed during the Napoleonic Wars to repel a British attack from the ocean. The British eventually landed at La Grande Chaloupe, to the west, in July 1810. The cannons stayed put, sunburnt and rusted by sea spray, giving the waterfront an unexpected charm. The neighboring prefecture, the former residence of the French East India Company governors, has been a listed historical monument since 1970 — it only opens to the public during Heritage Days.

⏱ 45 min
5

Jardin de l'État — Botanical collections and natural history museum in a colonial garden.

⏱ 75 min
6

Walk up Rue de Paris — it's the island's most history-laden artery. Called Grande Rue in 1725, Rue Royale in 1777, Rue Impériale in 1810, it only earned its current name in 1848. Stop at number 14: Maison Carrère, built in the early 19th century, now houses the tourist office (listed since 1998). Its permanent exhibition on the street's Creole families is worth 30 minutes. Look for the guétalis at the corners of properties — these elevated kiosks allowed ladies to watch the street without being seen.

⏱ 60 min
7

Rue de Paris ends at Jardin de l'État: 4 hectares landscaped between 1767 and 1773 for the French East India Company, planted with trees and spices brought back by botanist Pierre Poivre. At the back of the garden, step through the door of the Natural History Museum — the island's first museum, opened in 1855 in the former Legislative Palace of 1834. It holds 560,000 specimens. Don't miss the coelacanth from the Mozambique Channel: this living fossil fish, presumed extinct for 65 million years, is the collection's star piece.

The Maïdo viewpoint is a visual punch. At 2,190 m altitude, you look down over the Cirque de Mafate from more than 1,000 m above — the only cirque accessible solely on foot or by helicopter. The site draws 400,000 visitors a year and is the island's second-most-visited natural attraction. Real advice: leave before 6am from the coast. The sky clouds over between 9am and 10am almost without fail, and if you arrive too late, you'll see a wall of white clouds. At sunrise on a clear day, the view stretches to the island's east coast.

Hell Bourg

A village frozen in time where thermal springs once drew princes and governors

★★★★★

Climbing the D48 toward Salazie, park at the pull-offs to view the Voile de la Mariée waterfall from the road — the view is perfect from below, and you'll see a white sheet bursting from the cliff that breaks into multiple smaller falls. The waterfall is far more spectacular after rains. A local legend tells that Amanda, a young woman, fell into the precipice on her wedding night while trying to flee her father. Practical note: the approach trail has been closed by order since 2014, do not take it.

⏱ 30 min
5

Hell-Bourg — Restored Creole villas (Maison Folio) and village museum.

⏱ 75 min
6

Hell-Bourg owes its name to Baron de Hell, governor of Madagascar, during whose tenure goat hunters discovered hot springs in 1832. The thermal spa opened in 1852 attracted all of the island's high society: governors, foreign consuls, Malagasy princes. A cyclone swallowed the springs in 1948 and ended the golden era. The village kept its colorful Creole cases and won the Plus Beau Village de France label — the only one in France's overseas territories. Wander its lanes; every house has a plaque telling its story.

⏱ 60 min
7

Push open the wrought-iron gate of Maison Folio, rue Amiral Lacaze. This Creole home built in the 1860s, listed as a Historic Monument since 1989, is a perfectly preserved 19th-century time capsule. The guided tour (around €5, slots at 10am and 2:30pm) takes you through period-furnished rooms, the fretwork veranda, and the garden planted with geranium, vetiver, patchouli, and orchids. It's one of the few bourgeois Creole cases open to the public on the entire island.

The road to Cilaos is an experience before you even reach the village. Construction began in 1927 under the direction of engineer Mallet — inaugurated in 1932, it crosses three tunnels carved into friable rock and passes over a double-level bridge built to catch up on a construction delay. Over 35 km from Saint-Louis, you chain through more than 400 bends above the Bras de Cilaos. Stop at the viewpoints: the vistas of the UNESCO-listed ramparts and pitons are the best of the entire route. The road can be cut by rockfalls in the rainy season — check before setting out.

Cilaos comes from the Malagasy "tsilaosa" — "the place you never leave." You'll understand why upon arrival. Perched at 1,200 m altitude, ringed by Piton des Neiges (3,071 m, highest point in the Indian Ocean), Gros Morne (3,019 m) and Grand Bénare (2,898 m), it's the sunniest and driest of the three cirques. Stroll the main street to Mare à Joncs, a peaceful pond ideal for a break facing the ramparts.

⏱ 60 min

Cirque de Cilaos — Full-day hike in the Bras Rouge sector; basalt pools and waterfalls.

⏱ 75 min

Drop by the Maison de la broderie to see the "jours de Cilaos" — a lace-on-fabric technique created in 1900 by Angèle Mac Auliffe, multiple-time winner of the Meilleur Ouvrier de France award. Next door, the winemakers' cellars offer tastings of the only wine produced in Réunion, labeled Vin de Pays since 2004: red, rosé, and white grown on altitude terraces since the 1860s.

For seasoned hikers, the Piton des Neiges ascent (3,071 m) takes 2 days with a night at Caverne Dufour gîte. Depart before 4am to reach the summit at sunrise before clouds arrive. It often freezes at the summit even in austral summer — pack appropriate gear and book the gîte well in advance.

Étang-Salé beach is the island's largest black sand beach — 1.5 km lined with filaos. The sand, a mix of basalt, olivine, and coral eroded by the ocean, is deep black contrasting with turquoise water. Two zones: to the south, Bassin Pirogue is protected by a coral barrier and suitable for families; to the north, swimming is forbidden (strong currents and shark risk). Caution: black sand heats up very quickly, protect your feet or risk burns.

Saint-Pierre is the capital of the South and the island's only town with both a supervised beach and a lagoon. The Saturday morning market on the waterfront (Ravine Blanche side) finished 3rd in the TV show Votre plus beau marché on TF1 in 2018 and 2019. Also stroll the historic center: colorful Tamil temples and facades marked by the French East India Company era are worth a detour.

Cité du Volcan, on RN3 at Bourg-Murat, is the right place to understand what you'll see tomorrow. The museum was born in 1992 from an idea by volcanologist couple Maurice and Katia Krafft — the same who died filming the Pinatubo eruption in 1991. Fully renovated and reopened in August 2014, the 6,000 m² building offers a reconstructed lava tunnel, a 4D cinema, a 270° panoramic screen, and a bathyscaphe to explore underwater flows. The museum welcomes between 100,000 and 120,000 visitors per year. Allow minimum 2h.

On the road to Pas de Bellecombe, stop at the Nez de Bœuf viewpoint for the view over Piton des Neiges and the Plaine des Cafres meadows. Further on, Plaine des Sables is a totally surreal landscape of red and black ash — a vast flat, colorful expanse you drive through as if traveling on another planet. Get out for a few minutes to walk on these cold ashes.

Where molten earth met the ocean and a church survived the fire

The Pas de Bellecombe-Jacob hike is the circuit's highlight. From the parking lot at 2,311 m altitude, the trail descends into the Enclos Fouqué via a steep staircase, skirts Formica Léo — a small red cone dating from 1753 — and climbs back to the edge of Dolomieu crater: 1 km wide, over 300 m deep, a gaping chasm over a lunar landscape. Allow 12 km round trip, about 5h, 500 m elevation. Leave before 7am: fog settles mid-morning and can erase everything. SPF 50+ sunscreen, hat, and 2L water minimum — no shade in the enclos. ⚠️ Check OVPF the day before: in case of eruption or alert, the enclos is closed to the public.

⏱ 300 min
5

Grand Brûlé — Coastal lava flows and N2 road segments rebuilt over recent eruptions.

⏱ 75 min
6

Route des Laves (RN2 section between Sainte-Rose and Saint-Philippe) crosses the Grand Brûlé, the coastal zone of Enclos Fouqué. Panels line the route to date each flow. The most memorable remains April 2007 — the eruption of the century — which covered the road in places under 60 meters of lava. It took 7 months of work to reopen it. ⚠️ Check RN2 status same morning: the road can be closed during active eruptions.

Stop at Notre-Dame des Laves church in Piton-Sainte-Rose. Built in 1927, it miraculously survived the out-of-enclos eruption of April 8, 1977: the flow split into two arms that skirted the building while burning everything around it, stopping at the nave entrance. The access stairs were carved directly into the frozen lava. The neighboring former gendarmerie houses an interpretation space that details the eruption.

For this last morning, return to Rue de Paris and step through the door of Musée Léon Dierx at number 28. Opened in 1912 thanks to donations from Ambroise Vollard — an art dealer born in Saint-Denis who frequented Cézanne, Picasso, and Gauguin in Paris — it houses works by Gauguin, Caillebotte, and Maurice Denis. An exceptional collection for an Indian Ocean island; if you have 1h ahead don't miss it.
