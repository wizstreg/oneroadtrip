Ho Chi Minh

Former Saigon, metropolis of 9 million caught between colonial architecture and Asian fever

★★★★☆
📍 Notre-Dame Cathedral Saigon Ho Chi Minh📍 Central Post Office Saigon Ho Chi Minh📍 Reunification Palace Ho Chi Minh📍 Dong Khoi Street Ho Chi Minh
1

Built between 1877 and 1880 by architect Jules Bourard, Notre-Dame Cathedral is the first landmark you'll see walking up Dong Khoi Street—its twin 57.6-meter red towers dominate the Commune of Paris Square. Every material was imported from France: bricks from Marseille that have never lost their red color despite the tropical humidity, and stained glass from the Lorin workshop in Chartres. Six bronze bells weighing a total of 28.85 tons hang in the towers. The cathedral is currently under renovation—check if it's open before visiting—but the façade and the statue of Our Lady of Peace (granite from Rome, 1959) are worth seeing even from outside.

⏱ 45 min
2

Right across from the cathedral, the Central Post Office is one of the city's most beautiful colonial buildings—and one of the few where you can still mail a postcard beneath its soaring metal vaults. Designed by Gustave Eiffel and inaugurated in 1891, the wrought-iron structure evokes the great European train stations of the same era. On two large wall maps framed in wood decorating the side walls, you'll recognize the telegraph network of former Cochinchina. Push through the main doors: the place is still operational, with currency exchange counters, souvenir stands, and wooden telephone booths that still work.

⏱ 30 min
3

This is where the Vietnam War symbolically ended: on April 30, 1975, two North Vietnamese tanks crashed through the gates of this presidential palace, ending thirty years of conflict. The current building, designed by architect Ngô Viết Thụ in 1966 to replace the French Norodom Palace, has been preserved exactly as it was—reception halls, underground war room with military maps still in place, presidential lounge. Budget about an hour to explore everything. Arrive at opening around 7:30am to avoid tour groups, and don't miss the basement, which holds the site's true historical interest.

⏱ 60 min
4

The former Rue Catinat of the colonial era—the boulevard where French officers came to sip their pastis—is now called Dong Khoi ('general uprising'). Walking from the cathedral toward the river, you'll pass art deco and colonial façades, bookshops, art galleries, and silk boutiques in about ten minutes. This is where the Continental Hotel once stood, the setting for Graham Greene's novels. If you're there late afternoon, the terraces come alive and it's the best time to watch the ballet of motorbikes that defines Saigon.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The best way to taste Saigon on the first evening is to walk through the alleys between Bui Vien Street and Ben Thanh Market. Southern specialties differ from those in the North: expect plenty of banh mi (stuffed Vietnamese baguette), banh xeo (crispy rice crepes with cilantro and bean sprouts), and com tam (broken rice with grilled pork chop), Saigon's typical street dish. Local operators offer guided walking food tours that depart in the evening from District 1—a good way to avoid wandering alone. Don't eat too early: the best street stalls set up from 6pm onward.

⏱ 90 min

Late in the day, head down to Bach Dang Wharf, a few minutes' walk from the bottom of Dong Khoi Street. The Saigon River (which is not the Mekong—it's a secondary branch) is lined with a recently renovated promenade. Here you'll see the Bitexco Financial Tower with its lotus-shaped helipad on one side, and boats heading up to the delta on the other. The view gains color around 6:30pm when the neon lights start glowing—if you're looking for a typical photo of modern Saigon, this is the spot.

⏱ 30 min

Ho Chi Minh

Former Saigon, metropolis of 9 million caught between colonial architecture and Asian fever

★★★★☆
📍 War Remnants Museum Ho Chi Minh📍 Thien Hau Pagoda Cholon Ho Chi Minh📍 Binh Tay Market Ho Chi Minh📍 Jade Emperor Pagoda Ho Chi Minh📍 Cu Chi Tunnels Ho Chi Minh
1

Opened on September 4, 1975, just months after the fall of Saigon, this museum was initially called the 'Gallery of American Atrocities' before being renamed in 1995 when relations with Washington normalized. Housed in a three-story building covering 4,500 m², it preserves more than 20,000 documents, of which 1,500 are exhibited in 8 permanent thematic sections. In the courtyard, captured M41 tanks, helicopters, and fighter aircraft greet you at the entrance. Inside, prepare yourself for the Agent Orange room—the photographs are among the hardest you'll see in Vietnam, and it moves thousands of visitors each year. In 2018 the museum welcomed over one million people, 80% of them foreigners. Arrive at opening (7:30am) to visit in peace.

⏱ 90 min
2

Cholon—'big market' in Cantonese—is Saigon's Chinatown, about twenty minutes west of downtown. Thien Hau Pagoda, built in the early 19th century by the Teochew community, is dedicated to the goddess of the sea. The incense coils hanging from the ceiling create a particular atmosphere at any time of day. Two streets away, Binh Tay Market is the city's largest wholesale market: tropical fruits, spices, medicinal herbs, all sold in impressive quantities. The market's ochre-yellow façade, built in 1928, is one of Cholon's most photographed. Plan to go in the morning when activity is at its peak.

⏱ 90 min
3

This pagoda built in 1909 by the Cantonese community is Saigon's most atmospheric—far more laden with incense and devotion than the tourist temples. It's dedicated to the Jade Emperor, the supreme deity of the Taoist pantheon, and its interior accumulates dozens of painted wooden statues in dark alcoves. In the back courtyard, a pond houses giant tortoises—according to belief, releasing them into water brings good fortune. Barack Obama visited the site during his trip to Vietnam in 2016, which brought renewed interest. Go on weekdays to avoid weekends when families come to pray in large numbers.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The Cu Chi Tunnels are located about 70 km northwest of central Saigon, and getting there takes up the afternoon of this second day. This 250 km underground network was dug by hand starting in the 1940s: first against the French, it was considerably expanded by the Viet Cong for the 1968 Tet Offensive and sheltered up to 16,000 people. The tunnels are organized on three levels—3, 6, and 12 meters deep—and included hospitals, kitchens, and command rooms. The sections open to tourists have been widened to allow passage, but some passages keep their original dimensions: 60-70 cm wide by 80-90 cm high. Avoid if you're claustrophobic, and wear clothes you don't mind getting dirty.

⏱ 240 min

If you're returning from Cholon via District 3, make a detour to Tan Dinh Church on Hai Ba Trung Street: its bright pink façade is one of Saigon's most shared photos on social media. Built in 1876, it's the city's second-oldest Catholic church. The interior, however, is much more sober. The photographic effect peaks between 4pm and 6pm when the low-angle light brings out the pale pink of the façade.

⏱ 20 min

Stop 3

Can Tho

Capital of the Mekong Delta, gateway to floating markets and shaded waterways

★★★★☆
📍 My Tho Mekong Vietnam📍 Unicorn Island Mekong Vietnam📍 Ninh Kieu Wharf Can Tho📍 Can Tho Vietnam
1

My Tho, 70 km from Saigon, is the delta's first stop: this is where you board a sampan to glide through the Mekong's secondary branches. Unicorn Island (Thoi Son) is the region's most visited: its coconut groves and tropical fruit gardens, coconut candy workshops, and beekeeping farms welcome tour groups all day. If you want to avoid crowds, arrange to arrive in My Tho around 7:30-8am, before the tourist buses from Saigon that arrive mid-morning. The sampan ride through the arroyos, even brief (30-45 min), gives a first sense of the relationship delta inhabitants have maintained with the river for generations.

⏱ 120 min
2

En route to Can Tho, stop in Ben Tre, capital of the province where the Mekong subdivides into its nine branches before reaching the South China Sea. The city owes its nickname 'land of coconuts' to the plantations lining the canals for dozens of kilometers. Artisan workshops produce candies, milk, and coconut oil on-site using recipes passed down through generations—you can taste and buy directly. A rowing boat ride through the bamboo-shaded arroyos lasts about 45 minutes and completes the visit. Plan to get back on the road around 2pm to arrive in Can Tho by early evening.

⏱ 90 min
3

Can Tho is the delta's largest city, with 1.2 million inhabitants—and Ninh Kieu Wharf, at the riverside, is its lively heart. In the evening, locals stroll, street vendors set up, and restaurant boats light up along the water. It's from this pier that you'll depart tomorrow morning for the Cai Rang floating market. Take time to linger this first evening to scout the docks, dine at one of the riverside restaurants, and watch the sunset over the Can Tho River.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In Ben Tre's arroyos, boatmen paddle beneath a canopy of giant bamboo filtering the light. The silence is total, except for the sound of oars and birdsong—a complete break from Saigon's traffic. It's also the best place to see up close the stilt houses lining the canals and understand how river life actually works in the delta. Local operators organize these circuits on demand from Ben Tre's docks.

⏱ 45 min

From 6:30pm, Ninh Kieu Wharf transforms into an informal night market: street vendors set up with their carts offering southern specialties—bánh tráng trộn (puffed rice salad), hủ tiếu (noodle soup typical of the delta), and seasonal fruits like durian or mangosteen. It's a good opportunity to taste local cuisine before tomorrow's busy day. Go to bed early: wake-up for the floating market will be around 5am.

⏱ 60 min

Can Tho

Capital of the Mekong Delta, gateway to floating markets and shaded waterways

★★★★☆
📍 Cai Rang Floating Market Can Tho📍 Ben Tre Vietnam📍 Arroyos Can Tho Vietnam📍 Ninh Kieu Wharf Can Tho
1

Wake-up at 5am—no compromise possible. Cai Rang floating market, classified as national intangible cultural heritage since 2016, is the delta's largest with several hundred boats gathering from 4am on the Hau River, 30 minutes by boat from Ninh Kieu pier. It's a semi-wholesale market: vendors signal their specialty by hanging a few samples of the product atop a 3-to-5-meter bamboo pole planted on the bow—pineapples, melons, durians, rambutans. At 6:30-7am it's peak activity; by 9am there's not much left. Book your boat the day before from Ninh Kieu Wharf, and have breakfast on the water from a noodle soup boat—it's included in the experience.

⏱ 90 min
2

After the market, extend the morning on the water by going up the secondary canals irrigating the fruit gardens around Can Tho. These arroyos—a term inherited from French—connect villages that still live almost exclusively by waterway. You'll pass floating rice mills, houses whose terraces open directly onto the canal, and coconut groves leaning over the water. The vegetation changes completely with the season: dense and green in summer, more golden in the dry season. Local operators organize 1.5-to-2-hour circuits departing from Ninh Kieu Wharf.

⏱ 90 min
3

In villages accessible only by boat around Can Tho, you'll find small family workshops producing bánh tráng (dried rice cakes) and bún (rice vermicelli) using methods unchanged for generations. Rice paste is spread by hand on bamboo mats, then sun-dried on racks. Families agree to show you the process—bring a guide who speaks the local language to facilitate contact. It's one of the most authentic visits you'll make in the delta.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

If you didn't have time yesterday, return to spend the afternoon in Ben Tre to visit a coconut processing workshop. The province is Vietnam's top coconut producer and confectioneries (candies, cookies, creams) are a regional specialty known throughout the country. In workshops that local agencies include in their circuits, you'll see the complete chain: husking, grating, milk extraction, candy making. Local operators allow you to leave with a few packages as gifts.

⏱ 90 min

The Can Tho River, a branch of the Hau River crossing the city, offers spectacular sunsets from the wharf or from rooftop restaurant terraces. At 5:30-6pm, the light takes on an orange tint on boats returning from the day. It's also the right time to order a cà phê sữa đá (Vietnamese filter coffee on ice, a southern specialty) and observe river life before heading back tomorrow to Saigon.

⏱ 45 min

Ho Chi Minh

Former Saigon, metropolis of 9 million caught between colonial architecture and Asian fever

★★★★☆
📍 Ben Thanh Market Ho Chi Minh📍 Bitexco Tower Ho Chi Minh📍 Tan Son Nhat Airport Ho Chi Minh📍 Pham Ngu Lao Street Ho Chi Minh
1

The unofficial symbol of the city, Ben Thanh Market dates in its current form to 1914—the clock tower on the main façade is one of Saigon's most reproduced images. Inside, across 13,000 m², you'll find textiles, spices, medicinal herbs, handicrafts, and especially a food market with dozens of street food stalls. Beware the first aisles, clearly tourist-oriented with inflated prices: push into the center of the market to find stalls frequented by locals. Prices are obtained by bargaining—it's expected and accepted. The night market that sets up on surrounding streets from 6pm is more affordable.

⏱ 60 min
2

Before heading to the airport, spend a final hour in District 1 to explore streets that preserve the best of French colonial architecture: Ly Tu Trong, Hai Ba Trung, Ngo Duc Ke. You'll see former private mansions converted into art galleries or cafés, renovated art deco buildings, and the uninterrupted flow of scooters that defines Saigon's rhythm. The city has changed considerably since the 2000s with the appearance of towers and shopping centers, but the colonial fabric remains intact on these few streets.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Tan Son Nhat Airport is located about 8 km north of downtown—allow 30 to 45 minutes depending on traffic, which can be heavy midday. Vietnamese carriers (Vietnam Airlines, Vietjet, Bamboo Airways) operate several daily rotations to Phu Quoc (PQC) in about 1h10. Book your ticket in advance, especially in high season (December-March) when flights sell out several weeks ahead. Once you arrive in Phu Quoc, the airport is located in the center of the island—allow 15 minutes by taxi to reach the west coast beaches.

⏱ 70 min

If you still have an hour before heading to the airport, settle into one of District 1's multi-story cafés that overlook the street. Café culture is central to Saigon: locals spend hours in these spaces watching the street, phone or newspaper in hand. Order a cà phê đen đá (iced black coffee, as strong as an espresso) or a cà phê sữa đá (with sweetened condensed milk)—the two versions of Saigon's daily ritual.

⏱ 30 min

Phu Quoc

Vietnam's largest island, UNESCO reserve and white-sand beaches in the Gulf of Thailand

★★★☆☆
📍 Phu Quoc National Park Vietnam📍 Ganh Dau Fishing Village Phu Quoc📍 Dai Beach Phu Quoc Vietnam📍 Dinh Cau Temple Phu Quoc
1

More than 50% of the island's area is protected by a national park classified as a UNESCO biosphere reserve in 2010. It covers 31,422 hectares of primary forest and shelters 30 mammal species, over 200 bird species, and 50 reptile species—including about fifteen protected species. Hiking trails depart from the north of the island, notably toward Mount Chua (603 m, the highest point) or waterfalls accessible only in the rainy season. In the dry season (November-April), the forest is passable on foot without risk. Be aware that trails are poorly marked—go with a local guide for hiking.

⏱ 120 min
2

At the end of the road running along the northwest coast of the island, Ganh Dau is one of Phu Quoc's last fishing villages where daily life still unfolds as it did before the tourist boom. Sun-dried wooden boats, nets stretched on bamboo poles, stilt houses at the water's edge—the contrast with the hotel complexes in the south is total. Ganh Dau beach is renowned for its coral reefs accessible for snorkeling. In clear weather, you'll glimpse the Cambodian coast (Kampot) about thirty kilometers to the northwest.

⏱ 60 min
3

Dai Beach, on the northwest coast, is one of the island's least crowded despite its 20 km of white sand. It stretches south from Ganh Dau and the water is shallow for several dozen meters—ideal for children and inexperienced swimmers. The northern section remains virtually untouched by construction. Plan to be there late afternoon: Phu Quoc's west coast faces the sunset, and sunsets from this beach are among the island's most beautiful.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Returning to Duong Dong in the evening, don't miss the Dinh Cau rock, a natural formation at the edge of the fishing port on which a temple dedicated to Thien Hau, goddess of the sea venerated by fishermen for her protection, was built in 1937. Twenty-nine carved stone steps lead to the summit, from which you embrace the town of Duong Dong, the port, and the sun plunging into the Gulf of Thailand. It's one of the island's most photogenic spots. Arrive 30 minutes before sunset to secure a good spot.

⏱ 45 min

The best way to explore northern Phu Quoc is by scooter or motorbike, which operators rent by the day from Duong Dong. The main road goes up to Ganh Dau along the west coast with sea views at every turn. A few dirt tracks provide access to isolated beaches. Drive carefully on unpaved sections after rain—laterite can be slippery.

⏱ 120 min

Phu Quoc

Vietnam's largest island, UNESCO reserve and white-sand beaches in the Gulf of Thailand

★★★☆☆
📍 Sao Beach Phu Quoc Vietnam📍 An Thoi Archipelago Phu Quoc📍 Diving An Thoi Phu Quoc Vietnam📍 Dinh Cau Night Market Phu Quoc
1

Sao Beach, in the south of the island, is regularly designated as Vietnam's most beautiful beach. Its 7 km of fine white sand and crystalline turquoise water, backed by coconut palms and forest-covered hills, fully justify the reputation. The water is calm and shallow—perfect for family swimming. Arrive before 10am to enjoy the calm: group excursions arrive mid-morning. Several beach restaurants offer seafood grilled on site. If you have a mask and snorkel, some coral reefs accessible by swimming are located at the southern end of the beach.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Dinh Cau night market, in the heart of Duong Dong, is Phu Quoc's evening gathering place. From 6pm, about a hundred stalls set up along the canal: seafood (oysters, lobsters, squid, Ham Ninh crabs) that you choose live from aquariums and are cooked in front of you, but also souvenirs, Phu Quoc pepper, pearls, and handicrafts. Prices are reasonable if you move away from the first aisles. It's also the best place to buy Phu Quoc fish sauce (nước mắm)—one of the world's most renowned, made for 200 years using traditional methods.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The An Thoi archipelago, at the southern tip of Phu Quoc, groups 15 islets whose seabeds are among Vietnam's richest. The most renowned dive sites are around May Rut, Mong Tay, and Dam Ngang islets—colorful coral reefs, rays, turtles, schools of tropical fish. Local operators offer full-day boat excursions combining 2 or 3 diving and snorkeling stops with lunch on board. Boats depart from An Thoi port, 15 km south of Duong Dong. For scuba diving, book in advance and verify required certifications.

⏱ 240 min

After a morning of diving, return to Bai Sao for the afternoon. Late in the day, when excursions leave around 3-4pm, the beach regains its calm. The water stays warm all day (26-29°C in dry season) and the late afternoon light transforms the coconut palms into golden silhouettes. A few beach bars serve fresh coconuts and fruit juices—settle in until sunset before heading back to Duong Dong for the night market.

⏱ 60 min

Phu Quoc

Vietnam's largest island, UNESCO reserve and white-sand beaches in the Gulf of Thailand

★★★☆☆
📍 Ong Lang Beach Phu Quoc📍 Pearl Farm Phu Quoc Vietnam📍 Nuoc Mam Factory Phu Quoc Vietnam📍 Phu Quoc Airport PQC
1

Ong Lang is the west coast's quietest beach—less crowded than Long Beach in the south and more intimate than Bai Dai in the north. A series of small coves separated by rocks makes it particularly pleasant in low season. Bungalows and small boutique hotels line the beach without denaturing it. In the morning, the water is calm and the beach nearly deserted on weekdays. It's the ideal spot for a final swim in the Gulf of Thailand before the afternoon departure.

⏱ 90 min
2

Phu Quoc is nicknamed 'pearl island'—the Gulf of Thailand waters have been suitable for farming Pinctada maxima pearl oysters for decades. Pearl farms on the east coast (Ham Ninh sector) offer guided tours explaining the complete cycle of pearl culture: from nacre grafting to harvest, including sorting and pearl treatment. The process takes 18 months to 3 years depending on the desired size. At the end of the visit, you can buy directly on site—Phu Quoc cultured pearls are less known than those from Japan but offer good value.

⏱ 60 min
3

Phu Quoc fish sauce is the only one in Southeast Asia to benefit from protected geographical indication recognized by the European Union. Artisan factories in the Duong Dong quarter use mahogany barrels in which anchovy fish (cá cơm) and sea salt ferment for 12 to 15 months. At the end of fermentation, the sauce is pressed and filtered by hand. The smell in the factories is powerful—some visitors are surprised—but the visit is instructive. Local operators allow you to visit and buy directly on site.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Make the most of the final hours before departure with a seafood lunch at one of the beachfront restaurants around Ong Lang or Duong Dong. The specialty to order is steamed Ham Ninh crab with lemongrass—Ham Ninh, a fishing village on the east coast, has been renowned for its crabs for decades. Beachfront restaurants display their daily catch in tanks—you choose your fish live before it's prepared. Plan to be at the airport about 2 hours before your flight time.

⏱ 90 min

Phu Quoc International Airport (PQC) is located in the center of the island, about 10-15 minutes by taxi from most west coast hotels. It's served by several Vietnamese carriers to Ho Chi Minh City, Hanoi, and in recent years by direct international flights. In high season, return flights can be very crowded—check in online if possible to avoid long lines.
