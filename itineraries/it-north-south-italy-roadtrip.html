Trento

★★★★☆
1

Piazza Duomo forms the beating heart of Trento: the Cathedral of San Vigilio, built between the 12th and 16th centuries, faces the baroque Fountain of Neptune (1767). This is where numerous sessions of the Council of Trent (1545-1563) took place—one of the pivotal events of the Catholic Counter-Reformation.

⏱ 60 min
2

Castello del Buonconsiglio, begun in the mid-13th century, served for five centuries as the residence of Trento's prince-bishops. It comprises three distinct architectural cores: the medieval Castelvecchio, the Renaissance Magno Palazzo commissioned by Cardinal Bernardo Clesio (1485-1539), and the Baroque Giunta Albertiana. Don't miss the Torre Aquila and its Cycle of the Months—early 15th-century International Gothic frescoes attributed to a Bohemian artist, depicting peasant and aristocratic activities month by month. Legend has it that an underground passage once connected the castle to the cathedral.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Wander through the historic center's narrow lanes, admiring the painted facades characteristic of Trentino. Sample local wines—Trentino produces exceptional Pinot Grigio and Gewürztraminer—at a traditional enoteca in the center.

Verona

★★★★★
1

The Arena of Verona, built around 30 AD in local pink stone, measures 138 meters long by 109 meters wide. Italy's third-largest Roman amphitheater after the Colosseum and Capua's arena, it once held approximately 30,000 spectators. An earthquake in 1117 destroyed nearly all of the outer ring; only a four-arch fragment called the Ala survives. Since 1913, the arena has hosted an open-air opera festival every summer: Aida, Carmen, and La Traviata performed before 22,000 spectators, who hold candles that flicker across the ancient stone as darkness falls.

⏱ 75 min
2

Piazza delle Erbe occupies the site of Verona's ancient Roman forum. Markets, medieval fountains, and Renaissance palaces cluster around this rectangle surrounded by frescoed facades. The column topped by the Lion of Saint Mark recalls Venetian rule over the city from 1405 onward.

⏱ 60 min
3

Casa di Giulietta, at Via Cappello 23, is a 14th-century medieval house traditionally linked to the Cappelletti family, who may have inspired Shakespeare's Capulets. The balcony was added in 1936 by the city to match the literary imagination. Millions of visitors stick love notes on the walls each year.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Stroll the ramparts of Castelvecchio at sunset—this 14th-century fortress built by the Scaligeri family offers sweeping views. Then enjoy gelato or a spritz on Piazza Bra while watching the Arena light up against the evening sky.

Bologna

★★★★★
1

Piazza Grande in Modena, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1997 alongside the cathedral and Ghirlandina Tower, ranks among Italy's finest medieval squares. The Duomo di Modena, a Romanesque masterpiece begun in 1099 by architect Lanfranco and sculptor Wiligelmo, was consecrated by Pope Lucius in 1184. Its Porta della Pescheria on the north side features remarkable bas-reliefs depicting the twelve months and scenes from King Arthur's legend—an unusual pairing in Italian Romanesque art.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Torre Ghirlandina, the cathedral's bell tower, rises 86 meters and leans slightly like its famous cousin in Pisa. Its name derives from the stone ghirlande (garlands) adorning its Gothic summit. Climbing to the top rewards you with views across Modena's rooftops and the Po plains beyond.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Museo Enzo Ferrari, inaugurated in 2012 in the founder's birthplace, traces the history of Scuderia Ferrari from its Modenese origins. The main building, clad in ochre-yellow aluminum, houses racing cars and interactive exhibitions. For automotive enthusiasts, Ferrari's factory and museum in Maranello lie just 15 km south.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Visit the Mercato Albinelli, Modena's historic covered market, to discover traditional balsamic vinegar IGP—aged at least 12 years in progressively smaller wooden barrels. A visit to a local acetaia reveals the meticulous production process and offers tastings of different aging levels.

Bologna

★★★★★
1

Piazza Maggiore has been Bologna's gravitational center since the Middle Ages. The Basilica of San Petronio, begun in 1390 and still unfinished, was originally intended to surpass St. Peter's in Rome—the Pope opposed this, diverting funds to a nearby college. Its facade, half-clad in marble and half in bare brick, testifies to this forced halt to construction.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Due Torri—the Torre degli Asinelli (97 meters, built around 1109) and the Torre Garisenda (48 meters, visibly leaning since the 12th century)—are survivors of the more than 100 medieval towers that once defined Bologna's skyline. These towers symbolized the power of noble Bolognese families. Dante mentions the Torre Garisenda in the Divine Comedy. The Torre degli Asinelli, open to visitors, offers 498 steps to a panorama over the city's red rooftops.

⏱ 75 min
3

Bologna's porticoes, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in July 2021, extend over 62 km throughout the city. Their origin dates to 1288, when a municipal law required all new construction to include an arcade to accommodate the influx of students to the world's oldest university, founded in 1088. The portico leading to the Sanctuary of San Luca, stretching 3.8 km with 664 arches, is the longest covered portico in the world.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Explore the Quadrilatero, the old quarter of medieval guilds, where stalls of charcuterie, cheeses, and fresh pasta line the arcades. Bologna, nicknamed La Grassa (the Fat One), is the birthplace of mortadella, tagliatelle al ragù, and tortellini.

Firenze

★★★★★
1

Stop at Pisa's Piazza dei Miracoli to see the Leaning Tower, Baptistery, and Cathedral. The Tower of Pisa, begun in 1173, started tilting during construction of the third floor due to soft clay soil on one side. Its construction spanned two centuries, interrupted by wars and pauses. Consolidation work between 1990 and 2001 reduced the lean by 44 cm, returning it to the safe angle of the early 19th century. Galileo supposedly conducted his experiments on falling bodies from its summit—a tale likely apocryphal but firmly rooted in scientific legend.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Lunch at a trattoria in Pisa's historic center, away from the immediate tourist zone, before continuing on to Florence (1 hour).

Firenze

★★★★★
1

The Cathedral of Santa Maria del Fiore measures 153 meters long. Its dome, designed by Filippo Brunelleschi and built between 1420 and 1436, remains to this day the largest ever constructed in masonry: 45.5 meters in interior diameter, without a single wooden framework. Brunelleschi, trained as a goldsmith, invented unprecedented hoisting machines to lift materials. The interior of the dome is covered with Last Judgment frescoes begun by Vasari in 1572 and completed by Zuccari in 1579. Climbing the 463 steps to the summit provides 360° views across Florence.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Baptistery of San Giovanni, facing the Duomo, dates from the 11th century and ranks among Florence's oldest buildings. Its Gates of Paradise, created by Lorenzo Ghiberti between 1425 and 1452 in ten panels of gilded bronze depicting scenes from the Old Testament, were reportedly called the "gates of Paradise" by Michelangelo himself.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Ponte Vecchio, rebuilt in 1345 after an Arno flood, is the only bridge in Florence to survive World War II destruction—the retreating Germans in 1944 blew up all the others. Its shops have housed goldsmiths since 1593, when the Medici expelled the butchers whose smells offended their passage through the Corridoio Vasariano running above.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Stroll through the Oltrarno, the artisan quarter on the left bank of the Arno. Its lanes shelter furniture restoration workshops, leatherwork botteghe, and a neighborhood life less touristy than the center. Coffee on Piazza Santo Spirito in late afternoon.

Firenze

★★★★★
1

The Uffizi Gallery, commissioned by Cosimo I de' Medici in 1560 and designed by Vasari as administrative offices, opened to the public in 1769. Across 12,000 square meters and some 1,800 paintings, it concentrates the world's finest collection of Italian Renaissance art. The unmissables: Botticelli's Birth of Venus and Primavera, Leonardo da Vinci's Annunciation, Michelangelo's Doni Tondo (his only painting visible in Florence), and Titian's Venus of Urbino. In 1743, the last Medici heiress, Anna Maria Luisa, bequeathed the entire collection to Florence on condition that it never leave the city.

⏱ 150 min
2

The Accademia Gallery houses Michelangelo's David, carved between 1501 and 1504 from a 5.17-meter block of Carrara marble that two sculptors had previously abandoned. Michelangelo depicted David in the moment of concentration before battle, not in victory. The work stood in Piazza della Signoria until 1873, when it was moved indoors to protect it from weathering.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Sunset from Piazzale Michelangelo, the panoramic terrace south of the Arno, with Florence spread below and the Duomo as the focal point. Arrive 30 minutes before sunset to secure a spot on the parapet.

Siena

★★★★★
1

The Piazza del Campo, shell-shaped and divided into nine radiating sections—a symbolic tribute to the Council of Nine who governed Siena from 1287 to 1355—is one of Europe's most beautiful medieval squares. The Palazzo Pubblico (14th century), on the flat side, houses the Museo Civico with Ambrogio Lorenzetti's frescoes on Good and Bad Government (1338-1340), a political cycle unparalleled in Western medieval art. Twice a year, on July 2 and August 16, the square is covered with earth and ten horses representing the city's contrade compete in the Palio, a bareback race lasting about 90 seconds.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Torre del Mangia, built between 1325 and 1344, reaches 102 meters—exactly the same height as Siena's Duomo, asserting equality between civic and religious power. Its name comes from the nickname of its first bell-ringer, Giovanni di Duccio, called Mangiaguadagni ("earnings-eater"). At the tower's foot, the Cappella di Piazza was erected in 1352 by Sienese survivors of the Black Death as a sign of gratitude.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Duomo di Siena, built between 1215 and the 14th century in alternating white and green marble, is one of the finest examples of Italian Gothic. Its floor is unique in Italy: 56 panels of carved marble created by some forty artists over three centuries. The Piccolomini Library (1492), in the left nave, is decorated with frescoes by Pinturicchio depicting the life of Pope Pius II.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

San Quirico d'Orcia, a fortified village in the Val d'Orcia (UNESCO World Heritage since 2004), was a major stop on the Via Francigena, the medieval route linking Canterbury to Rome. Orvieto

★★★★☆
1

The Duomo of Orvieto, whose first stone was laid in 1290 by Pope Nicholas IV, is one of Italy's most accomplished Gothic cathedrals. The reason for its construction: in 1263, a Bohemian priest doubting transubstantiation saw blood seep from a consecrated host at Bolsena. The stained corporal was brought to Orvieto, and the Pope ordered a cathedral built to house it. The facade, completed according to Lorenzo Maitani's plans from 1308, is a Gothic triptych covered in golden mosaics depicting the life of the Virgin. The bas-reliefs on the lower section rank among the finest from the Middle Ages.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Chapel of San Brizio (or Cappella Nova) is the interior masterpiece of the Duomo. Fra Angelico began decorating it in 1447—vaults only—before being recalled to Rome. Luca Signorelli took over in 1499 and covered walls and vaults with a cycle of frescoes on the Apocalypse and Last Judgment in two years. The preaching of the Antichrist, the Resurrection of the Flesh, and the damned cast into hell constitute an ensemble of expressive violence unprecedented in Italian art—and which directly inspired Michelangelo for the Sistine Chapel.

⏱ 45 min
3

St. Patrick's Well (Pozzo di San Patrizio) was commissioned by Pope Clement VII after the Sack of Rome in 1527, to ensure Orvieto's water supply in case of prolonged siege. Built by Antonio da Sangallo the Younger between 1527 and 1537, this 62-meter-deep well is an engineering masterpiece: two independent helical ramps of 248 steps each allowed laden mules to descend by one and ascend by the other without ever crossing paths. Seventy windows illuminate the descent.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Explore the underground city: Etruscan galleries and wells carved into the volcanic tufa run beneath Orvieto's streets. Guided tours (approx. 1 hour) descend into this labyrinth of cellars where pigeons were once raised or harvests stored.

Roma

1

The Colosseum, whose construction began in 72 AD under Emperor Vespasian and was inaugurated in 80 under Titus, is the largest amphitheater ever built: 189 meters long, 156 meters wide, 48 meters high, with 80 arches, 80 entrances, and a capacity of approximately 50,000 spectators. Its underground—a network of galleries where gladiators and animals waited—was connected to the arena by trapdoors and hoisting systems. Gladiatorial combats, animal hunts, and public executions took place here for over four centuries.

The Forum was the civic, commercial, and religious heart of ancient Rome: temples, basilicas, triumphal arches, and the Sacred Way (Via Sacra) layer centuries of history upon each other. The Pantheon, built under Emperor Hadrian between 118 and 125 AD on the site of Agrippa's temple (27 BC), is the best-preserved Roman building in the world. Its unreinforced concrete dome measures 43.30 meters in diameter—exactly equal to its interior height. The central oculus of 8.7 meters is the sole source of light. On April 21, the mythical date of Rome's founding, the noon ray illuminates precisely the entrance portal. The painter Raphael is buried here, according to his last wishes, since 1520.

⏱ 45 min

Napoli

★★★★★
1

The excavations of Pompeii constitute the world's most striking archaeological site. In October 79 AD (not August, as long believed—recent excavations indicate the date of October 24-25), Vesuvius buried the city under several meters of ash and lapilli in less than 24 hours. Rediscovered by chance in 1748, the city covers 66 hectares, of which only three-fifths have been excavated. It reveals 1,435 buildings including 515 houses: baths, forum, amphitheater, lupanar, bakeries with millstones still in place, thermopolium (street restaurant) counters. The plaster casts of victims' bodies, made from 1861 onward by Giuseppe Fiorelli by injecting plaster into cavities left by decomposed bodies, remain the site's most haunting images.

⏱ 180 min
2

Mount Vesuvius, the only volcano on continental Europe to have erupted in the last hundred years (last eruption: 1944), reaches 1,281 meters. In 79 AD, the eruptive plume reached 30 km in height. The volcano has been a national park since 1995. The climb to the crater (approx. 200 m from the summit parking area) offers views into the caldera and across the Bay of Naples.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Spaccanapoli (the street that divides the city in two in a straight line) concentrates historic pizzerias, Baroque churches, and markets. Naples is the city where pizza was invented—the Margherita was created here in 1889 in honor of Queen Margherita of Savoy.

Amalfi

★★★★☆
1

The Cathedral of Sant'Andrea in Amalfi, built from 987 onward by Duke Mansone I, is the emblem of the ancient Maritime Republic of Amalfi—first of the four Italian Maritime Republics, which in the 10th-11th centuries controlled part of Mediterranean trade and counted approximately 70,000 inhabitants. It's accessed by a 57-step staircase from Piazza Duomo. The Arab-Norman facade blends golden mosaics, horseshoe arches, bicolor stripes, and ceramics. The bronze doors were cast in Constantinople in 1057—the first of their kind in Italy. The Cloister of Paradise (1266-1268), with its interlaced Moorish-influenced arches on twin columns, served as a cemetery for the Amalfitan nobility.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Arsenal of the Republic (Arsenale della Repubblica), a 12th-century shipyard preserved in its original state, houses the Museum of the Compass and Maritime Duchy. Amalfi's sailors were among the first in the West to use the compass for navigation. Around 1070, Amalfitan merchants established in Jerusalem founded the Hospital of Jerusalem—origin of the Knights Hospitaller of St. John.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Boat excursion from Amalfi's port to the Grotta dello Smeraldo (Emerald Grotto), a karst cavity discovered in 1932 whose waters take on an intense emerald color thanks to light filtered through an underwater opening at 4 meters depth. Swimming in a cove from the boat.

Ravello perches at 365 meters above the Tyrrhenian Sea, on a rocky promontory between Amalfi and Minori. Villa Rufolo, built in the 13th century by the Rufolo family—one of the most powerful in medieval Amalfi's Republic—blends Arab, Norman, and Gothic influences in a unique hybrid architecture. Its public gardens extend along the cliff edge. The Terrace of Infinity, a balcony lined with marble busts at 365 meters elevation, offers the Amalfi Coast's most famous panorama: the sea and sky merge on the horizon. Virginia Woolf, Greta Garbo, Winston Churchill, and Gore Vidal stayed here.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

After Ravello, descend to Maiori for an afternoon of relaxation on the Amalfi Coast's largest beach. Protected from winds in a natural cove, Maiori's sandy beach contrasts with the pebbles of the rest of the coast. Evening stroll along the waterfront promenade.
