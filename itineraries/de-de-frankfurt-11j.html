Frankfurt

Where banking towers rise above fifteen riverside museums

★★★★★
1

The Römerberg has been Frankfurt's central square since Roman times in the 1st century. On the western side stands the Römer—city hall since 1405, purchased from the family of the same name—displaying its iconic triple-gabled Gothic facade. Inside, the Kaisersaal on the first floor features portraits of 52 emperors, commemorating the coronation banquets held here for Holy Roman Emperors following their elections at the neighboring Kaiserdom. On the eastern side, the Ostzeile—a row of six half-timbered houses reconstructed between 1981 and 1984 based on 15th-16th century plans—frames the square. At the center, the Fountain of Justice has stood since 1543. In May 1933, the Nazis burned books on this very square.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Kaiserdom Sankt Bartholomäus rises on the foundations of a 7th-century Merovingian chapel—the fifth religious building on this site. Construction of the current Gothic structure in red sandstone began in 1315 and continued until the 15th century. Technically not a cathedral (Frankfurt was never an episcopal seat), the Golden Bull of 1356 designated it as the election church for Holy Roman kings, and from 1562 onward, ten emperors were crowned here. The tower reaches 95 meters and can be climbed via 328 steps. The building houses the Gloriosa, Germany's second-largest bell at 11,950 kg. Devastated by fire in 1867 and bombs in 1944, it was rebuilt twice.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Museumsufer runs along the Main River's southern bank for about 3 km. This cultural boulevard, developed starting in 1878, brings together fifteen institutions including the Städel, the Liebieghaus (sculpture), the Museum of World Cultures, and the German Film Museum. The museums are connected by Schaumainkai, a tree-lined promenade bordered by gardens. The Eiserner Steg footbridge, inaugurated in 1869 and rebuilt in 1926, links both riverbanks and offers one of the best viewpoints of the skyline.

⏱ 60 min
4

The Städel Museum is Germany's oldest civic museum foundation. Banker and spice merchant Johann Friedrich Städel established it in 1815 by bequeathing his own collection. The building on Schaumainkai dates from 1878. The collection spans 700 years of European art history: approximately 3,100 paintings, 660 sculptures, and over 100,000 drawings and prints. Works by Botticelli, Dürer, Cranach, Rembrandt, Vermeer, Monet, and Picasso fill the galleries. An underground extension inaugurated in 2012, adding 3,000 m², houses contemporary art.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Stroll along the Main River and take in the Frankfurt skyline from the Eiserner Steg bridge or the northern riverbank. The contrast between the Kaiserdom's spire and the glass towers of Deutsche Bank or Commerzbank—which earned the city the nickname "Mainhattan"—is particularly striking at day's end.

⏱ 45 min

The Frankfurt Book Fair (Frankfurter Buchmesse) takes place every October at the Messe Frankfurt exhibition center. Born in the Middle Ages on the Römerberg and formalized in the 15th century, it's now the world's largest book fair, with over 4,000 exhibitors. Public access is available during the final two days.

⏱ 180 min

The Museumsuferfest is Germany's largest outdoor museum festival, held annually in late August along the Main. For three days, the Museumsufer museums open their doors at reduced rates while live music stages pop up on both riverbanks.

⏱ 180 min

The Dippemess is Frankfurt's traditional funfair, held twice yearly (spring and autumn). It brings together rides, shooting galleries, and local food specialties.

⏱ 120 min

Wäldchestag is a festival unique to Frankfurt, held on Whit Tuesday in the Stadtwald, the large urban forest southwest of the city. Since the 19th century, locals have gathered there to picnic and listen to music—one of the few remaining local public holidays in Germany.

⏱ 180 min

Bad Homburg Vor Der Hohe

Thermal spa town in the Taunus crowned by a UNESCO-listed Roman fort

★★★★☆
1

The Grosser Feldberg peaks at 879 meters, the highest point in the Taunus and the state of Hesse. On clear days, the summit view extends to the Alps and Cologne Cathedral. The ascent through beech and spruce forests of the Naturpark Hochtaunus offers a dramatic shift from the Main River plain. Several marked trails lead to the summit from surrounding villages.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Naturpark Hochtaunus covers 240,000 hectares of forests, valleys, and ridges between the Main and Lahn rivers. It's one of Hesse's largest nature parks. The trail network exceeds 3,000 km. The vegetation is typically Rhenish: oak, beech, and hornbeam groves on the slopes, with some glacial relict species on the ridges.

⏱ 90 min
3

The historic center of Bad Homburg vor der Höhe retains its 17th-century Schloss—one of the few remaining residences of the Landgraves of Hesse-Homburg still standing—with its medieval white tower that has become the town's emblem. The Kurpark, landscaped in English style with century-old avenues, ranks among Germany's finest spa parks. In the 19th century, Bad Homburg was a fashionable resort for European nobility: Tsar Nicholas II, King Edward VII, and Emperor Wilhelm II were regular visitors. The first Monte Carlo casino is said to have been founded by businessmen who learned the trade here.

⏱ 60 min
4

The Roman fort of Saalburg, in the Taunus a few kilometers from Bad Homburg, is the only fully reconstructed fort along the Germanic Limes. Built in the late 1st century to house a cohort of 500 soldiers, it was abandoned during the Alamannic raids of 258 AD. Serious excavations began in 1853. In 1897, Wilhelm II—passionate about Roman history—ordered its complete reconstruction on the original foundations, an unprecedented project for a military installation. The site has been part of the UNESCO Upper Germanic-Raetian Limes inscription since 2005. The archaeological museum displays weapons, tools, and everyday objects found on site.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Spa session at the Taunus Therme in Bad Homburg, a complex featuring over 1,400 m² of pools and eight themed saunas. Bad Homburg's springs have been used therapeutically since the 17th century.

⏱ 120 min

Visit the Hessenpark open-air museum in Neu-Anspach, about ten kilometers from Bad Homburg. Over 100 historic buildings—half-timbered houses, barns, mills, workshops—have been dismantled and reassembled across 70 hectares. The site offers a tangible picture of Hessian rural architecture from the 16th to 19th centuries.

⏱ 150 min

Hike the Limes trail from Saalburg: portions of the fortifications (reconstructed ditch and palisade) remain visible in the Taunus landscape. A 2.4 km circular trail around the fort runs along preserved sections and marks the bases of former watchtowers.

⏱ 90 min

Wiesbaden

Where Roman springs meet Rheingau vineyards on imperial promenades

★★★★☆
1

The Kurhaus of Wiesbaden is the city's emblem. Inaugurated in 1907 under the impetus of Wilhelm II—who made Wiesbaden his spring residence and actively contributed to its development—this neoclassical building with its colonnade houses a casino (one of Germany's oldest, said to have inspired Dostoevsky's novel The Gambler), twelve salons, and a grand concert hall. It opens onto the Kurpark, landscaped in the 19th century in English style with a lake and fountains.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Hessisches Staatstheater of Wiesbaden, inaugurated in 1894 in neo-Baroque style, is considered one of Germany's most beautiful theaters. It hosts opera, ballet, and drama. The Maifestspiele festival—founded in 1896 for Wilhelm II's visits—attracts internationally renowned opera productions each year.

⏱ 45 min
3

Ascend the Neroberg summit (245 m) via the Nerobergbahn, a water-powered funicular inaugurated in 1888—one of the oldest still operating in Germany. At the summit, the Russian Orthodox Church of Saint Elizabeth hides among the trees with its five golden domes. It was built between 1897 and 1899 by Duke Adolf of Nassau in memory of his wife Elisabeth Romanova, daughter of Grand Duke Michael of Russia, who died in childbirth at age 19 on January 27, 1845. The princess's coffin still rests there. The view from the Monopteros—a temple-shaped belvedere—extends across the entire city and Rhine valley.

⏱ 90 min
4

The Museum Wiesbaden, founded in 1819, brings together modern and contemporary art collections under one roof—including one of the world's largest collections of works by Alexej von Jawlensky, the Expressionist painter who lived in Wiesbaden—along with natural history collections featuring pieces from the Duchy of Nassau.

⏱ 75 min
5

The Rheingau vineyards stretch across approximately 3,100 hectares between the Taunus and the Rhine's northern bank, around Rüdesheim am Rhein (35 km west of Wiesbaden). Riesling has been cultivated here since the Middle Ages—the first Johannisberg vineyard dates to 817. The south-facing slopes overlooking the Rhine and the thermal shadow of the Taunus create an exceptional microclimate. In Rüdesheim, the Niederwald cable car leads to the Germania statue, erected in 1883 to commemorate the founding of the German Empire.

⏱ 90 min
6

The Upper Middle Rhine Valley, between Bingen (opposite Rüdesheim) and Koblenz, has been a World Heritage Site since 2002 for its cultural landscapes of vineyards, castles, and villages. Along 65 km, the river corridor is dotted with rocky outcrops, medieval ruins, and terraced slopes—a classic route that can be explored on foot, by bicycle, or by boat.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Wine tasting of Rheingau wines at one of the wine bars in Wiesbaden's center—the city itself lies within the appellation. Riesling represents 80% of production; Spätburgunder (Pinot Noir) occupies most of the remaining parcels.

⏱ 90 min

The Rheingauer Weinwoche (Rheingau Wine Week) takes place every year in early August on Wiesbaden's Schlossplatz. Over 100 winemakers present their wines on the castle forecourt. It's one of Hesse's oldest and most popular wine festivals.

⏱ 120 min

Darmstadt

An Art Nouveau masterpiece crowned by a UNESCO hilltop colony

★★★★★
1

The Mathildenhöhe is Germany's most remarkable Art Nouveau site, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in July 2021. On this hill overlooking Darmstadt, Grand Duke Ernst Ludwig founded an artists' colony in 1899, bringing together architects, painters, and sculptors—including Joseph Maria Olbrich and Peter Behrens—under the motto: "Let my Hesse flourish, and with it art." Four international exhibitions (1901, 1904, 1908, 1914) produced an ensemble of 23 architectural elements: artists' houses, studios, the Swan Pavilion, a lily pond, and a grove of plane trees. These buildings became a laboratory of emerging modernism, inspired by both the British Arts & Crafts movement and the Vienna Secession.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Hochzeitsturm (Wedding Tower), standing 48.5 meters tall, is the iconic building of the Mathildenhöhe. Olbrich designed it for the third exhibition in 1908 as a gift from the city to Grand Duke Ernst Ludwig for his remarriage to Princess Eleonore. Locals call it the "Five Finger Tower" because of its five ascending arches. Since 1993, an annex of the registry office has been housed here—weddings celebrated at the top grant access to the panoramic platform.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Russian Chapel of Saint Mary Magdalene is integrated into the UNESCO site but predates the artists' colony. It was built between 1897 and 1899 by architect Louis Benois from Saint Petersburg, commissioned by Tsar Nicholas II—who had married Grand Duke Ernst Ludwig's sister Alix of Hesse (who became Empress Alexandra) in 1894. Its five golden domes stand out against the park's greenery and served as a place of worship during visits by the Russian imperial family.

⏱ 30 min
4

The Hessisches Landesmuseum Darmstadt is one of Germany's most comprehensive encyclopedic museums. It brings together art (including the world's largest collection of works by Joseph Beuys), archaeology, geology, and natural history. One gallery is devoted to fossils from the Messel Pit.

⏱ 90 min
5

The Messel Pit (Grube Messel), 9 km northeast of Darmstadt, is Germany's first natural site inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site (1995). This former oil shale quarry preserves the remains of over 40,000 specimens from a 47-million-year-old ecosystem—the Eocene epoch, when the first modern mammals were developing in a tropical forest. Discoveries include primitive horses the size of a dog (Eurohippus messelensis), primates, crocodiles, snakes, and insects with colors still visible. Access to the pit itself is by guided tour only. The finest specimens are displayed at the Senckenberg in Frankfurt and the Landesmuseum in Darmstadt. Fun fact: the pit nearly became a municipal landfill in the 1970s—strong citizen and scientific mobilization prevented it.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Guided tour into the Messel Pit: the descent into the quarry allows you to observe the layers of oil shale in which fossils are still extracted. Working preparation replicas are visible. The tour lasts approximately two hours.

⏱ 120 min

Explore Darmstadt's scientific quarter, nicknamed "Science City": the Technical University, GSI (heavy ion research center), ESA/ESOC (European Space Operations Centre), and EUMETSAT are all based here. The facade of the ESOC control center—from which European Space Agency satellites are operated—is visible from the street.

⏱ 60 min

Lorsch

Where a 9th-century Carolingian gateway guards Europe's oldest library ruins

★★★★☆
1

Lorsch Abbey was founded in 764 during the reign of Pepin the Short, Charlemagne's father. It became one of the most important cultural centers of the early Middle Ages, housing one of Europe's largest libraries and a renowned scriptorium. Upon the death of Louis II the German in 876, the abbey became the necropolis of the Carolingian kings of East Francia. Of this once immense complex, only the Königshalle (Torhalle) remains standing: a monumental 17-meter portico built around the mid-9th century with a polychrome facade alternating light and dark bands over arcades inspired by antiquity. Inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1991, it's one of the rare Carolingian buildings to have retained its original appearance. Its exact function remains debated—library, reception hall, or ceremonial gateway? The Lorsch Pharmacopoeia, written around 800 in the abbey, has been inscribed in UNESCO's Memory of the World Register since 2013 as the first herbal medicine treatise.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Walk through the Lorsch Abbey site: the footprints of disappeared buildings (basilica, cloister, monastic buildings) have been materialized in the lawns during the 2017 renovation by Topotek1. The medicinal herb garden behind the tithe barn is planted according to species mentioned in the Lorsch Pharmacopoeia.

Schotten

Portal to Europe's largest volcanic massif and Germany's oldest nature park

★★☆☆☆
1

Schotten is the gateway to the Naturpark Vulkanregion Vogelsberg, Germany's oldest nature park. The Vogelsberg is Europe's largest contiguous volcanic massif: it formed approximately 20 million years ago during a period of intense Tertiary volcanic activity. Basaltic lavas created a topography of rounded domes, peat bogs, and springs. The Vulkaneum in Schotten, a museum-interpretation center renovated in 2018, presents the geology and ecology of the massif with interactive stations.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Vogelsberg landscapes are characterized by their mixed beech and spruce forests, flowering meadows, and dry-stone hedges. The central plateau—the Oberwald—is covered with forests traversed by springs and small streams. The slopes are dotted with basaltic formations with distinctive silhouettes. The region forms a water recharge zone for much of Hesse.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Hoherodskopf (764 m) is the most iconic accessible summit in the Vogelsberg alongside the Taufstein (773 m). The Naturpark information center is located here, with a permanent exhibition on volcanic geology and the massif's biodiversity. In winter, cross-country ski trails are groomed on the plateau. In summer, a 7 km trail loops around the summit, passing through peat bog, streams, and several viewpoints.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Hike a section of the Vulkanring Vogelsberg, a circular long-distance trail that crosses the massif along its most remarkable geological formations: basalt flows, volcanic columns, and "Bilstein" cliffs. Nature guides offer guided excursions from Hoherodskopf.

⏱ 180 min

Cycling or e-biking in the Vogelsberg on the marked trail network. Elevation changes remain moderate on the plateau. Bicycles can be rented in several park villages.

⏱ 150 min

Nationalpark Kellerwald Edersee

Where UNESCO primeval beech forests tower above a 27-kilometer turquoise reservoir

★★★★☆
1

Kellerwald-Edersee National Park, created in 2004, covers 5,724 hectares in northern Hesse—the state's only national park. Part of its beech forests has been integrated since 2011 into the UNESCO site "Primeval Beech Forests of the Carpathians and Other Regions of Europe." These beech groves are among the best-preserved in Central Europe: some trees exceed 200 years of age and 30 meters in height. The forest is left to natural evolution without human intervention, creating "wilderness of tomorrow." Wildcats (which reappeared in 2007 after 60 years of absence), black storks, and 15 species of bats inhabit the forest.

⏱ 75 min
2

The park center in Vöhl-Herzhausen offers exhibitions on beech forest ecology and the Eder Dam, which closes off the park's eastern boundary. The dam, 400 meters long, was built in 1914 and holds back the Edersee—Germany's second-largest reservoir at 27 km long. During World War II, it was targeted in May 1943 by Operation Chastise, one of the first uses of the "bouncing bomb" by the RAF. The breach caused catastrophic flooding downstream.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Edersee's shores offer 50 km of waterfront to explore. The turquoise waters of the lake—fed by Kellerwald streams—are among the clearest in inland Germany. At low water levels, remnants of the village of Asel, submerged when the reservoir was filled in 1914, sometimes reemerge from the former shoreline.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hike through the UNESCO-listed beech forests on the Urwaldsteig Edersee, a 68 km trail that loops around the lake in 4 to 5 stages. The sections on the northern side, in the heart of the national park, are the wildest. The trail is perfectly marked with "U-E" in white on a blue background.

⏱ 210 min

Boat excursion or water activities on the Edersee: sailing, windsurfing, kayaking, and swimming at developed beaches. Pleasure boats tour the lake from the Waldeck pier.

⏱ 120 min

Kassel

City of the Brothers Grimm, documenta art, and Europe's grandest Baroque hillside park

★★★★★
1

Bergpark Wilhelmshöhe is Europe's largest hillside park (240 hectares) and was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2013. Art historian Georg Dehio described it as "perhaps the most grandiose combination the Baroque ever dared between architecture and landscape." Construction of the park began in 1689 at the initiative of Landgrave Carl of Hesse-Kassel and continued for 150 years. Reservoirs and a hydro-pneumatic system feed a 350-meter Baroque cascade, a sound-effect grotto, rapids, and the Great Fountain—a 50-meter geyser, the world's tallest when created in 1767. The water features operate every Wednesday, Sunday, and public holiday from May to October.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Hercules monument dominates the Bergpark from an elevation of 596 meters. The hammered copper statue, standing 8.30 meters tall, reproduces the 3rd-century Farnese Hercules. It's visible from across the city and serves as Kassel's symbol. Below the statue, a pyramidal octagon houses the head of the cascade. The entire ensemble—statue, octagon, and grotto—measures 71 meters in total height.

⏱ 60 min
3

Kassel's documenta is the world's most important contemporary art exhibition. Held since 1955 every five years for 100 days, it takes place across multiple city venues—including the Fridericianum (1779, continental Europe's first public museum), the Documenta-Halle, and urban spaces. Permanent works from past exhibitions are visible throughout the city year-round.

⏱ 90 min
4

The Grimmwelt, opened in 2015, is the world's largest exhibition space devoted to the Brothers Grimm's work. Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm worked in Kassel in the early 19th century, where they began collecting folk tales from the region. The annotated copies of Children's and Household Tales are inscribed in UNESCO's Memory of the World Register. The museum also presents their role as linguists and their contribution to the German dictionary.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Walk through the iconic documenta sites in the city: the Fridericianum on Friedrichsplatz, the Documenta-Halle, and the in-situ works scattered throughout Kassel's public spaces. Even outside exhibition periods, permanent installations are visible.

⏱ 90 min

Bergparkleuchten evening: several times a year, the Bergpark and its monuments are illuminated by colorful light installations. The water jets and cascades are lit up at nightfall, producing a particularly impressive spectacle on the Karlsberg slopes.

⏱ 120 min

Bad Hersfeld

A thermal town sheltering the world's largest Romanesque church ruins

★★★☆☆
1

The Stiftsruine of Bad Hersfeld is the world's largest Romanesque church ruin, stretching over 100 meters in length with a transept span exceeding 50 meters. The Benedictine abbey was founded in 769 by Lullus, Archbishop of Mainz, on a site where the monk Sturmius had settled as a hermit in 736. Charlemagne made it an imperial abbey that served for centuries as a pillar of royal power on the eastern frontier of the Frankish kingdom. The church was destroyed by the French in 1761 during the Seven Years' War, when it was being used as a powder magazine. The Romanesque Saint Catherine's Tower, dating from 1120, still stands and houses the Lullusglocke—a bell dated 1038, Germany's oldest dated bell still in use. Since 1951, the ruins have hosted the Bad Hersfeld Festival, one of Germany's most important theater festivals, every summer.

⏱ 75 min
2

The historic center of Bad Hersfeld, a Hessian spa town, has preserved a fabric of 15th and 16th-century half-timbered houses. The sacristan's house, dating from 1452, is the town's oldest. The Stadtkirche (town church) and the former buildings of the free imperial city form a cohesive ensemble around the pedestrian center.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Attend the Bad Hersfeld Festival, an annual theater festival held each summer in the Stiftsruine ruins since 1951. The open-air stage, set against nine-century-old Romanesque walls, hosts large-scale productions (Shakespeare, Schiller, musicals) with up to 1,500 spectators per evening.

⏱ 150 min
