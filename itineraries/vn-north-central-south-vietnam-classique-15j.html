Hanoi

Where millennial alleys meet lotus-filled lakes

★★★★★
1

Drop your bags and head out on foot into the Old Quarter without a fixed route - that's how you stumble upon the details tour groups never see. This district took shape around the Thang Long citadel in the 15th century, when craftsmen from across the Red River delta settled here organized by trade guilds. Each street bears the name of what was sold there - Hang Bac for silver, Hang Gai for silk, Hang Ma for votive paper offerings. Arrive early morning or evening when the pedestrian streets around the lake come alive; by day the traffic is already chaotic by 8am.

⏱ 120 min
2

Hoan Kiem Lake - Lake of the Restored Sword - takes its name from a 15th-century legend: Emperor Lê Loi is said to have returned his magical sword to a divine tortoise here after driving out the Ming invaders. The lake's last giant tortoise died in 2016; its taxidermied body is displayed in Ngoc Son Temple, installed on Jade Island since its 1865 restoration. You reach it via The Huc Bridge - fifteen spans of red lacquered wood. Plan your visit for late afternoon: when the lights come on around 6pm, the reflection of the red bridge in the water is worth lingering for.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Long Bien Bridge spans 1,682 meters across the Red River. Built from 1899 to 1902 by French engineers Daydé and Pillé, it was one of the longest in the Far East. American bombers hit it repeatedly between 1967 and 1972 - the scars of successive repairs on the metal spans are still visible. Cross it on foot or by bike in late afternoon: sunset over the river's braided channels and the cultivated fields on the sandbars below is photographed every evening by dozens of Hanoians.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Local operators offer cyclo rides from around the lake - it's the most pleasant way to navigate lanes too narrow for cars. Negotiate the route before you get in and specify that you want to cross Hang Bac, Hang Ma and pass by Dong Xuan market. Count on about an hour for an initial tour; drivers usually know shortcuts that GPS doesn't show.

⏱ 60 min

For your first Hanoi meal, settle onto a low stool in an Old Quarter alley. In the evening, Ta Hien Street transforms into an open-air bar where locals drink bia hoi - fresh draft beer brewed daily, less than a euro per glass. For charcoal-grilled bun cha, look for plumes of smoke in the side alleys: they signal the best spots that no one has put on an app.

⏱ 60 min

Hanoi

Where millennial alleys meet lotus-filled lakes

★★★★★
1

Founded in 1070 by Emperor Ly Thanh Tong to honor Confucius, Van Mieu housed Vietnam's first university from 1076. The complex is organized into five courtyards aligned north-south. Don't miss the third courtyard: 82 stone stelae resting on tortoises bear the names of 1,307 laureates of the imperial examinations between 1442 and 1779. Vietnamese students still come to touch the tortoises' heads before exams - you'll see them posing in their ao dai costumes. Arrive at opening (8:30am) to avoid school groups that arrive from 9:30am onward.

⏱ 90 min
2

The mausoleum is only open mornings and silence is required inside. Right next door, don't miss the One Pillar Pagoda (Chua Mot Cot) built in 1049 under King Ly Thai Tong: it rests on a single wooden pillar sunk into a lotus pond shaped like a blooming flower. The current structure is a 1955 reconstruction, the original having been destroyed by the French upon their departure - a detail that gives this small, seemingly modest pagoda an additional dimension.

⏱ 75 min
3

Built in 1011 by King Ly Thai To on the remains of a 7th-century Chinese fortress, the Thang Long citadel served as the seat of Vietnamese power for nearly 13 centuries - inscribed on UNESCO's list in 2010. In 2002, excavations for parliament construction unearthed millions of artifacts layered across multiple dynasties. Don't miss Bunker D67, used by the North Vietnamese army until 1975, visitable as-is with its red phones and tactical maps still in place.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Thang Long Theatre is Hanoi's reference venue for this traditional art of the Red River delta whose origins trace back to the 11th century. Puppeteers operate hidden behind a curtain, feet in water, manipulating lacquered wooden figures that dance and fish on the surface - the technique demands years of training. Performances last 50 minutes. Book ahead in high season.

⏱ 60 min

Don't leave Hanoi without trying egg coffee, a specialty invented in the 1940s when a barman replaced condensed milk - a scarce wartime commodity - with egg yolks beaten with sugar. The result is a thick, creamy foam crowning a robust robusta coffee, served in a bowl of hot water. Operators in the Old Quarter serve this drink upstairs in old tube houses - the best tables overlook Hoan Kiem Lake.

⏱ 30 min

Hanoi

Where millennial alleys meet lotus-filled lakes

★★★★★
1

If you only visit one museum in Hanoi, make it this one. Opened in 1997, it presents Vietnam's 54 official ethnic groups through collections of costumes, ritual objects, and full-scale reconstructions in the outdoor garden - Ede longhouses, Bahnar rong houses, Thai stilt houses you'll recognize tomorrow in Pu Luong. Texts are trilingual (Vietnamese, French, English). Allow at least 2 hours and don't skip the outdoor garden, especially early morning.

⏱ 120 min
2

Tran Quoc Pagoda is Hanoi's oldest - founded in the 6th century on the banks of the Red River, moved to its West Lake peninsula (Ho Tay, 500 hectares) in the 17th century. Its blood-red eleven-story tower reflects in the waters of the city's largest lake. Go in late afternoon: the golden light on the tower and calm lake, before evening traffic resumes on the causeway, is one of the capital's most beautiful views.

⏱ 60 min
3

Friday evenings and weekends, the streets around Hang Dao close to traffic and fill with stalls: clothing, crafts, street food, street performances. On weekdays, Phung Hung Street north of the Old Quarter hosts permanent mural galleries - a good backup plan. Either way, end the evening with a hot bowl of bun bo Hue seated on a plastic stool and watch the quarter settle down for the night.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Operators rent bikes in the Old Quarter - allow an hour round-trip to Long Bien Bridge. On the bridge, between trains and two-wheelers, you'll have views of the Red River's braided channels, the cultivated vegetable islands below, and mountains in the distance on clear days. It's one of Hanoi's most memorable free activities.

⏱ 45 min

Vietnamese banh mi was born from the encounter of the French baguette with local condiments: pork pâté, pickled carrot, cilantro and soy sauce. The best spots in Old Quarter alleys make sandwiches to order on a small grill. Pair it with a cold Hanoi Beer - the local brewery founded in 1890, still produced in the city.

Pu Luong

Where water wheels spin beneath terraced rice fields

★★☆☆☆
1

In Thai language, Pu Luong means the highest mountain. The nature reserve created in 1999 covers 17,600 hectares straddling Ba Thuoc and Quan Hoa districts in Thanh Hoa province. Ban Don is the valley's main Black Thai village: its wooden and bamboo stilt houses, elevated more than 2 meters, are built to avoid floods and snakes. If you're here in September-October, the terraced rice fields turn golden at harvest time.

⏱ 90 min
2

Ban Hieu perches above a waterfall of the same name, nestled in tropical vegetation. The village overlooks an amphitheater of terraced rice fields - the most photographed in the entire reserve. From the main path, a few minutes' walk is enough to reach the clear water pools perfect for swimming. Ask your local host to point out the path: it's not always marked for visitors.

⏱ 60 min
3

Don't leave Pu Luong without spotting the water wheels - these large wooden wheels 3 to 4 meters in diameter built along streams to irrigate elevated rice fields. More than 100 exist in the region, all handcrafted and maintained by villages. They turn continuously powered by the current and make a regular creaking sound you can hear from afar in the valley. Your local host will point out the most beautiful ones.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Local operators organize bamboo raft descents on the Ma River that flows through the valley. The current is gentle; from the water, you see terraced rice fields descending to the banks, water buffalo at the fords, and women washing clothes in pools. Bring closed sandals or shoes that can get wet.

⏱ 90 min

Several Thai families offer overnight stays in stilt houses. Dinner is prepared with garden produce - mountain chicken, bamboo shoots, sticky rice cooked in bamboo. Some hosts offer to participate in meal preparation or observe traditional weaving after sunset. Bring a simple gift (fruit, cookies): it's customary in mountain villages.

⏱ 180 min

Ninh Binh

Where limestone karsts rise from flooded valleys

★★★★★
1

Before taking the boat at Tam Coc, climb Mua Cave. About 480 steps carved into rock lead to the summit of a limestone karst, where the 360-degree view over Tam Coc valley is breathtaking: the Ngo Dong River snaking between sugar loaves and green rice fields. Leave before 8am to avoid heat and crowds, wear shoes with good grip: some steps are slippery in wet weather.

⏱ 60 min
2

Phu Hau village, 5 km from Tam Coc landing, sits at the end of an hour-and-a-half bike loop between limestone karsts and rice fields. Its car-free alleys and local temple are virtually empty of tourists. Operators rent bikes in Tam Coc and Ninh Binh hotels - the terrain is flat, accessible to all levels.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Before leaving Pu Luong, take advantage of dawn for the Ban Bang loop: a trail that follows terraced rice fields, crosses a bamboo bridge over a stream, and offers views of spinning water wheels. The morning air is cool, buffalo emerge from barns - it's the best time to photograph the valley in mist before hitting the road.

⏱ 90 min

Tam Coc means three caves: the limestone tunnels Hang Ca, Hang Hai, and Hang Ba that boats pass through on the Ngo Dong River. What surprises first-time visitors: the rowers use their feet to manipulate the oars, freeing their hands for other tasks. Avoid the hours between 10am and 2pm - the busiest. Arrive at the landing between 7am and 8am for the best light conditions.

Ninh Binh

Where limestone karsts rise from flooded valleys

★★★★★
1

Inscribed on UNESCO's list in 2014, Vietnam's first mixed natural-cultural site, Trang An covers 2,168 hectares of limestone karsts and flooded valleys. Excavations have revealed traces of human occupation over 30,000 years. The rowing boat circuit crosses 47 caves and 31 enclosed valleys between nearly vertical cliffs, with royal temples from the Dinh and Tran dynasties built into cliff niches. Choose route #3, the longest (3h), for the most impressive caves.

⏱ 180 min
2

Hoa Lu was Vietnam's first unified capital, founded in 968 by King Dinh Bo Linh after reunifying the country divided into twelve kingdoms. The capital remained here until 1010. Two temples built in the 17th and 18th centuries mark the location of the old palaces. The site is much less crowded than Trang An and Tam Coc - you'll often be alone with the guardians, and this calm is part of the charm.

⏱ 60 min
3

Bai Dinh Pagoda, inaugurated in 2010 after ten years of construction, extends over more than 1,000 hectares. On the main avenue, 500 blue stone arhat statues 2 to 2.5 meters each accompany visitors to the main temples. Tam The Temple houses three bronze Buddha statues of 50 tons each representing the Buddha of the past, present, and future. Electric tuk-tuks shuttle from the entrance - use them for the climbs.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Operators rent bikes in Tam Coc - allow an hour and a half for a loop between limestone karsts, rice fields, and small villages. The terrain is flat and the road uncrowded on weekdays. Stop in villages not marked on tourist maps: residents are welcoming and local temples are deserted.

Hue

Where the last emperors built palaces along the Perfume River

★★★★☆
1

Hue was Vietnam's imperial capital from 1802 to 1945 under the Nguyen dynasty. The ensemble of monuments has been UNESCO-listed since 1993. The citadel is surrounded by a 10 km enclosure and 6-meter-high walls pierced by about ten gates. The Meridian Gate (Ngo Mon) was reserved for the emperor's exclusive use - mandarins passed through side gates, soldiers through outer gates. Allow at least 2 hours and good shoes.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Palace of Supreme Harmony (Thai Hoa), site of grand ceremonies, has its throne supported by 80 lacquered wooden pillars. Behind, the Purple Forbidden City was destroyed about 80% during fighting in the Tet Offensive of January 1968. What remains is the Royal Library from 1821 - a mosaic-covered pavilion overlooking a square lake - and the Royal Theatre. These ruins under restoration are among Vietnam's most moving.

⏱ 75 min
3

Before entering the citadel, walk the ramparts from outside - their thickness can reach 21 meters in places, built according to Vauban fortification principles. The Flag Tower (Ky Dai), erected in 1807, stands 54.5 meters tall. During the 1968 Tet Offensive, the flag of the Democratic Republic of Vietnam flew there for 24 days - an episode that alone summarizes the brutality of the Battle of Hue.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Hue cuisine was born in imperial kitchens where chefs prepared up to 50 different dishes per meal for the emperor. Banh beo (small steamed rice cakes), bun bo Hue (spicy lemongrass beef soup), and banh khoai (crispy shrimp pancakes) are its emblems. Operators offer royal cuisine dinners in garden houses (nha vuon) with Nha Nhac accompaniment - court music listed on UNESCO's intangible heritage in 2003.

⏱ 90 min

The Perfume River (song Huong) flows through Hue, separating the old town to the north from the modern city to the south. A half-hour riverside walk at sunset, from Trang Tien Bridge toward the dragon boat landing, gives a first impression of the illuminated city. Restaurants on moored boats offer evening meals with views of both banks.

Notre-Dame Basilica of Saigon was built between 1877 and 1880 by architect Jules Bourard. Its red bricks came from Marseille - no local bricks were used. Can Tho

Where hundreds of boats meet each dawn on the Bassac

★★★★☆
1

Cai Rang floating market is the Mekong Delta's largest. Hundreds of boats loaded with fruits and vegetables gather each morning on the Bassac branch 6 km south of Can Tho. Wholesalers signal what they're selling by hanging their goods on a long bamboo pole planted at the bow - the sign-pole visible from afar, avoiding misunderstandings between boats. Wake at 5am and take a sampan from Ninh Kieu wharf. Order a bowl of hu tieu from one of the kitchen-boats circulating among wholesalers.

⏱ 120 min
2

Munirangsyaram Pagoda is one of Can Tho's most beautiful Khmer Buddhist pagodas: tiered colorful roofs, naga (sacred serpent) sculptures, walls painted with scenes from Buddha's life. The Khmer Krom, a Cambodian minority settled in the delta for centuries, practice a living Theravada Buddhist tradition here.

⏱ 45 min
3

Binh Thuy house, 6 km from Can Tho center, is one of the delta's most beautiful colonial houses - built in 1870 in a style mixing French neoclassicism and Asian ornaments. It was used as a set in Jean-Jacques Annaud's film adapted from Marguerite Duras's novel. The owner family still lives in the back rooms and opens the reception salons to visitors.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Operators organize excursions in Ben Tre province: sampan ride through channels shaded by coconut palms, visit to a coconut candy factory, then lunch with elephant-ear fish (ca tai tuong). It's one of the entire circuit's most relaxing and lush days - the boat's slow pace in narrow canals is an antidote to Saigon's bustle.

⏱ 240 min

Operators rent bikes in Can Tho to explore rural roads winding through rice fields, canals, and durian and longan orchards. The terrain is flat and the road uncrowded on weekdays - a way to see the delta at human height rather than from a motorized boat.

⏱ 90 min

Ho Chi Minh

Where colonial boulevards pulse with ten million lives

★★★★☆
1

On the return road to Saigon, Vinh Long city merits an hour-and-a-half stop. Its covered market, on the Co Chien branch of the Mekong, is lively in the morning: tropical fruits, dried fish, and sticky rice preparations. An Binh island, accessible by small ferry from Vinh Long, is covered with orchards and palm-shaded canals - a final delta immersion before the highway back.

⏱ 90 min
2

District 3 of Saigon, less touristy than District 1, concentrates some of the most appreciated cafés and restaurants among residents - spots in restored colonial villas whose inner courtyards are covered with green plants. It's here that Saigon creatives and artists gather in late afternoon. An hour in District 3 will show you a very different Saigon from classic tourist circuits.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

For your penultimate night in Ho Chi Minh City, Nguyen Hue pedestrian street in the evening gathers Saigonese families. Finish at a neighborhood restaurant with a final pho or fresh spring rolls - a way to close two delta days on a simple, local note.

⏱ 120 min

Several rooftop bars in District 1 offer panoramic views of illuminated Saigon's skyline - the Bitexco Financial tower and modern skyscrapers mixed with colonial villa roofs. Prefer spots in boutique hotels to major chains: the atmosphere is more local and the view often better.

⏱ 60 min

Ho Chi Minh

Where colonial boulevards pulse with ten million lives

★★★★☆
1

Tao Dan Park is District 1's largest green space. Before 8am, it transforms into a surprisingly calm living space: tai chi and open-air dance groups, elderly men at Chinese chess under century-old ficus trees, and bird enthusiasts for singing contests - each owner hangs their cage on a hook and the birds compete for best song. It's a slice of local life that few tourists take time to see, two minutes from downtown's grand hotels.

⏱ 60 min
2

If your flight is in the afternoon, a final hour in District 1 streets lets you see colonial buildings in morning light - fewer crowds, better photos. Ly Tu Trong Street, parallel to Dong Khoi, is lined with neoclassical villas, several housing embassies. The Garden of Mists (Cong Truong Quoc Te), a small square bordered by bougainvillea, is a beautiful shortcut between the cathedral and Reunification Palace.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Dong Khoi Street and its adjacent streets concentrate Saigon's best craft shops: lacquerware, embroidered silk, coffee, tea and spices. Ben Thanh market is a two-minute walk for final vacuum-sealed spices. Tan Son Nhat international airport is 7 km from downtown - always allow more than expected during rush hours, and 2h30 before flight time for international formalities.

⏱ 90 min

Before leaving for the airport, take time for a final bowl of pho or banh mi in a quarter alley. The best spots don't have signs in English - spot where locals stop with their scooters before heading to work. It's often the meal travelers remember best, because it's the one where you know you're going home.

⏱ 30 min
