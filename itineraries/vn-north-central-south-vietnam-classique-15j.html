Hanoi

★★★★★
1

Drop your bags and head out on foot into the Old Quarter without a fixed route - that's how you stumble upon the details tour groups never see. This district took shape around the Thang Long citadel in the 15th century, when craftsmen from across the Red River delta settled here organized by trade guilds. Each street bears the name of what was sold there - Hang Bac for silver, Hang Gai for silk, Hang Ma for votive paper offerings. Arrive early morning or evening when the pedestrian streets around the lake come alive; by day the traffic is already chaotic by 8am.

⏱ 120 min
2

Hoan Kiem Lake - Lake of the Restored Sword - takes its name from a 15th-century legend: Emperor Lê Loi is said to have returned his magical sword to a divine tortoise here after driving out the Ming invaders. The lake's last giant tortoise died in 2016; its taxidermied body is displayed in Ngoc Son Temple, installed on Jade Island since its 1865 restoration. You reach it via The Huc Bridge - fifteen spans of red lacquered wood. Plan your visit for late afternoon: when the lights come on around 6pm, the reflection of the red bridge in the water is worth lingering for.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Long Bien Bridge spans 1,682 meters across the Red River. Built from 1899 to 1902 by French engineers Daydé and Pillé, it was one of the longest in the Far East. American bombers hit it repeatedly between 1967 and 1972 - the scars of successive repairs on the metal spans are still visible. Cross it on foot or by bike in late afternoon: sunset over the river's braided channels and the cultivated fields on the sandbars below is photographed every evening by dozens of Hanoians.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Local operators offer cyclo rides from around the lake - it's the most pleasant way to navigate lanes too narrow for cars. Negotiate the route before you get in and specify that you want to cross Hang Bac, Hang Ma and pass by Dong Xuan market. Count on about an hour for an initial tour; drivers usually know shortcuts that GPS doesn't show.

⏱ 60 min

For your first Hanoi meal, settle onto a low stool in an Old Quarter alley. In the evening, Ta Hien Street transforms into an open-air bar where locals drink bia hoi - fresh draft beer brewed daily, less than a euro per glass. For charcoal-grilled bun cha, look for plumes of smoke in the side alleys: they signal the best spots that no one has put on an app.

⏱ 60 min

Hanoi

★★★★★
1

Founded in 1070 by Emperor Ly Thanh Tong to honor Confucius, Van Mieu housed Vietnam's first university from 1076. The complex is organized into five courtyards aligned north-south. Don't miss the third courtyard: 82 stone stelae resting on tortoises bear the names of 1,307 laureates of the imperial examinations between 1442 and 1779. Vietnamese students still come to touch the tortoises' heads before exams - you'll see them posing in their ao dai costumes. Arrive at opening (8:30am) to avoid school groups that arrive from 9:30am onward.

⏱ 90 min
2

The mausoleum is only open mornings and silence is required inside. Right next door, don't miss the One Pillar Pagoda (Chua Mot Cot) built in 1049 under King Ly Thai Tong: it rests on a single wooden pillar sunk into a lotus pond shaped like a blooming flower. The current structure is a 1955 reconstruction, the original having been destroyed by the French upon their departure - a detail that gives this small, seemingly modest pagoda an additional dimension.

⏱ 75 min
3

Built in 1011 by King Ly Thai To on the remains of a 7th-century Chinese fortress, the Thang Long citadel served as the seat of Vietnamese power for nearly 13 centuries - inscribed on UNESCO's list in 2010. In 2002, excavations for parliament construction unearthed millions of artifacts layered across multiple dynasties. Don't miss Bunker D67, used by the North Vietnamese army until 1975, visitable as-is with its red phones and tactical maps still in place.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Thang Long Theatre is Hanoi's reference venue for this traditional art of the Red River delta whose origins trace back to the 11th century. Puppeteers operate hidden behind a curtain, feet in water, manipulating lacquered wooden figures that dance and fish on the surface - the technique demands years of training. Performances last 50 minutes. Book ahead in high season.

⏱ 60 min

Don't leave Hanoi without trying egg coffee, a specialty invented in the 1940s when a barman replaced condensed milk - a scarce wartime commodity - with egg yolks beaten with sugar. The result is a thick, creamy foam crowning a robust robusta coffee, served in a bowl of hot water. Operators in the Old Quarter serve this drink upstairs in old tube houses - the best tables overlook Hoan Kiem Lake.

⏱ 30 min
$$$9.1Bella Premier Hotel & Rooftop Skybar

Hanoi

★★★★★
1

If you only visit one museum in Hanoi, make it this one. Opened in 1997, it presents Vietnam's 54 official ethnic groups through collections of costumes, ritual objects, and full-scale reconstructions in the outdoor garden - Ede longhouses, Bahnar rong houses, Thai stilt houses you'll recognize tomorrow in Pu Luong. Texts are trilingual (Vietnamese, French, English). Allow at least 2 hours and don't skip the outdoor garden, especially early morning.

⏱ 120 min
2

Tran Quoc Pagoda is Hanoi's oldest - founded in the 6th century on the banks of the Red River, moved to its West Lake peninsula (Ho Tay, 500 hectares) in the 17th century. Its blood-red eleven-story tower reflects in the waters of the city's largest lake. Go in late afternoon: the golden light on the tower and calm lake, before evening traffic resumes on the causeway, is one of the capital's most beautiful views.

⏱ 60 min
3

Friday evenings and weekends, the streets around Hang Dao close to traffic and fill with stalls: clothing, crafts, street food, street performances. On weekdays, Phung Hung Street north of the Old Quarter hosts permanent mural galleries - a good backup plan. Either way, end the evening with a hot bowl of bun bo Hue seated on a plastic stool and watch the quarter settle down for the night.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Operators rent bikes in the Old Quarter - allow an hour round-trip to Long Bien Bridge. On the bridge, between trains and two-wheelers, you'll have views of the Red River's braided channels, the cultivated vegetable islands below, and mountains in the distance on clear days. It's one of Hanoi's most memorable free activities.

⏱ 45 min

Vietnamese banh mi was born from the encounter of the French baguette with local condiments: pork pâté, pickled carrot, cilantro and soy sauce. The best spots in Old Quarter alleys make sandwiches to order on a small grill. Pair it with a cold Hanoi Beer - the local brewery founded in 1890, still produced in the city.

⏱ 60 min
$$$9.1Bella Premier Hotel & Rooftop Skybar

Pu Luong

★★★☆☆
1

In Thai language, Pu Luong means the highest mountain. The nature reserve created in 1999 covers 17,600 hectares straddling Ba Thuoc and Quan Hoa districts in Thanh Hoa province. Ban Don is the valley's main Black Thai village: its wooden and bamboo stilt houses, elevated more than 2 meters, are built to avoid floods and snakes. If you're here in September-October, the terraced rice fields turn golden at harvest time.

⏱ 90 min
2

Ban Hieu perches above a waterfall of the same name, nestled in tropical vegetation. The village overlooks an amphitheater of terraced rice fields - the most photographed in the entire reserve. From the main path, a few minutes' walk is enough to reach the clear water pools perfect for swimming. Ask your local host to point out the path: it's not always marked for visitors.

⏱ 60 min
3

Don't leave Pu Luong without spotting the water wheels - these large wooden wheels 3 to 4 meters in diameter built along streams to irrigate elevated rice fields. More than 100 exist in the region, all handcrafted and maintained by villages. They turn continuously powered by the current and make a regular creaking sound you can hear from afar in the valley. Your local host will point out the most beautiful ones.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Local operators organize bamboo raft descents on the Ma River that flows through the valley. The current is gentle; from the water, you see terraced rice fields descending to the banks, water buffalo at the fords, and women washing clothes in pools. Bring closed sandals or shoes that can get wet.

⏱ 90 min

Several Thai families offer overnight stays in stilt houses. Dinner is prepared with garden produce - mountain chicken, bamboo shoots, sticky rice cooked in bamboo. Some hosts offer to participate in meal preparation or observe traditional weaving after sunset. Bring a simple gift (fruit, cookies): it's customary in mountain villages.

⏱ 180 min

Ninh Binh

★★★★☆
1

Before taking the boat at Tam Coc, climb Mua Cave. About 480 steps carved into rock lead to the summit of a limestone karst, where the 360-degree view over Tam Coc valley is breathtaking: the Ngo Dong River snaking between sugar loaves and green rice fields. Leave before 8am to avoid heat and crowds, wear shoes with good grip: some steps are slippery in wet weather.

⏱ 60 min
2

Phu Hau village, 5 km from Tam Coc landing, sits at the end of an hour-and-a-half bike loop between limestone karsts and rice fields. Its car-free alleys and local temple are virtually empty of tourists. Operators rent bikes in Tam Coc and Ninh Binh hotels - the terrain is flat, accessible to all levels.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Before leaving Pu Luong, take advantage of dawn for the Ban Bang loop: a trail that follows terraced rice fields, crosses a bamboo bridge over a stream, and offers views of spinning water wheels. The morning air is cool, buffalo emerge from barns - it's the best time to photograph the valley in mist before hitting the road.

⏱ 90 min

Tam Coc means three caves: the limestone tunnels Hang Ca, Hang Hai, and Hang Ba that boats pass through on the Ngo Dong River. What surprises first-time visitors: the rowers use their feet to manipulate the oars, freeing their hands for other tasks. Avoid the hours between 10am and 2pm - the busiest. Arrive at the landing between 7am and 8am for the best light conditions.

⏱ 120 min
$$$9.7Tam Cốc Valley Eco Resort

Ninh Binh

★★★★☆
1

Inscribed on UNESCO's list in 2014, Vietnam's first mixed natural-cultural site, Trang An covers 2,168 hectares of limestone karsts and flooded valleys. Excavations have revealed traces of human occupation over 30,000 years. The rowing boat circuit crosses 47 caves and 31 enclosed valleys between nearly vertical cliffs, with royal temples from the Dinh and Tran dynasties built into cliff niches. Choose route #3, the longest (3h), for the most impressive caves.

⏱ 180 min
2

Hoa Lu was Vietnam's first unified capital, founded in 968 by King Dinh Bo Linh after reunifying the country divided into twelve kingdoms. The capital remained here until 1010. Two temples built in the 17th and 18th centuries mark the location of the old palaces. The site is much less crowded than Trang An and Tam Coc - you'll often be alone with the guardians, and this calm is part of the charm.

⏱ 60 min
3

Bai Dinh Pagoda, inaugurated in 2010 after ten years of construction, extends over more than 1,000 hectares. On the main avenue, 500 blue stone arhat statues 2 to 2.5 meters each accompany visitors to the main temples. Tam The Temple houses three bronze Buddha statues of 50 tons each representing the Buddha of the past, present, and future. Electric tuk-tuks shuttle from the entrance - use them for the climbs.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Operators rent bikes in Tam Coc - allow an hour and a half for a loop between limestone karsts, rice fields, and small villages. The terrain is flat and the road uncrowded on weekdays. Stop in villages not marked on tourist maps: residents are welcoming and local temples are deserted.

⏱ 90 min

Ninh Binh is known throughout Vietnam for its mountain goat meat. Goats graze on limestone cliffs - their meat is firmer and less fatty than lowland meat. Restaurants around Tam Coc landing serve grilled goat, hot pot, or salad with fresh herbs. Don't look for signs in English: follow groups of Vietnamese families dining together in the evening.

⏱ 60 min
$$$9.7Tam Cốc Valley Eco Resort

Ha Long

★★★★☆
1

Bai Tu Long - the little dragons playing in the sea - is the eastern part of Ha Long Bay, less crowded than the main sector. The bay as a whole comprises 1,969 limestone karst islets reaching up to 100 meters in height, inscribed on UNESCO's list in 1994. The improbably shaped karsts (elephant, fighting cock, stone dog) were sculpted by 500 million years of limestone erosion. An overnight aboard is the ideal format to see sunset and sunrise over these cliffs.

⏱ 300 min
2

About 1,000 to 2,000 people still live in floating houses among the bay's islands, mainly from fishing, aquaculture, and oyster farming. Vung Vieng village, accessible by kayak or rowboat from the junk, lets you see floating fish pens and houses resting on rafts. Ask the crew to include a stop - it's one of the rare chances to see how people actually live in the bay.

⏱ 60 min
3

Sunset from your junk's upper deck is one of the cruise highlights. The karsts gradually shift from gray to pink, then deep orange before the bay darkens. Local fishing boats return to the village, their lights blinking among the islands. Settle in with a local beer and don't pull out your phone for the first ten minutes: you'll see much better.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Kayaking is the only way to penetrate the interior lagoons - these hidden lakes inside islands, accessible through very low limestone tunnels. Leave early morning (7-9am): the light is soft, motor boats aren't circulating yet, and mist over the karsts creates a watercolor landscape. Most junks provide kayaks included in the price.

⏱ 120 min

Several white sand coves are accessible only by boat in the Bai Tu Long sector. Ask the junk captain to point out the least crowded spots - on weekdays and off-season, you can have an entire beach to yourself, surrounded by sheer limestone cliffs.

⏱ 60 min

Ha Long

★★★★☆
1

Thien Canh Son Cave is one of Bai Tu Long Bay's most beautiful: its stalactites and stalagmites form columns several meters tall in a succession of cathedral-sized chambers. Significantly less visited than Sung Sot Cave in the main sector. Climb to the summit opening to see the bay from inside the rock - a perspective that visitors who stay below never know.

⏱ 60 min
2

Wake before 6am: the morning mist rising over the karsts, the pink light coloring the cliffs, the first fishing boats - all this lasts less than an hour and exists nowhere else. If your junk offers a Tai Chi session on deck at 6:30am, take it: even without experience, basic movements are learned in minutes and the essential thing is to be there, still, facing the karsts.

⏱ 45 min
3

Disembarkation is usually around noon; the return drive to Hanoi takes 3 hours via highway. Operators organize direct transfers from the port to Noi Bai airport - practical if you have an evening flight. In Da Nang, a driver will be waiting for the 100 km drive to Hue.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Most junks offer a Tai Chi session on the upper deck at sunrise. The mist rising over the karsts at dawn creates an atmosphere that Ha Long Bay offers only to those who spend the night. Even without experience, basic movements are learned in minutes - it's mainly the opportunity to watch the sun color the cliffs pink and orange before the rest of the boat wakes.

⏱ 45 min

Operators organize transfers from the port to Noi Bai airport in Hanoi. The Hanoi to Da Nang flight takes about 1h20. Upon arrival in Da Nang, allow 2 hours' drive to reach Hue along the coast. Plan a photo stop at the top of Hai Van Pass if you pass before nightfall.

⏱ 240 min

Hue

★★★★☆
1

Hue was Vietnam's imperial capital from 1802 to 1945 under the Nguyen dynasty. The ensemble of monuments has been UNESCO-listed since 1993. The citadel is surrounded by a 10 km enclosure and 6-meter-high walls pierced by about ten gates. The Meridian Gate (Ngo Mon) was reserved for the emperor's exclusive use - mandarins passed through side gates, soldiers through outer gates. Allow at least 2 hours and good shoes.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Palace of Supreme Harmony (Thai Hoa), site of grand ceremonies, has its throne supported by 80 lacquered wooden pillars. Behind, the Purple Forbidden City was destroyed about 80% during fighting in the Tet Offensive of January 1968. What remains is the Royal Library from 1821 - a mosaic-covered pavilion overlooking a square lake - and the Royal Theatre. These ruins under restoration are among Vietnam's most moving.

⏱ 75 min
3

Before entering the citadel, walk the ramparts from outside - their thickness can reach 21 meters in places, built according to Vauban fortification principles. The Flag Tower (Ky Dai), erected in 1807, stands 54.5 meters tall. During the 1968 Tet Offensive, the flag of the Democratic Republic of Vietnam flew there for 24 days - an episode that alone summarizes the brutality of the Battle of Hue.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Hue cuisine was born in imperial kitchens where chefs prepared up to 50 different dishes per meal for the emperor. Banh beo (small steamed rice cakes), bun bo Hue (spicy lemongrass beef soup), and banh khoai (crispy shrimp pancakes) are its emblems. Operators offer royal cuisine dinners in garden houses (nha vuon) with Nha Nhac accompaniment - court music listed on UNESCO's intangible heritage in 2003.

⏱ 90 min

The Perfume River (song Huong) flows through Hue, separating the old town to the north from the modern city to the south. A half-hour riverside walk at sunset, from Trang Tien Bridge toward the dragon boat landing, gives a first impression of the illuminated city. Restaurants on moored boats offer evening meals with views of both banks.

⏱ 45 min
$$$9.4Meliá Vinpearl Hue

Hue

★★★★☆
1

Thien Mu Pagoda overlooks the right bank of the Perfume River 5 km from the citadel. Its 21-meter octagonal tower - built in 1844 under Emperor Thieu Tri - has seven levels each representing a reincarnation of Buddha. In the courtyard, don't miss the blue Austin Westminster: it's the car that transported monk Thich Quang Duc to Saigon in 1963, where he self-immolated in protest against the Diem regime - the image went around the world.

⏱ 60 min
2

Emperor Tu Duc's mausoleum (reign 1848-1883) is the most poetic of Hue's seven imperial tombs. Tu Duc himself designed it and came here to read, compose poems, and fish in an artificial lake during his lifetime - he ruled 36 years, the longest Nguyen dynasty reign, leaving no direct heir. The complex extends in a wooded setting of pines and frangipani 8 km from the center. If you only visit one tomb, choose this one.

⏱ 90 min
3

The penultimate emperor Khai Dinh (reign 1916-1925) had his mausoleum built during his lifetime - eleven years of work on a mountain 11 km from Hue. The facade mixes reinforced concrete columns and broken ceramic ornaments in a style you'll see nowhere else. Inside, three painted lacquer ceilings depict nine dragons in clouds. Climb the 127 steps: the view over Hue countryside is worth the elevation.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

An hour-long boat ride at sunset from Trang Tien Bridge offers a different perspective on Hue: silhouettes of pagodas and colonial villas stand out against the orange sky from the water. Operators offer dragon boats with Nha Nhac musicians aboard some evenings - ask your hotel to book the day before.

⏱ 60 min

Garden houses (nha vuon) are a Hue architectural specialty: mandarin residences surrounded by gardens shaded by banyan and magnolia trees, some having survived two centuries of wars and typhoons. Several families descended from imperial court officers welcome visitors to their ancestral homes. It's one of the rare places in Vietnam where you enter a private space knowing the hosts have lived here since the early 19th century.

⏱ 60 min
$$$9.4Meliá Vinpearl Hue

Hoi An

★★★★★
1

Hai Van Pass rises to 500 meters altitude; its 21 km of switchbacks separate Thua Thien-Hue and Da Nang provinces and two climate zones. You can pass in a few hairpins from overcast skies to blazing sun and turquoise sea. At the summit, remnants of French and American fortifications are still visible. Stop at Lang Co beach below: a white sand tongue between lagoon and sea, ideal for an hour's swim en route.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Japanese Covered Bridge (Chua Cau), built in 1593 by the Japanese community to link their quarter to the Chinese quarter, is Hoi An's emblem - it appears on the 20,000 dong Vietnamese banknote. Seven wooden spans covered by a lacquered roof, a sanctuary dedicated to the sea god midway, monkey and dog statues at each end representing the zodiac years of construction's start and finish. Come in the evening when lanterns light up.

⏱ 45 min
3

Tan Ky house (101 Nguyen Thai Hoc Street) is the most beautiful of Hoi An's old merchant houses - it dates from the 18th century and has been passed down through seven generations in the same family. Its carved woodwork mixes Japanese, Chinese, and Vietnamese influences. Push open the door: the interior, dark and cool, smells of old wood. The family still lives in the back rooms.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Lang Co is a white sand peninsula squeezed between Lap An lagoon and the South China Sea, at the foot of Hai Van Pass. The lagoon waters are calm and shallow, perfect for swimming. A few shacks serve fresh crabs and shrimp at local prices - an impromptu beach lunch before continuing to Hoi An.

⏱ 60 min

Hoi An by night is a different city from Hoi An by day. From 7pm onward, silk lanterns of all colors illuminate the alleys and reflect in the Thu Bon River. During full moon, residents turn off electric lights and keep only lanterns. Wander without a map: every alley conceals an inner courtyard or local temple that organized tours don't show.

⏱ 60 min

Hoi An

★★★★★
1

Phuc Kien Assembly Hall was built by merchant emigrants from China's Fujian province in the 17th century, dedicated to Thien Hau, goddess protector of sailors. Inside, incense coils 30 cm in diameter burn constantly, suspended from the ceiling - the columns of perfumed smoke create a dense atmosphere. It's a very active pagoda: respect worshippers in prayer.

⏱ 45 min
2

Tran Family Chapel (21 Le Loi Street) is an intact example of a Confucian communal house: ancestor tablets from seven generations are displayed and the family still holds annual ceremonies. The Museum of Trade Ceramics (80 Tran Phu Street) traces Hoi An's history as a porcelain export center to Japan and Southeast Asia: collections include fragments recovered from shipwrecks off the Cham Islands.

⏱ 60 min
3

Hoi An central market (cho Hoi An) borders the Thu Bon River. Before 9am, fishermen bring their catches and market gardeners their vegetables. You'll find spices and specialties to taste on site: mi quang (wide yellow turmeric noodles) and cao lau - these thick pork noodles whose recipe requires water from Hoi An wells and which authentically exists nowhere else in Vietnam.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hoi An is renowned for its silk and bamboo lantern workshops - you choose colors and pattern, the result is ready to take away in 45 minutes. Operators in the old town organize these workshops all day. It's a concrete way to understand how these objects omnipresent in the city are made.

⏱ 60 min

Operators offer cooking classes that begin with a guided tour of the central market to choose ingredients with a chef, followed by a 2-hour lesson on cao lau, banh xeo, and fresh spring rolls. Book the day before - classes fill up fast. It's one of the best ways to understand the logic of Vietnamese flavors you'll find throughout the country.

⏱ 180 min
$$$9.6Laluna Hoi An Riverside Hotel & Spa

Hoi An

★★★★★
1

My Son is the great Hindu sanctuary of the Champa kingdom, built between the 4th and 13th centuries in an enclosed valley 40 km from Hoi An, primarily dedicated to Shiva. French archaeologists rediscovered it in 1898. Of the 70 original towers and temples, about forty remain - some severely damaged by American bombing in 1969. UNESCO-listed since 1999. Arrive at opening for morning mist and the atmosphere that harsh midday light destroys.

⏱ 150 min
2

Tra Que village, 3 km north of Hoi An, grows vegetables and aromatic herbs on a peninsula surrounded by the Thu Bon River. These are the herbs that perfume Hoi An cuisine - Tra Que's rau thom is renowned throughout central Vietnam. Operators organize guided bike tours with gardening demonstrations and tastings. The terrain is flat, accessible to all.

⏱ 60 min
3

An Bang beach is 5 km from the old town. Less crowded than Cua Dai beach, An Bang keeps a quieter atmosphere with simple beach restaurants serving fresh seafood of the day. Swimming is pleasant from April to October; outside this period, waves can be strong.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Operators rent bikes in the old town to reach Tra Que. The road follows rice fields and market gardens - 20 minutes of calm pedaling. On site, village families welcome visitors for gardening demonstrations and tastings of specialties made with local herbs.

⏱ 90 min

Small boats depart from the Bach Dang Street landing for an hour on the Thu Bon River at sunset - the hour when the old town lights up with thousands of lanterns and low-angle light transforms ocher facades to gold. If you're here during full moon, residents turn off electric lights and keep only lanterns.

⏱ 60 min
$$$9.6Laluna Hoi An Riverside Hotel & Spa

Da Nang

★★★★☆
1

The Son Tra peninsula protects Da Nang bay. At the summit, Linh Ung Pagoda hosts Vietnam's tallest Quan Am statue: 67 meters on a 35-meter pedestal, visible from almost the entire city. The view embraces Da Nang bay, the Marble Mountains, and Hai Van Pass in clear weather. The peninsula's primary forest shelters one of Vietnam's last wild populations of red-shanked douc langur - watch in the trees along the road.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Marble Mountains are five marble and limestone hills bearing the names of the five elements: Water, Metal, Wood, Fire, and Earth. Thuy Son, the most accessible, is climbed via stairs carved into rock. Inside, caves shelter pagodas including Huyen Khong cave, whose ceiling opens to a natural light shaft. The region has been renowned for centuries for its marble carvers - workshops line the road below.

⏱ 90 min
3

My Khe is one of central Vietnam's longest beaches: 30 km of white sand, consistent swells appreciated by surfers. A few hours between the Marble Mountains and the airport transfer are enough to cool off. Don't linger too long if your flight is early afternoon.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Da Nang is renowned for fresh seafood: grilled lobster, salt shrimp, and soft-shell crabs. Operators have restaurants with feet in the sand on My Khe beach - a seafood lunch before the flight is one of the best culinary memories you'll bring back from central Vietnam.

⏱ 60 min

The Da Nang to Ho Chi Minh City flight takes about 1h15. Operators organize transfers between the beach or Marble Mountains and the airport. Upon arrival in Saigon, Tan Son Nhat airport is 7 km from downtown - allow 30 to 45 minutes depending on traffic.

Ho Chi Minh

★★★★☆
1

Notre-Dame Basilica of Saigon was built between 1877 and 1880 by architect Jules Bourard. Its red bricks came from Marseille - no local bricks were used. The two 57.6-meter bell towers house six bells cast in France in 1895. Just behind, the Central Post Office (1891) has a metal framework designed by engineers from Gustave Eiffel's school. The post office is still active - you can mail postcards there.

⏱ 45 min
2

On April 30, 1975, two North Vietnamese Army T-54 tanks crashed through this presidential palace's gate - the image went around the world and marks the end of the Vietnam War. The building has remained intact since: 95 rooms, underground war room with red phones and tactical maps in place, bunker and radio equipment from the 1970s. A UH-1 helicopter used for evacuations still sits on the roof. Allow 1h30 and take a guide on site.

⏱ 90 min
3

Dong Khoi Street - the former rue Catinat under the French - connects Notre-Dame Cathedral to the Saigon River waterfront. It's the backbone of colonial District 1, lined with villas and neoclassical buildings, several transformed into luxury hotels. The 1908 City Hall, at the end of pedestrian Nguyen Hue Avenue, is worth a look from outside. In the evening, the street comes alive with café terraces and local art galleries.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Ho Chi Minh City is among the world's best cities for street food. Bun bo Hue in plastic bowls, crispy banh mi, fresh spring rolls, hu tieu soup, and banh xeo crepes are found on every corner in District 1. For a more local atmosphere, seek out sidewalk stalls on streets adjacent to Ben Thanh. Vinh Khanh Street in District 4, 10 minutes by taxi, is where locals go for grilled seafood in the open air.

⏱ 90 min

Nguyen Hue pedestrian street comes alive each evening: Saigonese families gather, children roller-skate, flower vendors occupy sidewalks, and the colonial 1908 City Hall is illuminated in the background. It's a fine way to take Saigon's pulse on your arrival evening.

Ho Chi Minh

★★★★☆
1

This museum assembles photos, weapons, and testimonies on the Vietnam War (1955-1975). The photography hall is the most striking: images of civilian victims, consequences of Agent Orange across generations, photos from the My Lai massacre. American planes and helicopters in the outdoor courtyard include an F-5A fighter and a UH-1 helicopter. Arrive at opening (7:30am). Allow at least two hours.

⏱ 120 min
2

Cholon extends across Saigon's 5th and 6th districts. Thien Hau Pagoda, built in the early 19th century by Cantonese merchants, has its roof covered with terracotta figurines depicting classical Chinese opera scenes. Incense coils burn constantly on the ceiling. The neighboring Binh Tay market (1928) is the district's wholesale market: exotic fruits, spices, medicinal herbs in large quantities.

⏱ 90 min
3

Built in 1909 by the Cantonese community, this pagoda mixes Mahayana Buddhism, Taoism, and popular religion. Painted wooden sculptures depicting gods, demons, and celestial warriors fill every inch of the altars. In the courtyard pond, tortoises bask on rocks. It's an active pagoda: respect the silence of worshippers.

⏱ 45 min
4

Tan Dinh Church (1876) in District 3 is renowned for its garish pink facade that clashes violently with the colonial quarter's beige architecture. This bubble-gum-pink neo-Romanesque has become an unmissable Saigon photo spot. Push open the door to see the more sober interior.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Ben Thanh market, in its 1914 building, is a Saigon symbol. After 6pm, street stalls set up all around for the night market: about a hundred plastic tables under colorful umbrellas serve local specialties until midnight. Prices are negotiable. For something less touristy, streets perpendicular to Ben Thanh have pho restaurants frequented exclusively by locals.

⏱ 60 min

Cholon is Saigon's best place to lunch on dim sum (ha cao, xiu mai, lo bak go) in restaurants frequented exclusively by the Sino-Vietnamese community for decades. Operators offer guided food tours that include medicinal herb shops and artisanal tofu factories. Come preferably in the morning: dim sum is served until 11am only in most spots.

Ho Chi Minh

★★★★☆
1

The Cu Chi tunnel network, 70 km northwest of Saigon, is one of the Vietnam War's most astounding military achievements. These 250 kilometers of underground galleries began being dug in 1948 by bare-handed peasants. During the war, the tunnels sheltered hospitals, kitchens, dormitories, and meeting rooms on multiple levels down to 10 meters deep. You can crawl through a section enlarged for tourists and see reconstructed bamboo traps.

⏱ 180 min
2

Upon returning from Cu Chi tunnels, an hour at the Vietnam History Museum in District 1 offers a useful counterpoint: objects from prehistoric, Champa, and imperial dynasties that give a longer perspective on the country's history. The 1929 colonial building, mixing French style and glazed Asian tiles, is itself worth the look.

⏱ 60 min
3

Nguyen Hue pedestrian street comes alive each evening from 6pm onward: Saigonese families, children on roller skates, flower vendors, and the illuminated colonial 1908 City Hall in the background. It's one of the most pleasant ways to end the day after the tunnels in a light and festive atmosphere.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Dong Khoi Street and its adjacent streets concentrate Saigon's best craft shops: lacquerware, embroidered silk, culi coffee (mountain robusta), tea and spices. Avoid shops at luxury hotel entrances - seek out workshops behind facades where artisans work on site. Quality vacuum-sealed Vietnamese coffee is light to carry and makes excellent gifts.

⏱ 60 min

Ho Chi Minh City has several rooftop bars and cafés in District 1. The view of modern skyscrapers and illuminated colonial quarters in the evening is one of the best ways to end the day after the tunnels in a light atmosphere. Prefer terraces in boutique hotels to large international hotels: the atmosphere is more local.

Can Tho

★★★☆☆
1

My Tho is the Mekong Delta's first city from Saigon. Unicorn Island (Con Lan) is reached in 10 minutes by motorized sampan. Operators offer rides through channels under coconut and banana trees, tropical fruit tasting with honey tea, and a performance of don ca tai tu - the South's traditional music inscribed on UNESCO's intangible heritage since 2013. A good introduction to the delta's rhythm before reaching Can Tho.

⏱ 150 min
2

Ninh Kieu wharf runs along the Bassac branch of the Mekong and comes alive from 5pm onward: street stalls, families strolling, children at the water's edge. Boat-restaurants offer floating dinners by lantern light. It's from this wharf that you'll depart tomorrow at 5am for Cai Rang floating market.

⏱ 60 min
3

Ong Pagoda (or Quang Cong Pagoda), built in 1894 by Can Tho's Cantonese merchants, is one of the Mekong Delta's most beautiful. Dedicated to General Quan Cong (Guan Yu), it's adorned with colorful mosaics and terracotta figures imported from China. Its riverside location, a stone's throw from the central market, makes it an easy late afternoon stop.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Ben Tre, nicknamed the kingdom of coconuts, is crossed by a dense network of shaded canals. Operators organize rowing sampan rides through channels, with visits to an artisanal coconut candy factory (keo dua, a local specialty exported throughout Asia) and a coconut fiber rope works. The pace is slow, the landscapes lush.

⏱ 90 min

Ninh Kieu wharf restaurants serve local specialties rarely offered in Saigon: elephant-ear fish (ca tai tuong) wrapped in rice paper, steamed Mekong shrimp, and rice soup (chao ca) of the boatmen. Choose a restaurant overlooking the river and order by pointing at live fish in the tank.

Can Tho

★★★☆☆
1

Cai Rang floating market is the Mekong Delta's largest. Hundreds of boats loaded with fruits and vegetables gather each morning on the Bassac branch 6 km south of Can Tho. Wholesalers signal what they're selling by hanging their goods on a long bamboo pole planted at the bow - the sign-pole visible from afar, avoiding misunderstandings between boats. Wake at 5am and take a sampan from Ninh Kieu wharf. Order a bowl of hu tieu from one of the kitchen-boats circulating among wholesalers.

⏱ 120 min
2

Munirangsyaram Pagoda is one of Can Tho's most beautiful Khmer Buddhist pagodas: tiered colorful roofs, naga (sacred serpent) sculptures, walls painted with scenes from Buddha's life. The Khmer Krom, a Cambodian minority settled in the delta for centuries, practice a living Theravada Buddhist tradition here.

⏱ 45 min
3

Binh Thuy house, 6 km from Can Tho center, is one of the delta's most beautiful colonial houses - built in 1870 in a style mixing French neoclassicism and Asian ornaments. It was used as a set in Jean-Jacques Annaud's film adapted from Marguerite Duras's novel. The owner family still lives in the back rooms and opens the reception salons to visitors.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Operators organize excursions in Ben Tre province: sampan ride through channels shaded by coconut palms, visit to a coconut candy factory, then lunch with elephant-ear fish (ca tai tuong). It's one of the entire circuit's most relaxing and lush days - the boat's slow pace in narrow canals is an antidote to Saigon's bustle.

⏱ 240 min

Operators rent bikes in Can Tho to explore rural roads winding through rice fields, canals, and durian and longan orchards. The terrain is flat and the road uncrowded on weekdays - a way to see the delta at human height rather than from a motorized boat.

Ho Chi Minh

★★★★☆
1

On the return road to Saigon, Vinh Long city merits an hour-and-a-half stop. Its covered market, on the Co Chien branch of the Mekong, is lively in the morning: tropical fruits, dried fish, and sticky rice preparations. An Binh island, accessible by small ferry from Vinh Long, is covered with orchards and palm-shaded canals - a final delta immersion before the highway back.

2

District 3 of Saigon, less touristy than District 1, concentrates some of the most appreciated cafés and restaurants among residents - spots in restored colonial villas whose inner courtyards are covered with green plants. It's here that Saigon creatives and artists gather in late afternoon. An hour in District 3 will show you a very different Saigon from classic tourist circuits.

Activities

For your penultimate night in Ho Chi Minh City, Nguyen Hue pedestrian street in the evening gathers Saigonese families. Finish at a neighborhood restaurant with a final pho or fresh spring rolls - a way to close two delta days on a simple, local note.

Several rooftop bars in District 1 offer panoramic views of illuminated Saigon's skyline - the Bitexco Financial tower and modern skyscrapers mixed with colonial villa roofs. Prefer spots in boutique hotels to major chains: the atmosphere is more local and the view often better.

Ho Chi Minh

★★★★☆
1

Tao Dan Park is District 1's largest green space. Before 8am, it transforms into a surprisingly calm living space: tai chi and open-air dance groups, elderly men at Chinese chess under century-old ficus trees, and bird enthusiasts for singing contests - each owner hangs their cage on a hook and the birds compete for best song. It's a slice of local life that few tourists take time to see, two minutes from downtown's grand hotels.

⏱ 60 min
2

If your flight is in the afternoon, a final hour in District 1 streets lets you see colonial buildings in morning light - fewer crowds, better photos. Ly Tu Trong Street, parallel to Dong Khoi, is lined with neoclassical villas, several housing embassies. The Garden of Mists (Cong Truong Quoc Te), a small square bordered by bougainvillea, is a beautiful shortcut between the cathedral and Reunification Palace.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Dong Khoi Street and its adjacent streets concentrate Saigon's best craft shops: lacquerware, embroidered silk, coffee, tea and spices. Ben Thanh market is a two-minute walk for final vacuum-sealed spices. Tan Son Nhat international airport is 7 km from downtown - always allow more than expected during rush hours, and 2h30 before flight time for international formalities.

⏱ 90 min

Before leaving for the airport, take time for a final bowl of pho or banh mi in a quarter alley. The best spots don't have signs in English - spot where locals stop with their scooters before heading to work. It's often the meal travelers remember best, because it's the one where you know you're going home.
