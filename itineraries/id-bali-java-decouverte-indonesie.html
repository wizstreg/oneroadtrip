Yogyakarta

★★★★★
1

Borobudur sits on a hilltop at 265 metres above sea level, 40 km northwest of Yogyakarta — it is the largest Buddhist monument in the world. Built between 750 and 850 under the Sailendra dynasty, it rests on 55,000 m³ of andesite stone assembled without mortar and features 2,670 individual bas-relief panels covering 2,500 m² of carved surface. Its square base measures 123 metres per side and the central stupa rises to 35 metres. The temple was buried under volcanic ash from Mount Merapi for centuries before being rediscovered in 1814 by British governor Raffles, who sent 200 men to clear the jungle and expose it. Arrive before 7 am for sunrise over the stupas — after 9 am the tour groups descend and the site gets packed.

⏱ 120 min
2

Two kilometres from Borobudur along the same spiritual axis, Mendut Temple houses one of the finest Buddha statues in all of Southeast Asia: a 3-metre-tall seated Buddha flanked by two bodhisattvas, carved from a single block of stone. Local legend holds that a walled, paved road once connected Mendut, the small Pawon temple and Borobudur into a single pilgrimage route. Allow 30 minutes on site — it's far less crowded than Borobudur and all the more serene for it.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Set off on foot or by bicycle through the villages of the Kedu Plain at the foot of Borobudur: rice paddies, small neighbourhood temples, morning markets. This is one of the most fertile agricultural plains on Java, nicknamed 'the garden of Java' for centuries. If you're here in May or June, the Vesak (Waisak) celebrations bring thousands of Buddhist pilgrims who walk in procession from Mendut to Borobudur at the full moon.

Yogyakarta

★★★★★
1

Prambanan is the Hindu counterpart to Borobudur: built around 850 AD by King Rakai Pikatan of the Sanjaya dynasty, this complex of 240 Shaivite temples is the largest Hindu ensemble in Indonesia. The main temple dedicated to Shiva stands 47 metres tall. Some historians see it as a direct response to Borobudur, erected a few decades earlier by the rival Buddhist dynasty — and remarkably, Rakai Pikatan's wife was the daughter of the king who had overseen Borobudur's construction. Head there in the late afternoon for the low-angled light on the spires, and if possible stay for the open-air Ramayana Ballet in the evening: 200 costumed dancers performing the epic with the temple as a backdrop.

⏱ 120 min
2

Just 800 metres from Prambanan, Candi Sewu — 'the thousand temples' in Javanese — is Java's second-largest Buddhist complex after Borobudur. Its 249 towers, most of them in ruins, are guarded by imposing Dwarapala statues. The site sees very few visitors; take the time to wander through it — the atmosphere is far calmer than at its neighbour.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

If you have time at the end of the day, head up to the Ratu Boko site, a former royal palace perched on a plateau 200 metres above the plain. The ruins offer one of the best panoramas of Prambanan with Mount Merapi as a backdrop — come at sunset for the best effect.

Yogyakarta

★★★★★
1

The Kraton — 'the place where the king lives' in Javanese — is far more than a palace: it's a city within a city, where 25,000 people still live behind 3-metre-high walls stretching 1 km on each side. Founded in 1755 by Sultan Hamengkubuwono I after the signing of the Treaty of Giyanti, which split the Mataram kingdom between Yogyakarta and Surakarta, the Kraton is today the residence of Sultan Hamengkubuwono X, who also serves as governor of the territory. Visit in the morning only — the palace is reserved for the family in the afternoon. The abdi dalem, servants who devote their lives to the sultan, quietly sweep the black-sand courtyards in traditional dress.

⏱ 90 min
2

Steps from the Kraton, Taman Sari — 'flower garden' — is the former water castle built in 1758 by Hamengkubuwono I. Its pools served for the ablutions of the sultan's favourites: from a watchtower, the sovereign would choose which one would join him. Today this labyrinth of pools, arches and partially restored underground passages gives off a faintly mystical atmosphere. Legend even has it that a secret tunnel connects this place to Parangtritis beach, the abode of the goddess Nyai Loro Kidul.

⏱ 60 min
3

Malioboro, Yogyakarta's central avenue, is the batik trading hub of all Java. If you're after quality hand-made batik, push through the doors of the workshops in the old town rather than the shops on the main avenue. The Kota Gede neighbourhood, to the southeast, is renowned for its silversmiths — it was the former capital of the first Mataram sultanate in the 16th century.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

In the evening, catch a wayang kulit performance — shadow-puppet theatre using leather puppets — or a gamelan show at the Kraton or one of the city's cultural centres. The Kraton holds performances on weekday mornings; ask your hotel about evening show schedules. This is one of the oldest living art forms on Java, and Yogyakarta is its undisputed capital.

⏱ 120 min

Probolinggo

★★☆☆☆
1

The road from Yogyakarta to Probolinggo runs along Java's southern flank, crossing rice paddies and small towns. From Probolinggo, make a detour if possible to Madakaripura Waterfall, hidden at the end of a sacred gorge: according to Javanese legend, this is where the 14th-century general Gajah Mada retreated to meditate before disappearing. The main waterfall drops 200 metres into a moss-covered amphitheatre of cliffs — you'll need to wade through the river for the last 200 metres. Bring trousers you can roll up.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Probolinggo is the gateway to Mount Bromo. From the train station or bus terminal, take a minibus to Cemoro Lawang (about 2 hours of winding road through plantations). Settle into your accommodation early and get to bed before 10 pm: the alarm for sunrise goes off at 3 am.

Cemoro Lawang

★★★★☆
1

Wake-up at 3 am to reach the viewpoint at Mount Penanjakan (2,700 m) or King Kong Hill: at dawn, the Tengger caldera reveals itself beneath a sea of clouds. In the foreground, the perfect cone of Mount Batok (dormant); behind it, Bromo trails its permanent plume of smoke; in the background, Semeru — Java's highest point at 3,676 metres — erupts every 15 to 20 minutes. Bromo stands at 2,329 metres with a crater 800 metres across and 200 metres deep. From Cemoro Lawang, you can reach the viewpoint on foot in about 1 h 30 min in the dark — the quietest option to avoid the hundreds of jeeps.

⏱ 150 min
2

After sunrise, descend into the Sea of Sand of the Tengger caldera — a vast expanse of volcanic ash and rock stretching 10 km across. At the foot of Bromo, the Hindu temple Pura Luhur Poten watches over ceremonies: the Tenggerese are one of the last Hindu ethnic groups on Java. Climb the 250 steps to the crater rim and peer into its smoking depths. Every year in July, during the Yadnya Kasada festival, the Tenggerese throw offerings into the volcano to appease the gods.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

In the afternoon, once the jeeps have left, the caldera returns to a striking silence. Walk through this near-lunar landscape, between dunes of volcanic sand and the small Mongolian-style horses grazing near the ridges. If you stay until evening, the late-afternoon light on the surrounding volcanoes is every bit as rewarding as the morning show.

⏱ 90 min

Licin

★★☆☆☆
1

Kawah Ijen is one of only two places on earth where you can witness the blue-fire phenomenon — the other being in Iceland. It's not lava but sulphurous gases that ignite on contact with air above 220 °C, producing electric-blue flames up to 5 metres high. To see them, you must leave Paltuding by 2 am at the latest: the flames are only visible in darkness. The site officially opens at 4 am, but an earlier departure is possible with a guide. The crater also holds the most acidic lake in the world, with a pH of 0.2: the turquoise water is absolutely off-limits for swimming. Wear a gas mask — available for hire on site — especially for the descent into the crater.

⏱ 240 min
2

As you climb back out of the crater at sunrise, you'll pass the sulphur porters descending and ascending several times a day carrying 70 to 90 kg of solidified sulphur on their shoulders. The sulphur, bright yellow to orange, is extracted from pipes at the lake's edge and sold to the sugar and rubber industries. It is one of the hardest jobs in the world: these miners have an estimated life expectancy of 40 to 50 years. A discreet tip is welcome if you photograph them.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Late in the morning, after the climb, rest in Licin or head down to Banyuwangi to prepare for the ferry to Bali. Ketapang port is a 20-minute taxi ride from Banyuwangi; ferries to Gilimanuk depart every hour and the crossing takes about 45 minutes.

Ubud

★★★★☆
1

The Ketapang–Gilimanuk ferry drops you at the western tip of Bali. The drive to Ubud (2.5 to 3 hours) crosses the heart of the island, skirting the rice paddies of the Tabanan plain. Ubud sits in the hills at 200 metres above sea level, in a green valley cut by two sacred rivers. If you arrive in the late afternoon, start with the Campuhan Ridge Walk: this 2-km trail follows a ridge between two river valleys with open views over the Balinese countryside. It's one of the rare spots in Ubud where you can easily escape the tourist flow.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

In the evening, catch a traditional Balinese dance performance: legong, barong or kecak. The Ubud Royal Palace (Puri Saren) programmes shows almost every night in its torch-lit courtyards. The legong is the most refined — codified gestures, gold-embroidered costumes, expressive gaze — centuries old and inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list since 2015. Arrive 20 minutes early to get a good seat.

⏱ 90 min

Ubud

★★★★☆
1

Tirta Empul temple, founded in 926 AD under the Warmadewa dynasty, is one of the most sacred on Bali. Its name means 'gushing sacred spring' in Old Javanese. In the central courtyard, two purification pools fed by 30 carved spouts welcome Hindu Balinese pilgrims each morning who come to practise melukat — a purification ritual in which they pass under each jet in a precise order. Note that two of the 30 fountains are reserved for funerary rites. The temple is 30 minutes from Ubud towards Tampaksiring. Arrive early (before 9 am) to share the moment with locals rather than tour groups.

⏱ 90 min
2

A few minutes from Tirta Empul, Gunung Kawi temple dates from the 11th century. You'll descend around a hundred steps through terraced rice paddies to reach ten sanctuaries carved directly into the cliff of the Pakrisan river — a setting of humid jungle and mossy stone that rivals Angkor. Less well known than Tirta Empul, it's often deserted outside group-tour hours.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Tegalalang Rice Terraces, 10 km north of Ubud in the Gianyar regency, are inscribed on the UNESCO Intangible Heritage list. The subak irrigation system dates back to the 9th century, introduced by the sage Rsi Markandeya. The view is spectacular — coconut palms, emerald terraces stepping down the slope — but the place has become very touristy. The many Instagrammable structures (swings, nests) are pay-to-use. Stick to the farming paths between the plots for a more authentic experience.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Ubud Monkey Forest is home to over 600 long-tailed macaques in a 12-hectare grove that includes three Hindu temples dating from the 14th century. The monkeys roam completely free — stow your glasses, cameras and plastic bags before entering; they're particularly quick. The site is sacred to the Balinese, who come here regularly to pray at the temples.

⏱ 60 min

Sidemen

★★★★☆
1

Sidemen sits in the Karangasem valley, in eastern Bali, beneath the silhouette of Mount Agung (3,031 m). Here, the brochure Bali gives way to the agricultural, living Bali: women carry colourful offerings on their heads each morning, subak irrigation channels wind between the houses, and terraced rice paddies tumble down to the Telaga Waja river. Walk the marked trails between the terraced plots — allow 1.5 to 2 hours for the main loop with views of Agung.

⏱ 120 min
2

Pura Besakih, the 'Mother Temple' of Bali, is the island's largest Hindu temple complex: 86 temples and shrines terraced across the slopes of Mount Agung, to which the faithful attribute a history stretching back over 2,000 years. The temple is mentioned in texts from the 14th century. As you climb towards the main temple, Pura Penataran Agung, you pass through successive courtyards where incense burns, and the view over the caldera is striking. Arrive at opening (8 am) to avoid the fake guides, some of whom falsely claim that paid offerings are mandatory — they are not.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Telaga Waja river running through the Sidemen valley offers white-water rafting over roughly ten kilometres through jungle-covered gorges and waterfalls. Local operators run 2-to-3-hour descents from the village — it's gentler than the Ayung rapids near Ubud, suitable for all levels. Prefer the morning for cooler water.

Tukad Cepung waterfall, about an hour from Sidemen, is hidden inside a gorge: you squeeze between two rock walls through a narrow passage and wade through the river to emerge in a natural chamber where the morning sun pierces the cliff face and illuminates the falls in an almost mystical beam of light. 2

They still weave geringsing here, a double-ikat cloth requiring several years to make and produced nowhere else in the world. Entry is free and the village sees few tourists: it's one of the most authentic spots in eastern Bali.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Taman Ujung water palace (Sukasada), built in the early 20th century by the king of Karangasem, overlooks the sea with its infinity pools and ornamental pavilions. Partly destroyed by the 1979 earthquake before being restored, it offers a panoramic view over Bali's east coast and the neighbouring islands. Go in the late afternoon for the best light.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Virgin Beach (Pantai Pasir Putih), tucked into a small cove 10 km from Candidasa, is one of the rare white-sand beaches in eastern Bali. Less crowded than the southern beaches, it's a perfect stop for a midday swim before heading back up to Sidemen.

Munduk

★★★★☆
1

Munduk sits at 900 metres above sea level in the mountains of northern Bali, surrounded by coffee, cocoa and clove plantations. The village is the starting point for a 15-metre waterfall reachable on foot in 20 minutes from the centre — cold water and natural pool included. Further along the same trail, Banyu Wana Amertha waterfall actually comprises 4 successive falls, each quite different from the last. Bring closed shoes and clothes you don't mind getting wet.

⏱ 150 min
2

The floating temple Pura Ulun Danu Bratan rests on the waters of Lake Bratan at 1,200 metres above sea level, inside a former volcanic caldera holding three lakes — Bratan, Buyan and Tamblingan. Built in the 17th century under the reign of the King of Mengwi, it is dedicated to Dewi Danu, goddess of lakes and rivers, and plays a central role in the traditional Balinese irrigation system. Its tiered roofs (meru) reflect on the water in a perfect mirror when the lake is calm — go early in the morning before the fog sets in.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

From the panoramic road running along lakes Buyan and Tamblingan, a free viewpoint takes in the twin lakes separated by a thin strip of vegetation — they were a single lake until the 19th century, when a landslide split them apart. A little further on, the Banjar hot springs offer three pools of sulphurous water at 37 °C, fed by dragon-head-shaped fountains: perfect after a morning of hiking.

Seminyak

★★★★☆
1

Pura Tanah Lot sits on an isolated rock in the sea, 30 minutes north of Seminyak. It is one of Bali's seven sea temples — the Balinese believe these temples form a belt of spiritual protection around the island. Founded in the 16th century by Javanese priest Dang Hyang Nirartha during his pilgrimage to Bali, the temple is only accessible at low tide. Come at sunset for the temple's silhouette against a red sky — it's one of the most photographed images in Indonesia. Arrive an hour early to find a good spot.

⏱ 90 min
2

On Seminyak beach, Pura Petitenget is a discreet but very active temple: it's one of the links in the spiritual chain connecting Tanah Lot in the south to Uluwatu — ceremonies regularly set off from here towards the beach, with offerings entrusted to the waves at sunset. Visit on a Friday evening for the best chance of witnessing a ceremony.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Canggu beach, 10 km north of Seminyak, is home turf for surfers of all levels. Echo Beach's waves suit intermediates, while Batu Bolong's are right for beginners. Local operators offer 2-hour lessons. If you don't surf, settle into one of the beach clubs overlooking the ocean to watch the surfers at sunset.

The Jatiluwih Rice Terraces, a UNESCO World Heritage Site (subak irrigation system), are the largest and least touristy on Bali. The atmosphere is lush and mysterious, with tree ferns and orchids growing on the mossy stone walls. A sacred pool fed by a mountain spring adorns the entrance.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

In the evening in Seminyak, Jalan Oberoi and Jalan Kayu Aya concentrate the Balinese designer boutiques (batik, furniture, silver jewellery) and the restaurants. Uluwatu

★★★★☆
1

Pura Luhur Uluwatu is perched 70 metres above the Indian Ocean on the southwest tip of the Bukit peninsula. Founded in the 11th century by the Javanese sage Mpu Kuturan, it is one of Bali's six spiritual pillar temples (sad kahyangan), charged with protecting the island from harmful influences from the sea. Its petrified-coral blocks take on golden hues at sunset. Watch out for the colony of monkeys living within the grounds — they snatch sunglasses, bags and devices with remarkable efficiency. Arrive by 4 pm to enjoy the temple before the sunset crowds.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Uluwatu Kecak dance is a performance created in the 1930s by choreographer Walter Spies from an old trance ritual called Sanghyang. A chorus of 50 to 70 men chants 'cak' in rhythm — with no instruments at all — while dancers enact scenes from the Ramayana. The show is held outdoors on the cliff at 6 pm and 7 pm, with the sun setting over the ocean as a backdrop. Book your seats in advance during peak season — the stands fill up fast.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Padang Padang beach, wedged into a crack between two cliffs, is one of the best-known beaches on the Bukit peninsula — it served as a filming location for Eat, Pray, Love. Reached by stairs cut into the rock, it's tiny at high tide but perfect for watching local surfers ride the waves. Allow a 30-minute drive from Uluwatu. Before your flight, the fishermen's restaurants on Jimbaran Bay serve grilled-seafood platters on the sand, under the lights of the bay. The vibe is casual and local — arrive early to pick your table.
