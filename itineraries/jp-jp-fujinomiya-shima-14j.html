Fujinomiya

★★★★☆
1

Shiraito Falls awaits you at the foot of Mount Fuji's southwest slope: a curved 20-meter-high, 150-meter-wide cliff where springwater filtered through the volcano bursts forth in hundreds of white threads—hence the name "Shiraito" (silk thread). It's not a river feeding the falls: the Fuji's groundwater takes between 10 and 300 years to emerge here, at a rate of 1.5 tons per second, at 12°C year-round. Designated a UNESCO World Heritage component in 2013 as part of Mount Fuji's cultural site, these falls served as an ascetic training ground for pilgrim monks during the Edo period. Descend the steps to the basin—the mist is refreshing even in midsummer.

⏱ 60 min
2

Just a 5-minute walk upstream, don't miss Otodome Falls, narrower but more powerful, with a 25-meter vertical drop. Their name means "to stop the sound": according to legend, the Soga brothers wanted to silence the waterfall's roar so they could plot their revenge in complete secrecy. The contrast with Shiraito's gentleness is striking.

⏱ 20 min
3

Head back down to Fujinomiya to visit Fujisan Hongu Sengen Taisha, the head shrine of Japan's 1,300 Sengen shrines. Its origins date back to 27 BC, when it was erected to calm Mount Fuji's eruptions. The current buildings were constructed by Tokugawa Ieyasu in 1604, and the main hall (honden) displays a unique two-story architectural style called "sengen-zukuri," designated an Important Cultural Property. In spring, 500 cherry trees bloom along the Sakura no Baba approach—it's one of the region's premier hanami spots.

⏱ 45 min
4

Right next to the shrine, stop by the sacred Wakutama Pond, a Special National Natural Monument. Mount Fuji's snowmelt water surges through the volcanic rock with almost surreal clarity—pilgrims purified themselves here before beginning their ascent of the mountain. The Mount Fuji World Heritage Centre, just steps away, offers an interactive exhibition about the volcano with an observation terrace facing Fuji if the sky is clear.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Try Fujinomiya yakisoba, a local specialty of stir-fried noodles made with particularly elastic noodles, lard and sardine flakes, all cooked with Mount Fuji's spring water. Stalls serve them near the falls and in the town centre.

⏱ 30 min

Minamiizu

★★☆☆☆
1

Hirizo Beach is nicknamed "the last unexplored area of Izu"—and with good reason: it's only accessible by boat from Nakagi Port (10-minute crossing). The water here boasts Okinawa-rivalling transparency, with visibility exceeding 7 meters thanks to the Kuroshio Current sweeping away sediment. You'll see corals, clownfish, schools of sardines and occasionally sea turtles. Be warned: the beach is only open from early July through late September, it's rocky (bring water shoes), and August weekends are packed—come on weekdays or in September for relative calm.

⏱ 180 min
2

If you have time late in the day, push on to Cape Irozaki, the southern tip of the Izu Peninsula. Its white lighthouse offers a sweeping Pacific panorama, and on clear days you'll spot the offshore Izu Islands. It's also a prime sunset viewing point.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Snorkeling at Hirizo Beach—outfitters at Nakagi Port rent masks, snorkels and wetsuits. A guide who knows the day's best spots will take you to the most fish-rich zones. Plan a full day.

⏱ 240 min

Kawanehon

★★☆☆☆
1

The Dream Suspension Bridge (Yume no Tsuribashi) was voted one of the world's 10 suspension bridges to cross before you die by TripAdvisor. Ninety meters long and perched 8 meters above a dam lake of almost unreal cobalt blue (the Tyndall effect renders the water turquoise on sunny days), this wooden bridge sways noticeably underfoot—maximum 10 people at a time. Tradition holds that you should make a love wish at its centre. Be prepared: after crossing, you must climb 304 steep steps. Allow 30 minutes' walk from the hot spring village of Sumatakyo Onsen, through a tunnel renowned for its "healing wind."

⏱ 90 min
2

Okuoikojo Station, on the Oigawa Railway's Ikawa Line, appears to float in the middle of Nagashima Dam's emerald lake. Two red bridges of 195 and 286 meters connect it to the shores—you can walk across the pedestrian footbridge (the Rainbow Bridge) for a complete panorama of the station, lake and Southern Alps mountains. On weekends, a small pop-up café opens in the station chalet.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Take the Oigawa Railway steam train from Senzu Station—four locomotives built in the 1940s are still in service. The journey crosses terraced tea valleys and forested gorges. In summer, a Thomas the Tank Engine train runs for families.

⏱ 60 min

Relax in the onsen baths of Sumatakyo Onsen late in the day. The sulphurous water is renowned for softening skin—the Japanese call these springs "bijin no yu" (beauty bath). Several ryokan offer day-use bath access.

⏱ 60 min

Toyonemura

★★☆☆☆
1

Mount Chausu (1,358 m), Aichi Prefecture's highest peak, hosts the "Heavenly Flower Corridor": 400,000 moss phlox plants in six varieties—hot pink, white, light blue, purple—carpeting the summit slope from mid-May through early June. The spectacle is all the more striking as the Southern Alps rise in the background. Ride the panoramic chairlift to the summit and search for the heart-shaped pattern hidden in the flowerbed—the Japanese say it brings good fortune in love.

⏱ 90 min
2

At the summit, Chausu Terrace offers an unobstructed view of the Southern Alps range—it's one of the few easily accessible viewpoints at this altitude in the region. On clear days, the panorama extends for dozens of kilometres.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Toyone village is known for its mountain-water sturgeon farming—try the "Royal Fish," a local speciality served as dumplings or sashimi in village restaurants. Surprising in the Japanese mountains.

⏱ 45 min

In summer (July-August), the Chausu plateau offers outdoor activities: trout fishing, canoeing, hiking. At night, far from any light pollution, the starry sky is spectacular—you might even spot deer.

⏱ 60 min

Toyota

★★☆☆☆
1

Korankei is THE autumn foliage spot for the entire Tokai region—about 4,000 Japanese maples planted 400 years ago by San'ei, the 11th monk of Kojakuji Temple, blanket Mount Iimori in crimson and gold each November. The vermillion Taigetsukyo Bridge spanning the Tomoe River is the most famous photo spot. In the evening during the Maple Festival (throughout November), the mountain is illuminated until 9 pm—the golden effect on the leaves is striking. Off-season, come in March for wild violets or in spring for tender green shoots.

⏱ 90 min
2

Push on to Asuke town, 10 minutes from Korankei—its late Edo period houses (1776-1830) make it Aichi's first Traditional Building Preservation District. The Sanshu Asuke Yashiki museum-village offers weaving and indigo dyeing workshops in a mountain setting. Try gohei mochi (grilled rice skewers with sweet-savoury miso sauce)—it's THE local speciality.

⏱ 60 min
3

If you have energy, climb to Asuke Castle, reconstructed based on archaeological excavations—a rarity in Japan. From the keep at 301 meters altitude, the view over the town and forested valley is the area's finest. Allow 40 minutes' walk from Korankei.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Try gohei mochi at the stalls in Korankei or Asuke—these grilled rice skewers glazed with sweet-savoury miso-walnut sauce are the region's mountain speciality. Pair them with an ayu (freshwater trout) grilled on a stick.

⏱ 30 min

Tahara

★★☆☆☆
1

Cape Irago Lighthouse, at the tip of the Atsumi Peninsula, ranks among Japan's 50 finest lighthouses. Built in 1929, it dominates the point where the Pacific Ocean, Ise Bay and Mikawa Bay converge—the 360° panorama is breathtaking. On clear days, you'll glimpse the Shima Peninsula opposite. Come late in the day: the sunset bathes everything in orange.

⏱ 45 min
2

A few minutes from the lighthouse, Koiji Beach ("love road") is a pilgrimage site for Japanese couples. Right next door, the Hinode Stone Gate is a natural rock arch through which the sun rises in October and February—a spectacle photographers await each year.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The Atsumi Peninsula is known for its melon and chrysanthemum cultivation. Depending on the season, you'll find farms offering fruit picking (melons in summer, strawberries in spring). Enquire at the local tourist office.

⏱ 60 min

Tsushima

★★★☆☆
1

The Tsushima Tenno Wisteria Festival is one of Japan's most spectacular: a giant 275-meter-long trellis covering 5,034 square meters, with 12 wisteria varieties forming a purple, white and pink curtain from late April through early May. Walk beneath—the flower clusters brush your hair and the fragrance is intoxicating. In the evening, illuminations transform the trellis into a luminous vault. Outside the season, the park remains pleasant but the appeal is really limited to the wisteria.

⏱ 75 min
2

Tsushima Shrine, neighbouring the wisteria park, is dedicated to the kami of the sea and protection against epidemics. Its summer festival (July) is inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list—lantern-lit boats descend the river.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Tsushima is close to Nagoya—if you have free time, stroll along the canals crisscrossing this former merchant town. Local shops serve kushikatsu (fried breaded skewers) and tenmusu (onigiri with tempura shrimp), Owari region specialities.

⏱ 45 min

Yoro

★★☆☆☆
1

Yoro Falls (養老の滝), ranked among Japan's 100 most beautiful waterfalls, tumbles 30 meters into a rocky basin in the heart of dense forest. Legend has it that a dutiful son discovered a spring whose water turned into sake—Emperor Genshō was so impressed that he renamed the imperial era "Yoro" in 717. The trail from the car park climbs 30 minutes through the forest—in autumn, red maples frame the falls. Arrive early in the morning to avoid weekend crowds.

⏱ 60 min
2

On your way back down, detour to the Site of Reversible Destiny (養老天命反転地), a contemporary art park designed by Arakawa and Madeline Gins. The deliberately destabilising terrain (slopes, uneven ground, colour maze) is meant to "reverse" your destiny. It's disorienting, fun, and completely unique in Japan. Wear flat shoes.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Yoro Park is pleasant for a stroll—between cherry blossoms in spring and maples in autumn, there's always something in bloom. Stalls sell mochi and local green tea.

⏱ 30 min

Seki

★★☆☆☆
1

"Monet's Pond" (モネの池) is actually just an irrigation pond for Nemichi Shrine, but its transparency and water lilies have made it an Instagram sensation. The spring water is so clear that the orange, white and golden koi seem to swim in mid-air above the sandy bottom—the effect recalls Monet's Water Lilies. The best period runs from June to September when the water lilies bloom. Come in the morning: the flowers are open and the low-angle light penetrates the water. The pond is small—allow 15-20 minutes on site.

⏱ 30 min
2

Seki has been Japan's cutlery capital for 700 years—blacksmiths here forged katanas for samurai. Visit the traditional forging museum to see hand-forging demonstrations and understand ancestral techniques. You can also buy professional-quality kitchen knives in the district's shops.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Twice a year (early October and early April), Seki hosts a forging festival with public blade-making demonstrations—flames, sparks and hammering. If your dates align, it's a rare spectacle.

⏱ 60 min

Shirakawa Go

★★★★★
1

Shirakawa-go (UNESCO-listed since 1995) is a mountain village where 114 gassho-zukuri farmhouses survive—literally "hands joined in prayer," referring to the extreme roof pitch designed to bear snowfalls sometimes exceeding 2 meters. Some houses are over 250 years old and built without a single nail, using a wood-joinery system. Start with Wada House, the largest and oldest (over 300 years), designated an Important Cultural Property—climb to the 4th floor to see the space where silkworms were raised.

⏱ 60 min
2

Climb to Shiroyama Observatory (15 minutes' walk from the village) for Shirakawa-go's most famous panorama: thatched roofs aligned in the valley, framed by mountains and rice fields. ⏱ 30 min
3

Myozenji Temple and its two gassho-zukuri annexes form the village's finest ensemble—the temple bell is broadcast by NHK at New Year. The small attached museum traces the history of rural mountain life.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Spend a night in a gassho-zukuri minshuku (family guesthouse)—you'll sleep on futon and tatami, and the kaiseki dinner is prepared with local products: Hida beef, artisanal tofu, river fish. In the early morning, when day-trippers haven't yet arrived, the village is absolutely peaceful.

⏱ 120 min

Try doburoku, a cloudy, unfiltered sake produced locally. Shirakawa Hachiman Shrine hosts a doburoku festival in October—the brew is offered to visitors.

Inabe

★★★☆☆
1

Inabe Plum Grove (いなべ市農業公園 梅林公園) is the Tokai region's largest: 38 hectares, 100 varieties, over 4,000 trees. From early to late March, plum blossoms open in a gradient of red, pink and white—climb to the observatory at the hilltop to embrace the panorama with the Suzuka Mountains as backdrop. The delicate fragrance of ume (plums) perfumes the entire domain. The Ume Matsuri (plum festival) is held in March with plum-product tastings and picking.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Suzuka Mountains rising behind the grove offer accessible hiking trails—Mount Ryozen (1,099 m) is one of the range's most popular. If you don't have time to hike, at least stop at the Inabe River gorges, where turquoise water contrasts with limestone rocks. It's on the route between Shirakawa-go and Inabe.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Take advantage of the festival to try local specialities made from umeboshi (pickled plum) and umeshu (plum liqueur). Some farms offer plum picking that you can take home.

Nabari

★★☆☆☆
1

The 48 Waterfalls of Akame (赤目四十八滝) cascade along 4 km of forested gorge—in reality, there are well over 48, but the "Five Waterfalls of Akame" are the trail's stars. ⏱ 180 min

Activities

⏱ 30 min

After the hike, relax in one of Nabari's onsen—the town has several hot springs along the riverside. Ask your accommodation about baths open to day visitors.

Ise

★★★★☆
1

The Meoto Iwa (Married Rocks) of Futami are two sacred rocks linked by a braided rice-straw shimenawa rope—the larger measures 9 m, the smaller 4 m. From May to July, the sun rises between the two rocks, and in exceptional weather you'll glimpse Mount Fuji on the horizon. The rope is changed three times a year (May, September, December) during a Shinto ceremony. Come at sunrise—it's the most powerful moment, and there's almost no one around.

⏱ 30 min
2

Ise Jingu is Japan's most sacred shrine—dedicated to Amaterasu, sun goddess and ancestor of the imperial family. The Naiku (Inner Shrine) is rebuilt identically to itself every 20 years since the 7th century (the next reconstruction is scheduled for 2033). You won't see the main building, hidden behind palisades, but the path through the centuries-old cedar forest and the Uji Bridge spanning the Isuzu River are themselves a pilgrimage. Allow 1 to 2 hours.

⏱ 90 min
3

Leaving the Naiku, dive into Okage Yokocho, a recreated Edo-style shopping street running alongside the shrine. It's the place to try Ise udon (thick, soft noodles in dark broth), akafuku mochi (rice dumplings coated in red bean paste, a specialty since 1707) and tekone-zushi (marinated bonito sashimi on vinegared rice).

⏱ 45 min

Activities

If time permits, visit the Geku (Outer Shrine), dedicated to the kami of food, located 6 km from the Naiku. Less crowded, it offers an even more contemplative atmosphere. Traditional pilgrimage begins with the Geku before the Naiku.

⏱ 45 min

Shima

★★★☆☆
1

Goza Shirahama Beach is ranked among Japan's 100 best swimming beaches—its white sand and cobalt-blue water evoke the tropics rather than central Japan. The waves are gentle and the depth increases very gradually, making it a good spot for families. It's also popular with surfers when the swell picks up. Open in summer, the beach has showers and changing rooms.

⏱ 120 min
2

Climb to Yokoyama Observatory for a bird's-eye view of Ago Bay and its dozens of pine-covered islets—this is where cultured pearls have been cultivated since Mikimoto succeeded in producing the first cultured pearl in 1893. The ria (indented coastline) landscape is one of the Shima Peninsula's most photographed.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

In Osatsu, you can meet the ama—traditional female divers who have free-dived for abalone, sea urchins and seaweed for centuries. Some fisherwomen's huts (amagoya) welcome visitors for a fireside grilled seafood meal, served by the ama themselves. Book in advance.

⏱ 90 min
