Schlepzig

★★☆☆☆
1

Founded in 1985 in an 1818 farmhouse that's been a listed monument since 1960, the Bauernmuseum Schlepzig is the only museum in the Unterspreewald. Across 5,000 square meters you'll find farming tools from the Kaiser's era through the GDR, a reconstructed living room, and the clay oven where cake is still baked during demonstrations. Open April through October, Tuesday to Sunday from 10 a.m.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Take the towpath along the Spree to reach the heart of Schlepzig village, one of the rare Unterspreewald settlements to have kept its traditional farmhouse fabric intact. Twenty minutes is enough for a complete loop through the lanes.

⏱ 30 min

Lubben

★★★☆☆
1

Touristy, yes, but Lübben's Schlossinsel delivers on its promise. This six-hectare island in the town center brings together the castle museum (with its wappensaal), a water playground, and the SpreeLagune footbridge that stretches 107 meters above the water. Skip the Christmas market weekend—the queues spill onto the bridges.

⏱ 75 min
2

The scent of old timber still lingers in the Paul-Gerhardt-Kirche, a late-Gothic three-nave church named for the local Lutheran poet. The tower platform sits at 22 meters—Vera Städter, Brandenburg and Berlin's only Türmerin since 2011, leads 115 steps of history three times a week. Schedule details at luebben.de.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

You'll find a kahnfährmann first thing in the morning at the Schlossinsel's small harbor. Spree barges carry about twenty people and leave on demand. Budget 90 minutes for a basic loop; the fährmann poles standing, the pole is called a Rudel.

⏱ 90 min

Lubbenau

★★★★☆
1

Kitsch on the outside, but Lübbenau's Spreewald-Museum in the Torbogenhaus on Topfmarkt is worth the hour it asks. The museum recreates a regional town circa 1900 with a colonial grocery, furrier's workshop, and steam locomotive 99 57 03 from the Spreewaldbahn. Founded in 1899 by Paul Fahlisch, who launched tourism in the Spreewald.

⏱ 60 min
2

Lübbenauers simply call it 'der Große Hafen' and gather there every morning as if at the café. Lübbenau's Großer Spreewaldhafen is where Theodor Fontane embarked more than 150 years ago for his walks in the Hochwald. Today the Gurkenmeile lines up pickle producers along the quayside.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

If you have a choice, take the Wotschofska-Tour: 5.5 hours round-trip via motorless fließe to the forest inn on Alder Island. Departure 10:30 a.m., €28 per adult. The 2-hour version at €17 works if the afternoon is packed.

⏱ 330 min

You'll taste pickles from the Gurkenmeile: more than a dozen local producers offer different recipes with garlic, herbs, or mustard. The market runs every morning May through October. A fresh crunchy pickle is something else entirely from what you find in a jar.

⏱ 20 min

Lehde

★★☆☆☆
1

The weathered planks creak, the reed roofs smell of damp hay: Lehde's Freilandmuseum recreates four nineteenth-century farmhouses from different parts of the Spreewald. One of Brandenburg's oldest open-air museums. One family room where seven people slept in a single box bed—the panels present this as a fact hard to imagine today. Open April through October, 10 a.m.–6 p.m.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

You can only reach Lehde's Freilandmuseum on foot or by canoe from Lübbenau—no direct road. The path follows the Lehmgraben for 2 km from the Lübbenau-Ortsteil Lehde parking lot. Along the way, count the stufenholzbrücken, the stepped wooden bridges characteristic of the Spreewald.

⏱ 40 min

Leipe

★★☆☆☆
1

Less famous than Lehde, Leipe is the real island village. Perched on an 800-by-400-meter sandbank surrounded by the Spree and the Leiper Graben, it's home to a handful of residents and wasn't connected to the road network until 1969. Before that, the doctor, the postman, children going to school—everything was done by boat. The Heimatstube displays Sorbian costumes and utensils from another era.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

If you have a canoe or kayak, the fließe are calmest from Leipe. Several Lübbenau rental operators offer return shuttles; count on about 3 hours of paddling one-way from the Großer Hafen for a peaceful arrival in the village.

Burg Spreewald

★★☆☆☆
1

Built from 1915 to 1917 with 1.5 million clinker bricks, Burg's Bismarckturm rises 27 meters on the Schlossberg. During the Second World War the Wehrmacht used it as a radio center; in 1951 it was renamed 'Youth Tower'; it recovered its name on October 3, 1990, during a public celebration. Open April through November; closed in late May for the Spreewälder Sagennacht.

⏱ 40 min
2

You'll cross the Kur- und Sagenpark to reach the other end of Burg. This 60,000-square-meter park stages Sorbian mythology with themed sculptures—water spirits, forest geniuses. More instructive than a simple garden, though the bilingual panels are in German and Lower Sorbian only.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Two hours on a kahn from Burg is considerably quieter than Lübbenau. The Spreehafen Burg is one of the Spreewald's oldest harbors; the Kolonistentour goes up to villages founded by Frederick the Great. Budget around €20 per adult.

⏱ 120 min

Raddusch

★★★★☆
1

The exterior looks like a real ninth-century Slavic fortress—because it's an exact reconstruction. The Slawenburg Raddusch was excavated from 1984 to 1990 before being rebuilt from 1999 to 2003. The ringwall measured 57 meters in diameter, 10 meters wide, and 9 meters high. Inside the concrete shell: 130,000 years of Lower Lusatian archaeology, and the 'Götze von Raddusch,' an oak idol dated 926—unique in Brandenburg. Check the schedule before coming: the Slawenburg closes intermittently.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The 600-meter Zeitsteg runs from the Slawenburg to the shore of the flooding lake: archaeological panels on 130,000 years of human presence in Lower Lusatia. Count on 30 minutes round-trip, much longer if you linger by the Götze's display case.

Cottbus

★★★☆☆
1

Built in 1908 by Bernhard Sehring in just sixteen months, the Staatstheater Cottbus is Lusatia's only Jugendstil theater and Brandenburg's sole state theater. The façade reads like an Art Nouveau manifesto: panther on the roof, nine muses in a frieze. Architectural guided tours take place on Sundays.

⏱ 45 min
2

Cottbus's Spremberger Turm has dominated the Altmarkt since the thirteenth century—31 meters of medieval red brick at the end of the shopping street. The Altmarkt itself is ringed with Saxon Baroque and Classicist gabled houses: cafés are open from 8 a.m., terraces packed in summer.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

You'll stroll around the Altmarkt, surrounded by colorful façades, then head up Burgstraße to the Oberkirche St. Nikolai, the Niederlausitz's largest Gothic church. Cottbus's center can be walked in under an hour; stay on the Burgstraße / Mühlenstraße side for the concentration of cafés and shops.

⏱ 60 min

Branitz

★★★★☆
1

Two pyramids in the middle of a lake: nobody expects that in the Brandenburg plain. Hermann Fürst von Pückler-Muskau had Branitzer Park laid out between 1846 and 1871 on initially flat, arid terrain. The water pyramid is his tomb, covered in vines. The other, on dry land, was intended for his wife Lucie—she was never buried there. Park open year-round, schloss April through October.

⏱ 90 min
2

Inside, Schloss Branitz's Orientzimmer still smell of travel. Pückler, a great traveler, brought back from Egypt and the Near East enough to furnish several rooms in an orientalizing style unique in Brandenburg. The Carl Blechen collection in the restored rooms is, along with Berlin's Alte Nationalgalerie, one of the two largest holdings of this Cottbus-born artist.

⏱ 45 min

Sedlitz

★★★★☆
1

Locals call it 'der rostige Nagel,' the rusty nail—and it truly is. Sedlitz's Landmarke Lausitzer Seenland is a 30-meter Corten steel tower built in 2008 at the mouth of the Sorno canal into the Sedlitzer See. The rust color is intentional: it recalls the machinery of the former tagebau. From the platform you can make out the Sedlitzer See and three other lakes (Geierswalder, Partwitzer, and Großräschener). Free access, year-round.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Just north of the tower, the Schwimmender Steg reaches out over the Sedlitzer See: a floating walkway a few dozen meters long to see the lake at water level, without the 162 steps. Short but effective before heading out.

Senftenberg

★★★☆☆
1

A Renaissance fortress serving as a mining museum: few places combine that. Senftenberg's Schloss und Festung dates from the sixteenth century and now houses the Museum des Landkreises Oberspreewald-Lausitz. The showpiece is in the cellars: a full-scale reconstructed mine gallery shows coal extraction as it was practiced last century. Closed Mondays.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Senftenberger See deserves a stop: a former tagebau converted into a lake in the late 1960s, it now offers 18 kilometers of shoreline with fine sandy beaches and a stadthafen with an 80-meter seebrücke over the water. In summer, sailboats and paddleboarders share the water with rental electric boats.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

You'll embark from the Stadthafen to sail to the neighboring Geierswalder See via the Koschener canal. Electric boat rentals are on-site; count on 2–3 hours for a leisurely cruise and €15–25 depending on boat size.

⏱ 150 min

Lichterfeld

★★★★☆
1

At 80 meters up, the wind cuts through the structure and the rivets rumble: Lichterfeld's Abraumförderbrücke F60 is as much a physical experience as a historical one. Five hundred and two meters long and weighing 11,000 tons, the F60 operated for only 13 months in 1991–92 before being shut down for economic reasons. Nicknamed 'the reclining Eiffel Tower of Lusatia.' Ninety-minute guided tours starting at 11:30 a.m.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Bergheider See adjoins the F60: a body of water born from flooding the same tagebau, with a fine sandy beach accessible on foot from the parking lot. Twenty minutes' walk under the structure to finish the itinerary with your feet in the water.

⏱ 40 min
