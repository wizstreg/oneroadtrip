Kick off your Dublin stay at Temple Bar Food Market on Meeting House Square. Founded in 1997 by a collective of Irish farmers, this Saturday market runs from 10am to 4:30pm beneath a retractable canopy that shields you from the rain. Ride the lift to the Gravity Bar at the top: your complimentary pint comes with a 360° panorama across Dublin's rooftops. Allow 2 to 3 hours and book online to skip the queues.

⏱ 120 min
3

On arrival in Kilkenny, head straight for Parliament Street and the Smithwick's Experience. Franciscan monks brewed beer here from the 13th century at St Francis Abbey. In 1710 a man named John Smithwick founded his own brewery on the site—under the Penal Laws, he wasn't even allowed to display his name on the façade. The guided tour lasts roughly an hour: you'll mill malt, stir the mash and inhale the hops before finishing with a pint of red ale. The farm runs a charming café where you can taste cheese boards with house-made chutneys, plus a small petting farm with ducks, hens and miniature ponies. English Market Princes Street CorkBallymaloe Cookery School Shanagarry CorkMidleton Distillery Experience Midleton Cork

Cork's English Market has been the city's beating heart since 1788—one of the world's oldest municipal covered markets. The name comes from the Protestant corporation that established it, to distinguish it from the neighbouring 'Irish market'. Beneath red-brick arches and stained-glass skylights you'll find specialities you won't see anywhere else: drisheen (local black pudding), Cork spiced beef, buttered eggs and tripe. Chef Rick Stein called it the finest covered market in the British Isles. Head upstairs to the Farmgate Café, which cooks straight from the stalls below. Queen Elizabeth II made a point of visiting this market during her historic 2011 trip.

⏱ 90 min
2

If time allows, make the 40-minute detour to Shanagarry to explore Ballymaloe Cookery School, one of Ireland's most celebrated culinary schools, set on a 100-acre estate with organic vegetable gardens, orchards and glasshouses. Half-day classes are open to enthusiasts—book well in advance.

⏱ 90 min
3

Continue to Midleton (25 km east of Cork) for the distillery that produces Jameson. The 15-acre site started life as a woollen mill before being converted into a military barracks, then a distillery in 1825 by the Murphy brothers. You'll see the world's largest copper pot still—so enormous they had to assemble it on-site and build the warehouse around it. The guided tour takes you through oak-cask maturation cellars and ends with a tasting commentary. Go for the premium package if you'd like to sample rare expressions such as Redbreast or Midleton Very Rare.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

⏱ 120 min

Finish the day with an artisan cider tasting. County Cork is home to several producers who work local apples using traditional methods—slow fermentation, no additives. You'll discover dry, off-dry and sparkling ciders that bear no resemblance to industrial versions.

You'll smell the oak smoke as soon as you cross the threshold. The free visitor centre explains hot- and cold-smoking processes through videos in six languages and mosaics retelling the legend of the Salmon of Knowledge. The Swedish royal family visited in person in 2019 to taste their salmon. Just next door, don't miss the 'Taste the Atlantic – Salmon Experience', an interactive trail launched the same year.

⏱ 75 min
2

Continue to Ballyvaughan, 20 minutes from Lisdoonvarna, to visit Burren Gold Cheese dairy, housed in the Farm Shop at the Aillwee site. Since 1985 they've been making farmhouse gouda from the milk of a 65-strong herd of Holstein Friesians that graze on the wild grasses and flowers of the Burren, 5 km away. The cheesemaker milks the cows just once a day—which yields richer, creamier milk. You'll taste six variants: plain, oak-smoked (one of Ireland's last natural smokehouses), cumin, garlic-nettle, black pepper and piri-piri. Wheels aged 1 or 2 years develop surprising caramel and hazelnut notes. The cheese holds the GEOfood label of the Burren UNESCO Global Geopark—from field to plate, everything is produced within a few kilometres.

⏱ 60 min
3

If you're passing on a Friday or Saturday, detour via Ennis farmers market, 30 minutes south of Doolin. You'll meet Clare producers selling cheeses, smoked meats, breads and honey in the typical atmosphere of an Irish market town, far quieter than Dublin or Cork markets.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Join an edible-seaweed foraging session on the Burren coast. A guide will take you at low tide to identify dulse (reddish, nutty taste), sea lettuce and carrageen used as a natural gelling agent. You'll learn how to cook them—in salads, as seasoning or in seaweed butter. Bring rubber boots.

⏱ 90 min

Finish the evening with a seafood-chowder crawl through Doolin's pubs. This tiny village boasts three historic pubs renowned for traditional music and chowder—a thick, creamy seafood soup. Compare recipes from pub to pub: some add salmon, others crab, all serve a chunk of soda bread for mopping. The musical atmosphere in the evening is an incomparable bonus.

Day 7

Galway

Ireland's oyster throne, where Sheridans cheese reigns and September feasts the sea
Galway Market Church Lane GalwayGalway Atlantaquaria Salthill GalwaySheridans Cheesemongers Churchyard Street Galway
✈️ Book a flight🚗 Rent a car
1

Galway is the gastronomic capital of the west of Ireland. Start at Saturday's market on Church Lane, right in the city centre: Clarinbridge oysters, Connemara cheeses, artisan breads, crêpes and coffee from local roasters. Galway's bohemian spirit runs through every stall. If you're after a memorable meal, several city-centre restaurants build their menus around short supply chains with market producers.

⏱ 75 min
2

Swing by Galway Atlantaquaria in Salthill, Ireland's largest aquarium. You'll see Atlantic-coast species—rays, conger eels, lobsters, jellyfish—and understand the marine ecosystem that feeds all the region's seafood cooking. Kids love the touch pool where they can handle starfish and sea urchins.

⏱ 60 min
3

After the market, step into Sheridans Cheesemongers on Churchyard Street, right beside St Nicholas's Church. It all began in 1995 with a simple cheese stall at Saturday's market—today it's Ireland's most celebrated cheesemonger. The shop, long and narrow, stacks floor-to-ceiling wheels of Dutch gouda, comté, aged Irish cheddar and creamy blues. Ask the cheesemongers for advice: they'll let you sample half a dozen Irish farmhouse cheeses with anecdotes about each producer. Then climb upstairs to the wine bar for a cheese-and-charcuterie board with a glass of natural wine—the perfect spot for a light lunch facing the medieval church.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

If you're in Galway in late September, you'll land right in the middle of the International Oyster & Seafood Festival, the world's oldest oyster festival (founded in 1954). On the programme: oyster-opening competitions, tastings, concerts and street parades. Even outside festival season, Galway remains Ireland's best city for eating oysters—ask for Clarinbridge natives.

⏱ 120 min

Treat yourself to an oyster-and-champagne tasting class. Several operators in Galway offer lessons in opening oysters yourself, recognising varieties (flat, rock, wild) and pairing them with bubbles or a glass of stout. Reckon on roughly 90 minutes for the full session.

Day 8

Clifden

Connemara's colourful capital, framed by bog-grazed lamb and pier-smoked salmon
Clifden town centre ClifdenClifden Saturday Market ClifdenConnemara Smokehouse Bunowen Pier Ballyconneely
✈️ Book a flight🚗 Rent a car
1

Clifden is the 'capital of Connemara', a small, colourful town wedged between mountain and ocean. Start with a stroll along Main Street and Market Street—the windows of fishmongers and delicatessens overflow with smoked salmon, fresh crab and artisan pâtés. Several seafood restaurants display the day's catch on a chalkboard at the door.

⏱ 60 min
2

If you're there on a Saturday, Clifden's artisan market gathers local producers and craftspeople: goat's cheeses, sourdough breads, wild-berry jams, Connemara honey. It's small but authentic—a far cry from tourist markets.

⏱ 45 min
3

Fifteen minutes south of Clifden, on the Ballyconneely road, stop at Connemara Smokehouse at the end of Bunowen pier. It's the oldest smokery in Connemara, founded in 1979 by the Roberts family. The original smokehouse, christened 'Old Smoky', dates from 1946 and still runs on beech wood. You'll watch the master smoker fillet salmon by hand—he cuts 25 to 30 sides an hour, with a precision impossible to tell from a machine. Taste the cold-smoked wild salmon, smoked line-caught tuna (which chef Rick Stein serves at his Padstow restaurant in Cornwall) and gravlax cured in Irish whiskey. The view from the pier across the Twelve Bens and Grace O'Malley's castle is worth the trip alone. Guided tours in summer by reservation.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Attend a salmon-smoking workshop in the Clifden or Connemara area. You'll learn the steps—sea-salt brining, air-drying, slow smoking over oak or beech wood—and leave with your own piece of smoked salmon. These workshops are usually run by small, passionate producers who take the time to explain each gesture.

⏱ 120 min

Visit a Connemara lamb farm. Connemara lamb has European PGI status—the sheep graze freely on heather and bog-covered hills, giving the meat a unique herbal flavour. Some farms welcome visitors for a walk with the shepherd and his dogs, followed by a tasting of lamb dishes.

Westport

Sourdough mornings, Belgian-Irish brewing and Clew Bay oysters beneath Croagh Patrick
Cornrue Bakery Westport MayoWestport Country Market James Street WestportMescan Brewery Kilsallagh Westport

1

Step into Cornrue artisan bakery, on the heights above Westport. Founded in 2017, it started in an oven borrowed from a local restaurant, baking through the night. Today the team starts each morning at 6:15am to produce organic-flour sourdough loaves, scones and brown bread. The bread sells fast—arrive before 10am for the full selection. The shop also stocks seasonal vegetables, homemade jams and natural wines.

⏱ 45 min
2

Westport Country Market runs every Thursday from 8:30am to 1pm at the Pete Callaghan Centre near James Street. You'll meet Mayo producers selling artisan cheeses, honey, charcuterie and home-baked cakes. On Saturdays it's the Food & Craft Market on the Mall that takes over, from 9am to 5pm, with an even wider choice. Adapt your day to the right market.

⏱ 60 min
3

Ten minutes from Westport, at the foot of Croagh Patrick, Mescan Brewery is a microbrewery founded in 2013 by two retrained vets: Cillian, Irish, and Bart, Belgian. Their water comes from a spring that rises beneath the holy mountain. They brew Belgian-inspired beers—saison, tripel, dubbel, and even a lambic aged 3 years in oak casks. The informal tour lasts roughly 90 minutes, with tastings of seven beers and snacks. Cillian and Bart love recounting how they left veterinary medicine to launch into beer. Book ahead.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Embark on an oyster-farming trip on Clew Bay, the bay off Westport that harbours more than 300 islands and a long shellfish tradition. Local producers have cultivated oysters and mussels in these cold waters since the 1980s. Some offer tours of their oyster beds by boat, followed by a tasting on the quay with soda bread and a glass of stout. The view of Croagh Patrick from the bay is spectacular—hard to imagine a finer setting for eating oysters.

⏱ 120 min

Before hitting the road back to Dublin, detour via Killary Harbour, Ireland's only fjord, 40 minutes south of Westport. Mussels have been farmed here in open water for decades, fed by Atlantic currents. Operators offer boat trips on the fjord, framed by 700-metre mountains, with tastings of smoked and fresh mussels straight on the water. If you prefer dry land, a roadside food truck serves fjord mussels in a relaxed atmosphere facing the landscape.

For your final day, treat yourself to lunch at one of Dublin's fine-dining restaurants. Several tables in the city centre, around Parnell Square, offer tasting menus built around Ireland's finest producers—the same ones you've met throughout the trip. ⏱ 120 min
2

Stop by Fallon & Byrne, the food hall on Exchequer Street. This three-storey space—delicatessen on the ground floor, wine cellar in the basement, restaurant upstairs—gathers the best of Irish and European produce. Aged cheeses, artisan charcuterie, breads, pastries, olive oils... It's the perfect place to assemble a hamper of specialities to take home.

⏱ 60 min
3

Finish with a visit to Pearse Lyons Distillery, housed in the former St James's Church, a stone's throw from the Guinness Storehouse on James's Street. The 12th-century church has been beautifully restored and now shelters copper stills amid stained glass. The tour tells the story of Dublin distilling—once home to 37 distilleries—and ends with a tasting in the nave. A spectacular setting, at the crossroads of the sacred and the spirited.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Join an Irish-cocktails workshop at one of the city-centre cocktail bars. Reckon on roughly 90 minutes for the session, relaxed atmosphere guaranteed.

⏱ 90 min
