Ireland Food Trail: Farmers Markets and Artisan Distilleries

📅 11 days📍 10 stops🚗 942 km⏱ 13h30

From Dublin to the Atlantic coast, this 10-day circuit takes you to meet the farmers markets, craft distilleries and producers who are the pride of Irish gastronomy. Taste Burren smoked salmon, Galway oysters, Kilkenny cheeses and Midleton whiskey. Learn to brew, smoke, ferment and cook alongside passionate artisans.

✨ Highlights
  • Guinness Storehouse: seven immersive floors with tasting at the panoramic Gravity Bar, 9,000-year lease signed in 1759
  • Cork's English Market: covered market since 1788, visited by Queen Elizabeth II in 2011
  • Dingle Distillery: micro-distillery founded in 2012, gin infused with wild peninsula botanicals
  • Burren Smokehouse: family smokery since 1989, visited by the Swedish royal couple in 2019
📆 Best period

May, June, September

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 14 and 18 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Summer (June-August): Warmest weather, longest days, peak tourist season, highest prices, crowded attractions, festival season, vibrant atmosphere, advance booking essential.

Shoulder Season (April-May & September-October): Mild weather, fewer crowds, good value, comfortable temperatures, pleasant conditions, excellent photography, ideal balance.

Spring (March-May): Mild temperatures, blooming landscapes, fewer tourists, reasonable prices, longer days, fresh atmosphere, excellent touring.

Fall (September-November): Comfortable weather, harvest season, beautiful colors, fewer crowds, good value, cozy atmosphere, cultural events.

Winter (December-February): Cool wet weather, shortest days, lowest prices, minimal crowds, cozy pubs, indoor attractions, festive atmosphere.

🌍 What to discover in IE?

Emerald Isle with stunning landscapes, rich literary heritage, and warm hospitality. Experience traditional music, beautiful countryside, and authentic Irish culture. The country offers cultural depth with natural beauty and excellent storytelling traditions. Ireland provides cultural richness with natural wonders and warm hospitality.

📋 Important notes

Temple Bar Market runs on Saturdays only – plan Day 1 accordingly. Kenmare Farmers Market is held on Wednesdays. Westport Country Market is on Thursdays. Westport Food & Craft Market is on Saturdays. Book distillery visits in advance during high season.

Day 1

Dublin

Where Temple Bar market stalls meet Guinness heritage and Moore Street's cosmopolitan bustle

★★★★★
1

Kick off your Dublin stay at Temple Bar Food Market on Meeting House Square. Founded in 1997 by a collective of Irish farmers, this Saturday market runs from 10am to 4:30pm beneath a retractable canopy that shields you from the rain. You'll meet producers who've held their stalls since day one: sourdough bakers, farmhouse cheesemakers, artisan butchers and cold-pressed juice vendors. Arrive early—the sourdough sells out fast.

⏱ 75 min
2

Next, step inside the Guinness Storehouse at St James's Gate. The 1904 fermentation building was reimagined as a museum in 2000, its seven storeys shaped like a giant pint glass. On the ground floor, beneath a glass panel, you'll spot the 9,000-year lease signed by Arthur Guinness in 1759—for £45 a year. Ride the lift to the Gravity Bar at the top: your complimentary pint comes with a 360° panorama across Dublin's rooftops. Allow 2 to 3 hours and book online to skip the queues.

⏱ 120 min
3

Finish with a stroll down Moore Street, Dublin's oldest open-air market, trading since the 18th century. The street carries serious history—this is where the leaders of the 1916 Easter Rising, including Patrick Pearse, surrendered to British forces. Today, fruit-and-veg stalls jostle with Chinese grocers, African spice vendors and Indian sweet shops—a snapshot of modern, cosmopolitan Dublin. Don't miss the handmade dumplings and Bolivian plates tucked away in the basement mall.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Join an Irish cookery class to master the basics of soda bread, colcannon and boxty—dishes that look straightforward but require a deft hand. Several city-centre workshops offer 2- to 3-hour sessions in small groups, with all kit and ingredients provided. Book ahead, especially at weekends.

⏱ 120 min

In late afternoon, let a food-tour guide lead you through Temple Bar. You'll sample oysters, farmhouse cheeses and charcuterie while learning Dublin's culinary backstory, weaving between cobbled lanes and centuries-old pubs. Come hungry and wear comfortable shoes.

⏱ 150 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 76 km · 1h05
Day 2

Kilkenny

Medieval brewhouse turned red-ale shrine and award-winning farmhouse cheeses

★★★★☆
1

On arrival in Kilkenny, head straight for Parliament Street and the Smithwick's Experience. Franciscan monks brewed beer here from the 13th century at St Francis Abbey. In 1710 a man named John Smithwick founded his own brewery on the site—under the Penal Laws, he wasn't even allowed to display his name on the façade. The guided tour lasts roughly an hour: you'll mill malt, stir the mash and inhale the hops before finishing with a pint of red ale. If you're keen, opt for the Masters of Ale package to compare three different ales side by side.

⏱ 75 min
2

Wander through Kilkenny's farmers market, held in the medieval heart of town a stone's throw from the castle. You'll meet county producers selling cheeses, artisan breads, local honey and homemade jams. The atmosphere is friendly, and the stallholders love sharing the stories behind their goods.

⏱ 60 min
3

Drive 15 minutes south to Stoneyford to visit Knockdrinna, an artisan cheese dairy founded in 2004 by Helen Finnegan. She crafts goat's-, sheep's- and cow's-milk cheeses, including Kilree—a washed-rind goat's cheese that won Supreme Champion at the British Cheese Awards. The farm runs a charming café where you can taste cheese boards with house-made chutneys, plus a small petting farm with ducks, hens and miniature ponies. Full-day cheesemaking courses are available—well worth booking if the subject grips you.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Treat yourself to an Irish cheese-tasting workshop in Kilkenny town. Several local producers run guided sessions where you'll learn to tell an aged cheddar from a creamy blue, a fresh chèvre from a hard sheep's-milk cheese. Reckon on an hour, with bread-jam-cheese pairings that will reconcile you with Ireland's terroir.

⏱ 60 min

Visit an organic farm on the outskirts of Kilkenny. The county teems with small holdings that practise sustainable agriculture: free-range livestock, organic market gardens, wildlife hedgerows for biodiversity. Some farms welcome visitors for a guided walk through the fields, often with fresh-produce tastings to finish.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 110 km · 1h35
Day 3

Cork

A Georgian market hall that's fed Cork since 1788, plus Ireland's whiskey heartland

★★★★☆
1

Cork's English Market has been the city's beating heart since 1788—one of the world's oldest municipal covered markets. The name comes from the Protestant corporation that established it, to distinguish it from the neighbouring 'Irish market'. Beneath red-brick arches and stained-glass skylights you'll find specialities you won't see anywhere else: drisheen (local black pudding), Cork spiced beef, buttered eggs and tripe. Chef Rick Stein called it the finest covered market in the British Isles. Head upstairs to the Farmgate Café, which cooks straight from the stalls below. Queen Elizabeth II made a point of visiting this market during her historic 2011 trip.

⏱ 90 min
2

If time allows, make the 40-minute detour to Shanagarry to explore Ballymaloe Cookery School, one of Ireland's most celebrated culinary schools, set on a 100-acre estate with organic vegetable gardens, orchards and glasshouses. Half-day classes are open to enthusiasts—book well in advance.

⏱ 90 min
3

Continue to Midleton (25 km east of Cork) for the distillery that produces Jameson. The 15-acre site started life as a woollen mill before being converted into a military barracks, then a distillery in 1825 by the Murphy brothers. You'll see the world's largest copper pot still—so enormous they had to assemble it on-site and build the warehouse around it. The guided tour takes you through oak-cask maturation cellars and ends with a tasting commentary. Go for the premium package if you'd like to sample rare expressions such as Redbreast or Midleton Very Rare.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Treat yourself to an Irish baking class to perfect the scone, porter cake (stout cake) and proper Irish apple tart. Two-hour workshops run in Cork city centre and the surrounding area, often led by chefs trained at Ballymaloe.

⏱ 120 min

Finish the day with an artisan cider tasting. County Cork is home to several producers who work local apples using traditional methods—slow fermentation, no additives. You'll discover dry, off-dry and sparkling ciders that bear no resemblance to industrial versions.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 96 km · 1h23
Day 4

Kenmare

French chocolatier meets Atlantic oysters in this elegant Kerry gateway

★★★☆☆
1

Kenmare Farmers Market runs every Wednesday on the town's central square. Time your visit accordingly: the stalls sell local farmhouse cheeses, sourdough breads, smoked fish and organic vegetables grown in peninsula gardens. The atmosphere is laid-back, and producers love talking through their methods.

⏱ 75 min
2

Around Kenmare and Sneem, several artisan cheese dairies are worth a detour. They produce farmhouse cheeses from Jersey cow's milk in small batches—plain, aged, cumin or garlic-flavoured—sold mainly at local markets and to area restaurants. Check with Kenmare's tourist office to find out which dairies welcome visitors on the day you're passing through.

⏱ 60 min
3

Don't leave Kenmare without visiting Lorge chocolatier, located in Bonane 5 km along the Glengarriff road. Benoît Lorge, a French pâtissier from Lorraine, trained across the world—Corsica, southern France, Scotland—before settling in Kerry. He handcrafts rum-caramel bonbons, whiskey truffles and paprika pralines that supply Ireland's finest hotels. You assemble your own box by choosing each chocolate from the display counters—a pleasure in itself. Ninety-minute chocolate workshops are available for families and groups, and kids love them.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Treat yourself to an oyster tasting on Kenmare Bay. The bay's cold, nutrient-rich waters produce wild oysters with firm, briny flesh. Several operators offer tastings with white wine or stout pairings, right on the shoreline. If you're there late in the day, the light across the bay is superb.

⏱ 60 min

Make the most of Kerry's wild landscape with a wild-berry and edible-herb foraging session led by a local guide. Blackberries, sloes, wild garlic and mushrooms are easily found in the peninsula's hedgerows and woodlands, depending on the season. It's an original way to understand local cuisine through its humblest ingredients.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 72 km · 1h02
Day 5

Dingle

Where wild botanicals flavour craft gin and peninsula waves shape the catch

★★★★☆
1

Dingle Distillery, founded in 2012 in a former sawmill on Milltown Road, is one of the first of Ireland's new wave of micro-distilleries. It uses three bespoke copper pot stills made in Scotland, with a boil ball that encourages reflux and gives the spirit remarkable smoothness. The gin is infused with local botanicals: rowan berries, fuchsia, bog myrtle, hawthorn and heather—a pure distillation of the peninsula. The guided tour runs roughly 75 minutes, with tastings of whiskey, gin and vodka. Book online, especially in summer.

⏱ 80 min
2

Walk down to Dingle harbour to discover the fish market. Boats come in each morning with crab, lobster, mackerel and scallops caught in the bay. The atmosphere is raw and authentic—a long way from polished display cases. If you're there on a Friday, the Dingle Farmers Market on the square also offers local cheeses, artisan bread and ready meals.

⏱ 60 min
3

Stop at Murphy's Ice Cream on Strand Street, a local institution that makes its ice cream from 100% Irish ingredients: Kerry milk, Dingle sea salt, brown bread, salted-butter caramel. Try the Dingle Sea Salt or Brown Bread flavour—tastes you won't find anywhere else. The shop is tiny and often has a queue in summer, but it's worth the wait.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Join an Irish fish-and-chips workshop. Local operators in Dingle offer lessons in battering fish in beer batter, frying perfect chips and mastering mushy peas (mashed peas). You'll leave with simple recipes and cooking tips.

⏱ 90 min

If the weather plays along, board a fishing boat departing Dingle harbour. You'll watch traditional fishing techniques, with any luck spot dolphins in the bay, and might even return with fresh mackerel you can grill that evening. Bring warm, waterproof clothing—even in summer, the wind blows hard offshore.

⏱ 150 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 146 km · 2h05
Day 6

Doolin

Smokehouse salmon kissed by Atlantic winds, limestone pastures and trad-music nights

★★★☆☆
1

Stop in Lisdoonvarna, 10 minutes from Doolin, to visit the Burren Smokehouse. This family-run artisan smokery, founded in 1989 by Birgitta and Peter Curtin, produces organic smoked salmon that has won Great Taste Awards. You'll smell the oak smoke as soon as you cross the threshold. The free visitor centre explains hot- and cold-smoking processes through videos in six languages and mosaics retelling the legend of the Salmon of Knowledge. The Swedish royal family visited in person in 2019 to taste their salmon. Just next door, don't miss the 'Taste the Atlantic – Salmon Experience', an interactive trail launched the same year.

⏱ 75 min
2

Continue to Ballyvaughan, 20 minutes from Lisdoonvarna, to visit Burren Gold Cheese dairy, housed in the Farm Shop at the Aillwee site. Since 1985 they've been making farmhouse gouda from the milk of a 65-strong herd of Holstein Friesians that graze on the wild grasses and flowers of the Burren, 5 km away. The cheesemaker milks the cows just once a day—which yields richer, creamier milk. You'll taste six variants: plain, oak-smoked (one of Ireland's last natural smokehouses), cumin, garlic-nettle, black pepper and piri-piri. Wheels aged 1 or 2 years develop surprising caramel and hazelnut notes. The cheese holds the GEOfood label of the Burren UNESCO Global Geopark—from field to plate, everything is produced within a few kilometres.

⏱ 60 min
3

If you're passing on a Friday or Saturday, detour via Ennis farmers market, 30 minutes south of Doolin. You'll meet Clare producers selling cheeses, smoked meats, breads and honey in the typical atmosphere of an Irish market town, far quieter than Dublin or Cork markets.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Join an edible-seaweed foraging session on the Burren coast. A guide will take you at low tide to identify dulse (reddish, nutty taste), sea lettuce and carrageen used as a natural gelling agent. You'll learn how to cook them—in salads, as seasoning or in seaweed butter. Bring rubber boots.

⏱ 90 min

Finish the evening with a seafood-chowder crawl through Doolin's pubs. This tiny village boasts three historic pubs renowned for traditional music and chowder—a thick, creamy seafood soup. Compare recipes from pub to pub: some add salmon, others crab, all serve a chunk of soda bread for mopping. The musical atmosphere in the evening is an incomparable bonus.

⏱ 120 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 62 km · 54min
Day 7

Galway

Ireland's oyster throne, where Sheridans cheese reigns and September feasts the sea

★★★★☆
1

Galway is the gastronomic capital of the west of Ireland. Start at Saturday's market on Church Lane, right in the city centre: Clarinbridge oysters, Connemara cheeses, artisan breads, crêpes and coffee from local roasters. Galway's bohemian spirit runs through every stall. If you're after a memorable meal, several city-centre restaurants build their menus around short supply chains with market producers.

⏱ 75 min
2

Swing by Galway Atlantaquaria in Salthill, Ireland's largest aquarium. You'll see Atlantic-coast species—rays, conger eels, lobsters, jellyfish—and understand the marine ecosystem that feeds all the region's seafood cooking. Kids love the touch pool where they can handle starfish and sea urchins.

⏱ 60 min
3

After the market, step into Sheridans Cheesemongers on Churchyard Street, right beside St Nicholas's Church. It all began in 1995 with a simple cheese stall at Saturday's market—today it's Ireland's most celebrated cheesemonger. The shop, long and narrow, stacks floor-to-ceiling wheels of Dutch gouda, comté, aged Irish cheddar and creamy blues. Ask the cheesemongers for advice: they'll let you sample half a dozen Irish farmhouse cheeses with anecdotes about each producer. Then climb upstairs to the wine bar for a cheese-and-charcuterie board with a glass of natural wine—the perfect spot for a light lunch facing the medieval church.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

If you're in Galway in late September, you'll land right in the middle of the International Oyster & Seafood Festival, the world's oldest oyster festival (founded in 1954). On the programme: oyster-opening competitions, tastings, concerts and street parades. Even outside festival season, Galway remains Ireland's best city for eating oysters—ask for Clarinbridge natives.

⏱ 120 min

Treat yourself to an oyster-and-champagne tasting class. Several operators in Galway offer lessons in opening oysters yourself, recognising varieties (flat, rock, wild) and pairing them with bubbles or a glass of stout. Reckon on roughly 90 minutes for the full session.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 79 km · 1h08
Day 8

Clifden

Connemara's colourful capital, framed by bog-grazed lamb and pier-smoked salmon

★★★★☆
1

Clifden is the 'capital of Connemara', a small, colourful town wedged between mountain and ocean. Start with a stroll along Main Street and Market Street—the windows of fishmongers and delicatessens overflow with smoked salmon, fresh crab and artisan pâtés. Several seafood restaurants display the day's catch on a chalkboard at the door.

⏱ 60 min
2

If you're there on a Saturday, Clifden's artisan market gathers local producers and craftspeople: goat's cheeses, sourdough breads, wild-berry jams, Connemara honey. It's small but authentic—a far cry from tourist markets.

⏱ 45 min
3

Fifteen minutes south of Clifden, on the Ballyconneely road, stop at Connemara Smokehouse at the end of Bunowen pier. It's the oldest smokery in Connemara, founded in 1979 by the Roberts family. The original smokehouse, christened 'Old Smoky', dates from 1946 and still runs on beech wood. You'll watch the master smoker fillet salmon by hand—he cuts 25 to 30 sides an hour, with a precision impossible to tell from a machine. Taste the cold-smoked wild salmon, smoked line-caught tuna (which chef Rick Stein serves at his Padstow restaurant in Cornwall) and gravlax cured in Irish whiskey. The view from the pier across the Twelve Bens and Grace O'Malley's castle is worth the trip alone. Guided tours in summer by reservation.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Attend a salmon-smoking workshop in the Clifden or Connemara area. You'll learn the steps—sea-salt brining, air-drying, slow smoking over oak or beech wood—and leave with your own piece of smoked salmon. These workshops are usually run by small, passionate producers who take the time to explain each gesture.

⏱ 120 min

Visit a Connemara lamb farm. Connemara lamb has European PGI status—the sheep graze freely on heather and bog-covered hills, giving the meat a unique herbal flavour. Some farms welcome visitors for a walk with the shepherd and his dogs, followed by a tasting of lamb dishes.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 84 km · 1h12
Day 9

Westport

Sourdough mornings, Belgian-Irish brewing and Clew Bay oysters beneath Croagh Patrick

★★☆☆☆
1

Step into Cornrue artisan bakery, on the heights above Westport. Founded in 2017, it started in an oven borrowed from a local restaurant, baking through the night. Today the team starts each morning at 6:15am to produce organic-flour sourdough loaves, scones and brown bread. The bread sells fast—arrive before 10am for the full selection. The shop also stocks seasonal vegetables, homemade jams and natural wines.

⏱ 45 min
2

Westport Country Market runs every Thursday from 8:30am to 1pm at the Pete Callaghan Centre near James Street. You'll meet Mayo producers selling artisan cheeses, honey, charcuterie and home-baked cakes. On Saturdays it's the Food & Craft Market on the Mall that takes over, from 9am to 5pm, with an even wider choice. Adapt your day to the right market.

⏱ 60 min
3

Ten minutes from Westport, at the foot of Croagh Patrick, Mescan Brewery is a microbrewery founded in 2013 by two retrained vets: Cillian, Irish, and Bart, Belgian. Their water comes from a spring that rises beneath the holy mountain. They brew Belgian-inspired beers—saison, tripel, dubbel, and even a lambic aged 3 years in oak casks. The informal tour lasts roughly 90 minutes, with tastings of seven beers and snacks. Cillian and Bart love recounting how they left veterinary medicine to launch into beer. Book ahead.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Embark on an oyster-farming trip on Clew Bay, the bay off Westport that harbours more than 300 islands and a long shellfish tradition. Local producers have cultivated oysters and mussels in these cold waters since the 1980s. Some offer tours of their oyster beds by boat, followed by a tasting on the quay with soda bread and a glass of stout. The view of Croagh Patrick from the bay is spectacular—hard to imagine a finer setting for eating oysters.

⏱ 120 min

Before hitting the road back to Dublin, detour via Killary Harbour, Ireland's only fjord, 40 minutes south of Westport. Mussels have been farmed here in open water for decades, fed by Atlantic currents. Operators offer boat trips on the fjord, framed by 700-metre mountains, with tastings of smoked and fresh mussels straight on the water. If you prefer dry land, a roadside food truck serves fjord mussels in a relaxed atmosphere facing the landscape.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 217 km · 3h06
Day 10

Dublin

Where Temple Bar market stalls meet Guinness heritage and Moore Street's cosmopolitan bustle

★★★★★
1

For your final day, treat yourself to lunch at one of Dublin's fine-dining restaurants. Several tables in the city centre, around Parnell Square, offer tasting menus built around Ireland's finest producers—the same ones you've met throughout the trip. If you book ahead, you can discover cuisine that elevates Irish terroir ingredients with finesse and creativity.

⏱ 120 min
2

Stop by Fallon & Byrne, the food hall on Exchequer Street. This three-storey space—delicatessen on the ground floor, wine cellar in the basement, restaurant upstairs—gathers the best of Irish and European produce. Aged cheeses, artisan charcuterie, breads, pastries, olive oils... It's the perfect place to assemble a hamper of specialities to take home.

⏱ 60 min
3

Finish with a visit to Pearse Lyons Distillery, housed in the former St James's Church, a stone's throw from the Guinness Storehouse on James's Street. The 12th-century church has been beautifully restored and now shelters copper stills amid stained glass. The tour tells the story of Dublin distilling—once home to 37 distilleries—and ends with a tasting in the nave. A spectacular setting, at the crossroads of the sacred and the spirited.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Join an Irish-cocktails workshop at one of the city-centre cocktail bars. You'll learn to prepare a proper Irish Coffee (the secret: warm whiskey, unsweetened whipped cream), a Dingle-gin cocktail or an Irish whiskey Old Fashioned. Reckon on roughly 90 minutes for the session, relaxed atmosphere guaranteed.

⏱ 90 min

Close your food trail with dinner at a restaurant that celebrates Irish produce. After 10 days touring the country's markets, farms and workshops, you'll recognise on the menu the cheeses, meats, fish and beers you discovered along the route. The best possible summary of this journey.
