Epidaurus

★★★★☆
1

The Theatre of Epidaurus is arguably the most spectacular ancient theatre still standing anywhere in the world. Built in the 4th or early 3rd century BC on the slopes of Mount Kynortion, it originally had 34 rows of seats holding 6,200 spectators — the Romans later added 21 more rows, bringing capacity to nearly 14,000. Walk to the centre of the orchestra (the circular stone slab on the ground) and drop a coin: you'll hear it ring from the very last row, 60 metres away. Researchers at the Georgia Institute of Technology found that the limestone seats act as a natural acoustic filter, dampening low-frequency background noise while letting voices travel unamplified. Arrive in the morning to beat the tour groups that flood in after 10 am.

⏱ 60 min
2

Five hundred metres from the theatre, the Sanctuary of Asklepios served for six centuries as the foremost healing centre in the Greek world. Pilgrims journeyed from across the Mediterranean to practise incubation — sleeping in the abaton (a sacred dormitory) in the hope that the god of medicine would appear in their dreams and cure them. Excavations uncovered votive stelae describing healings — hernias, blindness, paralysis — that would make a modern physician smile, yet they attest to a belief system that endured for a millennium. The site has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1988.

⏱ 50 min
3

The Tholos of Epidaurus, dated to around 360 BC, is the most enigmatic structure in the sanctuary. This circular building featured a triple ring of columns — 6 Doric on the outside, 14 Corinthian on the inside — and its purpose remains hotly debated: healing rituals, a home for the sacred serpents of Asklepios, or an initiation chamber? Don't bother looking for the answer in the museum — it's not there either. Take the time instead to admire the beautifully preserved Corinthian capitals.

⏱ 35 min
4

The Epidaurus Museum, at the foot of the site, displays sculptures and architectural fragments unearthed during the excavations. Don't miss the scale models that reconstruct the sanctuary — they give a vivid sense of how vast this complex was at its Hellenistic peak, far larger than what you've just walked through. Allow about 45 minutes.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Test the theatre's acoustics for yourself: stand on the central slab of the orchestra and whisper a sentence. Ask a companion to climb to the top row — they'll hear you clearly. If you're on your own, drop a coin on the stone. Guides have been doing this demonstration for centuries, and it never fails to astonish. The Epidaurus Festival stages ancient tragedies every Friday and Saturday evening from June through September — if you're visiting in season, book ahead.

⏱ 20 min

Swimming in the coves between Paleá Epídavros and Nea Epidavros, just a few kilometres from the site: small pebble beaches hemmed in by pine trees, with crystal-clear water. The village of Paleá Epídavros has a fishing harbour lined with waterfront seafood tavernas — perfect for dinner.

⏱ 90 min

Try gemista: tomatoes and peppers stuffed with rice, fresh herbs and sometimes minced meat, slow-baked in the oven. It's a summer staple in Peloponnese tavernas and far more flavourful than the name suggests. Pair it with a glass of assyrtiko — a dry, mineral white with a crisp acidity that cuts beautifully through the stuffed vegetables.

⏱ 60 min

Nafplio

★★★★☆
1

The Palamidi Fortress towers over Nafplio from 216 metres above sea level. The Venetians built it between 1711 and 1714 under the direction of two French engineers, Lasalle and Levasseur — but the Ottomans under Ali Douad Pasha captured it as early as 1715, before it was even finished, slaughtering the entire garrison. Climb the stairs from the old town: legend says 999 steps, though actual counts range from 857 to 913. Bring water and avoid midday in summer. The view from the ramparts over the Argolic Gulf, the rooftops below and the tiny Bourtzi island is the finest panorama in the eastern Peloponnese.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Bourtzi is the small fortress perched on the islet at the harbour mouth, built by the Venetians in 1473 under the name Castello dello Soglio. Its history is worth a closer look: a chain stretching from Akronafplia to the islet once blocked enemy ships from entering the port — hence the Venetian nickname Porto Cadena. The Ottomans renamed it Bourtzi ("fortress"). Later, it served as the residence of the executioner from the Palamidi prison — a character the town wisely kept at arm's length. Small boats leave from the harbour roughly every 30 minutes in season.

⏱ 45 min
3

Nafplio's old town may be compact, but its Venetian alleyways, stone staircases and cascading bougainvillea make it one of the loveliest towns in the Peloponnese. Nafplio was the first capital of independent Greece in 1828 — it was here that Ioannis Kapodistrias, the country's first governor, was assassinated on the steps of the Church of Agios Spyridon in 1831. The commemorative plaque is still visible. Don't miss Syntagma Square with its cafés tucked under arcaded buildings: this is where the first legislators of modern Greece held their sessions.

⏱ 55 min
4

The Nafplio Archaeological Museum, housed in an 18th-century Venetian building on Syntagma Square, holds Mycenaean, Geometric and Classical collections from across the Argolid. Its star exhibit is a nearly complete Mycenaean bronze suit of armour dated to the 14th century BC, discovered at Dendra near Midea — one of the rarest surviving Bronze Age suits of armour in the world. Allow about 45 minutes.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Climb to the Palamidi Fortress on foot from the gate on Arvanitia Street in the old town. Start early in the morning (before 9 am in summer) to avoid the heat and catch the soft morning light over the gulf. If you'd rather spare your knees, the eastern access road (3.6 km by car) drops you right at the entrance.

⏱ 90 min

Stroll along Arvanitia Beach, a 10-minute walk from the old town along the coastal path beneath the Akronafplia fortress. It's a small pebble beach popular with locals, with transparent water and a direct view up to the cliffs and ramparts. Ideal for a late-afternoon swim after a day of sightseeing.

⏱ 75 min

Dinner in the old town — order the youvetsi: lamb braised slowly with orzo pasta in a thick tomato sauce, baked in a clay pot. It's the classic celebratory dish of the Argolid. Pair it with a red agiorgitiko from Nemea — the signature grape of the Peloponnese, velvety and fruity, grown just 40 km away.

⏱ 75 min

Nafplio

★★★★☆
1

Mycenae lies 25 km from Nafplio — an easy half-day trip from your base. Inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1999, it sits at the heart of the Mycenaean civilisation that dominated the eastern Mediterranean from the 15th to the 12th century BC. Right at the entrance, the Lion Gate greets you: built around 1250 BC, this monumental doorway is topped by an 18-tonne lintel and a relief of two lionesses in heraldic pose flanking a column. It is the oldest monumental sculpture in Europe, and the original bronze heads vanished in antiquity. Wear sturdy shoes — the site is steep and the stones are polished slippery by the centuries.

⏱ 30 min
2

Inside the Cyclopean walls, Grave Circle A is where the self-taught archaeologist Heinrich Schliemann unearthed royal tombs in 1876, finding gold death masks, inlaid swords and jewellery of staggering richness. It was in Shaft Grave V that he found the famous so-called "Mask of Agamemnon" — actually predating the Homeric king by three centuries, as we now know, but no matter: the original is in the National Archaeological Museum in Athens, and the replica in the Mycenae museum is well worth seeing.

⏱ 40 min
3

The Treasury of Atreus, a few hundred metres from the citadel, is one of the most audacious structures of the ancient world. This tholos (beehive) tomb dates to around 1250 BC — its dromos (entrance corridor) stretches 36 metres long and 6 metres wide. Once past the threshold (the lower lintel alone weighs 120 tonnes), you step into a circular chamber 14.6 metres across and 13.5 metres high, built entirely by corbelling. This dome remained the largest in the world until the Pantheon was raised in Rome in the 2nd century AD. It had already been looted in antiquity — but the sheer scale of the space is breathtaking.

⏱ 40 min
4

The Mycenae Museum, opened in 2007, stands at the entrance to the site. Modern and well-documented, it displays painted Mycenaean pottery, bronze weapons, jewellery and terracotta figurines recovered from local excavations. Look out in particular for the Linear B tablets — this syllabic script, the earliest form of written Greek, was only deciphered in 1952 by the British architect Michael Ventris.

⏱ 50 min

Activities

Explore the Mycenaean acropolis: climb to the royal palace at the top of the hill. The megaron (great hall) is recognisable by its four column bases. From up here, the view sweeps across the Plain of Argos and, on a clear day, all the way to the Argolic Gulf — a reminder of why this spot was chosen: absolute strategic command over the entire plain. The Cyclopean walls, up to 8 metres thick in places, are built of blocks so massive that the Classical Greeks attributed them to the Cyclopes — the only beings strong enough to lift them.

⏱ 60 min

On the way back to Nafplio, stop at Tiryns, the other Mycenaean citadel inscribed by UNESCO (just 5 km from the centre of Nafplio). Less dramatic than Mycenae but boasting even better-preserved Cyclopean walls up to 8 metres thick. The interior corbelled galleries — carved entirely from unmortared blocks — rank among the most astonishing feats of prehistoric Greek architecture.

⏱ 60 min

Try the real thing: homemade tzatziki in an old-town taverna in Nafplio — thick Greek yoghurt, grated and drained cucumber, garlic and dill — fresher and lighter than the tourist versions. Served as a mezze with warm pita bread and Kalamata olives, it's the opening ritual that signals a good evening ahead.

⏱ 45 min

Tripoli

★★★☆☆
1

Tripoli, the capital of Arcadia at 650 metres above sea level, deserves a brief stop for its Plateia Areos: a grand neoclassical square with fountains, open-air cafés and the archaeological museum building designed by architect Ernst Ziller — the same man who drew up the Royal Palace in Athens. Tripoli may not look like much at first glance, but it makes an ideal base for exploring the mountain heartland of Arcadia.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Tripoli Archaeological Museum houses collections from ancient Arcadia: sculptures, inscriptions, ceramics and mosaics from local sites such as Tegea and Mantinea. The Mycenaean finds from Arcadia are displayed here too, offering a glimpse into a region that most classical itineraries overlook entirely. Allow 40 to 50 minutes.

⏱ 45 min
3

Dimitsana, a mountain village 50 km from Tripoli, clings to a rocky spur high above the Lousios Gorge. Its cobbled lanes, stone tower-houses and abundance of churches make it one of the most beautiful villages in Arcadia. The library founded in 1764 once held 5,000 volumes before the 1821 revolution — when, according to the memoirs of Kolokotronis, the pages were torn out and used to make cartridges with locally produced gunpowder.

⏱ 45 min
4

The Open-Air Water Power Museum of Dimitsana, opened in 1997, is the only one of its kind in Greece. It showcases restored buildings housing a flour mill, a tannery, a gunpowder mill (which supplied the 1821 revolutionaries with ammunition) and a distillery. The working hydraulic mechanisms, explained with scale models, paint a vivid picture of pre-industrial life in Arcadia.

⏱ 50 min

Activities

Hike through the Lousios Gorge between the monasteries of Prodromos and Philosophos: a 45-minute to one-hour trail squeezed between blackened limestone cliffs, above a river whose waters Pausanias once described as the coldest in Greece. The Monastery of Prodromos (16th century), still home to monks, is literally glued to the cliff face — its wooden balconies jut out over a sheer drop of several dozen metres. The Monastery of Philosophos (rebuilt in 1691) is the easier starting point.

⏱ 120 min

Tasting session in Dimitsana or Stemnitsa: hilopites — small square egg noodles dried in the sun — and trachanas, a preparation of flour and fermented dried milk. These are the two quintessential mountain dishes of Arcadia, most often cooked into hearty winter soups. In summer, tavernas serve them lighter, topped with grated cheese. Arcadian fir honey, dark and thick, is among the finest in Greece.

⏱ 50 min

Olympia

★★★★★
1

The archaeological site of Olympia, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1989, is the birthplace of the Olympic Games — held here without interruption from 776 BC to 393 AD, a span of 1,169 years. The River Alpheios flows alongside, and the pines and olive trees lend the site an atmosphere unlike any other major Greek sanctuary. Set aside at least two hours for a full visit. Enter from the east and walk through the Altis (the sacred grove), heading uphill towards the Temple of Zeus.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Temple of Zeus, built around 460 BC by the architect Libon of Elis, measured 64 metres long by 28 metres wide — one of the largest Doric temples in Greece. All that remains are the columns lying where they fell, toppled by earthquakes in the 6th century AD. Inside once stood the chryselephantine statue of Zeus by Pheidias (c. 435 BC): 12 to 13 metres tall, made of gold and ivory over a wooden frame, and ranked as the third Wonder of the Ancient World. It was taken to Constantinople in the 5th century and destroyed in a fire.

⏱ 40 min
3

The ancient Olympic Stadium is reached through the vaulted tunnel that athletes once used to make their entrance — slip through this passage and picture the 40,000 spectators waiting on the other side. The track measures 212 metres long by 28 metres wide. The marble starting line is still visible. Give the stadion a go — sprint the full length: this was the blue-ribbon event of the earliest Games.

⏱ 40 min
4

The Workshop of Pheidias, west of the site, was definitively identified in 1958 thanks to a wine jug inscribed in Greek: "I belong to Pheidias." The workshop's dimensions match the cella of the Temple of Zeus exactly — the statue was first assembled here in separate pieces before being installed inside the temple. A Byzantine church was built over the workshop in the 5th century, then abandoned after the 6th-century earthquakes.

⏱ 25 min
5

The Archaeological Museum of Olympia ranks among the finest in Greece — some place it on a par with the National Archaeological Museum in Athens for the quality of its collection. It holds the world's largest collection of ancient bronzes. Its star exhibits: the Hermes Carrying the Infant Dionysos by Praxiteles (4th century BC), the Nike of Paionios in Parian marble (2.11 metres tall, the first monumental winged Victory ever sculpted, dated to 421 BC), and the pediments from the Temple of Zeus. Allow at least 1 hour 15 minutes.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Run the full length of the Olympic Stadium (212 metres) on the ancient track from the marble starting line: it's the ritual gesture every visitor makes, and it's all the more moving knowing it's exactly the same one athletes performed in the 8th century BC.

⏱ 20 min

An evening stifado in a taverna in the modern village of Olympia: a stew of rabbit (or beef) simmered in red wine sauce with pearl onions, cinnamon and cloves. It's one of the finest slow-cooked dishes in Greek cuisine, and the Elis region has a particularly fragrant version. Pair it with a glass of mavrodaphne from Patras — this sweet red wine, produced just 90 km away, is reminiscent of a light port.

⏱ 75 min

Evening walk along the banks of the Alpheios downstream from the site: the river winds between plane trees and reeds through a landscape free of cars and tourists. This is where ancient athletes came to purify themselves before the Games.

⏱ 45 min
