Upper Lusatia: Bad Muskau, Sorbian Country & Bautzen in 6 Days

📅 6 days📍 12 stops🚗 184 km⏱ 3h38

Bad Muskau → Kromlau → Weisswasser → Nochten → Knappenrode → Hoyerswerda → Schwarzkollm → Kamenz → Konigswartha → Kleinwelka → Bautzen → Gorlitz

Bad Muskau → Kromlau → Weisswasser → Nochten → Knappenrode → Hoyerswerda → Schwarzkollm → Kamenz → Konigswartha → Kleinwelka → Bautzen → Gorlitz

Upper Lusatia, in Saxony's far east, condenses into 6 days a UNESCO landscape park, a region of ponds and Sorbian tradition, and two spectacular old towns. From Bad Muskau to Görlitz, the itinerary chains together Kromlau's Devil's Bridge, Krabat's mill, Kleinwelka's dinosaurs, and the towers of Bautzen. A dense loop, ideal by car, between nature, industry, and heritage.

Bad Muskau

★★★★☆
1

Forget the neatly trimmed French gardens: Muskau Park plays a different tune. Prince Hermann von Pückler-Muskau designed it from 1815 onward as a living painting, with distant vistas and winding paths. A UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2004, it sprawls across roughly 830 hectares straddling the German-Polish border. Allow at least two hours for the palace park, and arrive early in the morning: the low-angle light on the Neisse River makes the early wake-up worthwhile.

⏱ 150 min
2

Rebuilt after the war, the Neues Schloss houses a permanent exhibition on Pückler, an unclassifiable character: writer, traveler, landscape architect, and notorious seducer. The facade regained its appearance in 2011 after a lengthy restoration. Climb the tower for a panorama over the park and town. Detail worth knowing: the tower exhibition and keep tour close occasionally, so check the dates before visiting.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Rent a bicycle at the Schlossvorwerk or hop into a horse-drawn carriage: Pückler himself recommended touring the park by carriage, so you might as well follow the master's advice. Carriages depart from the palace forecourt. The network of paths is extensive and the park immense, so two wheels save precious time. Avoid high noon in summer—shade is scarce on the vast lawns.

⏱ 90 min

If you have time, cross the Neisse via the Doppelbrücke and enter the Polish side, into Park Mużakowski. Same park, two countries: the majority of the 830 hectares lie east of the river, in Poland. This double border footbridge was erected in 2003. Bring your ID just in case, and good walking shoes, because distances stretch out quickly on the far bank.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 12 km · 15min

Kromlau

★★★★☆
1

A basalt arc so slender it seems unreal: the Rakotzbrücke, known as the 'Devil's Bridge,' reflects in Lake Rakotz to form a perfect circle. Built between the 1860s and 1880s in basalt stone, it cannot be crossed—you admire it from the shore. The reflection depends on wind and light, so aim for a calm morning. On fine days, you won't be alone in front of this backdrop.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Reducing Kromlau to its Instagram shot would be a shame: the Rhododendron Park surrounding the bridge deserves a proper stroll. Across roughly 200 hectares, you'll encounter curious stone structures, like the 'Basaltkirche' jutting out of the water and the 'Heaven and Hell' grotto. The rhododendron bloom runs from May to July—that's the prime time. Outside peak season, check the opening hours for the village cafés; many close.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 10 km · 14min

Weisswasser

★★★☆☆
1

Weißwasser was long a glass capital, and the Glasmuseum Weißwasser tells this industrial and local story. You follow the glassmaking process and the daily lives of the glassworkers who built the town. The nearby Waldeisenbahn once transported raw materials to the glassworks, hence the tight connection between the two. Plan an hour, and combine the visit with a ride on the train for coherence.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Board the Waldeisenbahn Muskau, the last forest railway of its kind preserved in Germany, with its tiny 600-millimeter gauge. Steam and diesel locomotives from the 1950s take you toward Kromlau or Bad Muskau, departing from Weißwasser's Teichstraße station. It runs mainly April through October, on weekends and public holidays. Buy your ticket at the station or directly from the conductor.

⏱ 90 min

If you're traveling with children, Tierpark Weißwasser breaks the museum pace without breaking the budget. This small zoo stays human-scale, ideal for an hour or two between stops. It complements the town's glass-and-train day nicely. Check the opening hours on-site before going—they vary by season.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 12 km · 15min

Nochten

★★★★☆
1

Stone giants deposited on the heath: the Lausitzer Findlingspark Nochten lines up more than 6,000 erratic boulders, torn from Scandinavia by glaciers and then unearthed here during coal mining. Opened in 2003 on a former opencast mine, it covers about twenty hectares divided into seven themed gardens. Allow one to two hours. Avoid windy and cold days—the site is wide open.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Climb up to the Feldzeichen, the high point of the Findlingspark, for an unobstructed view over Lusatia. Below, children rush toward 'Die Wüste,' an adventure area with boulders to climb, a labyrinth, and water features. The paths are stabilized gravel, so strollers and wheelchairs can get almost everywhere. The bistro at the visitor center covers lunch.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 25 km · 30min

Knappenrode

★★★★☆
1

The Energiefabrik Knappenrode occupies a former lignite briquette factory, state-of-the-art for its era. The last crew left in 1993; today, this site of the Saxon Museum of Industry tells the rise, fall, and future of the Lusatian coal basin. The red-brick halls provide the backdrop for the visit. Closed Mondays: schedule your stop another day.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Serious industrial museum on one side, playground on the other: at the Energiefabrik Knappenrode, the youngest race off into the slide-tunnel and labyrinth while adults marvel at the giant excavators. The enormous bucket-wheel machines convey the scale of work done to extract lignite. Factor in time for a coffee at the bistro in between. Workshops and temporary exhibitions rotate—glance at the schedule.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 8 km · 12min

Hoyerswerda

★★☆☆☆
1

The oldest building in town, Hoyerswerda Castle began life in the 13th century as a moated water fortress before being rebuilt as a Renaissance structure in 1592. Today it houses the municipal museum, which traces local history and the Sorbian roots of the region. An anecdote: Napoleon supposedly dined there for a few hours in 1813. A combination ticket covers both castle and neighboring zoo—better value if you do both.

⏱ 75 min
2

Stroll around Hoyerswerda's Marktplatz, past the Sorbenbrunnen (Sorbian fountain) and the old postal column. The Altes Rathaus, erected in the 15th century, closes off the square with its squat silhouette. A local curiosity: computer inventor Konrad Zuse took his high-school exams in the town's former lyceum. The old town can be covered quickly on foot; the Touristinformation at the Bürgerzentrum provides maps and guided-tour departures.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Zoo Hoyerswerda has a history that touches local hearts: residents built it themselves starting in the 1950s, in the former castle park. Across six hectares, it houses roughly 1,100 animals, including brown bears, a Siberian tiger, and zebras. A restaurant and playground occupy the park's center. With children, plan a half-day rather than a rushed hour.

⏱ 120 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 8 km · 12min

Schwarzkollm

★★★★☆
1

'Come to Schwarzkollm, to the Black Mill': that's the summons that opens the Sorbian legend of Krabat, the boy who became apprentice to a sorcerer miller. The Krabat-Mühle recreates this 'Black Mill' as a four-sided Lusatian farmstead—six buildings on a large site. You discover Sorbian traditions and the story behind the tale, via guided tour or audio guide. Ideal for families; check the dates for 'Krabat-Saga' performances.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Bake your own bread in the Krabat-Mühle's historic wood-fired oven: 'family baking days' must be reserved in advance. Around it, a discovery trail dotted with panels tells the Krabat saga to children. The site comes alive mainly around its events, so everything depends on the day's calendar. Outside special activities, the visit stays short—an hour suffices.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 30 km · 30min

Kamenz

★★★☆☆
1

Kamenz's red Rathaus stands out with its Venetian palazzo airs: built around 1847–1848 in an Italian neo-Renaissance style, it marks the old town. Kamenz is the birthplace of Enlightenment writer Gotthold Ephraim Lessing, born here in 1729. Worth knowing: the Lessing Museum is closed for renovations until spring 2027, so you follow his traces around town, not in the museum. Park near the center—everything is walkable afterward.

⏱ 45 min
2

Completed around 1510, the Klosterkirche St. Annen blends church and sacred-art museum on the northern edge of Kamenz's old town. It preserves a rare ensemble of carved late-Gothic altarpieces, among the religious treasures of Lusatia. The painting 'Law and Grace,' painted in 1542 by a pupil of Cranach, numbers among the notable pieces. Check opening hours—they're more restricted outside peak season.

⏱ 60 min
3

Climb the 117 steps of the Roter Turm, a remnant of Kamenz's old fortifications, a former guardpost turned prison. From its 32-meter height, the view sweeps across the old town's rooftops. The key is picked up at Kamenz-Information, so stop by before the ascent. The opening slot is limited—midday is usually your best bet.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

If blooms attract you, climb the Hutberg, the hill on Kamenz's outskirts, planted with rhododendrons and azaleas that burst into color in spring. The landscaped park rises gently to the Hutbergbühne, an amphitheater-style open-air stage. At the summit, a small viewing platform overlooks the town. Spring for flowers, summer for a concert: choose according to the program.

⏱ 75 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 20 km · 22min

Konigswartha

★★★★☆
1

At the heart of Germany's largest pond network, the Haus der Tausend Teiche serves as gateway to the Upper Lusatian Heath and Pond Landscape UNESCO Biosphere Reserve. The multimedia exhibition recounts carp farming, practiced here since the 13th century, and the wildlife of the reed beds. A large aquarium closes the visit. The center is located in Wartha; closed Mondays, open mainly during the day.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Ride a bike along the Seeadlerweg, the 'white-tailed eagle path,' which winds between the ponds from the reserve's center at Wartha. The dikes offer observation points over waterbirds; in the quiet, you may encounter herons and raptors. In August, the heath turns purple—it's the most beautiful time. Bring binoculars and mosquito protection near the water.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 12 km · 16min

Kleinwelka

★★★★☆
1

Hard to resist life-size dinosaurs: Saurierpark Kleinwelka, Germany's largest dinosaur park, lines up more than 200 sculptures across sixteen hectares. It all started with sculptor Franz Gruß's private garden, who shaped his first concrete dinos as early as 1978. Entry now passes through the 'Mitoseum,' opened in 2017. Open mainly April through November; buy tickets online to skip the queue.

⏱ 150 min

Activities

Lose yourself for real in the Irrgarten Kleinwelka, the maze attached to the dinosaur park. Hedges, dead ends, and false trails test young and old alike, especially at visit's end when legs tire. Count on half an hour to an hour depending on your sense of direction. Separate ticket from the Saurierpark—check if a combination deal exists on the day of your visit.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 7 km · 12min

Bautzen

★★★★☆
1

On its granite spur overlooking the Spree, the Ortenburg has watched over Bautzen for centuries, the former fortress of the Slavic Milzener tribe. Today it houses the Sorbisches Museum, dedicated to the Sorbian minority, and the regional administrative court. Climb the Matthiasturm for the view, accessible on guided tour. Start the day here, before afternoon crowds arrive.

⏱ 90 min
2

Built in 1558, the Alte Wasserkunst supplied Bautzen with water from the Spree. This tall round tower of 47 meters, now a technical museum, retains its old pumping machinery. For the price of 184 steps, the platform offers the finest panorama over the old town and river. Open year-round, with reduced hours in winter: check before climbing.

⏱ 60 min
3

Far from Pisa, Bautzen hides its own leaning tower: the Reichenturm leans roughly 1.4 meters to the northwest, standing 56 meters tall. From its platform, the gaze plunges onto the old town's roofs and the famous tower skyline. It opens April through October, during the day. Combine it with a loop of towers and bastions that summarizes a thousand years of history.

⏱ 45 min
4

Push open the door of Dom St. Petri, one of Germany's oldest and largest 'simultaneous' churches: Catholics and Protestants share the same building, separated by a grille. It's the only church in Saxony to function this way, with two organs, one per denomination. The calm here contrasts with the bustle of the lanes. Free entry, but respect services—the church remains an active place of worship.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Bautzen mustard deserves the gourmet detour: at the Senfmuseum, a small shop-museum, you taste local varieties far spicier than the industrial version. It's the perfect edible souvenir to slip into your bag. Tasting takes a few minutes, perfect between tower visits. Think about a jar or two for home—they travel well.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 40 km · 40min

Gorlitz

★★★★☆
1

Polished cobbles, arcades, and facades from five centuries: the Untermarkt forms the heart of Görlitz's old town, spared by World War II bombs. Görlitz is Germany's easternmost city and one of Europe's largest protected ensembles, with thousands of listed buildings. Test the 'Flüsterbogen,' this archway where you whisper on one side to be heard on the other. Visit early—morning light flatters the facades.

⏱ 75 min
2

Overlooking the Neisse, the Peterskirche, officially St. Peter und Paul, is Görlitz's great landmark. Its twin towers keep watch over the border, right above the river that separates Germany from Poland. The interior houses a grand organ renowned for its spectacular sonorities. Check the organ-presentation schedule before coming—they're not daily.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Follow the 'Walk of Görliwood,' a self-guided route marked by display cases spotting filming locations around town. Görlitz has served as backdrop for over a hundred films, including 'The Grand Budapest Hotel' and 'Inglourious Basterds,' hence its nickname. The old Art Nouveau department store hosted several scenes; it's worth a look, even from outside. Spot the numbered displays so you don't lose the thread.

⏱ 75 min

If you want to check off one more country in five minutes, cross the Altstadtbrücke on foot: the other bank of the Neisse is already Poland and the sister city of Zgorzelec. Since 1998, the two have formed a 'European city.' The finest view of Görlitz's old town is taken precisely from the Polish riverbank. Bring an ID, and treat yourself to a coffee on the Zgorzelec side.

⏱ 45 min
