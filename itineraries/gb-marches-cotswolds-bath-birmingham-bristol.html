Birmingham

★★★★☆
1

The Jewellery Quarter packs Britain's jewellery production into one square kilometre north of the city centre. According to Wikipedia, roughly 40% of UK-made jewellery still comes from here, and the local Assay Office hallmarks millions of pieces each year. The Chamberlain Clock marks the heart of the quarter. A short visit (90 minutes) is enough before heading to Ironbridge.

⏱ 90 min
2

Birmingham suffers from an unfair reputation abroad. The city has reinvented itself around its canals—longer than Venice's, as the oft-repeated claim goes. Gas Street Basin and Brindleyplace pack in restaurants and converted narrowboats. Allow an hour's stroll late afternoon before dinner. The centre is pedestrianised from Broad Street to the Bullring.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

If you're leaving early the next day, the Museum of the Jewellery Quarter is worth an hour: the former Smith & Pepper workshop was left untouched when the owners closed up in 1981. Tools, ashtrays, mugs—everything stayed exactly where it was. Guided tours only, departures every 30 minutes. The museum closes early on weekdays, so check times before you come.

⏱ 60 min

Ironbridge

★★★★☆
1

At the Iron Bridge of 1779, the world's first cast-iron bridge, you can almost hear the creak of carts that once crossed here. Abraham Darby III had it cast at Coalbrookdale as a technical showcase. The result is oddly graceful for a piece of iron cast 30 metres above the Severn. Approach from the south bank—the morning light catches the arches better.

⏱ 45 min
2

Beneath the trees, chimneys and rusted vats: that's Coalbrookdale. Abraham Darby's blast furnace (1709) is where coke-smelted iron was first produced. The Museum of Iron tells the story without glossing over pollution and workers' misery. Allow two hours. The Passport ticket opens all Ironbridge museums and pays for itself quickly.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

If you want to grasp the industrial revolution rather than dream about it, Blists Hill is the place. It's a full-scale recreated Victorian town with working workshops, a foundry, and a bank that changes your pounds into period shillings. Allow at least three hours—it's vast—and wear sturdy shoes because the cobbles show no mercy.

⏱ 180 min

Craven Arms

★★☆☆☆
1

Touristy, yes, but Stokesay delivers on its promise: English Heritage calls it the best-preserved medieval fortified manor house in England. Laurence of Ludlow, a wool merchant, built it in the 1280s and obtained his licence to crenellate in 1291. The great hall still has its 13th-century timber frame, and the half-timbered gatehouse is a rare 17th-century survival. Audio guide included; allow 90 minutes.

⏱ 90 min
2

Locals call it a manor, not a castle, and they're right. Stokesay was never designed to withstand a siege; the low hall windows, the large glazing—everything speaks of comfort over defence. During the Civil War, in 1645, the Royalist garrison surrendered without a fight. The curtain wall was dismantled afterwards, which gives the silhouette its distinctive look.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Park at the Discovery Centre in Craven Arms: parking is free and the 20-minute footpath along the Onny leads straight to the castle. It's prettier than the road approach, and you sometimes spot kingfishers. The castle tearoom allows dogs and serves homemade baguettes from April to October. Expect a paid car park if you want direct access.

⏱ 45 min

Church Stretton

★★☆☆☆
1

Wind whips through the heather and cuts your conversation short: welcome to the Long Mynd plateau. Seven miles long by three wide, mostly National Trust land. In the 19th century they called it 'Little Switzerland', and Church Stretton thrived as a spa town. The ridge belongs largely to sheep; the Welsh ponies too, running free.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Pole Bank tops out at 516 metres, the highest point in the massif. On very clear days, the view reaches the Cambrian Mountains in Wales. The track from Carding Mill Valley car park runs 6 km return and passes Lightspout Waterfall. Rocky path, walking boots essential. Fog descends fast: turn back if visibility drops.

⏱ 180 min

Stiperstones

★★★★☆
1

White quartzite blocks, fractured by freeze-thaw cycles from the last glaciation, standing upright on a bare ridge: the Stiperstones are unmatched in the country. The rock, formed roughly 480 million years ago, was deposited in shallow marine conditions. The ridge path is short but brutal. Manstone Rock, the highest tor, rises to 536 metres.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Locals call the summit tor the Devil's Chair, and legend has it that the Devil holds court there on the winter solstice eve. According to local lore, the Luftwaffe bombed the rock during the war, mistaking it for a town. The Bog Visitor Centre (a converted old school) car park is the proper starting point, parking free. Avoid in mist—the rock becomes a trap.

⏱ 150 min

Ludlow

★★★☆☆
1

Ludlow earns its reputation. Betjeman called it the loveliest town in England, and 500 listed buildings back him up. Broad Street cascades down from the Buttercross to Ludford Bridge, lined with timber-framed houses and Georgian fronts. The market runs twice a week on the square near the castle. The town's gastronomy has long been a recognised asset.

⏱ 90 min
2

Prince Arthur died here in 1502, aged sixteen, leaving Catherine of Aragon widowed from a husband she'd barely married. Henry VIII would become her second. The castle, founded by Walter de Lacy shortly after the Norman Conquest, later served as the seat of the Council of Wales and the Marches from 1473 to 1689. St Mary Magdalene's round chapel is one of only three still standing in England. Audio guide £5, genuinely useful.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

If you come on Thursday or Saturday, Castle Square market overflows with produce from Shropshire and neighbouring Powys: cheeses, cider, game in season. Otherwise, lunch at the Bear or the Charlton Arms for the view over the castle from the River Teme. The second-hand Castle Bookshop, opposite the castle, is worth a browse before heading back down.

⏱ 90 min

Stow On The Wold

★★★☆☆
1

The honey-coloured Cotswold stone hits you at once when you arrive in Chipping Campden: it's the local limestone, once quarried thanks to wool wealth. The High Street is wide, straight, lined with terraced houses nearly all from the 17th-18th centuries. Historian Trevelyan called it the finest street in England. It's also the starting point of the Cotswold Way, 102 miles to Bath.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Market Hall nearly went to the United States in pieces in the 1940s; locals raised the money to block the sale. Built in 1627 by Sir Baptist Hicks, it originally sheltered traders of butter, eggs and poultry—never wool, which was sold elsewhere. The interior flagstones bear the wear marks of four centuries of footfall.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Stow lives off tourists, but the soul of the place remains accessible if you come early. The market has run on its central square since 1107, and the narrow lanes ('tures') were used to funnel sheep: up to 20,000 animals could change hands in a single fair according to Daniel Defoe. Check the north door of St Edward's Church, framed by two ancient yews: Tolkien supposedly found his inspiration here for the Doors of Durin.

⏱ 90 min

Bourton On The Water

★★★★☆
1

Bourton calls itself the Venice of the Cotswolds. It's an exaggeration for five stone bridges over a shallow stream, but the overall picture works. The Windrush winds past low cottages, you sit beside it with an ice cream watching the ducks. Touristy in July-August, peaceful from late afternoon once the coaches leave.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

If you push through to Bourton at 4pm, you'll find a different village: the coaches depart, the locals reappear. The Model Village (a 1/9-scale replica of Bourton built in 1937) holds a child's attention for 30 minutes. Birdland, further on, has penguins and pelicans. Otherwise, walk to Lower Slaughter on the footpath, 1.5 km, quieter than the main village.

⏱ 90 min

1

Number 1 can be visited, restored as it would have been in the 1770s-90s: authentic Georgian middle-class atmosphere, and the basement kitchens alone are worth the entry.

Activities

Tintern

★★★★☆
1

Tintern was founded in 1131 by Cistercians, on the Welsh bank of the Wye. The current church, roofless since the dissolution of the monasteries in 1536, dates mainly from the 13th-14th centuries. Brunel never saw his bridge finished. The Clifton project, launched in 1831, was interrupted three times for lack of money, and completed in 1864—five years after Brunel's death at 53. Hawkshaw and Barlow took over the project, abandoning the Egyptian-inspired sphinxes planned for the towers. The bridge spans the Avon Gorge at 76 metres and remains an active traffic route.

⏱ 60 min
2

Bristol's Floating Harbour, dug in the early 19th century to trap the tide, now houses museums, restaurants and narrowboats. M Shed tells the city's story without glossing over its slave-trading past, and the toppled statue of Edward Colston is exhibited there, tagged, as a witness to June 2020. Allow 90 minutes for M Shed, it's free. Walk along the Harbourside on your way out.

Board the SS Great Britain, the first transatlantic ocean liner with an iron hull and screw propeller, launched in Bristol in 1843—another Brunel signature. The dock has been drained and you walk literally beneath the hull, on the basin floor. The audio guide recreates the eight-week voyage to Australia in third class. Allow two to three hours, ticket valid for a year if you want to return.
