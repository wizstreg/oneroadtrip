Vietnam's Central Highlands: Coffee, Ethnic Villages & Dalat Waterfalls

📅 7 days📍 7 stops🚗 500 km⏱ 11h15

Pleiku → Kon Tum → Buon Ma Thuot → Dalat

Pleiku → Kon Tum → Buon Ma Thuot → Dalat

From the red volcanic soils of Pleiku to the pine forests of Dalat, this 7-day circuit crosses Vietnam's least-known face. You'll travel from a crater lake to the axe-blade roofs of Bahnar Rong houses, from robusta coffee plantations to the spectacular waterfalls of Dak Lak, before finishing in the colonial city of Dalat at 1,500 meters elevation. Here, ethnic minorities—Jarai, Bahnar, Ede, M'nong, K'Ho—still inhabit these highlands as their ancestors did, and the gongs of the Tây Nguyên resound in villages barely signposted.

Stop 1

Pleiku

Where volcanic craters meet highland coffee terraces and Jarai traditions

★★★★★
📍 Lake T'Nung Bien Ho Pleiku📍 Village Plei Op Pleiku📍 Volcan Chu Dang Ya Pleiku📍 Mont Ham Rong Pleiku📍 Pagode Minh Thanh Pleiku
1

Six kilometers north of the city center, T'Nung Lake—called Bien Ho by the Vietnamese—fills the crater of a volcano that went dormant millions of years ago. Its deep blue color earned it the nickname 'The Eye of Pleiku'; Jarai legend tells how an entire village was swallowed by the earth on a day of disobedience, and the survivors' tears formed the lake. Local operators offer boat rides across its 250 hectares in a remarkably peaceful setting. Arrive early in the morning, before 8am, to enjoy the still water before Vietnamese tour groups descend around midday.

⏱ 75 min
2

Just a stone's throw from downtown, Plei Op village is one of the few Jarai communities still accessible on foot from Pleiku. Here you'll see the pointed-roof communal houses (nhà rông) and Jarai collective tombs—these little wooden 'houses of the dead' where the community deposits offerings and statues for several months until the spirit of the deceased finally departs. Jarai society is matrilineal: women inherit property and choose their husbands. Wear respectful clothing, and if possible, come with a French-speaking guide who knows the families—the welcome will be much warmer.

⏱ 60 min
3

Just north of town, the red volcanic soil gives way to vast tea plantations that stretch almost horizontally for hundreds of meters—an unusual landscape in a region typically more rolling. Buu Minh Pagoda nestles at the heart of these tea hills; built in a very peaceful setting, it offers a pleasant contrast to the bustle of Pleiku market. Take the opportunity to buy tea directly from local growers. Note: according to early-2025 traveler reports, some plots have been uprooted in certain areas to make way for more profitable coffee plants—check the current state of the plantations on-site.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Mount Ham Rong—'Dragon's Jaw'—rises 11 kilometers south of Pleiku and reaches over 1,000 meters elevation. From the summit, panoramic views sweep across the city, coffee and rubber-planted hills, and on clear days, the distant ridges of the Truong Son Range. The ascent takes about 45 minutes and presents no major technical difficulty, though trails can be slippery after rain—closed shoes essential. Climb late afternoon for the best light, and check the weather forecast: early morning mist can also make the panorama quite striking.

⏱ 90 min

From 5pm onward, Pleiku's night market comes alive and plunges you into the no-frills Vietnamese life of the highlands. Here you'll find coffee and tea purchased directly from growers in the surrounding area, plus dozens of local specialties to sample on-site. It's also a chance to observe the mix of Jarai, Bahnar, and Kinh ethnic minorities mingling in the aisles. Come hungry: dishes from the red earth lands—grilled meats, rice soups, and marinated meat skewers—are eaten standing up with your feet in the dust, and it's all the better for it.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 2

Kon Tum

Bahnar longhouses and a cathedral where timber meets stained glass

★★★☆☆
📍 Wooden Cathedral Kon Tum📍 Pont suspendu Kon Klor📍 Maison Rong Kon Klor📍 Dak Bla River Kon Tum📍 Kon Tum Museum
1

Built between 1913 and 1918 by French priest Guise Decrouille, Kon Tum's wooden cathedral is considered the finest church in the Central Highlands. It marries European Romanesque style with Bahnar stilt houses: its 100 wooden pillars are meticulously carved, and its stained-glass windows blend Christian motifs with scenes from local life. Behind the building sits Vinh Son Orphanage, which shelters over 700 children from ethnic minority groups, funded by the community's sisters. Come mid-morning to see light streaming through the colored windows and illuminating the wooden pillars.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Kon Klor suspension bridge spans 292 meters across the Dak Bla River, connecting downtown to the Bahnar and Jarai villages on the opposite bank. Its yellow-orange color stands out against the intense green of surrounding rice paddies and sugarcane fields. On the far side, Kon Klor's communal house (nhà rông) is regarded as the largest Rong house in the Tây Nguyên—entirely built from wood, bamboo, and thatched grass, with a spectacular roof that soars vertically like an axe blade. The best time to visit is late afternoon: the light gilds the bridge cables and reflects in the river at sunset.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Dak Bla is one of the few rivers in Vietnam to flow from east to west—counter to the majority of Central Highlands watercourses. It rises in Kon Plong district before traveling over 100 kilometers to Kon Tum, where it joins the Po Ko River to form the Se San River. Following its banks, you'll pass through Bahnar villages like Ro Ngao or Kon Ktu, with their vertical-roofed Rong houses and welcoming inhabitants. Plan to walk at least 3 kilometers along the shore to capture everyday life: basket fishing, cassava gardens, children playing in the water.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Situated on the banks of the Dak Bla, Kon Tum Museum brings together artifacts, photos, and documents on the history of the Central Highlands peoples: gongs, Bahnar and Jarai costumes, ritual objects, and traces of colonial history. Kon Tum province is home to over 330,000 inhabitants, the majority belonging to the Bahnar, Jarai, Brau, Gie Trieng, Ro Mam, and Xo Dang groups. If you're here in March or April, ask about the Kon Klor festival celebrating the harvest with dances, gongs, and a food fair—a rare moment to see the villages in celebration.

⏱ 60 min

Bún đỏ is a specialty you'll struggle to find elsewhere in Vietnam: white noodles that turn brick-red after simmering in a broth flavored with annatto, shrimp shells, and local spices. The dish is topped with meat from rice-paddy crabs, pork meatballs, and quail eggs. Local eateries serve it from early morning. Try it for breakfast as the locals do—it's the best time of day to grab a table without a queue.

⏱ 30 min

Stop 3

Kon Tum

Bahnar longhouses and a cathedral where timber meets stained glass

★★★☆☆
📍 Mang Den Kon Plong Kon Tum📍 Pa Sy Waterfall Mang Den📍 Dak Ke Lake Mang Den📍 Village Kon Bring Mang Den📍 Pine Forest Mang Den
1

Sixty kilometers northeast of Kon Tum, Mang Den sits at 1,200 meters elevation on the Truong Son Range plateau. Its name derives from the M'Nam term T'mang Deeng—'residence of the gods'—and legend tells that its seven lakes and three waterfalls were created by the tears of an avenging deity. The climate is cool year-round, between 16 and 20°C, and the vast pine forests—140,000 hectares—are worth the trip alone. The French came here in the early decades of the 20th century intending to build a hill station; their remnants, including a statue of the Virgin and Mang Den church, still stand. The road from Kon Tum via Highway 24 is itself a spectacle—mountain passes, mist-filled valleys, and the first pines appearing from 900 meters up.

⏱ 90 min
2

Pa Sy waterfall—whose name means 'three streams converging' in the Ro Mam language—is formed by the confluence of Mang Den's two largest watercourses. It cascades in a white veil from a rocky escarpment at around 1,500 meters elevation, deep in a virgin pine forest. A hundred steps lead down to the base of the falls; wear shoes with good grip. Nearby, Dak Ke Lake is the largest natural lake on the Mang Den plateau—you can take a boat ride in near-total silence, surrounded by forests and green hills.

⏱ 90 min
3

Kon Bring is one of four tourist villages in Kon Plong district, inhabited by the M'Nam people. Here you'll find a restored Rong house, basketry crafts, and rice-wine jars that the elders offer visitors during small welcoming ceremonies. The road there runs alongside terraced rice fields that turn red in September-October—if you're passing then, stop for photos. The M'Nam are also known for cultivating ginseng in the surrounding forests, a local medicinal plant that improves daily life in these still very isolated villages.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Mang Den's pine forests are crossed by very accessible trails, and the mountain's fresh air is a radical change from the plains of Vietnam. Set out early in the morning: light filters through the trunks in a light mist and the smell of resin fills the air. Local operators offer guides for routes that cross villages and reach the waterfalls—a good way to navigate this dense forest and not miss viewpoints over the valleys. In December-January, wild cherry trees bloom and give the area a surprisingly Japanese feel.

⏱ 120 min

If your guide can get you into a Bahnar village for the evening, don't miss the chance to taste rượu cần—rice wine drunk through a straw from a large communal jar, a welcoming and solidarity ritual among Central Highlands ethnic groups. Some villages near Mang Den also offer evenings with gong music—the Tây Nguyên gong ensembles have been inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list since 2005. Let your accommodation know in advance: this type of experience requires prior arrangement with a local contact.

⏱ 90 min
Homestay Nang Nghieng9.7

Stop 4

Buon Ma Thuot

Vietnam's coffee throne, where robusta plantations stretch to the horizon

★★★★☆
📍 World Coffee Museum Buon Ma Thuot📍 Village Ako Dhong Buon Ma Thuot📍 Dak Lak Museum Buon Ma Thuot📍 Lak Lake Dak Lak📍 Dray Nur Waterfall Dak Lak
1

Inaugurated in 2018 a few kilometers from downtown, the World of Coffee Museum is a bold architectural piece directly inspired by Ede longhouses—the wooden structure on stilts houses over 10,000 objects related to the history and cultures of coffee around the world, from Ethiopian origins to Asian roasting techniques. Buon Ma Thuot is the undisputed coffee capital of Vietnam, a country that ranks as the world's second-largest robusta coffee producer—and Dak Lak province concentrates a major share of this production. Allow at least an hour to tour the collections; the interactive exhibits trace the bean's journey from plantation to cup.

⏱ 75 min
2

Two kilometers from the center, Ako Dhong means in the Ede language 'the village of springs'—the hamlet is indeed built at the source of a large stream that flows through Buon Ma Thuot. It's one of the few Ede villages still integrated into the urban fabric of a major Central Highlands city. The traditional stilt longhouses (nha dai) can reach 15 to 100 meters in length and shelter several generations of the same matrilineal family. Ede society is matriarchal: the main staircase of the house—the women's staircase—is always on the right. An authentic café set in a 1980s-era longhouse lets you visit the interior in a setting surrounded by folk wooden statues.

⏱ 60 min
3

Fifty kilometers south of Buon Ma Thuot, Lak Lake is the second-largest natural freshwater lake in Vietnam. It spreads across a broad valley of rice paddies framed by volcanic hills, and its shores are inhabited by the M'nong people. At the lakeside Royal Villa—a residence built in 1929 by the French for Emperor Bao Dai, a keen hunter in these forests—you can still see period photos and original furnishings. Local operators offer rides in pirogues carved from precious wood, a centuries-old M'nong tradition for crossing the lake. Arrive late afternoon to enjoy the light on the paddies and mountains.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Designed in collaboration with Paris's Quai Branly Museum and inaugurated in 2011, Dak Lak Museum is housed in an imposing Ede-style concrete building. It presents the culture of the province's 31 ethnic minorities through photos, costumes, musical instruments—including UNESCO-listed gongs—and ritual objects. The section on the Ede is the most developed: they number around 200,000 members and are the most significant ethnic group in Dak Lak. Don't miss the French-language information leaflet available at the entrance, one of the clearest documents on Central Highlands ethnic groups.

⏱ 60 min

Operators at the lakeside offer rides in traditional M'nong pirogues—these craft carved from a single precious tree trunk are one of the symbols of water culture in the southern Central Highlands. The crossing takes you to M'nong villages on the far shore, where you can observe fishing techniques and wooden stilt houses. The dry season (November to April) is ideal: the lake is low and remarkably clear, and morning mist slowly lifts over the wooded hills around it.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 5

Dalat

A French mountain retreat where Art Deco villas nestle among pines at 1,500 meters

★★★★★
📍 Dray Nur Waterfall Dak Lak Dalat road📍 Night market Dalat📍 Lac Xuan Huong Dalat📍 Tran Hung Dao Street Dalat colonial villas
1

Leaving Buon Ma Thuot toward Dalat, make a stop at Dray Nur waterfall, one of the most famous in the Central Highlands. Dray Nur means 'female waterfall' in the Ede language—as opposed to nearby Dray Sap, called the 'male waterfall.' It drops about 30 meters over a 100-meter width, above the Serepok River, a major tributary of the Mekong. The site is surrounded by dense vegetation and former swimming pools. Allow an hour on-site to descend to the base of the falls and walk along the Serepok's banks.

⏱ 60 min
2

Dalat spreads at 1,500 meters elevation on the Langbiang plateau in the southern Central Highlands. The city takes its name from the Da Lach River—'river of the Lat,' the ethnic group that inhabited these hills before the French arrived. Xuan Huong Lake, downtown, was created by a dam in 1919; crescent-shaped, it has become the symbol of Dalat and locals' favorite sunset promenade. Walking or cycling along its shores lets you grasp the composite character of the city: early 20th-century French colonial villas, flower greenhouses, and recent hotels sit side by side amid pine vegetation.

⏱ 60 min
3

Tran Hung Dao Street concentrates some of Dalat's finest colonial villas, built in the 1920s-1940s for French administrators and civil servants who came to escape the heat of Saigon. Alexandre Yersin had discovered this plateau during an 1891 expedition and recommended establishing a hill station. Some villas are accessible, others still serve as hotels or private residences. If you have time, walk to the Bishop's Villa, one of the best-preserved buildings from the colonial period.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Dalat's night market is one of the liveliest in central Vietnam: dozens of stalls offer fresh strawberries, artichokes, passion fruit, and flowers—because Dalat is also the 'city of flowers' and supplies the majority of cut flowers consumed in Ho Chi Minh City. You'll also find bánh tráng nướng, these grilled rice crackers topped with egg, dried shrimp, and chili—dubbed 'Vietnamese pizza' by local youth—and freshly pressed strawberry juices. The market sits in the downtown district around the large covered market; arrive around 7pm when the atmosphere peaks.

⏱ 75 min

Dalat and its surroundings produce a high-altitude arabica coffee whose reputation is rapidly growing, counter to the robusta that dominates the rest of Vietnam. The K'Ho ethnic group has been cultivating coffee plants above 1,500 meters for several decades in cooperatives that ensure direct income-sharing with farming families. Cafés in town offer their beans for tasting; ask for filter coffee—cà phê phin—to appreciate the fruity, slightly acidic aromas of this mountain arabica.

⏱ 30 min

Dalat

A French mountain retreat where Art Deco villas nestle among pines at 1,500 meters

★★★★★
📍 Crazy House Hang Nga Dalat📍 Valley of Love Dalat📍 Pagode Linh Phuoc Trai Mat Dalat📍 Gare de Dalat art déco📍 Cascade Datanla Dalat📍 Domaine du Roi Bao Dai Dalat
1

The Crazy House—officially Hang Nga Guesthouse—is a surrealist structure begun in 1990 by architect Đặng Việt Nga, a PhD in architecture from Moscow University. It represents a network of tunnels, staircases, walkways, and ten themed rooms—eagle hall, tiger, ants—melded into an organic concrete form that evokes a giant tree or a Gaudí painting. The architect's stated goal was to reconnect inhabitants with nature through architecture. The entrance is open daily and you can wander freely through the winding galleries and rooftop terraces that overlook the surrounding residential neighborhood. Arrive right at opening at 9am to avoid the midday crowds.

⏱ 60 min
2

Designed in 1932 by French architects Moncet and Reveron and inaugurated in 1938, Dalat Railway Station is one of Vietnam's finest colonial buildings. Its three pointed roofs stylize the peaks of Langbiang or the hollows of the Central Highlands mountains, depending on sources. It was the terminus of an 84-kilometer railway to Phan Rang, abandoned during the Vietnam War. Since the 1990s, a 7-kilometer section has been reopened to Trai Mat village: small trains depart several times daily and drop you directly at Linh Phuoc Pagoda. The journey takes about 30 minutes and the scenery of pines and agricultural greenhouses rolling past is worth the ride alone.

⏱ 45 min
3

Eight kilometers from the center in Trai Mat village, Linh Phuoc Pagoda is nicknamed 've chai pagoda'—bottle pagoda. Completed in 1952, it's entirely covered in ceramic and broken-glass mosaics; the 49-meter dragon guarding the main entrance was reconstructed from 12,000 glass bottles. The seven-level main tower dominates the village from above. The whole is kitsch and monumental, and the contrast with the sobriety of classical pagodas is striking. If you take the train from Dalat station, you'll arrive directly in Trai Mat—the pagoda is a 10-minute walk from the terminus.

⏱ 60 min
4

Built between 1931 and 1942 for Emperor Bao Dai, the King's Domain is a French colonial Art Deco villa nestled in the pines of Dalat. Bao Dai, last of the Nguyen dynasty, abdicated in 1945 and died in exile in Paris in 1997. The interior has remained virtually intact: bedrooms, reception hall, the Emperor's study with his life-size bust. The 7 hectares of gardens are planted with century-old trees. Contrast the modesty of the ensemble with the grandeur of the Empire—it's instructive about the reality of Bao Dai's regime under French control.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Datanla waterfall plunges 20 meters between two vertical cliffs at the heart of a forest of century-old pines. The site's originality is the alpine coaster that carries you 4 kilometers through the pine forest to the base of the falls—a descent that's both fun and highly photogenic. According to the K'Ho who inhabited these forests, Datanla was once a refuge that allowed their warriors to resist Cham invaders. The site covers 312 hectares and includes wooden bridges, hiking trails, and a local restaurant. Allow at least an hour on-site; weekends see Vietnamese arrivals in numbers—prefer weekdays.

⏱ 75 min

The Valley of Love is a flower park 5 kilometers north of downtown, popular with Vietnamese honeymoon couples. Small bridges, artificial lakes, flower beds, and horses for hire for photos: the place is unapologetically kitsch and designed for selfies, but the flowers are real and the setting green. It's also a chance to observe Vietnamese newlyweds' photo rituals, a spectacle in itself. Don't expect a wild place—it's a manicured park—but the late-afternoon light makes it a pleasant spot for a relaxed stroll.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 7

Dalat

A French mountain retreat where Art Deco villas nestle among pines at 1,500 meters

★★★★★
📍 Village Lat Dalat ethnie K'Ho📍 Truc Lam Monastery Dalat cable car📍 Lac Tuyen Lam Dalat📍 Aéroport Lien Khuong Dalat
1

Less than 1 kilometer from the foot of Mount Langbiang, Lat village is the birthplace of the K'Ho ethnic group who gave their name to Dalat—Da Lach meaning 'river of the Lat.' A community of around 6,000 people lives here in stilt houses, preserving traditions of high-altitude arabica coffee cultivation and hand-embroidered textiles. It's an authentic village, less staged than some Central Highlands tourist sites, and residents are accustomed to a few visitors walking the lanes. If you want to climb Langbiang afterward (2,167 meters, 3 hours' walk from the village), local guides organize departures from Lat.

⏱ 60 min
2

Built in 1993-1994 on Phung Hoang Mountain, 5 kilometers south of central Dalat, the Truc Lam Zen monastery is the largest temple in Lam Dong province. It follows the Truc Lam Yen Tu tradition, founded in the 13th century by Emperor Tran Nhan Tong who abandoned his throne to become a Buddhist monk. About a hundred monks and nuns live here in active retreat. Access by cable car from Robin Hill, on a 2.4-kilometer cable, offers panoramic views of pines, flower greenhouses, and Dalat hills. At the mountain's base, Tuyen Lam Lake spreads over 350 hectares—you can take a boat ride before or after visiting the monastery. Arrive early morning for the light and before tour groups.

⏱ 90 min
3

Established in 1966 at the northern end of Xuan Huong Lake, Dalat Flower Gardens showcase hydrangeas, orchids, fuchsias, and roses in a verdant setting organized into greenhouses and open-air beds. Dalat supplies over 70% of the cut flowers distributed in southern Vietnam's major cities. The annual flower festival—held in December of even-numbered years—transforms the entire city into an extraordinary floral spectacle. Even outside the festival, the gardens merit a quick visit early morning before your departure to the airport.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Tuyen Lam Lake is the most beautiful lake in the Dalat region—350 hectares encircled by pine forests and hills. Operators on the shore offer boat rides of about 30 to 45 minutes that let you admire Truc Lam monastery from the water and skirt the wooded banks in restful calm. It's a fine way to close out your stay before taking the road to Lien Khuong airport, 30 kilometers south of Dalat. Schedule this activity early morning—the low-angle light on the lake is particularly beautiful before 9am.

⏱ 45 min

Lien Khuong airport sits 30 kilometers south of central Dalat, about 45 minutes by road. Daily flights serve Ho Chi Minh City (45 min) and Hanoi (2h15). Plan to arrive at the airport at least 90 minutes early: small Central Highlands airports can get congested on certain weekend days. If your flight is late in the day, you can leisurely descend Highway 20 which runs alongside coffee plantations and offers views of Lam Dong province hills—a beautiful visual conclusion to this red-earth circuit.
