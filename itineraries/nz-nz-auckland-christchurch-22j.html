☀️ What weather should you expect?

Summer (December-February): Peak season with warmest weather (18-25°C), long daylight hours, and best conditions for hiking, beaches, and outdoor activities. Highest prices and crowds.

Autumn (March-May): Excellent weather with comfortable temperatures (12-22°C), stunning fall colors, and fewer tourists. Great value season with stable weather conditions.

Winter (June-August): Cool temperatures (5-15°C) perfect for skiing, fewer crowds, and lower accommodation rates. Best time for Southern Alps snow sports and cozy indoor experiences.

Spring (September-November): Mild weather (10-20°C) with blooming landscapes, moderate crowds, and good value. Ideal for hiking and outdoor activities before summer crowds arrive.

Cathedral Cove (Te Whanganui-A-Hei) is accessible only on foot (45 min one-way from parking) or by kayak. Hahei-based operators offer sea kayak tours to Cathedral Cove via arches and marine caves. It's a different way to experience the site - from the water, the white limestone cliffs take on an entirely new dimension.

Rotorua

Geothermal city in Māori heartland, with geysers, acidic springs, and living traditional culture

★★★★☆
📍 Wai-O-Tapu Thermal Wonderland Rotorua📍 Redwoods Whakarewarewa Forest Rotorua📍 Te Puia Geothermal Park Rotorua
1

Wai-O-Tapu, 27 km south of Rotorua, is New Zealand's most photogenic geothermal site. The Champagne Pool measures 65 m in diameter and 62 m deep, formed 700 years ago during a hydrothermal eruption. Water reaches 74°C. Its edges are colored by trace amounts of arsenic, antimony, and gold. Lady Knox Geyser erupts daily at 10:15am - triggered with biodegradable soap.

⏱ 120 min
2

Whakarewarewa Redwoods Forest was planted in 1901 by the New Zealand government to test different species for commercial forestry. The Californian coast redwoods have exceeded 70 m. Hiking and mountain biking trails thread through the forest - the elevated Tree Walk offers views from 12 m above ground.

⏱ 90 min
3

Rotorua sits at the heart of the North Island's Māori country. The city smells of eggs - sulfur from geothermal activity is omnipresent. Te Puia combines a reconstructed Māori village, geysers including Pohutu (the Southern Hemisphere's most active with jets up to 30 m), and the National Māori Arts and Crafts Institute.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Operators offer complete Māori evenings - hangi (food cooked underground in heated stones), haka, and songs. It's one of the few ways to participate in a pōwhiri (welcoming ceremony) in an authentic cultural context rather than strictly tourist-oriented. Booking essential.

Waitomo

North Island village whose limestone caves shelter millions of endemic glowworms

★☆☆☆☆
📍 Waitomo Glowworm Caves📍 Ruakuri Cave Waitomo📍 Aranui Cave Waitomo
1

The Waitomo Caves lie 2.5 hours south of Auckland. 'Waitomo' comes from Māori: wai (water) and tomo (hole). The limestone forming these cavities was laid down 30 million years ago when the region lay beneath the sea. Māori chief Tane Tinorau first explored the caves in the 1880s on a raft woven from flax.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Waitomo Glowworm Cave offers a silent boat cruise beneath thousands of glowworms - actually larvae of fungus gnats (Arachnocampa luminosa), a species endemic to New Zealand and Australia. Their sticky mucus threads trap insects attracted by their bioluminescence. The illuminated ceiling resembles an inverted starry sky.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Black Labyrinth is the introductory version of black water rafting: 3 hours in Ruakuri Cave, combining tubing on an underground river, backward leaps into rapids, and floating beneath a glowworm-lit ceiling. No experience required. The Black Abyss version (5 hours) adds a 35-meter abseil and a flying fox through the illuminated gallery.

Stop 6

Taupo

Lakeside resort on New Zealand's largest lake, gateway to the Tongariro Alpine Crossing

★★★★☆
📍 Tongariro Alpine Crossing start Mangatepopo📍 Emerald Lakes Tongariro📍 Lake Taupo waterfront
1

The Tongariro Alpine Crossing is often cited as the world's finest one-day hike. The route covers 19.4 km and traverses the flank of Aoraki/Mount Tongariro, an active volcano - a small eruption hurled meter-wide blocks onto the track in August 2012. Budget 6 to 8 hours depending on pace. High point: Red Crater at 1,868 m.

⏱ 420 min
2

The Emerald Lakes, three craters filled with vivid green water, are the visual highlight of the crossing. The color comes from minerals leaching from the surrounding hydrothermal environment. Caution: active fumaroles emit scalding steam around the lakes - don't leave the marked trail.

⏱ 30 min
3

In the evening, Taupo borders New Zealand's largest lake (616 km², the size of Singapore). The lakefront offers a good base for recovering after the Crossing. If energy permits, Huka Falls 2 km north of the lake are accessible in 20 minutes - the Waikato River funnels through a 15 m wide gorge at 220,000 liters per second.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The shuttle from Taupo or Tongariro village is the only practical way to access the track: parking at the start is limited to 4 hours and the hike is linear (point A to point B). Local operators offer return shuttles with flexible end-of-day pickup.

New Plymouth

Taranaki coastal city dominated by the symmetrical stratovolcano rising to 2,518 m

★★★☆☆

1

Mount Taranaki rises to 2,518 m. This near-perfectly symmetrical stratovolcano is considered New Zealand's most beautiful and stood in for Mount Fuji in the film 'The Last Samurai' (2003). From central New Plymouth, on clear days, the snow-capped summit is visible from the coast. The Plateau Road allows you to drive up to the permanent snow zone at around 1,000 m.

2

The Len Lye Centre, opened in 2015, is the world's only museum entirely devoted to a kinetic artist - New Zealander Len Lye (1901-1980), pioneer of abstract cinema and animated sculpture. The undulating steel exterior is itself a sculpture. Inside, 'Wind Wand', his best-known work, stands on New Plymouth's seafront facing the ocean.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Taranaki coast offers several fine viewpoints of the volcano from the sea. Te Rewa Rewa Bridge, a white arched footbridge over the estuary, is a postcard classic with Taranaki as backdrop.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The walk around Mount Taranaki (Around the Mountain Circuit) covers 55 km and takes 3 to 5 days. But short trails from the Plateau or Dawson Falls allow you to see kāmahi and rātā forests, volcanic ravines, and alpine vegetation zones in under half a day. Local operators organize guided outings.

⏱ 120 min

New Plymouth's coastal walkway runs 13 km along rocky coastline between Port Taranaki and Bell Block. The 3 km between downtown and Te Rewa Rewa Bridge are accessible on foot or by bike in 30 minutes and provide a complete reading of the relationship between city, sea, and volcano.

Marahau

Gateway village to Abel Tasman National Park, departure point for kayaks and water taxis

★★☆☆☆
📍 Anchorage Bay Abel Tasman📍 Split Apple Rock Abel Tasman📍 Tonga Island Marine Reserve Abel Tasman📍 Cleopatra's Pool Abel Tasman
1

Abel Tasman National Park is New Zealand's smallest (22,530 hectares). It was created in 1942 at the initiative of conservationist Perrine Moncrieff, on the 300th anniversary of Dutch navigator Abel Tasman's arrival - who lost four crew members in a clash with Māori the day after anchoring. The park's coastline alternates golden sand beaches, granite cliffs, and subtropical forest.

⏱ 60 min
2

Split Apple Rock is a granite boulder weighing several tons, split in two with millimeter precision by frost-wedging - ice made the rock explode from inside. It's visible from kayak or shoreline. It's one of the park's most photographed sites.

⏱ 30 min
3

Cleopatra's Pool is a natural basin in a riverbed, with a water-polished rock slide. It sits 15 minutes' walk from the coast on a forest trail - a fine alternative in hot weather.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

A day of sea kayaking along the Coastal Track is Abel Tasman's signature experience. Paddling from Marahau to Anchorage Bay (4 hours) or Tonga Island Marine Reserve, you cross translucent waters, pass beaches inaccessible otherwise, and may spot seals, dolphins, and kaka parrots. Operators offer packages combining morning kayaking with water taxi return in the afternoon.

Punakaiki

West Coast hamlet famous for pancake-stack rocks and marine blowholes

★★☆☆☆
📍 Pancake Rocks Punakaiki📍 Paparoa National Park📍 Great Coast Road SH6 New Zealand
1

Punakaiki's Pancake Rocks are stratified limestone formations that genuinely resemble stacks of pancakes - hence the name. These layers formed 30 million years ago through alternating deposits of marine organism remains and fine sediments. In heavy swell, natural geysers (blowholes) shoot water several meters high from cracks in the rock. Allow 30 minutes for the loop from the car park.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Great Coast Road (SH6) between Nelson and Greymouth hugs the West Coast shoreline between sea and Southern Alps. It's one of New Zealand's most spectacular drives - cliffs, dense forests, black pebble beaches, and Alpine views appear without warning. Several photo stops are mandatory en route.

⏱ 120 min
3

Paparoa National Park, around Punakaiki, hosts the Great Walk of the same name, opened in 2020. The 55 km Paparoa Track crosses karsts, gorges, and tree fern forests. If you don't have time for the full trek, the Truman Track trail (30 min return) leads to a small beach enclosed between cliffs.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

In the evening or early morning, visitors search for wild kiwis on the black pebble beaches around Punakaiki - the West Coast is one of the few places you can spot them in the wild after sunset. Bring a red-light torch to avoid disturbing them.

Franz Josef

Village at the foot of a glacier descending into temperate rainforest - one of the world's fastest

★★☆☆☆
📍 Franz Josef Glacier viewpoint📍 Lake Matheson Fox Glacier📍 Okarito Lagoon West Coast
1

Franz Josef Glacier (Kā Roimata o Hine Hukatere in Māori) descends from 3,000 m to 300 m altitude in just 11 km - a slope so steep it advances up to 4 meters per day in summer, far faster than most glaciers worldwide. It's named after Emperor Franz Joseph I of Austria by geologist Julius von Haast in 1865. Since 2008, the glacier has been in rapid retreat - it's lost about 800 m in length since then. Access to the ice is helicopter-only, as the terminal is unstable.

⏱ 90 min
2

Lake Matheson sits 6 km south of Fox Glacier village. On calm mornings, the lake's tannin-stained waters reflect Aoraki/Mount Cook (3,724 m) and Mount Tasman (3,497 m) with photographic precision. The circular trail around the lake covers 4.5 km and takes about 1.5 hours. Arrive at dawn or late afternoon for the best light.

⏱ 90 min
3

Okarito Lagoon, 15 km north of Franz Josef, is New Zealand's largest intact coastal lagoon. It's one of the only places worldwide to see the kotuku (great white heron), a symbol of rarity in Māori culture. Local operators offer kayak tours at sunrise.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Operators offer heli-hikes on Franz Josef Glacier: the helicopter drops the group mid-height, on stable ice, and a certified guide equips you with crampons to explore crevasses, blue ice caves, and seracs. Weather decides everything - build in flexibility.

⏱ 210 min

Makarora's Blue Pools are an unmissable stop on SH6 between the West Coast and Wanaka. A suspension bridge spans the Makarora River, whose glacier-fed waters turn intense turquoise. Swimming is possible but water rarely exceeds 10°C. The return walk from the car park takes 45 minutes.

Wanaka makes a good base for a relaxed bar evening before the active days ahead. The town is less touristy than Queenstown while offering quality restaurants and craft breweries.

Wanaka

Matukituki Valley, 55 km west of Wanaka, enters Mount Aspiring / Tititea National Park. Aspiring (Tititea) peaks at 3,033 m - the only summit over 3,000 m outside the central Southern Alps. The road follows the Matukituki River between pastures, gorges, and beech forests.

The Rob Roy Glacier Track is one of the South Island's finest day hikes. The trail climbs through a narrow gorge covered in mosses and ferns before opening onto a glacial amphitheater with frontal views of Rob Roy Glacier. Avalanches regularly trigger from the walls - stay on the marked trail.

⏱ 240 min

Activities

For skydiving enthusiasts, Wanaka is one of the world's most beautiful tandem jump sites - the view of the lake and Southern Alps from 4,500 m is remarkable. Operators offer jumps all day in good weather.

Queenstown

Adventure town on Lake Wakatipu's shore, world bungy jumping capital since 1988

★★★★☆
📍 Skyline Gondola Queenstown📍 Gibbston Valley wineries Queenstown📍 Queenstown waterfront Lake Wakatipu
1

The Skyline Gondola rises 446 m above Queenstown in 8 minutes. From the summit, views embrace Lake Wakatipu (80 km long, Z-shaped), the Remarkables, and the Southern Alps. It's also the starting point for luge on a purpose-built track - the favorite sport of families and groups.

⏱ 90 min
2

Gibbston Valley, 25 km east of Queenstown in the Aroraki gorges, is New Zealand's highest-altitude wine region. Vines grow here at 320-450 m, in an exceptional continental microclimate. The region is renowned for Pinot Noir. The Cave Vineyard offers tours of its rock-carved cellar with tasting.

⏱ 120 min
3

Queenstown's lakefront stretches along the Esplanade and Steamer Wharf pier. The TSS Earnslaw steamships, built in 1912, still make regular cruises on Lake Wakatipu. A late-afternoon stroll along the lakefront, when the Remarkables are lit by low-angle light, alone justifies the stop.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Queenstown is the world capital of bungy jumping - it's here that Bungy New Zealand opened its first site in 1988 at Kawarau Bridge (43 m drop). Operators offer various heights from 43 m up to 134 m. For a more accessible alternative, the Shotover River jet boat at 80 km/h centimeters from granite walls is a local experience.

Te Anau is the essential starting point for Milford Sound. Lake Te Anau, at its foot, is the South Island's largest lake (342 km²). The town is small but well-equipped - it's the last petrol station before Milford Sound, 120 km away. Fill up here.

Te Anau

Stopover town on the South Island's largest lake, sole road access to Milford Sound

★★★☆☆
📍 Milford Sound cruise Piopiotahi📍 Mitre Peak Milford Sound📍 Bowen Falls Milford Sound📍 The Chasm Milford Road
1

Milford Sound / Piopiotahi is technically a fjord, not a sound - carved by glaciers over 100,000 years ago. The fjord extends 16 km from its head to the Tasman Sea, with walls plunging to 512 m depth. Mitre Peak, 1,683 m, rises almost vertically from the water's edge. Rudyard Kipling dubbed it 'the eighth wonder of the world'. It rains here an average 182 days per year - in rain, hundreds of ephemeral waterfalls simultaneously appear on the cliffs.

⏱ 120 min
2

The 2-hour cruise runs the length of the fjord to the Tasman Sea mouth. You pass Bowen Falls (160 m high), the fur seal rookery on their customary rocks, and possibly dusky dolphins - Milford hosts the world's southernmost population of wild common dolphins. The Harrison's Cove underwater observatory reveals black corals at just 10 m depth, a rare phenomenon due to the tannic freshwater layer filtering light.

⏱ 120 min
3

The Chasm sits 10 km from Milford Sound on SH94. The Cleddau River has carved giant potholes in the granite - circular forms polished by stones rotating in the current for thousands of years. The loop from the car park takes 20 minutes.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Operators offer kayaks in the fjord, departing from Milford terminal. Paddling beneath 1,200-meter walls and at the foot of waterfalls gives a sense of scale impossible to grasp from a boat. Morning sessions, before group arrivals, are quietest.

Stop 18

Owaka

Catlins township at the heart of a wild coastline with waterfalls, penguins, and a Jurassic fossil forest

★★★★★
📍 Purakaunui Falls Catlins📍 Nugget Point Lighthouse Catlins📍 Curio Bay petrified forest Catlins
1

Pūrākaunui Falls tumble in three tiers over 20 meters amid podocarp forest. They feature on a New Zealand postage stamp and in nearly every guidebook to the country - not without reason. The return trail from the car park takes 20 minutes. Bring your camera.

⏱ 45 min
2

Nugget Point is the Catlins' most iconic headland. The lighthouse, built 1869-1870, perches 76 m above the sea. Below, a series of gold-nugget-shaped rocks (the Nuggets) emerge from the waves. At Roaring Bay, just before the lighthouse, a viewing hide allows you to watch yellow-eyed penguins (hoiho) return from the sea late afternoon.

⏱ 75 min
3

Curio Bay is famous for its 180-million-year-old petrified forest, one of only three fossil forests accessible worldwide. At low tide, silicified trunks and roots are visible on the lava rock. At the bay's other end (Porpoise Bay), Hector's dolphins regularly come to play in the surf - they're the world's smallest marine dolphins.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The drive from Queenstown or Te Anau to the Catlins is long (3-4 hours) - it's a day of driving and successive stops. Plan the order of visits according to tides for Curio Bay and penguin arrival time at Nugget Point (dusk).

Dunedin

Scottish city of the south, whose Otago Peninsula shelters royal albatross and yellow-eyed penguins

★★★★★
📍 Otago Peninsula Dunedin📍 Royal Albatross Centre Taiaroa Head📍 Larnach Castle Dunedin📍 Dunedin Railway Station
1

Otago Peninsula stretches 25 km east of Dunedin. Taiaroa Head, at its tip, hosts the world's only mainland royal albatross colony - these birds can have a 3-meter wingspan and nest meters from visitors. The colony is active October to March. The interpretation center explains the scientific monitoring of banded birds.

⏱ 120 min
2

Yellow-eyed penguins (hoiho) come ashore at Sandfly Bay late afternoon. A wooden viewing hide allows you to watch without approaching. They're the world's rarest penguins - fewer than 4,000 individuals survive. The walk to the hide from Sandfly Bay car park takes 15 minutes.

⏱ 90 min
3

Dunedin Railway Station (1906) is often cited as New Zealand's finest station - a building of green limestone and dark granite adorned with mosaics. Dunedin's architecture generally bears the mark of Scottish settlers who founded the city in 1848: Victorian pubs, church spires, and even a castle (Larnach, 1871, accessible by bus or car from the peninsula).

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Operators offer guided evening outings to observe little blue penguins (kororā) on peninsula beaches. These tiny penguins (33 cm) are the world's smallest and return to shore after sunset. Unguided visits are prohibited to protect nests.

⏱ 120 min

Oamaru

Victorian town in white limestone, known for its little blue penguin colony and steampunk quarter

★★☆☆☆
📍 Oamaru Victorian Precinct📍 Steampunk HQ Oamaru📍 Oamaru Blue Penguin Colony
1

Ōamaru's Victorian Precinct is one of New Zealand's most intact preserved architectural ensembles. The white local limestone (Ōamaru Stone) façades mostly date from the 1870s-1890s, when the town enriched itself exporting wool and frozen meat. Push through the doors of old warehouses turned galleries, secondhand bookshops, and artisan workshops.

⏱ 90 min
2

Steampunk HQ is a permanent art installation housed in a former freight station. The steampunk universe - Victorian retro-futurism based on gears and steam - has found its global temple in Ōamaru. Interactive sculptures, a modified locomotive, and temporary exhibitions make this unlikely venue one of the country's most singular museums.

⏱ 75 min
3

Ōamaru's little blue penguin colony is New Zealand's most accessible. In the evening (generally from 7-8pm depending on season), hundreds of kororā march from the sea to their burrows in the limestone cliffs - the dusk procession, in dense groups, is a unique spectacle. Official grandstands allow observation without artificial light.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Sunday's market in the Victorian Precinct gathers antique dealers, local makers, and artisans. Ōamaru is renowned for its cheeses - the local dairy offers tastings and workshop tours.

Mount Cook

★★★☆☆
📍 Lake Pukaki Mount Cook📍 Hooker Valley Track Aoraki📍 Sealy Tarns Track Mount Cook📍 Aoraki Mount Cook summit view
1

Lake Pukaki is one of New Zealand's most striking images: a 67 km² lake with intense turquoise waters - this color comes from glacial flour in suspension, microscopic particles of rock ground by glaciers. SH80 runs along the lake's east shore for 40 km with Aoraki/Mount Cook (3,724 m, the country's highest peak) in the exact axis of the road in clear weather.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Hooker Valley Track is Aoraki/Mount Cook National Park's most accessible hike. It climbs the glacial valley for 10 km return, crosses three suspension bridges, and ends at Hooker Lake, a body of water at the direct foot of Hooker Glacier with floating icebergs in season. Mount Cook village has been UNESCO-listed since 1986 with the rest of Te Wahipounamu.

⏱ 210 min
3

The Sealy Tarns Track climbs in switchbacks from the village to two mountain lakes at 1,500 m, facing Aoraki's north face. The ascent is steep (1,000 wooden steps in early sections) but the view from Sealy Tarns over Hooker Valley and surrounding glaciers is among the park's finest.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

Aoraki/Mount Cook National Park is certified as a Dark Sky Reserve by the International Dark-Sky Association. On clear nights, the Milky Way and Magellanic Clouds (satellite galaxies visible to the naked eye only from the Southern Hemisphere) are spectacular. The Alpine Guides Observatory offers guided viewing sessions with telescopes.

Christchurch

Metropolis rebuilt after the 2011 earthquake, a laboratory of contemporary architecture undergoing renaissance

★★★★☆
📍 Christchurch Botanic Gardens📍 Avon River Punting Christchurch📍 Re:START Mall Christchurch📍 Christchurch Art Gallery
1

Christchurch Botanic Gardens spread across 21 hectares in a bend of the Avon River. Founded in 1863, they shelter over 10,000 plant species including a rose garden with 250 varieties. The Cunningham House glasshouse, built in 1923, houses giant water lilies (Victoria amazonica) and tropical plants.

⏱ 90 min
2

A punt ride on the Avon River follows the Botanic Gardens and the city heart. These flat-bottomed boats maneuvered with a pole were introduced by English settlers in the nineteenth century. The ride lasts about 30 minutes and crosses the post-earthquake downtown reconstruction zones.

⏱ 60 min
3

The 2011 earthquake (6.3 on the Richter scale) killed 185 people and destroyed 80% of Christchurch's historic center. The reconstruction has become a global laboratory for architecture and urbanism. The Re:START Mall, built from colorful shipping containers to temporarily replace the collapsed shopping center, has become a permanent attraction. The Transitional Cathedral (the 'Cardboard Cathedral') designed by Shigeru Ban is covered in structural recycled cardboard.

⏱ 90 min
4

Christchurch Art Gallery (Te Puna o Waiwhetū) reopened in 2015 after post-earthquake restoration. It holds the country's most significant New Zealand art collection outside Wellington, with Māori works and contemporary artists.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Christchurch's artisan market runs Saturday mornings in the Riccarton neighborhood - local producers, artisans, and street food stalls in a historic covered market setting. If your departure is Sunday or later, it's a beautiful way to end the journey.
