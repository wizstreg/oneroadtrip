Autumn (September-October): Excellent temperatures with spectacular fall foliage, harvest festivals, and comfortable weather. Perfect for outdoor activities, cultural events, fewer crowds than summer.

Summer (June-August): Peak season with warmest weather but highest crowds and prices. Excellent for northern regions, challenging in southwestern deserts, perfect for beaches and national parks.

Winter (December-February): Variable conditions from tropical Florida to snowy Colorado. Perfect for skiing, southern destinations, indoor attractions, lowest prices outside holiday periods.

Chicago to New Orleans Road Trip: Route 66 & the Mississippi

📅 16 days📍 15 stops🚗 2230 km⏱ 26h

Chicago → St Louis → Nashville → Memphis → Jackson → Natchez → New Orleans

Chicago → St Louis → Nashville → Memphis → Jackson → Natchez → New Orleans

From Chicago's towering skyline to New Orleans's brass band parades, this 15-day road trip traverses America's musical heartland. You'll cruise down Route 66 to St. Louis, explore Nashville's honky-tonks, dive into blues history in Memphis, and finish in the French Quarter where jazz was born. Between live music, Southern cuisine, and Mississippi River landscapes, it's a journey into the soul of America.

Chicago

Where skyscrapers were born and blues thrives on the shores of Lake Michigan

★★★★★
📍 Chicago Riverwalk📍 Millennium Park Chicago📍 Cloud Gate Chicago📍 Michigan Avenue Magnificent Mile Chicago
1

Start with the Chicago Riverwalk, a 1.25-mile promenade along the Chicago River. Built in phases between 2001 and 2016, it's divided into six "rooms" - The Marina, The Cove, The River Theater - each with its own character. In summer, you'll find kayak rentals, outdoor terraces, and after dark, Art on theMART projects video art across 2.5 acres of building facade. Allow a good hour to stroll it without rushing.

⏱ 60 min
2

Walk through the park to Millennium Park to see Cloud Gate, nicknamed "The Bean." This Anish Kapoor sculpture, unveiled in 2006, weighs 110 tons and measures 66 feet long by 33 feet high. Its 168 stainless steel plates are polished seamlessly - the reflection of the skyline and clouds is mesmerizing. Step beneath the 12-foot arch to discover the concave chamber that distorts your reflection. Arrive early morning to beat the crowds.

⏱ 45 min
3

Head up Michigan Avenue, nicknamed the Magnificent Mile. This 1.25-mile stretch between the Chicago River and Oak Street concentrates shops, Art Deco skyscrapers like the Wrigley Building (1924) and Tribune Tower (1925). Don't miss the Water Tower, one of the few buildings to survive the Great Chicago Fire of 1871.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Board an architecture cruise on the Chicago River. Over 90 minutes, you'll glide between skyscrapers while a guide narrates the story behind each building. It's the best way to understand why Chicago is considered the birthplace of the skyscraper - the Home Insurance Building, built here in 1885, was the world's first. Book online; late afternoon departures offer the most beautiful light.

⏱ 90 min

Chicago

Where skyscrapers were born and blues thrives on the shores of Lake Michigan

★★★★★
📍 Willis Tower Skydeck Chicago📍 Art Institute of Chicago📍 Grant Park Buckingham Fountain Chicago📍 Lake Michigan Chicago
1

Ascend to the Willis Tower Skydeck on the 103rd floor, 1,353 feet above the ground. When the building opened in 1973, it was the world's tallest tower - a title it held for 25 years. Today, it remains the highest observation deck in the United States. Dare to step onto The Ledge: glass boxes that extend 4.3 feet out into thin air. On clear days, the view spans four states. Pro tip: arrive 30 minutes before sunset to experience the city in daylight, twilight, and darkness.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Art Institute of Chicago ranks among the nation's greatest art museums, housing nearly 300,000 works. Installed since 1893 in a Beaux-Arts building constructed for the World's Fair, it holds the largest Impressionist and Post-Impressionist collection outside Europe - works by Monet, Renoir, Seurat. You'll also see Hopper's Nighthawks, Grant Wood's American Gothic, and Hokusai's The Great Wave. The Modern Wing, designed by Renzo Piano in 2009, adds 264,000 square feet. Budget at least 2 hours, though you could easily spend the day.

⏱ 150 min

Activities

End the day with a stroll along Lake Michigan, crossing Grant Park to Buckingham Fountain. Built in 1927 and modeled after Versailles's Latona Fountain, this 280-foot-diameter fountain stages water-and-light shows on summer evenings (May through mid-October). The setting with Chicago's skyline as backdrop is one of the city's most photographed scenes.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 3

St Louis

The 630-foot Gateway Arch marking the starting point of westward expansion

★★★★☆
📍 Route 66 Hall of Fame Museum Pontiac Illinois📍 Abraham Lincoln Tomb Springfield Illinois📍 Abraham Lincoln Presidential Library Springfield📍 Gateway Arch St Louis
1

First stop on Route 66: Pontiac, 100 miles south of Chicago. The Route 66 Hall of Fame & Museum occupies a former fire station and showcases memorabilia, neon signs, and vintage vehicles. Outside, the town's walls are covered with murals - about twenty to spot as you wander the streets. Allow 45 minutes for the museum and murals.

⏱ 45 min
2

In Springfield, stop at Oak Ridge Cemetery to pay respects at Abraham Lincoln's tomb. The 117-foot granite monument houses the remains of the 16th president and three of his four sons. At the entrance, rub the nose of Lincoln's bronze statue - it's a local tradition said to bring good luck, and the worn bronze proves it.

⏱ 45 min
3

Still in Springfield, pass by the Abraham Lincoln Presidential Library & Museum. Even without entering, the building and plaza are worth seeing. If you have time, the interior uses special effects and recreations to tell Lincoln's story - it's one of the nation's highest-rated presidential museums.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Between Pontiac and Springfield, take time to drive old Route 66 sections rather than the interstate. You'll encounter abandoned gas stations, vintage diners, and murals painted on grain silos. The Mother Road atmosphere remains intact on these stretches - keep your camera handy.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 4

Nashville

Country music's world capital with Broadway and the Grand Ole Opry

★★★★★
📍 Gateway Arch National Park St Louis📍 Old Courthouse St Louis📍 Broadway Nashville Tennessee📍 Country Music Hall of Fame Nashville
1

Start the morning in St. Louis at the foot of the Gateway Arch. Standing 630 feet tall, it's America's tallest monument and the world's tallest arch. Designed by Finnish-American architect Eero Saarinen in 1947 and built between 1963 and 1965, it commemorates westward expansion and Thomas Jefferson's role. Ride to the top in 4 minutes via capsule trams - the 16 windows offer views of the Mississippi and the city. Below, the museum (renovated in 2018) recounts the story of westward expansion. Book your tram slot online.

⏱ 90 min
2

Across from the Arch, visit the Old Courthouse, where enslaved people Dred and Harriet Scott filed their freedom suits in 1846. The case reached the Supreme Court and remains one of the most controversial decisions in American judicial history. Admission is free and the restored courtrooms are worth seeing.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Drive St. Louis to Nashville via I-64 then I-24 (about 4.5 hours). You'll cross southern Illinois then Kentucky - landscapes of wooded hills and farms. Arrive in Nashville by late afternoon. If you have energy, scout Broadway: the neon honky-tonk signs light up at dusk and live music pours from every doorway.

⏱ 90 min

Stop 5

Nashville

Country music's world capital with Broadway and the Grand Ole Opry

★★★★★
📍 Country Music Hall of Fame Nashville📍 Ryman Auditorium Nashville📍 12 South Nashville murals📍 Broadway Nashville honky-tonk
1

The Country Music Hall of Fame traces country music's evolution from Appalachian roots to today. The permanent collection includes instruments, stage costumes, and manuscripts from legends like Hank Williams, Patsy Cline, and Johnny Cash. The building itself, with its piano-key facade and tower evoking a radio antenna, nods to WSM, the station broadcasting the Grand Ole Opry since 1925.

⏱ 90 min
2

Step inside the Ryman Auditorium, dubbed the "Mother Church of Country Music." Built in 1892 by Captain Thomas Ryman as an evangelical tabernacle, it hosted the Grand Ole Opry from 1943 to 1974. This is where Hank Williams was called back for six encores of "Lovesick Blues" in 1949. A 6-foot circle of wood was cut from the original stage and inlaid into the current Opry House stage. Take the backstage tour to see dressing rooms dedicated to individual artists - the hall's acoustics, likened to an old fiddle, are exceptional.

⏱ 60 min
3

Detour through the 12 South neighborhood for its murals, including the famous "I Believe in Nashville." This residential area turned trendy district brims with independent cafes and vintage shops. The vibe is more laid-back than downtown - this is local Nashville.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In the evening, roam Broadway and its honky-tonks. Live music is free in most bars - artists play morning to night. Don't seek a particular venue: push through doors, catch a few songs, move to the next. The energy ramps up after 10 PM. If you prefer a more intimate experience, book a show at the Ryman itself - the Opry returns each winter from November to February.

⏱ 90 min
Drury Plaza Hotel Nashville Downtown9.4

Nashville

Country music's world capital with Broadway and the Grand Ole Opry

★★★★★
📍 Centennial Park Parthenon Nashville📍 John Seigenthaler Pedestrian Bridge Nashville📍 Grand Ole Opry House Nashville
1

At Centennial Park, you'll discover a full-scale replica of Athens's Parthenon - the world's only one. Built in 1897 for Tennessee's Centennial Exposition, it houses inside a gilded 42-foot statue of Athena, the largest indoor sculpture in the Western Hemisphere. The park itself, with its lake and shaded paths, is Nashville's green lung.

⏱ 60 min
2

Cross the John Seigenthaler Pedestrian Bridge, a 1909 road bridge converted to pedestrian use. Spanning 3,150 feet, it offers one of the best views of Nashville's skyline, especially at sunset. On the east side, you'll reach Germantown, the city's oldest neighborhood, with Victorian houses and restaurants.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

In the evening, attend a show at the Grand Ole Opry House. This weekly show has run since 1925 - it's the longest-running live radio music program. Each night, 8 or more artists take the stage in succession, from emerging talent to established legends. The 4,440-seat hall preserves at its center the famous wooden circle from the Ryman. Book ahead - Friday and Saturday nights fill fast.

⏱ 120 min

Stop 7

Memphis

Beale Street, Graceland, and Sun Studio - the heart of blues and rock

★★★★☆
📍 Beale Street Memphis📍 Tom Lee Park Memphis Mississippi River📍 Memphis Music Hall of Fame
1

After about 3 hours on I-40 from Nashville, you reach Memphis. Head to Beale Street, the birthplace of urban blues. This street, a National Historic Landmark, saw the passage of W.C. Handy (the "Father of the Blues"), B.B. King, and dozens of others. During the day, spot the 150 brass notes embedded in the sidewalks - each honors a Memphis musician. The atmosphere builds in the evening when bars open their doors to the street.

⏱ 60 min
2

Visit the Memphis Music Hall of Fame, steps from Beale Street. This compact museum honors the artists who built the city's musical reputation - from blues to rock, soul to rap. Interactive exhibits let you hear original recordings.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Late in the day, head to Tom Lee Park along the Mississippi for sunset. This 30-acre park, renovated in 2023, offers unobstructed views of the river and Memphis's bridges. Locals come here to run, picnic, and watch barges drift downstream. If you're lucky, an outdoor concert will enliven the evening.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 8

Memphis

Beale Street, Graceland, and Sun Studio - the heart of blues and rock

★★★★☆
📍 Stax Museum American Soul Music Memphis📍 Sun Studio Memphis📍 National Civil Rights Museum Lorraine Motel Memphis
1

The Stax Museum of American Soul Music occupies the exact site of the old Stax Records studio. From the 1960s, this studio shaped Memphis's soul sound - grittier and more brass-driven than Detroit's Motown. Otis Redding, Isaac Hayes, and Booker T. & the M.G.'s cut their hits here. The museum traces this history from Southern Baptist churches to hits that crossed the Atlantic. Don't miss the full-scale church recreation with its sound system - the chills are guaranteed.

⏱ 90 min
2

Sun Studio, at 706 Union Avenue, bills itself as the "birthplace of rock 'n' roll." In 1951, Sam Phillips recorded Jackie Brenston's "Rocket 88," considered the first rock record. In 1953, an 18-year-old Elvis Presley walked through the door to cut two songs for $3.25. The 45-minute guided tour puts you face-to-face with the original microphone - you can touch it. The studio still operates: after tours wrap, artists record through the night.

⏱ 60 min
3

The National Civil Rights Museum is built around the Lorraine Motel, where Martin Luther King Jr. was assassinated on April 4, 1968. Room 306, with its balcony, is preserved exactly as it was that day. The museum traces the complete history of the civil rights movement, from resistance to slavery through contemporary struggles. Budget at least 2 hours - it's an emotionally intense but essential visit.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

If you have energy left, head to Mud Island River Park for a walk along the Mississippi model - an 0.5-mile scale replica faithfully reproducing the river's course from Cairo, Illinois to the Gulf of Mexico. The view of Memphis's bridges from the island's point is superb.

⏱ 60 min

Memphis

Beale Street, Graceland, and Sun Studio - the heart of blues and rock

★★★★☆
📍 Graceland Memphis Elvis Presley📍 South Main Arts District Memphis📍 Memphis Pyramid Bass Pro
1

Graceland is the mansion Elvis Presley bought in 1957 for $102,000 - he was 22 years old. The audio-guided tour takes you through the living room, the Jungle Room (green carpet on the ceiling included), the billiard room, and the meditation garden where Elvis, his mother Gladys, and daughter Lisa Marie are buried. Only the ground floor is open - the upstairs remains private, as the family wished. Across the boulevard, the "Elvis Presley's Memphis" complex displays his cars (including the pink Cadillac), two private planes, and stage costumes. Budget half a day to see everything.

⏱ 210 min

Activities

Late afternoon, stroll through the South Main Arts District. This revitalizing neighborhood brims with art galleries, independent shops, and restaurants installed in old warehouses. The vintage trolley running down the street adds retro charm. This is also where you'll find Memphis's best barbecue joints - order a pulled pork sandwich, the local specialty.

⏱ 75 min

If time permits, ride to the top of the Memphis Pyramid, a 321-foot pyramid-shaped building converted into an outdoors store. The observation deck atop offers panoramic views of the Mississippi and the city - it's free if you buy a drink at the bar.

Stop 10

Jackson

Civil rights museums in Mississippi's capital city

★★★★☆
📍 Old Capitol Museum Jackson Mississippi📍 Mississippi Civil Rights Museum Jackson📍 Fondren District Jackson Mississippi
1

Heading down I-55 from Memphis (3-hour drive), you enter the heart of Mississippi. In Jackson, start with the Old Capitol Museum, housed in the former state capitol (1839). This Greek Revival building witnessed pivotal moments: Mississippi voted to secede here in 1861, and America's first married women's property rights law was passed here. Admission is free.

⏱ 60 min
2

Right next door, the Mississippi Civil Rights Museum recounts the civil rights struggle in the state that saw some of the movement's most violent episodes. Interactive galleries cover events like Medgar Evers's 1963 assassination and the Freedom Rides. It's a powerful complement to the Memphis museum. Budget 1.5 to 2 hours.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Late in the day, explore the Fondren District, Jackson's bohemian heart. This compact neighborhood brims with independent bookstores, art galleries, and Southern restaurants. It's the best spot in town to sample fried catfish or fried chicken - Mississippi cuisine deserves discovery.

⏱ 60 min

Natchez

Over a thousand antebellum mansions in the Mississippi's oldest town

★★☆☆☆
📍 Vicksburg National Military Park Mississippi📍 Natchez Bluff Park Mississippi River📍 Longwood Natchez Mississippi📍 Rosalie Mansion Natchez
1

Leaving Jackson, detour to Vicksburg National Military Park, an hour west. This 1,800-acre park commemorates the Siege of Vicksburg (May-July 1863), one of the Civil War's decisive battles. The 16-mile scenic drive winds past 1,340 monuments and markers. At the Visitor Center, an 18-minute film sets the context. Budget 1.5 to 2 hours for the loop with a few stops.

⏱ 105 min
2

Continue south on US-61 (1.5 hours) to Natchez, the oldest permanent settlement on the lower Mississippi, founded by the French in 1716. Climb to Natchez Bluff Park: from this bluff overlooking the Mississippi, the view of the river and Louisiana across is spectacular, especially at sunset. Benches and a promenade let you soak in the panorama.

⏱ 30 min
3

Natchez had more millionaires per capita than any other American city before the Civil War. Result: over 1,000 buildings listed on the historic register. Don't miss Longwood, America's unique octagonal mansion - its construction was interrupted by the war in 1861 and never completed. The workers' tools still sit in the attic, left there 160 years ago.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Stroll through downtown Natchez's historic streets to admire antebellum mansion facades. If a house is open for tours (Rosalie Mansion, Stanton Hall, or Melrose Estate), enter - the period-furnished interiors and gardens are remarkable. In March-April and September-October, the "Pilgrimage" opens twenty additional private homes.

⏱ 75 min

New Orleans

French Quarter jazz, brass band second lines, and beignets at Café du Monde

★★★★★
📍 Oak Alley Plantation Vacherie Louisiana📍 French Quarter New Orleans📍 Frenchmen Street New Orleans
1

En route to New Orleans, stop at Oak Alley Plantation in Vacherie (2.5 hours from Natchez). The alley of 28 bicentennial live oaks leading to the Greek Revival mansion (1839) is one of the South's most iconic images. The guided tour of the "Big House" and its 28 Doric columns lasts 45 minutes. But don't miss the slavery exhibition in the reconstructed slave quarters: it tells the individual lives of the 110 to 120 people enslaved here - an essential counterpoint to the site's beauty.

⏱ 120 min
2

Arrive in New Orleans late afternoon. Take a first walking tour of the French Quarter - Jackson Square, St. Louis Cathedral (the oldest Catholic cathedral in continuous use in the United States, consecrated in 1794), and the Pontalba Buildings (1849), considered America's oldest apartment buildings. The late-day atmosphere, when street musicians set up around the square, is magical.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

In the evening, head to Frenchmen Street rather than Bourbon Street. This is where locals come to hear jazz, brass bands, and funk - the clubs are smaller, the vibe more authentic, and the music often better. Many shows are free or have a cover of a few dollars. Let yourself drift from club to club.

Stop 13

New Orleans

French Quarter jazz, brass band second lines, and beignets at Café du Monde

★★★★★
📍 Jackson Square New Orleans📍 St Louis Cathedral New Orleans📍 Historic New Orleans Collection📍 Bourbon Street New Orleans
1

Resume exploring the French Quarter starting with The Historic New Orleans Collection on Royal Street. This free museum (except temporary exhibitions) occupies several historic buildings and presents archives, maps, and objects tracing Louisiana's history from the French colonial era. The interior courtyards are a peaceful haven steps from Bourbon Street's bustle.

⏱ 75 min
2

Walk Royal Street for its art galleries, antique shops, and wrought-iron architecture - the lace-like cast-iron balconies are New Orleans's visual signature. Continue to the French Market, America's oldest public market (founded 1791). Stalls of local produce, Cajun spices, and crafts stretch several blocks.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

New Orleans is one of America's culinary capitals. Use the day to sample the classics: gumbo (thick soup with roux base, andouille sausage, and shrimp), a po'boy (fried seafood sandwich on crusty French bread), and for dessert, beignets from Café du Monde - served hot and buried in powdered sugar since 1862. Grab an outdoor table and watch the world pass by.

⏱ 90 min

Stop 14

New Orleans

French Quarter jazz, brass band second lines, and beignets at Café du Monde

★★★★★
📍 Garden District New Orleans📍 Lafayette Cemetery No 1 New Orleans📍 St Charles Avenue Streetcar New Orleans📍 City Park New Orleans Sculpture Garden
1

Ride the St. Charles Avenue streetcar - in operation since 1835, it's the world's oldest continuously operating streetcar line. In 30 minutes, it carries you from downtown toward the Garden District. This residential neighborhood, developed in the 1830s-1860s by wealthy Americans who didn't want to live with Creoles in the French Quarter, lines up monumental Victorian and Greek Revival mansions beneath centuries-old oaks draped with Spanish moss.

⏱ 75 min
2

At the Garden District entrance, pass Lafayette Cemetery No. 1 (1833). This cemetery with above-ground tombs - necessary because the marshy soil prevents underground burial - has served as a film set for multiple movies. Admission is free but hours are limited - check before going.

⏱ 30 min
3

In the afternoon, head to City Park, one of America's largest urban parks at 1,300 acres - bigger than Central Park. The Sydney and Walda Besthoff Sculpture Garden displays about sixty contemporary sculptures beneath centuries-old oaks, with free admission. Further in the park, the New Orleans Museum of Art (NOMA) merits a detour for its French art collection and temporary exhibitions.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

In the evening, return to Frenchmen Street or catch a brass band show in the Marigny. The "second line" tradition - spontaneous brass band street parades - is the beating heart of New Orleans's musical culture. On Sundays, there are often second lines in Tremé, America's oldest African American neighborhood.

⏱ 90 min

Stop 15

New Orleans

French Quarter jazz, brass band second lines, and beignets at Café du Monde

★★★★★
📍 Warehouse District New Orleans📍 National WWII Museum New Orleans📍 Magazine Street New Orleans📍 Mississippi River New Orleans Algiers Point
1

The National WWII Museum in the Warehouse District regularly ranks among America's finest museums. Founded in 2000, it now occupies six buildings and recounts the entire global conflict with aircraft, tanks, submarines, and video testimonies from veterans. Budget at least 3 hours - some spend the entire day. Book online to skip the line.

⏱ 180 min
2

After the museum, walk up Magazine Street, the shopping artery crossing the Garden District and Uptown for 6 miles. Vintage shops, galleries, restaurants - it's the relaxed, local counterpart to Royal Street.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

For your final evening, take the free Algiers Point ferry. In 5 minutes, you cross the Mississippi and reach this quiet residential neighborhood offering the best view of New Orleans's skyline. Watch the sun set over the river with the city as backdrop - it's the perfect way to conclude this road trip.
