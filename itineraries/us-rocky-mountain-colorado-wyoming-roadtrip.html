✨ Highlights
  • Trail Ridge Road in Rocky Mountain NP: the highest continuously paved road in the United States at 12,183 feet
  • Grand Teton NP: the Grand Teton rises to 13,775 feet with no foothills to obscure the view from the valley
  • Black Canyon of the Gunnison: walls plunge 2,722 feet with a width of only 40 feet at the narrowest point
  • Great Sand Dunes NP: Star Dune reaches 750 feet—North America's tallest dunes
📆 Best period

June, July, August, September

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 9 and 13 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Spring (April-May): Perfect weather across most regions (15-25°C) with blooming landscapes, moderate crowds, and excellent conditions for national parks and cities. Ideal for diverse activities nationwide.

Autumn (September-October): Excellent temperatures with spectacular fall foliage, harvest festivals, and comfortable weather. Perfect for outdoor activities, cultural events, fewer crowds than summer.

Summer (June-August): Peak season with warmest weather but highest crowds and prices. Excellent for northern regions, challenging in southwestern deserts, perfect for beaches and national parks.

Winter (December-February): Variable conditions from tropical Florida to snowy Colorado. Perfect for skiing, southern destinations, indoor attractions, lowest prices outside holiday periods.

🌍 What to discover in US?

Continental diversity with iconic landmarks, cultural melting pot, and endless possibilities. Experience natural wonders, vibrant cities, and authentic American dreams from coast to coast. The country offers incredible diversity with modern amenities and cultural richness. United States provides life-changing experiences with natural beauty and cultural authenticity.

📋 Important notes

Rocky Mountain NP and Grand Teton NP require timed entry reservations during peak season (June–September)—book online before departure. Trail Ridge Road closed October to May approximately. Independence Pass (toward Aspen) closed off-season.

Stop 1

Denver Co

Colorado's capital at the mile-high mark, gateway to the Rockies and America's craft beer capital

★★★★☆
📍 Denver Union Station📍 Denver Art Museum📍 Larimer Square Denver📍 RiNo Art District Denver📍 Colorado State Capitol Denver
1

Drop your bags at Denver Union Station—built in 1914 in Beaux-Arts style and restored in 2014, this is where the train from the airport arrives. Beneath the grand glass atrium, local bars and restaurants have replaced the old ticket counters. All of LoDo is walkable from the front steps.

⏱ 45 min
2

Dedicate at least two hours to the Denver Art Museum—one of the most important Native American art collections in the United States, with over 18,000 works across two buildings. The Frederick C. Hamilton Pavilion designed by Daniel Libeskind, inaugurated in 2006, is worth the stop for its architecture alone. The Western American and pre-Columbian collections are outstanding.

⏱ 120 min
3

Climb the west steps of the Colorado State Capitol to stand exactly one mile high—5,280 feet above sea level in the famous Mile High City. The gilded dome, re-leafed in 1980 with 200 ounces of Colorado gold, gleams for miles on clear days. Free guided tours on weekdays.

⏱ 45 min
4

End your day at Larimer Square, Denver's oldest block: red brick facades from the 1870s, award-winning restaurants, and lively patios on a single city block. Arrive after 6 PM for the atmosphere at its peak—this is the spot for your first-night dinner.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Take a walk through RiNo (River North Art District): massive murals cover the facades of old industrial warehouses, and taprooms line a few blocks. Denver has more than 100 breweries in the city—it's one of America's craft beer capitals. An hour on foot is enough to hit three or four.

⏱ 90 min

Walk the 16th Street Mall, a mile-long pedestrian artery with a free shuttle every 5 minutes. Hop on and off at will to explore neighborhoods, food trucks, and street musicians—everyday Denver, not the guidebook version.

Stop 2

Boulder Co

Where red-tiled roofs meet the Flatirons and outdoor living becomes religion

★★★★☆
📍 Pearl Street Mall Boulder📍 Flatirons Boulder📍 Chautauqua Park Boulder📍 University of Colorado Boulder
1

Stroll Pearl Street Mall, pedestrianized since 1977: four blocks of red brick lined with independent shops, cafes, and award-winning restaurants, with the Flatirons as a backdrop. More than 70% of the businesses are local. Food & Wine ranked it among the ten best food streets in America.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Flatirons—five slabs of sandstone tilted more than 1,000 feet above the city—are impossible to miss. Chautauqua Park, at their base, gives access to about twenty trails. The First Flatiron Trail (2 miles round-trip) offers a bird's-eye view of the city—start at dawn to beat the crowds.

⏱ 120 min
3

Wander the University of Colorado Boulder campus, founded in 1876—its red sandstone buildings and red-tiled roofs make it one of the most beautiful campuses in the country. A stroll through the University Mall is enough to grasp the atmosphere of this town at 5,430 feet that has made the outdoors its identity.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

If you're here on a Saturday morning from May to October, don't miss the Boulder Farmers Market: local producers, food trucks, and artisans at the corner of 13th and Canyon Blvd. It's where the community gathers—ideal for assembling a picnic before the Flatirons.

⏱ 60 min

Boulder has several taprooms steps from Pearl Street. Avery Brewing and Twisted Pine are the local references—order a pint on the patio with the Flatirons in the background. It's the end-of-day ritual for locals, and you'll understand why this town is so appealing.

⏱ 75 min

Stop 3

Estes Park

★★★★☆
📍 Rocky Mountain National Park📍 Trail Ridge Road Rocky Mountain📍 Bear Lake Rocky Mountain📍 Alpine Visitor Center Rocky Mountain📍 Horseshoe Park Rocky Mountain
1

Leave before 8 AM for Trail Ridge Road—built between 1929 and 1938, it's the highest continuously paved road in the United States, cresting at 12,183 feet. After 9 AM, a timed entry reservation is mandatory in summer and overlook parking lots fill up. Over 48 miles between Estes Park and Grand Lake, 11 miles run above treeline.

⏱ 180 min
2

From Bear Lake, you have access to the park's most accessible hikes. The loop around the lake (0.9 miles, flat) offers perfect reflections of the mountains. Continue to Nymph Lake, Dream Lake, and Emerald Lake—3.5 miles round-trip—for a true immersion in alpine tundra.

⏱ 150 min
3

Horseshoe Park, at the park's east entrance, is where elk gather in September and October for the rut—the bugling of the bulls echoes through the valley at dawn and dusk. Even outside rutting season, deer and pronghorns regularly graze the meadows—have your binoculars ready from the entrance.

⏱ 60 min
4

Stop at the Alpine Visitor Center at 11,796 feet: it's the highest visitor center in the National Park System. The deck offers views of the Never Summer Mountains and glacial valleys. Twenty minutes is enough to acclimate to the altitude before heading back down to the trails.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

From Rock Cut at 11,930 feet, the Tundra Communities Trail (0.6 miles) takes you across tundra that needs 1,000 years to regenerate. Interpretive signs point out plants that are 250 years old yet barely grow 2 inches high. Stay strictly on the marked trail.

⏱ 45 min

In the evening in Estes Park, walk downtown along the Fall River. Souvenir shops, ice cream parlors, and restaurants line a few blocks. The atmosphere is classic American mountain town—unpretentious and pleasant after a day at altitude.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 4

Lander Wy

Wyoming's climbing capital at the foot of the Wind River Range and Sinks Canyon

★★★★☆
📍 Sinks Canyon State Park Lander📍 Museum of the American West Lander📍 Eagle Bronze Foundry Lander📍 Popo Agie Falls Trail Lander
1

Six miles south of Lander, Sinks Canyon State Park harbors a rare phenomenon: the Popo Agie River (pronounced "po-po-jah") vanishes into a limestone cavern—The Sinks—then reappears 400 meters downstream in a pool filled with 3- to 5-pound rainbow trout. The water takes two hours to travel those 400 meters underground—the exact route remains unknown. Buy pellets from the dispenser at The Rise overlook and feed the trout from the platform.

⏱ 90 min
2

Stop by the Museum of the American West: it features a reconstructed pioneer village with blacksmith shop, schoolhouse, and period cabins, as well as the history of the Shoshone and Arapaho tribes whose reservations surround Lander. The exhibits are well-documented and far from the usual Far West tourist clichés.

⏱ 75 min
3

Don't leave Lander without visiting Eagle Bronze Foundry on Poppy Street: this family foundry produces the majority of bronze statues in Wyoming and Montana. A $5 guided tour takes you into the workshops, from original modeling to final patina. Jacob, the founder's grandson, often guides groups himself.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Take the Popo Agie Falls Trail (3.5 miles round-trip, 590 feet elevation gain) from Sinks Canyon to a waterfall in a pine forest. The trail crosses fields of wildflowers in June-July. At the base of the falls, a natural pool with icy water attracts the brave on hot days.

⏱ 150 min

End the day at Lander Brewing Company, steps from Main Street. After the canyon, a pint on the patio is in order—the menu revolves around local Wyoming beef and the burgers are famous throughout the region.

⏱ 60 min

Pinedale Wy

Quiet stopover on US-191 with access to Fremont Lake and the Wind River peaks

★★★★☆
📍 Fremont Lake Pinedale Wyoming📍 Skyline Drive Elkhart Park Pinedale Wyoming
1

Detour to Fremont Lake, 4 miles from Pinedale: the 7th deepest lake in the United States (696 feet) and 2nd largest natural lake in Wyoming. Its translucent waters reflect the granite ridges of the Wind River Range. The marina overlook offers a panorama of peaks exceeding 13,000 feet—a quick stop but a memorable one.

⏱ 45 min
2

Take Skyline Drive from Pinedale: 14 miles to the Elkhart Park Trailhead at 9,020 feet. The overlook just before the terminus offers the best car-accessible panorama of the Wind River Range—Fremont Peak (13,745 feet) and seven other summits over 13,000 feet identified on an interpretive sign. In autumn, the aspens turn gold in September.

Stop 6

Jackson Wy

Where elk antler arches frame the square and Grand Teton rises beyond the boardwalks

★★★★★
📍 Jackson Town Square Wyoming📍 National Museum of Wildlife Art Jackson📍 Snake River Overlook Grand Teton📍 National Elk Refuge Jackson Wyoming
1

Settle into Jackson's Town Square, framed by its four elk antler arches. Historic saloons like the Million Dollar Cowboy Bar have kept their saddles bolted to the bar stools since the 1930s. Art galleries and outdoor shops line the square—the capital of high-end Wild West.

⏱ 60 min
2

Stop at Snake River Overlook in Grand Teton NP: this is where Ansel Adams photographed the Tetons and the Snake River in 1942. The river meanders in the foreground, the spires of the Teton Range rise 7,000 feet behind—nothing has changed. Arrive at golden hour.

⏱ 45 min
3

Reserve an hour and a half for the National Museum of Wildlife Art, carved into the cliff overlooking the National Elk Refuge: 5,000 works dedicated to wildlife—paintings, sculptures, and prints by artists like Bierstadt and Russell. In winter, 6,000 to 8,000 elk winter in the refuge visible from the museum windows.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Float the Snake River with a guide. Inside Grand Teton NP, the current is gentle (Class I–II)—perfect for wildlife watching: ospreys, beavers, moose, and occasionally black bears on the banks. The float lasts 2–3 hours. Barker-Ewing and Triangle X are the top-rated operators.

⏱ 180 min

Grab breakfast at The Bunnery on Cache Street—Jackson's most popular table since 1975, ideal before leaving early for the park. Local cuisine revolves around bison, elk, and trout. For dinner, Gather offers a menu focused on Jackson Hole producers.

⏱ 90 min

Jackson Wy

Where elk antler arches frame the square and Grand Teton rises beyond the boardwalks

★★★★★
📍 Jenny Lake Grand Teton National Park📍 Cascade Canyon Trail Grand Teton📍 Schwabacher Landing Grand Teton📍 Oxbow Bend Grand Teton📍 Jackson Lake Lodge Grand Teton
1

Prepare yourself: the reflection of the Tetons in Jenny Lake exceeds every photo you've seen. The Jenny Lake Visitor Center is housed in a 1920s photo studio. Take the ferry (10 minutes) to reach the foot of Cascade Canyon without the extra 2-mile walk.

⏱ 60 min
2

Wake up early for Schwabacher Landing: this beaver pond 6 miles from Moose Junction offers the park's most perfect reflection of the Tetons. Before sunrise, morning mist hangs over the water and moose graze on the banks. Gravel access road, passable in regular cars.

⏱ 60 min
3

Set up at Oxbow Bend, east of Signal Mountain: the Snake River's lazy meander attracts moose, trumpeter swans, herons, and white pelicans. When there's no wind, Mount Moran (12,605 feet) reflects in the water—bring binoculars and a telephoto lens.

⏱ 60 min
4

Walk into Jackson Lake Lodge, built in 1955 and designated a National Historic Landmark—its 60-foot-high windows frame a frontal view of Mount Moran and the Teton Range. Even if you're not staying here, it's one of the most spectacular rooms in the American West.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Take the ferry for Cascade Canyon Trail (5 miles from the landing, 12 miles on foot)—Grand Teton NP's signature hike. After the granite canyon, you emerge onto an alpine meadow framed by the peaks of the Tetons. Leave early with water and food for silence before the groups arrive.

⏱ 300 min

In the evening, drive up Signal Mountain: the 5-mile road leads to a 360° panorama of Jackson Hole valley, the Tetons to the west, and the Gros Ventre Mountains to the east. It's the park's best car-accessible viewpoint for sunset.

⏱ 60 min

Jackson Wy

Where elk antler arches frame the square and Grand Teton rises beyond the boardwalks

★★★★★
📍 Aerial Tram Jackson Hole Mountain Resort📍 String Lake Grand Teton📍 Mormon Row Grand Teton📍 Taggart Lake Trail Grand Teton
1

Choose String Lake for your last morning in the park: between Jenny Lake and Leigh Lake, it's the quietest spot to walk along the base of the Tetons just a few hundred meters from the walls. The flat 2-mile trail offers direct views of Teewinot Mountain. In summer, the shallow waters are warm enough for swimming—a rare luxury in a national park.

⏱ 90 min
2

Don't miss Mormon Row on Antelope Flats Road: the Moulton Barn, a wooden barn from the 1890s, is one of the most photographed homesteads in the United States with the snow-capped Tetons behind. Arrive before 9 AM to have the scene to yourself.

⏱ 60 min
3

For this last day in the park, opt for Taggart Lake Trail (3.5 miles round-trip)—less crowded than Cascade Canyon, gentle climb to a glacial lake framed by moraines. The rock slabs testify to the glaciers that shaped the valley 15,000 years ago.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Take the Jackson Hole Aerial Tram from Teton Village: in 12 minutes, you ascend 4,139 vertical feet to reach the summit of Rendezvous Mountain at 10,450 feet. The panorama sweeps the entire Teton Range, Jackson Hole valley, and on clear days, all the way to Yellowstone. Hiking trails depart from the summit.

⏱ 120 min

In the evening, drive slowly on Antelope Flats Road and Gros Ventre Road at sunset—elk, bison, coyotes, and black bears are regularly spotted at dusk. Stay in your vehicle or keep 100 meters distance: park rules and common sense.

Stop 9

Saratoga

★★★★★
📍 Hobo Hot Springs Saratoga Wyoming📍 Wolf Hotel Saratoga Wyoming📍 North Platte River Saratoga Wyoming📍 Medicine Bow National Forest Saratoga
1

The Hotel Wolf, built in 1893 by German immigrant Frederick Wolf, is Saratoga's oldest brick building still in operation. Step into the bar to feel the atmosphere of a Wyoming that hasn't changed in a century—regulars play cards, elk heads watch from the walls, and the wooden bar dates to the original.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Hobo Hot Springs, in the municipal park on Walnut Street, are free and open 24/7. The Cheyenne, Ute, and Arapaho considered these springs neutral territory for centuries—they called them "the place of magic waters." The main pool temperature ranges from 100 to 118°F, with an area called the Lobster Pot that reaches 120°F. Changing rooms and showers on site.

⏱ 90 min
3

The North Platte River running through Saratoga is designated a blue ribbon trout stream—one of the best trout fishing destinations in the American West. Even without a rod, the banks of Veterans Island Park offer a peaceful walk along the water with the Snowy Range Mountains (12,013 feet) as a backdrop.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Soak in the Hobo Hot Springs at sunset then plunge into the North Platte River just behind the pools to cool off—the local tradition. The river water gets a slight boost from the springs but remains brisk. This is the Saratoga experience in all its simplicity.

⏱ 90 min

Snowy Mountain Brewery, on site at the Saratoga Hot Springs Resort, is Wyoming's 3rd oldest craft brewery. Order a pint at Snowy Mountain Pub after the springs—it's the local version of the beer-and-hot-springs ritual that punctuates Saratoga evenings.

⏱ 60 min

Steamboat Springs Co

Yampa River mountain town with locals' bars and Strawberry Park Hot Springs

★★★★☆
📍 Steamboat Springs downtown Colorado📍 Strawberry Park Hot Springs Steamboat Springs📍 Old Town Hot Springs Steamboat Springs
1

Steamboat Springs owes its name to early French explorers who, hearing a spring bubbling along the Yampa River, thought they glimpsed the paddle wheels of a steamboat. The spring disappeared during railroad construction in 1908, but the town retains its character as a real Colorado mountain town—not a ski-resort boutique village, but a real town with locals' bars on Lincoln Avenue.

⏱ 45 min
2

Strawberry Park Hot Springs, 6 miles north of town, are carved into rock at 160°F at the source and naturally cooled to 100–108°F in the pools. The setting is wild—forest, boulders, steam in the cold air. Reservations required on weekdays, first-come on weekends.

Aspen Co

Victorian mining town turned alpine sophistication at 7,995 feet

★★★★☆
📍 Aspen Downtown Colorado📍 Wheeler Opera House Aspen📍 Maroon Bells Aspen Colorado📍 Independence Pass Colorado
1

Spend an hour in downtown Aspen to feel the atmosphere of this Victorian town set at 7,995 feet in a valley framed by four ski resorts. The Wheeler Opera House, inaugurated in 1889, still hosts concerts, and art galleries line the main street. The atmosphere is surprisingly peaceful for such a renowned town.

⏱ 75 min
2

Detour to the Maroon Bells—Maroon Peak (14,163 feet) and North Maroon Peak (14,019 feet)—the two most photographed peaks in Colorado. Maroon Lake in the foreground creates the perfect reflection. In summer, a shuttle bus is mandatory between 8 AM and 5 PM from Aspen Highlands parking—take it early for morning light.

⏱ 120 min
3

Take time to drive up Independence Pass (12,095 feet) on CO-82: the road follows the Roaring Fork River, crosses through spruce forests, and emerges onto open tundra. At the pass, the Sawatch Mountains stretch as far as the eye can see. Closed November to late May—motorhomes over 35 feet prohibited.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Rent a bike and cruise down the Rio Grande Trail, a former railroad bed turned bike path that follows the Roaring Fork River for 25 miles to Glenwood Springs. In the right direction, you descend without hardly pedaling—take the RFTA shuttle back up.

⏱ 180 min

After the road, try Meat & Cheese on Hopkins Ave: a beautiful charcuterie and cheese board from Colorado with a selection of natural wines. Aspen also has several MICHELIN-recommended restaurants for those who want to go further.

Stop 12

Montrose Co

Staging town for Black Canyon of the Gunnison, 15 miles from the south entrance

★★★☆☆
📍 Black Canyon of the Gunnison South Rim📍 Painted Wall Black Canyon📍 Chasm View Black Canyon📍 Warner Point Trail Black Canyon
1

Drive the 7 miles of South Rim Road stopping at each overlook—Black Canyon of the Gunnison isn't America's largest canyon, but it's the most terrifyingly vertical. At Warner Point, the walls drop 2,722 feet and are only 40 feet wide at the canyon bottom. The gneiss and schist rock is 1.7 billion years old—among the oldest exposed on the surface in North America.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Painted Wall, Colorado's tallest cliff, raises its 2,250 feet of granite striped with pink pegmatite veins. You can see it from several South Rim Road overlooks—go early morning or late afternoon: the colors of the stripes change completely with the angle of the sun.

⏱ 45 min
3

Stop at Chasm View: the canyon's width (1,100 feet) is less than its depth (2,230 feet)—mathematically dizzying. The Gunnison River below descends 43 feet per mile, versus 2.3 feet for the Colorado in the Grand Canyon. On calm days, its roar carries up to the rim.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Take the Oak Flat Loop Trail (2 miles, moderate) that descends 400 feet below the canyon rim through pine forests clinging to the slope. You're in the canyon without really descending into it—the views of the walls from below are radically different. Warning: it's often 10°C warmer in the canyon.

⏱ 90 min

Black Canyon is designated a Dark Sky Park—stay after sunset if you arrive in late afternoon. On a moonless night, the Milky Way is visible to the naked eye from the South Rim overlooks. You probably won't see this many stars anywhere else on the trip.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 13

Cortez Co

Gateway to Mesa Verde and the ancient Puebloan Southwest

★★★☆☆
📍 Cliff Palace Mesa Verde National Park📍 Balcony House Mesa Verde📍 Chapin Mesa Museum Mesa Verde📍 Mesa Top Loop Road Mesa Verde📍 Far View Sites Mesa Verde
1

Book your Cliff Palace tour in advance on recreation.gov ($5/person): 150 rooms and 23 kivas in a sandstone alcove, built between 1190 and 1270 by the Ancestral Puebloans. In 1888, two cowboys lost in a snowstorm were the first Europeans to see it. The hour-long guided tour descends into the site via stone ladders—exactly as its occupants did.

⏱ 90 min
2

For Balcony House, prepare yourself: a 32-foot ladder planted in the cliff, then a 2-foot-wide tunnel to crawl through—exactly how the Puebloans entered their homes to defend themselves. The guided tour lasts 1 hour. Not recommended for the claustrophobic, highly recommended for everyone else.

⏱ 75 min
3

Stop by the Chapin Mesa Archeological Museum before your guided tours: the 30-minute intro to 1,400 years of Puebloan history—ceramics, tools, reconstructions—radically changes how you read the sites. Just behind, Spruce Tree House (3rd largest site in the park) is accessible without a guide.

⏱ 75 min
4

Take the Mesa Top Loop Road (6 miles) for the remains of 10th-century villages—well before the cliff dwellings. Park Point, the park's highest point at 8,571 feet, offers a 360° view of the Four Corners states.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Finish the day on the Petroglyph Point Trail (3 miles, loop) that follows the canyon walls to a panel of petroglyphs carved in the rock. It's one of the few trails accessible without a guide in the Chapin Mesa area—pick up the map at the museum before leaving.

⏱ 120 min

In the evening in Cortez, sit down at Absolute Bakery & Café—the reference table in town for a simple, local dinner. If curiosity strikes, the Four Corners Monument is 40 miles west: the only place in the United States where four states meet at a point.

⏱ 75 min

Stop 14

Alamosa Co

San Luis Valley town 30 miles from North America's tallest dunes

★★★★☆
📍 Great Sand Dunes National Park📍 Star Dune Great Sand Dunes📍 Medano Creek Great Sand Dunes📍 Zapata Falls Alamosa Colorado
1

Climb Star Dune, North America's tallest dune at 750 feet. These dunes formed 440,000 years ago when Lake Alamosa dried up and winds carried sand toward the curve of the Sangre de Cristo Mountains—where it still accumulates. Count on 2 to 3 hours round-trip and never go without water: the surface can reach 150°F in summer.

⏱ 180 min
2

If you're here from May to early July, don't miss Medano Creek: a snowmelt stream that creates a real sand beach with running water at the base of the dunes. Kids splash, photographers set up low-angle shots. By July–August, it's often dried up—check conditions first.

⏱ 60 min
3

Detour to Zapata Falls, 6 miles south of the park: to reach them, you wade upstream for 100 meters, headlamp recommended for the last section through rock. The reward is welcome coolness after a morning in the dunes.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Go sandboarding or sand sledding on the dune slopes: boards rent outside the park for $15–20 per day. The descent is short but the climb on foot is solid cardio. Avoid 11 AM to 3 PM in summer—the sand becomes literally scorching.

⏱ 120 min

Great Sand Dunes is ranked among the quietest national parks in the United States. If you camp at Piñon Flats, walk a mile into the dunefield after midnight—the Milky Way is visible to the naked eye at 7,515 feet elevation, and the silence of a sand desert under the stars has no equivalent.

Stop 15

Colorado Springs Co

Where Pikes Peak looms over Garden of the Gods, free forever since 1909

★★★★☆
📍 Garden of the Gods Colorado Springs📍 Pikes Peak Highway Colorado Springs📍 Balanced Rock Garden of the Gods📍 Kissing Camels Garden of the Gods
1

Park at the Garden of the Gods Visitor Center and set out on foot—the park has been free since 1909, when Charles Perkins bequeathed it to Colorado Springs with one condition: "free forever." Its 300-million-year-old red sandstone formations were tilted vertical by the same tectonic uplift that pushed up Pikes Peak. Cathedral Spires, Kissing Camels, Balanced Rock—everything is within a 10-minute walk.

⏱ 90 min
2

Drive up Pikes Peak via the Pikes Peak Highway—19 miles of paved road from Cascade to 14,115 feet. The song America the Beautiful was composed after a climb to the summit in 1893. At the top, the cafeteria serves donuts fried at 14,115 feet. Toll $15/person, allow 2.5 hours round-trip.

⏱ 150 min

Activities

Before heading back to Denver (1h15 on I-25) or Colorado Springs airport, take one last bike ride through Garden of the Gods. The park offers marked bike trails among the red formations—Pikes Peak as a backdrop, free entry, and 6 million visitors a year who'll prove you right for coming back.
