Balikpapan

Oil city founded in 1897, essential gateway hub into East Kalimantan

★★★☆☆
📍 Kampung Atas Air Balikpapan📍 Margomulyo Mangrove Forest Balikpapan📍 Kemala Beach Balikpapan📍 Monpera Park Balikpapan
1

Balikpapan, nicknamed 'the oil city' of Borneo, was born on 10 February 1897 when the Dutch company Mijnbouw Maatschappij struck its first wells. Today it is a modern port city built into the hillsides around a vast bay, and the main entry point for East Kalimantan. From the airport, head straight for the waterfront to gauge the scale of the bay — tankers, phinisi sailing boats and fishermen's canoes share the anchorage. The contrast between the glass towers of the oil district and the stilt houses along the shore tells the story of the city in a single glance.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Margomulyo Mangrove Forest lines the seafront north of Balikpapan. You walk along wooden boardwalks built through thick mangrove stands, where proboscis monkeys and long-tailed macaques are regularly spotted. The trail takes about an hour and is mostly flat. It's a fine first contact with Borneo's coastal ecosystems before heading inland.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Kemala Beach, in the southwest of the city, is Balikpapan's most accessible beach. Lively bars line the sand and locals gather at sunset — the view across the bay as the sky turns orange is a fitting welcome to Kalimantan. Not a postcard beach, but a genuine slice of everyday Indonesian life.

⏱ 90 min

The Kebun Sayur craft market is a surprise for anyone expecting a vegetable market — its name means 'vegetable garden' but the stalls overflow with Dayak souvenirs, Kalimantan pearls, local fabric and carved ironwood. It's the best place in Balikpapan to pick up handicrafts before heading into the interior.

Day 2

Balikpapan

Oil city founded in 1897, essential gateway hub into East Kalimantan

★★★☆☆
📍 Samboja Lestari Orangutan Sanctuary Kalimantan📍 Bukit Bangkirai canopy bridge East Kalimantan📍 Sun Bear Sanctuary Samboja📍 Sungai Wain Protection Forest Balikpapan
1

Samboja Lestari is one of the world's best-known orangutan sanctuaries, run by the Borneo Orangutan Survival Foundation (BOSF). Located 38 km from Balikpapan (about 1.5 hours by car), the reserve covers 1,800 hectares of forest that has been replanted tree by tree since 2001 — over a million trees representing 1,000 species. Around 120 orangutans and 70 sun bears rescued from deforested areas are being rehabilitated here. Visits take place at 8 am and 1 pm, each lasting about 4 hours. Don't miss feeding time on the floating islands designed for orangutans that cannot be released into the wild — the animals' behaviour during feeding is extraordinary.

⏱ 180 min
2

About 1.5 hours' drive from Samboja, the Bukit Bangkirai hills shelter a 1,500-hectare dipterocarp forest where bangkirai hardwood trees tower 40 to 50 m, some 150 years old and up to 2.3 m in diameter. The canopy bridge, stretching 64 m at a height of 30 to 40 m and connecting five bangkirai trees, offers vertiginous views over Borneo's emerald jungle. Arrive early — gibbons, hornbills and red langurs are most active in the morning.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The protected forest of the Sungai Wain river, 40 km from Balikpapan, is one of the last fragments of primary lowland forest in the region. A 3-hour guided trek through the dense canopy offers chances to spot orangutans, sun bears, hornbills and dozens of butterfly species. The humidity is intense — bring plenty of water and wear long sleeves.

⏱ 120 min

Day 3

Bontang

Industrial town and gateway to Kutai National Park and its wild orangutans

★★★★☆
📍 Kutai National Park Prevab orangutan station📍 Sangkima Forest Walkway Kutai📍 Teluk Kaba bay Bontang📍 Kutai National Park entrance Sangatta
1

Kutai National Park is one of Indonesia's oldest: declared a nature reserve in 1934 by the Sultan of Kutai, it covers 192,709 hectares of primary lowland rainforest. The Prevab research station, one of the country's oldest for orangutan studies, is the starting point for guided treks with rangers. The forest is classic lowland dipterocarp — trees soaring 30 to 40 m, vines, and an incredible soundscape of bird calls and gibbon song. The orangutans here are completely wild, with no guarantee of a sighting — which is precisely what makes the experience so valuable. Over 300 bird species have been recorded in the park, including 7 species of hornbill.

⏱ 180 min
2

The Sangkima forest walk, accessible from Bontang in about an hour, is the third gateway into Kutai Park. Less demanding than the Prevab sector, it follows boardwalks through mangroves and lowland forest housing one of Borneo's oldest trees — an ulin (ironwood), a virtually rot-proof endemic species. Proboscis monkeys, macaques and hornbills are commonly spotted along the boardwalk.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Teluk Kaba bay, between Bontang and Sangatta, is reached in 45 minutes by speedboat from Bontang port. It's one of the park's best-kept secrets: a sandy cove backed by primary forest where green turtles come to nest. The snorkelling offshore reveals healthy coral and reef fish. Bring your own mask — facilities are minimal.

⏱ 120 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 136 km · 2h
Day 4

Samarinda

Capital of East Kalimantan on the Mahakam River, habitat of the freshwater Pesut dolphin

★★★★☆
📍 Mahakam River cruise Samarinda📍 Samarinda waterfront Tepian📍 Dayak village Mahakam river📍 Pesut Mahakam dolphin observation
1

The Islamic Center Samarinda mosque (Baitul Muttaqien) is the second-largest mosque in Southeast Asia after Jakarta's Istiqlal. Its seven golden minarets, dome spanning 35 m and 15,000-person capacity dominate the Samarinda skyline. The building, completed in 2018 after 20 years of construction, blends Kutai Malay, Ottoman and Andalusian architectural influences. Non-Muslims can visit outside prayer times — the panoramic terrace offers a sweeping view of the Mahakam.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Mahakam River is Borneo's third-longest watercourse at 980 km, from its sources in the central highlands down to the Makassar Strait. In Samarinda, the river is about 500 m wide and teeming with activity: cargo barges loaded with coal, fishermen casting nets, and klotok wooden boats that serve as the local bus network. Rent a klotok from the main wharf and head upstream for a few hours — the further you go, the wilder the riverbanks become.

⏱ 120 min
3

The Pesut Mahakam is the freshwater Irrawaddy dolphin (Orcaella brevirostris) — the official emblem of East Kalimantan province. Fewer than 80 individuals remain in the Mahakam, making it one of the most critically endangered freshwater cetacean populations on earth. The best viewing zone is between Muara Kaman and Muara Muntai, about 3 hours upstream from Samarinda. Sightings are not guaranteed but the river journey itself through flooded forests and stilt villages is reward enough.

⏱ 90 min
4

The Pasar Pagi neighbourhood is the historic trading heart of Samarinda, with the Great Mosque Darussalam built in 1882 by Sultan Aji Muhammad Sulaiman and the old Chinese trading quarter nearby. The streets are noisy, narrow and lined with food stalls — a genuine slice of Mahakam-river-town life without any tourist gloss.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Mahakam Bridge promenade in Samarinda is lit in rainbow colours at night. Floating cafés moored alongside the bridge serve coffee and fried snacks while you watch the cargo barges drift past. It's the most popular evening hangout in town.

⏱ 90 min

Local operators run canoe excursions on Mahakam tributaries to visit Dayak villages upstream of Samarinda. The houses are more modest than the longhouses of upper Kalimantan, but the hospitality is genuine — expect offers of tuak (palm wine) and stories about the river. Allow a full half-day.

Day 5

Berau

Departure base for the Derawan archipelago and its 500 coral species

★★☆☆☆
📍 Tanjung Batu port Berau speedboat Derawan📍 Berau Kalimarau airport📍 Labuan Cermin lake Biduk-Biduk Berau📍 Derawan Islands departure point
1

Berau is the provincial town that serves as the gateway to the Derawan archipelago — one of the world's top marine biodiversity hotspots, ranked second globally for coral diversity with over 500 species. The town itself is compact and functional. From Berau, speedboats leave from Tanjung Batu port (about 2 hours from the town centre) for the 1-to-2-hour crossing to the islands.

⏱ 60 min
2

Tanjung Redeb, Berau's capital, is split by the confluence of two rivers — the Kelay and the Segah — which merge to form the Berau River. The Gunung Tabur Palace, built in 1872 and now a museum, was the seat of the Gunung Tabur sultanate. Inside you'll find royal weapons, traditional costumes and historical photographs. The views from the palace over the river junction are the best in town.

⏱ 60 min
3

Not far away on the Kelay River, the Sambaliung Palace is the other relic of the two rival sultanates created in the early 18th century from the original Berau sultanate. Smaller and less restored than Gunung Tabur, it has a quieter charm and fewer visitors.

⏱ 60 min
4

If you arrive early in Berau, make the detour to Labuan Cermin lake in the Biduk-Biduk sub-district, about 6 hours south of Berau. This extraordinary two-layer lake has freshwater on the surface and saltwater on the bottom, creating such clarity that the rock formations below are visible as if through glass. It's a long drive for a single stop but absolutely worth it if your schedule allows.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The evening in Berau is the time to prepare for the coming days in the archipelago: book your diving and snorkelling trips, check the latest conditions at Kakaban's jellyfish lake (temporary closures are possible) and stock up on sunscreen and cash — ATMs and shops are scarce on the islands.

Day 6

Derawan

Main island of the Derawan archipelago — macro-diving paradise and sea-turtle haven

★★★★★
📍 Derawan Island beach snorkeling Berau📍 Pulau Derawan dive site blue-ringed octopus📍 Pulau Gusung sandbar Derawan📍 Sangalaki Island manta ray Derawan
1

Derawan Island is the main hub of the eponymous archipelago — 31 islands and reefs spread over 160 km of coastline. Drop your bags at your guesthouse and head straight to the jetty: green turtles swim just metres from the wooden piers, surfacing to breathe every few minutes. The island is small enough to walk around in an hour. The waters immediately offshore teem with life — clownfish, nudibranchs, seahorses and juvenile reef sharks are visible in waist-deep water.

⏱ 120 min
2

Pulau Gusung is a white sandbar submerged at high tide and exposed at low tide, 10–15 minutes by boat from Derawan. At its peak, it's a ribbon of pure white sand surrounded by turquoise water with no vegetation at all — one of the most photogenic spots in the archipelago. Bring sun protection — there is zero shade.

⏱ 60 min
3

Sangalaki Island, a 2-hour speedboat ride from Derawan, is one of the world's best stations for swimming with manta rays. The mantas gather here year-round to feed on plankton concentrations. The island is also a major green-turtle nesting site — between June and September, up to 50 turtles come ashore each night to lay eggs.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Night diving off Derawan is an experience apart: turtles sleep on the seabed, octopuses come alive, and phosphorescent plankton light up the water column with every fin stroke. Several dive operators on the island offer guided night dives — book in the morning for an evening slot.

Day 7

Derawan

Main island of the Derawan archipelago — macro-diving paradise and sea-turtle haven

★★★★★
📍 Kakaban jellyfish lake stingless Indonesia📍 Kakaban Barracuda Point dive site📍 Maratua atoll Big Fish Country dive📍 Maratua island Bajau village
1

Kakaban's lake is one of the strangest natural curiosities on the planet. Kakaban — whose name means 'to embrace' in the local language — is a raised coral atoll whose interior lagoon became cut off from the sea roughly 2 million years ago. Trapped inside, four species of jellyfish evolved to lose their stinging cells entirely (they had no predators to defend against). Today you can swim among millions of translucent, pulsating, completely harmless jellyfish in warm, slightly brackish water. The sensation of being surrounded by these gentle creatures in an enclosed jungle lake is unlike anything else.

⏱ 120 min
2

Maratua is the largest atoll in the Derawan archipelago, perched at the edge of the continental shelf where the seabed plunges into the deep Sulawesi Sea. The diving here is known as Big Fish Country: barracuda walls, grey reef sharks, eagle rays, whale sharks (seasonal) and giant trevally patrol the outer reef. The coral gardens on the inside of the atoll are equally spectacular, with pristine table corals and visibility regularly exceeding 30 m.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

In the late afternoon on Maratua, stroll through one of the island's Bajau villages and observe the stilt houses built over the reef flat. The Bajau — also known as 'sea nomads' — have lived on or above the water for centuries. Their freedivers are legendary, hunting fish at depths of 20 m or more on a single breath. The village sunset over the turquoise lagoon is a quiet, memorable end to the day.

⏱ 90 min

Tarakan

Island-city of northern Kalimantan, 1942 Japanese target and proboscis-monkey sanctuary

★★☆☆☆
📍 Mangrove Conservation Area proboscis monkeys Tarakan📍 Tarakan WWII history museum📍 Amal Beach Tarakan📍 Australian Monument Tarakan WWII
1

Tarakan is an island-city in northern Kalimantan with a particularly intense wartime history: it was the first target of the Japanese invasion of the Dutch East Indies on 11 January 1942, seized for its oil reserves. The Allied recapture in May 1945 was one of the last major battles of the Pacific War. Today, remnants of bunkers and Japanese tunnels can be visited in the hills above the town. The small WWII memorial park near the airport recounts the sequence of events.

⏱ 90 min
2

Right in central Tarakan, the Mangrove and Proboscis Monkey Conservation Reserve is a small green surprise: a boardwalk trail through dense mangrove that is home to a resident population of proboscis monkeys — the big-nosed primates endemic to Borneo. The best viewing times are early morning and late afternoon when they come to the trees nearest the boardwalk.

⏱ 75 min
3

Amal Beach, 11 km from the town centre, is Tarakan's best seaside spot. Under the coconut palms, stalls sell fried fish and fresh coconut water while local families enjoy the weekend. The water is warm but murky — it's more a social beach than a snorkelling destination.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Tarakan's Orchid Garden (Taman Anggrek) brings together dozens of wild orchid species from Borneo, including the famous Black Orchid (Coelogyne pandurata), a rare endemic species that has become the floral emblem of East Kalimantan. The garden is small but beautifully maintained and worth a 45-minute visit.

Banjarmasin

City of a thousand rivers, Lok Baintan floating market since the 16th-century Banjar sultanate

★★★☆☆
📍 Lok Baintan Floating Market Banjarmasin sunrise📍 Muara Kuin Floating Market Banjarmasin📍 Masjid Sultan Suriansyah Banjarmasin📍 Banjarmasin canal boat tour
1

Banjarmasin, the 'city of a thousand rivers' in South Kalimantan, sits just centimetres above sea level on a delta laced with canals, tributaries and tidal channels. Life here revolves around water: houses are built on rafts or stilts, transport runs by klotok boat, and the daily rhythms of trade, worship and socialising all happen on or beside the river. The Sabilal Muhtadin Grand Mosque, rising above the Martapura River, is the city's landmark — its green dome and minarets are visible from every bridge.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Lok Baintan floating market is one of Indonesia's oldest living traditions: women in wide-brimmed hats paddle jukung canoes loaded with fruit, vegetables, cakes and spices, trading boat-to-boat in the middle of the Martapura River. The market has operated in some form since the Banjar sultanate in the 16th century. Arrive by klotok from the Banua Anyar wharf (about 45 minutes upstream) before 7 am to see the market at peak activity — by 9:30 it's winding down.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

A klotok cruise through Banjarmasin's canals reveals the city's daily life better than any walking tour: women washing clothes on the riverbank, children diving off jetties, floating mosques moored in side channels and vendors paddling from house to house. The network of waterways is vast — allow at least 2 hours to explore the inner canals beyond the main river.

Banjarmasin

City of a thousand rivers, Lok Baintan floating market since the 16th-century Banjar sultanate

★★★☆☆
📍 Martapura diamond market South Kalimantan📍 Cempaka diamond digging traditional Banjarmasin📍 Pasar Terapung Muara Kuin Banjarmasin📍 Museum Lambung Mangkurat Banjarbaru
1

Martapura, 45 km from Banjarmasin, is the diamond and gemstone capital of Kalimantan. The Pasar Intan (diamond market) is a bustling hall where dealers display loose stones — diamonds, sapphires, amethysts and agates mined from the surrounding hills. Bargaining is expected. The nearby Cahaya Bumi Selamat mosque, built almost entirely from gemstone-encrusted materials, is an architectural curiosity worth a stop.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Lambung Mangkurat Museum in Banjarbaru houses a remarkable ethnographic collection on Banjar culture — ceramics, Banjar wedding costumes, ancient weapons and documents tracing the region's history from the Hindu Negara Dipa kingdom to the Islamic Banjar sultanate and Dutch colonisation. Allow about an hour.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Muara Kuin floating market, at the confluence of the Kuin and Barito rivers, is the oldest of Banjarmasin's floating markets. Smaller and more authentic than Lok Baintan, it caters almost exclusively to locals. Come before 7 am — the trading is over by dawn.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Radap Rahayu dance, a traditional Banjar dance depicting angels descending from heaven to bring wisdom, is occasionally performed at cultural centres or during ceremonies in the Banjarmasin area. Ask your accommodation if any performances are scheduled during your stay — it's one of the region's most graceful art forms.

Pelaihari

Small town in southern Kalimantan with green rolling hills dubbed Teletubbies Hills

★★☆☆☆
📍 Rimpi Hills Teletubbies Hills Pelaihari South Kalimantan📍 Pelaihari scenic hills sunset South Kalimantan📍 Tanah Laut regency South Kalimantan nature
1

Pelaihari is the capital of Tanah Laut district ('sea of land'), 66 km from Banjarmasin. The area is known for its Rimpi Hills — undulating green hills that locals have nicknamed 'Teletubbies Hills' for their resemblance to the children's-show landscape. The hills are covered in low grass and scattered trees, offering panoramic views of the southern Kalimantan coast. The best time to visit is late afternoon when the light softens.

⏱ 90 min
2

Bajuin Waterfall, 10 km from Pelaihari on the foothills of the Meratus range, comprises three successive drops in a forested gorge. The pools at the base are swimmable — the water is cool and surprisingly clear for a jungle stream. The trail takes about 3 hours round trip from the road.

⏱ 120 min
3

The road from Banjarmasin to Pelaihari hugs the southern coast of Kalimantan, allowing stops at Sarang Tiung and Swarangan beaches — quiet stretches of sand where local fishing communities dry their catch in the sun. These are not resort beaches, but genuine working coastline with a character all their own.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Sunset over the Rimpi Hills is Pelaihari's main draw: settle on the principal ridge and watch the green mounds turn golden as the light fades. The spot is popular with locals on weekends but quiet during the week. Bring a picnic — there are no vendors on the hill.

Day 12

Pontianak

The only capital in the world sitting exactly on the equator — Tugu Khatulistiwa monument since 1928

★★★☆☆
📍 Tugu Khatulistiwa Equator Monument Pontianak📍 Keraton Kadriah Sultan Palace Pontianak📍 Kapuas River cruise Pontianak📍 Betang longhouse replica Pontianak
1

Pontianak is the only provincial capital in the world to sit exactly on the equator — a distinction that has earned it the Tugu Khatulistiwa monument, first erected in 1928 by a Dutch expedition and since rebuilt as a full-scale landmark. A red line painted on the ground marks the 0° latitude. Twice a year, at the March and September equinoxes, vertical objects cast no shadow at noon — a phenomenon that draws small crowds of curious visitors.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Keraton Kadriah, the royal palace of the Pontianak sultanate founded in 1771 by Syarif Abdurrahman Alkadrie, stands on the banks of the Kapuas River. The yellow wooden building houses a museum of royal regalia, weapons, textiles and photographs. The palace courtyard, shaded by old frangipani trees, is one of the most peaceful spots in a noisy city.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Kapuas River is Indonesia's longest at 1,143 km, crossing the heart of West Kalimantan to the South China Sea. In Pontianak, the river is wide, muddy and busy — cargo boats, ferries and fishing canoes jostle for space. A short boat ride from the city centre gives you a feel for the sheer scale of this waterway, which serves as the main artery for communities deep in the Borneo interior.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

The Betang House — a reconstructed Dayak longhouse on Jalan M.T. Haryono in Pontianak — offers a preview of what awaits in Putussibau. The building demonstrates the communal architecture of the Dayak: a single roof sheltering an entire community, with shared corridors and individual family rooms. Guides explain the social structure and customs linked to longhouse living.

The Flamboyan market, on the banks of the Kapuas in Pontianak, is the city's liveliest popular market, especially in the early morning. Fish, tropical fruit, spices and Dayak handicrafts fill the stalls. The riverside setting adds atmosphere — sampans unload produce straight onto the quayside.

The Tua Pek Kong pagoda and Pontianak's Chinatown bear witness to the Chinese presence in the city since the late 18th century. Hakka miners arrived in the Sambas and Monterado goldfields and gradually settled along the Kapuas. The temple, one of the oldest in West Kalimantan, is a colourful focal point in a neighbourhood of traditional Chinese shophouses.

Activities

In the afternoon, head to the Kapuas riverbanks for a sunset sampan ride — the stilt houses, floating mosques and fishermen casting nets in the golden light make for a fitting farewell to Pontianak. Day 14

Putussibau

Last urban outpost on the upper Kapuas, guardian of the Dayak longhouses

★★☆☆☆
📍 Dayak longhouse betang Semangkok Putussibau📍 Betung Kerihun National Park entrance West Kalimantan📍 Kapuas River Putussibau upper📍 Sungai Utik Iban Dayak longhouse

Putussibau, 800 km upstream on the Kapuas from Pontianak, is the last town before the primary forest of the Borneo heartland. Accessible by a 1-hour flight from Pontianak, it sits at the confluence of several Kapuas tributaries and serves as the base for expeditions into Dayak Iban and Dayak Kayan territory. The town is quiet, dusty and utterly authentic — there's no tourist infrastructure beyond a handful of guesthouses and warungs.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Sungai Utik longhouse, about 1.5 hours by road from Putussibau, is considered by many to be the most authentic inhabited Iban betang on Borneo. The structure, some 200 m long and housing over 300 people from 28 families, sits on the banks of the Utik River. The community has fiercely defended its surrounding forest against logging companies and was recognised with a UNDP Equator Prize in 2019. Visitors can sleep in the longhouse, share meals and witness daily rituals — advance arrangement through a Putussibau guide is essential.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Betung Kerihun National Park, 2 hours from Putussibau by river along the Embaloh, covers 800,000 hectares of tropical rainforest — one of the largest intact forest blocks on Borneo. The park shelters wild orangutans, clouded leopards, sun bears and over 300 bird species. Multi-day treks with a mandatory guide follow river-and-trail routes connecting remote Dayak villages deep in the forest.

Danau Sentarum National Park, a 4-hour canoe ride downstream from Putussibau, is the largest wetland on the island of Borneo — 132,000 hectares of seasonal lakes, peat swamp forest and flooded grasslands. The park is home to over 240 freshwater fish species, many found nowhere else. Between October and January, the lakes swell to form an inland sea; in the dry season, they shrink to isolated pools teeming with fish. Dayak Iban fishing communities live inside the park in floating houses that rise and fall with the water level.

Return to Putussibau before the last flight to Pontianak. Before leaving, stroll along the Kapuas riverbanks upstream of the town — the wooden jetties, canoes and the forest pressing in from both banks give you one final image of the Borneo interior. Buy a bag of locally grown Kapuas coffee from the market as a souvenir.

Activities

Putussibau has a small airport with daily connections to Pontianak (PSU→PNK, 1-hour flight). From Pontianak, connections to Jakarta, Surabaya or Kuching (Malaysia) are available. Check schedules well in advance — rotations are limited and can change. The journey ends here, at the edge of one of the last great rainforests on earth.
