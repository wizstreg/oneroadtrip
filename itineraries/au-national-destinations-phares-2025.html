Australia Grand Tour: Natural Wonders and Aboriginal Heritage

📅 12 days📍 11 stops🚗 11677 km⏱ 38h27

Melbourne and its street art laneways, Cairns and the Great Barrier Reef, the Wangetti Trail facing the Coral Sea, the swirling white-sand spirals of Whitehaven, ancient rock art of Kakadu, Darwin's wild Top End, the quokkas of Rottnest and the whale sharks of Ningaloo. A grand tour of Australia from east to west, from the tropics to the coastal desert.

✨ Highlights
  • Kakadu National Park: some of the world's oldest rock art, dual UNESCO listing for nature and culture
  • Ningaloo Reef: swim with whale sharks (March-July) and humpback whales (July-Oct)
  • Whitehaven Beach and Hill Inlet: the world's purest silica sands
  • Rottnest Island: 10,000 wild quokkas, beaches and snorkeling without cars
  • Darwin and the Top End: crocodiles, ethnic markets and gateway to Arnhem Land
📆 Best period

May, June, July, August, September

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 14 and 18 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Peak Season (December-February): Summer holidays, warmest weather, perfect for beaches, highest prices, most crowded attractions, school holidays.

Shoulder Seasons (March-May, September-November): Excellent weather, fewer crowds, moderate prices, ideal for most regions and outdoor activities.

Winter (June-August): Perfect for tropical north (dry season), ski season in Snowy Mountains, cooler in south, lower prices except ski areas.

Regional Variations: Northern Australia best in dry season (May-October), southern regions pleasant year-round, consider vast distances and climate zones.

🌍 What to discover in AU?

Unique wildlife, stunning beaches, and vast Outback create the ultimate adventure destination. From Sydney Opera House to Great Barrier Reef and Uluru's spiritual presence. Experience Aboriginal culture, world-class wine regions, and cosmopolitan cities alongside dangerous animals. Australia offers life-changing experiences across diverse landscapes and vibrant cities.

Day 1

Melbourne

Where laneway coffee culture meets world-class street art

★★★★★
1

Federation Square opened in October 2002 on a former railway platform. Its deconstructivist architecture, designed by LAB Architecture, wraps facades in 470,000 panels of sandstone, zinc and glass - a visual jolt against the neoclassical Flinders Street Station across the road.

⏱ 45 min
2

Hosier Lane is Australia's most celebrated open-air street art gallery. The walls are constantly renewed with fresh works: stencils, murals, paste-ups. Banksy left pieces here until 2010, and the laneway remains a living canvas for local and international artists.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Royal Arcade dates from 1869 - it's the oldest continuously operating shopping arcade in Australia. Beneath its Victorian glass canopy, two statues of Gog and Magog have flanked the clock since 1892, striking the hours with ceremonial precision.

⏱ 40 min
4

The National Gallery of Victoria, founded in 1861, is Australia's oldest art museum. Its permanent collections span Australian, Asian and European art. The grand stained-glass ceiling at the entrance, installed in 1968, remains an icon of the building.

⏱ 90 min
5

The St Kilda Pier shelters one of the rare urban colonies of little penguins (Eudyptula minor). The tiny birds return at nightfall, waddling back to their nests. Flash photography is prohibited to avoid disturbing them.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Explore the central laneways: Degraves Street, Centre Place, AC/DC Lane. These pedestrian alleys concentrate specialty coffee roasters, independent galleries and facades blanketed in vibrant murals - Melbourne's hidden cultural arteries.

⏱ 90 min

Queen Victoria Market, operating since 1878 across 7 hectares, is the largest covered market in the Southern Hemisphere. Fresh produce, local crafts, European delis - an immersion into everyday Melbourne life.

⏱ 75 min

Hop on the free City Circle Tram for a loop around the CBD: it connects Flinders Street, Docklands, Spring Street and Bourke Street in 30 to 45 minutes, with onboard commentary about Melbourne's architecture and history.

⏱ 45 min

Day 2

Echuca

Paddle steamer capital on the mighty Murray River

★★☆☆☆
1

The Port of Echuca, built in 1865, was at its peak in the 1870s the largest inland port in Australia. Its red gum wharf stretched 332 metres and welcomed more than 240 paddle steamers a year, ferrying wool, wheat and timber downriver to Melbourne.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Discovery Centre traces the golden age of river trade on the Murray. Interactive exhibits, working steam engines and boatmen's stories bring to life the pioneers who transported cargo through Australia's inland waterways.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Murray - the world's third-longest navigable river after the Nile and the Amazon - meanders over 2,000 km from the foothills of Kosciuszko to the Southern Ocean. The Yorta Yorta Aboriginal people have lived along its banks for more than 40,000 years.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Board an authentic paddle steamer for a one-hour cruise - PS Canberra (built 1913), PS Pevensey or PS Emmylou. The captain explains the steam workings and recounts tales from the port's heyday as you chug past river red gum forests.

⏱ 60 min

The Silo Art Trail links several grain silos painted by Australian artists between Echuca and Sea Lake - the country's largest outdoor gallery. A few of the most spectacular murals are within an hour's drive from town.

⏱ 90 min

Walk or cycle the Murray River banks to spot black swans, Australian pelicans and royal herons among the river red gum forests. These towering eucalypts supplied the timber for the port's wharves in the 19th century.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 2781 km · 3h
Day 3

Cairns

Gateway to the Great Barrier Reef and ancient rainforests

★★★☆☆
1

The Bulmba-ja Arts Centre (formerly Cairns Art Gallery) is a space dedicated to First Nations arts of Queensland and regional contemporary creation. Its rotating exhibitions showcase works by Djabugay, Yidinji and Kuku Yalanji artists.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Tanks Art Centre occupies three former underground fuel storage tanks built during World War II. These concrete cylinders, 16 metres tall, now host installations, concerts and contemporary art exhibitions in a hauntingly unique setting.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Cairns Esplanade Lagoon is a 4,800 m² saltwater swimming pool, open 24/7 and free. Built in 2003 beside the coastal wetlands, it offers a safe swimming spot in a city without an urban sandy beach - and protection from marine stingers.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Board the Kuranda Scenic Railway: 34 km of track inaugurated in 1891 climbs through the Barron Gorge in 1h45. Fifteen tunnels and forty bridges thread through World Heritage-listed tropical rainforest clinging to the mountainside.

⏱ 105 min

The Tjapukai Aboriginal Cultural Park, 15 km north of Cairns, offers immersive experiences with Djabugay traditional custodians: boomerang throwing, fire-starting by friction, dance ceremonies and decoding ancient rock art.

⏱ 120 min

From Cairns port, a day trip to Green Island - a 12-hectare coral cay 27 km offshore - allows you to snorkel on the fringing reef directly from the beach, without needing a boat to reach the reef.

⏱ 480 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 29 km · 35min
Day 4

Palm Cove

Where centuries-old melaleucas frame a tropical paradise

★★★☆☆
1

Palm Cove is a miniature seaside village - 2,450 residents - tucked 27 km north of Cairns. Its esplanade is lined with an avenue of centuries-old melaleucas (over 500 years), their roots plunging into the sand like natural sculptures.

⏱ 45 min
2

Port Douglas, 40 km further north, is a fishing village turned tropical resort town. Its main street, Macrossan Street, packs restaurants and boutiques two steps from Four Mile Beach - a 6-km sweep of sand facing the Coral Sea.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Tackle the first section of the Wangetti Trail from Palm Cove Jetty. This 7.8 km route to Ellis Beach (Grade 3, about 3h one-way) crosses Macalister Range National Park along ancient Djabugay pathways. Carry at least 3 litres of water.

⏱ 180 min

From Palm Cove or Port Douglas, tour operators offer half-day snorkeling trips to the outer Great Barrier Reef. Green turtles, parrotfish and rays are regularly spotted in these pristine waters.

⏱ 240 min

Drive the Captain Cook Highway between Palm Cove and Port Douglas at sunset: the road snakes between the forested escarpments of the Macalister Range and the Coral Sea, with plunging views over Double Island and Trinity Beach.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 658 km · 8h
Day 5

Airlie Beach

Launching point to the Whitsundays and Earth's purest silica beaches

★★★★☆
1

Whitehaven Beach stretches for 7 km along Whitsunday Island. Its sand contains 98% pure silica - delivered by ocean currents over millions of years. So fine it doesn't retain the sun's heat, it squeaks beneath your feet. The Ngaro, traditional custodians, called them the 'whispering sands'.

⏱ 180 min
2

Hill Inlet, at the northern end of Whitehaven, is a tidal estuary where white sand and turquoise waters swirl in shifting spirals with the tides. The lookout above Tongue Bay, reached via a 20-minute walk, offers the Whitsundays' most photographed panorama.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Whitsunday archipelago comprises 74 islands within the Great Barrier Reef Marine Park, named by Captain Cook who sailed through in June 1770 - on Whitsunday. Only a handful of islands are inhabited; the rest are protected as national park.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Book a day cruise from Port of Airlie to Whitehaven Beach and Hill Inlet. Catamarans and speedboats make the crossing in 1h to 1h30. Access to Whitsunday Island is exclusively by boat - there are no roads.

⏱ 480 min

From Airlie Beach or Hamilton Island, a scenic flight by seaplane or light aircraft reveals Heart Reef - a naturally heart-shaped coral formation - and the turquoise gradients of the archipelago, visible only from the air.

⏱ 60 min

In the evening, the Airlie Beach Lagoon - a free outdoor saltwater pool - offers safe swimming in tropical waters without the risk of irukandji (box jellyfish). A secure alternative to the archipelago's beaches, open year-round.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 2721 km · 3h
Day 6

Darwin

Where crocodiles cruise past sunset markets

★★★★☆
1

The Museum and Art Gallery of the Northern Territory holds the most extensive collection of Aboriginal art in the Territory. It also houses archives of Cyclone Tracy, which devastated Darwin on Christmas Day 1974, plus a section devoted to marine species of the Timor Sea.

⏱ 90 min
2

Crocosaurus Cove, right in the heart of Mitchell Street, houses the world's largest display of Australian reptiles. The saltwater crocodiles here can weigh up to 700 kg. The attraction features the only public-access 200,000-litre freshwater stingray aquarium.

⏱ 90 min
3

The George Brown Darwin Botanic Gardens, 2 km from the centre, cover 42 hectares. Founded in 1886, they gather wild orchids, oil palms, heliconias and a collection of First Nations medicinal plants from the Top End.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

On Thursdays and Sundays (April to October), the Mindil Beach Sunset Market gathers over 200 stalls beside the Timor Sea. Indonesian, Thai, and Australian Creole cuisine, Indigenous crafts, didgeridoos and a blazing sunset backdrop - a Darwin institution.

⏱ 120 min

Dare to try the Cage of Death at Crocosaurus Cove: a transparent cage is lowered into the pool of an adult saltwater crocodile. Fifteen minutes face-to-face with one of the world's largest predators.

⏱ 60 min

A sunset cruise on Darwin Harbour reveals the extent of the port - the third-largest natural harbour in the world. It was from this harbour that Japan bombed Darwin on 19 February 1942: 188 aircraft, more than at Pearl Harbor.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 292 km · 3h30
Day 7

Kakadu

Where 30,000 years of rock art meets untamed floodplains

★☆☆☆☆
1

Ubirr is a rock outcrop on the banks of the Nadab floodplain in the East Alligator region. Its rock galleries document 20,000 years of continuous human occupation: X-ray style paintings showing skeletons of barramundi and turtles, Mimi spirits with thread-like bodies, and a depiction of the thylacine (Tasmanian tiger) - a species extinct from the mainland for over 2,000 years.

⏱ 90 min
2

Burrunggui (Nourlangie Rock) is a sandstone promontory of the Arnhem Land escarpment. Its shelters contain paintings layered over millennia: creator ancestors, Namarrgon the 'Lightning Man', and depictions of the first European buffalo that arrived in the 1880s. More than 200,000 visitors come here annually.

⏱ 90 min
3

Kakadu covers 20,000 km² (the size of Slovenia), UNESCO World Heritage-listed since 1981 for both natural AND cultural values simultaneously - a rare dual inscription. The park contains over 5,000 rock art sites, some estimated at more than 30,000 years old.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Take the Ubirr panoramic trail to the Nadab lookout (500 m, 20 min). At sunset, the view over the floodplains, the Arnhem Land escarpment and swirling flocks of birds is among Australia's most breathtaking.

⏱ 60 min

A dawn cruise on Yellow Water Billabong offers sightings of wild saltwater crocodiles, sea eagles, jabiru storks and Asian otters in the rice-grass of the floodplain - an untamed wetland teeming with life.

⏱ 120 min

Visit the Warradjan Aboriginal Cultural Centre near Cooinda: this museum shaped like a pig-nosed turtle explains the six seasons of the Bininj/Mungguy calendar, Dreaming rituals and the links between clans and their Country.

⏱ 90 min

In the dry season (May-October), reach Jim Jim Falls via dirt track (4WD recommended, 60 km from Arnhem Highway). The falls plunge 150 m from the escarpment into a natural plunge pool surrounded by red sandstone walls.

⏱ 240 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 25 km · 30min
Day 8

Jabiru

Gateway to Arnhem Land and Kakadu's deepest secrets

★☆☆☆☆
1

Jabiru is the only town within Kakadu National Park, founded in 1982 for workers of the Ranger uranium mine. It serves as a logistical base for exploring the park's most remote eastern sites.

⏱ 30 min
2

Injalak Hill, in Kunwinjku Country just at the entrance to Arnhem Land (Gunbalanya), bears hundreds of rock paintings and engravings. It's one of the most accessible and densest sites in the region. Visits must be conducted with a traditional owner guide.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Cross the East Alligator River by boat to reach Gunbalanya (Oenpelli) in Arnhem Land. An entry permit is mandatory, issued by the Northern Land Council. The community welcomes guided tours led by Kunwinjku traditional owners.

⏱ 240 min

'Ethical Adventures' tours in Marrithiyal Country offer bush experiences with traditional custodians: identifying medicinal plants, understanding seasonal hunting systems and decoding ceremonial paintings.

⏱ 180 min

From Jabiru, a scenic flight by light aircraft over the Arnhem Land floodplains at sunset reveals a golden sea of rice grass dotted with giant termite mounds - landscapes otherwise inaccessible during the wet season.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 3584 km · 3h
Day 9

Perth

Australia's most isolated capital, brilliant on the Swan River

★★★★☆
1

Kings Park covers 400 hectares atop Mount Eliza, overlooking the Swan River and Perth's skyline. Founded in 1872, it's one of the world's largest urban parks. Its botanic garden shelters over 3,000 species of Western Australian native plants.

⏱ 90 min
2

Fremantle, 20 km southwest, is Perth's historic port, heritage-listed for its preserved Victorian architecture. Founded in 1829, its honey-coloured limestone streets concentrate the 1855 colonial prison, the 1897 covered market and craft breweries.

⏱ 120 min
3

The Art Gallery of Western Australia, founded in 1895, holds the state's largest collection of Australian art. Its permanent rooms cover desert Aboriginal art from the west, colonial paintings and international contemporary art.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Cruise down the Swan River from central Perth to Fremantle by public ferry (45 min). The view over the Heirisson Island wetlands and riverside parklands is more scenic than driving.

⏱ 90 min

Fremantle Markets (open Fri-Sun) is Perth's liveliest market, in an 1897 covered market building. Fresh produce, Swan Valley wines, local crafts and food stalls jostle beneath the steel-and-glass canopy.

⏱ 90 min

Fremantle Prison (1855) is UNESCO World Heritage-listed since 2010 among the Australian Convict Sites. Guided tours reconstruct 19th-century prison life, including the underground solitary confinement cells.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 43 km · 52min
Day 10

Rottnest Island

Where 10,000 quokkas rule a car-free island paradise

★★★★★
1

Wadjemup (Rottnest Island) - 'place where spirits cross the water' in Noongar language - was an island connected to the mainland until 7,000 years ago. Dutch explorer Willem de Vlamingh named it Rottenest ('rats' nest') in 1696, having mistaken the quokkas for giant rodents.

⏱ 30 min
2

The island counts 63 beaches, 20 bays and 12 inland salt lakes. Its turquoise waters shelter coral reefs and marked underwater trails (snorkeling trail) accessible directly from shore without needing a boat.

⏱ 90 min
3

Over 10,000 quokkas live on Rottnest - the world's largest wild population of this vulnerable marsupial. Free from natural predators on the island, they're active during the day, often approaching humans, but it's illegal to feed or touch them under penalty of fines.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hire a bike as soon as you arrive on the ferry: it's the only personal transport on the island (private cars are banned). A 22 km circuit loops the island, with stops at bays, Cathedral Rocks and the Cape Vlamingh lighthouse.

⏱ 180 min

Snorkel the marked underwater trails at The Basin or Little Armstrong Bay: corals, tropical fish and dolphins are regularly present. In season (September-December), humpback whales cruise past the island.

⏱ 120 min

A Wadjuk Noongar guide from the Rottnest Island Authority offers cultural walks tracing 30,000 years of Aboriginal occupation of Wadjemup and the painful history of the Aboriginal prison (1839-1903).

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 1300 km · 13h
Day 11

Coral Bay

Where the reef meets the shore and whale sharks gather

★★☆☆☆
1

Ningaloo Reef extends 260 km along the Coral Coast - Australia's longest fringing reef and one of the longest in the world. UNESCO World Heritage-listed in 2011, it's accessible directly from Coral Bay beach without needing a boat, less than 0.5 km from shore.

⏱ 120 min
2

Ningaloo Reef shelters exceptional marine biodiversity: over 300 coral species, 500 fish species, 650 molluscs, 600 crustaceans and 155 sponges. The Baiyungu and Yinigudira traditional custodians have occupied this coast for over 32,000 years - shell artefacts among the oldest in the world have been discovered here.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Pull on a mask and snorkel straight from Coral Bay beach. The reef is a few metres from shore: marine turtles, resident manta rays and schools of multicoloured fish are visible on beginner-level snorkels.

⏱ 120 min

From March to July, Ningaloo hosts the world's largest documented aggregation of whale sharks (Rhincodon typus): 300 to 500 individuals drawn by the synchronized coral spawning. Swimming with the world's largest fish in these crystalline waters is the Coral Coast's flagship experience.

⏱ 360 min

From July to October, migrating humpback whales cruise along Ningaloo. Regulated in-water whale encounters allow you to swim just metres from these 15-tonne cetaceans - a humbling, life-changing experience.

⏱ 360 min

Cape Range National Park, 80 km north via Exmouth, unfolds red limestone gorges across the peninsula's spine. Turquoise Bay (snorkeling reef drift) and Mandu Mandu Gorge complete the terrestrial exploration of the UNESCO site.

⏱ 240 min
