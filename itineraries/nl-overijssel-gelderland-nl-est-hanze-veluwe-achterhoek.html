Day 1

Zwolle

1

Built in 1409, two years after Zwolle joined the Hanseatic League, the Sassenpoort is the sole surviving city gate from the old ramparts. Its 101,000 trachyte and sandstone blocks bear witness to the commercial wealth of the era. It's open Wednesdays and Saturdays from 11 AM to 5 PM, entrance €3, and features a permanent Hanseatic hall accessed via the medieval staircase.

⏱ 45 min
2

Touristy but well done: Zwolle's Grote Markt is ringed by 16th-century merchant facades and a café terrace tucked into the Wijndragerstoren tower. A stone's throw away, Sint-Michaelskerk displays a statue of the archangel on its roof — there's a green version on the square below, look for it. Saturday's open-air market remains one of the liveliest in Overijssel.

⏱ 60 min
3

The scent of old paper and oiled wood greets you at Van der Velde in de Broeren, a bookshop installed since 2013 in a neo-Gothic church from 1901. The shelves climb all the way to the vaults, a café occupies the choir. An hour vanishes easily. Avoid Sunday mornings when foot traffic is heaviest.

⏱ 50 min

Activities

Take the star ramparts trail around the historic centre: the star-shaped outline of the old fortifications remains legible in the canal layout. The 2.4-km walk, marked by the tourist office, passes beneath the Sassenpoort and alongside several towers still standing.

⏱ 60 min

If the sun cooperates, climb the 236 steps of the Peperbus to the first landing at 51 metres: the tower of the Onze-Lieve-Vrouwebasiliek soars to 75 metres but public access stops there, and the view over the polders and the IJssel is already clear. Check opening hours at the basilica before coming.

⏱ 40 min

Day 2

Giethoorn

1

Unbearably touristy on July weekends, but virtually deserted at 8 AM: that's when Giethoorn reveals its true face. Founded in the 13th century, this village in Overijssel province is structured around 90 km of canals and 180 wooden bridges connecting peat islets. The thatched-roof farms — some dating from the 17th and 18th centuries — have no road in front of their doors. Mail is delivered by boat.

⏱ 90 min
2

The splash of an oar replaces the car horn in Giethoorn, an officially car-free village integrated into Weerribben-Wieden National Park. The reed beds lining the canals are harvested each year using traditional methods to maintain biodiversity. The park covers a vast protected wetland north of the village, ideal for observing waterfowl at dawn.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Rent an electric whisperboat as soon as you arrive — rental companies open early and the first hours are the quietest. Count on an hour and a half for the main circuit of inner canals. Electric boats are encouraged to protect the banks and wildlife. Avoid high-season weekends: the narrow canals can be gridlocked.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 35 km · 40min
Day 3

Kampen

1

Kampen's own residents call their town an 'open-air museum'. It's not an exaggeration: with nearly 500 monuments in its historic centre alone, Kampen packs more heritage per square metre than many provincial capitals. The square around the golden bridge — adorned with gold wheels since the Middle Ages — and the IJssel quays form a coherent and still-living ensemble.

⏱ 45 min
2

Built in the 14th century, the Bovenkerk, or Sint-Nicolaaskerk, dominates the IJssel waterfront with its Gothic nave. It houses an impressive organ and stained glass worth lingering over. A few steps away, the Koornmarktspoort is the town's oldest medieval gate — you can climb it for a panorama over the river. Kampen was also the first Dutch town to produce its own cigars: the De Eenhoorn factory still offers tours.

⏱ 50 min

Activities

The IJssel waterfront stretches for several hundred metres under the setting sun. Walk the quays up to the town bridge to see the golden wheels that have adorned its pylons since the Middle Ages. The waterside terraces come alive in the evening; the Proeflokaal De Stomme van Campen, with a view over the IJssel, serves local beers.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 16 km · 20min
Day 4

Elburg

1

A perfect square, and it's real: Elburg's fortified town measures 250 by 415 metres, laid out in 1392 by Duke William I of Guelders to protect his Hanseatic town. The grid plan is flawless — only one street remains curved, the Krommesteeg, by accident of terrain. Parking is outside the walls, a two-minute walk. In summer, crowds can be heavy: arrive before 10 AM.

⏱ 40 min
2

The Vischpoort is the only gate still standing since 1592, when an opening was punched through the old defensive tower to let fishing boats pass. On the town side it has windows; on the sea side it's blind — its closed face once served as an oil lamp lighthouse for fishermen. Museum Elburg is installed in the former 15th-century Agnietenklooster, courtyard entry free.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The green ramparts are a surprise: the Groene Wallen Wandeling follows the 16th-century fortifications from the outside, between moats and meadows. The complete circuit is less than 2 km. Along the way, spot the Deetman ropewalk, the oldest working rope-making facility in the Netherlands, visible from the Noorderwal.

⏱ 50 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 28 km · 30min
Day 5

Hattem

1

Fairy-tale pages hanging on brick walls: the Anton Pieck Museum set up shop in this small Hanseatic town in 1984 on purpose — its medieval streets look exactly like the scenes the artist drew. The museum, opened by Pieck himself at age 89, reconstructs his original studio in the basement with his 1,500-kg engraving press. Adult entry around €12.50.

⏱ 75 min
2

Born in 1895 in Den Helder, Anton Pieck designed the Sprookjesbos at Efteling and illustrated dozens of books and calendars recognizable everywhere in the Netherlands. Forty years an art teacher, he held his romantic line against all modernist currents. The art schools of the time dismissed it as mere nostalgia; his prints still sell today in the thousands.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

If you appreciate old organs, the route between the museum and the De Fortuin windmill passes through the Dijkpoort, one of Hattem's two medieval gates. The 1742 mill is sometimes active on weekends. The small town can be walked entirely in under an hour; the views over the IJssel from the heights are among the clearest in the region.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 35 km · 40min
Day 6

Apeldoorn

1

Built between 1684 and 1686, Paleis Het Loo was the residence of the House of Orange-Nassau until 1962 before becoming a national museum. Reopened in 2023 after major renovations, entry is now via an underground passage beneath a glass roof pierced by light. The Baroque gardens, designed according to the vision of William III and Mary II, have been restored to their original state. Closed Mondays; adult ticket around €21.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Baroque gardens are impeccable: they were designed by Claude Desgots, Le Nôtre's nephew, to impress royal visitors. Symmetrical parterres, fountains and sculpted hedges recreate the 17th-century state. In spring, the flowering is particularly spectacular. Allow at least an hour just for the gardens.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Stop by the royal stables to see the exhibition of carriages and ceremonial vehicles. The space is less visited than the palace apartments and offers a striking contrast between the splendour of 18th-century coaches and the royal family's first automobiles. Count on 30 to 40 minutes.

⏱ 40 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 20 km · 25min
Day 7

Otterlo

1

A museum lost in the forest, yet this is where the Van Goghs are — not Amsterdam. The Kröller-Müller Museum, opened in 1938 in the heart of De Hoge Veluwe National Park, houses the world's second-largest Van Gogh collection, with nearly 90 paintings and more than 180 drawings. Access requires a combined park + museum ticket; buy it online to avoid weekend queues.

⏱ 180 min
2

Ninety Van Gogh paintings, and the silence between them. The Kröller-Müller sculpture garden covers 25 hectares of forest scattered with works by Rodin, Moore and other 20th-century artists. The walk from the car park takes twenty minutes; no rush, it's the largest sculpture garden in the Netherlands.

⏱ 90 min
3

Jachthuis Sint Hubertus is a total work: this hunting lodge by architect H.P. Berlage, former residence of the Kröller-Müllers, is one of the most important Dutch monuments. Accessible only by guided tour, book online. It's located 3 km from the museum in the park, accessible on foot or by bike.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Grab a free white bike from the Otterlo entrance — the park stocks 1,800 across its three entrances. It's the only way to move freely through the 5,500 hectares of heath, woods and dunes of De Hoge Veluwe. Red deer and wild boar are easily spotted in the morning on the open heaths.

⏱ 120 min

If you love contemporary sculpture, the Kröller-Müller's 25-hectare garden deserves a visit independent of the museum: works are discovered around forest paths, unmarked. The Museonder, an underground museum about park wildlife, suits children well and takes less than an hour.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 25 km · 30min
Day 8

Arnhem

1

The costumed actors at the Nederlands Openluchtmuseum really live their roles: brewer, blacksmith, steam tram conductor. This 44-hectare open-air museum in Arnhem relocates authentic buildings from all Dutch regions and populates them with characters who speak of the past in the present tense. Plan a full day. Museum Pass accepted.

⏱ 240 min
2

Arnhem deserves better than its 'bridge too far' reputation. Its Sonsbeek Park, right in the centre, hosts contemporary sculptures scattered across terraced lawns overlooking the town. The park is entirely free and little visited by foreign tourists — a real breath of fresh air.

⏱ 60 min
3

In Sonsbeek Park, sculptures emerge from the morning mist among old beeches. The park dates from the 19th century and its landscape design makes it one of Gelderland's finest urban parks. It connects the Openluchtmuseum to Arnhem's city centre on foot in a pleasant hour's walk.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

If you're there on a weekday, the Openluchtmuseum is far less crowded than weekends: actors have more time for visitors and the interactive workshops are less saturated. Count on a minimum three hours to see the brewery, mill, farm and antique tram sections. The steam tram circles the site.

⏱ 60 min

The John Frost Bridge plunges toward the IJssel: a plaque commemorates the September 1944 battle. The walk across takes ten minutes and the viewpoint over Arnhem's panorama is the finest in town. The Airborne Museum at Hartenstein, in Oosterbeek (15 km), is the reference museum on Operation Market Garden if you want to delve deeper.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 35 km · 40min
Day 9

S Heerenberg

1

Kasteel Huis Bergh dates back to the 13th century — archives mention the site as early as 1227 — and ranks among the largest medieval castles in the Netherlands. Surrounded by moats, it houses a collection of early Italian period paintings, medieval manuscripts and northern European works from the 16th century. The gardens and inner courtyard are freely accessible; the guided castle tour costs €8 per adult.

⏱ 90 min
2

The chained manuscripts in Huis Bergh's armoury and the black torches of the keep recall that this tower has never been taken since its construction. The castle archive, whose oldest piece dates from 1227, is one of the best preserved in Gelderland. An audio guide in several languages traces the history of the Van den Bergh lords.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Climb Huis Bergh's defensive tower to see the Montferland lands spread out toward Germany, just a few kilometres away. The estate comprises around 500 hectares of forests and farmland surrounding the castle. Concerts and exhibitions are held regularly in season — check the programme before coming.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 35 km · 40min
Day 10

Bronkhorst

1

Official city of 170 inhabitants: the sign says 'stadsgrens' — city limits — after just a few dozen metres. Bronkhorst received its city rights in 1482 and has never grown beyond its medieval core since. Red-brick houses with decorated facades line three main streets; a complete walk takes under twenty minutes. It's the kind of place you want to see before the 'Instagram spot' signs appear.

⏱ 30 min
2

Bronkhorst's chapel dates from 1344, former seigneurial chapel of the castle of the lords of Bronckhorst, of which only the Slotheuvel remains, a castle mound visible at the village exit. The chapel now serves as a cultural centre with occasional exhibitions. It's the only standing medieval monument in Bronkhorst; its bell tower is visible from every point in the village.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Take the little cable ferry across the IJssel to Brummen — a few minutes' crossing, a few euros. It's one of the few ferry links still operating on this stretch of river. On the other side, a short walk leads to the first IJssel levees with a view of Bronkhorst from the water.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 18 km · 25min
Day 11

Vorden

1

Eight castles on 34 km — too good to be true, yet it's the reality of the cycling loop around Vorden, signposted since 1913. Most are private residences off-limits, which makes them all the more fascinating. Kasteel Vorden, visible from the main road, is open six days a week with a freely accessible audio guide.

⏱ 60 min
2

Kasteel Hackfort: its underground passage dating from the 14th century is one of the few parts open to the public, two afternoons a week. The moestuin — historic kitchen garden dating back to around 1790 — is a must-see for garden lovers. The coetshuis brasserie cooks with vegetables from the garden. The first mention of Hackfort in the archives dates from 1392.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Rent a bike in the village for the Achtkastelenroute, marked with yellow hexagonal signs from Vorden market square. The 34 km on paved roads pass Hackfort, Den Bramel, De Wildenborch and five other castles. A guided version is offered Wednesdays and Saturdays in summer.

⏱ 180 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 12 km · 18min
Day 12

Ruurlo

1

Carel Willink's surrealist canvases — camels before Versailles, landscapes with nuclear power plants — hanging in the 14th-century halls of Kasteel Ruurlo form a striking contrast. Museum MORE has installed around thirty of his works in this monumental setting since 2017. Access to the castle rooms requires the museum ticket; the English landscape park and orangery are free.

⏱ 75 min
2

Kasteel Ruurlo has housed Carel Willink's works since 2017. The initiative came from collector Hans Melchers, who wanted to offer this 20th-century realist painter a worthy showcase. The castle rooms date back to the 14th century and have been restored combining several centuries of architecture. The whole forms one of Gelderland's most singular art museums.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Locals call this maze their 'dwaaltuin' — wandering garden. The Doolhof Ruurlo, right next to the castle, was laid out in 1891 on the orders of a Miss Van Heeckeren: 1,188 metres of paths within a hectare of trimmed hedges. Open April through late October. It's not the same owner as the museum; count on a separate entrance fee.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 28 km · 35min
Day 13

Zutphen

1

Zutphen residents chained their books in the 16th century for one unbeatable reason: theft. The Librije of Sint Walburgiskerk, built between 1561 and 1564, is the only chained library still in its original state in the Netherlands — one of only two remaining in Europe along with Cesena in Italy. The collection numbers around 750 volumes, including first editions of Copernicus and Erasmus. Guided visit only, throughout the opening day.

⏱ 60 min
2

Sint Walburgiskerk dates from the 11th century in its oldest parts. It houses a 1395 candle crown, a 1527 copper baptismal font, and the 1643 Hendrick Bader organ, an instrument restored to original condition in 1996. The tower can be climbed on weekends and during school holidays, for a clear view over Zutphen and the IJssel. Church entry €10, tower an additional €5.

⏱ 60 min
3

Looking down over the IJssel from the top of the Walburgiskerk tower at over 200 steps rewards the effort. Zutphen, former Hanseatic city whose golden age stretched from the 13th to 16th century, preserves a dense historic centre with still-inhabited merchant facades. The Hof van Heeckeren, a 17th-century urban palace opposite the Walburgiskerk, houses two museums under one roof.

⏱ 50 min

Activities

If you appreciate craft beer, the Stadsbrouwerij Cambrinus on the Houtmarkt has brewed its own beers since 2006, in a hall with copper kettles visible from the tables. The café also offers a selection of Dutch specials. Count on an hour in the evening after visits.

⏱ 60 min

Stroll along the Berkelkade beside the Berkel River where it joins the IJssel at Zutphen: Hanseatic warehouses converted into apartments and restaurants line the quay. It's the most photographed axis in town, especially in the evening when the facades reflect in the water.

⏱ 40 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 16 km · 22min
Day 14

Deventer

1

Vast, lively and ringed by Renaissance facades, the Brink is astonishing: for a town this size, such architectural coherence is rare in the Netherlands. Museum De Waag, a late Gothic building at the centre of the square whose archives mention its weighing function as early as the 14th century, became a museum in 1915. The terraces come alive from lunch onward and on Saturday mornings a market has been held here since the Middle Ages.

⏱ 75 min
2

Deventer's Bergkwartier is one of the best-preserved historic quarters in the Netherlands, with its medieval alleys around the 12th-century Bergkerk. The Muntentoren housed Deventer's last mint until 1708; the Proosdij is one of the oldest stone houses in the Netherlands. In December, the Dickens Festival transforms this quarter into a Victorian stage set with more than 900 costumed characters.

⏱ 60 min
3

The December Dickens Festival: kitsch, unapologetic, but the 900 costumed characters roaming the Bergkwartier streets in the dead of a winter night eventually impress. Outside the festival, the Grote of Lebuïnuskerk at the edge of Grote Kerkhof offers a tower climb to 46 metres above the IJssel.

⏱ 50 min

Activities

Climb the Grote of Lebuïnuskerk from Grote Kerkhof square: the tower at 46 metres dominates the IJssel at its bend. Deventer is mentioned as early as the 9th century under the name 'Daventre'. The view from the tower confirms its strategic positioning on the river, which explains its medieval power.

⏱ 40 min

The scent of Deventer Koek has floated over the entire Brink for at least four centuries. This spiced honey cake is indeed the town's speciality, sold at the Deventer Koekwinkel on the corner of the square. A bite is mandatory before leaving. The almond-filled version is the most popular locally.

⏱ 20 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 40 km · 40min
Day 15

Zwolle

1

Museum de Fundatie deserves a return stop: its egg-shaped roof — nicknamed 'De Wolk' — is clad with 55,000 blue-white ceramic tiles from Makkum, added in 2012-2013 to the 19th-century neoclassical building. The collection includes Mondrians and Van Goghs, and temporary exhibitions rotate constantly.

⏱ 60 min
2

Stop at Grote Markt square for one last coffee on the terrace before the road. The square is bordered by 15th and 16th-century merchant facades and the Sint-Michaelskerk closes the perspective. Saturday's market is one of the largest in Overijssel, with several hundred stalls depending on the season.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

If it's a Wednesday or Saturday, the Sassenpoort is accessible from 11 AM to 5 PM for €3: a quick visit to the Hanseatic hall inside the gate closes the journey on the Hanse logically. Between the gate's opening in 1409 and today's return, the Dutch Hanseatic circuit is complete.

⏱ 45 min
