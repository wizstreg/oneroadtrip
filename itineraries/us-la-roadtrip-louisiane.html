Louisiana Road Trip: New Orleans and Cajun Country

📅 10 days📍 10 stops🚗 460 km⏱ 5h50

New Orleans → Gonzales → Baton Rouge → Breaux Bridge → Lafayette

Louisiana isn't visited—it's lived: three days exploring New Orleans' French Quarter amid jazz, beignets, and wrought-iron balconies, then River Road with its history-laden plantations, Baton Rouge and its bullet-pierced capitol, and finally Cajun country—Lafayette, Breaux Bridge, the Atchafalaya bayous, and Avery Island's Tabasco factory. A 10-day loop where music, cuisine, and encounters shape the journey as much as the landscapes.

✨ Highlights
  • New Orleans' French Quarter, founded in 1718, with Jackson Square and the nation's oldest continuously active Catholic cathedral
  • River Road plantations: Oak Alley (28 centuries-old oaks), Whitney (sole museum dedicated to slavery), and Laura (Creole plantation from 1805)
  • Atchafalaya Basin, America's largest swamp, crossed by an 18-mile elevated highway
  • Avery Island and Tabasco sauce, produced in the same location since 1868, aged three years in oak barrels
📆 Best period

March, April, May, October, November

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 8 and 12 days.

10-day loop starting from New Orleans. Car rental essential from day 4 onward. Best time to visit: March through May (mild weather, festivals, crawfish season). Watch out for heat and humidity in summer (June-September). New Orleans is easily explored on foot and by streetcar for the first 3 days.

Day 1

New Orleans

Birthplace of jazz and Mardi Gras, founded in 1718

★★★★★
📍 Jackson Square New Orleans📍 St Louis Cathedral New Orleans📍 Cabildo New Orleans📍 Cafe Du Monde New Orleans📍 French Market New Orleans📍 Frenchmen Street New Orleans
1

Start at Jackson Square, the heart of the French Quarter since 1721. This 2.5-acre plaza, modeled after Paris's Place des Vosges, witnessed Louisiana's transfer from France to the United States in 1803 during the Louisiana Purchase. At the center stands an 1856 equestrian statue of General Andrew Jackson. Around you: artists display their work along the wrought-iron fence (200 permits issued annually), jazz musicians perform on the sidewalks, and tarot readers offer their services—all in a delightfully chaotic New Orleans fashion.

⏱ 45 min
2

Facing the square, St. Louis Cathedral is the oldest continuously active Roman Catholic cathedral in the United States. The current building dates from 1789, rebuilt after the great fire that devastated the French Quarter in 1788. On either side stand the Cabildo (former seat of Spanish colonial government, now Louisiana State Museum) and the Presbytère (Mardi Gras museum). Cathedral admission is free—take a look at the rococo-style altar.

⏱ 40 min
3

Cross Decatur Street to the French Market, the oldest public market in the United States (operating since 1791). You'll find stalls selling local produce, Cajun spices, hot sauce, and souvenirs. At the market's end, the old U.S. Mint building houses the New Orleans Jazz Museum—it's the only building in the U.S. to have served as a mint for both the United States and the Confederacy.

⏱ 50 min
4

Walk up Royal Street—the French Quarter's gallery and antique dealer street, much quieter than Bourbon Street. The wrought-iron balconies adorned with ferns are a sight in themselves. Look for the initials forged into the railings: they identify the Creole families who built these houses in the early 19th century.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Late afternoon, settle into a café terrace overlooking Jackson Square for café au lait and beignets—the local specialty served here since the Civil War. Beignets are squares of fried dough covered in powdered sugar, always served in threes.

⏱ 30 min

Evening, head to Frenchmen Street (not Bourbon Street—locals will tell you). This is where the city's real jazz clubs are concentrated. You can listen to live music in several clubs without paying cover—just walk from bar to bar and follow what moves you. Jazz was born here in New Orleans in the early 20th century, and Frenchmen Street is its beating heart today.

⏱ 90 min

Day 2

New Orleans

Birthplace of jazz and Mardi Gras, founded in 1718

★★★★★
📍 Garden District New Orleans📍 St Charles Streetcar New Orleans📍 Lafayette Cemetery No 1 New Orleans📍 Magazine Street New Orleans📍 Audubon Park New Orleans
1

Take the St. Charles Avenue streetcar from Canal Street—the oldest continuously operating streetcar line in the world, designated a National Historic Landmark in 2014. The wooden cars date from 1923-1924, with no air conditioning—open windows are part of the charm. The ride costs $1.25 (exact change required). Get off at Washington Avenue to enter the Garden District.

⏱ 25 min
2

The Garden District is the neighborhood of grand mansions built by wealthy Americans after the 1803 Louisiana Purchase. Unwilling to live with the Creoles in the French Quarter, they built their own palaces on the other side of Canal Street. You'll see Greek Revival, Italianate, and Queen Anne-style homes, shaded by centuries-old oaks draped with Spanish moss. Stroll Prytania, Coliseum, and First Streets for the finest facades.

⏱ 60 min
3

Lafayette Cemetery No. 1, at the corner of Washington Avenue and Prytania Street, is one of New Orleans' famous "cities of the dead" cemeteries. Because the water table is so high, graves are built above ground in marble and brick vaults. This cemetery dates from 1833 and has been used as a filming location for several movies and series. Check opening hours before visiting—access is sometimes restricted.

⏱ 30 min
4

Head down Magazine Street, parallel to St. Charles Avenue, for six miles of independent shops, galleries, and restaurants. This is the best spot for non-touristy shopping: antiques, vintage clothing, bookstores, contemporary art galleries. If you're hungry, po'boys (giant sandwiches stuffed with fried seafood or meat) are the specialty to order here.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Continue by streetcar to Audubon Park (Calhoun Street stop). This 350-acre park lined with centuries-old oaks borders the Mississippi River. You can walk, jog, or simply sit beneath the trees. Audubon Zoo is at the back of the park if you're traveling with children.

⏱ 60 min

Return by streetcar downtown and enjoy a free evening. If you want to explore the Creole culinary scene, restaurants in the Warehouse District (between the French Quarter and Garden District) offer a good balance of local cuisine and less touristy atmosphere than Bourbon Street.

⏱ 45 min

Day 3

New Orleans

Birthplace of jazz and Mardi Gras, founded in 1718

★★★★★
📍 Mardi Gras World New Orleans📍 National WWII Museum New Orleans📍 Treme neighborhood New Orleans📍 Bourbon Street New Orleans📍 Vue Orleans observation deck
1

Start the morning at Mardi Gras World, a massive warehouse on the Mississippi riverfront where Mardi Gras floats are built. The guided tour lasts about an hour and you'll see artisans at work on giant papier-mâché and fiberglass sculptures—some over 20 feet tall. New Orleans' Mardi Gras attracts more than a million visitors annually, and the floats you see here parade down St. Charles Avenue during carnival season (January-February).

⏱ 75 min
2

Head to the National WWII Museum, ranked among America's finest museums. Founded in 2000 in New Orleans because this is where Andrew Higgins designed the landing craft used on D-Day. The museum spans several buildings and you could easily spend a full day—allow at least 2 to 3 hours for the main exhibitions. The planes, tanks, and submarines on display are authentic.

⏱ 150 min
3

Late afternoon, ascend to Vue Orleans, the observation deck on the 34th floor of the former World Trade Center. The 360-degree view covers the Mississippi, French Quarter, Superdome, and Lake Pontchartrain. The interactive exhibit on the same floor traces the city's history through its various colonial periods.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Make a detour through the Tremé neighborhood, the oldest African American neighborhood in the United States. This is where enslaved people gathered on Sundays at Congo Square (now in Louis Armstrong Park) to play music and dance—a site considered one of jazz's birthplaces. The neighborhood gave its name to the HBO series.

⏱ 45 min

Last evening in NOLA: if you haven't done it yet, take a stroll down Bourbon Street for the atmosphere (loud, touristy, but it's a must at least once). Bars are open around the clock and you can walk with your drink in the street—it's legal here, unlike most American cities.

⏱ 60 min

Day 4

Gonzales

River Road stopover, self-proclaimed jambalaya capital of the world

★★★★★
📍 Whitney Plantation Wallace Louisiana📍 Oak Alley Plantation Vacherie📍 Laura Plantation Vacherie📍 River Road Louisiana plantations
1

Leave New Orleans via River Road (LA-18) heading west—this route follows the Mississippi for 75 miles between NOLA and Baton Rouge. Before the Civil War, this was one of America's wealthiest regions: sugarcane plantations stretched as far as the eye could see. First stop 30 miles out: Whitney Plantation in Wallace. This is Louisiana's only museum entirely dedicated to the history of slavery. Three memorials, period slave cabins, and an 1870 church built by freedmen tell the story of those who lived and worked here by force. The guided tour lasts 90 minutes—it's intense and necessary.

⏱ 100 min
2

Continue 15 miles to Vacherie for Oak Alley Plantation, Louisiana's most famous. Its avenue of 28 live oaks planted in the early 18th century forms a quarter-mile vegetative tunnel between the road and the house. The Greek Revival mansion dates from 1839, built by Jacques Roman. Six replica slave cabins have been reconstructed based on archaeological excavations, and the walls bear the names of more than 220 people enslaved on this property between 1836 and 1865.

⏱ 90 min
3

Four miles from Oak Alley, Laura Plantation offers a striking contrast. This is a Creole plantation—no white columns here, but a colorful cypress wood house raised on brick pillars in Louisiana vernacular style. The estate dates from 1805 and was managed by Creole women for four generations. The tour is narrated from the manuscript of Laura Locoul, born here in 1861. This is also where the Br'er Rabbit stories were collected, told by the plantation's Senegalese slaves.

⏱ 80 min

Activities

If you still have time, drive 3 miles south to San Francisco Plantation, the most colorful on River Road—its painted facade recalls New Orleans houses. Otherwise, head to Gonzales (30 minutes) for the night. Gonzales proclaims itself the "Jambalaya Capital of the World"—if you find a local restaurant open, order some and judge for yourself.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 40 km · 35min
Day 5

Baton Rouge

Louisiana's capital, crowned by the nation's tallest state capitol

★★★★☆
📍 Louisiana State Capitol Baton Rouge📍 Capitol Park Museum Baton Rouge📍 USS Kidd Baton Rouge📍 LSU Campus Baton Rouge📍 Old State Capitol Baton Rouge
1

Arrival in Baton Rouge, Louisiana's capital. Begin at the Louisiana State Capitol, America's tallest state capitol: 450 feet and 34 stories, built in 1932 under Governor Huey Long's direction. The man deserves attention: a controversial populist nicknamed "the Kingfish," he was assassinated in this very building's lobby in 1935. You can still see the bullet hole in the marble. The 27th-floor observation deck offers views over the Mississippi and the city—free admission.

⏱ 60 min
2

Next door, the Capitol Park Museum traces Louisiana's history through interactive exhibits. The section on Cajun and Creole cultures is particularly rich—you'll see a traditional pirogue, zydeco instruments, and a reconstructed bayou fishing camp.

⏱ 60 min
3

On the Mississippi riverbank, the USS Kidd is a World War II destroyer permanently moored and open for tours. The ship participated in the Battle of Okinawa in 1945. You can descend into the berthing areas, climb on deck, and explore the engine room. The adjacent veterans museum completes the visit.

⏱ 60 min
4

Stop by the Old State Capitol, the former state capitol that resembles a medieval Gothic castle—a surreal vision on the Mississippi's banks. Mark Twain called it a "monstrosity" in his writings. The building dates from 1847 and now houses a political history museum with a spectacular spiral staircase beneath a stained-glass dome.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Late afternoon, stroll LSU's campus (Louisiana State University). The grounds are shaded by centuries-old oaks and bordered by Italian Renaissance-style buildings. If it's football season (September-November), try to catch a game at Tiger Stadium—game nights in Baton Rouge are an experience unto themselves, with giant barbecues and electric atmosphere.

⏱ 60 min

Day 6

Breaux Bridge

Crawfish capital of the world since 1959, on Bayou Teche's banks

★★★★★
📍 Henderson Levee Road Louisiana swamp tour📍 Atchafalaya Basin swamp📍 Breaux Bridge downtown Cajun📍 Bayou Teche Breaux Bridge📍 Lake Martin Louisiana
1

Leave Baton Rouge via I-10 heading west. After 40 minutes, you'll cross the Atchafalaya Basin on an 18-mile elevated highway bridge over America's largest swamp—the view is striking, especially in the morning when mist floats above the cypress trees. Exit at Henderson (exit 115) for a bayou boat tour. Local operators offer 90-minute to 2-hour tours through the basin's channels: you'll see alligators, great blue herons, egrets, and turtles in a landscape of bald cypress draped with Spanish moss.

⏱ 120 min
2

After the bayou, drive 15 minutes to Breaux Bridge, nicknamed "Crawfish Capital of the World" by Louisiana's legislature since 1959. The small downtown on the banks of Bayou Teche has authentic Cajun charm: painted wooden houses, art galleries, music shops, and restaurants that smell of roux and cayenne. Walk across the bridge spanning the bayou—the first bridge here was built in 1799 by Acadian pioneer Firmin Breaux.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Make a detour to Lake Martin, between Breaux Bridge and Lafayette (10 minutes). This nature preserve harbors a colony of herons, egrets, and ibis—during nesting season (March-June), the trees are literally covered with white birds. You can drive along the lake on Rookery Road or rent a kayak to weave between the cypress trees. Alligators are common—keep your distance.

⏱ 60 min

Evening in Breaux Bridge, look for a restaurant with live Cajun music. On Saturday mornings, several venues offer a "zydeco breakfast"—breakfast accompanied by live zydeco music, with people getting up to dance between tables. If you're here on a weekday, inquire about evening "Cajun jams."

⏱ 75 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 15 km · 15min
Day 7

Lafayette

Heart of Acadiana, where Cajun music and zydeco come alive

★★★★☆
📍 Vermilionville Lafayette Louisiana📍 Cathedral of St John the Evangelist Lafayette📍 Acadiana Center for the Arts Lafayette📍 Downtown Lafayette murals
1

Welcome to Lafayette, heart of Acadiana and self-proclaimed "Happiest City in America." This morning, head to Vermilionville, a living history museum village on the banks of Bayou Vermilion. This site recreates Acadian life from 1765 to 1890 through 19 restored buildings. Period-costumed artisans demonstrate weaving, wood carving, and Cajun cooking. The on-site restaurant, La Cuisine de Maman, serves gumbo and crawfish étouffée prepared according to traditional recipes.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Cathedral of St. John the Evangelist, downtown, dates from 1916, and its centuries-old oak on the front lawn (the Cathedral Oak, estimated at over 500 years) is one of Louisiana's most photographed trees. The adjacent cemetery, with its above-ground tombs, recalls those of New Orleans.

⏱ 30 min
3

Explore downtown Lafayette on foot: Jefferson Street concentrates Cajun craft shops, galleries, and colorful murals. The Acadiana Center for the Arts schedules exhibitions and concerts year-round. Lafayette has more restaurants per capita than most American cities—you'll eat well at every meal.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Evening, look for a Cajun dance or zydeco night. The difference: Cajun music is played on accordion and fiddle, sung in French; zydeco adds the frottoir (washboard) and faster rhythms drawn from blues and R&B. Both are partner dances, with Cajun waltz or two-step. Even if you don't know how to dance, locals will show you—that's the spirit of Acadiana.

⏱ 90 min

Day 8

Lafayette

Heart of Acadiana, where Cajun music and zydeco come alive

★★★★☆
📍 Avery Island Tabasco Factory Louisiana📍 Jungle Gardens Avery Island📍 Shadows on the Teche New Iberia📍 New Iberia downtown Louisiana
1

Day trip heading south: drive to Avery Island (50 minutes from Lafayette), birthplace of Tabasco sauce. The McIlhenny family has produced the famous hot sauce here since 1868—same recipe, same place, five generations. Peppers are grown on the island, crushed with local salt, then aged three years in oak barrels (the same ones used for bourbon). The factory tour shows you the entire process, and the shop offers varieties unavailable elsewhere.

⏱ 60 min
2

Don't leave without exploring Jungle Gardens, 170 acres of botanical gardens and wildlife sanctuary on Avery Island. The garden was created by Edward Avery McIlhenny in the early 20th century. You'll see Bird City, a nesting colony of snowy egrets he saved from extinction in 1895 by raising 8 young birds. Today, thousands of egrets nest there each spring. The park also features a 12th-century Buddha brought from China and an impressive bamboo grove. Warning: signs indicate the presence of black bears—this is not a joke.

⏱ 75 min
3

On the way back to Lafayette, stop in New Iberia at Shadows-on-the-Teche plantation. This 1834 red-brick mansion, bordered by oaks on the banks of Bayou Teche, is one of the few plantations managed by the National Trust for Historic Preservation. Downtown New Iberia also deserves a tour—it's a quiet Cajun town with colorful Creole houses.

⏱ 50 min

Activities

Return to Lafayette for dinner. Order a dish you haven't tried yet: boudin (spiced rice and pork sausage, 100% Cajun specialty), gumbo (thick soup based on roux, okra, and seafood or andouille), or crawfish étouffée (crawfish simmered in butter-and-spice sauce).

⏱ 60 min

Day 9

New Orleans

Birthplace of jazz and Mardi Gras, founded in 1718

★★★★★
📍 Bayou Lafourche Louisiana📍 Thibodaux Louisiana📍 Jean Lafitte Barataria Preserve📍 Bywater neighborhood New Orleans
1

Return route to New Orleans, but not via the interstate. Take US-90 eastbound then branch onto LA-1 along Bayou Lafourche, nicknamed "the longest cul-de-sac in the world"—a straight road following the bayou for dozens of miles, lined with houses on stilts and shrimp boats moored along the banks. Stop in Thibodaux, a small Cajun town on the bayou, for coffee and a photo of the historic downtown.

⏱ 45 min
2

Arriving in the New Orleans area, make a detour through Barataria Preserve at Jean Lafitte National Historical Park (free admission, 30 minutes south of NOLA). Elevated wooden trails traverse a cypress and palmetto swamp where you can observe alligators, nutria, and water birds without needing a boat. The main trail is 2.2 miles and accessible to all.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Return to New Orleans late afternoon. Take advantage of this last evening to explore the Bywater neighborhood, east of the French Quarter. This is the city's creative district: giant murals, alternative galleries, cocktail bars in former warehouses. The atmosphere is less touristy than the French Quarter—this is where the city's artists and musicians live.

⏱ 90 min

Day 10

New Orleans

Birthplace of jazz and Mardi Gras, founded in 1718

★★★★★
📍 City Park New Orleans📍 New Orleans Museum of Art📍 Besthoff Sculpture Garden New Orleans📍 Canal Street New Orleans
1

Last day in New Orleans—spend it on what you've missed. If you haven't been to City Park yet, now's the time. This 1,300-acre park (larger than New York's Central Park) features 800-year-old oaks, the New Orleans Museum of Art (NOMA), and the Sydney & Walda Besthoff Sculpture Garden—a contemporary sculpture garden outdoors among cypress trees and lagoons, free and open to all.

⏱ 90 min
2

Take the Canal Street streetcar (City Park/Museum line) to get there—it's another historic line that crosses Mid-City, a residential neighborhood experiencing renewal with good restaurants and local bakeries.

⏱ 25 min

Activities

Final hours in NOLA: return to the French Quarter for one last café au lait, one last beignet, one last piece of jazz in the street. Louis Armstrong Airport (MSY) is 25 minutes by car from downtown. If you're returning your rental car, allow some margin—traffic on I-10 can be heavy during rush hour.
