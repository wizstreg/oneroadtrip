Ampuis

★★☆☆☆
1

Better preserved than Rome's Pantheon - yes, really. The Temple of Augustus and Livia in Vienne has survived intact since the 1st century BC, partly because it served as a courthouse, then a church, then a grain depot depending on the era. The Corinthian facade and its fluted columns dominate Place du Palais, right in the city center. Free entry, explanatory panel in front - allow 20 minutes, longer if you're curious.

⏱ 25 min
2

Vienne's ancient theatre, carved into the hillside of Pipet following its natural slope, could seat 11,000 spectators in the 1st century. It's still operational - the Jazz à Vienne festival has been held here every July since 1981 and attracts world-class headliners. Outside festival season, climbing to the upper tiers offers views over the Rhône that few tourists bother with: they all stay down below.

⏱ 45 min
3

On the right bank of the Rhône facing Vienne, the museum of Saint-Romain-en-Gal presents one of France's richest Gallo-Roman collections - 200 m² of mosaics, frescoes, tools, workshop reconstructions. The site is the former commercial port of Roman Vienne, excavated since the 1960s over 7 hectares. Under your feet, intact archaeological layers still remain. Closed Mondays.

⏱ 75 min
4

Côte Brune and Côte Blonde, just above Ampuis - one on brown soils of mica and amphibole, the other on decomposed pale granite - produce two different styles of Syrah 500 meters apart. This is where it gets serious. The terraces at slopes steeper than 40°, some so narrow only a single hiker can pass at a time, explain why certain parcels are still worked with winches. Pull over roadside to watch: the vines literally cling to the cliff face.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Maison Guigal at Château d'Ampuis is the absolute reference for Côte-Rôtie. Marcel Guigal released his first 'La Mouline', 'La Landonne', 'La Turque' cuvées in 1978 - three wines that put the Northern Rhône on the global map. The cellar tour is elaborate, pedagogical, and genuinely impressive. Book on the website, limited slots.

⏱ 90 min

Dinner in Ampuis or Condrieu on the Rhône riverbank. The river is calm at this point, lights from villages on the opposite bank reflecting in the water. If you want a simple local table, the riverside inn in Condrieu will do - save your gastronomic ammunition for Le Beau Rivage tomorrow.

⏱ 90 min

Tournon Sur Rhone

★★☆☆☆
1

Château-Grillet from the road: 3.5 hectares in walled clos, its own AOC since 1936 - France's smallest, absolute monopoly of a single family since the 18th century. The wine, a dry white Viognier aged in oak, trades around 100 euros per bottle. You can't visit. You stop, look at the walls, and understand that in wine, rarity has a very precise geography.

⏱ 15 min
2

Saint-Joseph vines stretch over 50 km of terraces on the right bank of the Rhône, from Condrieu down to Cornas. It's the longest appellation in the Northern Rhône - and the most heterogeneous: between the granite parcels of the north and the alluvial soils of the south, the same AOC name can cover very different wines. The road running along the terraces above Tournon offers the best river views of the entire route.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Domaine Georges Vernay in Condrieu is where Viognier almost disappeared - in the 1960s, the appellation had shrunk to just 8 hectares. Georges Vernay held firm, planted, convinced others. His daughter Christine now perpetuates an absolute reference for Northern Rhône whites. The visit is sober, the tasting precise. Book ahead.

⏱ 75 min

Lunch at Le Beau Rivage in Condrieu - one Michelin star on the riverbank, Rhône Valley market cuisine with a remarkably well-curated local wine list. This is one of the rare tables where the sommelier explains why the same Viognier from Vernay and one from Cuilleron are so different 2 km apart. Reserve.

⏱ 120 min

Domaine Pierre Gonon in Mauves, Saint-Joseph appellation, produces confidential wines that Northern Rhône enthusiasts snap up - and which remain hard to find outside the estate. Family visit, no fuss. If you leave with bottles of red, wait at least five years before opening them.

⏱ 60 min

Tain Lhermitage

★★☆☆☆
1

The Hermitage hill is compact - barely 136 hectares in total - but it produces what some consider France's greatest red wine. Climb on foot from Tain: 30 minutes via the vineyard trail, passing parcels of Chapoutier, Jaboulet, Chave. The Saint-Christophe chapel at the summit dates from the 12th century. The view over the Rhône and the Alps on clear days is worth the breathless climb.

⏱ 90 min
2

Built in 1825 by engineer Marc Seguin, the suspension bridge between Tain and Tournon was the first of its kind in France. Seguin had filed the patent for braided metal cable the same year - technology that still equips most of the world's suspension bridges two centuries later. The bridge is still in service for pedestrians and bikes. Free crossing, unbeatable view of the Hermitage hill from mid-bridge.

⏱ 20 min
3

Valrhona set up in Tain in 1922 for one simple reason: the quality of Rhône water. The Cité du Chocolat, opened in 2013, traces the journey from bean to bar with well-designed scenography - and above all, tasting workshops where you learn to distinguish a 64% from a 72% blind. Result: you leave with different criteria for buying chocolate, and probably too many bars in your bag.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Maison Chapoutier offers a commented walk through the Hermitage vines with a winemaker - the rare opportunity to walk the classified 'Le Méal' or 'L'Ermite' parcels while explaining why the soil changes character every ten meters. Michel Chapoutier converted the entire estate to biodynamics in the 1990s: all labels have been in Braille since 1996. Paid visit, booking required.

⏱ 120 min

Comparative tasting of Hermitage red vs Hermitage white at Chapoutier or Jaboulet: same hill, same altitude, but the Marsanne and Roussanne of the white give a wine of absurd longevity - great white Hermitage vintages hold 30 to 50 years in the cellar. Ask to taste a white of at least ten years: it's no longer at all the wine you imagine when you hear 'Rhône white'.

⏱ 60 min

Vaison La Romaine

★★★☆☆
1

Cornas is pure Syrah without compromise: no co-fermented Viognier like in Côte-Rôtie, no blending, no safety net. The granite terraces above the village yield wines that demand ten years' patience before they begin to open. Auguste Clape, who ran the estate until his death in 2014, produced Cornas from 1955 that are still drinkable today. His son and grandson continue. Quiet village, no boutique or tourist cellar - this is the Northern Rhône without the marketing.

⏱ 45 min
2

The ruins of Château de Crussol have dominated Saint-Péray since the 12th century, 200 meters above the village. Foot climb in 40 minutes from the base: the effort is real but the view over the Rhône valley and Valence opposite is worth it. The castle stones have been exposed since its destruction in the 17th century - no guided tour, no ticket, no interpretation. Just ruins and a panorama.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Tasting of sparkling Saint-Péray at a local producer - Alain Voge or Domaine du Tunnel. It's the only méthode champenoise sparkling wine from the Rhône, made from Marsanne and Roussanne, and it's almost unknown outside the region. Dry, mineral, with fine bubbles: completely different from Champagne, and very different from what you'd expect from a 'Rhône white'. Rare chance to taste something you won't easily find elsewhere.

⏱ 60 min

Nougat stop in Montélimar at Arnaud-Soubeyran, one of the most serious houses in town - founded in 1837, unchanged recipes, local almonds and honey. Montélimar nougat has had PGI status since 2010: at least 28% almonds and 25% honey to earn the name. Buy the hard kind, not the soft - it's the original.

⏱ 30 min

Gigondas

★★☆☆☆
1

Vaison-la-Romaine is an anomaly: an almost complete Roman town still legible beneath the current city. The Villasse quarter and Puymin quarter together cover several hectares of open excavations - paved streets, columns, thermal baths, houses with their original door thresholds. The 1st-century ancient theatre, restored, still hosts performances in summer. And the Roman bridge has spanned the Ouvèze for 2,000 years, still in service for pedestrians. Allow half a day to see everything properly.

⏱ 150 min
2

The Dentelles de Montmirail are jagged limestone ridges that surge 600 meters from the valley floor - the effect is striking seen from below, from the vines of Gigondas or Beaumes. From the ridges themselves, the panorama embraces Mont Ventoux, the Alpilles and on clear days the Alps. Several marked trails leave from Gigondas village - allow 2 hours for the classic loop.

⏱ 120 min
3

Beaumes-de-Venezia and its sweet Muscat: here, white Grenache is fortified with alcohol during fermentation, which stops sugar transformation and yields a wine between 15 and 21° alcohol, golden, sweet, with aromas of orange blossom and apricot. Completely different from Sauternes - simpler, more direct, less complex, much more affordable. Serve chilled as an aperitif or with lightly sweetened desserts.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Lunch at L'Oustalet in Gigondas - one Michelin star in a village of 600 inhabitants, which says something about the gastronomic density of this corner of Provence. Vaucluse terroir cuisine with local products worked with precision. The wine list is naturally centered on the Dentelles: Gigondas, Vacqueyras, Beaumes - a lesson in wine geography while eating. Reserve.

⏱ 150 min

Tasting at Domaine du Cayron or Château de Saint-Cosme in Gigondas, AOC recognized in 1971. Grenache dominates here (often over 80%), complemented by Syrah and Mourvèdre. These are fleshy, sunny wines with firm tannins that need 5 to 8 years to soften. Saint-Cosme also offers Vacqueyras cuvées from just next door - the chance to compare the two appellations with the same winemaker.

⏱ 75 min

Chateauneuf Du Pape

★★★☆☆
1

Only two wall sections remain of the Avignon popes' castle - the rest was dynamited in 1944 by retreating Germans. But from these ruins on the hill above the village, the view over the vineyard and the Rhône below is beautiful enough to justify the climb. The famous 'galets roulés de Crau' - those large white pebbles carried by the glacial Rhône - are visible from here, white and round in the parcels below. They store the sun's heat and release it to the vine roots at night. That's Châteauneuf.

⏱ 30 min
2

13 authorized grape varieties - Grenache, Syrah, Mourvèdre, but also Cinsault, Counoise, Vaccarèse, Terret Noir and six others. No other French AOC authorizes as many. The Musée Brotte in the village center explains the appellation's history, pioneering in France: this is where the first geographical delimitation regulation for wines was born in 1923, precursor to the AOC system. Small museum, free entry, instructive in 45 minutes.

⏱ 45 min
3

Tavel, on the right bank of the Rhône, is France's oldest rosé appellation and one of only two exclusively rosé AOCs in France along with Clairette-de-Die. Not a pale fashionable rosé: a powerful Grenache rosé, between 13.5 and 15° alcohol, that ages well for two to three years and accompanies a daube or lamb without blushing. In neighboring Lirac, the reds are often better value than Châteauneuf 500 meters away.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Château de Beaucastel is the biodynamic reference of Châteauneuf - and one of the rare estates to truly use all 13 authorized grape varieties in its blends, not just the three main ones. The Perrin family has held the estate since 1909. The visit is serious, the tasting rigorous. Expect wines that demand patience: standard Beaucastel red needs 8 to 12 years to be at its best. Booking required.

⏱ 90 min

Lunch on the terrace at Le Verger des Papes, beneath the destroyed castle walls, with views over the vineyard. Direct Provençal cuisine - tapenade, lamb, local cheeses - and a wine list centered on village producers. Not the most ambitious table of the route, but the location more than compensates. Book for the terrace.

⏱ 90 min

Avignon

★★★★★
1

Europe's largest Gothic palace - by far: 15,000 m², twice Notre-Dame de Paris in floor area. The Palais des Papes was built in less than twenty years between 1335 and 1352, when the papacy had relocated to Avignon to escape Italian wars. Inside, the rooms are large, tall, and mostly empty since revolutionaries pillaged the furniture and frescoes. It's impressive precisely because it's bare: the scale becomes legible without the decorative. Audio guide included in ticket, essential.

⏱ 120 min
2

On the Pont Saint-Bénézet - the famous 'pont d'Avignon' - people didn't really dance 'on' the bridge but 'under' the bridge, according to historians. Four arches remain of the original twenty-two, cut off in the middle of the Rhône since 17th-century floods. What's interesting: seeing how far it went, imagining the river crossed at this precise spot for five centuries. Entry ticket, allow 30 minutes.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Halles d'Avignon covered market operates every morning except Monday. Local Provençal producers, Vaucluse goat cheeses, olives, herbs, Cavaillon melons in season. If you still have a bit of trunk space, this is where you make final purchases. Saturday morning is the liveliest - and most crowded.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Lunch at restaurant Sevin, one Michelin star at the foot of the Palais des Papes - cuisine of the moment, seasonal Vaucluse products, attentive service without stiffness. This is the right table to finish a seven-day circuit through the vineyards: the wine list tours the Rhône valley from north to south, like the route just completed. Reserve.

⏱ 150 min

If time permits: 30 km south of Avignon, the Pont du Gard. The Roman three-tiered aqueduct (49 meters high, 275 meters long, built in the 1st century without mortar) supplied Nîmes with water over 50 km for several centuries. It's so well built it has survived 2,000 years without major restoration. Swimming possible in the Gardon river below in summer. Allow half a day.

⏱ 180 min
