Patmos

★★★★☆
1

The Monastery of Saint John the Theologian has commanded the heights above Chora since 1088—the year monk Christodoulos, commissioned by Byzantine Emperor Alexios I Komnenos, laid the first stones atop the ruins of an Artemis temple. A UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1999, the complex resembles a fortress from the outside—high grey walls, corner towers—but the interior holds a revelation: a library housing 4,000 documents including the celebrated Codex Purpureus, Saint Mark's Gospel inscribed in silver letters on purple-dyed parchment, one of the world's oldest illuminated manuscripts. Allow at least 90 minutes to explore the museum and central courtyard. In high season, arrive at opening to beat the tour groups.

⏱ 90 min
2

Halfway between Skala port and Chora, the Cave of the Apocalypse is carved into living rock—this is where Saint John, exiled to the island by Emperor Domitian around AD 95, supposedly dictated Revelation to his disciple Prochoros. Look closely at the wall: a three-lobed fissure in the stone symbolizes, according to tradition, the voice of the Trinity. The atmosphere is otherworldly—cool and silent even in peak crowds—contrasting sharply with the island's blazing white sunlight. Plan for 45 minutes on site.

⏱ 45 min
3

Chora, the medieval village that grew around the monastery starting in 1138, is one of the rare Greek settlements to have evolved continuously since the 12th century—UNESCO cites this unbroken lineage as a criterion for World Heritage status in its own right. Lose yourself in its cobbled lanes between whitewashed houses with wooden balconies: several mansions once belonged to wealthy Aegean sea captains and bear witness to past prosperity. In the evening, this is one of the island's finest spots to watch golden light settle over the rooftops.

⏱ 60 min
4

Skala, Patmos's main port, is the island's beating heart: fishing boats, inter-island catamarans, waterfront tavernas and colorful shops. This is where you'll take the pulse of daily life—far removed from the monastery's contemplative atmosphere. If you're here on a Sunday, you'll often encounter pilgrims in traditional dress disembarking for Holy Thursday Mass, when the abbot reenacts the washing of the feet in the monastery courtyard.

⏱ 45 min
5

In Chora's lanes, push open the doors to the Zoodochos Pigi monastery, founded in 1607, and the Annunciation (Evangelismos), founded in 1937—two active convents of nuns that prove Patmos remains a living religious center, extending well beyond the main monastery alone. The sisters receive visitors discreetly but genuinely: dress respectfully.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Walk from Skala to Chora on the flagstone path that winds between fig trees and dry-stone walls—about 40 minutes. It's the same route pilgrims have followed for centuries. The panorama over the island's bays widens at every turn. If you prefer to drive, note that Chora's parking is tiny and fills quickly in July-August.

⏱ 50 min

Take a day trip to Patmos's southern beaches—Psili Ammos is accessible only by boat or via a 45-minute hiking trail from Diakofti. The sand is fine and the bay sheltered, yielding water of rare calm. Water taxis depart from Skala throughout the morning.

⏱ 180 min

'Pouggia,' small cakes filled with almonds and cinnamon, are Patmos's signature sweet, tied to monastic tradition. Look for them in Skala bakeries—available year-round, but especially tender when they emerge from the oven in the morning. 'Revithia fournou,' chickpeas baked slowly overnight with olive oil and herbs, appear on most Chora taverna menus on weekends.

⏱ 30 min

Patmos revived viticulture in 2011 after more than a century of abandoned vineyards. The local Fokiano grape yields a mineral, rare white reminiscent of Cycladic wines—ask for it in Chora tavernas. It doesn't always appear on the written menu, but proprietors nearly always have a bottle tucked away.

⏱ 30 min

Leros

★★★☆☆
1

The Kastro of Panagia, perched on Apityki hill 200 meters above the sea, is visible from the ferry long before you dock at Leros. Built by the Byzantines in the 11th century and reinforced by the Knights of Rhodes in the 15th, it shelters the miraculous icon of the Virgin who, according to legend, emerged from the sea of her own accord before being installed in the powder magazine chapel. Climb the 300 steps from Platanos—the view of Agia Marina and Panteli bays spreading on either side of the hill is one of the Dodecanese's finest. The 1745 iconostasis and the bronze-and-silver bell cast by Russian artisans are worth the ascent.

⏱ 75 min
2

Platanos, Leros's former capital, is a quiet village squeezed between two hills, with sand-colored neoclassical houses and small squares shaded by plane trees. This is where the island's administrative heart beats, far from the port bustle. Take time to wander its lanes—several buildings date from the 19th century, when local shipowners were at the height of their prosperity.

⏱ 45 min
3

Lakki is one of Greece's most bizarre architectural curiosities. Founded in the 1930s by the Italians under the name Porto Lago, this port was designed from scratch according to fascist rationalist principles: wide boulevards, buildings with rounded facades, a cinema and town hall resembling beached ocean liners. Lakki Bay is one of the Mediterranean's largest natural harbors. It's hard not to feel disoriented strolling these nearly deserted streets off-season—like wandering a forgotten 1930s film set.

⏱ 60 min
4

The War Museum, installed in an authentic underground tunnel at Merikia, is unique in Greece. The Italians excavated it as a munitions depot; in 1943, the Battle of Leros left scars here when the Germans bombarded for 42 days before seizing the island from the British, Greeks and Italians. The museum, opened in 2005, preserves weapons, uniforms, munitions and maps from the 1912-1943 period. Allow 45 minutes.

⏱ 45 min
5

Alinda's Belleni Tower, built between 1925 and 1927 by expatriate Parisi Belleni, is a neo-Gothic turreted stone building that now houses the island's folklore museum. Exhibits include the watch from the destroyer 'Queen Olga,' sunk in the harbor during the 1943 battle, and artwork painted by political prisoners interned on Leros under Greece's dictatorship (1967-1974).

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Leros's beaches are perfect for families: Alinda, the longest, is lined with tamarisks and its shallow waters suit children. Vromolithos offers a beach bar and crystalline water. Panteli, at the castle's foot, is the ideal spot in late afternoon when light settles on the ruined windmills along the ridge. For diving, Xirokampos in the south is renowned for its World War II wrecks—a good dozen ships and planes rest at 10 to 30 meters depth.

⏱ 120 min

'Koukouvades,' fish marinated with rosemary, are Leros's signature seafood specialty. 'Pitakia,' pies stuffed with fava beans, and 'Kakavia,' bouillabaisse prepared with fish pulled from caïques that very morning, are essential at tavernas in Panteli or Agia Marina. For dessert, 'Melopita'—honey-and-mizithra-cheese cake—is the local sweet touch.

⏱ 60 min

Ride a scooter (ideal for this reasonably sized island) up to the castle at sunset, when the last visitors have left and bats begin circling the battlements. The panorama of both bays in orange light is one of the Dodecanese's most memorable views. Scooter rentals are available at Agia Marina and Lakki ports.

⏱ 60 min

Karpathos

★★★★☆
1

Olympos is arguably the Dodecanese's most spellbinding village. Founded between the 7th and 9th centuries on the heights of Mount Profitis Ilias to escape Saracen raiders, it remained cut off from the rest of the island for centuries—the paved road only opened in 2011. The result: women still wear traditional embroidered costumes daily (not for tourists), the local dialect preserves Doric Greek phrasings, and single-room houses are built in intact medieval architecture. The 75 windmills along the ridge, many still operational, are the island's symbol. Plan a full day.

⏱ 180 min
2

Pigadia, Karpathos's capital and main port, is the island's logistical hub. Its natural semicircular harbor is one of the Dodecanese's most sheltered. Take time to visit the archaeological museum housed in the Prefecture Palace: Mycenaean ceramics, ancient coins, Roman statues—the collection documents 3,000 years of human presence on the island.

⏱ 60 min
3

Aperi, Karpathos's former capital until the 19th century, clings to the hillside with stone houses and flower-filled courtyards. The village is famous for its grand 19th-century mansions built by wealthy shipowners—some feature neoclassical facades that seem transported from another Mediterranean entirely. It's also one of the island's best-maintained villages.

⏱ 45 min
4

Menetes, at 350 meters elevation in the southern hinterland, is the birthplace of the Karpathos lyre—the traditional stringed instrument that has accompanied dances for centuries. The Church of the Dormition of the Mother of God is considered the island's most beautiful. The view across the sea from the village edge at sunset alone justifies the detour.

⏱ 40 min
5

Apella Beach is one of the Dodecanese's most photographed: a fine-sand arc framed by red schist cliffs and bathed in turquoise water. Access is via a track from the main road (about 15 minutes on foot from the parking area). There are no tavernas or parasols for rent—bring water and a picnic.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

If you're in Olympos on August 15th, don't leave under any circumstances: the Feast of the Dormition of the Virgin is celebrated here with epic songs transmitted orally for generations, costumed dances, and music played on lyre and tsambouna (local bagpipe) until dawn. Diaspora residents return from abroad just for this.

⏱ 120 min

The hike between Olympos and the port of Diafani descends about 8 km via a mountain trail that overlooks the sea. The view is vertiginous and the vegetation changes at every elevation. Count on 2.5 to 3 hours of walking. Boats connect Diafani to Pigadia for the return journey.

⏱ 180 min

'Makarounes' are Olympos's signature handmade pasta: shaped by hand through rolling between the palms, they're served with melted butter, fried onions and grated mizithra. You'll find them in Olympos tavernas—one of the rare places where they're still made fresh on site every morning. 'Kserotigana,' ribbons of fried dough drizzled with honey, are served at festivals.

⏱ 45 min

Astypalea

★★★☆☆
1

Astypalea's Chora is a cascade of cubic white houses tumbling down a rocky hillside to the old port of Pera Gialos. At the summit looms Querini Castle, built of dark stone by the eponymous Venetian family after 1413—contrasting spectacularly with the village's white walls below. The ramparts once served as exterior walls for three-story houses built against them inside. The 1956 earthquake left ruins within, but the monumental entrance remains intact and the view from the top over the two bays forming the island's isthmus is extraordinary. Free admission.

⏱ 60 min
2

The eight windmills aligned along the ridge of Chora, between the village and the castle, are Astypalea's symbol. Their position allowed them to capture north and south winds interchangeably according to season. One has been converted into the municipal library, another into a shop. In the evening, this is the locals' meeting point before descending to the tavernas.

⏱ 30 min
3

Pera Gialos, the old port at Chora's foot, is Astypalea's most authentic spot: painted wooden fishing boats, tavernas with plastic tables facing the sea, fish market in the morning. This is where the island truly lives, far from the tourist animation of Chora. Stroll the quay in late afternoon when fishermen return.

⏱ 30 min
4

The Church of Panagia Portaïtissa (18th century), just below the castle, is considered one of the Dodecanese's most beautiful: its carved and gilded wooden iconostasis was built over nine years by Agios Anthimos of Cephalonia, who was blind according to tradition. A copy of the Panagia Portaïtissa icon was brought from Mount Athos.

⏱ 25 min
5

Pera Gialos's archaeological museum sits directly on the old port waterfront. It houses finds ranging from prehistory to the medieval era, including pieces from the Kylindra necropolis—a unique burial site where 2,700 infant skeletons interred in jars were discovered on the castle hill's flank, spanning a thousand-year period. A local cult whose significance archaeologists still debate. The museum is small but dense: allow 30 to 40 minutes.

⏱ 35 min

Activities

Astypalea has the shape of a butterfly—two islands connected by an isthmus less than 100 meters wide. Explore the eastern part (Exo Nisi) by scooter or quad: the wild beaches of Kaminakia, Vatses and Agios Konstantinos are accessible via dirt tracks, with electric-blue water and zero tourist infrastructure. Bring water and provisions.

⏱ 180 min

Reserve half a day to reach Vathy by water taxi from Pera Gialos. This natural fjord on the island's east—accessible only by sea or via a poor-condition track—is one of the Dodecanese's most timeless atmospheres: a silent fishing village at the end of a narrow sea arm surrounded by arid hills. A single taverna serves the morning's catch. Boats generally depart late morning.

⏱ 180 min

Have a drink in front of Chora's windmills at sunset—it's the locals' gathering spot. Illuminated Chora gives the impression the village is either climbing the mountain or suspended in the void, depending on the viewing angle. The atmosphere shifts dramatically once night falls.

⏱ 60 min

Astypalea's 'Kopanisti' is a pungent, fermented soft-paste cheese served as mezze. The local 'Pouggia' are filled with almonds and mastiha (mastic resin), a perfumed variation on the specialty you may have tasted in Patmos. Ask for them in Pera Gialos pastry shops.

⏱ 30 min

Nisyros

★★★☆☆
1

Stefanos Crater, at the center of Nisyros's caldera, is one of the Aegean's most striking geological experiences. The caldera measures 4 km in diameter and Stefanos itself spans 300 meters across and 30 meters deep. The last phreatic eruption occurred in 1888, but hydrothermal activity is very present: sulfur fumaroles, ground hot and yellow underfoot, bubbling mud pots—all with a frank rotten-egg odor. Arrive before 9 am: in peak season, up to 1,000 day-trippers from Kos land in the morning. Closed shoes mandatory—the ground burns in places.

⏱ 75 min
2

Mandraki, Nisyros's capital, is a compact village with white houses and lanes paved with pebble mosaics—a technique you'll find in squares and even private courtyards. The archaeological museum covers more than 3,000 years of island history, from the Neolithic to the Byzantine era. The waterfront with its tavernas comes alive in the evening, when the day-trippers have departed and the island regains its calm.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Monastery of Panagia Spiliani—'Our Lady of the Cave'—is built directly inside the ruins of the Knights of Saint John castle, on a 30-meter rock above Mandraki. Access is via a staircase of more than 130 steps. The monastery is partly carved into natural rock: the atmosphere inside the cave-sanctuary, cool and dark, contrasts with the blinding whiteness of the Aegean. The 1725 iconostasis and library preserve manuscripts and liturgical objects of great value. From August 6-15, celebrations for the Dormition of the Virgin draw pilgrims from across the Dodecanese.

⏱ 45 min
4

Nikia, perched on the caldera's rim, is one of Greece's most photogenic villages. Its central square is entirely paved with pebble mosaics, bordered by colorful-shuttered houses. From here, the plunging view onto Stefanos Crater below is vertiginous—this is the ideal spot to grasp the volcano's scale. Two small tavernas serve on the square.

⏱ 50 min
5

Paleokastro, Mandraki's ancient acropolis, is remarkably well-preserved from the Classical era (6th-4th century BC). Its black volcanic stone walls run nearly 2 km in perimeter and some blocks weigh several tons. From the ramparts, the view embraces the port, the sea and neighboring islands. The climb takes about twenty minutes from Mandraki.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Hike between Nikia and the caldera on the trail that descends from the village rim to the crater floor—about 45 minutes down on a marked path that crosses landscapes of yellow sulfur and black rock. You transition from a white village with colored shutters to a near-lunar landscape in under an hour. The ascent is strenuous: count on 1 hour and bring water.

⏱ 120 min

The baths at Loutra, 1 km from Mandraki on the road to Pali, exploit sulfurous hot spring waters. The old-fashioned 19th-century thermal station has something quaintly authentic that grand spa complexes have lost. The waters are reputed for joints and skin. Access is free for outdoor baths.

⏱ 60 min

'Soumada,' a beverage made from bitter almond extract mixed with cold water, is Nisyros's signature non-alcoholic specialty—refreshing and fragrant. 'Pitia,' focaccia topped with tomatoes, onions, olives and herbs, is sold in Mandraki bakeries from morning. For a heartier meal, 'Bogana' (lamb braised with garlic and potatoes) and 'Kapamas' (goat stuffed with rice and cooked in tomato sauce) are the island's two traditional main dishes.

⏱ 45 min

Emborios, semi-abandoned on the caldera rim, has been reinventing itself in recent years: ruined houses are being restored one by one by artists and Athenians. A small café terrace overlooks the volcano directly—it's one of the island's most dramatic viewpoints. Come in the evening when raking light makes the fumaroles smoke.
