Oxford

★★★★☆
1

Oxford without Christ Church or Blenheim—this is where you realize what the rest of the route won't be. Favor the streets of Jericho and the Ashmolean Museum (founded 1683, collection of 120,000 objects) before heading to the banks of the Cherwell. In the evening, the pubs along the lanes around St Giles close early—arrive before 9pm.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

The scent of damp stone rises from the Cherwell's banks at dusk—that's when you take a punt. Hire a flat-bottomed boat from Cherwell Boathouse or Magdalen Bridge (around £20/hr, no booking needed on weekday evenings). Minimal effort, maximum effect.

Henley On Thames

★★★☆☆
1

The pink hippo on the sign watches the eights row past—it's the official mascot of the Leander Club, founded in 1818 and the world's first single-sport club according to their records—159 Olympic and Paralympic medals since 1908. The pink building by the water is open to non-members for dinner by reservation.

⏱ 30 min
2

Closed since September 2025, David Chipperfield's River & Rowing Museum (1998, the architect's first major UK project) remains visible from the promenade. The trust is seeking a new use for the green oak building—a page turning for Henley. Check before making a special trip.

⏱ 20 min
3

Henley Bridge has spanned the Thames since the 18th century—five stone arches, vista over the river from Hart Street, Georgian red brick on either side. Outside the Henley Royal Regatta (early July), the town is quiet and legible. Free.

Wallingford

★★☆☆☆
1

Locals still call it 'the burg'—Alfred the Great ringed it with earth ramparts in the 9th century to stop the Vikings, and the grid plan is still there. The grass banks of the Kinecroft rise 10 meters high. An entire town built in haste as a fortress, and it's survived 1,200 years of history. Free access.

⏱ 30 min
2

The grass ramparts are worth the detour—but the ruins of the Norman castle (built around 1067 on William the Conqueror's orders, never taken by siege) are the other face of the same place. The castle was the last to fall to the Parliamentarians in 1646, after Oxford. The gardens are freely accessible. The motte still measures 13 meters high.

Dorchester On Thames

★★☆☆☆
1

The Augustinian abbey founded in 1140 far exceeds its village size in scale—70 meters long for 1,000 inhabitants. The Jesse Tree window from 1330 is a unique fusion of sculpture, stained glass, and stone tracery: Christ's family tree is literally carved into the stone and extended in painted glass. Noted by Pevsner as a masterpiece.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Spectacular, almost deserted—compared to Christ Church in Oxford 13 kilometers away, Dorchester Abbey receives a fraction of the visitors for content ten times rarer. Also look for the lead font from 1170, one of the very rare English survivors. Free entry, open daily from 8am.

Abingdon On Thames

★★☆☆☆
1

Look for the notice at the County Hall entrance: 'Of all market buildings with an open ground floor, this is the largest in England,' Pevsner said. Built between 1678 and 1682 by Christopher Kempster—the man who carved the stone for St Paul's Cathedral with Wren. Baroque in Oxfordshire limestone, giant Corinthian pilasters. Museum free, roof access £2.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Sweet buns have been falling from the sky since 1761: at every major royal occasion, the mayor and councillors in ceremonial robes climb onto the County Hall roof and throw hundreds of currant buns to the crowd below. The tradition has been documented since the coronation of George III. No event scheduled? Look for bun-throwing newspapers in the ground-floor museum.
