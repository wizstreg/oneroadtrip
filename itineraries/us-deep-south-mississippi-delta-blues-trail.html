Memphis Tn

★★★★☆
1

At 450 Mulberry Street, the pale turquoise facade of the Lorraine Motel looks nearly unchanged from April 4, 1968. A wreath hangs on the second-floor balcony outside Room 306 - the spot where Martin Luther King Jr. was shot while talking to Jesse Jackson below. Walter Bailey had bought and renamed the hotel in 1945 after his wife Loree and the jazz song 'Sweet Lorraine'; during segregation it hosted Aretha Franklin, Otis Redding, and Ray Charles, all passing through to record at nearby Stax. The National Civil Rights Museum opened here in 1991 and was overhauled in a $27.5 million renovation in 2013-2014. Budget two and a half hours minimum - the 260-artifact span covers five centuries of civil rights history. Arrive before 10 a.m. on weekdays to beat school groups.

⏱ 150 min
2

The building at 706 Union Avenue is shockingly small - a converted auto-glass repair shop that Sam Phillips rented in 1950 for $150 a month. He opened Memphis Recording Service on January 3 that year, ripped out the tin roof, replaced it with acoustic tiles, then spent weeks clapping across every inch of the room to map its echo. That obsession produced the 'slapback' reverb on Elvis Presley's July 5, 1954 recording of 'That's All Right (Mama).' In March 1951, Jackie Brenston and his Delta Cats recorded 'Rocket 88' here - widely considered the first rock 'n' roll single. Designated a National Historic Landmark in 2003, the studio still records at night. Tours run daily; book online - they sell out on weekends. You get to touch the same microphone Elvis used.

⏱ 60 min
3

Jim Stewart and Estelle Axton - a brother and sister - bought an old movie theater on McLemore Avenue in 1959 and installed their recording equipment. From 1960 to 1974, a string of hits followed that still stop you cold: Otis Redding's 'Try a Little Tenderness', Booker T. & the M.G.'s 'Green Onions', Isaac Hayes' Academy Award-winning 'Theme From Shaft.' Of the 900 singles recorded here, 167 charted in the pop top 100. The original building was razed after Stax went bankrupt in the 1970s; the current museum opened May 2003, rebuilt on the same site, with over 2,000 exhibits including Hayes' gold-trimmed 1972 Cadillac Eldorado. The recreated Studio A alone is worth the trip. Give yourself two hours.

⏱ 120 min
4

Beale Street was founded in 1841, and by the 1920s it had become the main artery of Black Memphis - barber shops, blues clubs, churches, and a 2,000-seat park auditorium where President Theodore Roosevelt spoke in 1902. W.C. Handy moved here from Clarksdale in 1903 and wrote 'Beale Street Blues' in 1916; a teenage Elvis Presley absorbed the sound by hanging around the clubs in the 1950s. Congress recognized it as the 'Home of the Blues' in 1977. Today the three-block entertainment district runs live music every night. Arrive early evening to walk the street before the crowds arrive, then stay for a set at B.B. King's Blues Club or the Rum Boogie Cafe.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Every day at 11 a.m., a red carpet unrolls in the Peabody Hotel lobby and five North American Mallards ride the elevator down from their rooftop Duck Palace - a $200,000 marble enclosure with a bronze fountain. The tradition started as a tipsy prank in 1933 when general manager Frank Schutt put live hunting decoys in the lobby fountain after a hunting trip; by 1940, former circus trainer Edward Pembroke had formalized the march and served as Duckmaster for 50 years. The Peabody at 149 Union Avenue has been the social hub of Memphis since 1925. Arrive 20-30 minutes before 11 a.m. to claim a front-row spot along the red carpet.

⏱ 45 min

An evening blues walk starting at Beale and Second, working east through the neon-lit blocks, is the right way to end a Memphis day. Stop at the W.C. Handy statue in Handy Park, then duck into a bar for a live set - cover charges are typically under $10 and bands rotate all night. The street stays open until 5 a.m. on weekends. For the most authentic sound, look for smaller venues away from the main tourist drag; Thursday through Saturday brings the best bands.

Clarksdale Ms

★★☆☆☆
1

Three oversized electric guitars rise from a concrete island at the corner of North State Street and DeSoto Avenue - the old intersection of Highways 61 and 49. The sign reads 'The Crossroads.' Whether Robert Johnson really met the Devil here around 1930 is unprovable, but what's documented is the transformation: Son House heard Johnson play in 1930 and described him as near-unlistenable; two years later Johnson returned with a technique that left House speechless. His fewer than 30 recordings became foundational to the blues and influenced everyone from Eric Clapton to the Rolling Stones. Worth a stop, a photo, and a minute of silence.

⏱ 20 min
2

Inside a 1918 freight depot built for the Yazoo and Mississippi Valley Railroad, the Delta Blues Museum has been running since 1979 - Mississippi's oldest music museum. Its collection centers on the culture that produced the music: the sharecropper cabin where Muddy Waters lived on Stovall Farms sits in the main gallery. Exhibits trace the migration of bluesmen north along Highway 61 to Memphis, St. Louis, and Chicago. Don't miss the listening stations and the artifacts annotated in Son House's own hand. The museum is at #1 Blues Alley Lane. Allow 90 minutes; pick up the printed Clarksdale walking map at the desk.

⏱ 90 min
3

Ground Zero Blues Club opened in May 2001 in a former cotton-grading warehouse, deliberately unrefurbished. Mismatched chairs, Christmas lights, graffiti walls: it looks like it's never been cleaned up, which is exactly the point. Academy Award-winning actor Morgan Freeman co-founded it with then-Clarksdale mayor Bill Luckett and Howard Stovall to give local musicians a regular venue. Live music runs Wednesday through Saturday, usually from around 9 p.m.; cover is typically $10. Freeman still shows up occasionally. Come for dinner too - the Southern food is genuine.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Ten minutes south of downtown, Hopson Plantation is where the first successful mechanical cotton picker was field-tested - the machine that made hundreds of thousands of Delta sharecroppers redundant and set off the Great Migration north. One of the original pickers stands on the property. The old commissary is now a bar; the Shack Up Inn next door rents genuine sharecropper shacks as accommodation. An hour here gives the blues its economic and social backstory more concisely than any museum exhibit.

⏱ 60 min

Red's Lounge on Sunflower Avenue is the real thing: a cinderblock juke joint with a wood-burning stove and no stage, musicians set up in the corner. Smaller, rawer, and more local than Ground Zero, it's where Delta blues residents actually go on Friday and Saturday nights. Red Paden has been running it for decades. No cover most nights, no cocktail menu - cold beer and loud music in a room the size of a living room.

⏱ 120 min

Greenville Ms

★☆☆☆☆
1

Leland, 10 miles east of Greenville on Highway 61, produced Jimmy Reed, Little Milton, Eddie Cusic, and blues-rock guitarist Johnny Winter, whose father ran a local cotton business. The Highway 61 Blues Museum on the main drag covers around 80 Delta blues artists from the surrounding area, with instruments, photographs, and hand-annotated festival posters. Blues musician Son Thomas lived here for decades; his son Pat Thomas still plays in the museum most days, unpaid and purely for love of the music. Leland is also Jim Henson's hometown - the Kermit the Frog exhibit is next door if the kids need a break.

⏱ 60 min
2

Robert Johnson recorded 'They're Red Hot' about tamales in 1936, and that tradition runs deep. Greenville, which declared itself the Hot Tamale Capital of the World in 2012, is the best place in the Delta to taste it. Delta tamales are smaller than Mexican ones, made with cornmeal instead of masa, heavily spiced, and simmered rather than steamed - the recipe most likely crossed between Mexican migrant farm workers and Black laborers in the cotton fields in the early 20th century. Scott's Hot Tamales on MLK Boulevard uses a family recipe dating to 1950, wrapping each one by hand in corn shucks. Doe's Eat Place on Nelson Street has served tamales alongside its steaks since the 1940s. Budget an hour for lunch.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The drive south from Clarksdale on Highway 61 is itself a full activity: flat Delta farmland, bayous, shotgun houses, grain elevators, Mississippi Blues Trail markers appearing every few miles. Stop at each one - they're brief, factual, and densely specific, each giving a name, a date, a story. By the time you reach Greenville you'll have covered the arc from Charley Patton through the Great Migration. Keep the windows down and drive slowly; the light over Delta cotton fields in morning hours is worth photographing.

⏱ 120 min

Lake Washington, 20 miles east of Greenville, is an oxbow lake - one of the crescent-shaped bodies of water left when the Mississippi River shifted course. Blues musicians played around its shores for generations, and the surrounding county roads look largely unchanged from the sharecropping era. An evening drive around the lake, stopping to look at the old plantation structures, closes the day with a quieter look at the landscape that produced all this music. Pack mosquito repellent between May and September.

Vicksburg Ms

★★★☆☆
1

Established February 21, 1899, the Vicksburg National Military Park protects 1,800 acres of battlefield where Grant besieged Confederate defenders for 47 days - May 18 to July 4, 1863. When Vicksburg surrendered, the Confederacy was split in two and the Union held the entire Mississippi River. The park's 1,325 monuments and markers are second in number only to Gettysburg's, with 20 miles of reconstructed trenches and 144 emplaced cannon. Drive the 16-mile tour road first, then walk the key positions - the sight lines explain the siege more clearly than any text panel ever could.

⏱ 150 min
2

The USS Cairo is the only surviving Civil War ironclad gunboat in the country, and it sits under a shelter inside the military park looking exactly as battered as you'd expect after 102 years submerged in the Yazoo River. Confederate forces sank it on December 12, 1862 with an electrically detonated mine - the first U.S. warship ever lost to a floating mine - going down in 12 minutes. It was raised in 1964 and transferred to the National Park Service in 1972. The attached museum holds thousands of crew artifacts recovered from the wreck: boots, combs, pocket knives, remarkably intact after a century in the mud. Budget 60 minutes.

⏱ 60 min
3

General Pemberton reportedly watched Union naval attacks on the Mississippi from the bell tower of the Old Court House during the siege - and when Vicksburg fell on July 4, 1863, Grant's troops marched in and raised the Union flag from the same roof. The Greek Revival building at 1008 Cherry Street was constructed between 1858 and 1860 by enslaved craftsmen; it nearly came down in 1939 before historian Eva Whitaker Davis rallied the community and saved it, reopening it as a museum in 1948. Inside: Civil War newspapers printed on wallpaper when paper ran out, the tie Jefferson Davis wore at his Confederate presidential inauguration, and Native American Mississippian-culture ceramics. National Historic Landmark since 1968.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Along Vicksburg's floodwall, artist Robert Dafford's 32 life-sized murals run for several hundred feet, first unveiled April 12, 2002. They depict the city's history from Native American settlement through the Civil War, the steamboat era, and the 20th century. Walk the entire length - each panel has a text plaque, and the Mississippi River overlooks at Louisiana Circle or Navy Circle off South Washington Street are among the most dramatic on the entire river. Lincoln called Vicksburg 'the key' to winning the war; standing above the water on the bluffs, you see why.

⏱ 75 min

Solly's Hot Tamales on Washington Street has been open since 1939 - Vicksburg's version of the Delta tamale, made to a three-generation recipe that's subtly different from Greenville's. Dinner here or at one of the other restaurants along Washington Street closes a dense Civil War day with food that's rooted in the living culture of the region. The walkable stretch of Washington Street near the park has several good local options.

Jackson Ms

★★★☆☆
1

The Two Mississippi Museums opened December 9, 2017 - the state's bicentennial - and the Mississippi Civil Rights Museum is the only state-operated civil rights museum in the country. Its eight galleries run from the Middle Passage through Reconstruction, Jim Crow, and the movement years 1945-1975. At the building's heart, a 40-foot sculpture lights up every 30 minutes with gospel songs that powered the marches; when it activates, it stops people mid-conversation. The Emmett Till section alone justifies the trip: the Bryant's Grocery doors are here, alongside the original Jet magazine pages from 1955 that broke the story open nationwide. Free every Sunday. Allow three hours.

⏱ 180 min
2

By 1908 one-third of Farish Street was Black-owned - doctors, banks, jazz clubs, a hospital, record studios that would shape American music. Medgar Evers ran the NAACP's state field office from above the Big Apple Inn, the restaurant that's been serving pig ear sandwiches since Mexican immigrant Juan Mora opened it in 1939. The district was added to the National Register of Historic Places in 1980; by 1995 the National Trust listed it among America's Most Endangered Places. Walk the nine-block stretch for the Blues Trail markers and the restored Alamo Theatre. Lunch at the Big Apple Inn - order the pig ears, which Mora invented from a box of butcher scraps he refused to throw away.

⏱ 75 min
3

Pulitzer Prize-winning author Eudora Welty lived at 1119 Pinehurst Street from 1925 until her death in 2001 - 76 years in the same house, writing nearly all her fiction in the same bedroom. She bequeathed the Tudor Revival home and its entire contents to the State of Mississippi; every wall is still lined with books in the positions she left them. Her mother Chestina designed the garden the same year the family moved in, planting over 40 varieties of camellias that bloom through winter. Designated a National Historic Landmark in 2004. Tours are guided only - reserve ahead. The 3 p.m. tour ends in the garden, which is the right way to close the trip.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Mississippi Blues Trail runs through Jackson with markers most visitors walk right past - the Subway Lounge plaque marks a legendary basement club; the Ace Records marker on Capitol Street celebrates one of the South's most successful independent labels of the 1950s. Download the Mississippi Blues Trail app before arriving, then spend two hours following markers on foot through downtown and out toward the Medgar Evers home site. Each marker is a dense paragraph of verified history; the cumulative walk gives Jackson its full musical and civil rights context before catching a flight.

⏱ 120 min

F. Jones Corner and Johnny T's Bistro & Blues bookend the Farish Street nightlife revival - Johnny T's sits in the same building as the old Crystal Palace, where Duke Ellington and Louis Armstrong performed in the 1940s and 1950s. Both venues run live music on weekend nights with a nominal cover. The pineapple vodka at F. Jones Corner is a local institution. This is where Jackson residents actually go on a Friday night, which makes it the more interesting option.
