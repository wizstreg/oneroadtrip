Secret Extremadura: From Plasencia to Zafra Through Valleys and Dehesas

📅 9 days📍 8 stops🚗 420 km⏱ 6h

Plasencia → Hervas → Coria → Caceres → Merida → Zafra → Jerez De Los Caballeros

Plasencia → Hervas → Coria → Caceres → Merida → Zafra → Jerez De Los Caballeros

This 8-day slow travel itinerary crosses the two most overlooked halves of Extremadura: the mountainous north with its cherry valleys, medieval Jewish quarters, and the monastery where Charles V chose to die, then the south with its dehesas, Templar villages, and the astonishing Olivenza—a Portuguese town on Spanish soil. Between Plasencia and Zafra, you'll encounter 1,700-year-old Roman walls, a ghost village emptied for a dam that never flooded it, and the finest Manueline architecture outside Portugal.

Stop 1

Plasencia

★★☆☆☆
📍 Catedral Nueva Plasencia📍 Catedral Vieja Plasencia📍 Plaza Mayor Plasencia📍 Judería Plasencia📍 Murallas Plasencia📍 Acueducto Plasencia
1

Plasencia keeps its treasures close to the chest. Founded in 1186 by King Alfonso VIII of Castile, it boasts a unique quirk in Spain: two cathedrals stuck side by side. The Catedral Vieja, begun in the 13th century, is a fine example of Romanesque-Gothic transition—sober, compact, with a baroque altarpiece and a stunning ivory Christ. The Catedral Nueva, started in the 15th century, was never finished—but what exists is breathtaking: soaring ribbed vaults, a magnificent altarpiece attributed to Gregorio Fernández, and a heady mix of flamboyant Gothic, Renaissance, and Baroque. They're visited together—don't choose, see both.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Plaza Mayor of Plasencia has been the beating heart of the city since medieval times. Irregularly shaped and ringed by soportales (arcades) lined with bars and restaurants, it hosts a market every Tuesday that's been running uninterrupted since the 12th century. Grab a terrace seat and watch the unhurried rhythm of this 40,000-soul town that carries itself like a regional capital.

⏱ 30 min
3

Plasencia is part of the Red de Juderías de España—the network of Spain's historic Jewish quarters. The Jewish community settled here from the city's founding in 1186. Traces survive in two neighborhoods: the old judería de la Mota (around today's Parador, a former San Vicente Ferrer convent built on the site of the first synagogue confiscated in 1477) and the "new judería" around Calle Trujillo and Calle Zapatería. In these alleys, plaques set into the pavement bear the names of Jewish families who lived here—Yuçe Alaçán, Abraham Almale, Samuel... Don't miss the Jewish cemetery of El Berrocal outside the walls: it's the only preserved Jewish burial ground in Extremadura, with about twenty anthropomorphic tombs carved into rock, facing Jerusalem.

⏱ 60 min
4

Walk the circuit of Plasencia's medieval ramparts, still well-preserved along much of their length. You'll pass through the Trujillo, Coria, and Berrozana gates and cross paths with the acueducto—a restored medieval aqueduct running through the San Antón neighborhood. The Palacio del Marqués de Mirabel, built on the site of the old judería de la Mota in the 15th century, is worth a look for its facade and garden (visible from the street).

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Northern Extremadura's cuisine is generous and underrated. Try Torta del Casar—a creamy soft-paste cheese eaten with a spoon—migas extremeñas (fried breadcrumbs with chorizo and pimiento), jamón ibérico de bellota, and Valle del Jerte cherries in season. The Plaza Mayor and surrounding streets are packed with tapas bars where you'll eat well and abundantly.

Stop 2

Hervas

★★☆☆☆
📍 Garganta de los Infiernos Valle del Jerte📍 Los Pilones Valle del Jerte📍 Cabezuela del Valle📍 Judería Hervás📍 Puente Fuente Chiquita Hervás📍 Calle Rabilero Hervás
1

Devote the morning to the Valle del Jerte, one of Extremadura's most spectacular landscapes. The name comes from Arabic "xerete"—crystalline river—and that's exactly what it is: a deep V-shaped valley framed by the Sierra de Tormantos and the Traslasierra mountains, where dozens of gargantas (gorges) run with mountain water. The valley holds 1.5 million cherry trees whose simultaneous flowering, usually late March to early April, transforms the slopes into a vast white mantle—this is the Cerezo en Flor festival, declared a National Tourist Interest celebration. In summer, the gargantas become natural swimming spots of rare beauty.

⏱ 30 min
2

The valley's jewel is the Reserva Natural de la Garganta de los Infiernos, 7,000 protected hectares since 1994—Extremadura's second most-visited natural area. The circular trail (about 7 km, 2 hours) starts from the Interpretation Centre and descends to Los Pilones—giant's kettles carved into granite by water over centuries, forming natural pools of crystalline water where you can swim in summer. The path threads through oak, chestnut, and cherry forests, with waterfalls at every turn. Motor access is forbidden—tour operators offer trips in authorized 4x4s to the trailhead.

⏱ 150 min
3

Heading down the valley, stop at Cabezuela del Valle, whose historic center is classified as a Conjunto Histórico-Artístico. Its narrow streets with half-timbered chestnut houses, overhanging balconies, and heraldic doorways make it one of the valley's prettiest villages. The church's detached tower and the Plaza de la Independencia deserve a photo stop.

⏱ 30 min
4

In the afternoon, head to Hervás in the neighboring Valle del Ambroz. This village has one of Spain's best-preserved juderías—classified as Conjunto Histórico-Artístico since 1969. The Jewish community settled here after the 1391 pogroms, welcomed by the Duke of Béjar. At its peak in the 15th century, nearly 50 Jewish families lived here. Walk down Calle Abajo, the main street of the barrio judío: the adobe and timber-framed chestnut houses, streets so narrow you could touch both walls with outstretched arms (Travesía del Moral is 55 cm wide—one of Spain's narrowest), Stars of David carved on facades, and street names like "Amistad Judeo-Cristiana" (Jewish-Christian Friendship) all testify to a past that remains very present.

⏱ 60 min
5

Finish at the Puente de la Fuente Chiquita, a sturdy medieval granite bridge spanning the Río Ambroz at the foot of the judería. It's one of Hervás's most photographed images, especially in autumn when the chestnuts turn golden. At number 19 Calle Rabilero, a building wider than the others occupies the site of the old synagogue. In July, the Fiesta de los Conversos reenacts medieval life and the 1492 expulsion in an open-air performance by the river.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Hervás and Valle del Ambroz cuisine centers on the chestnut—which provides timber for houses, autumn fruit, and accompaniment to many dishes. In autumn (late October through December), the Otoñada celebrates chestnuts with markets, tastings, and walks through centuries-old chestnut groves. The rest of the year, try dulces sefardíes (Sephardic-inspired pastries) that some Hervás restaurants offer on themed menus.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 3

Coria

★★★★★
📍 Monasterio de Yuste Cuacos de Yuste📍 Catedral de Coria📍 Murallas Romanas Coria📍 Puente sin río Coria📍 Granadilla pueblo abandonado
1

Start the day at the Monastery of Yuste, a European Heritage site nestled in the Sierra de Gredos foothills near Cuacos de Yuste. This is where Charles V—Charles I of Spain and V of the Holy Roman Empire, the most powerful man in the world—chose to retire after abdicating in 1556 in favor of his son Philip II. He arrived on February 3, 1557, with an entourage of about sixty people. His chamber was positioned against the church choir so he could attend mass from his bed—he suffered from terrible gout that confined him to his litter. He died here on September 21, 1558, at age 58, from malarial fevers. The visit shows both cloisters (one Gothic, one Renaissance), the 15th-century church, and the emperor's apartments—surprisingly austere for the man who'd ruled an empire where the sun never set.

⏱ 90 min
2

Head to Coria in the afternoon (50 km, 45 minutes). This town is one of Spain's oldest—the ancient Caurium of the Vettones, which became a Roman municipium. Its Roman walls, built between the 3rd and 4th centuries, are probably the best-preserved as a whole set in all of Europe: over 1,100 meters of ramparts with about twenty square towers and four gates, two of them original Roman (Puerta del Sol and Puerta de la Guía). The walls are 8 meters thick in places. Walk the circuit—it's about a 30-minute stroll that gives you vertigo when you think these stones have been standing for 1,700 years.

⏱ 45 min
3

Coria's cathedral, built between the 15th and 18th centuries on the remains of a Visigothic church, blends Gothic, Plateresque, and Baroque styles. Its Plateresque facade is remarkable, as is its Churrigueresque tower offering superb views over town and countryside. The cathedral guards an extraordinary relic: what's claimed to be the tablecloth from Christ's Last Supper, attested in a papal bull from 1404. Admission is free and opening hours are generous (closed Monday mornings).

⏱ 40 min
4

Don't miss the bridge without a river—Coria's most surreal image. This five-arched stone bridge, built in the 16th century probably on earlier Roman foundations, spanned the Río Alagón. But the river changed course—likely gradually following floods, worsened by the 1755 Lisbon earthquake—and now flows several hundred meters away. Result: a bridge without a river and a river without a bridge. Walk under the arches for the best photo, especially late afternoon.

⏱ 20 min
5

If daylight permits, detour 30 minutes to Granadilla, a fascinating ghost village. This fortified villa of Almohad origin (9th century), which governed 15 municipalities under its jurisdiction in the Middle Ages, was emptied of inhabitants in 1955 when Franco's regime decided to build the Gabriel y Galán dam. Cruel irony: the water never reached the village. Declared a Conjunto Histórico-Artístico in 1980, it was partially restored through an educational program. You can visit the cobbled lanes, the circular wall (one of Spain's best-preserved alongside Lugo and Ávila), and the 15th-century castle built by the Duke of Alba, whose terrace offers striking views over the reservoir and hills.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

In the evening in Coria, try dulces de las monjas—cloistered convent sweets. The town's convents sell handmade pastries (quemados, dulces de yema) through a rotating hatch, without seeing the nuns who make them. It's a typically Spanish tradition that persists in Extremadura's small towns.

⏱ 30 min

Stop 4

Caceres

★★★★☆
📍 Mirador Salto del Gitano Monfragüe📍 Castillo de Monfragüe📍 Ciudad Monumental Cáceres
1

En route south, cross through Monfragüe National Park, one of Europe's top raptor-watching sites. Stop at the Salto del Gitano viewpoint: from this lookout above the Tajo River, you'll observe—almost guaranteed—griffon vultures gliding above their cliff-nesting colonies, black storks, Spanish imperial eagles, and sometimes black vultures. During breeding season (February–June), the spectacle is guaranteed. Allow 1 hour for the viewpoint, more if you climb to Monfragüe castle for 360° views over the dehesas.

⏱ 90 min
2

Arrive in Cáceres late afternoon. If you haven't yet seen the Ciudad Monumental (UNESCO since 1986), treat yourself to at least a twilight stroll through its lanes—when day-trippers have left and the golden-stone Renaissance palaces light up. Plaza de Santa María, the Bujaco tower, and the Palacio de los Golfines de Abajo deserve a look even in passing. If you've already visited Cáceres, simply enjoy the evening on a Plaza Mayor terrace.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Cáceres is a tapas town. The bars around Plaza Mayor and Calle Pintores serve generous portions of jamón ibérico, torta del casar, and pimientos asados. Order a vino de pitarra—an artisanal local wine you'll hardly find outside Extremadura.

⏱ 90 min

Stop 5

Merida

★★★★★
📍 Teatro Romano Mérida📍 Puente Romano Mérida
1

Mérida is the ancient Augusta Emerita, capital of the Roman province of Lusitania founded in 25 BC, and one of the best-preserved Roman sites outside Italy (UNESCO since 1993). If you haven't visited yet, absolutely don't miss the Roman theatre—built between 16 and 15 BC, it still hosts a classical theatre festival every summer. Its scaenae frons with Corinthian columns, 17 meters high, is one of the most spectacular in the Roman Empire. Right next door, the amphitheatre (8 BC, 15,000 seats) completes the picture.

⏱ 60 min
2

Cross the Puente Romano over the Guadiana—792 meters long, 60 arches, one of the longest Roman bridges still standing. The crossing is pedestrian and offers beautiful views over the city and Arab Alcazaba at twilight. If you've already seen Mérida, simply pass through town and enjoy the evening dining near the Temple of Diana.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Mérida's cuisine is that of the dehesa—the oak-pasture landscape covering much of Extremadura. Try Iberian pork in all its forms (lomo, presa, secreto), caldereta de cordero (lamb stew), and wines from Tierra de Barros, the wine zone extending south toward Almendralejo.

Stop 6

Zafra

★★★★☆
📍 Alcázar de los Duques de Feria Zafra📍 Plaza Grande Zafra📍 Plaza Chica Zafra📍 Colegiata de la Candelaria Zafra📍 Puerta de Jerez Zafra
1

Zafra is nicknamed "little Seville" for its white streets, flower-filled patios, and Andalusian atmosphere—but it's stayed far quieter than its big neighbor. Start with the Alcázar de los Duques de Feria, a 15th-century fortress flanked by nine crenellated towers, now converted to a Parador. Hernán Cortés stayed here before sailing off to conquer Mexico. Even if you're not spending the night, step inside to see the arcaded interior courtyard and coffered ceilings—Parador staff are used to curious visitors.

⏱ 40 min
2

Zafra has two adjoining squares forming the town's heart. Plaza Grande, vast and airy, is lined with porticoes sheltering cafés and terraces. Plaza Chica, more intimate, was the medieval market site—the stone columns bordering it still bear the marks of standard measures carved by merchants to verify weights and lengths. The covered passage connecting the two squares is a charming detail.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Colegiata de la Candelaria, from the 15th century, is Zafra's main church. Its main altarpiece is impressive and its position on Plaza Grande makes it a natural landmark. Just next door, the Convento de Santa Clara (15th century) shelters remarkable altarpieces and wrought-iron grilles.

⏱ 30 min
4

Enter Zafra through the Puerta de Jerez, one of Extremadura's finest town gates. From there, wander the historic center's streets, declared a Bien de Interés Cultural—the whitewashed palaces, wrought-iron balconies, and patios glimpsed through half-open doors justify the town's nickname. In the evening, the atmosphere on Plaza Grande's terraces is delightful.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Zafra sits at the heart of Tierra de Barros, Extremadura's main wine region. Tour operators offer bodega visits in the area, especially toward Almendralejo (30 km), the region's "wine capital." Extremadura wines, long underrated, have improved tremendously—try reds from tempranillo and garnacha, and whites from pardina and cayetana.

⏱ 120 min

If you prefer staying in town, Zafra hosts a lively flea market on Thursdays in Plaza Grande, a direct legacy of the medieval fairs that made the town prosperous. The Feria Internacional Ganadera (livestock fair) in September–October is one of Lower Extremadura's major events—if you're there then, don't miss it.

Zafra

★★★★☆
📍 Iglesia Santa María Magdalena Olivenza📍 Castillo de Olivenza📍 Museo Etnográfico Olivenza📍 Santa Casa Misericordia Olivenza
1

Dedicate the day to Olivenza, 85 km northwest of Zafra. It's one of Spain's most unusual places: a Portuguese town on Spanish soil. Founded by the Templars in the 13th century, it remained Portuguese for five centuries (1297–1801) before being annexed by Spain during the brief War of the Oranges. Portugal has never recognized this annexation—officially, Olivenza is still claimed by Lisbon. Result: black-and-white Portuguese-style cobblestones, Manueline architecture, and azulejos coexist with Spanish signage. Since 2010, street names are posted in both languages.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Igreja de Santa María Magdalena is Olivenza's masterpiece and the finest example of Manueline architecture outside Portugal—after Lisbon's Jerónimos Monastery. Commissioned by King Manuel I in 1510 and completed in 1560, it has three naves resting on eight twisted granite columns typical of Manueline style. The walls are covered with 18th-century blue-and-white azulejos, and six baroque altarpieces complete the ensemble. In 2012, Spanish internet users voted it "Spain's most beautiful corner"—a delicious paradox for an utterly Portuguese monument.

⏱ 45 min
3

Olivenza's castle, built by the Templars then reinforced under Portuguese kings, has a keep tower 37 meters high—the tallest on the entire Spanish-Portuguese border. Erected in 1488 on flat terrain, it had to compensate through height what the land didn't provide in natural defense. It's accessed by a ramp (no stairs)—so it's accessible to all. From the top, the panorama sweeps across the plain to the Guadiana and Portugal. The castle also houses the Museo Etnográfico González Santana, one of Extremadura's richest ethnographic museums.

⏱ 60 min
4

Stop by the Santa Casa de la Misericordia, founded in 1501 by King Manuel I himself. The chapel's interior walls are covered with 18th-century Portuguese azulejos depicting Old and New Testament scenes—a remarkable collection. The town hall on Plaza de la Constitución preserves a Manueline doorway that's the town symbol.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Olivenza is a gastronomic border post: you'll eat migas extremeñas alongside bacalhau Portuguese-style, and convent pastries blend traditions from both countries. Lunch in the historic center and taste both cuisines—it's the only place in Spain where that's possible in the same village.

Stop 8

Jerez De Los Caballeros

★★★★★
📍 Fortaleza Templaria Jerez de los Caballeros📍 Iglesia San Bartolomé Jerez de los Caballeros📍 Iglesia La Encarnación Jerez de los Caballeros📍 Torre Sangrienta Jerez de los Caballeros
1

Jerez de los Caballeros is the final stop on this itinerary—and what a finale. Birthplace of Vasco Núñez de Balboa, the first European to sight the Pacific Ocean in 1513, it has a spectacular historic center dominated by the silhouettes of its baroque towers. The Templar fortress, built in the 13th century when the Knights Templar controlled this frontier with Muslim Andalusia, overlooks the town from a hilltop. It's in the Torre Sangrienta (Bloody Tower) that Jerez's last Templars were supposedly massacred in 1312 during the dissolution of the order. The legend is probably exaggerated, but it fits the mood of the place.

⏱ 45 min
2

Jerez's two baroque churches are among Extremadura's finest. The Iglesia de San Bartolomé has a bell tower covered in colorful azulejos that recalls Seville's bell towers—it's the town's iconic image. The Iglesia de La Encarnación, with its own slender baroque tower, completes an urban landscape you'd swear was Andalusian. Climb the sloping streets to discover the noble palaces, carved coats of arms, and flower-filled patios that built the reputation of this conquistador town.

⏱ 40 min
3

On Plaza de Vasco Núñez de Balboa, a statue honors the town's most famous son. Born here around 1475, Balboa sailed for the New World in 1500 and crossed the Isthmus of Panama in 1513 to become the first European to see the Pacific Ocean. The plaza also offers fine views over the surrounding hills covered in holm oaks—the dehesa landscape that characterizes all of Lower Extremadura.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Jerez de los Caballeros sits at the heart of the jamón ibérico de bellota production zone—the world's finest ham. Black Iberian pigs roam free in the dehesas, feeding on holm oak acorns during the montanera (autumn). If you're passing October through February, you'll see the pigs under the oaks—a unique landscape. Regional tour operators offer visits to ham-curing facilities with tastings. Even out of season, every bar in Jerez serves hand-sliced jamón ibérico—it's the best way to close this Extremadura journey.
