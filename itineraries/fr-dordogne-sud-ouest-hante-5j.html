Sarlat La Caneda

★★★★☆
1

Sarlat survived the Middle Ages by accident: the Hundred Years' War and the Wars of Religion spared it, which was rare enough that André Malraux made it his pilot city for heritage preservation in 1962. Sixty-five protected monuments within 15 hectares - one of the densest concentrations in France. The Maison de La Boétie is worth stopping for: a Renaissance façade with three levels of sculpted windows, built in 1530, and inside (in theory) an 18-year-old philosopher who wrote the Discourse on Voluntary Servitude while his contemporaries fought religious wars. Wander for an hour, note the contrast between the dark stone and tourists in shorts, then take the road to Domme - the real reason for the day.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Lantern of the Dead is Sarlat's most honest monument: eleven meters of Romanesque cylindrical tower, 12th century, and nobody has ever known exactly what it was for. Funerary lantern? Memorial monument? Tomb of an English king trapped by the Sarladais in 1147 according to an unverified local tradition? Eight hundred years later, specialists still argue. It's rare for a medieval monument to keep its mystery intact - and not for lack of trying. Free access, five minutes suffice.

⏱ 15 min
3

Friday the 13th of October 1307 - the date does have a history - Philip the Fair triggers the simultaneous arrest of all the Templars in France. Sealed orders sent a month in advance to the bailiffs, opened that very morning. Two to three thousand knights rounded up in a single day. At Domme, 70 of them end up in the two towers of the Porte des Tours, built in 1281. For eleven years, knowing that Jacques de Molay burns in Paris in 1314 and that the Order is dissolved, they carve into the stone what they have to say - pattée crosses, crucifixions, inscriptions 'Clemens est antichristus'. In 1970, Canon Tonnelier attributes all this to the Templars. In 2018, an archaeological study challenges this attribution: 'place of waiting, even idleness'. The debate remains open. What's certain: the originals are fading, you're watching them disappear. Guided tour €4 - without a guide, you understand nothing.

⏱ 60 min
4

From the Cliff Walk, the Dordogne unfolds its meanders 250 meters below, and three medieval castles appear all at once in the frame - Castelnaud, Beynac, Marqueyssac. In October, the low late-afternoon light brings out the towers as if they were cut with a scalpel. Twenty minutes, free access - it's the best moment of the day to understand why they built on high ground.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Lunch in Sarlat: Le Bistrot, place du Peyrou - classic Périgord bistro, duck breast, foie gras, gizzard salad, €25-40. If you're there on a Saturday morning, the market at place de la Liberté (truffles, foie gras, walnuts, porcini in season) is the real first act of the journey.

⏱ 90 min

Dinner and night in Domme or Sarlat. For atmosphere: L'Esplanade in Domme, hotel-restaurant with panoramic terrace over the valley, €130-220, dinner €45-65. Otherwise below the village, La Belle Étoile on the banks of the Dordogne in Cénac (€100-180). In Sarlat, Le Présidial in a former 17th-century courthouse, Périgord cuisine, €45-70.

⏱ 120 min

Rocamadour

★★★★☆
1

Thirty-five meters in diameter, seventy-five meters drop before reaching the underground river. In the Middle Ages, Lot tradition said the Devil in frustration had dug this hole with a kick of his hoof - as good an explanation as any for a chasm nobody understood. More down-to-earth: villagers threw the bodies of executed criminals and suicides here, who couldn't be buried in sacred ground. Édouard-Alfred Martel, in 1889, found several medieval human skeletons at the bottom. Today the underground network spans 50 km of identified galleries - some branches still unexplored. The boat ride on the river lasts 500 meters at a constant 13°C. Bring a jacket, really. Ticket €17, advance booking required in season.

⏱ 90 min
2

The arrival via the L'Hospitalet viewpoint - east side, before descending into town - is mandatory. The entire citadel appears from above: village, sanctuaries, castle, each at a different level of the cliff, 150 meters of elevation change. It's dizzying and somewhat absurd, like a town built by someone who wasn't afraid of heights. Park at L'Hospitalet and walk down rather than taking the shuttle - the descent gives the measure of the thing. The sanctuary was born around 1166 from a discovery of an incorrupt body in a cave; in the 12th-13th centuries, hundreds of thousands of pilgrims climbed here each year. The third pilgrimage site of Christian West after Rome and Compostela. Henry II Plantagenet comes here barefoot in 1170 - shortly after indirectly commissioning the assassination of Thomas Becket. Kings confess too.

⏱ 30 min
3

The 216 steps of the Pilgrims' Stairway were climbed on one's knees, with a stop at each step to pray, loaded with chains symbolizing sins. Henry II did this for a full week. In the Notre-Dame Chapel at the summit: the Black Virgin, walnut wood statue from the 12th century, 70 cm, still venerated today. And stuck in the cliff above, Durandal - Roland's sword. According to tradition, Roland dying at Roncevaux in 778 threw it toward Rocamadour to protect it from the Saracens. Six hundred kilometers as the crow flies. Physically impossible, but the sword has been there since the 12th century. The neighboring pilgrims' cemetery receives for 800 years those who die en route - notably in 1348-1350, when thousands of sick people flock to the Black Virgin during the Black Death. Most die on the spot. Castle ticket €3, sanctuaries free.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Lunch in Padirac village or at Auberge de Padirac, regional Lot cuisine, €25-40. No particular atmosphere, but the meal between the chasm and Rocamadour is welcome.

⏱ 60 min

Night in Rocamadour (Chez Anne-Marie, simple cuisine €30-40, or Le Balcon au Château with plunging view €60-90) or in Cahors (1h southwest, Le Balandre Michelin-starred €60-100). Cahors is worth the detour for the 14th-century Pont Valentré - 6 fortified towers over the Lot, and the local legend of the Devil who helped build it in exchange for the first soul to cross it.

⏱ 120 min

Mirepoix

★★★☆☆
1

First shock on arriving in Albi: the cathedral looks like a prison. Blind walls of red brick without lateral windows, 78-meter bell tower, massive plan - it's deliberate. Inquisitor Bishop Bernard de Castanet begins construction in 1282; he justifiably feared a popular revolt, because his predecessors had burned heretics a few hundred meters away. A fortress-cathedral was the only model that seemed tenable to him. Completed in 1480, it's the world's largest brick cathedral - 113 meters long, 200 years of construction. And then you enter. Complete reversal: 18,500 m² of vaults painted in blue, the largest painted surface of the Middle Ages in France, choir in lace of flamboyant Gothic stone. Penitentiary exterior, opulent interior. The message is as political as it is spiritual. Free ticket for the nave, €5 for the choir.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Last Judgment fresco (1474-1484), 270 m² under the organ, is clinically disturbing. The damned to the right of Christ aren't vaguely tormented - they're flayed with anatomical precision, muscles and viscera visible, tortured according to their sin: misers drink molten metal, gluttons eat toads, the lustful are impaled. It's propaganda of fear, made by people who had burned heretics for real and wanted the people to understand what awaited them. Detail added by a 17th-century bishop: a door pierced right through the middle of the fresco to access a chapel, destroying the Christ-Judge and half of Paradise. The damage has never been restored. Local peasants of the time saw this as a sign that the bishop didn't like looking Christ in the face.

⏱ 45 min
3

Thirty meters to the left of the cathedral, the Berbie Palace was the former episcopal palace-prison - heretics judged at Albi awaited their sentence in its cellars. Today the building houses the Toulouse-Lautrec Museum, the world's largest collection of works by the artist born here in 1864. The paradox is clear: the place where the Inquisition locked up its suspects now preserves posters from Parisian cabarets. The original 14th-century dungeons, with prisoners' graffiti still visible on the walls, aren't accessible to the public - reserved for researchers with authorization. What you can't see sometimes has more presence than what's shown. Ticket €12.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Lunch in Albi: L'Épicurien for gastronomic (€50-80), or Le Lautrec near the cathedral for something simpler (€35-50). The Vieux Pont over the Tarn, built in 1040 and one of the oldest bridges in France still in service, deserves 15 minutes' walk from the cathedral.

⏱ 90 min

En route to Mirepoix (2h southeast), possible stop at Cordes-sur-Ciel (30 minutes north of Albi) - medieval bastide founded in 1222 by Raymond VII of Toulouse for Cathar refugees driven from the plains. Perched at 287 meters, often wrapped in morning fog that makes it appear suspended in the clouds. Free visit. Night in Mirepoix: Hôtel Le Comptoir Saint Hubert (€90-150) or Maison de Mirepoix (€130-200). The arcaded square of Mirepoix, with its carved half-timbered houses from the 13th-15th centuries, is one of the most beautiful medieval spaces in the Southwest.

⏱ 90 min

Carcassonne

★★★★☆
1

There are places where numbers suffice to tell the story. Eleven months of siege. Two hundred Cathars who refuse to recant. A pyre at the foot of the pog on March 16, 1244. No trace of bodies - the fire was so intense it left only ashes. The castle visible today isn't the Cathar castrum of 1244, demolished after capture and rebuilt by Guy II de Lévis, the French lord installed by the king. But the pog, the elevation, the Pyrenees in the background - all that is intact since the 13th century. The climb from the village takes 20-30 minutes on irregular limestone trail: hiking boots essential. In October, with Ariège fog at the valley bottom and wind on the plateau, the place doesn't need anything added. Ticket €7.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Prat dels Cremats - Field of the Burned in Occitan - is 200 meters below the parking lot, free access. A 1960 stele bears the inscription: 'Als catars, als martirs del pur amor crestian. 16 de març 1244.' No monument, no museum, just a field and a stone. Among the names we know thanks to Bernard de Caux's registers: Cathar Bishop Bertrand Marty, Deacon Raymond Aiguilher, and Esclarmonde de Péreille - daughter of the castle's lord, born after 1224, thus under 20 years old at the time of the pyre. Of 200 dead that day, historians have been able to identify 64 names. The other 136 are anonymous. This is where the journey takes on its full meaning.

⏱ 30 min
3

The archaeological museum of Montségur, in the village below, is small and unpretentious - ceramics, coins, arrowheads from the 1243-1244 siege. The arrowheads, in particular, are what strikes most: found in the exact archaeological layer of the siege, where they fell. We may know what we know, but holding a catalog and seeing the photo of a 13th-century arrowhead still planted in the pog's earth, that recalibrates something. Allow 30 minutes.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Lunch in Montségur village: Auberge du Lys or Le Costumé, Ariège cuisine without frills, €25-40. The aligot and local sausage are worth the detour.

⏱ 60 min

Road to Carcassonne (1h30 via A66). Option: stop at Château de Puivert (42 km northeast of Montségur) - another less-frequented Cathar fortress, with a museum of medieval instruments (unique collection in Europe, lute, vielle, medieval trumpet), 2h visit. Night in Carcassonne. For maximum atmosphere: Hôtel de la Cité within the medieval citadel, €280-500, you have the ramparts to yourself from 7pm when tour buses have left. Otherwise Le Donjon (€130-220) or Hôtel Le Trois Couronnes in the lower town (€90-150). Dinner in the citadel: Comte Roger or Auberge des Lices, €35-55.

⏱ 120 min

Carcassonne

★★★★☆
1

Touristy, but genuine - and far more impressive at night. The medieval citadel of Carcassonne (UNESCO 1997) is Europe's largest fortress: 3 km of ramparts, double walls, 52 towers, 2,500 years of continuous occupation since the Gallic oppidum. Detail few guides mention: the pointed slate roofs of the towers are an invention of Viollet-le-Duc (restoration 1853-1879), not the Middle Ages. The 5th-century Visigothic towers had flat roofs. Viollet-le-Duc restored 'in the style of the period,' which gave him certain liberties. By day, 3 million visitors per year. After 7pm when tour buses have left: the alleys to yourself, the ramparts in silence, the sound of swifts. If you sleep in the citadel, it's the experience of the journey.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Château Comtal, former residence of the Trencavel viscounts, houses the Inquisition Tower - the place where heretics were interrogated, and sometimes tortured, between 1234 and the end of the 14th century. In the central room, reproductions of interrogation registers: questions asked of the accused, answers extracted under torture, sentences. Between 1234 and 1330, the Carcassonne Inquisition judges more than 5,000 people according to Aude Archives registers - 900 death sentences, 2,000 life imprisonments. Raymond-Roger Trencavel, the viscount who surrendered his city in August 1209 after 15 days of siege, died in captivity in his own castle. Probably poisoned. Castle + ramparts ticket €11.50, allow 2h.

⏱ 120 min
3

The Inquisition Museum (7 rue Saint-Jean, 12th-century building in the citadel) presents the complete procedure - anonymous denunciation, arrest, interrogation, torture, sentence - and the instruments that went with it. The most striking case exhibited here: Bernard Délicieux, Franciscan (circa 1260-1320), who publicly contested the Carcassonne Inquisition's methods for twenty years. He was tried, sentenced to life imprisonment in the Inquisition Tower, and died there. A monk who opposes the Inquisition ends up exactly where the Inquisition puts its suspects. Ticket €9, 45 minutes.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Lunch in the medieval citadel: Comte Roger or L'Adresse, regional Aude cuisine, €35-50. Corbières lamb in season, cassoulet from Castelnaudary (40 km away) offered throughout the region - this is the real version, not reconstituted cassoulet.

⏱ 75 min

Before the TGV: two options depending on available time. The Châteaux de Lastours (30 minutes north) - four Cathar fortresses aligned on a ridge, all taken during the Crusade, panorama over the Montagne Noire, ticket €7. Or the Abbey of Saint-Hilaire (20 minutes south) - founded in the 8th century, where monks allegedly invented blanquette de Limoux in 1531, a century before champagne, visit €5 with tasting in neighboring cellars. Carcassonne TGV station 4 km north of center - TGV to Paris in 5h30, or to Toulouse in 1h for return flight.

⏱ 90 min
