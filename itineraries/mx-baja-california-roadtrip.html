⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 7 and 11 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Dry Season (November-April): Peak tourist season with minimal rainfall, comfortable temperatures (20-28°C), and perfect beach weather. Highest prices but ideal conditions for all activities.

Shoulder Season (May, October): Good weather with brief rains, moderate prices, and fewer crowds. Excellent value while maintaining comfortable conditions for most regions.

Hurricane Season (June-September): Hot, humid weather with potential storms on Caribbean and Pacific coasts. Lowest prices and lush landscapes, but weather monitoring essential.

Regional Variations: Northern deserts hot year-round, central highlands mild, coastal areas tropical. Timing depends on specific destinations and preferred activities.

🌍 What to discover in MX?

Vibrant culture with ancient civilizations, incredible cuisine, and diverse landscapes. Experience Maya heritage, beautiful beaches, and authentic Mexican traditions. The country offers cultural immersion with natural beauty and culinary excellence. Mexico provides complete cultural experiences with historical significance and natural wonders.

📋 Important notes

The only linear road trip in the Mexico collection - 1,400 km one-way from Tijuana to Los Cabos, never retracing your route. You'll cross three distinct worlds: the wine-growing northern Baja (Tijuana, Valle de Guadalupe, Ensenada), the central desert with its whales (Guerrero Negro, San Ignacio and its UNESCO rock art), then the Sea of Cortez with its reefs (Loreto, La Paz, Cabo Pulmo). No other itinerary in the collection covers the peninsula. Important logistical note: this is a one-way road trip, and rental car drop-off fees range from $200 to $500 USD depending on the company - verify at booking. Gray whale season in Guerrero Negro runs from January to March. Outside this window, the trip is still absolutely worth it, but Day 6 loses its signature moment.

⚠️ SAFETY NOTE: While major tourist destinations (Riviera Maya, Puerto Vallarta, etc.) are generally safe with good security, some regions have elevated crime risks due to organized crime. Avoid border areas with high violence rates and check government travel advisories for current regional conditions. Stick to tourist zones and use reputable transportation.

Tijuana

Where mariachi meets street art in Mexico's vibrant border metropolis

★★★★☆
📍 Avenida Revolución Tijuana📍 Centro Cultural Tijuana CECUT📍 Mercado El Popo Tijuana📍 Calle Coahuila Tijuana
1

Avenida Revolución is Tijuana's historic spine - souvenir shops, artisan galleries, colorful facades. Stroll south to reach Plaza Santa Cecilia, the city's oldest square, where mariachis perform from late afternoon onward.

⏱ 60 min
2

CECUT - Centro Cultural Tijuana - is the most important cultural institution in northwestern Mexico. Opened in 1982, it welcomes over 1.5 million visitors annually. Its spherical concrete IMAX dome earned it the popular nickname 'La Bola' (The Ball). Inside: the Museo de las Californias, a 995-seat auditorium, and El Cubo, a 1,500 m² international exhibition space. Spot it from the street - it's the giant sphere on Paseo de los Héroes, 2 km from the border.

⏱ 90 min
3

Mercado El Popo is Tijuana's liveliest traditional market. Spices, artisanal cheeses, tropical fruits, medicinal herbs: this is where locals do their shopping. Allow 45 minutes to explore and snack.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Tijuana has established itself in recent years as a Mexican street food capital. Calle Coahuila and the alleys around CECUT concentrate the best cantinas: birria tacos dipped in consommé, shrimp ceviche, mariscos at the counter. Dine early - the best spots close at 9pm.

Valle De Guadalupe

Desert valley where 90% of Mexican wine flows from 150 sun-soaked estates

★★★★★
📍 Adobe Guadalupe Winery Valle de Guadalupe📍 Finca la Carrodilla Valle de Guadalupe📍 Monte Xanic Valle de Guadalupe📍 Fauna Restaurant Valle de Guadalupe
1

Valle de Guadalupe: 150 wineries tucked into a desert valley 90 minutes from Tijuana. The valley produces 90% of Mexican wine - varietals like Nebbiolo and Tempranillo in ancient granite seabed soil. Set out on the Ruta del Vino early in the morning: the fog clings between the vines and vanishes before 10am.

⏱ 60 min
2

Monte Xanic is one of the founding estates of the valley's wine movement. Established in the 1980s, it was among the first cellars to prove Guadalupe's terroir potential internationally. The tasting room overlooks the vineyards - views across the entire valley.

⏱ 90 min
3

Finca la Carrodilla is a pioneering organic wine estate in the region. The raw-stone cellar and biodynamic vineyards make it a standout on the Ruta del Vino. Arrive at opening time to avoid tour groups.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Dine at one of the chef-driven restaurants on the estates. Fauna, at Dominio Bruma, is the most sought-after address: Baja Med cuisine, wood fire, outdoor tables among the cacti. Corazón de Tierra, a few kilometers away, ranked among Latin America's 50 best restaurants. Reservations absolutely essential in high season.

Ensenada

Pacific port where fish tacos reign and craft beer flows freely

★★★☆☆
📍 Malecón Ensenada📍 La Bufadora Ensenada📍 Mercado Negro Ensenada📍 El Mirador Ensenada
1

Ensenada's malecón stretches for several kilometers along the bay. This is where the fish taco stands cluster - a local specialty: fried fish or shrimp in tempura batter, crema, salsa verde, crisp cabbage in a corn tortilla. Show up at the port's fish market early in the morning to watch fishermen unload their catch.

⏱ 75 min
2

La Bufadora is a natural marine geyser carved into the cliff face of the Punta Banda peninsula, 20 km south of Ensenada. Water rushes into an underwater cavity and erupts up to 27 meters high at high tide. It's one of the most powerful marine geysers in the Northern Hemisphere. The road to reach it hugs the bay - worth the drive in itself.

⏱ 75 min
3

Climb up to the El Mirador viewpoint, north of town. 180° view over the bay and port - the best vantage point in Ensenada. Perfect in late afternoon when the light is low.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Ensenada has built a reputation as Baja California's craft beer capital. Several artisanal breweries have opened downtown - the scene is young, experimental, and prices remain accessible. Take advantage of the evening to linger before hitting the road tomorrow.

San Quintin

Where oyster beds meet the edge of Baja's great central desert

★★★★☆
📍 Bahía de San Quintín Baja California📍 Dunes San Quintín📍 Valle de San Quintín📍 Zona Militar San Quintín
1

Between Ensenada and San Quintín, the road changes character dramatically. The vineyard carpet gives way to agricultural plains, then desert. Candelabra cacti gradually appear. Take a break in the expanses of sand and coastal dunes north of San Quintín - the contrast with the Pacific is striking.

⏱ 60 min

The bay of San Quintín is a shallow lagoon renowned for its oyster farming. Local oystermen have worked here for decades. As you approach the water's edge, you'll see oyster beds aligned in the bay. Buy directly on-site - San Quintín's Pacific oysters have a pronounced briny taste.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

San Quintín is a transition stop - a quiet day before the major desert crossing. Take advantage of it to cruise the Pacific coast by car at sunset: the wild beaches north of town are deserted and wind-beaten. A few hours to breathe before tomorrow's 5-hour drive.

Stop 5

Guerrero Negro

Gateway to gray whale lagoons and salt ponds visible from space

★★★★★
📍 Laguna Ojo de Liebre Guerrero Negro📍 Salines Exportadora de Sal Guerrero Negro📍 Guerrero Negro Baja California
1

The San Quintín → Guerrero Negro drive (about 5 hours) is the road trip's most spectacular leg. Highway 1 cuts straight through the central desert: 15-meter cardon cacti, ochre dust devils, absolute silence. Pass the 28th parallel that marks the border between Baja California and Baja California Sur - there's a monumental eagle sculpture at the roadside.

⏱ 30 min
2

Upon arrival, take a look at the Guerrero Negro salt flats. Operated by Exportadora de Sal (a Mexican-Japanese Mitsubishi joint venture), they produce about 9 million tons of salt annually. The evaporation ponds are so vast they're visible from space. The town takes its name from an American whaling ship - the Black Warrior - that ran aground here in 1858.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

If you arrive in whale season (January-March), inquire immediately upon arrival to book a lancha outing for tomorrow morning on Laguna Ojo de Liebre. It's the largest of three breeding lagoons for Eastern Pacific gray whales - up to 2,000 individuals converge here each winter to give birth. Pangas typically depart at 8am or 11am for a 3-hour excursion.

San Ignacio

Palm oasis harboring baroque missions and ancient rock art in the sierra

★★☆☆☆
📍 Laguna Ojo de Liebre observation baleines Guerrero Negro📍 Salines Guerrero Negro📍 San Ignacio Baja California Sur
1

Morning on Laguna Ojo de Liebre - in season, gray whales sometimes come right up to brush against the boats. Females arrive from Alaska and Siberia after a 10,000 km journey to give birth in these warm, shallow waters. Calves weigh about 700 kg at birth. Off-season (April-December), the lagoon boat outing is replaced by a visit to the salt works and the berrendo peninsular reserve, a pronghorn antelope endemic to the peninsula.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

In the afternoon, take the road toward San Ignacio (about 2 hours). After hours of flat desert, the San Ignacio oasis appears like a scenic error: 80,000 date palms, fig trees, orange trees massed in a depression of the peninsula. Settle onto a terrace on the central plaza to decompress after the day.

San Ignacio

Palm oasis harboring baroque missions and ancient rock art in the sierra

★★☆☆☆
📍 Mission San Ignacio Kadakaamán📍 Sierra San Francisco peintures rupestres📍 Plaza San Ignacio Baja California
1

The Jesuit mission of San Ignacio Kadakaamán was founded in 1728. It's one of the best-preserved in all of Baja California - its black volcanic stone facade and baroque bell tower stand on the palm-shaded plaza. It served as a base for missionaries reaching the Cochimí communities in the surrounding mountains. Step inside: the acoustics are remarkable and the original ornaments are still in place.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Sierra de San Francisco harbors one of the world's most important rock painting ensembles, listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1993. These frescoes, dated between 100 BCE and 1300 CE, depict humans and animals in red, black, white, and yellow on rock walls several meters high. Some paintings are believed to be between 7,000 and 9,000 years old based on carbon dating. To access the Cueva del Raton site (the most accessible), go to the INAH office to the left of the mission - they arrange permits and mandatory local guides. Count on a 4x4, 2 hours on a dirt track, 1 hour on foot.

⏱ 240 min

Activities

In the evening, join the locals on the plaza of San Ignacio. It's one of the few plazas on the peninsula still bustling in the evening - a few restaurants around the central kiosk, kids on bikes. The contrast with the hours of desert crossed is total.

Loreto

Where California's first mission faces turquoise islands across the gulf

★★☆☆☆
📍 Mission Nuestra Señora de Loreto📍 Malecón Loreto Baja California📍 Museo de las Misiones Loreto📍 Mission San Javier Loreto
1

The San Ignacio → Loreto road swings to the peninsula's east flank. You leave the Pacific for the Sea of Cortez - colors shift: the water turns from gray-green to deep turquoise, the Sierra de la Giganta mountains plunge straight into the sea. Stop at the overlooks above the coast for the final 40 kilometers.

⏱ 30 min
2

The mission of Nuestra Señora de Loreto Conchó, founded on October 25, 1697 by Jesuit father Juan María de Salvatierra, is the first permanent mission established in the Californias - the 'mother of all missions' according to the consecrated expression. It served as the starting point for a network of 27 missions that would extend all the way to Alta California. Today, the original church still stands at the heart of the town. Inside: the Museo de las Misiones, with religious objects from the 16th and 17th centuries.

⏱ 75 min
3

Loreto's malecón is one of the peninsula's most tranquil waterfronts. A few restaurants, fishing boats, the national park islands visible a few kilometers out. Settle in late afternoon to watch the pelicans return.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Optional excursion: the San Javier mission, 38 km into the mountains, is considered the most beautiful mission in Baja California. Built between 1699 and 1758, its carved stone baroque facade is in exceptionally preserved condition. Count on 1 hour on a dirt track through the sierra to reach it.

Bahía de Loreto National Park protects 790 km² of the Sea of Cortez, including five desert islands. Isla Coronado, 45 minutes by boat, is the most visited: white sand beach, water at 24°C in season, Humboldt penguins on the rocks. Buoys mark the snorkeling zone - you'll encounter groupers, parrotfish and, with a bit of luck, dolphins approaching the boat.

⏱ 240 min

Activities

California sea lions frequent the rocks of Isla Coronado. Boat trips allow close approaches - some operators offer swimming with them in marked zones. Late in the day, return to the malecón for a plate of chocolate clams, the clam endemic to Loreto waters.

La Paz

Baja capital opening doors to sea lion colonies and UNESCO archipelagos

★★☆☆☆
📍 Malecón La Paz Baja California📍 Mercado Municipal La Paz📍 El Tecolote La Paz📍 Playa Balandra La Paz
1

The Loreto → La Paz road descends southward along the Sea of Cortez. Stop at Bahía Concepción, a turquoise body of water enclosed between two chains of arid hills - beach campgrounds, flat water, zero infrastructure. It's one of the most photogenic bays on the peninsula.

⏱ 60 min
2

La Paz's malecón stretches 5 km along the bay. At sunset, the whole town gathers here. Look for the bronze dove sculpture commemorating the rescue of Isla Espíritu Santo in the 1990s - when Mexican and American activists bought the island to prevent casino construction. The bay turns orange in minutes - take your time.

⏱ 75 min
3

La Paz's municipal market is smaller than Tijuana's but more authentic. Catch of the day, giant gulf shrimp, aromatic herbs. Taste the machaca (shredded dried beef) at one of the adjoining fondas.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

If you have energy after the drive, push on to Playa Balandra (25 km from La Paz) for a first glimpse of the gulf waters. This enclosed bay is protected - the water is flat, transparent, Caribbean-colored. Locals come here to swim on weekends. Regulated entry, limited visitor numbers.

La Paz

Baja capital opening doors to sea lion colonies and UNESCO archipelagos

★★☆☆☆
📍 Isla Espíritu Santo La Paz📍 Los Islotes sea lions La Paz📍 Archipiélago Espíritu Santo Parque Nacional
1

Isla Espíritu Santo is a national park and UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2005. The island is formed of brick-red volcanic cliffs dropping into turquoise water - no permanent infrastructure, no inhabitants. Boats leave from La Paz port for a 90-minute crossing. First stop: Los Islotes, rocky islets north of the archipelago where the gulf's largest California sea lion colony concentrates - up to 800 individuals. Pups approach divers out of curiosity.

⏱ 300 min

Activities

After Los Islotes, the pangas head up toward the island beaches. La Ensenada Grande, on adjacent Isla Partida, was voted one of the world's 12 most beautiful beaches by The Travel magazine. Snorkeling on the reef, kayaking in the coves, beach lunch with ceviche prepared on board. This is the trip's highlight - don't go in bad weather.

Stop 12

Cabo Pulmo

Tiny village safeguarding the gulf's only living hard coral reef

★★☆☆☆
📍 Cabo Pulmo National Marine Park📍 Snorkeling Cabo Pulmo📍 Playa Los Arbolitos Cabo Pulmo
1

Cabo Pulmo is a village of 300 inhabitants on the peninsula's east coast. Its coral reef, over 20,000 years old, is the only living hard coral reef in the entire Gulf of California - a unique ecosystem listed by UNESCO. In the 1990s, the village's fishing families themselves campaigned for a total ban on commercial fishing. Result: in 10 years, biomass increased 460%, a world record cited by the National Geographic Society. More than 800 marine species now inhabit the park's 7,000 hectares.

⏱ 60 min
2

Snorkeling from Los Arbolitos beach requires no boat: wade into the water, swim 50 meters, and you're on the reef. Schools of jacks dense as clouds, groupers, resting turtles, foraging parrotfish. Waters are clear down to 30 meters depth.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

Boat excursions leave the village several times daily for the park's best dive spots: El Bajo, Los Morros, El Cantil. Certified local guides, modest rates, small groups. Book at the Cabo Pulmo Divers office on the main beach.

San Jose Del Cabo

Colonial heart of Los Cabos where art galleries line cobbled streets

★★☆☆☆
📍 Centro histórico San José del Cabo📍 Art Walk San José del Cabo📍 Galería San José del Cabo📍 Cabo San Lucas Arco
1

San José del Cabo has an authentic colonial center - no permanent party, no noise: cobbled lanes, an 18th-century iglesia, contemporary Mexican art galleries in converted colonial houses. Thursday evenings, the 'Art Walk' brings together about twenty galleries open simultaneously from 6pm to 9pm (October to June). It's the calm, cultured version of Los Cabos.

⏱ 120 min
2

30 km from San José, Cabo San Lucas is the other end of the spectrum: packed marina, bustling beaches, seaside bars. The Arco - two rocks sculpted by erosion at the junction of the Pacific and Gulf of California - is visited by panga from the marina. Playa del Amor, accessible only by boat, lies just behind. The Pacific water is cold, the gulf water warm - you feel the difference 10 meters apart.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Last dinner in Los Cabos: the dining scene is solid - mariscos tacos at the San José marina, chef-driven restaurants on the water in Cabo San Lucas. Catch the sunset from El Médano, Cabo's main beach - it's the only swimmable beach at the point, the others are dangerous (Pacific currents).
