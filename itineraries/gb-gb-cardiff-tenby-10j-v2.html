Stop 1

Cardiff

Where Roman walls meet Victorian Gothic fantasy in Wales' capital castle

★★★★☆
📍 Cardiff Castle Cardiff📍 Cardiff Victorian Arcades📍 Cardiff Market Hall📍 Principality Stadium Cardiff📍 Cardiff Bay Mermaid Quay📍 Wales Millennium Centre Cardiff📍 Roald Dahl Plass Cardiff
1

Cardiff Castle packs 2,000 years of history within its walls. The Romans established their first fort here around AD 55, then the Normans built a stone keep around 1091—its artificial motte, rising over 10 metres, still dominates the grounds. But what will truly take your breath away is the interior: in the 19th century, the 3rd Marquess of Bute—then reputed to be the richest man in the world—commissioned architect William Burges to transform the castle. The result: neo-Gothic apartments of absolute extravagance, smothered in mosaics, gilded woodwork and stained glass. Allow at least 90 minutes to explore the apartments, the Norman keep and the WWII air-raid shelters hidden in the walls.

⏱ 90 min
2

Cardiff's historic centre is threaded with Victorian and Edwardian covered arcades—the Royal Arcade, opened in 1858, is one of the oldest in the UK. Inside you'll find independent shops, cafés and record stores, sheltered from Welsh rain. The Morgan Arcade and Castle Arcade are also worth exploring for their cast-iron structures and suspended bow windows. Take your time wandering: this is where Cardiff still feels like Cardiff, before the standardised pedestrian precincts took over.

⏱ 60 min
3

Llandaff Cathedral, 3 km northwest of the centre, deserves a detour if you have time. Founded in the 6th century, rebuilt after the 1941 bombing, it houses an Epstein sculpture—Christ in Majesty—suspended above the nave. The place is quiet and often deserted: a welcome counterpoint to the bustle of the city centre.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The Principality Stadium—74,500 seats, retractable roof in 20 minutes, the UK's first stadium with a moveable roof—offers guided tours on non-match days. You'll visit the changing rooms, the players' tunnel and walk right up to the pitch edge. Fun fact: the pitch is grown outdoors then rolled inside for matches. Opened in June 1999, it hosted the Rugby World Cup in its first year, the 2017 Champions League final (Real Madrid v Juventus), and concerts by Beyoncé, Ed Sheeran and Oasis. Book your tour in advance.

⏱ 90 min

Cardiff Bay is worth exploring whenever the sun makes an appearance. The former coal docks—once the world's largest coal-exporting port—have been transformed into a 200-hectare freshwater lake thanks to a barrage completed in 1999. Stroll along Mermaid Quay, peek at the Senedd (the Welsh parliament, a glass building by Richard Rogers), then walk the barrage to Penarth—2 km with sea views. Doctor Who fans will recognise Roald Dahl Plass: it was the fictional entrance to the Torchwood hub.

⏱ 90 min

Cardiff

Where Roman walls meet Victorian Gothic fantasy in Wales' capital castle

★★★★☆
📍 Nash Point Lighthouse Llantwit Major📍 Dunraven Bay Southerndown📍 Nash Point Beach Vale of Glamorgan📍 Glamorgan Heritage Coast Centre📍 St Fagans National Museum of History Cardiff
1

The Glamorgan Heritage Coast is one of South Wales' most spectacular stretches—14 miles of limestone cliffs between Aberthaw and Newton, often deserted on weekdays. Nash Point is the jewel: a promontory of striated cliffs with geological layers visible to the naked eye, scattered with rock pools and ammonite fossils. Descend to the rocky beach at low tide to hunt for your own specimens—it's a serious activity here, not a vague pastime. The cliffs are unstable in places; stick to marked paths.

⏱ 75 min
2

Nash Point Lighthouse was lit in 1832, in response to the wreck of the steamer The Frolic the previous year: 78 passengers drowned on that sandbank. Designed by James Walker, chief engineer of Trinity House, it operated with a keeper until 1998—Wales' last manned lighthouse, the UK's second-last. The large square fog house with its horns on the roof still stands beside the lighthouse. Local legend claims that on stormy nights in the 19th century, smugglers would tie lanterns to their sheep at the cliff edge to fool mariners into thinking they were navigating safe waters.

⏱ 45 min
3

Dunraven Bay, 7 km northwest of Nash Point, offers another face of the coast: a wide beach of pebbles and sand at the foot of a cliff and a vanished castle. The Glamorgan Heritage Coast Centre is based here—free, useful for understanding the area's geology and wildlife. The beach is popular with surfers and geologists: you'll find Triassic oyster fossils called 'Devil's toenails'.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The coastal path between Dunraven Bay and Nash Point (about 7 km return) is one of the finest walks on the Glamorgan coast. The path follows a grassy limestone plateau with constant views over the Bristol Channel and, on clear days, as far as the Devon coast. Bring waterproof shoes—the path can be muddy after rain—and a waterproof jacket, even in summer.

⏱ 150 min

If the coast has whetted your appetite for culture, St Fagans National Museum of History, 6 km west of Cardiff, is one of the UK's best open-air museums—and it's free. Over 40 historic buildings have been dismantled and reconstructed on this 40-hectare site, from 15th-century Welsh cottages to industrial revolution workers' houses. Worth seeing if you have a free evening.

⏱ 120 min

Stop 3

Brecon

A Georgian market town cradled between soaring peaks and winding valleys

★★★★☆
📍 Pen y Fan Brecon Beacons📍 Pont ar Daf car park Brecon Beacons📍 Cribyn Brecon Beacons📍 Brecon town centre📍 Brecknock Museum and Art Gallery Brecon
1

Pen y Fan is the highest peak in southern Britain—886 metres. From its flat ridge, on a clear day, the view sweeps across the entire Gower Peninsula and the Bristol Channel. Its Welsh name literally means 'the summit of the peak', and it was once known as Cadair Arthur—Arthur's Chair. The path from the Pont ar Daf car park (on the A470) is the most direct: 3.2 km one way, 450 metres of ascent. It's the 'motorway' that regulars love to mock, but the summit view is worth any crowd. Arrive before 9am in summer if you want the ridge to yourself.

⏱ 180 min
2

If you tackle the Pen y Fan Horseshoe, the summit of Cribyn (795 m) comes naturally into the itinerary: it overlooks Pen y Fan from its eastern ridge and offers a different angle—more open towards the Neuadd Reservoir and the central hills. The ridge between the two summits is narrow in places; beware of gusts. Bring waterproofs and proper boots whatever the forecast in Brecon below—conditions change fast at 800 metres.

⏱ 90 min
3

Brecon is a pleasant Georgian market town, set in the Usk Valley between the Brecon Beacons and the Black Mountains. Wander around the 11th-century cathedral and the Monmouth & Brecon Canal—one of Wales' best-preserved canals, where you can hire a boat for the day.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

For a shorter, quieter ascent, the Cwm Gwdi car park (north side) offers a more intimate approach to Pen y Fan via the Cefn Cwm Llwch ridge: fewer people, spectacular views over the glacial valley and a cirque lake. The loop is about 8.5 km with 545 m of ascent—allow 3 to 4 hours.

⏱ 210 min

The Brecon Mountain Railway runs from Merthyr Tydfil up to Pontsticill Reservoir across the moorland—a 7 km journey by steam train with views of Pen y Fan. Ideal if your legs are tired after the morning's hike. Check the timetable before you go.

⏱ 90 min

The Blaen-y-Glyn waterfalls, in Talybont Forest, are among the least visited in the Brecon Beacons—which is precisely their charm. A series of short falls cascade through a wooded ravine, reached in 20 minutes from the forest car park. Perfect for a late afternoon after the morning's effort.

⏱ 75 min

Brecon

A Georgian market town cradled between soaring peaks and winding valleys

★★★★☆
📍 Sgwd yr Eira waterfall Ystradfellte📍 Four Waterfalls Walk Brecon Beacons📍 Pontneddfechan waterfall walk📍 Ystradfellte village📍 Henrhyd Falls Coelbren
1

Waterfall Country, between Pontneddfechan and Ystradfellte, packs more waterfalls per square kilometre than anywhere else in Britain. The Mellte and Hepste rivers plunge through wooded gorges carved into red sandstone, creating a succession of falls each with its own character. Pontneddfechan is the best entry point for walkers coming from the south—the Elidir Trail starts here, and the toilets and café at the Waterfall Centre are in the village.

⏱ 60 min
2

Sgwd yr Eira—'the fall of snow' in Welsh—is the undisputed star of the Four Waterfalls Walk. Nine metres wide, it drops in an almost horizontal curtain over the River Hepste, and a narrow path cut into the rock lets you walk literally behind the veil of water. This path was used by shepherds to get their flocks across to the other side. Behind the fall, the noise is deafening, the ground slick and slippery, the air cold and saturated with droplets—you see daylight filtered through the water curtain like through frosted glass. Prepare to get drenched even in fine weather, put your phone in a waterproof pocket before approaching, and wear grip-soled shoes. The descent from the main path involves about 170 steep steps.

⏱ 90 min
3

Henrhyd Falls, about fifteen kilometres further west near Coelbren, is the tallest waterfall in South Wales—27 metres. It's less visited than the Four Waterfalls and worth the detour if you have time. Access from the National Trust car park takes 20 minutes on foot.

⏱ 60 min
4

The village of Ystradfellte is the ideal starting point for exploring the eastern part of Waterfall Country. Tiny—a handful of farms and an inn—but well positioned between Sgwd Clun-Gwyn, Sgwd Isaf Clun-Gwyn and Sgwd yr Eira. The Cwm Porth car park (2 km south) gives access to the Four Waterfalls loop.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The Four Waterfalls Walk is a 7 km loop linking Sgwd Clun-Gwyn, Sgwd Isaf Clun-Gwyn, Sgwd y Pannwr and Sgwd yr Eira. The route is well marked but the descents to the waterfalls are steep, muddy and slippery—proper walking boots are essential. Allow between 2h30 and 4h depending on your pace. The waterfalls are most impressive in spring and autumn after rain; in summer, the flow can be low.

⏱ 180 min

The Elidir Trail loop from Pontneddfechan combines the path along the River Neath with the crossing of the four waterfalls—a more ambitious version (12 km) that encounters fewer people and offers viewpoints from the ridges. This loop starts and ends at the Waterfall Centre in Pontneddfechan, where a café is available.

⏱ 210 min

Mumbles

Where oyster dredgers once hauled millions and a tramway ran to the sea

★★☆☆☆
📍 Llyn y Fan Fach Llanddeusant📍 Picws Du Black Mountain Carmarthenshire📍 Llyn y Fan Fawr Brecon Beacons📍 Bannau Sir Gar ridge
1

Llyn y Fan Fach is a 10-hectare glacial lake set at 510 metres altitude at the foot of the Carmarthen Fans escarpment—the western flanks of the Brecon Beacons. Allow 1h30 drive from Mumbles to get there (via Ammanford), but the site is so little visited and so wild that the journey is more than justified. It's rarely crowded, utterly wild and wrapped in legend: according to Welsh tradition, a fairy would emerge from it to seduce shepherds. One of them married her on condition he would never strike her three times. He broke his promise, she returned to the lake taking all the cattle with her—but her sons became the Physicians of Myddfai, the first documented lineage of herbalist-physicians in Wales, in the 12th century. You reach the lake on foot from the Llanddeusant car park in 40 minutes.

⏱ 90 min
2

Picws Du (749 m) is the westernmost summit of the Carmarthen Fans ridge. Climb from the lake to reach the knife-edge: on one side the void of the glacial cirque, on the other the grassy steppe descending towards Carmarthenshire. The panorama over Bannau Sir Gâr—the official name of this range of summits since the national park was renamed in 2023—and over the Welsh plain is one of the widest in South Wales.

⏱ 90 min
3

Llyn y Fan Fawr, the lake's big brother, lies 3 km to the east along the ridge. If you have the energy, continue to this second glacial lake from the summit of Picws Du—the complete loop from Llanddeusant car park is about 13 km and remains fairly isolated, with few walkers.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Carmarthenshire Fans loop from Llanddeusant car park (13 km route) makes a complete circuit of Llyn y Fan Fach, climbs Picws Du, traverses the ridge to Fan Brycheiniog and descends. It's one of the most solitary and wildest walks in the Brecon Beacons—bring map and compass, mobile signal is non-existent. About 500 m of ascent.

⏱ 270 min

Mumbles

Where oyster dredgers once hauled millions and a tramway ran to the sea

★★☆☆☆
📍 Oystermouth Castle Mumbles📍 Mumbles Pier Swansea Bay📍 Mumbles Lighthouse📍 Langland Bay Swansea📍 Caswell Bay Gower📍 Bracelet Bay Mumbles
1

Mumbles is the kind of place you've never heard of but never want to leave. A seaside village perched at the tip of Swansea Bay, it owes its strange name to the French 'mamelles'—a reference to the two rocky islets at the end of the promontory. This village was once one of the UK's first oyster ports: in 1871, 16 million oysters a year were landed here, shipped by train to London. Disease in the 1920s killed the industry, but the name Oystermouth (the village's old name) remains everywhere. Start with Oystermouth Castle, perched on a hill overlooking the bay—built in the 12th century by the Normans, it's under ongoing restoration.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Mumbles promenade, between Oyster Wharf and Bracelet Bay, is the village's beating heart. You'll encounter families, cyclists, retirees with dogs. Mumbles Pier opened in 1898 as the terminus of an electric tramway—the world's first fare-paying railway transport network, opened in 1807 with horse traction, converted to steam in 1877 then electrified in 1929 before closing in 1960. Don't miss the pier mouth and the view of Mumbles lighthouse, built in 1793 on the outer islet.

⏱ 75 min
3

Langland Bay is the first beach west of Mumbles, reached on foot in 30 minutes via the coastal path. Protected by limestone cliffs, it's a sandy beach with restored Victorian bathing huts—a 1920s postcard setting. Surfing is possible here; Caswell Bay, the next bay along (another 20 min walk), is better known for its waves.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

The coastal path between Mumbles and Caswell Bay is a 6 km return walk along limestone cliffs with sweeping views over Swansea Bay, the Mumbles islands and the open sea. The path passes Limeslade Bay and Bracelet Bay before climbing the cliffs to Langland and Caswell. Bring grip shoes—some sections are rocky.

⏱ 150 min

In the evening, settle on the Mumbles seafront to watch the sunset over Swansea Bay. The orange light on the two islets with the white lighthouse is a scene regularly photographed from the end of the pier.

⏱ 45 min

Stop 7

Rhossili

Five miles of Atlantic sand bookended by a dragon's spine and a timber ghost

★★☆☆☆
📍 Three Cliffs Bay Gower📍 Pennard Castle Gower📍 Pennard Burrows dunes Gower📍 Oxwich Bay Gower📍 Port Eynon Gower
1

Three Cliffs Bay is often cited as the most beautiful beach in Wales. Three limestone arches plunge straight into the sea at the end of a long sandy beach crossed by an estuary you must ford at low tide—which adds to the end-of-the-world feel. No car access to the beach: allow 25 minutes on foot from the Southgate or Pennard car park. The effort is amply rewarded.

⏱ 90 min
2

On the ridge overlooking Three Cliffs Bay, the ruins of Pennard Castle emerge from the dunes. Built by the Normans in the 12th century to control the coastline, it has been gradually invaded by sand since the 14th century—local legend says it was buried as divine punishment. Access from the coastal path is immediate and the view from the ruined walls over the bay is striking.

⏱ 45 min
3

On the way to Rhossili, Oxwich Bay deserves a stop: a wide 3 km beach facing south, sheltered by a pine forest and classified as a National Nature Reserve. The Oxwich Burrows dunes behind the beach are one of Gower's largest dune systems, home to rare plant species.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The coastal loop from Southgate around Three Cliffs Bay and Pennard Burrows is one of the best walks in Gower: 8 km, little elevation, scenery that changes every ten minutes between cliffs, dunes, estuaries and beaches. Pass behind Pennard Castle for the plunging view over Three Cliffs from the rear.

⏱ 150 min

Tenby

Medieval ramparts embrace pastel houses tumbling down to a harbour unchanged for centuries

★★★★☆
📍 Rhossili Bay beach Gower📍 Worms Head Rhossili📍 Helvetia shipwreck Rhossili📍 Rhossili Down National Trust📍 Rhossili visitor centre
1

Rhossili Bay is a 5 km Atlantic sand beach, closed to the north by the Llangennith dunes and to the south by the Worm's Head promontory. From the cliffs of Rhossili village, the view across the entire bay is one of the most photographed in Wales. The National Trust car park at the top of the cliff is often full at weekends in summer—arrive before 10am or after 5pm.

⏱ 60 min
2

Worm's Head is a marshy island shaped like a dragon—worm comes from the Old Norse ormr, serpent or dragon—connected to the mainland by a rocky causeway accessible only 2h30 either side of low tide. Several people have been trapped by the tide trying to return on foot, including the poet Dylan Thomas himself, stranded there for a whole night. The National Coastwatch Institute office, at the start of the causeway, displays tide times: check them without fail before crossing. Grey seals regularly haul out on the rocks.

⏱ 120 min
3

The Helvetia wreck is one of Gower's most famous. This Norwegian three-master was carrying 500 tonnes of timber from Canada to Swansea when a storm forced it to take refuge in Rhossili Bay on 1 November 1887. The anchors didn't hold, the wind shifted, the ship ran aground on the sand—the entire crew survived. The wooden ribs of the hull, buried in the sand for over 130 years, appear and disappear depending on storms and tides. Visible at low tide, about 30 minutes' walk from the cliff path.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The walk to the end of Worm's Head (about 1.5 km from the causeway) gives access to a natural arch, a blowhole and seabird colonies. Allow a minimum of 2h30 on the island to have time to return before the tide cuts off the passage. Don't attempt the crossing if the sea is rough.

⏱ 180 min

Sunset at Rhossili is renowned throughout the Gower Peninsula. From the clifftop above the beach, you watch the sun descend directly into the sea with Worm's Head in the foreground. Bring a picnic and wait—it's worth the patience.

⏱ 60 min

Tenby

Medieval ramparts embrace pastel houses tumbling down to a harbour unchanged for centuries

★★★★☆
📍 Tenby Five Arches medieval walls📍 Tenby Harbour Pembrokeshire📍 Castle Hill Tenby📍 St Mary's Church Tenby📍 Tudor Merchant's House Tenby📍 Tenby North Beach📍 Caldey Island ferry Tenby
1

Tenby is a medieval town whose walls are among the best preserved in Britain—Grade I listed, they form an almost complete triangle around the old quarter. The first stones date back to the 13th century, under the Earls of Pembroke. In 1260, the Welsh prince Llewelyn ap Gruffydd sacked the town: the new walls, higher and better reinforced, date from this reconstruction. Jasper Tudor—uncle of the future Henry VII—had them repaired and raised in 1457. Enter through the Five Arches, the D-shaped barbican gate still standing on the east side, and lose yourself in the cobbled lanes sloping down to the harbour.

⏱ 90 min
2

St Mary's Church in Tenby is one of the largest medieval churches in Wales. Inside, look for the memorial to Robert Recorde—a Tenby native, this 16th-century mathematician invented the '=' sign. Another memorial is dedicated to Mayor Thomas White, who, according to local tradition, helped Jasper Tudor and the young Henry Tudor flee the town through underground tunnels in 1471 to reach the harbour and sail to Brittany—a journey that preceded Henry's conquest of the English throne in 1485.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Tudor Merchant's House, at the bottom of Quay Hill, is a 15th-century house run by the National Trust. A wealthy Tenby merchant's Tudor-era dwelling is carefully recreated—period furniture, tapestries and fragments of original frescoes. Allow 30 minutes.

⏱ 30 min
4

Castle Hill, at the tip of the promontory, offers the classic Tenby panorama: the pastel houses of the harbour to the left, North Beach to the right, and offshore the silhouette of St Catherine's Island with its Victorian fort. This island played the role of a high-security prison in Sherlock series 4. The Tenby Museum (Wales' oldest independent museum, opened in 1878) is housed in the ruins of the medieval castle at the summit.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The boat crossing to Caldey Island takes 20 minutes from Tenby harbour. This private island has been inhabited by Cistercian monks since 1929, who produce their own chocolate and perfumes made from herbs. Access is seasonal (generally April to October). Outside ferry hours, tour Tenby's three main beaches (North, South and Castle Beach) by following the clifftop path.

⏱ 120 min

★★★★☆
📍 Bosherston Lily Ponds Pembrokeshire📍 Broad Haven South beach Pembrokeshire📍 Barafundle Bay Stackpole📍 Stackpole Quay National Trust📍 St Govan's Chapel Pembrokeshire📍 Green Bridge of Wales Stack Rocks
1

Bosherston Lily Ponds are a network of three artificial lake arms created in the 18th century by the Cawdor family by damming an estuary—an idea born after a visitor noticed how a high tide transformed the valley into a romantic lake. Now a National Nature Reserve managed by the National Trust, the ponds are covered with white water lilies in June and July. Over 20 species of dragonfly have been recorded here, otters live here, and herons constantly perch on the jetties. The circular walk around the ponds (3 km) is flat and accessible to all.

⏱ 75 min
2

Broad Haven South, at the end of the ponds, is an immense beach—golden sand, dunes, and in the centre a limestone monolith isolated from the sea called Church Rock, rising like a cathedral. It's a serious bodyboarding beach in swell conditions; in calm weather, the water is transparent and shallow.

⏱ 60 min
3

Barafundle Bay is regularly ranked among Europe's most beautiful beaches. Nestled between cliffs and accessible only on foot (15 minutes from Stackpole Quay), it never suffers from the overcrowding of beaches with adjacent parking. Golden sand, turquoise water in fine weather, no facilities on the beach itself—bring your picnic. The stone arch with the National Trust sign above the beach is one of Pembrokeshire's most recognisable images.

⏱ 90 min
4

At the end of the day, push on to St Govan's Chapel: a tiny 13th-century chapel (possibly older) wedged in a vertical fissure in the coastal cliffs at the bottom of a 52-step staircase. A hermit is said to have lived there, according to legend surrounded by rocks that closed in to protect him from his enemies. Check before leaving if the area is open—it's sometimes closed for military exercises (Castlemartin firing range).

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The Bosherston-Broad Haven South-Barafundle Bay coastal loop is the best circuit for this day: 10 km from the Stackpole Quay or Bosherston car park, alternating between paths around the ponds and clifftop coastal path. The section between Broad Haven South and Barafundle passes Stackpole Head, a limestone promontory with rock stacks in the sea. Allow 3 hours. In June-July, the lily pads on the ponds are in flower—don't miss this moment.
