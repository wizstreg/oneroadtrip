Polignano A Mare

Where whitewashed alleys cling to limestone cliffs above the Adriatic

★★★★☆
1

First stop 22 km south of Bari: Mola di Bari, a fishing port most visitors drive straight through—and all the better for those who pause. The Castello Angioino, built in 1279 on the orders of Charles I of Anjou to defend the new town from pirates, plants its star-shaped polygonal silhouette right on the harbour. Entry to the courtyard is free; you can climb up and enjoy the view over the marina. Behind the castle, spot the Chiesa Matrice San Nicola, which blends Apulian Romanesque and Renaissance styles in unabashedly eclectic fashion.

⏱ 60 min
2

And then Polignano a Mare appears, perched atop its limestone cliffs. First reflex: the Ponte Borbonico, a 19th-century stone bridge spanning the cove. Vertiginous view down onto Lama Monachile, the pebble beach trapped between two rock faces—the postcard shot of Puglia, the one you see all over Instagram. Fair warning: the beach measures just a few dozen metres, bracing but packed from noon onwards. The town itself conceals an old quarter of twisting lanes, with poems painted on the walls by a local artist and a three-metre bronze statue dedicated to Domenico Modugno, the town's native son who wrote Volare in 1958.

⏱ 90 min
3

Beneath the town opens the Grotta Palazzese, one of 80 natural cavities riddling the coastline, transformed in 1730 by Duke Leto into a ballroom modelled on Versailles. Today it's a restaurant suspended 24 metres above the waves—you needn't dine there (prices match the setting), but it's worth a look from the sea. The best approach is a boat or kayak excursion among the coastal caves.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Excursion by gozzo (the traditional local boat) or kayak from Polignano port, to see the town from the sea and enter the coastal caves—the Grotta Azzurra Monachile glows electric blue when light enters from below. Reckon on around 1h30. Since 2010 the cliffs have hosted a leg of the Red Bull Cliff Diving World Series (dives from over 30 metres).

⏱ 90 min

Evening passeggiata through Polignano's historic centre. The lanes light up, terraces set out their tables, you grab a wood-fired focaccia barese. Taste the mortadella di Martina Franca (PDO) and a glass of Primitivo di Manduria—local, robust, perfect after a day on the road.

⏱ 90 min

Putignano

Keeper of Europe's most ancient carnival, dancing since 1394

★★★★☆
1

Conversano takes a little effort: 7 km inland, on a plateau at 219 metres, locals call it the Athens of Puglia. The Castello Acquaviva dominates Piazza della Conciliazione—built around 1054 by the Normans on the megalithic ruins of ancient Norba, enlarged from the 15th century by the Acquaviva d'Aragona counts who occupied it until 1806. The Torre Maestra, around 25 metres high, is the sole surviving Norman tower. Inside, the picture gallery displays the cycle La Gerusalemme liberata by the Neapolitan Paolo Finoglio (17th century), directly commissioned by Giangirolamo II Acquaviva to glorify his family.

⏱ 75 min
2

Putignano on its hill at 372 metres, famous for what is officially Italy's oldest carnival: the 632nd edition in 2026, first records from 1394. The origin? Knights of Malta transfer the relics of Saint Stephen from coastal Monopoli inland to escape the Saracens. Peasants busy grafting their vines (the propaggine) down tools and follow the procession singing. The 26th of December still opens with the Festa delle Propaggini, and carnival closes on Shrove Tuesday. The historic centre deserves getting lost in—Palazzo del Balì (seat of the Knights of Malta), Porta Barsento, a mesh of vicoli.

⏱ 75 min
3

Head to Castellana Grotte to descend underground. On 23 January 1938, speleologist Franco Anelli lowered himself into the Grave, a 60-metre chasm open to the sky—for centuries peasants had seen the souls of suicides flying with bats there. He discovered behind a hidden passage a complex of 3,348 metres and 122 metres depth, the most spectacular in Italy. The tour descends as far as the Grotta Bianca, considered one of the whitest caves in the world. Constant temperature: 16.5°C. The full circuit covers 3 km and lasts 2h.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Cherry break in Conversano in June: the town is ringed by orchards, and the Notti della contea (in August) re-enact the splendours of the Acquaviva county with medieval parades and stalls. Out of season, walk to the Regional Nature Reserve of the Lakes of Conversano and Gravina di Monsignore, a karst landscape of six lakes, ideal for birdwatching and cycling.

⏱ 60 min

Tasting of farinella in Putignano. This fine flour of roasted chickpeas and barley fed peasants for centuries before becoming the carnival emblem. Sprinkled over fresh tomatoes, figs or a hunk of bread with oil, it's rustic, smoky, surprising. Also try the capocollo de Martina Franca, a regional cured meat smoked over oak wood.

⏱ 60 min

Carovigno

A quiet white town guarding the gates to Torre Guaceto's wild shores

★★★★☆
1

Quick morning detour via Selva di Fasano, a hamlet perched at 400 metres amid an oak forest—fresh air, panoramas over the coast and the Valle d'Itria. It's been the hill station for Apulians since the early 20th century. A few Liberty villas, game restaurants, and the belvedere sweeping from Ostuni to the Adriatic. Brief halt but refreshing after yesterday's underground galleries.

⏱ 45 min
2

Ceglie Messapica, one of the most authentic towns of the Valle d'Itria—less touristy than Ostuni, which lies right next door. Self-proclaimed and recognised gastronomic capital of Puglia: one of its restaurants won the region's first Michelin star in 1996. In the centre, the Castello Ducale (14th century, on Norman foundations) flanks Piazza Plebiscito, with its clock tower. Four Messapian megalithic circuits from the 8th century BC still encircle the old town.

⏱ 75 min
3

Carovigno, final stop: whitewashed historic centre à la Ostuni but quieter, dominated by the Castello Dentice di Frasso (15th-17th century), recognisable by its spur tower. The town is small, you can walk round it in an hour, but it mainly serves as a base for the nature reserve.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Torre Guaceto Nature Reserve: 3,300 hectares, 8 km of Adriatic coastline preserved between Brindisi and Carovigno, jointly managed by WWF Italia and the state. Sandy beaches framed by dunes up to 10 metres high, marshes where herons and bitterns nest, and nesting zone for the Caretta caretta turtle. The 16th-century Aragonese tower that gave the site its name (from the Arabic Al Gawsit, the place of fresh water) guards the entrance. Options for walking, cycling (dedicated trail from Serranova), diving and snorkelling.

⏱ 180 min

Aperitif in Ceglie Messapica on Piazza Plebiscito, when the square transforms into an open-air stage (mid-July to August, almost every evening: jazz, classical, folk). The Antico Caffè, founded in 1861 on Corso Garibaldi, still serves espresso just as it did on opening day—this is where the village elders gather for their daily ritual.

⏱ 75 min

Oria

Where Frederick II's triangular castle crowns a hill of sacred memories

★★★★☆
1

San Vito dei Normanni, named after the Norman knights in the 11th century who made it one of their strongholds. The Castello Dentice di Frasso, a Norman-Swabian fortress transformed into a Baroque residence, holds the central square. But San Vito's true treasure hides in the countryside: the cripta di San Biagio, a rock-cut church carved in the 12th century, preserves an exceptionally well-conserved cycle of Byzantine frescoes. Limited access; check before setting out.

⏱ 60 min
2

Mesagne, 13 km from Brindisi, in the Salento plain. The Castello Normanno-Svevo, erected in the 11th century and substantially remodelled by Frederick II in the 13th, now houses the Ugo-Granafei archaeological museum with a fine Messapian collection. The old town is best discovered on foot: Porta Nuova, Renaissance palaces, Baroque façades less exuberant than in Lecce but more human in scale. Mesagne has stayed off the main tourist circuits—this is authentic Salento, where locals still genuinely gather in the square.

⏱ 75 min
3

Oria to finish the day, perched on its hill with views across the plain to the Ionian Sea. The Castello Svevo, triangular plan with three towers (Quadrata, del Cavaliere, del Salto), witnessed in November 1225 the marriage of Frederick II to Isabella of Brienne. The Jewish quarter (giudecca) preserves the trace of an important community that gave birth to the physician Sabbataï Donnolo in the 10th century. The Cathedral of Santa Maria Assunta, with its 38-metre majolica dome—the highest in Salento—holds the relics of Saint Barsanuphius, a 5th-century hermit brought from Palestine in 873.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Late-afternoon stroll through Oria's Jewish quarter, a maze of white alleys tumbling steeply from the acropolis. At every turn, a view over the olive groves. It's calm, almost silent, and the sunset light takes on the characteristic orange hue of the inland Salento.

⏱ 60 min

Grottaglie

Italy's clay capital, where fifty cave-workshops shape the living tradition

★★★☆☆
1

Grottaglie, Italy's pottery capital since the Middle Ages—its name derives from the Latin Kriptalys, in reference to the tuff caves peppering its territory that have become workshops. The Quartiere delle Ceramiche spreads along Via Francesco Crispi, below the Castello Episcopio (14th century). Some fifty shops carved into the rock, wood-fired kilns still in operation, and that scent of clay wafting from interior courtyards. Spot the pumi, those large ceramic apples set on balconies (fertility symbols inherited from the goddess Pomona) and the galletto, the emblematic blue cockerel.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Castello Episcopio, in its historic-centre setting, houses the Museo della Ceramica with around 400 pieces covering 2,700 years of history—from the 8th century BC (Messapian ceramics from masseria Vicentino) right through to the 17th-century Bianchi di Grottaglie, those white majolicas glazed with tin oxide that won the town European renown. Devotional, archaeological and nativity sections: a complete panorama of local clay art.

⏱ 60 min
3

Detour through Grottaglie's historic centre above the potters' quarter. Chiesa Madre, noble palaces, and the gravina di San Giorgio dividing the two districts—a miniature example of those limestone ravines we'll explore tomorrow. The town is also known as the town of taralli, those little savoury dough rings found in every bakery.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Wheel-throwing workshop with a master potter—several workshops in the quarter offer one- to two-hour taster sessions. They put your hands in the clay, you turn your first bowl (which will probably go wonky), and you leave with your piece after firing. Hands-on experience and far more instructive than a simple guided tour.

⏱ 90 min

If you land in late July during Vino e Musica, the potters' quarter transforms into an open-air tasting trail: Salento wines (Primitivo, Negroamaro), street food, buskers in the lanes. And in August, the Cinzella Festival mixes music and cinema. Outside events, a simple aperitivo on Piazza Regina Margherita, watching the sun set over terraces loaded with capasuni (giant jars), is enough.

⏱ 90 min

Laterza

Where bread, majolica and a canyon deeper than anywhere else define the soul

★★★★☆
1

Massafra, nicknamed the Thebaid of Italy for the concentration of rock-cut churches in its 11 ravines. Descend the 125 steps of the Baroque staircase leading to the bottom of the Gravina della Madonna della Scala (4 km long, 40 metres deep, once the Valley of Roses). At the bottom, the Baroque sanctuary of Madonna della Scala, construction of which began in 1729 on the site of the miracle of the hinds: according to legend, two deer hunted by hunters knelt around 1300 before the Byzantine fresco of the Virgin hidden in the rock. 200 metres on, the Farmacia del Mago Greguro, a complex of 12 interconnected monastic cells carved into the tuff, where Basilian monks stored their medicinal herbs.

⏱ 90 min
2

Castellaneta, birthplace of Rudolph Valentino, the great seducer of silent cinema born here in 1895. The Museo Valentino, housed in the former convent of Santa Chiara, holds thousands of posters, films, stage costumes and personal items—€3.50, guided tour and video included. The Gravina di Castellaneta, known as Gravina Grande, carves 10 km long and over 100 metres deep just behind the historic centre. Several rock-cut churches—Padre Eterno, Santa Maria del Pesco—remain virtually inaccessible there, but the borgo's viewpoints offer vertiginous vistas.

⏱ 75 min
3

Laterza, highlight of the day. Laterza PDO bread, baked in traditional wood-fired ovens, scents the streets morning and afternoon: dark crust, soft crumb, round format, you find it nowadays as far as Milan. The MuMa, Museo della Maiolica, tells the story of 17th-18th century Laterza majolica with a well-designed multimedia tour. And the Fontana dei Mascheroni, right in the centre, its bronze mascarons spouting water, long served as trough and washhouse for the locals.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hike along trail no. 3 of the LIPU oasis above the Gravina di Laterza: 11 km return, considered the Grand Canyon of Puglia. 12 km long, up to 400 metres wide, vertical walls over 200 metres high. It's one of the deepest gorges in Europe, protected by the Terra delle Gravine Regional Nature Park since 2005. You can spot the lesser kestrel, the eagle owl, and with a bit of luck the Egyptian vulture.

⏱ 180 min

Gourmet stop in Laterza: the focaccia laertina, usually topped with olive oil, oregano and tomato, comes out of the ovens mid-day. In the evening, the rosticcerie line up their charcoal grills—local meats, bombette (meat rolls stuffed with cheese), everything goes on the fire. It's what Apulians call cucina dei fornelli, a social ritual as much as a meal.

⏱ 60 min

Taranto

Ancient Spartan colony cradling the finest Hellenistic gold in Italy

★★★☆☆
1

Ginosa to start the day, last rock-dwelling village before Taranto. Its ravine, like Massafra's, is riddled with troglodyte dwellings inhabited until the 19th century. The borgo perched above served as a location for Pier Paolo Pasolini in 1964 for some scenes from The Gospel According to St. Matthew. At Easter, the rock-cut village of Casale hosts a living Via Crucis that mobilises the whole community.

⏱ 60 min
2

Taranto at last, founded by the Spartans in 706 BC, the sole Laconian colony in Italy. The Città Vecchia sits on an island linked by two bridges to the mainland. White lanes, crumbling palaces under restoration, fish markets—Taranto is an active fishing port, Puglia's second fleet. The Castello Aragonese, erected in 1481 by Ferdinand I of Aragon on Byzantine foundations, guards the entrance to the Mar Piccolo. Free tour offered by the Italian Navy which now occupies it.

⏱ 75 min
3

MArTA, the National Archaeological Museum, is the climax of the itinerary. Housed in the former Alcantarini convent, it's the second archaeological museum in southern Italy after Naples. The Ori di Taranto—navicella earrings, diadems, rings set with scarabs—represent the pinnacle of Hellenistic goldwork. The Tomba dell'Atleta (5th century BC) displays the skeleton of a champion surrounded by his trophies, visible via a mirror above. The Zeus of Ugento, a 6th-century bronze statuette, came home after a long journey via Los Angeles. Open daily 8.30am-7.30pm, €10.

⏱ 150 min

Activities

Stroll along the Lungomare Vittorio Emanuele III, recently renovated as a pedestrian zone. The view sweeps across the Mar Grande, the island of San Pietro and, at sunset, you sometimes spot dolphins in the gulf—Taranto has remained a relatively pristine marine zone despite the large steelworks marking the other shore.

⏱ 60 min

Farewell dinner built around cozze tarantine—Taranto mussels are PDO, farmed in the Mar Piccolo since Greek antiquity. On the typical menu: mussels au gratin, orecchiette with turnip greens, tielle (rice-potato-mussel mix), washed down with a Primitivo di Manduria. L'Osteria del Vicolo, in the old town, is a good address to wrap things up.

⏱ 120 min
