Prague

★★★★★
1

Old Town Square (Staroměstské náměstí) packs six centuries of history into a single plaza. In the corner of the town hall, the Astronomical Clock (Orloj) has chimed every hour since 1410: the twelve apostles parade past two windows while the crowd tilts their heads in unison. It's brief, a bit kitschy, but the mechanism remains one of the oldest still ticking in the world. Come before 9 a.m. or after 8 p.m. to dodge the selfie wall.

⏱ 60 min
2

The cobblestones of Charles Bridge (Karlův most) gleam at dawn, deserted. Launched in 1357 under Charles IV, this Gothic stone bridge served for centuries as the only crossing between the two banks of the Vltava. Thirty Baroque statues line it today; touch the bronze plaque of St. John of Nepomuk—tradition credits him with bringing luck. At 7 a.m. you'll have it nearly to yourself; by noon, it's a human river.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Descend into the medieval underground of the Old Town, beneath the town hall. These vaulted cellars were the original ground floors, buried when street levels were raised against flooding. The guided tour recounts executions and legends, far from the din of the square above. Count on about 50 minutes, and choose the option with tower access for the finest view over the rooftops.

⏱ 60 min

Kutna Hora

★★★★☆
1

Hard to top the spectacle of St. Barbara's Cathedral (Chrám svaté Barbory). This Gothic vessel with tent-like roofs was begun in 1388 and finished only in 1905, starved of funds when the silver veins ran dry. St. Barbara is the patron saint of miners, fitting for a city built on mines. Covered knees and shoulders required; combine your ticket with Sedlec Ossuary to save money.

⏱ 75 min
2

Listed as UNESCO heritage in 1995, the Sedlec Ossuary (Kostnice) unsettles as much as it fascinates. Beneath the chapel, the bones of tens of thousands of deceased have been arranged into décor: the central chandelier supposedly contains at least one of every bone in the human body, garlands of skulls, a coat of arms carved from bone. It's macabre and strangely peaceful. Time-slot tickets mandatory—purchase in advance: capacity is limited and it overflows fast.

⏱ 45 min
3

More discreet than its neighbors, the Italian Court (Vlašský dvůr) hides the heart of medieval power. This is where they minted the Prague groschen, the silver coin that built the kingdom of Bohemia's wealth. Italian artisans worked here, hence the name. The tour shows the mint workshops and the royal chapel. Opt for a guided visit: without a guide, the rooms say little.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Don the white cape and follow your guide into a medieval silver mine at the Czech Silver Museum (Hrádek). You descend a narrow gallery carved more than five hundred years ago. In places you must crouch low; water seeps, it's 50°F even in August. Book early—slots sell out fast in summer—and skip if you're claustrophobic.

⏱ 90 min

Kolin

★★☆☆☆
1

St. Bartholomew's Cathedral (Chrám svatého Bartoloměje) dominates Kolín with its Gothic towers. We owe it partly to Petr Parléř, the architect of Prague's St. Vitus Cathedral, which places this Elbe town in a prestigious lineage. The bell tower opens for visits and serves as a viewpoint over the rooftops and the river. Check the belfry hours before climbing—they're restricted off-season.

⏱ 45 min
2

In the silence of the old ghetto, the Renaissance synagogue of Kolín surprises. Founded at the turn of the 14th and 15th centuries, it has crossed the centuries with much of the Jewish quarter around it intact—rare in Bohemia. The interior, sober and bright, opens when a guardian is present. Ask at the tourist office that day: opening often depends on availability.

⏱ 30 min
$$$★9.1Spa Hotel Felicitas
🚗Drive to next stop: 22 km · 28min

Kourim

★★☆☆☆
1

The Kouřim open-air museum (skanzen) deserves the detour for one simple reason: it's one of the rare Czech open-air museums gathering peasant architecture from all of Bohemia. Created in 1972 to save houses threatened by dam construction, it lines up wooden farmsteads, a forge, and thatched cottages from the 17th to 19th centuries, furnished period-style. Count 45 minutes to an hour; late afternoon, low light, is most beautiful.

⏱ 60 min
2

Built in the town's heart, St. Stephen's Basilica (kostel svatého Štěpána) hides two curiosities. Under the church, the Gothic crypt of St. Catherine opens to visitors; in the bell tower, bells mounted "upside down" ring in reverse—a rare case. The building dates to the Middle Ages, when Kouřim was a royal town more important than today. Limited opening: aim for weekends or ask at the neighboring regional museum.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Sign up for a traditional craft workshop at the Kouřim skanzen. Several Saturdays a year, you learn an ancient skill and leave with your own object—more meaningful than a shop souvenir. The ethnographic programs follow the calendar of peasant festivals, so content changes by season. Check the program on the museum website before coming: not everything's offered every week.

⏱ 90 min

Cesky Sternberk

★★★★☆
1

Český Šternberk Castle (hrad) has overlooked the Sázava since 1241. Founded by Zdeslav of Divišov, who drew his name from the star (Stern) on his coat of arms, it still belongs to the same Sternberg family nearly eight centuries later. Under austere Gothic walls hide Baroque salons and a library of several thousand volumes. Access is on foot from the river; bring good shoes.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

In fine weather, paddle the Sázava by canoe at the foot of the castle. The river threads through the forests of Posázaví, with a few livelier passages that entertain without real danger. The view of the castle from the water is worth the paddle stroke. Rent from a local club and check water level: too low in midsummer, some stretches scrape.

⏱ 120 min
$$$★9.5Krčma na statku
🚗Drive to next stop: 12 km · 18min

Sazava

★★★★☆
1

Founded in 1032, the Sázava Monastery (Sázavský klášter) was a rare hub of Slavonic culture, where scribes wrote in Old Church Slavonic rather than Latin. St. Procopius, its founder, remains the guardian figure. The complex blends Romanesque, Gothic, and Baroque remnants at the river's edge. Its curious treasure: a "Madonna of Sázava" where the Virgin seems to scold the Child. Mostly guided tours; check off-season hours.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Push on to the Sázava glassmaking center after the monastery. The town preserves an old glass tradition, and the František glassworks opens its workshop-gallery to visitors: you watch blowing and cutting and leave with a piece if the mood strikes. It's artisanal, not industrial. Pair it with a short stroll along the Sázava riverbanks, pleasant in good weather.

⏱ 60 min
$$$★9Hotel SEN
🚗Drive to next stop: 25 km · 30min

Konopiste

★★★☆☆
1

Konopiště is less a castle than a frozen obsession. Archduke Franz Ferdinand, Habsburg heir, bought it in 1887 and crammed it with hunting trophies: his ledgers claim some 300,000 animals killed. He installed electricity, central heating, and an elevator—mad modernity for the era. Here he lived before his assassination in Sarajevo in 1914, the spark of World War I.

⏱ 75 min
2

The rose garden at Konopiště unfolds its beds south of the castle. Laid out by Franz Ferdinand and his wife Sophie at the turn of the 20th century, it arrays hundreds of rose varieties, complemented by greenhouses with exotic plants. In June the fragrance is heady and the beds explode. Outside flowering season, interest drops sharply: time your visit late May through June to enjoy it fully.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Walk to the lake through the 555-acre landscaped park surrounding Konopiště. Paths, statues, and ponds compose an English garden designed as scenery, far from the crowds massed before the castle. The leisurely loop takes a good hour. Park below, near Benešov, and walk up: the upper lot saturates fast on weekends.

⏱ 75 min
$$$★9Hotel SEN
🚗Drive to next stop: 30 km · 35min

Dobris

★★★☆☆
1

The original parquet still creaks underfoot at Dobříš Castle (zámek Dobříš). This small Rococo palace from the mid-18th century belongs to the Colloredo-Mannsfeld family; its furnished interiors tell the lifestyle of the aristocracy. Tasty detail: under the communist regime, Czech writers came here to stay and write. Guided tour of the salons, then exit on the garden side.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Two gardens answer each other at Dobříš: the French, geometric, and the English, wilder. The first unrolls geometric beds just behind the castle; the second, more untamed, invites the long stroll. The gardens rank among the country's finest. Admission is sometimes separate from the castle: check if your ticket covers both, and come in dry weather—the gravel paths handle rain poorly.

⏱ 60 min
$$$★9.1Esmarin wellness hotel
🚗Drive to next stop: 12 km · 18min

Pribram

★★★☆☆
1

Holy Mountain (Svatá Hora) has watched over Příbram for more than three centuries. This Baroque complex is one of Bohemia's great Marian pilgrimage sites; it bears the title of minor basilica. At its center, a Gothic statue of the Virgin, carved according to legend from pear wood. A long covered stairway climbs there from the town.

⏱ 60 min
2

Opened in 1886, the Mining Museum of Příbram (Hornické muzeum) is the largest of its kind in the country. Installed in the buildings of the Březové Hory mines, it unrolls the silver-lead past of one of the major mining towns of the old Austro-Hungarian Empire. Extraction ceased in 1978. Several circuits; budget generously if you want to do everything—the site is extensive.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The little mine train at Březové Hory plunges underground, hard hat required. The museum offers a real immersion: underground galleries stretching several kilometers, a slide down to a giant water wheel, old steam machines. Kids love it. Bring warm clothes—it's chilly underground—and book the guided descents that leave in groups.

⏱ 90 min
$$$★9Hotel JURKOS
🚗Drive to next stop: 7 km · 12min

Vojna

★★★★☆
1

Minutes from the basilica, the Vojna Memorial chills the blood. This communist labor camp imprisoned opponents of the regime, often forced to work the uranium mines nearby. Barracks and watchtowers have been preserved. It's the brutal underside of the Baroque postcard, to be seen to understand 20th-century Czech history. Visit rather in season; check opening before making the detour.

⏱ 60 min
$★9Penzion Stará Fořtovna Brdy

Slapy

★★★★☆
1

The Slapy Dam, completed in 1955, blocks the Vltava with a concrete wall 65 metres high. You can walk along the crest for a view plunging on one side over the reservoir, on the other over the river below.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Svatojánské proudy, once the most feared rapids on the Vltava, were drowned by the dam. Pleasure boats run in season and follow the deep meanders between the wooded slopes.

⏱ 30 min
3

The lake's slopes are dotted with chata, the wooden cabins where Praguers have spent weekends for generations. A lookout above the shore captures the scale of this very Czech leisure landscape.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Settle onto a beach at Lake Slapy in warm weather. It's Prague's leisure reservoir: swimming, paddle boats, launches, and hundreds of wooden cottages (the chata) clinging to wooded slopes. The atmosphere is family-friendly, no frills. The water warms up properly only in July-August; outside summer, stick to the views—swimming stings.

⏱ 90 min
$$$★9.4Palace Resort Jíloviště
🚗Drive to next stop: 12 km · 20min

Maj

★★★★☆
1

From the Máj viewpoint, the Vltava draws a dizzying meander below. The panorama plunges onto the Slapy dam lake, a reservoir some 25 miles long, filled in the 1950s. The concrete wall, about 210 feet high, is part of the "Vltava cascade." The trail is short but climbs steeply; wear decent shoes and keep an eye on children near the edge.

⏱ 45 min
$$$★9.4Palace Resort Jíloviště

Karlstejn

★★★★☆
1

Karlštejn ticks every fairytale-castle box, yet everything is real. Charles IV founded it in 1348 to shelter the Bohemian crown jewels and his relics, on a cliff overlooking the Berounka. It's one of the country's most visited castles, hence crowded. The Chapel of the Holy Cross, with 129 panels painted by Master Theodoric, requires a separate booking. Buy your tickets online, especially for the chapel circuit.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Climb on foot to the castle through the village of Karlštejn, lanes lined with shops and taverns. The climb takes about twenty minutes and warms the calves. Below, the hillsides carry an old royal wine tradition; a few cellars offer tastings of Czech wines. Leave the car below—access to the summit is forbidden—and park at the village entrance, which fills fast on weekends.

⏱ 60 min
$$$★9.4Palace Resort Jíloviště

Velka Amerika

★★★★☆
1

Velká Amerika slices a turquoise gash into the limestone plateau near Mořina village. This abandoned limestone quarry, about half a mile long and up to 330 feet deep, flooded after extraction stopped: logical nickname, the "Czech Grand Canyon." Tunnels link it to other quarries with names like Malá Amerika or Mexiko. Descent to the bottom is forbidden and fined; stay on the overlooks above.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

If you like to walk, loop around the Velká Amerika quarry on the rim trail. The loop runs along the void and offers the best views of the blue water and white walls. Count a good hour, no real difficulty. Stay cautious near unsecured edges, and park along the road: a local often asks a few euros for the spot.

⏱ 75 min
$$$★9.4Palace Resort Jíloviště
🚗Drive to next stop: 18 km · 25min

Koneprusy

★★★★☆
1

Over a mile long, the Koněprusy Caves (Koněpruské jeskyně) form Bohemia's largest underground network. You admire the "roses of Koněprusy," calcite concretions shaped like flowers, and guides recount that a clandestine medieval counterfeiter's workshop was discovered here. The site, under the Golden Horse (Zlatý kůň) hill, has yielded prehistoric remains. It's 50°F: bring a jacket, even in summer.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Climb the Golden Horse (Zlatý kůň) hill after the cave visit. The summit overlooks the Bohemian karst and its old quarries; the open view rewards a short scramble. The spot is calm, far from the Karlštejn crowds nearby. Combine both in the same half-day, but check cave hours: guided tours only, by time slots.

⏱ 45 min
$$$★9.6Apartmány Pekárna 1898
🚗Drive to next stop: 35 km · 45min

Krivoklat

★★★★☆
1

Křivoklát Castle (hrad) hides deep in the royal forests west of Prague. Founded in the 12th century, it was the favorite hunting rendezvous of the kings of Bohemia, especially Wenceslas IV. Its great Gothic hall is one of the country's vastest, and the Fürstenberg library holds tens of thousands of volumes. The alchemist Edward Kelley was imprisoned here. Count about 100 minutes for the grand circuit.

⏱ 100 min

Activities

If the forest calls you, set out walking in the protected reserve of Křivoklátsko around the castle. These dense woods, kept wild for centuries as a royal hunting reserve, shelter rich fauna and flora. Trails follow the Berounka, beloved of canoeists. Bring water and a map: the trail network is vast and cell service sometimes absent.

⏱ 90 min
$$$★9.7Centrum Rosenbaum
🚗Drive to next stop: 20 km · 25min

Lany

★★★★☆
1

Lány speaks mainly to those interested in Czech history. The château is the presidential summer residence since 1921, but it remains closed to the public: it's the T. G. Masaryk Museum, facing the château, that recounts the first Czechoslovak president. Recreations, personal objects, trenches from the Great War. Open since 2000, it tours in an hour. Check opening days—variable off-season.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Walk to Masaryk's grave in Lány's modest cemetery. The first president, his wife Charlotte, and his children rest there; under the communist regime, this discreet place became a symbol of democracy honored in secret. It's sober and loaded with meaning. Pair with a stroll along the Křivoklát forest edge before heading back toward Prague.

⏱ 45 min
$$★10U Dobroty

Prague

★★★★★
1

Prague Castle, founded in the 9th century on the left bank of the Vltava, is considered the world's largest medieval castle complex at some 750,000 square feet. Climb through the Hradčany quarter to reach the three successive courtyards, the second of which holds the National Gallery of the Castle and the third St. Vitus Cathedral.

⏱ 60 min
2

St. Vitus Cathedral, begun in 1344 under Charles IV on plans by the Frenchman Matthias of Arras, was completed only in 1929—585 years of construction. His successor Peter Parler, summoned at age 23 to direct the project, invented net vaults of unmatched audacity for the era. Inside, the Chapel of St. Wenceslas is studded with semi-precious stones and 14th-century frescoes. The adjoining vault, locked by seven locks whose keys each belong to a different state dignitary, shelters the Bohemian crown jewels.

⏱ 75 min
3

Golden Lane (Zlatá ulička), tucked against the north rampart of the castle, is a row of colorful little houses from the 16th century. Legend attributes the name to alchemists whom Emperor Rudolf II supposedly lodged here to transmute metals into gold, but archives show the actual residents were arquebusiers and craftsmen.

⏱ 30 min
4

The Church of St. Nicholas in Malá Strana, built between 1704 and 1755 by Christoph then Kilian Ignaz Dientzenhofer, is one of the masterpieces of Central European Baroque. Its 246-foot dome dominates the rooftops of the left bank. Mozart played the organ here during his Prague stays.

⏱ 30 min
5

Explore Josefov, Prague's former Jewish ghetto. The Old-New Synagogue, built around 1270 in Cistercian Gothic style, is the oldest synagogue in Central Europe still in use. It is linked to Rabbi Löw (1520-1609), to whom tradition attributes the creation of the Golem—a clay creature animated by a magic formula to protect the Jewish community. Its attic supposedly still harbors the dormant Golem. In 1389, during the deadliest pogrom in Prague's history, more than 3,000 Jews were massacred in this quarter.

⏱ 75 min
6

The Old Jewish Cemetery, founded in the early 15th century, is one of Europe's oldest. For lack of space, the dead were buried in layers up to 12 deep, explaining the spectacular crowding of some 12,000 gravestones. Rabbi Löw's tomb is perpetually covered with small prayer notes.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In the evening, walk the Vltava quays between Charles Bridge and the Legion Bridge to see the illuminated castle reflected on the river. The view from Kampa Island, accessible by a staircase under Charles Bridge, is particularly striking.

⏱ 60 min
