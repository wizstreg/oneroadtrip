Szentendre

Where Serbian refugees built a Baroque riverside town of artists and icons

★★★☆☆
1

Begin at Fő tér square, the historic heart of Szentendre—a compact town of 25,000 inhabitants founded in the 17th century by Serbian refugees fleeing Ottoman occupation. At the center stands a plague column topped with a cross, erected by Serbian merchants in the 18th century to commemorate the town's escape from the plague epidemic. Arrive early: tour buses descend from 10am onward.

⏱ 60 min
2

Step inside the Margit Kovács Museum, the most visited museum in all of Hungary outside Budapest. Margit Kovács (1902-1977) was Hungary's greatest 20th-century ceramicist—her terracotta figurines, hovering between folk art and expressionism, radiate a striking intensity. Allow 45 minutes to take it all in.

⏱ 60 min
3

Bogdányi Street, just behind the main square, is lined with artists' studios, galleries, and craft boutiques. Since 1926, an artists' colony has thrived here, drawn by the light and the town's almost Mediterranean atmosphere—Szentendre has been twinned with Salon-de-Provence since 1976. Wander without a plan and duck into any open workshops.

⏱ 45 min
4

Don't miss the Belgrade Church, seat of the Serbian Orthodox bishopric in Hungary. It houses a remarkable collection of 18th-century Serbian icons and rare liturgical objects, witnesses to the Serbian community that shaped the town's identity.

⏱ 45 min
5

On the town's outskirts, the Skanzen—an open-air ethnographic museum—deserves a half-day on its own. Over 400 reconstructed traditional buildings trace the evolution of Hungarian rural architecture from the 18th to the 20th century, with 50,000 period objects. In summer, folk shows featuring dances and traditional games are staged on-site. Wear good walking shoes.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Evening stroll along the Danube from the town center to Pap Island, accessible by a pedestrian bridge. The spot is quiet, popular with locals, and offers lovely river views at sunset.

⏱ 60 min

Visegrad

Medieval citadel commanding the Danube from its 350-meter throne

★★★☆☆
1

The Citadel of Visegrád crowns a 350-meter hill above the Danube—a strategic position defended since Roman times. The castle's construction dates back to the 13th century, after the Tatar invasion of 1241: King Béla IV had it built using his Byzantine wife's dowry. In the 14th century, under Charles I Robert of Anjou, Visegrád became the capital of the Kingdom of Hungary. In 1335, the kings of Hungary, Bohemia, and Poland met here for a historic diplomatic summit. Climb to the terrace for the panorama over the Danube Bend—one of the finest viewpoints in all of Hungary.

⏱ 90 min
2

Down in the lower town, visit the ruins of the Royal Palace. In the 15th century, under Matthias Corvinus, Visegrád was one of the great centers of the Renaissance in Central Europe—a papal ambassador described it in 1483 as 'paradise on earth.' The palace was sacked and destroyed during the Ottoman invasion of 1544. Archaeological excavations have uncovered remarkable elements, now displayed in reconstructed chambers.

⏱ 60 min
3

Solomon's Tower, at the foot of the hill, is a six-story hexagonal tower dating from the 13th century. Its name comes from a legend: King Solomon was supposedly imprisoned here on the orders of Saint Ladislaus I, though historians attribute that event to a different fortress. It served as a prison during the 16th-century Ottoman occupation.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

For walkers, a 4.5 km loop trail links Solomon's Tower to the citadel via forest paths. Elevation gain of 252 meters, so bring good shoes—the rocky sections can be slippery in wet weather. Allow 1.5 to 2 hours.

⏱ 100 min

A few kilometers away, the Rám-szakadék Gorge near Dömös offers a spectacular hike through limestone ravines with moss-covered boulders, narrow walls, and small waterfalls. One of the region's most beautiful natural walks.

⏱ 120 min

Esztergom

Where Hungary's first Christian king was born beneath Central Europe's grandest dome

★★☆☆☆
1

The Basilica of Esztergom rises from its hilltop as the largest church in Hungary and the largest basilica in Central Europe. It measures 118 meters long, 49 meters wide, and its dome soars to 100 meters. Construction lasted from 1822 to 1869, on the site of a cathedral founded in 1010 by King Saint Stephen. For its 1856 inauguration, Franz Liszt composed and conducted his 'Esztergom Mass' in the presence of Emperor Franz Joseph. Climb to the top of the dome for a 360° panorama over the city, the Danube, and Slovakia across the water.

⏱ 90 min
2

Inside, don't miss the Bakócz Chapel, built between 1506 and 1507 in red marble by Italian craftsmen. A masterpiece of the Hungarian Renaissance, it was dismantled into 1,600 pieces in 1823, then moved 20 meters to be incorporated into the new basilica. The crypt holds the tombs of Hungarian cardinals, including Cardinal Mindszenty, who died in exile in Vienna in 1975 and was repatriated in 1991 only after the last Soviet soldier had left, as he had wished.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Primate's Palace houses the Christian Museum, the third-largest collection of sacred art in the world after the Vatican and Munich. Esztergom served as Hungary's capital from the 10th to the 13th century—it was here that King Saint Stephen, Hungary's first Christian king, was born in 969.

⏱ 60 min
4

Cross the Maria Valeria Bridge to set foot in Slovakia, in the small town of Štúrovo (formerly Párkány). The original bridge was destroyed by the Germans in 1944 and rebuilt only in 2001. A 20-minute walk to feel concretely the border drawn in 1920.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Late-afternoon walk along the Danube riverbank from the basilica hill down to the historic town center, passing the Ottoman remains—a stone table commemorates Sultan Suleiman, who died here during the 1566 siege.

⏱ 60 min

Godollo

Sisi's violet-hued sanctuary, far from the suffocating courts of Vienna

★★☆☆☆
1

The Royal Palace of Gödöllő is the largest baroque palace in Hungary and the second-largest in Europe, built between 1741 and 1749 for Count Antal Grassalkovich I, a close associate of Empress Maria Theresa. The Hungarian nation gifted it to the imperial couple after the Austro-Hungarian Compromise of 1867. Empress Elisabeth—Sisi—made it her favorite residence: she spent over 2,000 days here throughout her life, mainly in spring and autumn. She fled the rigid protocol of Vienna's Hofburg; here, she could ride horses, receive her Hungarian friends, and live at her own pace. Her apartments, decorated in violet—her favorite color—are presented in their 1867 state.

⏱ 90 min
2

The 166-square-meter rococo ballroom, in white and gold, is the palace's most spectacular room. The palace theater, designed for 100 spectators, is one of Hungary's oldest preserved theaters—it had been destroyed by the installation of false ceilings in 1867 and was only reconstructed in 1986. After World War I, the palace fell into neglect; under the Soviet regime, it served as a psychiatric asylum and then a barracks. Its restoration began in 1996.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walk through the palace grounds, where the flower beds in front of Sisi's apartments were planted each year with violets and pansies—the empress's favorite flowers—by the estate's gardeners.

⏱ 45 min

Five kilometers from the palace, an equestrian park offers carriage rides through the surrounding countryside and demonstrations of traditional Hungarian horsemanship—the csikós, Magyar horsemen, are the pride of the region.

⏱ 90 min

Heviz

Europe's largest thermal lake, where water lilies bloom year-round at 33°C

★★★☆☆
1

The thermal lake of Hévíz is a natural curiosity without equal: at 4.5 hectares, it's the largest natural thermal lake in Europe (and one of the two largest in the world). Water gushes from a spring 40 meters deep at a rate of 410 liters per second—this flow renews the entire lake in 28 hours. The temperature ranges between 33 and 36°C in summer, and never drops below 23°C in winter. Roman coins have been found at the bottom, proof that the site was already exploited 2,000 years ago. On the surface, pink water lilies cover part of the lake—a species introduced in the 19th century that's now part of the landscape.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Bathing in the lake is Hévíz's central experience. The water, slightly sulfurous, contains calcium, magnesium, bicarbonate, and mild radon, reputed to treat rheumatic and joint pain. Bring a float (rentals on-site) to drift effortlessly and fully enjoy the water lilies' spectacle. Plan on 2 to 3 hours minimum.

⏱ 150 min

After your soak, stroll through Hévíz's town center, its cafés and local shops. The town lives almost exclusively from thermal tourism—500 people work there directly, including 85 masseurs and 28 physicians attached to the thermal complex.

⏱ 60 min

1

Just 6 km from Hévíz, Keszthely is home to Festetics Palace, Hungary's third-largest château. Its construction began in 1745 under Kristóf Festetics, a nobleman of Croatian descent ennobled by Maria Theresa. With 101 rooms arranged around a U-shaped courtyard, the baroque building stands out for its exceptional state of preservation—it didn't suffer during the Soviet period, unlike most Hungarian palaces. Its greatest treasure is the Helikon Library, the only intact aristocratic library in Europe with over 86,000 volumes, including numerous Enlightenment agricultural treatises.

3

The Balaton Museum, opposite the palace, presents the lake's geology, fauna, and history through modern exhibitions. Lake Balaton—a few hundred meters away—is the largest lake in Central Europe, stretching 77 km long and 14 km wide.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walk to Városy Beach on the shores of Lake Balaton, a 10-minute walk from the town center. In summer, the lake's shallow waters (averaging 2 to 3 meters) make it ideal for swimming and water sports.

Pecs

Where the crescent and the cross share the same dome in Hungary's southernmost city

★★★★☆
1

Széchenyi Square, at Pécs's heart, is dominated by a building that encapsulates the city's entire history: the Gazi Kasim Pasha Mosque-Church, built in 1543 using stones from the pre-existing medieval Gothic church—a striking architectural mise en abyme. Converted to a Catholic church in 1702, it retains its Ottoman features: dome, arched windows, and mihrab oriented toward Mecca. At the altar, the Bible sits alongside the Quran, and the Ottoman lamp serves as the sanctuary lamp. Outside, the Catholic cross surmounts the crescent. It's the largest surviving example of Ottoman architecture in Hungary.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Cathedral of Saints Peter and Paul is recognizable from afar by its four neo-Romanesque towers. Its construction dates back to around 1040, but the current building was erected by architect Friedrich von Schmidt between 1883 and 1891. Pope John Paul II made it a minor basilica in 1990. The Pope celebrated Mass here during his visit to Hungary—one of the rare times he visited a former Soviet bloc country.

⏱ 45 min
3

Beneath the cathedral's forecourt and in the surrounding quarter, the Early Christian Necropolis of Sopianae has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2000. The first Christian communities buried their dead here in the 4th century—one of the most important early Christian necropolises in Europe outside Italy. Burial chambers adorned with biblical frescoes are open to visitors.

⏱ 60 min
4

The Zsolnay Quarter, a former ceramics factory converted into a cultural complex for Pécs's 2010 European Capital of Culture title, is well worth a visit. Zsolnay ceramics, recognizable by their iridescent gold and green glazes, decorated some of Art Nouveau's most important buildings across Europe. The factory was founded in 1853, and its history is presented in a museum of 700 objects.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Stroll through the historic center's pedestrian streets, from Király Street to Ferencesek Street. Pécs has a faintly Mediterranean atmosphere—surrounded by lavender, fig trees, and pomegranates, it's nicknamed Hungary's most southerly city. Look for other Ottoman traces: the Jakovali Hassan Mosque and its fully preserved 16th-century minaret.

⏱ 75 min

Eger

The castle that held back thirty thousand Turks with wine-stained beards and iron will

★★☆☆☆
1

Eger Castle (Egri Vár) dominates the city from its hill—and throughout Hungary, its history resonates as a symbol of national resistance. In 1552, Captain István Dobó held off a siege by an Ottoman army thirty times superior in number. Legend has it that the defenders drank red wine that stained their beards, and the Turks believed they were drinking bull's blood—giving its name to Egri Bikavér wine. Today a cannon is fired daily at 3:52 pm to commemorate the event. The interactive museum in the former bishop's palace recounts this episode in detail.

⏱ 90 min
2

Eger's neoclassical basilica, built between 1831 and 1836 by József Hild, is Hungary's second-largest church after Esztergom. Its orange façade with Corinthian columns dominates the central square. Inside, the trompe-l'oeil ceiling depicting the Council of Trent (1545-1563) is one of the country's most celebrated frescoes. Next door, the Eszterházy Líceum houses a spectacular baroque library and, on the 9th floor, an 18th-century camera obscura—a mirror system projects a real-time image of the city onto a round table. Climb to the terrace for the 360° view over Eger and the Bükk Mountains.

⏱ 75 min
3

Don't leave without seeking out the minaret—the only one still standing in Hungary after 1,500 years of Ottoman presence in the country. This dark 17th-century tower, around a corner in the town center, has about a hundred narrow steps. You can climb it: the view over Eger's rooftops is worth the effort. It's closed from November to March.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The Valley of Beautiful Women (Szépasszony-völgy) lies 1.5 km from the center—a street carved into the rock and lined with over 200 troglodyte wine cellars open to the public. This is where you'll taste Egri Bikavér at its source, accompanied by bread and Hungarian cheese. The atmosphere is convivial, tables spill out in the evening. Allow at least 1.5 hours.

⏱ 90 min

Tokaj

Where liquid gold flows from volcanic cellars, aged since before Bordeaux was born

★★☆☆☆
1

Tokaj, at the confluence of the Tisza and Bodrog rivers, is the capital of a wine region inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2002. Tokaji Aszú—'the wine of kings and the king of wines' according to Louis XIV—has been produced here without interruption since the 16th century. The region is also the world's first delimited wine appellation, classified by royal decree in 1737, predating Bordeaux's classification by 18 years. The Tokaj Wine Museum, in an 18th-century neoclassical building, traces this history with period tools, documents, and an accessible 16th-century cellar.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Rákóczi Cellar, built in the early 15th century, is one of the region's largest and oldest—it belonged successively to kings, princes, and generals. It was from this cellar that Prince Francis II Rákóczi sent wine to Louis XIV in 1703 to secure his support against the Habsburgs. Descend into the labyrinth of galleries carved from volcanic rock for a tasting of Furmint and Aszú.

⏱ 75 min
3

Tokaj's Great Synagogue (1889), in neo-Romanesque style, testifies to the Jewish community that represented over 40% of the town's population in the late 19th century—one of the highest proportions in Hungary. A walk to the confluence of the two rivers offers a unique view of the vineyard landscape that made Tokaj world-famous.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

For serious wine lovers, book in advance for a winery visit with guided tasting—dry Furmint, Szamorodni, and Aszú 5 puttonyos in progression. The vineyards on south-facing volcanic slopes offer spectacular views over the Hungarian plain. The harvest season (September-November) is the liveliest, but reservations are essential in high season.

⏱ 120 min

Budapest

Twin cities straddling the Danube, where Art Nouveau bathhouses meet underground ruin bars

★★★★★
1

Returning to Budapest allows you to close the loop with a day in the capital. The Hungarian Parliament on the Danube's banks is one of the world's largest parliamentary buildings: 268 meters long, 96 meters high—a symbolic figure, the year 896 marking the Hungarians' arrival in the Carpathian Basin. Its construction lasted from 1885 to 1904. The guided interior tour, with the Crown of Saint Stephen displayed under high security, is essential—book online before arriving.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Fisherman's Bastion, on Buda Hill, offers Budapest's most photographed panorama of the Danube, Parliament, and bridges. Built between 1895 and 1902 in neo-Gothic and neo-Romanesque styles, its seven conical towers represent the seven Magyar chieftains who founded the country.

⏱ 60 min
3

For a final plunge into Hungarian thermal culture, the Széchenyi Baths are Budapest's most iconic and accessible: a 1913 neo-baroque complex in Városliget Park, with 15 pools including three outdoor ones at 38°C. On weekend evenings, DJ sets are organized—the famous 'Sparty.' Allow 2 to 3 hours.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

In the evening, explore the ruin bars quarter in Erzsébetváros—the Jewish quarter of the 7th district. Szimpla Kert, the first opened in 2001, has become an institution: bars installed in dilapidated building courtyards and decorated with salvaged furniture. The atmosphere is unique in Europe.

⏱ 120 min
