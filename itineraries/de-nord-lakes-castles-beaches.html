Berlin

★★★★★
1

The Brandenburg Gate was built between 1788 and 1791 on the orders of King Frederick William II of Prussia, designed by Carl Gotthard Langhans and inspired by the Propylaea of the Athens Acropolis. During Berlin's division, it stood trapped in the border strip, inaccessible to Berliners on both sides. It was here, in front of this gate, that Reagan issued his 1987 challenge to Gorbachev. Visit in the late afternoon when the floodlights come on—the way light catches the columns completely transforms the atmosphere.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Memorial to the Murdered Jews of Europe occupies 19,000 square meters between Brandenburg Gate and Potsdamer Platz, on what was once the death strip of the Wall. New York architect Peter Eisenman placed 2,711 concrete stelae of varying heights on gently undulating ground—as you walk toward the center, the blocks rise above your shoulders and the ground drops away, creating a deliberate sense of oppression. Inaugurated on May 10, 2005, the memorial includes an 800-square-meter underground information center: reading aloud all the victims' names would take 6 years, 7 months, and 27 days.

⏱ 75 min
3

Head to Friedrichshain to walk along the East Side Gallery, the longest remaining section of the Berlin Wall: 1,316 meters along Mühlenstraße, between Ostbahnhof and the Oberbaumbrücke bridge. Starting in January 1990, 118 artists from 21 countries came to paint the eastern side of the Wall. The most famous murals: the fraternal kiss between Brezhnev and Honecker by Dmitri Vrubel, inscribed with "My God, help me survive this deadly love"; and the blue Trabant breaking through concrete, painted by Birgit Kinder. The entire gallery was restored in 2009 for the 20th anniversary of the Wall's fall.

⏱ 60 min
4

Karl-Marx-Allee, a 2-kilometer Stalinist boulevard laid out between 1952 and 1960, offers a concrete glimpse into East German monumental urbanism. The buildings lining it—in the so-called "Soviet wedding cake" style—are now protected as historic monuments and represent one of the best-preserved Communist-era architectural ensembles in Europe.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Tiergarten park stretches across 210 hectares in the heart of Berlin—it was the royal hunting ground before being converted into a public park in the 19th century. Rent a bike to cross it from end to end, or push on to the former Tempelhof Airport, now converted into a vast public space where Berliners picnic on the old runways.

⏱ 90 min

On Sundays, Mauerpark in Prenzlauer Berg hosts one of Berlin's largest flea markets, with a giant open-air karaoke session in the afternoon. More compact, the Arkonaplatz market is perfect for hunting down GDR-era objects and vintage furniture.

⏱ 90 min

Neuruppin

★★★☆☆
1

Stop in Oranienburg, 35 kilometers north of Berlin, to visit the Sachsenhausen Memorial. Built in 1936 by prisoners from other camps, it was designed as the SS "model camp": its triangular architecture allowed a handful of guards to monitor the entire complex from a single central tower. Between 1936 and 1945, more than 200,000 people were interned here and approximately 84,000 died. This is where Himmler established his headquarters and where the first methods of mass extermination, including gas vans, were tested. After 1945, the Soviets reused the site until 1950 to imprison their own political opponents.

⏱ 180 min
2

Neuruppin, nicknamed Fontanestadt, is the birthplace of two of Prussia's greatest cultural figures: architect Karl Friedrich Schinkel, born March 13, 1781, and writer Theodor Fontane, born December 30, 1819. Fontane's father's pharmacy still stands on the central square. A devastating fire razed nearly the entire town in 1787—Schinkel was six years old, and his father, a superintendent, died of exhaustion shortly after. The town was rebuilt between 1788 and 1796 in a rigorous neoclassical style that makes it one of the best-preserved classicist ensembles in northern Germany. Fontane himself thought the town resembled a suit too large for the wearer.

⏱ 45 min
3

Walk to the Klosterkirche Sankt Trinitatis by the lakeside—it was the first Dominican monastery east of the Elbe, founded in 1246. Its two tall neo-Gothic towers, built between 1904 and 1907 from plans inspired by Schinkel, are visible from the entire lakeside promenade. Just behind it, the old high school where both Schinkel and Fontane were students bears the inscription on its pediment: CIVIBUS AEVI FUTURI—"To the citizens of the future."

⏱ 40 min
4

The Tempelgarten was laid out for Crown Prince Frederick during his stay in Neuruppin between 1732 and 1736. Here, Georg Wenzeslaus von Knobelsdorff—who would later build Sanssouci in Potsdam—created his very first work: a round temple completed in 1735.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Stroll along the Ruppiner See, Brandenburg's longest lake at 14 kilometers. Excursion boats sail in summer under the name Kronprinz Friedrich, and the view from the water of the Klosterkirche's twin towers is well worth the trip.

⏱ 60 min

Plau Am See

★★★★☆
1

Plau am See received its town charter in the 13th century and retains a compact historic center with medieval lanes and half-timbered houses around the river port. The town hall, designed in Dutch Renaissance style, is one of the most striking buildings on the central square.

⏱ 40 min
2

Climb up to the ruins of Plau Castle, which still dominate the center from their promontory. Two steps away, the Gothic church of Sankt Marien deserves a stop for its distinctive bell tower and austere interiors typical of northern Germany.

⏱ 35 min
3

The Hubbrücke is the town's liveliest spot: this historic lift bridge spans the Elde River right in the center and regularly raises to let pleasure boats pass. Settle into a terrace table by the quay to watch the maneuver—it's Plau am See's essential ritual.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The Plauer See, Mecklenburg's third-largest lake at 38 square kilometers, is surrounded by reeds where cranes and ospreys nest. The bike path around it extends for 55 kilometers—a 10-kilometer section toward Bad Stuer passes the lift bridge and follows wild shorelines.

⏱ 120 min

Schwerin

★★★★☆
1

Schwerin Castle sits on an island between Lake Schwerin and the Burgsee, connected to the shore by a bridge. The first traces of a fortification date to 973, mentioned by Arab merchant Ibrahim ibn Ya'qub. In 1160, Henry the Lion seized control of the Slavic fort. Its current appearance—953 rooms, onion domes, grand gilded cupola—results from a neo-Renaissance reconstruction between 1843 and 1857, supervised by architects Demmler and later Stüler at the request of Grand Duke Frederick Francis II. Approach it head-on from the town across the bridge: this is when you understand why it's often compared to Neuschwanstein. The castle was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage site in 2024.

⏱ 90 min
2

Schwerin Cathedral is a remarkable example of northern German brick Gothic. Construction began in the 13th century and continued into the 15th century. Its 117-meter spire dominates the entire town—climb it for an unobstructed view of the twelve lakes surrounding Schwerin.

⏱ 40 min
3

The Schwerin State Museum, housed in a neoclassical building facing the Old Garden, holds a collection of 17th and 18th-century Flemish and Dutch painting: works by Rubens, Rembrandt, and Frans Hals hang alongside German Romantic paintings. It's one of the strongest provincial collections in northern Germany.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Walk along the shores of Lake Schwerin and through the Schlossgarten, the baroque garden behind the castle. The town encompasses twelve lakes within its perimeter—locals cycle the circuit.

⏱ 60 min

Kuhlungsborn

★★★★☆
1

Make a stop in Wismar en route—the Hanseatic old town has been a UNESCO World Heritage site since 2002. The Marktplatz, one of northern Germany's largest, is surrounded by red brick Gothic facades. The town belonged to Sweden from 1648 to 1803, which gave it a rare hybrid German-Scandinavian architecture in the region.

⏱ 60 min
2

Kühlungsborn is Mecklenburg's largest spa resort, founded in the late 19th century when three independent coastal villages merged in 1938. The promenade follows the sea for several kilometers, lined with bathing villas with wooden facades typical of Bäderarchitektur style. Descend to the beach through one of the access points between the streets—fine sand stretches as far as the eye can see.

⏱ 45 min
3

The pier at Kühlungsborn East measures 240 meters and extends into the Baltic Sea—it's the best spot to watch ferries offshore and observe sunsets over the water.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Rent a bike and head into the Kühlung Forest, 133 hectares of beech and pine that extend directly behind the resort. You'll find Bronze Age burial mounds, enormous glacial erratics deposited by ice sheets, and paths that emerge onto coastal panoramas. The Strandkorb—the woven wicker beach chair typical of Baltic beaches—awaits you on the sand for your afternoon break.

⏱ 120 min

Bastorf Lighthouse stands on Cape Bukspitze, a few kilometers west of Kühlungsborn. At 20.8 meters tall, it's one of Germany's smallest lighthouses but one of the best positioned—it towers on a cliff at a remarkable height above the sea.

⏱ 60 min

Rostock

★★★★☆
1

Rostock was the former Hanseatic capital in the north—its Lübeck town rights were granted on June 24, 1218. The Kröpeliner Strasse, the main pedestrian thoroughfare, connects the medieval Kröpeliner Tor gate to the Neuer Markt: walk along it to see the red brick facades characteristic of Hanseatic Gothic parade past, rebuilt after World War II bombing destroyed about half the city.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Marienkirche is the largest of Rostock's three historic churches, built in brick in the mid-13th century in Gothic style. Its masterpiece is a 15th-century astronomical clock—the only one of its kind still running with its original mechanism. It indicates not only the time, but also moon phases, zodiac signs, and the position of the sun.

⏱ 45 min
3

Rostock University, founded in 1419, is Germany's third-oldest still operating and northern Europe's oldest after St Andrews. Its main building on Universitätsplatz sets the tone for the entire city.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Head to Warnemünde, Rostock's coastal district 16 kilometers north of the center. Follow the Alter Strom—the old river arm that serves as a harbor for sailboats, fishermen, and excursion boats—to the lighthouse built in 1897, clad in white enameled bricks and 30 meters tall. Warnemünde beach stretches for more than 3 kilometers, with Strandkörbe lined up on the fine sand.

⏱ 120 min
