New Delhi

★★★★★
1

Start with the Red Fort (Lal Qila), commissioned by Shah Jahan in 1639 and completed in nine years. Its red sandstone walls soar up to 33 meters on the city side - you'll spot them from afar as you approach from Chandni Chowk. Inside, don't miss the Diwan-i-Khas (Hall of Private Audiences), which once housed the famous Peacock Throne, stolen by the Persians in 1739. The Nahr-i-Behisht, a canal that flowed through the pavilions, evoked the Quranic paradise. Allow 90 minutes to tour the 120-acre complex, a UNESCO site since 2007.

⏱ 90 min
2

Five minutes' walk from the Red Fort, Jama Masjid awaits - India's largest mosque. Shah Jahan built it between 1650 and 1656 using 5,000 artisans, at a cost of one million rupees. Its inner courtyard can hold 25,000 worshippers. If you're up for it, climb the 130 steps of the southern minaret - the panoramic view sweeps across Old Delhi all the way to Connaught Place, an alignment deliberately created by British architect Lutyens. Remove your shoes and cover your shoulders before entering.

⏱ 60 min
3

Push on to Chandni Chowk, Delhi's oldest market, designed in 1650 by Princess Jahanara, Shah Jahan's favorite daughter. The name means 'moonlight square' - a central pool once reflected the moon. Today the canal is gone but the chaos remains. Lose yourself in Khari Baoli, Asia's largest spice market, where mountains of turmeric and chili make your eyes sting. Dariba Kalan has been the jewelers' street since Mughal times. Parathe Wali Gali has served stuffed parathas for generations - it's the obligatory street food stop.

⏱ 90 min
4

Walking up Chandni Chowk, stop at Gurudwara Sis Ganj Sahib, built at the spot where the ninth Sikh guru, Tegh Bahadur, was executed by the Mughals in 1675 for refusing to convert to Islam. The temple welcomes everyone - you can watch devotees at prayer and, if you wish, join the langar (free communal meal). Cover your head with a scarf provided at the entrance.

⏱ 40 min
5

End the day at Raj Ghat, Gandhi's memorial, on the banks of the Yamuna. A simple black marble platform marks the spot where the Mahatma was cremated on January 31, 1948, one day after his assassination. An eternal flame burns constantly. The place is stark and moving - plan to remove your shoes. The surrounding park is planted with trees gifted by world leaders.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Take a cycle-rickshaw ride through the lanes of Old Delhi - it's the best way to navigate the maze of bazaars without exhausting yourself. The drivers know the shortcuts and will show you hidden corners invisible on foot. Negotiate the price before climbing aboard.

⏱ 45 min

Sample Old Delhi street food: morning chole bhature (chickpeas and fried bread), freshly fried jalebi in Chandni Chowk, and smoky kebabs from the alley behind Jama Masjid. Bring your appetite - and napkins. If you're cautious about hygiene, target stalls packed with locals.

⏱ 60 min

In the evening, catch the sound and light show at the Red Fort, which traces Mughal history and the struggle for independence. The show lasts about an hour and takes place after sunset. Check schedules by season - in winter it starts around 6:30pm.

⏱ 60 min

New Delhi

★★★★★
1

Switch to New Delhi. Begin with Humayun's Tomb, the subcontinent's first garden-tomb and the Taj Mahal's prototype. Built in 1570 by Empress Bega Begum for her husband, the second Mughal emperor, it blends red sandstone and white marble in a Persian four-quartered garden (charbagh). The central dome rises to 47 meters. Inside chambers hold the tombs of more than 150 Mughal family members - hence the nickname 'dormitory of the Mughals.' A UNESCO site since 1993, beautifully restored by the Aga Khan Trust.

⏱ 90 min
2

Head to Qutub Minar, the world's tallest brick minaret at 72.5 meters. Construction began in 1199 under Qutb-ud-din Aibak, after his victory over Delhi's last Hindu king, and was completed by his successor Iltutmish around 1220. The tower has five stories, 379 steps (closed to the public since a 1981 accident that killed 45 people in a stampede), and tapers from 14.3m diameter at the base to 2.7m at the top. Don't miss the 4th-century iron pillar in the courtyard - it's resisted rust for 1,600 years and no one quite knows why.

⏱ 75 min
3

Pass by India Gate, Delhi's triumphal arch, 42 meters tall, built in 1931 to honor the 70,000 Indian soldiers who died in World War I. Since 2022, an eternal flame burns beneath the nearby canopy, the National War Memorial. In the evening the monument is illuminated and the esplanade transforms into a family promenade spot - join the Delhiites picnicking on the lawn.

⏱ 40 min
4

If you still have energy, detour to the Lotus Temple, a Bahá'í House of Worship completed in 1986, whose flower-shaped structure has 27 white marble petals. Iranian-Canadian architect Fariborz Sahba drew inspiration from the lotus, a symbol common to Hinduism, Buddhism, and Islam. Inside is a vast silent meditation space - no sermons, no religious images. Enter, sit down, and enjoy the calm after Delhi's chaos.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Stroll through Lodhi Gardens, a 36-hectare park housing tombs from the Sayyid and Lodi dynasties (15th-16th centuries) amid manicured lawns. Delhiites jog here in the morning - it's the perfect spot to breathe between visits.

⏱ 45 min

Explore Connaught Place, the commercial heart of New Delhi, a set of white colonnaded buildings arranged in concentric circles, designed by the British in the 1930s. The restaurants and cafés lining the arcades are perfect for a lunch break. Head down to the underground Palika Bazaar if you fancy Indian-style shopping.

⏱ 60 min

Late in the day, visit the Nizamuddin Dargah, Delhi's most revered Sufi shrine, dating from the 14th century. On Thursday evenings, qawwali musicians sing mystical poetry in the courtyard - the atmosphere is intense and hypnotic. Cover your head as a sign of respect.

⏱ 60 min

Amritsar

★★★☆☆
1

You arrive in Amritsar by train (count around 6 hours from Delhi on the Shatabdi Express). Drop your bags and head straight for the Golden Temple - Harmandir Sahib. It's Sikhism's holiest site, and probably one of the world's most beautiful religious buildings. The temple sits at the center of a 150m-by-150m sacred pool (the Amrit Sarovar, 'pool of nectar'), connected to land by a 60-meter marble causeway. Maharaja Ranjit Singh covered the upper stories with 750kg of pure gold in the 1830s. Four entrances symbolize openness to all castes and religions. Arrive early in the morning to watch sunrise on the golden dome - the reflection in the water is breathtaking.

⏱ 120 min
2

Right next to the Golden Temple, push through the narrow gate of Jallianwala Bagh. This is where on April 13, 1919, British General Dyer ordered his troops to fire on an unarmed crowd of thousands gathered for the Baisakhi festival. About 379 dead officially - likely far more. Bullet marks are still visible on the walls, and the well into which dozens jumped to escape the gunfire is preserved. The Flame of Liberty memorial, a 9-meter red sandstone pylon, was inaugurated in 1961. The place is stark and poignant - take time to read the panels.

⏱ 60 min
3

Don't miss the Partition Museum, housed in Amritsar's old town hall. It traces the 1947 division of India and Pakistan through testimonies, personal objects, and photographs. It's one of the few museums worldwide dedicated to this event, which displaced 15 million people and killed between one and two million.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Late afternoon, drive to the Wagah border (28km, 45 min). Every evening at sunset, Indian and Pakistani border guards perform the 'Beating Retreat' ceremony - a surreal spectacle of high kicks, exaggerated martial postures, and glares, followed by the simultaneous lowering of both flags. 25,000 spectators on the Indian side, a soccer stadium atmosphere. Arrive at least 90 minutes early to get a good seat.

⏱ 150 min

Eat at the Golden Temple langar - the world's largest free kitchen. Every day over 50,000 meals are prepared and served by volunteers: dal, chapati, vegetables, and kheer. Everyone is welcome, without distinction. Sit in rows on the floor and let yourself be served. The experience is as spiritual as it is gastronomic.

⏱ 45 min

If you return from Wagah in the evening, go back to the Golden Temple at night. The illuminated building reflects in the pool - it's even more magical than by day. Chants from the Guru Granth Sahib echo in the cool air and devotees circle the pool. A moment of absolute peace after the tumult of the Wagah ceremony.

⏱ 45 min

Dharamshala

★★★☆☆
1

Dharamsala is about 5 hours by road from Amritsar - the road gradually climbs toward the Himalayan foothills. Your destination is McLeod Ganj, 'little Lhasa,' perched at 1,770m altitude. Since 1959, this is where the Dalai Lama has lived, after fleeing Chinese-occupied Tibet. Begin with the Tsuglagkhang complex, the spiritual heart of the Tibetan community in exile. The main temple houses a 3-meter golden statue of Buddha Shakyamuni, flanked by Avalokiteshvara and Padmasambhava. The atmosphere is peaceful - monks in crimson robes, prayer wheels spinning, whispers of mantras.

⏱ 90 min
2

Right next door, the Kalachakra Temple contains frescoes depicting the 722 deities of the mandala, painted over three years by three master painters under the Dalai Lama's personal direction. Photography is forbidden, but the detail level of the mandalas is stunning.

⏱ 30 min
3

Visit the Tibet Museum at the complex entrance. On two floors, it traces Tibet's history as an independent nation, the Chinese occupation since 1949, and exile. Testimonies from political prisoners and archival photos are deeply moving. Allow at least 45 minutes.

⏱ 45 min
4

Walk down to Namgyal Monastery, the Dalai Lama's personal monastery, founded in 1564. If you're there around 1:30pm (except Sundays), you can watch the monks' debates in the courtyard - a fascinating spectacle where arguments are punctuated with theatrical hand-clapping. The monks are usually delighted to chat with visitors.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walk to Bhagsu Waterfall, about 2km from McLeod Ganj. The trail crosses Bhagsu village and climbs toward the waterfall through rocks. Bring walking shoes. At the top, several cafés with valley views serve momos (Tibetan dumplings) and butter tea.

⏱ 90 min

Wander McLeod Ganj's lanes - Tibetan shops sell singing bowls, thangkas (fabric paintings), woolen shawls, and turquoise jewelry. Cafés serve authentic Tibetan cuisine: thukpa (noodle soup), steamed or fried momos, and the famous yak butter tea. A world apart from the Indian plains.

⏱ 60 min

Take a thangka painting or Tibetan cooking class - several centers in McLeod Ganj offer half-day workshops. It's a concrete way to touch Tibetan culture beyond surface tourism.

⏱ 120 min

Manali

★★★☆☆
1

The Dharamsala to Manali road is long (about 8 hours) but spectacular - it winds through the Kangra and Kullu valleys, between apple orchards, turquoise rivers, and snowy peaks. Plan stops for the views. Once in Manali, settle in and start with Hadimba Devi Temple, nestled in a cedar forest 2km from town. Built in 1553 by Maharaja Bahadur Singh, it's a four-tiered wooden and stone pagoda temple, dedicated to Hadimba, a demoness from the Mahabharata who became a goddess after marrying warrior Bhima. The architecture is unique in India - the wooden doors are carved with detailed mythological figures.

⏱ 60 min
2

Climb up to Old Manali, 2km upstream from the modern town. This stone and wood village has kept its Himalayan charm despite the influx of trendy cafés and guesthouses. Traditional wooden and slate houses cling to the slope, and lanes open onto clear views of the peaks. This is where the hippie community settled in the 1970s - the vibe remains laid-back.

⏱ 60 min
3

Detour to Vashisht, a thermal village 3km from Manali. Natural hot springs feed public pools where locals and travelers bathe side by side. The Vashisht temple, dedicated to the Vedic sage of the same name, is a fine example of Himachali wooden architecture with detailed carvings on the lintels.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walk along the Beas River, which flows through the Kullu valley. In season (May to July, September to October), operators offer whitewater rafting between Pirdi and Jhiri - a 14km stretch with class II to III rapids, perfect for a first experience.

⏱ 120 min

Taste Himachali specialties: siddu (steamed bread stuffed with nuts and spices), madra (chickpeas in yogurt sauce), and fresh trout caught from the Beas, grilled or curried. Old Manali restaurants have the best menus.

⏱ 60 min

Late in the day, climb to Manu Temple, dedicated to sage Manu, considered in Hindu mythology as humanity's creator - Manali takes its name from him. The temple is modest but the path offers clear valley views.

⏱ 40 min

Manali

★★★☆☆
1

Second day in Manali, dedicated to the heights. Head to Solang Valley (14km, 30 min), a natural amphitheater surrounded by snowy peaks. Depending on the season, you'll find paragliding, zorbing, and quad rides (summer), or skiing and sledding (winter). The cable car takes you up to 3,000m altitude with plunging views over the Kullu valley. Arrive early - crowds show up after 10am.

⏱ 120 min
2

If the pass is open (May to November), push on to Rohtang Pass at 3,980m altitude - the name means 'pile of corpses' in Persian, recalling travelers who died attempting the crossing. The 51km road from Manali crosses landscapes that change dramatically: deodar forests, then alpine meadows, then bare rock and snow. At the summit, the view embraces the Pir Panjal glaciers on one side and Lahaul valley on the other. Bring warm clothes even in summer. A permit is mandatory - request it online the day before.

⏱ 180 min
3

Alternative if Rohtang is closed: take the Atal Tunnel, the world's longest high-altitude road tunnel (9.02km), inaugurated in 2020. It connects Kullu valley to Lahaul valley in 30 minutes instead of 4 to 6 hours via the pass. On the other side, Sissu village and its waterfall offer a first glimpse of desert Lahaul - a striking contrast with Manali's greenery.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Try tandem paragliding from Solang Valley or Marhi - Himalayan thermals carry you above the valley for 15 to 20 minutes. The sensation of soaring facing the 6,000-meter massif is unforgettable. Operators offer the flight with GoPro video included.

⏱ 60 min

Stop at Vashisht hot springs on the way back if you didn't visit them yesterday. After a day at altitude, a bath in naturally hot sulfurous water is the best remedy for fatigue. Locals come at dawn - late afternoon you'll have more space.

⏱ 45 min

Chandigarh

★★☆☆☆
1

Chandigarh is a transit stop between Manali and Rishikesh (310km from Manali, about 8 hours drive). But the city is worth a pause: it's the only Indian city planned by Le Corbusier in the 1950s, after Partition. The Capitol Complex, UNESCO-listed in 2016, groups the High Court, Secretariat, and Assembly - a monumental raw concrete ensemble that startles in the Indian context. The Open Hand, a 26-meter-tall sculpture, turns freely with the wind and symbolizes peace.

⏱ 75 min
2

Don't miss the Rock Garden, the work of one man - Nek Chand, a road inspector who secretly built a sculpture garden from recycled urban waste (pottery shards, construction debris, broken bangles) on 16 hectares of government land. Discovered in 1975, the site was threatened with demolition, but popular support saved it. Today it's India's second most visited site after the Taj Mahal. The mosaic figurines lined up by the hundreds create a striking visual effect.

⏱ 75 min
3

If you have an hour free, walk along Sukhna Lake, an artificial lake created in 1958 by building a dam across a seasonal stream. Locals take their evening stroll here, and boats are available. The late-day light on the water with the Shivaliks in the background is superb.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Take a tour of Sector 17, Chandigarh's pedestrian shopping center, designed by Le Corbusier. The arcades and plazas recall a European city transplanted to India. It's a good place to eat - Punjabi cuisine here is among the country's best.

⏱ 60 min

Visit the Rose Garden (Zakir Hussain Rose Garden), which spreads over 12 hectares and houses more than 1,600 rose varieties. It's Asia's largest rose garden - and in season (February-March), the spectacle is enchanting.

⏱ 40 min

Rishikesh

★★★☆☆
1

Rishikesh - the 'world capital of yoga' - sits where the Ganges leaves the Himalayan foothills for the plains. The atmosphere is radically different from Delhi's chaos: here cows, sadhus in orange, and backpackers in yoga pants coexist on the ghats. Start by crossing Ram Jhula, the 137-meter suspension bridge built in 1986, linking ashrams on both banks. The view of the turquoise Ganges, framed by forested hills, sets the tone.

⏱ 30 min
2

Visit the Beatles Ashram (officially Chaurasi Kutia), Maharishi Mahesh Yogi's former ashram where John Lennon, Paul McCartney, George Harrison, and Ringo Starr stayed in 1968 to study Transcendental Meditation. Abandoned for decades, the site has reopened to visitors and the ruined buildings are covered with colorful graffiti and murals honoring the Fab Four. They composed much of the White Album here. The place is strangely peaceful despite its fame.

⏱ 60 min
3

Walk down to Swarg Ashram, the pedestrian quarter on the Ganges' left bank. No cars here - only lanes lined with ashrams, temples, and small vegetarian restaurants. Bharat Mandir, Rishikesh's oldest temple, is dedicated to Vishnu and allegedly dates from the 12th century.

⏱ 45 min
4

Late afternoon, join Triveni Ghat or Parmarth Niketan for Ganga Aarti - the evening ceremony where dozens of priests wave large oil lamps toward the river while mantras resound. Hundreds of small floating lamps (diyas) are placed on the water. The atmosphere is intense and deeply moving - arrive 30 minutes early to find a good spot on the steps.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Go rafting on the Ganges - Rishikesh is India's number one spot for this activity. Several routes from 9 to 36km are available, with class II to IV rapids. The classic Shivpuri to Rishikesh stretch (16km, 3-4h) passes the 'Roller Coaster' and 'Golf Course' rapids. Book the day before with local operators.

⏱ 210 min

Take a one or two-hour yoga class at one of countless ashrams. Even if you've never practiced, this is the dream place to start - beginner classes are everywhere. The International Yoga Festival happens every March at Parmarth Niketan.

⏱ 90 min

Haridwar

★★☆☆☆
1

Haridwar is only 25km from Rishikesh but the vibe is totally different - here, no hippies or trendy cafés. It's one of Hinduism's seven holiest cities, where the Ganges officially enters the plains. The city's heart is Har Ki Pauri, the most sacred ghat, where a footprint of Vishnu is said to be carved in stone. This is where millions of pilgrims come for ritual bathing. Every 12 years, Haridwar hosts the Kumbh Mela, the world's largest human gathering - over 100 million people in 2021.

⏱ 75 min
2

Take the cable car (or walk up, 45 min) to Mansa Devi Temple, perched on a Shivalik ridge 300 meters above the city. The panoramic view sweeps over Haridwar, the Ganges, and the Gangetic plains stretching to the horizon. The temple is dedicated to goddess Mansa, who grants wishes - you'll see red threads tied by devotees.

⏱ 60 min
3

On the opposite hill, Chandi Devi Temple (also cable-car accessible) allegedly dates from the 8th century and was founded by philosopher Adi Shankaracharya. The walk up (3km) crosses a forest with valley views - pleasant in the morning when it's cool.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Attend Ganga Aarti at Har Ki Pauri at sunset - it's older and more 'authentic' than Rishikesh's, less packaged for tourists. Thousands of devotees crowd the ghats, temple bells ring, incense smoke mingles with river mist. Place a diya (floating lamp) on the Ganges and watch it drift in the current.

⏱ 60 min

Wander through Bara Bazaar, Haridwar's main market. You'll find vessels for carrying Ganges water home (a sacred souvenir for Hindus), rudraksha (prayer beads), deity statues, and traditional sweets. The atmosphere is frenetic and colorful.

⏱ 45 min

Sample Haridwar's strict vegetarian cuisine - the entire city is vegetarian (alcohol and meat are banned). Dhabas (eateries) around Har Ki Pauri serve complete thalis, kachori, and thick lassi. Haridwar's chole bhature is famous across North India.

⏱ 45 min

Agra

★★★★★
1

Long drive from Haridwar (about 7 hours). Arrive in Agra early afternoon if you leave early. Drop your things and rush to the Taj Mahal - preferably late afternoon when the golden light sets the white marble ablaze. Shah Jahan had it built between 1632 and 1653 in memory of his wife Mumtaz Mahal, who died giving birth to their fourteenth child. 20,000 workers and 1,000 elephants labored for 22 years. The central dome rises to 73 meters, and the semi-precious stone inlays (carnelian, lapis lazuli, turquoise, jade) in white marble are mind-blowingly delicate. UNESCO-listed and voted one of the New Seven Wonders of the World in 2007.

⏱ 120 min
2

2.5km upstream along the Yamuna, Agra Fort is the city's other jewel. Built in red sandstone by Emperor Akbar from 1565, then embellished in white marble by Shah Jahan, it served as the Mughal imperial residence for generations. This is where Shah Jahan spent his last eight years, imprisoned by his own son Aurangzeb, with only a view of the Taj Mahal he'd built for his wife. From the octagonal tower of Musamman Burj, the perspective on the Taj is heartbreaking.

⏱ 90 min
3

Late afternoon, cross the river to Mehtab Bagh ('moonlight garden'), situated directly opposite the Taj Mahal across the Yamuna. It's the best vantage point to photograph the monument at sunset, without the main site's crowds. The garden has been restored and the manicured lawns offer a peaceful setting.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

If you're an early riser, return to the Taj Mahal at dawn - the gates open at sunrise and morning mist over the Yamuna creates an unreal atmosphere. Crowds are much smaller than during the day. This is when professional photographers choose to shoot.

⏱ 90 min

Wander through Kinari Bazaar, Agra's old market near Jama Masjid. Artisans work marble inlaid with semi-precious stones (pietra dura) using the same techniques employed to decorate the Taj Mahal. You'll see workshops open to the street where artisans cut, polish, and inlay by hand.

⏱ 60 min

Taste petha, Agra's signature sweet - a translucent candy made from caramelized white gourd, flavored with rose, saffron, or coconut. Shops around the Taj offer dozens of varieties.

⏱ 20 min

Agra

★★★★★
1

Day dedicated to Agra's surroundings. Start with Fatehpur Sikri (37km, 45 min), the 'city of victory' built by Emperor Akbar between 1571 and 1585 as the new Mughal capital. The site has been UNESCO-listed since 1986. Akbar had it built after visiting Sufi saint Sheikh Salim Chisti, who predicted an heir's birth - a prediction that came true. But the city was abandoned after just 14 years, probably due to water shortage. Result: a perfectly preserved red sandstone ghost city, without later centuries' additions.

⏱ 60 min
2

At Fatehpur Sikri, don't miss the Panch Mahal, a five-story palace entirely open to the winds, built on the Persian windcatcher tower model. The Diwan-i-Khas (Hall of Private Audiences) is famous for its central pillar carved in Gujarati style, from which four walkways branch - Akbar received representatives of different religions here for his famous interfaith debates. Buland Darwaza (Victory Gate), 54 meters high, is one of the world's largest entrance gates.

⏱ 60 min
3

Within the mosque enclosure, Sheikh Salim Chisti's tomb is a white marble jewel inlaid with jalis (pierced stone screens) of extraordinary delicacy. Childless women come to tie red threads making a wish - the tradition has lasted over four centuries.

⏱ 30 min
4

Returning toward Agra, stop at Itimad-ud-Daulah's tomb, nicknamed the 'Baby Taj.' Built between 1622 and 1628 by Empress Nur Jahan for her father, it's the first Mughal monument entirely covered in white marble with semi-precious stone inlays - the pietra dura technique that would reach its peak at the Taj Mahal a few years later. The surrounding garden is far less crowded than the Taj and equally photogenic.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Visit a pietra dura (marble marquetry) craft workshop in Agra's artisan quarter. Artisans will show you techniques inherited from Mughal times - cutting, polishing, and inlaying lapis lazuli, malachite, and carnelian in marble. Count 30 to 45 minutes for a complete demonstration.

⏱ 45 min

If you have time, push on to Akbar's tomb at Sikandra (10km north of Agra). The emperor who advocated religious tolerance designed his own mausoleum - an astonishing mix of Hindu, Muslim, Buddhist, and Christian elements. The park houses herds of deer and langur monkeys.

Orchha

★★★☆☆
1

Long road from Agra (about 7 hours) but Orchha is worth every kilometer. This city founded in the 16th century by Rajput chief Rudra Pratap Singh, on the banks of the Betwa, is one of central India's hidden gems. A UNESCO World Heritage candidate, it has kept a charm that Jaipur or Udaipur lost long ago. Start with Jehangir Mahal, built by King Bir Singh Deo to welcome Mughal emperor Jahangir. Its circular towers, fluted domes, and jali balconies blend Mughal and Rajput styles with rare elegance. Climb to the roof - the view over the river and cenotaphs is sumptuous.

⏱ 60 min
2

Right next door, the older Raja Mahal preserves wall frescoes of surprising beauty for a building of this era. Peacocks, elephants, and geometric motifs dance across the Darbar Hall walls. Narrow passages and spiral staircases add to the feeling of exploration - you often have rooms to yourself.

⏱ 45 min
3

Walk down to the cenotaphs (chhatris) along the Betwa - 14 funerary monuments honoring Bundela kings, built on high stone platforms with panchayatan-style domes. Their reflection in the river water at sunset is one of India's most photogenic images. Climb to the top of Bir Singh Deo's cenotaph for a panorama over all of Orchha. Bonus: a colony of long-billed vultures (critically endangered species) nests in the structures.

⏱ 60 min
4

Visit Ram Raja Temple, India's only temple where Rama is worshipped as a king rather than a god. Uniformed guards render him military honors every day. The neighboring Chaturbhuj temple offers magnificent views from its accessible roof, and Laxmi Narayan temple (1662) preserves detailed wall frescoes on its interior walls.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Attend the Jehangir Mahal sound and light show in the evening, which traces Bundela dynasty history. The palaces illuminated at night create a magical atmosphere.

⏱ 60 min

Rent a bicycle and tour Orchha at your own pace - the town is compact enough to explore everything by pedaling. Paths along the Betwa cross bucolic landscapes with palaces in the background.

⏱ 90 min

Khajuraho

★★★☆☆
1

Khajuraho is about 4 hours from Orchha. UNESCO-listed since 1986, it's one of India's most extraordinary sites. Of the 85 temples built by the Chandela dynasty between 950 and 1050 AD, 25 have survived - and their sculptures are stunningly refined. Start with the Western Group, the most spectacular. Kandariya Mahadeva temple is the largest and most elaborate: its walls are covered with nearly 900 sculptures depicting deities, celestial nymphs (apsaras), and the famous entwined couples (mithuna). Contrary to belief, erotic scenes represent only about 10% of sculptures - the rest shows daily life, war, music, and dance.

⏱ 90 min
2

Lakshmana temple, dedicated to Vishnu, is one of the best preserved. Built under King Yashovarman around 950, it rests on a high platform adorned with a continuous frieze of sculptures - elephants, horses, battle scenes, and court life. Take time to walk completely around the temple to appreciate the sculpture progression, from the earthly world at the base toward the divine at the summit.

⏱ 45 min
3

Don't neglect the Eastern Group, 20 minutes' walk away. The Jain temples of Parsvanath and Adinath are less famous but equally finely carved, with floral motifs and apsaras of remarkable grace. The contrast between Hindu and Jain temples side by side testifies to the Chandela era's religious tolerance.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Attend the sound and light show within the Western Group enclosure - it recounts Chandela history and temple construction. The show lasts about 50 minutes and takes place after sunset. The dramatic nighttime lighting of the temples alone is worth the trip.

⏱ 60 min

Explore Khajuraho's small Archaeological Museum, which displays sculptures recovered from destroyed temple sites. Some pieces match the quality of those still in place on the temples.

⏱ 40 min

Walk through Khajuraho village late afternoon - it remains surprisingly rural and quiet despite tourism. Local markets sell stone and wood carvings that imitate (with varying success) the temple style.

⏱ 40 min

Varanasi

★★★★☆
1

Domestic flight Khajuraho → Varanasi (about 1h30 with stopover or direct if available). Varanasi - or Benares, or Kashi - is the world's oldest continuously inhabited city. Nothing prepares you for the sensory shock of the ghats along the Ganges: Manikarnika's funeral pyres burning 24/7, sadhus covered in ash, buffaloes bathing, fishing boats and kids playing cricket - all at once, within 50 meters. Start by descending Dashashwamedh Ghat steps, the main ghat, and let yourself be absorbed by the spectacle.

⏱ 60 min
2

Lose yourself in the old city's lanes (galis) - a labyrinth so narrow even rickshaws can't pass. Shops overflow with Benares silk saris, jewelry, colored powders, and sweets. You'll encounter tiny temples at every corner, sacred cows, funeral processions, and playing children. It's India's most organized chaos.

⏱ 75 min
3

Visit Kashi Vishwanath Temple (Golden Temple), one of the 12 most sacred Shiva jyotirlingas. The current temple dates from 1780, built by Queen Ahilyabai Holkar of Indore, after the previous one was destroyed by Aurangzeb. The dome is covered with 820kg of gold donated by Maharaja Ranjit Singh. Access for non-Hindus may be restricted to certain areas - inquire beforehand.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In the evening, attend Ganga Aarti at Dashashwamedh Ghat. Seven young priests in saffron robes perform synchronized movements with multi-tiered oil lamps, while mantras and bells echo in the incense-laden air. The ceremony lasts 45 minutes and begins at sunset (6pm in winter, 7pm in summer). Arrive 1 hour early for a good spot on the steps, or book a boat on the Ganges to watch the spectacle from the water - it's even more impressive.

⏱ 90 min

Taste silver-leaf lassi in old city shops, morning kachori sabzi, and chena dahi vada (lentil fritters with yogurt). Varanasi street food is legendary - and entirely vegetarian in the old city.

⏱ 45 min

Varanasi

★★★★☆
1

Wake before dawn for a sunrise boat ride on the Ganges - this is Varanasi's magic moment. Mist rises over the river, first golden rays touch the ghats, devotees descend the steps for ritual ablutions, pyres smoke gently. Your boatman will row you along the 84 ghats over about 5km - each has its specialty and story. Manikarnika is the main cremation ghat, where pyres never go out. Stay respectful and don't photograph cremations.

⏱ 90 min
2

After breakfast, take an auto-rickshaw to Sarnath (10km, 30 min), the place where Buddha gave his first sermon after enlightenment - an event known as 'Setting in Motion the Wheel of Dharma.' The Dhamek Stupa, a massive 43-meter-high cylinder adorned with Gupta-era floral motifs, marks the exact spot. The Ashoka pillar that stood here bore the famous four-lion capital that became India's national emblem (visible on every rupee note).

⏱ 60 min
3

Sarnath's archaeological museum preserves the original of Ashoka's four-lion capital (4th century BC), along with Buddhist sculptures of extraordinary grace - notably a Gupta-era Buddha considered one of the world's finest. Allow at least 45 minutes.

⏱ 45 min
4

Back in Varanasi, walk down to Assi Ghat, the southernmost ghat, calmer than Dashashwamedh. It's the meeting point for Benares intellectuals and artists - you'll find small cafés and a relaxed vibe. The morning aarti (Subah-e-Banaras) here is more intimate than Dashashwamedh's evening one.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Visit a Banarasi silk weaving workshop. The silk saris woven here, with gold and silver motifs, are among India's most precious textiles. Some workshops welcome visitors and will show you the painstaking work on the loom - a sari can take from 15 days to 6 months.

⏱ 45 min

Late afternoon, detour to Banaras Hindu University (BHU) campus, one of Asia's largest universities, founded in 1916. Bharat Kala Bhavan, its museum, houses a remarkable collection of Indian miniatures, sculptures, and ancient textiles.

⏱ 60 min

Bodh Gaya

★★★☆☆
1

Road from Varanasi (250km, about 5 hours). Bodhgaya is Buddhism's holiest site - this is where Siddhartha Gautama attained Enlightenment under a fig tree (pipal) about 2,500 years ago, becoming the Buddha. The Mahabodhi Temple, UNESCO-listed since 2002, marks this exact spot. The 55-meter pyramidal tower, rebuilt between the 5th and 6th centuries, dominates the site. Behind the temple, the descendant of the original Bodhi tree still stands - you'll see monks from all Buddhist traditions meditating at its feet, from Tibetans in maroon robes to Thais in orange.

⏱ 90 min
2

Tour the international monasteries surrounding Mahabodhi Temple - each Buddhist country has built its own in its architectural style. The Thai monastery with its golden roof, the minimalist Japanese temple, the colorful Tibetan monastery, the Chinese temple, and the Burmese temple offer a Buddhist world tour in a single walk. The whole area covers about 2km and is easily done on foot.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Great Buddha Statue (25 meters tall), inaugurated in 1989 by the Dalai Lama, sits in a Japanese garden south of the main temple. It depicts Buddha in meditation posture, seated on a lotus - the 10 smaller statues at the base represent his principal disciples.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Join a guided meditation session at one of the monasteries - several offer free sessions for visitors, usually in the morning. The experience of meditating at the very place where Buddha attained Enlightenment has particular resonance, whether you're Buddhist or not.

⏱ 60 min

In the evening, return to Mahabodhi Temple for evening prayers. Monks light hundreds of lamps around the temple and Bodhi tree - the atmosphere is deeply calm. The contrast with Varanasi's frenetic energy is total.

⏱ 45 min

Kolkata

★★★★☆
1

Train from Gaya (closest station to Bodhgaya) to Kolkata (about 7 hours - take the Rajdhani or Duronto Express). Kolkata, formerly Calcutta, was the British Raj capital until 1911. It's a city of violent contrasts: colonial splendor and misery, boiling intellectual culture and urban chaos. Start with Victoria Memorial, a white marble palace built between 1906 and 1921 honoring Queen Victoria. Its architecture blends Italian Renaissance, Mughal, and Venetian styles. The interior museum traces Calcutta's history under the British - paintings, weapons, manuscripts, and a reconstruction of the 'Black Hole of Calcutta.'

⏱ 90 min
2

Walk to Howrah Bridge (Rabindra Setu), the world's busiest steel bridge - about 100,000 vehicles and countless pedestrians cross it daily. Built in 1943 without any bolts or rivets (entirely riveted), it spans the Hooghly over 705 meters. The best time to cross on foot is early morning, when flower merchants carry their marigold garlands to Mallick Ghat market just below.

⏱ 30 min
3

Visit the Indian Museum, India's oldest and largest museum, founded in 1814. Its collections are sprawling: dinosaur fossils, Gandhara Buddhist sculptures, Mughal miniatures, Egyptian mummies, meteorites, and an entire gallery dedicated to Indian textiles. Allow 90 minutes minimum - the building is immense.

⏱ 90 min
4

Tour St Paul's Cathedral, eastern India's largest Gothic building, built in 1847. The stained glass and wooden framework recall English churches, but the tropical climate imposed modifications - airy verandas and large windows. The contrast with Hindu temples is striking.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

In the evening, dine at one of Park Street's restaurants, Kolkata's gastronomic artery. Bengali cuisine is considered one of India's most refined - fish (hilsa is king), mustard, five-spice blend (panch phoron), and rasgulla (fresh cheese balls in syrup) for dessert. Kolkata is also the birthplace of puchka (Bengali panipuri) - street vendors serve them everywhere.

⏱ 60 min

Ride a Kolkata tram - it's India's last operating tram network, in service since 1902. The rattling cars cross congested streets at snail's pace - it's not efficient transport, it's an experience.

⏱ 30 min

Kolkata

★★★★☆
1

Second day in Kolkata, devoted to authentic Kolkata. Start with Mallick Ghat flower market, under Howrah Bridge - arrive before 7am to see mountains of orange marigolds, roses, and jasmine unloaded from boats. The colors and energy are incredible. It's Asia's largest flower market, with over 2,000 vendors.

⏱ 45 min
2

Push on to Kumartuli, the potter artisans' quarter where Durga statues are made for the annual Durga Puja festival - Kolkata's biggest celebration. Hundreds of workshops open to the street mold straw and clay statues reaching up to 5 meters tall. Even outside festival season, watching artisans at work is worth the trip.

⏱ 45 min
3

Visit Kalighat Temple, one of Hinduism's 51 Shakti Peethas, dedicated to goddess Kali - indeed, Kali gave Calcutta its name. The current temple dates from 1809, but the site has been venerated for centuries. The atmosphere is intense: lines of devotees, flower and incense offerings, priests in trance. Non-Hindus are generally accepted in the outer courtyard.

⏱ 45 min
4

Head to College Street, the world's largest concentration of bookstores after Buenos Aires. Sidewalks disappear under piles of books - university textbooks, Bengali novels, Tagore poetry, and all sorts of rarities. Coffee House café, an institution since 1942, was Bengali intellectuals' meeting place - Satyajit Ray, Amartya Sen, and Subhas Chandra Bose all hung out here.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Visit Mother Teresa's House (Missionaries of Charity), where the saint lived and worked from 1953 until her death in 1997. Her tomb lies in the ground floor chapel. A small museum traces her life and work among Kolkata's poorest. Entry is free and the place is imbued with moving simplicity.

⏱ 45 min

Take a boat ride on the Hooghly (Ganges branch) at sunset. From the water, you'll see the illuminated Howrah Bridge pass by, Kolkata's ghats, and old colonial mansions lining the river. Several operators offer one-hour cruises from Prinsep ghats.

⏱ 60 min

End the evening with a Bengali classical dance or music performance - Kolkata is India's cultural capital, birthplace of Satyajit Ray's cinema and Ravi Shankar's music. Nandan and Rabindra Sadan halls regularly program events.

⏱ 90 min

Bhubaneswar

★★★☆☆
1

Train from Kolkata to Bhubaneswar (about 7 hours on the Shatabdi). The 'city of a thousand temples' closes this circuit with a return to Indian spirituality's roots. Lingaraj Temple, Bhubaneswar's largest and most venerated, dates from the 11th century. Its 55-meter tower (deul) dominates the city - it's the finest example of Kalinga architectural style. Entry is reserved for Hindus, but you can observe the temple from a raised platform outside the enclosure, originally installed for Lord Curzon.

⏱ 45 min
2

Rajarani Temple (11th century) is nicknamed the 'love temple' for its sculptures of entwined couples and celestial nymphs recalling Khajuraho, on a smaller scale. Unlike Lingaraj, it's open to all. The surrounding garden is peaceful - the golden sandstone sculptures in late afternoon light are magnificent.

⏱ 40 min
3

Visit Udayagiri and Khandagiri caves, carved in the 2nd century BC by King Kharavela to house Jain monks. Udayagiri ('sunrise hill') has 18 caves adorned with detailed bas-reliefs showing court scenes and elephant processions. Rani Gumpha ('queen's cave'), on two levels, is the most spectacular. Khandagiri, opposite, offers a panoramic view over Bhubaneswar from its summit.

⏱ 75 min
4

If you have time, detour to Dhauli (8km south), where Emperor Ashoka, after the bloody Battle of Kalinga in 261 BC that killed over 100,000, had a crisis of conscience that converted him to Buddhism. The rock edicts of Ashoka carved in stone are among India's oldest texts. The white Shanti Stupa (Peace Stupa), built in 1972 by the Japanese at the hilltop, offers clear views over rice paddies.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Explore Ekamra Haat market, a permanent craft market where Odisha artisans display their specialties: stone carvings, patachitra (cloth paintings), Cuttack silver filigree jewelry, and ikat fabrics. It's the ideal spot for quality souvenirs at reasonable prices.

⏱ 45 min

Taste Odisha cuisine: dalma (lentils with vegetables and coconut), pakhala bhata (rice fermented in water - Odisha's emblematic dish, served cold), and chhena poda (caramelized fresh cheese cake), a specialty unavailable elsewhere in India.
