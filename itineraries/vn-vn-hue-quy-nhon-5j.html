Hue

★★★★☆
1

The Meridian Gate, known as Ngo Mon, delivers your first stunning impression of the citadel. Built in 1833 under Emperor Minh Mang, it's crowned by the Five Phoenix Pavilion whose central roof in yellow tiles—a color reserved exclusively for imperial use—served as the platform from which the emperor proclaimed his decrees. Pass through the central portal, once reserved for the sovereign alone, and take time to look up: the nine roofs of the pavilion seen from the inner courtyard resemble five phoenixes in flight. Just beyond opens the Palace of Supreme Harmony, the throne hall whose purple and yellow lacquered columns support a ceiling adorned with 80 gilded dragons. Arrive at opening time (7am) to beat the tour groups that crowd the central courtyard between 9am and 11am.

⏱ 60 min
2

Behind the Throne Palace stretched the Purple Forbidden City, the private residence of the emperor and his family—the only people authorized to enter. The fighting in 1968 during the Tet Offensive destroyed most of it, and what you walk through today is partly a recent reconstruction financed by UNESCO and the Vietnamese government. What makes this place poignant is precisely this emptiness: the restored foundations and gardens tell their story as much through what's missing as through what remains. The Royal Theatre (Duyet Thi Duong), built in 1826, is one of the best-preserved buildings—performances of nhã nhạc court music, listed as UNESCO intangible heritage, still take place there on certain days.

⏱ 50 min
3

Before diving into the inner complexes, take time to observe the outer rampart from the bank of the Perfume River. The walls, 6 meters high and 21 meters thick, were built from 1803 following Vauban's principles adapted by Nguyen engineers: moats on all four sides, ten two-story brick gates, bastions at the corners. The Flag Tower (Ky Dai), erected in 1807 on three superimposed brick drums, reaches 54.5 meters including the reinforced concrete flagpole installed in 1948—this is the tower featured on 50,000 dong banknotes. Allow 15 minutes to walk around the base and choose your photo angle.

⏱ 25 min

Activities

Dong Ba Market, located north of Trang Tien Bridge on the north bank of the Perfume River, has been Hue's central market since the early 19th century. The ground floor is devoted to spices, fresh herbs, and ingredients for royal cuisine: fine Hue chilies, dried shrimp, moc tom shrimp paste. Come between 4pm and 6pm when the bánh khoai vendors—crispy pancakes filled with shrimp and pork—set up under the awnings outside. Don't leave without trying mạch nya, a malt confection typical of Hue that you'll find almost nowhere else in Vietnam.

⏱ 60 min

After visiting the citadel, treat yourself to a stroll along the quays that line the Perfume River—its name comes from the frangipani flowers that fell into its waters in autumn and perfumed the city. Trang Tien Bridge, rebuilt in 1899 by Gustave Eiffel's engineers on six metal spans, is beautifully illuminated in the evening. Small restaurants along the quays offer bánh bèo—steamed rice cakes topped with dried shrimp and fried onions, one of Hue's most typical street specialties—for just a few thousand dong per plate.

⏱ 60 min

Hue

★★★★☆
1

Thien Mu Pagoda stands on a hill overlooking the Perfume River 3 kilometers west of the citadel. Its octagonal tower Phuoc Duyen, 21 meters high and spread over seven stories—each representing an incarnation of Buddha—has been Hue's emblem since its construction in 1844 by Emperor Thieu Tri. The pagoda itself is older: founded in 1601 by Lord Nguyen Hoang, it's one of the most venerated Buddhist sites in central Vietnam. In the courtyard, you'll find a bell from 1710 weighing 2,052 kilograms and a stele from 1715 placed on the back of a marble turtle. If possible, take a boat from the city center—arriving by river is far more beautiful than by road.

⏱ 45 min
2

6 km southwest of the citadel, Tu Duc's tomb is the most visited of the Nguyen mausoleums. Emperor Tu Duc (1829-1883), who ruled longer than any other emperor of the dynasty with 36 years on the throne, had it built between 1864 and 1867 as a retirement residence during his lifetime: he would come to compose poetry and sail on Luu Khiem Lake from his pavilion on stilts. The site comprises nearly 50 buildings over more than 120,000 m², all named with the word Khiem—'modesty' in Vietnamese. In the Stele Pavilion, a stone engraved with 4,935 words composed by the emperor himself recounts his life and regrets. Visit early in the morning for soft light on the ponds.

⏱ 75 min
3

Khai Dinh's tomb, perched on Chau Chu hill 10 km from Hue, stands in stark contrast to all other Nguyen mausoleums. Built between 1920 and 1931 for the dynasty's twelfth emperor, it embraces an unabashed blend of French neoclassical architecture, Hindu art, and Vietnamese tradition—Roman pilasters, reinforced concrete, and an interior entirely covered with mosaics of porcelain and colored glass. The three ceilings of Thien Dinh Palace are decorated with frescoes depicting the nine dragons hidden in the clouds, executed by craftsman Phan Van Tach. Legend has it that Khai Dinh had a secret tunnel dug connecting his tomb to the Forbidden City.

⏱ 50 min
4

Hue's garden houses, called nha vuon, are dwellings built of black jackfruit wood by mandarins and wealthy merchants between the 17th and 19th centuries. Their layout follows a strict code: main house facing south according to feng shui principles, surrounded by a walled garden and a pond. About ten of them are open to visitors in the Kim Long district west of the citadel. Push open the door of An Hien House: a three-bay frame structure with 48 lacquered columns, woodwork carved with lucky bats, and a garden with banana trees and frangipanis. You can have tea there with the family who still lives in it—a conversation far more interesting than any information panel.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Tour operators offer sunset cruises aboard wooden sampans from the quays at the foot of Trang Tien Bridge. These boats sail upstream toward Thien Mu Pagoda as the sun sets over the citadel, then drift back down enjoying the low light on the banks. Plan for between 45 minutes and 1h30 depending on the boat you choose. Some boats offer live nhã nhạc court music on board—check with your accommodation the day before so you don't miss this option.

⏱ 90 min

Hue's cuisine stands apart in Vietnamese gastronomy: inherited from imperial court cooks, it's renowned for its refinement and pronounced taste for chili. Two dishes not to miss tonight: bún bò Huế, beef and pork shank vermicelli soup in a spicy lemongrass broth—radically different from northern pho—and bánh khoai, crispy pancakes filled with shrimp and bean sprouts dipped in peanut sauce. The Dong Ba district and the small alleys around the market concentrate the best informal spots; avoid the overly touristy restaurants around Trang Tien Bridge.

⏱ 90 min

Da Nang

★★★★☆
1

Between Hue and Da Nang, leave the national highway and take the old road that climbs for 21 km up to 496 meters altitude, overlooking both the South China Sea and the tropical forests of the Annamite Range. The 6.2 km tunnel opened in 2005 diverted most truck traffic, making this pass much quieter. This passage served as the border between the Champa Kingdom and Dai Viet since 1306; at the summit, the Hai Van Quan gate restored in 2023 dominates the old French bunkers riddled with bullet impacts, reused by American forces under the name 'Cloudy Hill.' In clear weather, you can simultaneously see Da Nang Bay to the south and Lang Co Beach to the north—plan a photo stop at both ends.

⏱ 90 min
2

Just after the northern descent from the pass, Lang Co Beach stretches along a peninsula squeezed between the lagoon and the open sea. It's a 20 to 30-minute stop for a photo or quick swim—the setting with the mountain backdrop is particularly photogenic and the beach is almost always empty of foreign tourists. Don't linger too long: the road to Da Nang still takes another 30 to 40 minutes from here.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Son Tra Peninsula juts into the sea north of Da Nang, covered by a semi-wild forest classified as a nature reserve. Linh Ung Pagoda, entirely rebuilt in 2010 on a site founded in the 18th century, is dominated by a 67-meter white Buddha statue—the tallest in Vietnam at the time of its inauguration. From the statue's base, the view embraces Da Nang Bay and, on clear days, the Cham Islands on the horizon. The road climbing up passes through a forest where endemic red-shanked douc langurs live—keep your bags closed if you stop.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

My Khe Beach borders Da Nang for more than 10 km, wide and straight with fine sand and regular waves. Nicknamed 'China Beach' by American soldiers who came there on leave during the war, it's now Da Nang's urban beach. Locals swim there from 5am before the heat, and again after 5pm—these are the times when you'll find it most lively and least crowded. If you arrive between 5pm and 7pm, street vendors offer fresh coconuts and grilled corn at the water's edge.

⏱ 60 min

Da Nang is renowned throughout Vietnam for the quality of its seafood—locals say it's thanks to the cold currents flowing down from the north that enrich the coastal waters. The waterfront district south of My Khe Beach concentrates dozens of grill restaurants. Don't miss mi quang, a local specialty: wide rice noodles in a dense turmeric sauce, topped with shrimp, roast pork, and roasted peanuts. It's different from anything you ate in Hue.

⏱ 75 min

Hoi An

★★★★★
1

Between Da Nang and Hoi An, the Marble Mountains (Ngu Hanh Son, the 'Five Element Mountains') rise from the coastal plain like stage sets. These five limestone peaks each bear the name of an element—Water, Wood, Fire, Metal, Earth—and are riddled with caves in which Buddhist sanctuaries have been installed since the 15th century. Climb the 156 steps of Thuy Son Mountain to Huyen Khong Cave, whose natural opening in the ceiling lets in a vertical beam of light that strikes directly on the large marble Buddha. From the summit, you see both My Khe Beach and Da Nang Airport—a striking panorama.

⏱ 90 min
2

Hoi An's Japanese Bridge is one of the most photographed monuments in central Vietnam. Built in 1593 by Japanese merchants to connect their quarter to the Chinese traders' district, this 18-meter covered bridge bears dog statues at one end and monkeys at the other—tradition says it was started in a year of the dog and completed in a year of the monkey. In 1653, a pagoda was added on the north side. In 1719, Lord Nguyen Phuc Chu named it Lai Vien Kieu, 'bridge to welcome visitors from afar.' Arrive between 6pm and 8pm when the lanterns are lit and the daytime crowds have dispersed. From the bridge, push open the door of Tan Ky House at 101 Nguyen Thai Hoc, built over 200 years ago: its architecture blends Japanese, Chinese, and Vietnamese influences in a dark jackfruit wood frame.

⏱ 75 min
3

Hoi An's old town, listed as UNESCO heritage in 1999, preserves more than 1,100 timber-framed buildings, some dating back to the 17th century. The historic center can only be explored on foot or by bicycle—cars don't circulate there. Wander through the alleys around Nguyen Thai Hoc and Bach Dang streets, walk along the Thu Bon River where pink and yellow lanterns float on the surface. If you pass near the tailors' street around Tran Phu, know that Hoi An is one of the cheapest places in the world to have clothes custom-made in 24 to 48 hours—order tonight to pick up before your departure.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

During each full moon, all electric shops in the old town turn off and only silk lanterns light the alleys—if your stay coincides with this date, don't miss this event for anything. On other evenings, the old town remains beautiful after 7pm: street vendors offer toasted bánh mì, chè (sweet bean dessert with coconut milk), and rice pancakes. Along the Thu Bon River, children sell lanterns to place on the water to honor ancestors—the sight of dozens of lights drifting in the current is one of the trip's most powerful images.

⏱ 120 min

Hoi An

★★★★★
1

The Phuc Kien Assembly Hall is the most imposing of Hoi An's six Chinese assembly halls. Built from 1690 by immigrants from Fujian Province in southern China, it served simultaneously as a place of worship, a retirement home for sailors in distress, and a community center. Entry through the blue and white ceramic gate opens onto a courtyard with a pond for turtles and carp, then onto the main temple dedicated to Thien Hau, goddess of the sea. The altars covered with spiral incense sticks suspended from the ceiling create a dense and fragrant atmosphere you won't easily forget. Come in the morning to avoid tour groups.

⏱ 45 min
2

Hidden in an alley off Nguyen Thai Hoc Street, the Tran Family Chapel is one of Hoi An's best-preserved ancestral houses. Built over two centuries ago to venerate the ancestors of a mandarin family, it's still inhabited by their descendants—who maintain it and give tours themselves. The three-bay wooden structure, the ceiling engraved with bats carrying coins (symbols of happiness), the altars with ancestral tablets and yellowed photos: everything here remains alive, not museumified. Push open the door—it's one of the few places in Hoi An where you have a real conversation with locals.

⏱ 30 min
3

Hoi An's Central Market, on Tran Phu Street facing the Thu Bon River, is active from 5am for fishermen bringing their night catches. At lunchtime, the interior space transforms into a maze of food stalls. Don't miss cao lầu: these thick rice noodles served with roast pork and fresh herbs are made, according to tradition, only with water from Hoi An's well. Bring cash—stalls don't take cards, and prices are set by looking at the customer.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The afternoon is ideal for choosing between two of Hoi An's emblematic craft activities. Tour operators offer half-day cooking classes including a visit to the central market, learning three or four local recipes, and eating the meal you cooked. If cooking doesn't appeal to you, opt for a silk and bamboo lantern-making workshop—Hoi An is one of the last cities in Vietnam where this craft is still practiced by families who've passed it down through generations. One to two-hour workshops welcome beginners with no experience. Book the day before from your accommodation.

⏱ 180 min

Leave the touristy old town and cross the bridge to An Hoi district, on the other side of the Thu Bon River. The alleys are quieter there, the artisan shops more authentic, and you'll find a few wood carving and bamboo boat-making workshops that still work the old-fashioned way. This is also where local restaurateurs gather in the evening—no multilingual menus, just plastic tables and fresh fish grilled over embers.

⏱ 60 min

Hoi An

★★★★★
1

40 km southwest of Hoi An, in a valley enclosed by mountains, the Cham temples of My Son are one of Southeast Asia's most significant archaeological sites—often dubbed 'Vietnam's little Angkor.' Construction of the sanctuary was initiated in the 4th century by King Bhadravarman of the Champa Kingdom (349-361) and continued until the 13th century; at its peak, the site comprised over 70 red brick tower-sanctuaries dedicated to Shiva, assembled without visible mortar using a technique archaeologists still haven't fully understood. American bombing in 1969 destroyed much of it. Arrive at opening time (6am) to see morning light graze the carved stones and avoid the heat of midday hours—a traditional Cham dance performance is given in the morning near the entrance.

⏱ 150 min
2

3 km northeast of the old town, Tra Que village has cultivated fresh herbs since the 15th century in gardens flooded by water from the Thu Bon River: chives, Thai basil, mint, coriander, rau ram. The quality of these herbs is such that Hoi An's restaurants claim them as a distinctive ingredient. Tour operators offer bicycle circuits from the old town that include visiting a garden, a demonstration of traditional seaweed composting, and sometimes a cooking class on a family farm. The round-trip ride takes 30 minutes each way on a flat road along the river—suitable for all levels.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

An Bang Beach is located 5 km northeast of the old town—20 minutes by bicycle or 10 minutes by taxi. Less crowded than Cua Dai Beach (partly eroded in recent years), An Bang is the favorite beach of long-term travelers in Hoi An. Beach chairs, seafood restaurants with feet in the sand, and bars offering fresh fruit juices line the waterfront. It's the ideal spot for a beach break after the long morning excursion to My Son. The sea is generally calm here in the dry season.

⏱ 90 min

End this last evening in Hoi An on the Thu Bon River. Tour operators offer outings in round boats (thuyền thúng)—these basket-shaped boats that are one of the visual symbols of the Thu Bon delta—at sunset from the old town quays. Some circuits include a net fishing lesson or placing lanterns on the water. The warm evening light on the old town's yellow facades seen from the water is a viewpoint like no other—book the day before from your accommodation.

Quy Nhon

★★★☆☆
1

Halfway between Hoi An and Quy Nhon, the village of Sa Huynh in Quang Ngai Province deserves a 30-minute stop. The beach is beautiful and uncrowded, with clear water and fresh fruit vendors roadside. Sa Huynh is mainly known to archaeologists: it was here that the first remains of Sa Huynh culture (500 BC - 200 AD) were discovered in the early 20th century, a pre-Cham civilization that already mastered iron metallurgy and maritime trade with Indonesia and the Philippines.

⏱ 30 min
2

20 km northwest of Quy Nhon, the Banh It Towers rise on a rocky spur overlooking a landscape of rice paddies and coconut groves. This group of four independent towers, built between the 11th and 13th centuries by the Champa Kingdom, is one of the best-preserved Cham ensembles in Binh Dinh Province: the main tower (kalan), the fire tower (kosagrha), the entrance tower (gopura), and the storage tower. Bas-reliefs depict Apsara dancers, elephants, and scenes from Hindu mythology. Climb up to the towers to enjoy the panoramic view over the coastal plain—prefer late afternoon when the light turns golden.

⏱ 60 min
3

Quy Nhon is one of central Vietnam's least-known beach destinations—and that's precisely its charm. The main beach stretches 5 km through the heart of the city, lined with a bike path, and locals swim there from dawn and after 5pm. After 6 hours on the road, you'll appreciate putting your feet in the water: the town is calm, not touristy, and locals are spontaneously welcoming to foreign travelers who rarely pass through here.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Beachfront restaurants in Quy Nhon offer barbecue-grilled seafood—shellfish, langoustines, octopus—in small establishments with feet in the sand. Prices are two to three times lower than in Da Nang or Nha Trang for comparable quality. Don't miss the regional specialties: salt and chili sautéed shrimp (tôm rang muối) and jumping shrimp pancakes (bánh xèo tôm nhảy), a specialty unique to Binh Dinh with firm-fleshed rock shrimp.

Quy Nhon

★★★☆☆
1

The Thap Doi Towers are one of the few pairs of Cham towers still standing in the heart of a modern Vietnamese city. Built between the late 10th and early 15th centuries during the reign of King Yanpuku Vijaya, these two red brick towers are decorated with bas-reliefs depicting dancing monkeys, elephants, lions, and deer in homage to Hindu deities. They're located 3 km from the city center in Dong Da district, surrounded by a public garden with coconut trees—a striking contrast between the millennium-old and the daily life of locals strolling at the foot of the towers in the morning. Visit them early, before leaving for Ky Co.

⏱ 40 min
2

Eo Gio—'the strait of wind'—is a spectacular coastal formation 20 km northeast of Quy Nhon, in Nhon Ly commune. Two mountain chains plunge into the sea creating a passage where wind rushes through and waves strike black basalt cliffs with such force you'll hear it before seeing anything. A pedestrian path with red railings follows the cliff for several hundred meters—photos from this overlook path are among the most spectacular on the central coast. Below, a beach of oval, colorful pebbles spreads like a natural carpet. Allow 45 minutes to an hour for the complete circuit.

⏱ 75 min
3

Even if you opt for the boat excursion (see activities), take time to observe Ky Co from the water during approach: the cove nestled between cliffs, white sand, and turquoise waters form a tableau that explains why this beach is often compared to Southeast Asia's best. The mountain's coastal vegetation descends almost to the sea's edge, a rare natural setting on this generally more arid coast.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Ky Co Beach is accessible only by boat from the fishing village of Nhon Ly (10 to 12 minutes by motorboat). Tour operators offer combined Ky Co - Eo Gio excursions with morning departure from Quy Nhon: the drive to Nhon Ly, the boat shuttle, a seafood lunch on the beach, and snorkeling equipment are included. Coral is visible at shallow depth and tropical fish are plentiful. Book the day before from your hotel—places are limited in high season (July-August) but abundant in spring.

⏱ 180 min

Before heading back to Phu Cat Airport, treat yourself to one last seafood meal by the sea. Binh Dinh region specialties include lemongrass clams, grilled octopus, and ca bống fish soup. Plan to leave the table at least 2 hours before your flight time to account for the drive, formalities, and potential traffic on Highway 1. Phu Cat Airport serves Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City—check your flight the day before.
