☀️ Weather

Dry Season (May-October): Peak season with minimal rainfall, comfortable temperatures (24-28°C), and trade winds. Best weather for outdoor activities but highest prices and crowds.

Shoulder Season (April, November): Good weather with brief showers, moderate accommodation rates, and fewer tourists. Excellent balance of conditions and value.

Wet Season (November-April): Hot, humid weather (26-32°C) with potential cyclones, but lower prices and lush landscapes. Afternoon rains common, mornings often clear.

Cyclone Season (December-March): Higher storm risk requiring flexible planning and comprehensive insurance, though many cyclones pass without major impact to tourism.

Papeete

Where the market's dawn chorus meets harbor-side food trucks under Moorea's watchful gaze

★★★★★
1

Papeete's market, Mapuru a Paraita, occupies a two-story covered building right in the city center. Vanilla pods, handmade monoï oil, fresh tiare flower crowns, and colorful tifaifai quilts pile high on the stalls. Sunday mornings from 4 a.m. onward, Tahitian families flood the aisles—it's the most authentic time to visit.

⏱ 75 min
2

Step inside Notre-Dame Cathedral, recognizable by its bright yellow facade and bell tower dominating downtown. Built starting in 1856 and completed in 1875, it houses stained glass windows that filter the tropical light and offer welcome coolness.

⏱ 40 min
3

Head to Paofai Gardens along the waterfront. This planted promenade facing the harbor offers an unobstructed view of Moorea's silhouette. Outrigger canoes (va'a) train here in late afternoon.

⏱ 50 min
4

The Pearl Museum traces the history of pearl farming in French Polynesia. In 45 minutes, you'll learn to distinguish the luster, orient, and shapes of Tahitian black pearls.

⏱ 45 min
5

Walk up Boulevard Pomaré to spot the street art murals created during the Ono'u festival. International artists have transformed several downtown facades into an open-air gallery.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

The roulottes at Place Vaiete set up every evening from 6 p.m. facing the harbor. Poisson cru with coconut milk, chao mein, grilled shrimp—a local institution under the stars.

⏱ 60 min

If you arrive on a Sunday, head to Paofai Protestant Temple to hear the polyphonic religious singing. Worshippers wear hats and white outfits—a unique sonic and visual experience.

Papeete

Where the market's dawn chorus meets harbor-side food trucks under Moorea's watchful gaze

★★★★★
1

The three Faarumai waterfalls are at PK 22 on the east coast, 22 km from Papeete. The first, Vaimahuta, plunges 80 meters down a basalt wall—five minutes' walk from the parking area. The other two, Haamaremare Rahi and Iti, require an additional 20 minutes on a steeper trail.

⏱ 75 min
2

Stop at the Arahoho Blowhole, just beside the waterfalls. The ocean rushes into a lava tube carved by erosion beneath the road and shoots a powerful jet of water landward. In Tahitian, Arahoho means 'the road that roars.' The spectacle depends on the swell—the stronger it is, the more impressive the spray.

⏱ 40 min
3

Take the coastal road to Pointe Vénus in Mahina (PK 10). The white 25-meter lighthouse, built in 1867, marks where Europeans landed in 1767. Black sand beach and views of Moorea at sunset.

⏱ 50 min
4

The Tahara'a lookout, at the start of the east coast leaving Papeete, offers from its landscaped terrace a panorama over the lagoon, the coral barrier, and the Tahiti Iti peninsula.

⏱ 30 min
5

The Museum of Tahiti and Her Islands in Punaauia on the west coast (PK 15) traces the natural and cultural history of Polynesia. Collections of tapa cloth, traditional canoes, and basalt tools. Check opening days before visiting.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Swimming at a black sand beach along the east coast. The water is warm year-round but currents can be strong—respect the flags and stick to supervised areas.

⏱ 45 min

Vaiava Beach (PK 18, Punaauia) offers white sand on the west side and easy snorkeling above coral heads, with direct views of Moorea.

⏱ 60 min

Paopao

Between two bays pierced by volcanic spires, life slows to the rhythm of outrigger paddles

★★★☆☆
1

The ferry crosses the Vaiare pass and reveals Moorea, Tahiti's sister island. From the dock, the trident-shaped silhouette of its mountains dominates: Cook's Bay on the left, Opunohu Bay on the right, separated by the sacred Mount Rotui (899 m).

⏱ 45 min
2

Climb the shaded little road to Opunohu Lookout, at 240 meters altitude in the heart of a 9-km-diameter crater. The 360° panorama embraces the two bays, eight mountain ranges, the lagoon, and pineapple fields below.

⏱ 60 min
3

On the way down, stop at the Opunohu Agricultural School. Students produce tropical fruit jams and valley pineapple juice. Archaeological sites (marae) are nearby, along the marked trail.

⏱ 45 min
4

Marae Titiroa, a Polynesian stone temple nestled in the vegetation of Opunohu Valley, testifies to the ancient Ma'ohi religion. Explanatory panels line the site.

⏱ 30 min
5

Ta'ahiamanu public beach, in Opunohu Bay, unfurls a strip of white sand bordered by coconut palms. The shallow, calm water makes it a family-friendly swimming spot.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Snorkeling in the lagoon on the Cook's Bay side: butterflyfish, surgeonfish, and small rays glide above corals accessible from the beach. Mornings offer optimal visibility.

⏱ 60 min

Lagoon boat tour between the two bays. You cruise at the foot of Mount Rotui, encountering stingrays and small blacktip reef sharks in the shallow waters around the motus.

⏱ 90 min

Fare

An island where forty stone temples whisper of ancient chiefs and the sea still remembers their names

★★★☆☆
1

The village of Fare, on Huahine Nui's west coast, is the arrival point on the island. A few streets lined with craft shops, a small harbor, and a white sand beach facing the lagoon—the atmosphere is that of a Polynesian village frozen in time. No traffic lights, no luxury resorts: Huahine cultivates its wild side.

⏱ 45 min
2

Take the road to Maeva village in the island's northeast. It's the largest archaeological site in French Polynesia: about forty marae (stone temples) stretch between Matairea hill and the shores of Lake Fauna Nui. Panels explain the function of each structure—some served for ceremonies, others for the investiture of chiefs.

⏱ 75 min
3

At the edge of Lake Fauna Nui, you'll see the stone fish traps, restored and still used by locals. These basalt enclosures date back several centuries—fish enter with the rising tide and are trapped at low tide. An ingenious technique that still works today.

⏱ 40 min
4

The community museum of Fare Pote'e, a large traditional house rebuilt on stilts at the lake's edge, displays archaeological objects found at the Maeva site: mother-of-pearl fishhooks, basalt adzes, and bone ornaments.

⏱ 35 min

Activities

Outrigger canoe tour on Huahine Nui's lagoon. The water is deep blue on the pass side and translucent turquoise on the motu side. You'll likely encounter rays and green turtles—Huahine is one of the islands where they're most present.

⏱ 90 min

Late afternoon, settle on Fare beach for sunset. Fishing canoes return to port, children play in the warm water—a quiet moment, far from Bora Bora's tourist bustle.

⏱ 45 min

★★★☆☆
1

Huahine is actually two islands—Huahine Nui to the north and Huahine Iti to the south—linked by a bridge at Maroe. Cross this bridge and head south: vegetation thickens, dwellings become scarce, the road follows an increasingly wild lagoon.

⏱ 30 min
2

Avea Bay, on Huahine Iti's southwest coast, is one of Polynesia's most beautiful anchorages. White sand, coconut palms leaning over the water, and a shallow lagoon that shifts from emerald green to cobalt blue. Few people—this is the anti-Bora Bora.

⏱ 60 min
3

Marae Anini, at Huahine Iti's southern tip, extends on a rocky promontory facing the ocean. This basalt stone temple served for navigation-related ceremonies—logical for an island from which great canoes departed for the Leeward Islands.

⏱ 40 min
4

Return via the east coast through Parea village. Gardens overflow with breadfruit, papayas, and banana trees. If you meet a local, chances are good they'll offer you a grapefruit—hospitality in Huahine isn't a myth.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Snorkeling in Avea Bay's lagoon: the water is calm, shallow, and the corals are in good condition. Parrotfish, damselfish, and sometimes green turtles grazing the seagrass beds. Mask and snorkel are enough.

⏱ 60 min

Island tour by bike or scooter. Huahine is about 30 km around—count on half a day by bike with stops. The road is flat, shaded, and you'll cross sleeping villages, coconut groves, and viewpoints over the lagoon.

⏱ 150 min

Rangiroa is home to a vineyard—yes, wine on a Pacific atoll. The estate produces white and rosé wine from grape varieties adapted to the tropical climate. The vines grow on coral sand and yield two harvests per year. The visit is short but surprising.

Visit to a pearl farm on the atoll. The grafter shows you how they insert a nucleus into the Pinctada margaritifera oyster, then explains the 18-month wait before harvest. Tuamotu mother-of-pearl produces pearls with green, aubergine, and peacock reflections—different from Society Islands pearls.

3

Activities

Scuba diving at Avatoru Pass—Rangiroa's second major spot. You'll encounter schools of jacks, grey sharks, Napoleon wrasses, and sometimes hammerhead sharks between December and March. Even for experienced divers, it's a highlight.

1

Fakarava is an atoll classified as a UNESCO biosphere reserve—one of the few in the world. Sixty kilometers long and 25 wide, it's pierced by two passes that rank among the planet's best dive sites.

⏱ 30 min
2

Rotoava village, in the atoll's northeast, is Fakarava's only real village. A few hundred inhabitants, a white coral church, houses on stilts, and a fishing wharf. The church, built entirely of coral blocks by missionaries, is worth a stop for its unique architecture.

⏱ 45 min
3

The north pass, Garuae, is Polynesia's widest—1,600 meters of opening. From the shore, you see the current tracing turquoise lines in the darker water. It's through this pass that sharks, rays, dolphins, and schools of tuna transit.

⏱ 35 min
4

Walk along the lagoon shore, south of Rotoava. The sand is white, scattered with shells and polished coral pieces. Not a sound, not a building—just the lagoon, coconut palms, and wind.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Snorkeling at the entrance to Garuae Pass, on the lagoon side. The incoming current brings crystal water and schools of tropical fish. Stay in the calm zone and let the fish come to you—the density of underwater life is stunning.

⏱ 60 min

Sunset from Rotoava beach. With no relief on the horizon, the sun plunges directly into the Pacific—a perfect orange disk that disappears in seconds.

★★★★★
1

Back in Tahiti for the final two days, head toward the peninsula (Tahiti Iti), often ignored by visitors. The Taravao isthmus connects the two parts of the island—beyond it, the road narrows, vegetation thickens, tourists disappear.

⏱ 30 min
2

Marae Arahurahu in Paea (PK 22.5 west coast) is Tahiti's best restored. Its terraced stone platform, surrounded by coconut palms, still hosts reenactments of traditional ceremonies.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Maraa Caves, carved in the coastal cliff at Paea, shelter pools of crystal freshwater in a fern setting. A quick and surprising stop.

⏱ 30 min
4

Climb to the Taravao plateau, at 600 meters altitude. The road winds through pastures (yes, cows in Tahiti) and opens onto a panorama embracing the entire north coast, lagoon, and Moorea in the distance. On clear days, it's one of the island's most beautiful viewpoints.

⏱ 50 min
5

Descend toward the south coast and Teahupo'o village, famous in the surfing world for its mythical wave. From shore, you don't see much—the wave forms on the reef, 500 meters offshore. But the village itself, wedged between mountain and lagoon, is worth the detour for its atmosphere.

⏱ 40 min
6

Continue to the road's end on the east side, toward Tautira village. The road stops there—beyond is the Pari cliff, accessible only on foot or by boat. The village is quiet, bordered by a river and a protected lagoon.

⏱ 35 min

Activities

Swimming in Teahupo'o's lagoon, on the inside (not the wave side). The water is calm, warm, and the bottom sandy. Some operators offer boat trips to see the wave up close—even without surfing, the spectacle of the swell rising on the reef is impressive.

⏱ 90 min

Lunch at a local peninsula snack bar: poisson cru, ma'a tinito (Sino-Tahitian dish), or steak-frites—everyday Tahitian cooking, far from tourist restaurants.

⏱ 45 min

3

Activities

Last-minute shopping at the market or downtown shops: vanilla pods, handmade monoï, hand-painted pareos. Fresh tiare crowns won't survive the journey, but their fragrance on the plane is worth the gesture.

⏱ 45 min

Transfer to Faa'a airport, 5 km from Papeete. Arrive with margin—check-in for international flights opens 3 hours before departure. The last Polynesian sunset is visible from the tarmac.
