☀️ What weather should you expect?

Cool Season (October-March): Perfect weather with comfortable temperatures (20-30°C), minimal rainfall, and ideal conditions for desert exploration and outdoor activities. Peak tourist season.

Winter Peak (December-February): Most comfortable temperatures (18-26°C) with pleasant evenings perfect for cultural exploration and desert camping. Highest accommodation rates but best weather.

Shoulder Season (March-April, October-November): Good weather with moderate temperatures, fewer crowds, and reasonable prices. Excellent balance of comfort and value.

Hot Season (May-September): Very hot temperatures (35-48°C) making outdoor activities challenging, though coastal areas more bearable. Lowest prices but extreme heat limits activities.

Stop 1

Muscat

Where the Sultan's Grand Mosque meets two-century-old souqs and Portuguese harbour forts

★★★★☆
📍 Sultan Qaboos Grand Mosque Muscat📍 Mutrah Corniche Muscat📍 Mutrah Souq Muscat📍 Mutrah Fort Muscat📍 Al Alam Palace Muscat📍 Fort Jalali Muscat
1

Start with the Sultan Qaboos Grand Mosque - it's open to non-Muslims Saturday through Thursday, 8am to 11am. Dress modestly: shoulders, knees covered, and women must cover their heads. The mosque was built between 1995 and 2001 across 416,000 m² and can hold 20,000 worshippers. Step into the main prayer hall and look up at the chandelier: 8 m in diameter, 14 m tall, 8 tonnes, fitted with 1,122 Swarovski crystal bulbs. Beneath your feet lies the world's largest hand-woven Persian carpet made from a single piece - 70 m × 60 m, 21 tonnes, completed over 4 years by 600 Iranian weavers. On your way out, climb to the gallery roof to see the five minarets symbolizing the five pillars of Islam, the tallest reaching 91.5 m.

⏱ 90 min
2

Head to Muttrah in early afternoon. The corniche runs along the harbour for about 3 km, lined with latticed facades and moored dhows. Muttrah Souq dates from the 1820s - locals still call it 'Al Dhalam', the market of darkness, because of the covered alleys that blocked out the heat. The main entrance faces the sea, beneath a salmon-coloured dome. Inside, follow the scent of frankincense to the aisles of silver jewellery and khanjars. The vendors are noticeably less pushy than elsewhere in the region - take your time to wander without pressure. Muttrah Fort, built by the Portuguese in the 1580s, dominates the eastern end of the port - climb up for the view over the bay.

⏱ 90 min
3

Old Muscat: a 20-minute walk from Muttrah, but another era. Al Alam Palace, the Sultan's official residence, stands between its blue and gold columns facing the sea. You can't go inside, but the facade is worth the stop. Just behind, Forts Jalali and Mirani (both Portuguese, 16th century) face each other across the bay - they were linked by chains stretched across the harbour entrance to block enemy ships. Walk along the waterfront to both forts, though don't expect crowds: this district sees far fewer visitors than Muttrah.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Stroll and sunset at Al Qurum Beach, a few kilometres west. The beach is long, clean and bordered by a public park. In late afternoon, Omani families gather here - a good local atmosphere, far from the tourist circuits.

⏱ 60 min

Evening at Muttrah Souq: the market comes alive again after 4pm, when local crowds replace cruise ship tourists. It's the best time to wander without pressure, stop for a lemon-mint in a corniche café and watch the dhows light up as the sun sets over the harbour.

Stop 2

Tiwi

A coastal fishing village where canyon walls frame emerald pools and hidden waterfalls

★★☆☆☆
📍 Bandar Al Khayran Qantab Muscat📍 Hawiyat Najm Park Bimmah Sinkhole📍 Tiwi Beach Oman
1

Before leaving Muscat, detour to Bandar Al Khayran bay, a few kilometres from Qantab. The road ends at a headland overlooking a series of rocky fjords and little turquoise coves - one of the cleanest coastal panoramas near the capital, and there's almost always a breeze.

⏱ 45 min
2

120 km from Muscat, you can't miss Hawiyat Najm Park - the sign is clearly visible on Route 17. Descend the stairs to the sinkhole: 70 m long, 50 m wide, 20 m deep, filled with turquoise water that mixes fresh and seawater (the sea is barely 600 m away). Locals once thought this hole had been carved by a meteorite - hence its Arabic name, 'the falling star'. The geological reality is more mundane: the collapse of a karst cave in the limestone. You can swim - the water is fresh on the surface, with a slight saline influence at depth. Small fish will nibble your feet. Dress modestly with shoulders and knees covered to enter the park: the guards are strict about this.

⏱ 75 min
3

Tiwi announces itself with a small pebble beach and white-and-blue fishing hamlets nestled between cliff and sea. Not much to 'visit' as such - it's the atmosphere that makes the detour worthwhile: boats drawn up on the sand, children playing in the alleys, goats on the walls. The village is also the starting point for Wadi Shab, which you'll do tomorrow morning.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Drive Muscat → Bimmah → Tiwi: the coastal road (Route 17) runs along the Gulf of Oman with views of black mountains plunging straight into the sea. Several photo stops practically impose themselves. Allow 2 hours of leisurely driving with stops.

Stop 3

Sur

The last city in Arabia where seven shipyards still craft wooden dhows by hand

★★★☆☆
📍 Wadi Shab Parking Oman📍 Sur Dhow Yard Oman📍 Al Ayjah Lighthouse Sur Oman📍 Sur Corniche Oman
1

Set off for Wadi Shab early - ideally at 8-9am. 'Shab' means gorge between cliffs, and the name is well earned. To enter the wadi, you first cross a river by boat (a few hundred baisa per person return). On the other side, a 45-minute walk on a rocky path between date palms and vertical walls brings you to the first emerald pool. Keep going: the pools beyond are more beautiful and less crowded. For the cave, you need to swim for about fifteen minutes, pass under a rock through a narrow passage (head above water, no diving required), and you emerge into an open-air cavern with a waterfall. Allow 3 to 4 hours return. Never descend into a wadi in bad weather: flash floods are deadly.

⏱ 180 min
2

In the afternoon, head to Sur and its shipyard. Sur is the last city in Oman to perpetuate traditional dhow construction - seven active yards, in the purest artisan tradition. Workers build to order, using techniques passed down for centuries. Push open the yard gate (west side of the Al Ayjah suspension bridge) - you'll generally be allowed to wander around.

⏱ 60 min
3

Finish with the Al Ayjah quarter, on the other side of the 204 m suspension bridge built in 2010. Climb to the lighthouse: an 18 m conical tower with three levels, with panoramic views over the lagoon, sea and watchtowers that once guarded the harbour entrance. Below, wander the alleys of Al Ayjah - white houses with carved wooden doors, a few goats, the atmosphere of a 1950s fishing village.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Boat crossing at the entrance to Wadi Shab: just a few metres but essential to access the trail. The car park is under the motorway bridge (search for 'Wadi Shab Parking' on maps, not just 'Wadi Shab').

Sur

★★★☆☆
📍 Wadi Tiwi entrance Oman📍 Mibam Village Wadi Tiwi📍 Al Ayjah Fort Sur Oman
1

Wadi Tiwi is also called 'the gorge of nine villages'. From the coast, a road climbs deep into the canyon for several kilometres, passing from hamlet to hamlet clinging to the cliff face. Drive to the end of the road (4x4 recommended for the last few kilometres), then continue on foot. Falaj - ancestral irrigation channels - run along the terraces of date palms and water the hanging gardens. The village of Mibam is one of the most picturesque with its dry-stone houses among the crops. The atmosphere is much wilder and less touristy than Wadi Shab.

⏱ 120 min
2

Back in Sur, complete your visit to Al Ayjah which you may not have finished yesterday. Al Ayjah Fort (open weekdays) is a 300-year-old square bastion with round towers. Just steps away, the three watchtowers at the entrance to the lagoon were used to watch over dhows entering the harbour - and to stretch chains between them in case of threat.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Walk and swim in the pools of Wadi Tiwi: between the villages, the wadi bed holds several natural pools of clear water. The spot sees few tourists - respect the locals and avoid swimwear visible from the houses.

Stop 5

Wahiba Sands

Where 100-metre dunes roll across 12,500 km² of wilderness walked by Bedouins for centuries

★★☆☆☆
📍 Wadi Bani Khalid Oman📍 Al Wasil Oman desert📍 Sharqiya Sands Wahiba Sands Oman
1

Stop at Wadi Bani Khalid on the way. Access is by paved road without difficulty. Steps descend to emerald pools carved into white rock that contrasts with the surrounding desert aridity. The lower pools easily fill with local families at weekends - if you're seeking tranquillity, head to the upper pools by walking up the canyon a few hundred metres. Moqal Cave, accessible after a short passage between rocks, shelters bats and an interior body of water.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Sharqiya Sands - long called Wahiba after the local Bedouin tribe - stretch 180 km north-south and 80 km east-west, covering about 12,500 km². The northern dunes can reach 100 m high. The Royal Geographical Society led an expedition here in 1986 and identified 16,000 species of invertebrates in this desert everyone thought was lifeless. Arrive in late afternoon from Al Wasil for the sunset light on the orange dunes - the colours change before your eyes.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Track/sand driving from Al Wasil into the heart of the dunes with a 4x4 (deflate tyres before entering - the camps on site can guide you). If you lack experience on sand, providers at the desert entrance offer transfers. Avoid the sand between April and October: the heat is exhausting.

⏱ 90 min

Walk along dune ridges and stargazing at night: far from any artificial light, the Wahiba Sands sky is one of Oman's best for naked-eye astronomy. Bring warm clothing for the night - even in winter, temperatures drop quickly at dune altitude.

Nizwa

Once the soul of Islamic Oman, now crowned by a drum-shaped fort from the age of empires

★★★★☆
📍 Nizwa Fort Oman📍 Nizwa Souq Oman📍 Nizwa Old Town Oman📍 Falaj Daris Nizwa
1

Nizwa Fort looks like no other: its circular tower, 36 m in diameter and 30 m high, dominates the city like a giant drum. It was built by Imam Sultan bin Saif Al Ya'rubi between 1649 and 1661, on the foundations of a 12th-century castle. From the top, 23 cannons could cover 360°. Climb to the top of the tower to see the sea of date palms surrounding the city, with the Hajar massif as backdrop. Inside, look for the trapdoors in the access corridor: defenders poured boiling date syrup through them onto attackers. Allow 1h30 for the tower and castle.

⏱ 90 min
2

Nizwa Souq adjoins the fort. It's one of the most authentic in the country, frequented as much by locals as tourists. The silver and khanjar section (the curved Omani daggers) is renowned throughout the sultanate. Take time to pass through the unrestored old part (Souq Ash Sharqi) east of the main entrance - fewer tourists, more old-world atmosphere. Don't miss the date stalls: one large shop offers more than fifteen local varieties to taste.

⏱ 75 min
3

In late afternoon, stroll the alleys of the old town at the foot of the ramparts. Spot Falaj Daris - one of five Omani aflaj inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage list. This underground irrigation canal, active for centuries, continues to feed Nizwa's palm groves. If you're here on a Friday, the livestock market runs from 7am to 9am outside the souq: herders in white dishdasha parade goats and sheep before buyers sitting in a circle. It's an unchanging ritual worth the early wake-up.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Drive desert → Nizwa via Bidiyah and Izki: the countryside gradually changes, dunes give way to mountains and palm groves. It's one of the most beautiful landscape transitions of the circuit.

1

Set off early for Jebel Shams. On the way, stop at the Wadi Ghul viewpoint at the canyon entrance: the walls drop sheer into a gorge that can exceed 1,000 m deep in places - the 'Grand Canyon' of Arabia. Jebel Shams is Oman's highest point at 3,009 m, but its summit is military - the paved road climbs to about 1,950 m. From the viewpoint facing the vendors, the plunging view over the canyon is striking.

⏱ 45 min
2

Descending back towards Nizwa, detour to Misfat Al Abriyeen. This oasis village clings to the gorge wall, with its stone and clay houses, terraces of banana and mango trees, and aflaj murmuring between the narrow alleys. The access road is narrow and winding - leave your car at the bottom and walk up. It's one of the best-preserved villages in the Western Hajar.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Mountain driving: Nizwa → 4x4 checkpoint → Jebel Shams plateau → Balcony Walk → Misfat → return Nizwa. The road to the plateau is entirely paved but requires a 4x4 (police check at Birkat Al Mawz). Allow about 6 km of climbing after the checkpoint.

⏱ 180 min

Balcony Walk - trail W6: 8 km return hike from the hamlet of Al Khitaym at 1,900 m altitude. The path runs along the canyon edge and ends at the abandoned village of As Sab beneath a 250 m rock arch. Allow 3 to 4 hours. Red, white and yellow waymarking - impossible to get lost. The wall drops 500 m to the left at times: not recommended for those with vertigo. Bring more water than you think you need - there's nothing on site.

1

Jebel Akhdar - 'the green mountain' - owes its name to the terraced crops covering the Sayq plateau at 2,000 m altitude. Pomegranates, peaches, apricots, walnuts and especially Damask roses are cultivated here, distilled into rosewater by villagers using the same stills for generations. In April, the air is fragrant. Stop at Diana Viewpoint for a panorama over the Wadi al Ayn gorges, before parking the car and walking between the villages of Al Ayn and Al Aqur on trail W18 (waymarked, 1h one way). The terraced crops and stone mosques are worth every metre.

⏱ 120 min
2

Wadi Bani Habib is a canyon cut into the plateau where you leave the car at the end of the road to descend on foot towards the ruins of an old abandoned village. At the bottom of the wadi, fruit trees - peaches, almonds, pomegranates - still grow wild, relics of gardens past. Climb the stairs carved into the rock from the car park for a plunging view over the ruins and vegetation at the canyon floor.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Controlled ascent to Jebel Akhdar (4x4 checkpoint at Birkat Al Mawz), viewpoints by car and short walks between villages. The paved road climbs 32 km from Birkat Al Mawz to the plateau - magnificent in itself, with 180° views over the Hajar Mountains. Descent then towards Muscat via the Al Hamra plain.

Muscat

★★★★☆
📍 Royal Opera House Muscat📍 Bandar Al Rowdha Marina Muscat📍 Riyam Park Muscat
1

The Royal Opera House Muscat, inaugurated in 2011 by order of Sultan Qaboos, is one of the capital's most refined buildings. Even if you don't attend a performance, the exterior is worth the detour: white columns, manicured gardens, architecture blending Islamic style with references to European theatre. Check the programme - international companies perform regularly between October and April.

⏱ 45 min
2

Bait Al Zubair, housed in a restored 19th-century Omani house, is Muscat's most endearing private museum. Its collections cover traditional costumes, silver jewellery, weapons, musical instruments and everyday objects - everything the National Museum treats on a grand scale, here experienced at human scale, in low-ceilinged rooms with wooden beams. Allow 1h.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Boat trip from Bandar Al Rowdha marina: dolphin watching (common and bottlenose dolphins, present year-round in the Gulf of Oman) and snorkelling in Bandar Khayran bay. Operators offer 2 to 3 hour outings. Book the day before in high season.

⏱ 180 min

Climb to Riyam Park in late afternoon: from the car park, a few minutes' walk to the giant white concrete incense burner overlooking the bay. The panorama over Forts Jalali and Mirani, the mountains and dhows below is one of Muscat's best - and the site is almost always empty of tourists.

Muscat

📍 National Museum Oman Muscat Old Muscat📍 Al Mouj Marina Muscat
1

The National Museum of Oman, opened in 2016 in Old Muscat, displays 5,466 objects across 4,000 m² of permanent galleries - prehistory, maritime history, arms and armour, aflaj, Islam. The maritime gallery is particularly remarkable with its dhow models and antique navigation instruments. The museum was the first in the Middle East to adopt Arabic Braille for the visually impaired. Allow 2h. Admission fee, closed Mondays.

⏱ 120 min
2

To finish, head to Al Mouj Marina, northwest of Muscat. This waterfront developed in the 2010s is the opposite of Old Muscat: yachts at berth, terrace restaurants, cafés open in the evening. This is where Muscat shows its contemporary side - and a good spot for a final meal by the water before heading to the airport.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Free time for last-minute shopping at Muttrah Souq: frankincense (Omani incense, exported since antiquity via caravan routes to Egypt and Rome), silver jewellery, decorative khanjars. Avoid the shops directly opposite the cruise ships - prices match the foot traffic.
