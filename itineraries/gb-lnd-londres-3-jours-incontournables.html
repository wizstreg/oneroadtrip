What to See in London: Westminster, Museums & Tower Bridge

📅 7 days📍 21 stops🚗 35 km⏱ 1h10

London racks up superlatives effortlessly: the world's oldest public museum, the planet's largest Turner collection, Crown Jewels guarded by ravens whose legend says they uphold the monarchy. From Westminster to Soho, 21 stops to understand a city that has never stopped reinventing itself.

✨ Highlights
  • Most major museums (British Museum, National Gallery, Tate Modern, Natural History Museum) offer free admission
  • Changing of the Guard at Buckingham Palace (Mon, Wed, Fri, Sun at 11am) - check official calendar
  • Tower Bridge still opens around 800 times per year - lift times available online
  • Notting Hill Carnival in late August: over a million participants across two days
📆 Best period

May, June, September, October

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 7 and 11 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Summer (June-August): Warmest weather, longest daylight, peak tourist season, highest prices, festival season, ideal for all regions.

Spring (April-May): Mild weather, blooming gardens, fewer crowds, moderate prices, excellent for countryside, comfortable temperatures.

Fall (September-October): Pleasant weather, autumn colors, fewer crowds, good value, comfortable for sightseeing, cozy pub atmosphere.

Winter (November-March): Cold temperatures, shortest days, Christmas markets, lowest prices, indoor attractions, cozy cultural experiences.

🌍 What to discover in GB?

Rich history with royal heritage, diverse cultures, and iconic landmarks from London to Edinburgh. Experience pub culture, literary heritage, and beautiful countryside. The country offers excellent transportation with cultural depth and historical significance. United Kingdom provides cultural richness with historical importance and modern amenities in compact island nation.

Day 1

Big Ben

Where Victorian iron and glass tower above Parliament since 1859

★★★★☆
1

Look up the moment you approach Parliament: the Elizabeth Tower - which everyone calls Big Ben - soars to 96 metres. Big Ben is actually the name of the 13.7-tonne bell that first chimed on 11 July 1859, only to crack a few months later. Engineers simply rotated it 90 degrees and reduced the hammer size - and it's been ringing ever since. Each clock face measures 7 metres in diameter and comprises 312 pieces of glass. Since 2023, overseas visitors can book guided tours to climb the 334 steps to the belfry - places sell out fast, so book well ahead on the UK Parliament website.

⏱ 45 min
2

From Westminster Bridge, you get the best overall view of the Elizabeth Tower with the Thames in the foreground. It's the classic starting point for photographing the tower with a red double-decker bus - arrive early morning to avoid the crowds. At night, the Gothic facade of the Palace of Westminster lights up in gold, creating one of the city's most beautiful nocturnal panoramas.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Walk along the north bank of the Thames from Westminster Bridge towards Westminster Pier. You can board a river bus there to reach the South Bank or Tower Bridge in minutes - a far more pleasant way than the Tube to move between riverside sites.

⏱ 45 min

Day 2

Westminster Abbey

A thousand years of coronations within Gothic stone walls

★★★★★
1

Westminster Abbey was built from 1245 on the orders of Henry III, who wanted to rival the great Gothic cathedrals of France. Since William the Conqueror on 25 December 1066, 40 monarchs have been crowned here - the only exceptions are Edward V, likely murdered in the Tower of London before his ceremony, and Edward VIII, who abdicated before the scheduled date. More than 3,300 people are buried here, including 30 sovereigns. Plan at least 90 minutes to see it properly, and take the audio guide - without it, you'll miss a lot.

⏱ 90 min
2

Don't miss Poets' Corner in the south transept: Chaucer, Shakespeare (memorial), Dickens, Tennyson, Kipling are commemorated here. Dickens ended up there against his wishes - he'd requested a quiet burial in Kent. The Dean of Westminster orchestrated the move to add prestige to the place. Lord Byron was refused for his 'scandalous life' - his memorial stone wasn't laid until 1969, 145 years after his death.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Chapter House, accessible from the cloisters, houses Britain's oldest door - dated to the 1050s through dendrochronology. The cloisters themselves are the only free access to the Abbey: if budget is tight, at least enter through there to soak up the atmosphere.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Attend Evensong if your timing allows - the evening choral service is free and open to all. It's an experience with nothing touristy about it: you're in a working abbey, with a professional choir, in one of England's most beautiful Gothic naves. Check timings on the Abbey website before you go.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 1 km · 2min
Day 3

Buckingham Palace

Royal residence where scarlet guards stand watch over monarchy

★★★★☆
1

Buckingham Palace became the official royal residence in 1837 under Queen Victoria - before her, sovereigns lodged at St James's Palace. The East facade you see from The Mall was redesigned in 1913 by Sir Aston Webb, along with the Queen Victoria Memorial in front of the gates. In summer (generally July to September), the State Rooms open to the public - book online, morning slots are less crowded.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Changing of the Guard takes place on Mondays, Wednesdays, Fridays and Sundays at 11am on the Palace forecourt, for a ceremony lasting about 45 minutes. Arrive 60 to 90 minutes early for a good spot - the steps of the Victoria Memorial offer the best elevated view. The ceremony can be cancelled in heavy rain, so check the official calendar that morning on the Household Division website.

⏱ 90 min

Cross St James's Park towards Trafalgar Square via Birdcage Walk. This 23-hectare royal park offers an unusual view of Buckingham Palace from the central bridge - with the lawns and lake, it's one of the best vantage points for the Palace.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 1 km · 2min
Day 4

Trafalgar Square

Where Nelson's column pierces the sky above four bronze lions

★★★★☆
1

Nelson's Column measures 51.5 metres - exactly the distance from HMS Victory's mainmast to the main deck. This is no coincidence. The statue of Nelson at the top (5.5m in Scottish sandstone) looks towards the Admiralty, and towards Cape Trafalgar in Spain where he died in 1805. The bronze lions at the column's base weren't added until 1867 - Landseer, an animal painter (not sculptor), worked from a real lion's corpse, but decomposition had already begun: as a result, the paws resemble a cat's. Also look for the northwest fourth plinth: since 1999 it's hosted rotating contemporary artworks, often surprising.

⏱ 30 min
2

The National Gallery has bordered the square's north side since 1838. Admission is free - which is an anomaly for a museum of this calibre. You'll find around 2,300 works: Van Eyck, Botticelli, Leonardo da Vinci, Rembrandt, Turner, Monet, Van Gogh. If you only have an hour, focus on the Sainsbury Wing rooms for Flemish and Italian primitives. Allow at least 2 hours if you want to cover the essentials.

⏱ 120 min
3

Facing the National Gallery, spot the statue of George Washington - standing on American soil imported specially, as Washington had sworn never to set foot on British ground. It's a gift from the State of Virginia presented in 1921.

⏱ 10 min

Activities

In late afternoon, the square comes alive with street performers and musicians - the atmosphere is very different from the touristy morning. Sit on the fountain steps and watch: Trafalgar Square is one of the few places in London where all layers of the city genuinely intersect.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 1 km · 2min
Day 5

London Eye

A giant wheel turning slowly above the Thames since 2000

★★★★★
1

The London Eye was inaugurated on 31 December 1999 by Tony Blair - but didn't admit its first paying passenger until 9 March 2000. The structure stands 135 metres tall, with 32 air-conditioned capsules (one for each London borough) that can hold 25 people each. The cabins are numbered 1 to 33 - number 13 is deliberately omitted. A complete rotation takes 30 minutes at 0.9 km/h: the wheel doesn't stop, you board while it's moving. From the top, the view extends 40km on a clear day. Book online to skip the queue. For the best light, go an hour before sunset.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The South Bank, along the south bank of the Thames from the London Eye to Tate Modern, is one of London's liveliest pedestrian routes. You'll pass the BFI Southbank, the National Theatre (opened 1976), and under the arches of Hungerford Bridge where street performers play constantly. Count on 45 minutes to an hour if you really stroll.

⏱ 60 min

In the evening, the London Eye lights up with over 5,500 LEDs - the colours change according to events. The view is most spectacular from Westminster Bridge or from the north bank. If you're there on a weekday evening off-season, crowds are noticeably lighter than weekends.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 1 km · 3min
Day 6

Tate Britain

Where Turner's storms and sunsets blaze across gallery walls

★★★★☆
1

Tate Britain, opened in 1897 on the banks of the Thames at Millbank, houses the world's largest collection of works by J.M.W. Turner (1775-1851). Turner bequeathed to the nation nearly 300 oil paintings and some 30,000 watercolours and works on paper - the Clore Gallery, accessible from the main entrance, displays about a hundred works permanently, free of charge. If you only know Turner for his misty seascapes, you'll be surprised: he also painted the 1834 fire of Parliament from the Thames bank, and his painting documenting the scene is one of the museum's most striking.

⏱ 90 min
2

Beyond Turner, Tate Britain covers British art from the 16th century to today - Hogarth, Constable, Bacon, Hockney. If you're there in summer, the central Duveen Hall is a monumental neoclassical nave entrusted each year to an artist for a temporary and free installation.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Take the Tate Boat from Millbank Pier - it links Tate Britain to Tate Modern along the Thames, stopping at the London Eye. It's an original way to connect the two museums in 20 minutes, with views of both banks. The boat ticket includes a discount at the other Tate.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 3 km · 5min
Day 7

Tate Modern

An industrial cathedral reborn as a temple of contemporary art

★★★★★
1

Tate Modern has occupied the former Bankside Power Station since 2000, built between 1947 and 1963 by architect Giles Gilbert Scott - the same man who designed the red London telephone box. The power station closed in 1981 after oil price increases. Swiss architects Herzog & de Meuron won the conversion competition in 1995 from 148 candidates: their masterstroke was preserving the turbine hall intact, an industrial nave 155 metres long and 35 metres high, now dedicated to gigantic installations. Admission to the permanent collections is free.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Blavatnik Building extension, opened in 2016, increased exhibition space by 60%. Go up to level 10 for the free panoramic terrace - you have St Paul's facing you, Tower Bridge to the right, the City to the left. It's one of the best free viewpoints in London.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Cross the Millennium Bridge on foot - this pedestrian bridge, inaugurated in 2000 and closed two days later due to excessive vibrations (reopened in 2002 after correction), directly links Tate Modern to St Paul's Cathedral on the north bank. The view from the middle of the bridge, with St Paul's on one side and Tate on the other, is one of London's finest.

⏱ 20 min

Shakespeare's Globe Theatre is a 5-minute walk east of Tate Modern. This reconstruction of the 1599 Elizabethan theatre opened in 1997 and presents open-air performances from April to October. You can stand in the 'groundling pit' for cheaper access. Even off-season, the guided tour of the theatre is enlightening.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 2 km · 4min
Day 8

Tower Of London

Norman fortress where ravens guard the Crown's glittering secrets

★★★★★
1

The White Tower was built by William the Conqueror from 1078 - it's the building that gives its name to the whole fortress. On a 5-hectare site, the Tower of London has served successively as a royal residence, state prison, armoury, royal menagerie and Royal Mint for over 500 years. The Jewel House contains the Crown Jewels - 23,578 precious stones, including St Edward's Crown (2.23kg of solid gold) and the 317-carat Cullinan II diamond. Book online and arrive early morning - queues for the Jewel House can reach an hour in high season.

⏱ 120 min
2

In 1671, Colonel Thomas Blood attempted to steal the Crown Jewels disguised as a priest. He was arrested steps from the exit. Astonishingly: Charles II not only pardoned him but granted him a royal pension. The Yeomen Warders (the 'Beefeaters') offer guided tours included in the ticket - count on an hour for their route.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Watch for the Tower ravens - by royal decree, at least six must remain at all times, as legend has it that if the ravens leave the Tower, the monarchy will collapse. Each has a name and they're under the care of a Yeoman Warder appointed as Ravenmaster.

⏱ 20 min

Day 9

Tower Bridge

Victorian engineering marvel lifting its Gothic arms over the Thames

★★★★☆
1

Tower Bridge was inaugurated on 30 June 1894 by the future Edward VII. Built over 8 years by 432 workers, it cost £1.18 million. Its Gothic stone appearance is an illusion: the structure is entirely steel, clad in Cornish granite and Portland stone to harmonize with the nearby Tower of London. Each bascule weighs 1,200 tonnes, with a 410-tonne counterweight allowing them to lift in 5 minutes. In 1894, the bascules lifted 6,194 times - 17 times per day. Today: about 800 times a year. Check lift times on the bridge website before your visit.

⏱ 90 min
2

The glass walkways at 43 metres height offer a plunging view over the Thames - the glass floor installed in 2014 lets you look directly beneath your feet at the cars and river. In the Engine Rooms, the original steam engines are still in place: until 1976, 30 people worked here continuously, with over 20 tonnes of coal consumed per week.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

For the best photographs of Tower Bridge, position yourself on the south bank, east side of the bridge - you have the Tower of London in the background and morning light on the north facade. In the evening, from London Bridge (800 metres to the west), the view of illuminated Tower Bridge with the City is unbeatable.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 0 km · 1min
Day 10

St Katharine Docks

Where merchant ships once moored, luxury yachts now drift quietly

★★★★☆
1

St Katharine Docks is the last major historic dock still visible near central London. Opened in 1828 after the demolition of an entire neighbourhood (11,000 people displaced), it was intended for precious goods - ivory, sugar, coffee, wool. Closed in 1968 for failing to adapt to containers, it was converted into a pleasure marina. Today, luxury yachts sit alongside historic barges in a basin metres from the Tower of London. The atmosphere is calm and surprising for anyone arriving from the tourist chaos of Tower Bridge.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Stroll along the pontoons and observe the sailboats and barges moored - some are lived in year-round. The restaurant and bar terraces around the dock are pleasant in late afternoon. From St Katharine Docks, you can walk to Wapping High Street in 10 minutes to see the old warehouses converted into flats - one of the most authentic Docklands neighbourhoods.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 1 km · 3min
Day 11

The Shard

A glass pyramid piercing the clouds above London Bridge

★★★★☆
1

The Shard is designed by Italian architect Renzo Piano, who conceived it as a 95-storey 'vertical city'. The building stands 309.6 metres tall and has dominated London's skyline since its inauguration on 5 July 2012. The viewing platform 'The View from The Shard' occupies levels 68 to 72 - at 244 metres, you have a 360° view extending up to 64 kilometres on a clear day. During 2011 construction, a fox moved into level 72 by entering through the stairs. It survived several weeks feeding on workers' leftovers - it's now called Romeo and is the building's official mascot. Book online, and prefer late afternoon slots for the light on Tower Bridge.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

A 5-minute walk from the Shard, Borough Market is one of London's oldest food markets - present on this site since at least the 13th century. You'll find British and European producers, cheeses, charcuterie, fresh oysters and quality street food. Open Wednesday to Saturday. Avoid Friday lunchtime - it's peak rush.

⏱ 60 min

Southwark Cathedral, steps from the market, is London's oldest Gothic church - its nave dates from the 13th century. Shakespeare baptized his brother Edmund there in 1607. Admission is free and the serenity of the place contrasts radically with the market bustle just outside.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 5 km · 10min
Day 12

Camden

★★★★☆
1

Camden Town takes its name from Charles Pratt, 1st Earl Camden, who developed the area from 1791 intending to make it a genteel residential suburb. The opening of Regent's Canal in 1820, then the railway, transformed the neighbourhood into an industrial hub populated by workers, gin distilleries and warehouses. The first market opened in 1974 with 16 craft stalls. Today, it attracts over 250,000 visitors per week. Weekends are packed - if you can, come on a weekday for a more humane atmosphere.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Stables Market, housed in the old Victorian stables and horse tunnels, is the most authentic part of the market. Horses were used to tow barges along Regent's Canal - in 1939, over 800 horses still passed annually through the stables' veterinary hospital. The brick arches under the railway tracks now house alternative fashion shops, vintage furniture and craft stalls.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walk along Regent's Canal east or west from Camden Lock - it's one of London's most pleasant pedestrian routes, between colourful barges, street art and hidden cafes under bridges. Westward, you reach Regent's Park and London Zoo in 30 minutes on foot. Eastward, you reach King's Cross and its converted warehouses.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 2 km · 3min
Day 13

Regents Park

Royal parkland where roses bloom and theatre echoes under stars

★★★★☆
1

Regent's Park was designed by John Nash from 1811 commissioned by the Prince Regent (future George IV), who wanted a royal residence surrounded by gardens. The residence never materialized, but the 166 hectares of parkland remained. Queen Mary's Gardens contain one of England's largest rose gardens with over 12,000 roses of 85 varieties - at their peak in June. The Open Air Theatre, active since 1932, presents Shakespeare plays and musicals from April to September.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Climb Primrose Hill, the hill north of the park that peaks at 78 metres - it's one of the best free panoramas over London's skyline, with the Shard, London Eye and City clearly identifiable. The spot is particularly popular with Londoners in the evening and on weekends.

⏱ 45 min

London Zoo is on the park's northern edge since 1828 - it's the world's oldest scientific zoo still in operation. Allow half a day if you want to see it all. The Gorilla Kingdom, designed by architect Berthold Lubetkin in the 1930s, is listed for its modernist architecture.

⏱ 120 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 2 km · 5min
Day 14

British Museum

Where the Rosetta Stone unlocked the voice of ancient empires

★★★★★
1

The British Museum opened in 1759 - it's the world's first public national museum. Its 8 million objects span two million years of human history. The Rosetta Stone, its most visited object, is displayed in Room 4: carved in 196 BC, it bears the same decree in hieroglyphs, Demotic and Greek. It was this parallel that enabled Champollion to decipher hieroglyphs in 1822, opening ancient Egypt to modern understanding. It arrived in London in 1802 after French capitulation in Egypt - its potential return to Egypt is an active debate. Admission is free, plan at least 2.5 hours.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Great Court, covered by a glass dome designed by Norman Foster and opened in 2000, is Europe's largest covered public square. It surrounds the circular Reading Room from 1857, where Marx, Lenin and Darwin worked. The north wing houses the Greek rooms with the Parthenon friezes, which Greece has been claiming back since 1983.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

If the collections are too vast to digest alone, several operators offer themed guided tours of 1.5 to 2 hours concentrating on the 20 most important works. The museum app offers free self-guided audio tours in several languages. The Bloomsbury neighbourhood around the museum deserves a walk - its 18th-century Georgian squares are among London's finest.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 2 km · 3min
Day 15

Piccadilly Circus

Neon lights blazing above an angel mistaken for Eros

★★★★☆
1

Piccadilly Circus was created in 1819 at the junction of Piccadilly and Regent Street, laid out by John Nash for the Prince Regent. The Shaftesbury Memorial Fountain at the centre - often mistaken for Eros - actually represents the Angel of Christian Charity. It was inaugurated in 1893 and is the first statue cast in aluminium in London. The giant illuminated advertising panels appeared in the 1910s; they were switched off during both world wars for blackout. Piccadilly Circus is far livelier in the evening than during the day.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

From Piccadilly Circus, Carnaby Street is a 5-minute walk north - symbol of Swinging London in the 1960s, when fashion boutiques and musicians like the Rolling Stones converged there. The surrounding network of alleys (Kingly Court, Newburgh Street) retains an independent character. More elegant, Regent Street deserves a walk for its Georgian architecture.

⏱ 60 min

Leicester Square, a 3-minute walk east, is the heart of the West End's theatre and cinema district. The TKTS booth on the square sells discounted tickets for evening shows on the day - if you want to see a musical without paying full price, come in early afternoon.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 1 km · 2min
Day 16

Covent Garden

Where street performers spin magic beneath iron and glass

★★★★☆
1

The Covent Garden Piazza was laid out in the 1630s by architect Inigo Jones commissioned by the 4th Earl of Bedford - it's London's first residential square, inspired by Italian piazzas. Market gardeners settled there from 1654, then theatres, taverns. In 1662, Samuel Pepys notes in his diary seeing Britain's first Punch and Judy show there. The market moved in 1974. The central neoclassical building with its cast-iron glass canopy dates from 1830. Today, street performers who play under the portico of St Paul's Church must pass an audition to get their pitch.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Royal Opera House, built in its current form in 1858, is the third theatre on this site - the previous two burned down. Here Handel's first operas were created from 1735, and the Royal Ballet opened its inaugural season in 1946. You can freely go up to the Floral Hall terrace for a view over the piazza.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The London Transport Museum, in the former flower hall on the southeast corner of the Piazza, traces 200 years of London transport - Victorian horse-drawn trams, early Tube trains, 1930s motor coaches. If you don't go in, Seven Dials is worth the detour: this seven-street junction, laid out in 1693, retains its independent shops and neighbourhood cafes.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 6 km · 11min
Day 17

Notting Hill

Pastel terraces hiding the soul of Europe's greatest carnival

★★★☆☆
1

Notting Hill draws its character from its complex social history: a poor neighbourhood in the 1950s, it welcomed a large Caribbean community who launched the Notting Hill Carnival in 1966 - today Europe's largest street carnival with over a million participants each late August. The pastel facades you see resulted from 1980s-90s gentrification. Portobello Road is the market's main street: on Saturdays, antiques and bric-a-brac stalls extend nearly a kilometre from Notting Hill Gate to Golborne Road. Avoid Saturdays if you hate crowds.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Electric Cinema on Portobello Road is one of Britain's oldest working cinemas - opened in 1910. Its interior preserves its leather armchairs and two-seater sofas. Westbourne Grove, the street parallel to Portobello, concentrates the neighbourhood's best independent fashion and design shops - more local atmosphere, less touristy.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 2 km · 5min
Day 18

Natural History Museum

Where a blue whale dives forever through Victorian air

★★★★★
1

The Natural History Museum opened in 1881 in its neo-Romanesque South Kensington building designed by Alfred Waterhouse. Hintze Hall has housed Hope since 2017 - a female blue whale 25.2 metres long (221 bones, 4.5 tonnes) stranded in Wexford harbour, Ireland in March 1891 and purchased by the Museum for £250. She's suspended from the ceiling in a diving plunge posture - the only blue whale in the world presented in this position. In the 1960s, an estimated 400 blue whales remained worldwide; it was in London in 1966 that their commercial hunting was banned. Admission is free.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Dinosaur galleries (Blue Zone) house some of the most complete fossils ever found in the UK. The Minerals gallery (Red Zone) includes fragments of Martian and lunar meteorites. Two hours minimum to see it properly.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

South Kensington concentrates three free national museums within a 300-metre radius: the Natural History Museum, Science Museum and Victoria & Albert Museum. If time permits, push on to the V&A - its fashion, design and decorative arts collections are among the world's finest.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 1 km · 3min
Day 19

Hyde Park

Royal wilderness where speakers shout and swans glide serenely

★★★★★
1

Hyde Park covers 142 hectares in the heart of London - Henry VIII made it a royal hunting park in 1536 and it remains Crown property. The Serpentine, a 40-acre artificial lake dug in 1730, offers swimming, pedalos and kayaking in summer. Speaker's Corner, in the park's northeast corner since 1872, is one of the few places in the world where anyone can speak on any subject without prior permission - the tradition dates back to public executions on this site in the 18th century. On Sunday mornings, speakers still debate everything and nothing.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

In summer, the Serpentine Lido offers open-water swimming in the lake - a practice available since 1930. The Serpentine Galleries (two pavilions on the lake shores) exhibit contemporary art for free. Each summer, a world-renowned architect designs a temporary pavilion - Zaha Hadid, Frank Gehry, Toyo Ito have all participated.

⏱ 60 min

Kensington Gardens, adjacent to Hyde Park, houses Kensington Palace - an official residence since the 17th century and Princess Diana's home until 1997. The Kensington State Rooms are open to the public during the day. The Kensington neighbourhood to the south is one of London's finest for a walk through Victorian residential streets.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 2 km · 4min
Day 20

Oxford Street

Europe's busiest shopping artery pulsing with half a million souls daily

★★★★☆
1

Oxford Street is Europe's busiest shopping street - around 500,000 visitors daily at peak times. The street follows the route of an old Roman road and took on its commercial vocation in the 18th century. Selfridges (opened in 1909 by American Harry Gordon Selfridge, who introduced the concept of decorative window displays to London) is the most emblematic - its Christmas windows are an annual event. Avoid Saturday afternoons if you're allergic to crowds.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Bond Street, a parallel street to the north, is the address of fashion houses and jewellers - Cartier, Tiffany, Burberry, Chanel have their flagship stores there. Mayfair, the residential neighbourhood south of Bond Street, is one of the world's most expensive in real estate terms and one of the finest for an aimless stroll. If you play Monopoly, you're on the board's most expensive square.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 1 km · 1min
Day 21

Soho

Where outcasts and artists built London's beating bohemian heart

★★★★☆
1

Soho developed from the 17th century as the neighbourhood of Huguenot refugees fleeing France after the Revocation of the Edict of Nantes in 1685. This tradition of welcoming marginalized communities persists: Soho successively became the Victorian artistic bohemia neighbourhood, of jazz in the 1950s, of rock in the 1960s, then the heart of Britain's LGBTQ+ community. Old Compton Street has been its living centre since the 1970s. In the evening, Wardour Street and its surroundings concentrate an exceptional density of bars, restaurants and concert venues for such a small area.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The West End begins east of Soho - Shaftesbury Avenue alone concentrates six theatres. The TKTS booth on Leicester Square sells discounted tickets for evening performances on the day - turn up in early afternoon to have choice. London theatres often offer 'standing tickets' sold an hour before the show for a fraction of the price.

⏱ 180 min

For dinner, Soho offers one of London's most diverse restaurant offerings in such a compact area - Vietnamese cuisine on Wardour Street, Japanese izakayas on Brewer Street, trattorias on Dean Street. Frith Street houses one of London's oldest jazz clubs still active, open since the 1950s - check the programme before you go.

⏱ 120 min
