Big Ben

★★★★★
1

Look up the moment you approach Parliament: the Elizabeth Tower - which everyone calls Big Ben - soars to 96 metres. Big Ben is actually the name of the 13.7-tonne bell that first chimed on 11 July 1859, only to crack a few months later. Engineers simply rotated it 90 degrees and reduced the hammer size - and it's been ringing ever since. Each clock face measures 7 metres in diameter and comprises 312 pieces of glass. Since 2023, overseas visitors can book guided tours to climb the 334 steps to the belfry - places sell out fast, so book well ahead on the UK Parliament website.

⏱ 45 min
2

From Westminster Bridge, you get the best overall view of the Elizabeth Tower with the Thames in the foreground. It's the classic starting point for photographing the tower with a red double-decker bus - arrive early morning to avoid the crowds. At night, the Gothic facade of the Palace of Westminster lights up in gold, creating one of the city's most beautiful nocturnal panoramas.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Walk along the north bank of the Thames from Westminster Bridge towards Westminster Pier. You can board a river bus there to reach the South Bank or Tower Bridge in minutes - a far more pleasant way than the Tube to move between riverside sites.

Westminster Abbey

★★★★★
1

Westminster Abbey was built from 1245 on the orders of Henry III, who wanted to rival the great Gothic cathedrals of France. Since William the Conqueror on 25 December 1066, 40 monarchs have been crowned here - the only exceptions are Edward V, likely murdered in the Tower of London before his ceremony, and Edward VIII, who abdicated before the scheduled date. More than 3,300 people are buried here, including 30 sovereigns. Plan at least 90 minutes to see it properly, and take the audio guide - without it, you'll miss a lot.

⏱ 90 min
2

Don't miss Poets' Corner in the south transept: Chaucer, Shakespeare (memorial), Dickens, Tennyson, Kipling are commemorated here. Dickens ended up there against his wishes - he'd requested a quiet burial in Kent. The Dean of Westminster orchestrated the move to add prestige to the place. Lord Byron was refused for his 'scandalous life' - his memorial stone wasn't laid until 1969, 145 years after his death.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Chapter House, accessible from the cloisters, houses Britain's oldest door - dated to the 1050s through dendrochronology. The cloisters themselves are the only free access to the Abbey: if budget is tight, at least enter through there to soak up the atmosphere.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Attend Evensong if your timing allows - the evening choral service is free and open to all. It's an experience with nothing touristy about it: you're in a working abbey, with a professional choir, in one of England's most beautiful Gothic naves. Check timings on the Abbey website before you go.

⏱ 60 min

Buckingham Palace

★★★★★
1

Buckingham Palace became the official royal residence in 1837 under Queen Victoria - before her, sovereigns lodged at St James's Palace. The East facade you see from The Mall was redesigned in 1913 by Sir Aston Webb, along with the Queen Victoria Memorial in front of the gates. In summer (generally July to September), the State Rooms open to the public - book online, morning slots are less crowded.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Changing of the Guard takes place on Mondays, Wednesdays, Fridays and Sundays at 11am on the Palace forecourt, for a ceremony lasting about 45 minutes. Arrive 60 to 90 minutes early for a good spot - the steps of the Victoria Memorial offer the best elevated view. The ceremony can be cancelled in heavy rain, so check the official calendar that morning on the Household Division website.

⏱ 90 min

Cross St James's Park towards Trafalgar Square via Birdcage Walk. This 23-hectare royal park offers an unusual view of Buckingham Palace from the central bridge - with the lawns and lake, it's one of the best vantage points for the Palace.

Trafalgar Square

★★★★☆
1

Nelson's Column measures 51.5 metres - exactly the distance from HMS Victory's mainmast to the main deck. This is no coincidence. The statue of Nelson at the top (5.5m in Scottish sandstone) looks towards the Admiralty, and towards Cape Trafalgar in Spain where he died in 1805. The bronze lions at the column's base weren't added until 1867 - Landseer, an animal painter (not sculptor), worked from a real lion's corpse, but decomposition had already begun: as a result, the paws resemble a cat's. Also look for the northwest fourth plinth: since 1999 it's hosted rotating contemporary artworks, often surprising.

⏱ 30 min
2

The National Gallery has bordered the square's north side since 1838. Admission is free - which is an anomaly for a museum of this calibre. You'll find around 2,300 works: Van Eyck, Botticelli, Leonardo da Vinci, Rembrandt, Turner, Monet, Van Gogh. If you only have an hour, focus on the Sainsbury Wing rooms for Flemish and Italian primitives. Allow at least 2 hours if you want to cover the essentials.

⏱ 120 min
3

Facing the National Gallery, spot the statue of George Washington - standing on American soil imported specially, as Washington had sworn never to set foot on British ground. It's a gift from the State of Virginia presented in 1921.

⏱ 10 min

Activities

In late afternoon, the square comes alive with street performers and musicians - the atmosphere is very different from the touristy morning. Sit on the fountain steps and watch: Trafalgar Square is one of the few places in London where all layers of the city genuinely intersect.

⏱ 30 min

London Eye

★★★★☆
1

The London Eye was inaugurated on 31 December 1999 by Tony Blair - but didn't admit its first paying passenger until 9 March 2000. The structure stands 135 metres tall, with 32 air-conditioned capsules (one for each London borough) that can hold 25 people each. The cabins are numbered 1 to 33 - number 13 is deliberately omitted. A complete rotation takes 30 minutes at 0.9 km/h: the wheel doesn't stop, you board while it's moving. From the top, the view extends 40km on a clear day. Book online to skip the queue. For the best light, go an hour before sunset.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The South Bank, along the south bank of the Thames from the London Eye to Tate Modern, is one of London's liveliest pedestrian routes. You'll pass the BFI Southbank, the National Theatre (opened 1976), and under the arches of Hungerford Bridge where street performers play constantly. Count on 45 minutes to an hour if you really stroll.

⏱ 60 min

In the evening, the London Eye lights up with over 5,500 LEDs - the colours change according to events. The view is most spectacular from Westminster Bridge or from the north bank. If you're there on a weekday evening off-season, crowds are noticeably lighter than weekends.

⏱ 30 min

Tate Britain

★★★★☆
1

Tate Britain, opened in 1897 on the banks of the Thames at Millbank, houses the world's largest collection of works by J.M.W. Turner (1775-1851). Turner bequeathed to the nation nearly 300 oil paintings and some 30,000 watercolours and works on paper - the Clore Gallery, accessible from the main entrance, displays about a hundred works permanently, free of charge. If you only know Turner for his misty seascapes, you'll be surprised: he also painted the 1834 fire of Parliament from the Thames bank, and his painting documenting the scene is one of the museum's most striking.

⏱ 90 min
2

Beyond Turner, Tate Britain covers British art from the 16th century to today - Hogarth, Constable, Bacon, Hockney. If you're there in summer, the central Duveen Hall is a monumental neoclassical nave entrusted each year to an artist for a temporary and free installation.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Take the Tate Boat from Millbank Pier - it links Tate Britain to Tate Modern along the Thames, stopping at the London Eye. It's an original way to connect the two museums in 20 minutes, with views of both banks. The boat ticket includes a discount at the other Tate.

Tate Modern

★★★★☆
1

Tate Modern has occupied the former Bankside Power Station since 2000, built between 1947 and 1963 by architect Giles Gilbert Scott - the same man who designed the red London telephone box. The power station closed in 1981 after oil price increases. Swiss architects Herzog & de Meuron won the conversion competition in 1995 from 148 candidates: their masterstroke was preserving the turbine hall intact, an industrial nave 155 metres long and 35 metres high, now dedicated to gigantic installations. Admission to the permanent collections is free.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Blavatnik Building extension, opened in 2016, increased exhibition space by 60%. Go up to level 10 for the free panoramic terrace - you have St Paul's facing you, Tower Bridge to the right, the City to the left. It's one of the best free viewpoints in London.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Cross the Millennium Bridge on foot - this pedestrian bridge, inaugurated in 2000 and closed two days later due to excessive vibrations (reopened in 2002 after correction), directly links Tate Modern to St Paul's Cathedral on the north bank. The view from the middle of the bridge, with St Paul's on one side and Tate on the other, is one of London's finest.

⏱ 20 min

Shakespeare's Globe Theatre is a 5-minute walk east of Tate Modern. This reconstruction of the 1599 Elizabethan theatre opened in 1997 and presents open-air performances from April to October. You can stand in the 'groundling pit' for cheaper access. Even off-season, the guided tour of the theatre is enlightening.

Tower Of London

★★★★★
1

The White Tower was built by William the Conqueror from 1078 - it's the building that gives its name to the whole fortress. On a 5-hectare site, the Tower of London has served successively as a royal residence, state prison, armoury, royal menagerie and Royal Mint for over 500 years. The Jewel House contains the Crown Jewels - 23,578 precious stones, including St Edward's Crown (2.23kg of solid gold) and the 317-carat Cullinan II diamond. Book online and arrive early morning - queues for the Jewel House can reach an hour in high season.

⏱ 120 min
2

In 1671, Colonel Thomas Blood attempted to steal the Crown Jewels disguised as a priest. He was arrested steps from the exit. Astonishingly: Charles II not only pardoned him but granted him a royal pension. The Yeomen Warders (the 'Beefeaters') offer guided tours included in the ticket - count on an hour for their route.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Tower Bridge

★★★★☆
1

Tower Bridge was inaugurated on 30 June 1894 by the future Edward VII. Built over 8 years by 432 workers, it cost £1.18 million. Its Gothic stone appearance is an illusion: the structure is entirely steel, clad in Cornish granite and Portland stone to harmonize with the nearby Tower of London. ⏱ 60 min

Kensington Gardens, adjacent to Hyde Park, houses Kensington Palace - an official residence since the 17th century and Princess Diana's home until 1997. The Kensington State Rooms are open to the public during the day. The Kensington neighbourhood to the south is one of London's finest for a walk through Victorian residential streets.
