☀️ Weather

Dry Season (May-September): Peak tourist season with minimal rainfall, clear skies, and excellent conditions for Machu Picchu and highland trekking. Highest prices but best weather.

Shoulder Season (April, October): Good weather with moderate crowds and prices. Excellent balance of conditions and value, still dry enough for most activities.

Wet Season (November-March): Higher rainfall, especially in highlands, but lush landscapes and fewer tourists. Inca Trail closed in February, coastal areas remain accessible.

Regional Variations: Coast has minimal rainfall year-round, highlands have distinct wet/dry seasons, Amazon rainforest hot and humid with variable precipitation.

Lima

Peru's gastronomic capital between colonial quarters and Pacific cliffs

★★★★★
1

Lima is a city of 11 million people sprawling through the coastal fog facing the Pacific—and the Plaza Mayor is where it all began. Pizarro founded the city right here on January 18, 1535, and the bronze fountain at the center of the square dates from 1650: it's been standing since Louis XIV was a child. Take your time and turn slowly on your heel. On one side, the Government Palace, where the changing of the guard happens daily at noon to the sound of El Cóndor Pasa. Across from it, the 17th-century baroque cathedral, rebuilt multiple times after earthquakes—Francisco Pizarro himself is buried inside. The atmosphere in late afternoon, when the plaza fills with Limeños and pigeons take flight in mass formations, is that of a city that has seen everything for five centuries.

⏱ 60 min
2

A few steps from the Plaza Mayor, the San Francisco convent is one of the most impressive colonial constructions in Latin America. Work began as early as 1535, but the complex took its current form in the 17th century after the devastating 1655 earthquake. Don't miss the catacombs: an underground labyrinth where approximately 70,000 bodies were interred between the 17th and 19th centuries, with bones arranged in geometric patterns in open wells. The library holds 20,000 volumes including some 15th-century incunabula. The guided tour lasts about 45 minutes, in Spanish or English.

⏱ 75 min
3

In Miraflores, the Parque del Amor marks the start of the Malecón, the clifftop promenade that runs along the Pacific. The park takes its name from the large mosaic by artist Victor Delfín depicting an embracing couple. Follow the Malecón north or south to enjoy ocean views and watch paragliders launching from the cliff edge. In late afternoon, the light on the water is particularly beautiful.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

In the evening, head up to Barranco, Lima's bohemian quarter, just a few minutes by taxi from Miraflores. Cross the Bridge of Sighs (Puente de los Suspiros), a 19th-century wooden bridge that Limeños cross with their eyes closed so their wishes will come true. Then descend the Bajada de los Baños, a colorful street-art-covered alley that tumbles down to the beach, before climbing back up to Barranco's central plaza, where free concerts happen on some evenings. The neighborhood's restaurants and bars stay lively late into the night.

Lima

Peru's gastronomic capital between colonial quarters and Pacific cliffs

★★★★★
1

The Museo Larco, in the Pueblo Libre neighborhood, is probably the finest pre-Columbian art museum in South America. Founded in 1926 by Rafael Larco Hoyle, it's housed in an 18th-century colonial mansion built atop a 7th-century pyramid. Its collection exceeds 45,000 pieces—ceramics, textiles, gold and silver—spanning 4,000 years of history. What sets it apart: its storage facility is open to the public, allowing you to wander among shelves lined with tens of thousands of ceramics. The erotic gallery, dedicated to Mochica pieces related to fertility rituals (100-800 CE), is one of the most famous in the world. Allow at least 2 hours.

⏱ 120 min
2

In late afternoon, on an ordinary Miraflores street—between a Starbucks and a real estate agency—a 25-meter-high pyramid rises without warning. The Huaca Pucllana has been here since the 5th century, built by the Lima civilization long before the Incas. In the 1940s, real estate developers wanted to demolish it to build apartments—it wasn't excavated and protected until 1981. Today seven pyramids are visible across the six-hectare site, and archaeologists still work continuously among the adobe bricks. Go in the evening if you can (Wednesday to Sunday, 7pm to 10pm): the site is illuminated, the pyramids cut against Lima's dark sky with lit skyscrapers in the background—it's the image of this city in a single frame.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

In the morning, before the Larco museum, make a detour through the Surquillo No. 1 market, a stone's throw from Parque Kennedy in Miraflores. It's one of the best places in Lima to see the ingredients of Peruvian cuisine: dozens of potato varieties, multicolored aji peppers, exotic Amazonian fruits. It's lively, photogenic, and far less touristy than the downtown markets.

Paracas

Coastal desert where sea lions and Humboldt penguins populate the rocky Ballestas Islands

★★☆☆☆
1

The Ballestas Islands excursion is the highlight of the Paracas stop—book it the evening before or the morning of from the El Chaco port. Boats depart between 8am and 10:30am. En route to the islands, the pilot skirts the cliff to show the Candelabra, a 180-meter geoglyph carved into a hillside's sand, whose origin remains debated (Paracas culture, over 2,000 years ago). Then the islands draw closer and the sound show begins before the visual one: thousands of birds—Guanay cormorants, pelicans, boobies—and colonies of South American sea lions occupy every rock. The Humboldt penguin, the only penguin species in Peru, is also visible. You don't disembark on the islands, but the view from the boat is more than sufficient. Bring a windbreaker: the sea breeze is cold even in summer.

⏱ 120 min
2

In the afternoon, head into the Paracas National Reserve, created in 1975 across 335,000 hectares of desert and coastline. Many travelers stop at the Ballestas Islands and miss what's often the best part of this stop. Stop at Playa Roja, a beach whose sand pulls toward reddish-ochre due to the presence of feldspar—the contrast with the Pacific blue is striking. Continue to the cliffs of La Catedral, a large natural arch that once jutted into the sea and collapsed in 2007 during an earthquake. The landscape—arid desert plunging into the ocean—is one of Peru's most singular.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Arriving from Lima (~3.5 hours by road), settle in and take an evening stroll through the El Chaco village. The waterfront is lively in the evening, with several restaurants serving ceviche and fresh fish. It's a chance to eat some of Peru's best ceviche right where the fish comes out of the water.

Huacachina

South America's only natural oasis ringed by hundred-meter-high dunes

★★★☆☆
1

Huacachina is a natural lagoon formed by groundwater that surfaces in the Ica desert—the only true natural oasis in South America according to most geographers. The oasis is surrounded by palm trees and ringed by dunes reaching locally 100 meters in height. The village of 115 residents that developed around the lake was long a spa resort for wealthy Lima families (as early as the 1940s). Walk around the lagoon (about 30 minutes), then climb the crest of the nearest dune before 10am or after 4pm—the heat is intense at midday. Local legend tells of an Inca princess who, fleeing a hunter, wept and created the lake from her tears.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The main Huacachina activity: dune buggy followed by sandboarding, in late afternoon. Operators offer outings in four-wheeled buggies (carros areneros) that tear through the dunes with an experienced driver. At stops, you descend slopes on sandboards, either standing for the more skilled or flat on your stomach for beginners. The outing typically ends at sunset from atop a dune. Count on about 2 hours. Bring sunglasses and a scarf: sand flies everywhere.

⏱ 120 min

A visit to a bodega in Ica, 5km from the oasis, is a lovely way to start the day. The Ica region is the birthplace of Peruvian pisco—the city has over 80 artisanal cellars. Operators offer tours with tastings of pisco sour prepared on-site.

⏱ 90 min

Nazca

Desert plain where the Nazca culture carved its mysterious giant geoglyphs

★★★★☆
1

The Nazca Lines flyover in a small plane is the day's central activity—and the only way to truly grasp the site's scale. The geoglyphs were traced by the Nazca culture between 200 BCE and 600 CE across 500 km² of desert, by removing the layer of dark oxidized stones to reveal the lighter soil beneath. Among the most striking figures: the hummingbird (93m long), the spider, the monkey (150m), the condor, and the astronaut. Inscribed as UNESCO heritage in 1994, their function remains debated—the most solid current hypotheses link them to water-related rituals. The flight lasts 30 to 35 minutes. Warning: the plane banks alternately right and left—if you're prone to motion sickness, notify the pilot in advance. Book the day before from the Nazca aerodrome.

⏱ 75 min
2

In the afternoon, head to the Chauchilla cemetery, about twenty kilometers from Nazca, with a local operator. This open-air necropolis in the desert was used successively by the Nazca and Wari cultures. Mummies are displayed in situ in their opened tombs—some still retain their long hair, funerary textiles, and facial expressions, thanks to the extremely dry climate. The site is impressive, sometimes disturbing, and won't suit all travelers. The preservation is remarkable across more than 1,000 years.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

If you prefer to avoid the plane flight, the alternative is the Nazca mirador, 20km north of town, from which you can observe three geoglyphs directly from the ground: the tree, the hands, and the lizard. The tracing technique—the contrast between dark stones and light soil—is clearly visible at this scale. Entry is modest.

Arequipa

The white city in volcanic sillar dominated by El Misti and its 16th-century monastery

★★★★☆
1

After nine hours on the road from Nazca—or an overnight bus waking in the dark at improbable altitudes—Arequipa first announces itself through El Misti, that perfect 5,822-meter volcano dominating the city like a stage set. Then the houses appear, all in the same cream color, nearly white: that's sillar, a porous volcanic stone that Arequipeños have been quarrying since the 16th century. The entire city is built from the lava of its own volcanoes. Drop your bags, have a coca tea for the altitude (we're at 2,330m), and take a first turn around the Plaza de Armas: it's one of the most beautiful colonial plazas on the continent, UNESCO-listed in 2000, and after the dunes and desert of recent days, its arcades and café terraces create an immediate desire to stay.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Yanahuara mirador is a ten-minute walk from the center. You reach it via a lovely neighborhood plaza, with its church and old men on benches—and then you pass under a series of white sillar arches engraved with verses by Arequipa authors speaking of their city. On the other side of the arches: El Misti, alone in the sky, perfectly conical. In late afternoon, when the sun descends on its left, the summit gradually reddens. If you have just one photo to bring back from Arequipa, this is where to take it.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Explore the San Lázaro quarter, Arequipa's oldest, a few steps from the Plaza de Armas. Its cobbled streets and small white sillar houses give a sense of the 16th-century city. It's also in this area that you'll find several picanterías—Arequipa's traditional restaurants, serving dishes like rocoto relleno (stuffed hot pepper) or chupe de camarones (river shrimp soup). If you arrive by overnight bus, take advantage of the afternoon to rest before tomorrow's big day.

⏱ 90 min

Arequipa

The white city in volcanic sillar dominated by El Misti and its 16th-century monastery

★★★★☆
1

The Santa Catalina monastery is the unmissable site in Arequipa. Founded in 1579 by Doña María de Guzmán, a wealthy widow who gave everything for its construction, this 20,000 m² (two-hectare) complex housed up to 500 nuns living entirely cut off from the outside world—until 1970, when it opened its doors to the public. It resembles a miniature village: streets with Andalusian names (Calle Córdoba, Calle Sevilla), flowered patios, fountains, private cells with walls painted red, blue, and white. Everything is built in volcanic sillar. Allow at least 2 hours, ideally starting with a guided tour (available in French), then a second self-guided visit for photos.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Museum of Andean Sanctuaries (Museo Santuarios Andinos) houses Juanita, the 'Ice Maiden'—one of the world's best-preserved mummies. This Inca girl of approximately 12 to 14 years was discovered in September 1995 by archaeologist Johan Reinhard on the Ampato volcano (6,310m), where she had been sacrificed around 1450 during the Capacocha ritual. She's preserved in a refrigerated capsule maintained around -20°C. Note: from January to April, Juanita is placed in conservation rest and replaced by other mummies. The mandatory guided tour (about 1 hour, available in French) begins with a 20-minute documentary on the discovery expedition.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

End the day at the San Camilo market, downtown Arequipa's large covered market. Fruits, Andean vegetables, spices, local cheeses, freshly squeezed juices—this is where the city provisions itself. The city's picanterías typically serve until early afternoon: don't miss the solterito, a cold salad made with corn, fava beans, cheese, and olives that's quintessentially Arequipeño.

Puno

Lakeside city at 3,812m on Titicaca, birthplace of the Uros reed islands

★★★★☆
1

The Uros floating islands are 6km from Puno port, a 30-minute boat ride. These artificial islands—between 60 and 120 depending on sources—are entirely constructed from successive layers of totora reeds that grow in the lake. The technique is ancestral: totora roots form a floating base, onto which are laid dried reed layers 80cm thick, anchored by eucalyptus poles. The lower layers rot and are constantly replaced. On site, residents explain how to build an island, how their community functions—and will offer you a short outing in a balsa, the traditional totora boat. The tourist visit is dense: if you prefer a calmer atmosphere, ask to see the Titinos islands, a less-frequented neighboring community.

⏱ 120 min
2

Taquile Island, 2 hours by boat from Puno, lives at a rhythm hardly changed in centuries. On this island without cars or electricity (aside from a few solar panels), men have been knitting since age 8—they make the hats and textiles, while women handle weaving. Hat color indicates marital status: white for bachelors, red for married men. This textile art has been UNESCO intangible heritage since 2008. The lake views from the island's terraces, at 3,950m altitude, are among the journey's finest. The lunch served on-site—quinoa soup, grilled trout, herbal tea—is simple and delicious.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

The Uros + Taquile excursion is ideally done as a day trip from Puno port, with a local operator. Leave early (before 8am) to see the Uros in the morning, then navigate to Taquile for lunch and afternoon. Bring good walking shoes as Taquile is explored on foot via sloping trails, and sunscreen—at this altitude, the sun burns even on cloudy days.

Cusco

Former Inca capital where stone foundations still support Spanish convents

★★★★★
1

The Puno → Cusco tourist bus route with stops is one of the journey's most beautiful road days. In one direction, the flat gray pampa extending toward Titicaca. In the other, the first slopes of the Vilcanota valley, green, descending toward Cusco. Women in colorful aprons sell wool hats and hot chicha from blankets laid on the roadside. Behind them, snow-capped peaks. Get back on the bus before altitude sickness sets in.

Raqchi is one of the least-visited and most surprising Inca sites. The temple of Wiracocha (creator god in Inca cosmology) is the only Inca building with two naves and the only one with interior columns—22 columns in stone and adobe. 3

The village of Andahuaylillas houses the 'Sistine Chapel of the Andes': a seemingly modest village church whose interior is entirely covered in 17th-century baroque frescoes of exceptional density and fineness. The Jesuits commissioned local artisans to paint biblical scenes with Andean faces. If you haven't seen Cusco's frescoes, this is where you'll grasp their measure.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Arrival in Cusco in the evening. The former Inca capital, at 3,400m altitude, is one of the continent's most history-dense cities. The first evening is ideal for a relaxed stroll around the Plaza de Armas, without rushing. The illuminated facades of the cathedral and La Compañía convent are spectacular. Restaurants around the plaza serve good versions of ceviche de trucha and lomo saltado.

You climb from downtown Cusco on foot—30 to 40 minutes on a gentle slope—and then the walls appear suddenly, without warning. The fortress overlooks the entire city, and in clear weather, the view extends to the mountains closing the valley.

2

At 9km from Cusco, Tambomachay is an Inca hydraulic site: carved stone channels continuously flow spring water into ceremonial niches. Water was sacred to the Incas, and this site beautifully illustrates that.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Ollantaytambo

Living Inca village with its fortress of fifty-ton monoliths

★★★★☆
1

Chinchero, 28km from Cusco at 3,762m altitude, is known for its textiles. Women in traditional costume give demonstrations of natural dyeing and weaving—it's educational and authentic. The colonial church built on Inca foundations deserves a look, as do the surrounding ruins.

⏱ 60 min
2

Moray is one of the most surprising Inca sites in the Sacred Valley. It's not a fortress or temple: it's an agricultural research center. Concentric circular terraces sink into the earth like a natural amphitheater, creating a 15°C temperature difference between the site's top and bottom. The Incas tested crops from different altitudes and regions here—which partly explains the 2,000 potato varieties cultivated in Peru.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Maras salt pans are a unique visual spectacle: 3,000 terraced pools fed by a natural salt spring compose a white, ochre, and brown mosaic depending on harvest state. These salt pans have existed since before the Incas and are now cooperatively managed by local families. Annual production is 160 to 200 tons. Stop on the access road for the panoramic view of the entire site.

⏱ 60 min
4

Pisac has Inca ruins perched on a ridge above the modern village—the ensemble is military, religious, and agricultural at once, and the terraces follow the curves with remarkable precision. The village below holds a lively market daily on the main plaza.

⏱ 75 min
5

Ollantaytambo is one of the few Inca towns still inhabited: the village streets follow exactly the original Inca layout, with running water channels lining each street. The fortress dominating the town is built in red porphyry on monumental terraces—here Inca Manco Cápac II repelled the conquistadors in 1536, one of the rare Inca military victories against the Spanish.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

This Sacred Valley day is ideally done by taxi or private car from Cusco, with a driver who knows the sites. Cusco operators also offer guided day tours. Plan lunch at a restaurant in Urubamba, at the valley bottom, before reaching Ollantaytambo for the night.

1

The Ollantaytambo fortress deserves a thorough morning visit, before groups arrive. The Temple of the Sun, atop the terraces, is unfinished—the Incas were stopped cold by the Spanish conquest. Six 50-ton red porphyry monoliths remain standing on the platform, from a quarry located across the valley. ⏱ 90 min
2

Pumamarka is a little-known small Inca fortress, about 1 hour's walk from Ollantaytambo. The site is little-visited and often empty—making it one of the most authentic Sacred Valley experiences. Llamas and alpacas graze freely among the ruins. The view of the surrounding mountains is excellent. The trail between Pumamarka and Ollantaytambo follows an ancient Inca irrigation canal for several kilometers—it's a section of the Inca Trail accessible to all, free, and one of the region's finest walks.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

In late afternoon, climb to the Pinkuylluna granaries on the hill overlooking Ollantaytambo to the north. These Inca storehouses perched on the cliff served to store the Empire's provisions at altitude (coolness better preserved food). The ascent takes 20 to 30 minutes. The view of the fortress from above is the best in all Ollantaytambo.
