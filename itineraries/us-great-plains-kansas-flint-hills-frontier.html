Wichita Ks

★★★☆☆
1

You cross one of two cable-stay pedestrian bridges - each shaped like a bow and arrow - to reach the 5-ton steel figure that Kiowa-Comanche artist Blackbear Bosin donated to the city in 1974. The plaza around the base describes Plains Indian ways of life through interpretive panels. Bosin designed the piece in 1968 and lived only six years after its dedication before dying in 1980. Access is free around the clock until midnight, and the nightly Ring of Fire makes the after-dark visit worth planning for.

⏱ 45 min
2

Step onto the dirt roads of this 23-acre open-air museum and you are in Wichita between 1865 and 1880, when the Chisholm Trail ran right through here. Over 60 buildings line the grounds - more than 40 of them fully furnished - and costumed interpreters demonstrate blacksmithing, printing, and carpentry in real time. Established in 1952, it holds accreditation from the American Alliance of Museums, a distinction fewer than 3 percent of U.S. museums earn. Go on a weekday morning to avoid school groups.

⏱ 120 min
3

You ride a glass elevator past Ivan the T. rex skeleton to reach three floors of a restored 1914 brick warehouse in Old Town, where an Egyptian mummy, a 34-foot Tylosaurus fossil from the Kansas Cretaceous sea, Roman emperor coins, and a section of the Berlin Wall share the same building. The museum opened in 2001 as the Museum of Ancient Treasures, moved to this Old Town address in 2003, and now draws around 45,000 visitors a year. It specializes in nothing - and that eclecticism is the whole point.

⏱ 90 min
4

The largest art museum in Kansas opened in 1935 on an endowment left by Louise Caldwell Murdock, who in 1915 instructed that her estate fund an American art collection in memory of her husband. Today the permanent collection exceeds 10,000 works - Edward Hopper, Georgia O'Keeffe, Winslow Homer, Mary Cassatt, and Charles Russell among them. General admission to the permanent collection is free, and the Art Garden with outdoor sculpture by Tom Otterness and Henry Moore is open 24 hours.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Walk the brick-lined grid of Old Town after dinner and you are in one of the American Planning Association's 'Great Places in America' - a designation it received in 2008 for its adaptive reuse of late-19th-century warehouses. The Hotel at Old Town was the Keen Kutter brand warehouse in 1906, billed at the time as the largest warehouse in the world, and its lobby still displays the largest authentic Keen Kutter collection in existence. Start at River City Brewing Co. or Nortons Brewing Co. before working your way through more than 100 businesses in the district.

⏱ 90 min

Follow the trail markers embedded in the sidewalks and you are walking the original Chisholm Trail, which ran along the east side of Wichita from 1867 to 1871. Jesse Chisholm, for whom the trail is named, established a trading post here in the 1860s; Wichita was incorporated in 1870 and became a Texas cattle railhead in 1872. Pick up the self-guided map at the Wichita-Sedgwick County Historical Museum, housed in the original 1890 City Hall with its 170-foot clock tower.

⏱ 60 min

Cottonwood Falls Ks

★★☆☆☆
1

You walk out of the 1881 Spring Hill Ranch visitor center and the wind hits you immediately - this is what 170 million acres of North America once felt and sounded like before the plow came. Established on November 12, 1996 as the 370th unit of the National Park System, this 10,894-acre preserve protects the only National Park unit devoted entirely to the tallgrass prairie. Less than 4 percent of the original grassland survives today, and nearly all of it is here. The three-story German bank barn on site has 19,000 square feet of storage space; the 1882 Lower Fox Creek schoolhouse is a short walk uphill. A bison herd roams the pastures; over 40 miles of trails cross the rolling ground.

⏱ 180 min
2

Stand at the base of Broadway Street and look up: the 113-foot red mansard roof of this 1873 courthouse lords over the entire town. Architect John G. Haskell - also the first architect of the Kansas State Capitol - designed it for 600 residents who approved a bond issue in 1871. Workers quarried local limestone by hand and hauled blocks weighing up to seven tons by horse-drawn wagon from a nearby quarry. Inside, a three-story walnut spiral staircase rises from logs cut along the Cottonwood River. Self-guided tours run Monday-Friday, free of charge.

⏱ 45 min
3

Walk the three-block main street between the stone bridge over the Cottonwood River and the courthouse steps and you are in a town whose limestone went into the U.S. Capitol building in Washington, D.C. By 1885, six quarries near here were shipping magnesian limestone by rail to every state in the country. Art galleries, antique stores, and a few restaurants line the street. On select summer Friday evenings, the Emma Chase Café hosts Friday Night Music, with local fiddlers performing on the porch.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Tune your radio to 1680 AM before you leave town - three transmitters along Highway 177 broadcast the history, ecology, and fire management of the Flint Hills as you drive. The 47-mile route from Council Grove south to Cassoday passes through uninterrupted tallgrass prairie with no billboards and almost no buildings. Spring burns (typically April) turn entire hillsides orange at night; the hills are vivid green through June and rust-red by October. Five miles south of Cottonwood Falls, a designated scenic overlook gives the best panoramic view of the rolling rangeland.

⏱ 120 min

Take the Southwind Nature Trail (1.75 miles) and you will pass the bison pasture without needing a guide - just check the NPS website for current herd locations before you go. The preserve has over 40 miles of trails total, from short loops near the historic buildings to the 3.5-mile Two Section Trail that climbs to a scenic overlook. Spring and early summer bring prairie wildflowers in waves: purple coneflower, prairie turnip, and Indian paintbrush. Mornings are best for birding; greater prairie-chickens are visible near the preserve in spring.

Topeka Ks

★★★☆☆
1

You walk into Monroe Elementary, a two-story Italian Renaissance Revival brick building from 1926 that was one of four segregated schools serving Topeka's African American children. Oliver Brown and 12 other parents filed suit in 1951, the Supreme Court consolidated their case with four others, and on May 17, 1954 it ruled that 'separate educational facilities are inherently unequal.' The school closed in 1975, nearly went to auction, and was saved by the Trust for Public Land before opening as a National Historic Site in 2004 on the decision's 50th anniversary. Ranger-led programs and an orientation film deepen the visit.

⏱ 90 min
2

Look up from the rotunda and you are 304 feet below the tip of a copper dome that stands taller than the one in Washington, D.C. (288 feet), though with roughly half the diameter. Construction ran from 1866 to 1903 - 37 years for $3.2 million. John Steuart Curry's 1930s second-floor murals include Tragic Prelude, depicting an oversize, raging John Brown flanked by flames and a tornado - the only portrait of a convicted traitor in any U.S. state capitol. The dome tour climbs 296 steps with no elevator; reserve your spot in advance.

⏱ 75 min
3

Stroll into the Reinisch Rose Garden and you are surrounded by over 4,500 rosebushes in 180 varieties, laid out in 1930 by Chicago landscape architect Emmett Hill on land donated by the heirs of Civil War veteran Guilford Gage in 1899. The garden is one of 23 All-America Rose Selections test gardens in the United States, which means serious horticultural work happens here alongside the wedding photos. The Topeka Zoo within the same 160-acre park houses a walk-through tropical rainforest, one of the first in the U.S. The 1908 carousel still runs to its original Wurlitzer band organ.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Walk through 30,000 square feet of Kansas history and you will pass a full-sized Atchison, Topeka & Santa Fe locomotive, a Southern Cheyenne-style tipi, a reconstructed 1950s diner, and a strong section on the Bleeding Kansas era that many historians consider the true opening of the Civil War. The Oregon and Santa Fe Trail galleries are particularly well done, and the overall arc - from prehistoric times through the 20th century - gives good context for everything you have seen and will see on this loop.

⏱ 90 min

Pick up the free map at the Brown v. Board visitor center and follow Topeka's civil rights geography beyond Monroe School. Constitution Hall at 429 S. Kansas Avenue - called the 'Free State Capitol' - is where Free State supporters drafted the Topeka Constitution in 1855 rejecting slavery; it also served as a stop on the Underground Railroad and reopened as a museum in late 2025 after extensive restoration. A community mural painted by volunteers faces the park from across the street. Topeka's Brown v. Board site was added to the U.S. Civil Rights Trail in 2021.

⏱ 60 min

Lawrence Ks

★★★☆☆
1

Walk six blocks from 6th Street to South Park and you are inside a National Register Historic District with 108 contributing properties, most built between 1856 and 1953. At 729-731, the House Building is the only commercial structure to survive Quantrill's 1863 raid intact. The Eldridge Hotel at 701-703 was Quantrill's main tactical target; the current five-story brick building dates from 1928, and each August 21 a white sheet is hung from a window to mark the anniversary. Free State Brewing Company at 636 Massachusetts opened in 1989 as the first legal brewery in Kansas in over a century.

⏱ 90 min
2

You enter through the marble foyer of the 1888 Watkins National Bank Building at 1047 Massachusetts Street - marble staircases, stained-glass windows, and mosaic floors intact - and get the most thorough account of Quantrill's Raid in any Lawrence museum. The free permanent collection covers Douglas County history from Bleeding Kansas through the 20th century. James Patti's sculpture of Langston Hughes as a boy is here; Hughes grew up in Lawrence and based his 1930 novel Not Without Laughter on his childhood in this town.

⏱ 60 min
3

Climb Mount Oread to the KU campus and you are on a National Historic District that opened in 1866 - one year after Quantrill's raid nearly prevented Lawrence from funding the new university at all. The Spencer Museum of Art (over 45,000 objects) and the KU Natural History Museum are both free and both worth at least 30 minutes each. James Naismith, the inventor of basketball and the only KU coach with a losing record, coached here and is buried in Lawrence.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Download the free 1.5-mile self-guided walking tour from explorelawrence.com and follow Quantrill's path through 10 surviving sites - from South Park, where guerrillas staged their approach, to the Methodist Church that became a makeshift morgue by evening. The raid killed 20 percent of the town's male population and left 85 widows. Quantrill himself had lived in Lawrence under an alias and knew the town intimately before leading 450 men into it at dawn. The Watkins Museum also runs a paid Bleeding Kansas Bus Tour on select summer Saturdays for a wider sweep of the region.

⏱ 90 min

End your day the way Lawrence does: with a pint and a plate on Mass Street. Free State Brewing Company has poured its flagship Ad Astra amber ale - named for the Kansas state motto, 'To the stars through difficulty' - since opening in 1989 as the first legal brewery in Kansas in over a century. For dinner, the Mad Greek at 9th and Massachusetts does flaming saganaki tableside. The Granada at 1020 Massachusetts, a Beaux-Arts venue that opened in 1912 with a 35-foot vaulted ceiling, hosts live music most nights under a chandelier the size of a small car.

⏱ 120 min

Larned Ks

★☆☆☆☆
1

Walk the 400-foot square parade ground and you can see all nine original buildings surrounding you - something no comparable fort in the region can claim. Between 1866 and 1868, Captain A.F. Rockwell replaced the original sod-and-adobe structures with sandstone quarried locally, and every one of them still stands. Seven are open to visitors: barracks, officers' quarters, post hospital, blacksmith shop, commissary, and the commanding officer's quarters, all furnished to their 1860s-1870s appearance. The 10th U.S. Cavalry - one of the first two all-Black cavalry regiments in the Army - was stationed here from 1867 to 1869. Five miles southwest, original Santa Fe Trail wagon ruts remain visible in the prairie grass.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Drive west from Lawrence on US-56 and you are on the route that traders followed from 1821 to 1872, moving goods between Missouri and Santa Fe, New Mexico. The land flattens dramatically west of Hillsboro. Consider a 15-minute detour to Pawnee Rock State Park, 9 miles northeast of Larned off US-56 - a sandstone prominence where Santa Fe Trail scouts watched for Comanche and Kiowa raiders, now free and open year-round with interpretive panels and views across the Arkansas River valley.

Dodge Ks

★★★☆☆
1

You are standing on the original Boot Hill Cemetery site - the burying ground for men who 'died with their boots on.' Front Street around you recreates downtown Dodge City as it looked in 1876, and the 20,000-plus artifacts in the complex include more than 200 original firearms. The Long Branch Saloon here is modeled on period photographs of the original, which opened around 1874, burned in 1885, and hosted the June 1883 Dodge City Peace Commission photograph - one of the most reproduced images of the Old West. In summer, gunfight reenactments run at noon and 6 p.m. The Kansas Cowboy Hall of Fame and a Gunsmoke exhibit are also on site.

⏱ 150 min
2

Walk through the only limestone house in Dodge City and you are in the oldest building in the city still standing on its original foundation. Bavarian immigrant John Mueller had 24-inch-thick walls quarried from Sawlog Creek, 12 miles northeast of town - the same quarry that supplied Fort Dodge - and hired fellow German craftsman William Strubel to hand-build the walnut spiral staircase, its newel post inset with bird's-eye maple. Built between 1879 and 1881, the house passed to only one other family: the Schmidts, who kept it until 1968. The desk where Dodge City founder Robert M. Wright wrote his 1913 book Dodge City: The Cowboy Capital is still inside.

⏱ 60 min
3

Drive nine miles west of Dodge City on US-50 and you reach a National Historic Landmark where 2 miles of wagon ruts survive across 140 acres of unplowed prairie - among the longest and best-preserved sections of the entire trail. For 60 years, wagon wheels cut this path through the sod, sometimes four abreast. The ruts are most visible when the sun is low; visit in late afternoon for the best light on the depressions. A short interpretive boardwalk explains the site; it also preserves remnants of the 1880s Soule Canal irrigation project.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walk Wyatt Earp Boulevard and you are on the route of the original Front Street, where 16 saloons operated simultaneously during the cattle trade's peak years. The Dodge City Trail of Fame embeds bronze boot prints and narrative plaques for Wyatt Earp, Bat Masterson, Doc Holliday, and other figures from the era. The El Capitan bronze sculpture nearby depicts a full-scale Texas Longhorn steer, commemorating the five million head that ended their drives here. The Wyatt Earp statue outside Boot Hill marks the spot where Earp enforced the 'Deadline' at the railroad tracks - south of which anything went.

⏱ 60 min

Book ahead and spend the evening in the Long Branch, where a variety show has run every summer since 1957 - one of the longest-running seasonal variety shows in the country. Miss Kitty, singing bartenders, can-can dancers, and gunfighters re-enact the entertainment that Chalk Beeson's five-piece orchestra once provided nightly at the original saloon. The original Long Branch, named by owner William Harris after Long Branch, New Jersey, served Anheuser-Busch beer kept cold in summer by ice shipped by train from Colorado, and hosted gambling from five-cent chuck-a-luck to thousand-dollar poker pots.

⏱ 150 min

Wichita Ks

★★★☆☆
1

You walk into a $62 million building that Canadian architect Moshe Safdie designed specifically to sit on the banks of the Arkansas River - the geometric toroid shape is a deliberate response to the waterfront site. The museum opened April 1, 2000, and Safdie later applied lessons from it to the Crystal Bridges Museum in Bentonville and the Kauffman Center in Kansas City. Inside, the 'Design, Build, Fly' exhibit lets you rivet an airplane wing, test landing gear, and design an aircraft seat - Wichita-specific content for a city where Cessna, Beechcraft, and Learjet all built their planes. The Boeing Dome Theater shows films on a 60-foot, 360-degree screen; dome tickets can be purchased separately.

⏱ 150 min
2

Step into the museum adjacent to the Keeper of the Plains and you are looking at 39 original works by Blackbear Bosin - the Kiowa-Comanche artist who created the sculpture outside. The permanent collection also includes traditional Plains Indian beadwork, ceremonial objects, and historical photography. The museum itself was the direct result of community organizing by Native residents of Wichita through the 1960s, who lobbied the city for a gathering space where all Plains tribes could share their culture.

⏱ 60 min
3

Cross the Arkansas River to Delano, which was founded in the 1870s just outside Wichita's city limits precisely so that gambling, brothels, and saloons could operate without interference from Wichita lawmen. Train-masters who visited the establishments are said to have carried their red caboose lanterns, which is where the expression 'red-light district' comes from. Wichita annexed Delano in 1880. Today it is an eclectic stretch of independently owned shops, galleries, and restaurants along West Douglas Avenue, with public art celebrating the Chisholm Trail throughout.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Rent a kayak or bike from Boats and Bikes on the river and you can reach all five Museums on the River institutions - Exploration Place, the Wichita Art Museum, Old Cowtown Museum, Botanica Wichita, and the Mid-America All-Indian Center - on a 2.5-mile paved loop. The cable-stay pedestrian bridges crossing both rivers to the Keeper plaza were specifically designed in the shape of bows and arrows, part of the $20 million restoration completed in 2007. The loop is free to walk year-round; Boats and Bikes operates April through October.

⏱ 90 min

Choose your farewell meal from three Wichita institutions. Old Mill Tasty Shop at 604 E. Douglas has run its marble-countered soda fountain since 1932, with blue-plate specials changing daily and malts mixed by hand. NuWay Burgers on West Douglas Avenue has served its signature 'crumbled' ground beef patty - unique to the chain - since 1930. In Delano, Hopping Gnome Brewing brews small-batch beers in a 1920s commercial building on West Douglas. All three are within easy reach of ICT airport for an early departure.

⏱ 120 min
