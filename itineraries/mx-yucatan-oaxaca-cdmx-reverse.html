⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 9 and 13 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Dry Season (November-April): Peak tourist season with minimal rainfall, comfortable temperatures (20-28°C), and perfect beach weather. Highest prices but ideal conditions for all activities.

Shoulder Season (May, October): Good weather with brief rains, moderate prices, and fewer crowds. Excellent value while maintaining comfortable conditions for most regions.

Hurricane Season (June-September): Hot, humid weather with potential storms on Caribbean and Pacific coasts. Lowest prices and lush landscapes, but weather monitoring essential.

Regional Variations: Northern deserts hot year-round, central highlands mild, coastal areas tropical. Timing depends on specific destinations and preferred activities.

🌍 What to discover in MX?

Vibrant culture with ancient civilizations, incredible cuisine, and diverse landscapes. Experience Maya heritage, beautiful beaches, and authentic Mexican traditions. 📋 Important notes

Oaxaca De Juarez

UNESCO-listed Zapotec capital and sanctuary of Mexican gastronomy.

★★★★★
📍 Zócalo Oaxaca de Juárez📍 Mercado 20 de Noviembre Oaxaca📍 Andador Turístico Oaxaca📍 Catedral Metropolitana Oaxaca
1

The Zócalo is the essential starting point. Around this central plaza—a UNESCO World Heritage site—stand the 16th-century Catedral Metropolitana and the Palacio de Gobierno with Arturo García Bustos's murals narrating the entire history of the state. Set down your coffee and watch: this is where the city breathes.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Mercado 20 de Noviembre, built in 1862, is the sanctuary of Oaxacan cuisine. Step into the Pasillo de Humo—that smoke-filled corridor where dozens of vendors grill tasajo (thinly sliced beef), chorizo, and cecina over charcoal asadores. Choose your meat at the counter, sit at the stone tables, and wait. An experience worth the journey on its own.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Andador Turístico, a pedestrian street connecting the Zócalo to the Templo de Santo Domingo. In the evening, musicians play at the terraces, painters exhibit on the sidewalks. It's the living axis of the colonial city, to be explored without any particular destination in mind.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Chocolate de agua session at the market: Oaxacan chocolate is prepared with water (not milk), ground on-site with cinnamon and sugar. The molinos at the market let you choose your proportions. Order a hot jícara with pan de yema—egg yolk brioche, a local specialty.

⏱ 30 min

First glass of mezcal at a vineta in the centro: Oaxacan mezcal comes from wild agaves—espadín, tobalá, tepeztate—distilled in artisanal palenques. Ask for a young mezcal (joven) for your first taste. It's different from tequila: smokier, more complex.

Oaxaca De Juarez

UNESCO-listed Zapotec capital and sanctuary of Mexican gastronomy.

★★★★★
📍 Monte Albán archaeological zone Oaxaca📍 Templo de Santo Domingo de Guzmán Oaxaca📍 Museo de las Culturas de Oaxaca📍 Barrio Jalatlaco Oaxaca
1

Monte Albán opens at 8am—be there at opening. The morning light is perfect on the Gran Plaza, a 300m by 150m esplanade perched 400 meters above the central valleys. This Zapotec capital founded around 500 BC once had up to 40,000 inhabitants spread over 20 km². Allow 2.5 hours for the visit. The site is 10 km from Oaxaca, a 20-minute drive.

⏱ 150 min
2

The Templo de Santo Domingo de Guzmán features one of Mexico's most impressive Baroque facades. Construction began in 1572 and lasted over a century. Inside, gilded stucco covers the nave from floor to ceiling. Don't miss the Museo de las Culturas de Oaxaca in the adjoining former convent: it displays the 600 pieces of Mixtec treasure discovered in Monte Albán's Tomb 7 in 1932—jewelry in gold, jade, and turquoise.

⏱ 90 min
3

The barrio Jalatlaco, east of the centro, is the city's best-preserved colonial neighborhood. Its cobblestone lanes, ochre and pink-facade houses, bougainvillea spilling over walls: this is where artists and photographers set up their tripods. Look for murals by artist Miguel Ángel Hernández that bring the walls to life.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Walk through the Jardín Etnobotánico de Oaxaca, adjacent to the Santo Domingo convent: two hectares of giant cacti, agaves, and medicinal plants used by indigenous cultures for centuries. Guided tour only—essential to understand what grows here.

⏱ 60 min

Oaxaca De Juarez

UNESCO-listed Zapotec capital and sanctuary of Mexican gastronomy.

★★★★★
📍 San Bartolo Coyotepec Oaxaca📍 Santo Tomás Jalieza Oaxaca📍 Ocotlán de Morelos Oaxaca📍 Santiago Matatlán mezcal Oaxaca
1

San Bartolo Coyotepec sits 11 km south of Oaxaca. This village of 600 families produces barro negro, a shiny black pottery unique in the world. The current technique dates back to the 1950s, when Doña Rosa Real discovered that polishing the clay while still damp with a quartz stone before firing produces a deep metallic black finish. Enter the family workshops to see the hand-turned wheel and closed-kiln firing that deprives the clay of oxygen.

⏱ 75 min
2

Santo Tomás Jalieza, a few kilometers away, specializes in backstrap loom weaving, a pre-Hispanic technique where the weaver attaches the loom to their own waist and uses their body tension to keep the threads taut. Village women produce belts, bags, and blouses with geometric Zapotec patterns.

⏱ 60 min
3

If you're there on a Friday, don't miss the Ocotlán de Morelos market: one of the most authentic in the central valleys. Wool blankets, medicinal herbs, dried meats, ceramics—vendors arrive from surrounding villages before dawn.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Mezcal tasting in Santiago Matatlán, dubbed the world mezcal capital. The mezcal route passes through this village where over a hundred artisanal producers (palenques) distill their agaves in clay stills. Ask to visit an active palenque: you'll see agave piñas cooking in earth pits, distillation in wooden vats, and taste different expressions of espadín and cuishe.

UNESCO-listed Zapotec capital and sanctuary of Mexican gastronomy.

★★★★★
📍 Cuajimoloyas Pueblos Mancomunados Oaxaca📍 Benito Juárez Sierra Norte Oaxaca📍 Pueblos Mancomunados cloud forest Oaxaca
1

Cuajimoloyas, 2 hours by mountain road from Oaxaca, is the most accessible village of the Pueblos Mancomunados. It sits at over 2,700m elevation. In summer, the undergrowth overflows with edible mushrooms. In winter, the cloud forest wraps century-old pines in perfect silence. This is where the Sierra Norte opens up to hikers.

⏱ 180 min
2

Benito Juárez, a pioneer village of Zapotec community ecotourism, features a spectacular suspension bridge and viewpoints overlooking the central valleys. On clear days, you can spot the Oaxaca plain 2,400m below. The communities manage the fireplaced cabañas, meals, and guides themselves.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Trek or mountain bike between Cuajimoloyas and Benito Juárez on marked trails (red for main routes). Over 100 km of trails cross the 8 mancomunados villages. The path between these two villages follows pine and oak forests, passes a 30m waterfall, and offers 180° views of the Sierra Madre. Local guides available for Spanish-language accompaniment.

⏱ 240 min

Zipline and suspension bridge at Cuajimoloyas: the communities have built a 1km zipline and suspension bridge over a forested canyon. An option for those wanting adrenaline between hikes.

Oaxaca De Juarez

UNESCO-listed Zapotec capital and sanctuary of Mexican gastronomy.

★★★★★
📍 Hierve el Agua Oaxaca petrified waterfalls📍 Teotitlán del Valle Oaxaca rugs📍 Mitla archaeological zone Oaxaca
1

Hierve el Agua, 70 km east of Oaxaca, is one of Mexico's strangest geological formations. Springs saturated with calcium carbonate have deposited their minerals for millennia, creating two petrified waterfalls that overlook the valley from a cliff over 200m high. The large waterfall measures 60m high by 90m wide. Natural pools allow for swimming. The view from the cliff edge is breathtaking.

⏱ 150 min
2

Mitla, on the return route: this 14th-century Zapotec archaeological site is famous for its stone mosaic fretwork—geometric patterns assembled without mortar that entirely cover palace facades. Unique in Mesoamerica. The Spanish built a red church right in the middle of the site, reusing Zapotec stones.

⏱ 75 min
3

Teotitlán del Valle, 30 minutes from Oaxaca, is the world capital of Zapotec rugs. In family workshops, weavers work on large pedal looms introduced by the Spanish in 1535. Wools are dyed with cochineal (nopal cactus insect, which gives red), indigo (blue), walnut shell (brown), and cempasúchil flower (yellow). A quality rug can take several weeks.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Swimming in Hierve el Agua's natural pools: the water ranges between 22 and 27°C, rich in minerals, pleasant especially from March to June. A loop trail (45 min) descends to the foot of the waterfalls for a different angle. Bring good shoes—the altitude makes the climb harder than it appears.

⏱ 75 min

Puerto Escondido

Giant surf at Zicatela and nighttime bioluminescence at Manialtepec.

★★★☆☆
📍 Playa Zicatela Puerto Escondido📍 Playa Carrizalillo Puerto Escondido📍 La Punta Puerto Escondido surf
1

The descent from Oaxaca to the coast (via San José del Pacífico) takes 6 to 7 hours on tight switchbacks through the Sierra Madre del Sur. The landscape changes dramatically—cloud forest, then tropical, then the first ocean scents. Puerto Escondido appears by late afternoon. Drop your bags and head down to see Zicatela before dinner.

⏱ 60 min
2

Playa Zicatela is one of the world's most renowned surf breaks: the Mexican Pipeline generates hollow tubes of 3 to 6 meters breaking over shallow sand. For non-surfers, settle on the beach with a coconut juice and watch: it's a spectacle in itself. Don't swim—the currents and wave power are deadly for untrained swimmers.

⏱ 90 min
3

Playa Carrizalillo, west of Puerto Escondido, is the local swimming beach. A protected cove accessible by a 167-step stairway from the cliff. Calm water, gentle waves, clean sand. Vendors offer kayak and paddleboard rentals.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Surf lesson at La Punta: this stretch of beach at the western end of Zicatela receives gentler, more consistent waves—perfect for beginners. Local instructors offer 2-hour sessions including equipment and instruction. La Punta is also Puerto Escondido's most laid-back neighborhood, with its small fish restaurants and hammocks.

Puerto Escondido

Giant surf at Zicatela and nighttime bioluminescence at Manialtepec.

★★★☆☆
📍 Mercado Benito Juárez Puerto Escondido📍 Laguna Manialtepec Puerto Escondido bioluminescence📍 Puerto Escondido downtown market
1

The Mercado Benito Juárez of Puerto Escondido is the local market, far from tourist circuits. Vegetables, tropical fruits, medicinal herbs, handmade huaraches. A good hour to understand what the people of the Oaxacan coast actually eat.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Nighttime bioluminescence tour at Laguna de Manialtepec, 18 km west of Puerto Escondido. This 15km coastal lagoon harbors microscopic plankton that glows blue-green with the slightest movement. On new moon nights, every gesture in the water triggers a luminous trail. Providers organize kayak or boat outings, followed by a swim in the glowing water under the stars. Book ahead—spots are limited to about ten people per group.

⏱ 180 min

Daytime kayak through Manialtepec mangrove: morning outings navigate tunnels of red mangroves, where egrets, frigatebirds, ibises, and storks nest. Ornithologist guides count over 150 observable species. Another way to discover the same lagoon, in completely different light and atmosphere.

Puerto Escondido

Giant surf at Zicatela and nighttime bioluminescence at Manialtepec.

★★★☆☆
📍 Parque Nacional Lagunas de Chacahua Oaxaca📍 Zapotalito lanchas Chacahua📍 Playa Chacahua Oaxaca
1

Parque Nacional Lagunas de Chacahua, created in 1937, protects 14,175 hectares of coastal lagoons, mangroves, dunes, and tropical forests between Puerto Escondido and Santiago Jamiltepec. From the village of Zapotalito (1.5 hours by road), a lancha crosses the two large lagoons—Pastoría and Chacahua—through mangrove tunnels. On the banks: white egrets, iguanas, American crocodiles sunning themselves.

⏱ 120 min
2

Playa Chacahua, accessible only by boat: a long strip of white, deserted sand facing the open Pacific. A few palapas serve grilled fish. The waves roll gently—ideal for bodysurfing. The kind of place you talk about for years afterward.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Visit the Cocodrilario de Chacahua: this breeding center houses over 200 American crocodiles of all sizes, from hatchlings to 2m adults. The program aims to reintroduce the species into the lagoons, where it had nearly disappeared in the 1970s due to poaching.

⏱ 45 min

Bird watching during the lancha crossing. The park harbors over 150 species, including the roseate spoonbill (Platalea ajaja) with spectacular pink plumage, brown pelican, magnificent frigatebird, and migrating storks. Ask the pilot to slow down in narrow channels.

⏱ 90 min

Mazunte

Peaceful three-street village renowned for sea turtle conservation.

★★★☆☆
📍 Centro Mexicano de la Tortuga Mazunte📍 Punta Cometa Mazunte Oaxaca📍 Playa Rinconcito Mazunte
1

The Centro Mexicano de la Tortuga is one of Mexico's most active sea turtle conservation centers. It protects olive ridley, black, hawksbill, and leatherback turtles—from nesting (July to January) to release. You'll see the animals in their different developmental stages. For decades, Mazunte was Mexico's turtle hunting center; the creation of the center in 1991 radically changed the community's relationship with these animals.

⏱ 75 min
2

Punta Cometa, the southernmost promontory of Oaxaca state, is the best sunset spot on the Oaxacan Pacific coast. The path from Mazunte beach climbs 15 to 20 minutes on a rocky trail. At the end, rocks plunge into the Pacific with a 180° view. Locals call this place "Cerro Sagrado"—sacred hill—and it was already used as an astronomical observatory in pre-Hispanic times. Arrive 45 minutes before sunset to find a good rock.

⏱ 90 min
3

Playa Rinconcito, a 10-minute walk from central Mazunte, is the quietest of the village's three beaches. A few palapa restaurants under coconut palms, few people even in high season. The current is strong to the west—swimming on the east side only.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Swimming and marine wildlife observation at San Agustinillo, 1.2 km from Mazunte on foot. This 1,300m beach naturally divides into two parts: west side protected by rocks, gentle waves, ideal for swimming; east side, stronger swells for bodysurfing. From December to March, whale families sometimes swim in the bay.

Huatulco

Nine wild bays and the largest reef on the Mexican Pacific.

★★★★★
📍 Bahía San Agustín Huatulco snorkeling📍 Bahía Cacaluta Huatulco📍 La Crucecita Huatulco centro
1

Bahía San Agustín, westernmost of Huatulco's 9 bays, harbors what the Mexican Tourism Council describes as the largest coral reef on the Mexican Pacific. The bay is protected by its geography, maintaining calm, clear water. Two beaches flank it: San Agustín and Cacalutilla. At 14 km from La Crucecita on a dirt track, it remains pristine on weekdays.

⏱ 150 min
2

La Crucecita, Huatulco's urban center, sits about ten minutes from the beaches but offers all services: markets, Oaxacan cuisine restaurants, handicrafts (barro negro, mezcal, chocolate). Its central plaza is framed by colonial arcades. A good base for exploring the bays by day.

⏱ 75 min
3

Bahía Cacaluta: this kilometer-long beach protected by an island was the filming location for Alfonso Cuarón's film Y Tu Mamá También. It backs onto dunes and remains deserted on weekdays. Accessible by a 2.5km trail from the road, or by boat from Santa Cruz.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Snorkeling at Bahía San Agustín: the reef spreads from shore, between the bay's rocks. Rays, sea turtles, groupers, parrotfish. Restaurants on the beach rent masks and snorkels. Water is clear enough to see the bottom at 5-6m depth.

Huatulco

Nine wild bays and the largest reef on the Mexican Pacific.

★★★★★
📍 Santa Cruz marina Huatulco boat tour📍 Bahía Maguey Huatulco📍 Bahía Chachacual Huatulco national park📍 La Entrega snorkeling Huatulco
1

Boat tour of Huatulco's bays from Santa Cruz marina: in one day, a panga visits 5 to 7 of the 9 bays. The route passes La Bufadora—a rock formation that shoots a 10m water jet in heavy swells—and follows the cliffs of Huatulco National Park (119 km²). Stops at Maguey (2 hours swimming on golden sand) and Chachacual (the wildest, accessible only by sea).

⏱ 300 min
2

La Entrega, accessible by road from Santa Cruz (2.5 km): a wide beach with calm waters and a good coral reef 50m offshore. Snorkeling equipment rental on-site. One of the few snorkeling spots accessible without a boat.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Diving or snorkeling during the boat tour: tour guides stop in bays where water is clearest. If you have your own equipment, save it for Bahía San Agustín or La Entrega—stops at crowded bays during daytime are sometimes brief.

⏱ 90 min

Evening in La Crucecita: plaza restaurants offer Oaxacan coastal cuisine—shrimp ceviche, fish tacos, tlayudas with seafood. This is the last night on the coast: take advantage to taste local mezcal at a plaza mezcalería.
