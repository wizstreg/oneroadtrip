Salisbury

★★★★☆
1

The needle-sharp spire soars 123 metres into the Wiltshire sky—it's been Britain's tallest church spire since 1561. The cathedral was built in just 38 years (1220-1258), giving it a rare stylistic unity among England's great Gothic cathedrals. In the Chapter House, one of the four original Magna Carta manuscripts from 1215 is on display: the best preserved of the four surviving copies. The nave clock, dating from around 1386, is considered the world's oldest working mechanical clock. Avoid Sunday mornings when access is restricted to services.

⏱ 120 min
2

Ten minutes' walk from the centre, the Town Path runs along Avon water meadows and offers the postcard view of the spire—yet it's rarely crowded. The Cathedral Close, England's largest, is ringed by inhabited medieval and Georgian houses. The National Trust's Mompesson House opens its eighteenth-century drawing rooms to visitors. Best in late afternoon when the low-angled light turns the stone golden.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Stroll through the Cathedral Close in the evening after the tour buses have departed. The Old Mill pub at the end of Town Path serves local Badger Ale with views over the river.

Wilton

★★★☆☆
1

Sober facade, explosive interior: the Double Cube Room measures 18 metres long, 9 metres wide, 9 metres high, with painted ceiling, gilding, and Van Dyck family portraits hung on the walls since 1653. The D-Day landings were planned from these State Rooms in 1944. Inigo Jones and John Webb's Palladianism perhaps reaches its English zenith here. The house still belongs to the Earls of Pembroke, the family installed by Henry VIII after the dissolution of Wilton Abbey. Open Easter to October, £20.

Old Wardour Castle is England's only hexagonal castle, built around the 1390s for Lord Lovell. Besieged twice during the English Civil War, it has remained a romantic ruin since 1644. Stourton

★★☆☆☆
1

The smell of damp earth and rhododendrons greets you at the path entrance, then the lake appears suddenly with the Pantheon and its columns reflected in the water. Henry Hoare II designed this garden between 1743 and 1780, inspired by paintings of Claude Lorrain and Virgil's Aeneid: each temple marks a symbolic stage. The circuit of the lake is 2.5 km. The rhododendrons, added by his grandson after 1791, are the April-May spectacle. Arrive before 10am: the morning light on the lake is optimal and the tour buses haven't yet disgorged their passengers. National Trust, around £20.

⏱ 180 min
2

Henry Hoare II was in mourning when he began the project: five family members died between 1727 and 1743. The garden was his response. This changes how you read the dark grotto and the stairway up to the Temple of Apollo, god of light. Stourhead House (Palladian, 1721) holds Chippendale the Elder furniture and the Hoare painting collection. Open separately from the garden.

⏱ 60 min
3

A triangular red-brick tower, built to celebrate... the end of a war in France and the accession of George III. Henry Hoare II commissioned this 49-metre folly from architect Henry Flitcroft, completed in 1772. In 1944, an American aircraft struck it, killing all five crew members and taking off the top 10 metres. The tower was restored in 1986 using a helicopter. From the top: views across Somerset, Wiltshire and Dorset. 205 steps. NT, £5, open weekends April to September.

Warminster is an unremarkable garrison town except for one thing: since Christmas 1964, thousands of witnesses have reported unexplained sounds and unidentified luminous objects, dubbed 'The Warminster Thing'. Journalist Arthur Shuttlewood of the Warminster Journal documented the phenomenon for years. Sceptics point to the military bases on Salisbury Plain and their nocturnal exercises. Enthusiasts still climb Cley Hill to scan the skies. No official explanation has ever been given.

Free, parking on B3098.

Devizes

1

Excavated in 1808 by William Cunnington, the Bush Barrow Chieftain was buried within sight of Stonehenge with the richest grave goods ever found in a British Bronze Age tomb. His gold lozenge, daggers and amber ornaments returned to Devizes in 1985 after a long stay at the British Museum. The Wiltshire Museum displays 30 Bronze Age gold pieces in its permanent gallery opened in 2013. Free, Long Street, closed Monday-Tuesday.

⏱ 90 min

A boat takes on average 4 to 6 hours to traverse them. The walk takes about an hour one way. Paying car park Canal & River Trust, Rowde Road.

Avebury

★★★★★
1

If Stonehenge felt too controlled, too distant from the stones, here's Avebury. The world's largest stone circle is bisected by a village, its pubs and houses. You touch the megaliths, you picnic between them. The henge measures over 28 acres and includes two inner circles. UNESCO-listed alongside Stonehenge, the site dates from around 2600 BC. Alexander Keiller Museum in the village: collection of finds from the site. Circle entrance free, NT car park charges apply.

⏱ 90 min
2

Archaeologists nicknamed it 'the avenue of lovers'—the stones of West Kennet Avenue alternate between squat (feminine) and slender (masculine) according to an old hypothesis. The avenue connects the Avebury henge to the Sanctuary site, 2.5 km to the southeast. The first 100 pairs from the original period have been partially restored. Free walk from the village.

⏱ 50 min
3

Low, dark, cool: the burial chamber of West Kennet Long Barrow requires you to stoop to enter. The barrow dates from around 3650 BC and measures 100 metres long. Stuart Piggott's excavations (1955-56) uncovered the remains of at least 46 individuals in five chambers. The barrow was deliberately sealed around 2000 BC with large sarsen slabs. Torch recommended for the interior. Free access, 10-minute walk from Silbury Hill car park.

Thirty-nine metres of chalk piled up by hand around 2400 BC: Silbury Hill is Europe's tallest prehistoric artificial mound. Nobody knows why it was built. Nineteenth and twentieth-century excavations found no burial, no significant artefacts. It's visible from the A4 road, on-site parking, but climbing is prohibited to preserve the site. External view only.

Newbury

★★★★☆
1

The museum is free, small, and far better than its address in a Jacobean clothworkers' building might suggest. The West Berkshire Museum documents the two Battles of Newbury from the English Civil War: in September 1643, the Earl of Essex's Parliamentary army halted the Royalist advance in the fields of Wash Common; in October 1644, the second battle around Donnington Castle deepened the Royalist quagmire. Historians consider the First Battle of Newbury a turning point in the Civil War. Open Wednesday, Thursday, Saturday and Sunday, 11am-4pm. Free.
