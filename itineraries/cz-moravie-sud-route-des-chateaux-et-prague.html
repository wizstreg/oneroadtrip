Prague

★★★★★
1

Prague Castle, founded in the 9th century on the left bank of the Vltava River, is considered the world's largest medieval castle complex at 70,000 square meters. Climb through the Hradčany district to reach three successive courtyards—the second houses the National Gallery of the Castle, while the third contains St. Vitus Cathedral.

⏱ 60 min
2

St. Vitus Cathedral, begun in 1344 under Charles IV following plans by French architect Mathieu d'Arras, was only completed in 1929—585 years of construction. His successor Peter Parler, called to lead the project at just 23 years old, invented daringly intricate net vaulting unprecedented for the era. Inside, the St. Wenceslas Chapel is encrusted with semi-precious stones and 14th-century frescoes. The adjoining vault, sealed with seven locks whose keys belong to seven different state officials, houses the crown jewels of Bohemia.

⏱ 75 min
3

Golden Lane (Zlatá ulička), tucked against the castle's northern rampart, is a row of colorful 16th-century cottages. Legend attributes the name to alchemists Emperor Rudolf II supposedly housed here to transmute metals into gold, but archival records show the actual residents were arquebusiers and craftsmen.

⏱ 30 min
4

Descend to Charles Bridge (Karlův most), whose foundation stone was laid on July 9, 1357, at 5:31 a.m.—a palindromic date and time (1-3-5-7-9-7-5-3-1) chosen by Charles IV's astrologer to guarantee the structure's longevity. Its 515 meters are flanked by 30 Baroque statues added between 1683 and 1714. Look for the bronze plaque beneath the statue of St. John of Nepomuk, a priest thrown into the Vltava in 1393 by order of King Wenceslas IV: touching the kneeling monk is said to bring good luck.

⏱ 60 min
5

St. Nicholas Church in Malá Strana, built between 1704 and 1755 by Christoph and then Kilian Ignaz Dientzenhofer, is one of Central Europe's Baroque masterpieces. Its 75-meter dome dominates the left bank skyline. Mozart played the organ here during his visits to Prague.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

In the evening, stroll the Vltava embankments between Charles Bridge and Legion Bridge to see the illuminated castle reflected on the river. The view from Kampa Island, accessible via a stairway beneath Charles Bridge, is particularly striking.

⏱ 60 min

Prague

★★★★★
1

Old Town Square (Staroměstské náměstí) has been Prague's beating heart since the 10th century. On its southern wall stands the Astronomical Clock (Orloj), installed in 1410 and modified around 1490—Europe's oldest astronomical clock still in operation. Its dial represents Earth at the center of the solar system according to Ptolemy, simultaneously displaying local time, sidereal time, and the positions of the Sun and Moon. Every hour, the twelve apostles parade through the upper windows, flanked by figures of Vanity, Greed, Death, and the Turk. Legend has it that city councilors blinded its creator so he could never reproduce this masterpiece elsewhere.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Church of Our Lady before Týn, whose two asymmetrical 80-meter towers have dominated the square since the 14th century, served as Prague's main Hussite church for nearly a century. Astronomer Tycho Brahe, court astronomer to Emperor Rudolf II, is buried here.

⏱ 30 min
3

Explore Josefov, Prague's former Jewish ghetto. The Old-New Synagogue, built around 1270 in Cistercian Gothic style, is Central Europe's oldest synagogue still in active use. It's linked to Rabbi Löw (1520-1609), whom tradition credits with creating the Golem—a clay creature animated by a magical formula to protect the Jewish community. Its attic is said to still harbor the dormant Golem. In 1389, during the deadliest pogrom in Prague's history, over 3,000 Jews were massacred in this quarter.

⏱ 75 min
4

The Old Jewish Cemetery, established in the early 15th century, is one of Europe's oldest. Due to space constraints, the dead were buried in layers up to twelve deep, explaining the spectacular crowding of some 12,000 gravestones. Rabbi Löw's tomb is perpetually covered with small prayer notes.

⏱ 45 min
5

In the afternoon, take an excursion to Karlštejn Castle, 30 km southwest of Prague. Built between 1348 and 1365 following plans by Mathieu d'Arras, it was conceived by Charles IV as a stronghold for the imperial crown jewels of the Holy Roman Empire. The 60-meter Great Tower houses the Chapel of the Holy Cross, whose walls are encrusted with semi-precious stones and adorned with 129 panels painted by Master Theodoric—the world's largest ensemble of Gothic panel paintings. Charles IV himself would enter this chapel barefoot as a sign of humility.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

In the evening, settle onto a terrace on Old Town Square to watch the apostles' procession on the Orloj—the spectacle repeats every hour until 11 p.m.

⏱ 60 min

Kutna Hora

★★★★☆
1

The Sedlec Ossuary is an underground chapel within the Church of All Saints containing the bones of 40,000 to 70,000 people—victims of the Great Plague and Hussite Wars of the 14th and 15th centuries. According to legend, an abbot returning from Jerusalem in 1278 scattered earth from Golgotha across the cemetery, rendering it so sacred that thousands of families across Europe wished to be buried here. In 1870, woodcarver František Rint bleached the bones with lime and fashioned them into chandeliers, coats of arms, and garlands that entirely line the chapel—including a chandelier containing at least one specimen of every bone in the human body.

⏱ 45 min
2

St. Barbara's Church, whose construction began in 1388 under Petr Parléř—the same architect who worked on Prague's St. Vitus Cathedral—is dedicated to the patron saint of miners. Its triple tent-shaped vault, a masterpiece by Benedikt Ried from the 15th century, and its frescoes depicting miners at work and coin minting testify to the wealth that earned Kutná Hora the title of Bohemia's second city, inscribed on UNESCO's World Heritage List since 1995.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Italian Court (Vlašský dvůr), founded in the 13th century by King Wenceslas II, served as the Bohemian kingdom's principal mint for two centuries. Kutná Hora's miners then extracted a third of Central Europe's silver production, financing among other projects the construction of St. Barbara's.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Descend into a reconstructed medieval silver mine beneath Kaňk Hill. The galleries dug from the 13th century extend as deep as 250 meters underground. Hard hats and miner's smocks are provided on-site.

⏱ 90 min

Jicin

★★☆☆☆
1

Prachovské Skály, 5 km northwest of Jičín, are the most famous rock formations in Bohemian Paradise (Český ráj), the world's first UNESCO Global Geopark in the Czech Republic. These sandstone columns and labyrinths formed over 100 million years ago in a shallow sea. Erosion has sculpted towers reaching 30 meters, narrow corridors, and rock windows. One gorge bears the name 'Emperor's Passage' since Emperor Franz I of Austria had himself carried through it by sedan chair around 1804 rather than walk.

⏱ 30 min
2

Fifteen kilometers south of Prachovské Skály, the ruins of Trosky Castle are the symbol of Bohemian Paradise. Founded in the second half of the 14th century by Čeněk of Wartenberg, the castle was built atop two basalt volcanic plugs 47 and 57 meters high, christened Baba (the Old Woman) and Panna (the Maiden). Never conquered by the Hussites, it was finally burned by the imperial army in 1648 during the Thirty Years' War. Its ruined towers now offer panoramic views over the surrounding forests and rock cities.

⏱ 60 min
3

Not far from Trosky ruins, the rock town of Hrubá Skála is a labyrinth of 400 sandstone towers reaching up to 60 meters in height, perched in deep forest. Hrubá Skála Castle, first mentioned in 1353 as the seat of the Valdštejn family, was rebuilt in Renaissance style in the 16th century and then neo-Gothic in the 19th century. Its viewing platforms offer sweeping views over this sandstone maze.

⏱ 45 min
4

Valdštejnovo Square in Jičín, dominated by the 52-meter Baroque Valdice Gate tower, is the heart of this town that Duke Albrecht von Wallenstein intended to transform into his duchy's capital in the 17th century. The avenue of 1,200 lime trees planted by his order, stretching 2 km, leads from the gate to Valdice Castle.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Hike through Prachovské Skály on marked trails. Three circuits are offered: yellow (1.5 km, 45 min, very accessible), red (2.5 km, 1h30, recommended for families), and green (3.5 km, 2h, most spectacular with the best viewpoints over rock towers and forests). At the entrance, look for the passage nicknamed Mouse Hole—a sandstone corridor so narrow you must squeeze through sideways.

⏱ 150 min

Karlovy Vary

★★★★☆
1

Karlovy Vary (Carlsbad in German) received royal town status from Charles IV in 1370, after the emperor discovered thermal springs here during a hunting trip. The town possesses 12 main hot springs and approximately 300 secondary ones. The most powerful, Vřídlo, erupts at 73°C with a flow of 2,000 liters per minute, forming a natural 12-meter geyser. Inscribed on UNESCO's World Heritage List in 2021 under 'Great Spa Towns of Europe,' the city has welcomed Goethe, Chopin, Schiller, Karl Marx, and Peter the Great beneath its colonnades.

⏱ 30 min
2

Stroll along the Teplá River discovering the colonnades that line the spa center. The Mill Colonnade (Mlýnská kolonáda), built between 1871 and 1881 by Josef Zítek in neo-Renaissance style, is the most imposing: 124 columns support a 132-meter gallery sheltering five springs. Along the way, taste the thermal water served in small spouted cups—spa tradition dictates sipping it slowly to avoid irritating the stomach.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Imperial Spa (Císařské lázně), completed in 1895 in French neo-Renaissance style by Viennese firm Fellner & Helmer, is the town's most monumental spa building, classified as a national cultural monument. After extensive restoration completed in 2023, its interior adorned with carved woodwork and murals depicting illustrious visitors from centuries past is once again accessible.

⏱ 45 min
4

The Church of St. Mary Magdalene, built between 1732 and 1736 by Kilian Ignaz Dientzenhofer, is the town's most remarkable Baroque monument. Its dynamic facade and complex interior plan exemplify rare Bohemian Baroque. The neighboring Market Colonnade, an elegant wood-and-iron structure designed by Fellner & Helmer in 1882, shelters three springs including the legendary Charles IV Spring.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Take the funicular up to Diana Observation Tower for sweeping views over the town nestled in its bowl of forested hills, then sample a glass of Becherovka—the 20-herb-and-spice liqueur produced exclusively in Karlovy Vary since 1807.

⏱ 60 min

Indulge in a thermal spa treatment at one of the downtown establishments, which offer mineral baths, massages, and treatments using spring waters reaching temperatures up to 73°C.

⏱ 90 min

Plzen

★★★☆☆
1

St. Bartholomew's Cathedral, whose tower reaches 102 meters, is the Czech Republic's tallest church tower. It dominates Republic Square (náměstí Republiky), the country's largest medieval square after Prague. Inside, the Madonna of Plzeň (circa 1390) is a particularly precious Gothic sculpture.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Great Synagogue of Plzeň, built in 1893, is the world's third-largest synagogue after those in Budapest and New York. Its facade blends Moorish and Romanesque styles, with two 50-meter towers. It can accommodate 2,000 worshippers.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Pilsner Urquell Brewery, founded in 1839 by Plzeň's burghers, produced its first brew on October 5, 1842, under the direction of Bavarian brewmaster Josef Groll. By combining local soft water, Saaz hops, and pale malt, Groll invented that day the world's first pale lager—a style that today represents over 80% of global beer production. The name Pilsner Urquell, meaning 'original source from Pilsen,' was trademarked in 1898 to combat proliferating imitations. Tour the historic cellars dug beneath the city where beer still fermented in beech casks.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Join a guided brewery tour to see the traditional triple-decoction brewing process and taste unfiltered Pilsner Urquell drawn directly from the barrel—the only chance to experience the beer before filtration and pasteurization.

⏱ 90 min

Descend into the town's historic underground—a network of cellars and passages carved beneath the streets since the 13th century—and visit the Brewery Museum housed in a medieval Gothic malthouse.

⏱ 60 min

Cesky Krumlov

★★★★★
1

Český Krumlov Castle is the Czech Republic's second-largest castle complex after Prague, with 40 buildings spread across 17 hectares around five courtyards. Founded in the 13th century by the Vítek family, it passed to the Rosenbergs in 1302—their emblem, a five-petaled rose, appears throughout the town. Under the Rosenbergs in the 16th century, the fortress was transformed into a sumptuous Renaissance residence. Inscribed on UNESCO's World Heritage List since 1992, the complex preserves a remarkable peculiarity: its moats have housed live bears since the 16th century, a tradition maintained to this day.

⏱ 90 min
2

In the fifth courtyard, the Baroque theatre built in 1765-1766 for the Schwarzenberg family is one of only two Baroque theatres in the world entirely preserved in their original condition—the other is at Drottningholm in Sweden. Its complex machinery, painted scenery, costumes, and orchestra pit all date from the 18th century.

⏱ 45 min
3

Descend into the old town encircled by a meander of the Vltava. Its cobblestone lanes bordered by Gothic, Renaissance, and Baroque facades form one of the best-preserved medieval ensembles in Central Europe. St. Vitus Church, founded in 1309 and rebuilt between 1407 and 1439 in Flamboyant Gothic style, houses the tomb of Wilhelm von Rosenberg.

⏱ 60 min
4

The Egon Schiele Art Centrum traces the Austrian Expressionist artist's 1911 sojourn in this town, his mother's birthplace. Schiele lived here several months with his companion Wally Neuzil before being driven out by inhabitants scandalized by his lifestyle.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Paddle a canoe or kayak down the Vltava's meanders around the castle—a loop of several kilometers that allows you to admire the complex from the water. Local outfitters offer half-day rentals.

⏱ 120 min

Telc

★★★★☆
1

Zachariáš of Hradec Square is one of Europe's best-preserved Renaissance squares. Its elongated triangle shape is ringed by arcaded houses with unique pastel facades, all rebuilt after a devastating 1530 fire. Zachariáš of Hradec, returning from a trip to Genoa in 1551 captivated by the Italian Renaissance, undertook to transform Telč into a model town with Italian master architects. Inscribed on UNESCO's World Heritage List since 1992, this historic center is considered the most perfect example of Italian Renaissance art north of the Alps.

⏱ 60 min
2

Telč Castle, transformed from a Gothic fortress into a Renaissance residence beginning in 1553, is one of the Czech Republic's best-preserved Renaissance castles. The Great Golden Hall, extending the entire length of one wing, possesses an extraordinary carved coffered ceiling from the 16th century. Construction was partly financed by the mining dowry of Zachariáš's first wife, Kateřina of Valdštejn.

⏱ 75 min
3

Climb the 153 steps of St. James Church tower for panoramic views over the square and the three ponds encircling the town—medieval reservoirs that once served as natural moats and fish farms.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Walk or cycle the trails along the ponds for shifting perspectives of Renaissance facades reflected in the water.

⏱ 60 min

Brno

★★★★☆
1

Špilberk Castle, founded around 1277 by King Přemysl Otakar II, began as a Gothic royal residence before being transformed into a Baroque fortress in the 17th century. From the 18th century onward, the Habsburgs made it Central Europe's most dreaded prison. Its underground casemates held political prisoners from across Europe—Italian writer Silvio Pellico spent eight years here and wrote the book My Prisons. Now the city museum, it offers panoramic views over Brno from its ramparts.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Cathedral of Sts. Peter and Paul, whose two Gothic spires reach 84 meters, has dominated Brno from Petrov Hill since medieval times. A local legend recounts that in 1645, during the Swedish siege, defenders rang the noon bells an hour early—at 11 a.m.—tricking Swedish General Torstenson, who had promised to lift the siege if the town hadn't fallen by noon. Ever since, Brno's bells ring noon at 11 a.m.

⏱ 45 min
3

Villa Tugendhat, built by Ludwig Mies van der Rohe between 1929 and 1930 for the industrialist Tugendhat family, has been inscribed on UNESCO's World Heritage List since 2001. Its large living room opening onto the sloping garden, its partitions in golden onyx and rosewood, and its motorized bay windows that retract into the floor were revolutionary for the era. The Gestapo requisitioned it in 1939. Ironically, this villa hosted Václav Klaus and Vladimír Mečiar in 1992 when they signed the dissolution of Czechoslovakia.

⏱ 75 min
4

The St. James Church Ossuary, discovered only in 2001 beneath a downtown square, is Europe's second-largest ossuary after the Paris Catacombs. It holds the bones of 50,000 people—victims of plague epidemics and Hussite Wars from the 14th to 18th centuries.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Stroll through the Cabbage Market (Zelný trh), a lively Baroque square at the cathedral's foot where a market has been held since the Middle Ages. In the evening, explore the bars and breweries of the Veveří student quarter, which reveals Brno's other face: a young, vibrant city far from tourist circuits.

⏱ 90 min
