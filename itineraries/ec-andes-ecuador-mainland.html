Quito

★★★★★
1

Start at Plaza Grande, the beating heart of Quito's historic center—designated UNESCO's very first World Heritage Site in 1978, the first cultural site on Earth to receive this honor. The square is flanked by the Carondelet Palace (presidential seat), the Metropolitan Cathedral, and the Archbishop's Palace, all arranged around this plaza founded in the 16th century atop Inca ruins. The historic center holds 24 churches, 32 museums, and 13 plazas—grab a bench, settle in, and watch the shoeshine workers and street vendors go about their day.

⏱ 45 min
2

Step inside La Compañía de Jesús church, just off Plaza Grande. Its construction took 160 years (1605-1765), and the result is breathtaking: the entire nave is covered in gold leaf—about 52 kilos remain after the pillaging that followed the Jesuit expulsion in 1767. Look for the 250 carved faces in the pulpit and the massive 1879 painting of Hell. Photos aren't allowed inside, but free guided tours in Spanish or English decode every corner. Budget a full hour.

⏱ 60 min
3

Continue to the San Francisco monastery, one of South America's largest religious complexes. Built starting in 1534—the same year Quito was founded by the Spanish—it occupies an entire city block and houses over 3,500 works from the Quiteño school of art. The austere facade gives no hint of the riches inside. Don't miss the Cantuña chapel, wrapped in local legend: an indigenous craftsman supposedly sold his soul to the devil to finish the work on time.

⏱ 50 min
4

Late in the day, climb up to El Panecillo, the hill that rises 175 meters above downtown. The 41-meter winged Virgin perched on top is an enlarged replica of the Virgen de Legarda kept in San Francisco church. From up here, the view spreads across the entire city wedged between the flanks of Pichincha volcano. Take a taxi up—the walking route isn't recommended for safety reasons. Try to arrive for the late-afternoon light.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

In the evening, wander down Calle La Ronda, Quito's oldest street. Once a haunt of poets and musicians, it now hosts artisan workshops, galleries, and bars serving canelazo—a hot drink made with aguardiente, cinnamon, and fruit juice. Try the colaciones, artisan sugar-coated candies that have been made here since colonial times.

⏱ 75 min

Quito

★★★★★
1

Before leaving Quito, climb the towers of the Basílica del Voto Nacional, the largest neo-Gothic basilica in the Americas. Standing 115 meters tall, construction began in 1884 and it remains officially unfinished—local legend says the world will end the day it's completed. What makes this church unique are its gargoyles: instead of classic demons, you'll see iguanas, Galápagos tortoises, and condors carved into the stone. Climb the staircases and metal ladders up to the bell tower—the 360° view over Quito is worth every step.

⏱ 60 min
2

Head north (about 30 minutes) to Mitad del Mundo. This 30-meter tower topped with a globe was built between 1979 and 1982 to honor La Condamine's 1736 French geodesic mission. Fun detail: the monument sits 240 meters off from the true 0°0'0'' latitude. The classic photo with one foot in each hemisphere remains a must. The complex also houses a planetarium and pavilions on indigenous cultures.

⏱ 50 min
3

Right next door, the Intiñan museum sits on the actual equatorial line (measured by military GPS). Guides lead you through experiments: balancing an egg on a nail, watching water swirl in different directions. It's playful and a bit touristy, but the explanations about indigenous peoples are well done. Budget 45 minutes for the guided tour.

⏱ 45 min
4

Ten minutes away by car, stop at the viewpoint for Pululahua Geobotanical Reserve. This volcanic crater, 5 km across, is one of only two inhabited craters in the world—families farm the land at 2,600 meters elevation. If you have time, descend into the crater via the trail (about 30 minutes down, steeper coming back).

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Back in Quito by late afternoon, ride the TelefériQo, one of the world's highest cable cars. In 10 minutes it hauls you up to 4,050 meters on Pichincha's flanks. The view across Quito and the Avenue of the Volcanoes is spectacular—assuming clouds don't block everything. Go before 3pm to maximize your chances. Bring a windbreaker; the cold at this altitude catches people off guard.

⏱ 90 min

Riobamba

★★☆☆☆
1

A driving day along the Avenue of the Volcanoes, the 400-km Andean corridor named by Alexander von Humboldt in 1802. From Quito, you head due south between two rows of volcanoes—Cotopaxi, Tungurahua, Chimborazo loom on the horizon. Take a midway break in Ambato, about 2.5 hours down the road.

⏱ 30 min
2

Ambato, nicknamed "the city of flowers and fruits," doesn't appear in classic guidebooks but deserves a stop. Founded in the 16th century, it was destroyed three times by earthquakes (1698, 1797, 1949) and rebuilt each time. Stroll around Plaza Montalvo and visit the Casa y Mausoleo de Juan Montalvo—this great Ecuadorian writer died in Paris in 1889, and his embalmed body rests in a Greek-style mausoleum. If you pass through on a Monday, the big market takes over the streets: indigenous people from the Pilahuín communities descend from the mountains to sell their harvests.

⏱ 60 min
3

Arrive in Riobamba by late afternoon after another 1.5 hours of driving. Perched at 2,750 meters and nicknamed "the Sultana of the Andes," Riobamba sits at the foot of Chimborazo. Take a walk around Plaza Maldonado where the cathedral stands—its facade is one of the rare remnants of the original city, founded in 1534 near Colta lagoon before the 1797 earthquake forced the move.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Head up to Parque 21 de Abril to see Riobamba with Chimborazo as backdrop—the volcano clears more often late in the day than in the morning. Enjoy the evening with some hornado (roast pork), a Sierra specialty. The small restaurants around Mercado La Merced are the most authentic.

⏱ 60 min

Riobamba

★★☆☆☆
1

Head to Chimborazo, 35 minutes from Riobamba. At 6,268 meters, it's Ecuador's highest peak—and more importantly, the point on Earth's surface farthest from the planet's center, surpassing Everest thanks to the equatorial bulge. The road crosses the wildlife reserve of nearly 60,000 hectares. Keep your eyes open: you'll likely spot vicuñas, wild cousins of the llama reintroduced here in the 1990s. They're not skittish and will lift their heads to study you with curious eyes—but drive slowly, they often cross the road.

⏱ 40 min
2

Drive up to the Hermanos Carrel refuge at 4,800 meters—accessible without special equipment. From there, a trail leads in 40 minutes to the Edward Whymper refuge at 5,000 meters, named after British alpinist Edward Whymper, already famous for conquering the Matterhorn, who reached this summit on January 4, 1880 with the Carrel brothers. At this altitude, every step weighs heavy: go slowly, hydrate constantly. If the sky clears, the glaciated summit appears above you.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

On the way back, stop at the community of La Moya (3,200 m). Their Mountain Museum tells the story of the hieleros, men who climbed Chimborazo to fetch blocks of ice to sell at markets. The last known hielero, Baltazar Ushca, practiced this trade alone for decades. His son-in-law hopes to create a memorial trail in his honor. A shared meal with locals will give you a rare glimpse into Andean life.

⏱ 90 min

Active alternative: agencies in Riobamba offer a mountain bike descent from Carrel refuge (4,800 m) down to San Juan village (3,100 m)—33 km and 1,750 meters of descent through the páramo. Duration about 6 hours, accessible without particular experience.

⏱ 360 min

Cuenca

★★★★☆
1

Long driving day (4-5h) between Riobamba and Cuenca, but the detour through Ingapirca makes it fascinating. At 3,160 meters elevation, you'll discover Ecuador's most important archaeological site. First occupied by the Cañari people from around 500 AD, then conquered by the Incas around 1487, it features an elliptical Temple of the Sun—unique in the entire known Inca world. The diorite stones are assembled without mortar with surgical precision. Charles Marie de La Condamine was the first European to describe it in 1739.

⏱ 75 min
2

The guided tour (mandatory, included in the ticket) lasts about 40 minutes and covers the Temple of the Sun, agricultural terraces, and a natural rock whose shape evokes an Inca face. The museum inaugurated in 2018 displays ceramics, textiles, and tools from both civilizations. Don't expect Machu Picchu—the site is modest in size—but the story of Cañari-Inca fusion is captivating. If you have energy, a 30-minute trail descends into the Intihuayco valley.

⏱ 50 min
3

Arrive in Cuenca by late afternoon. Detour through the Turi viewpoint on the hill south of the city. From there, you'll have the best panoramic view of Cuenca and its sky-blue domes—the evening light brings out the red tile roofs and bell towers. It's the perfect postcard to open your Cuenca stay.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Enjoy the evening for a first meal in this city renowned for its Andean gastronomy. Mote pillo (white corn sautéed with eggs and onions) is the local specialty. The small restaurants around the market are the best. Cuenca is very pleasant on foot in the evening, the pedestrian streets are well-lit and the atmosphere is much calmer than Quito.

⏱ 60 min

Cuenca

★★★★☆
1

Start at Parque Calderón, the city's heart, and the Catedral de la Inmaculada Concepción that overlooks it. Its blue domes are Cuenca's symbol—visible from everywhere. Begun in 1885 and completed only in 1975, it was supposed to have two additional towers topped with cupolas, but the architect had to abandon the plan: the ground couldn't support more weight. The interior floors are Carrara marble imported from Italy. Climb the 155 steps to the domes for a plunging view over the historic center.

⏱ 60 min
2

Across the way, don't miss the Catedral Vieja (El Sagrario), one of Latin America's oldest churches, built in 1557. Now transformed into a religious art museum, it surprises with its pastel ceiling paintings—very different from the typical gilded church interiors. Entrance is free.

⏱ 30 min
3

Two blocks from the cathedral, the Plaza de las Flores is enchanting. The permanent flower market, run by women in traditional dress, overflows with roses, lilies, and orchids—Ecuador is one of the world's largest flower exporters. The white Carmen de la Asunción church (1730) forms a dazzling backdrop. This plaza was voted one of the world's most beautiful flower markets.

⏱ 30 min
4

Walk down to the Pumapungo Museum, built on the ruins of the ancient Inca city of Tomebamba—"the Puma's Gate." The open-air archaeological site shows remnants of an Inca administrative center, and the adjoining botanical garden presents over 200 native Andean plants. The indoor museum covers Ecuador's ethnographic history and indigenous cultures. Free admission.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Late in the day, walk down Calle Larga to the Barranco neighborhood along the Tomebamba River. The colorful facades overlooking the river form one of Ecuador's most photographed landscapes. Take the opportunity to visit a Panama hat workshop—here they call them "sombrero de paja toquilla." The Cuenca region is the heart of this artisan production inscribed on UNESCO's intangible heritage list. A good hat takes weeks of weaving.

Cuenca

★★★★☆
1

Day trip to Cajas National Park, just 40 minutes from Cuenca toward Guayaquil. This 28,544-hectare park, created in 1977 and designated a UNESCO biosphere reserve, holds over 230 lakes of glacial origin between 3,100 and 4,450 meters elevation. The páramo landscapes—these high grassy moorlands—recall Scotland on an overcast day. A remnant of the Inca Trail crosses the park for 4 km between Luspa cave and Mamamag lake. Entrance is free but registration with passport is mandatory.

⏱ 60 min
2

For an accessible hike, do the loop around Laguna Toreadora (trail #1, 2.2 km, ~1h30) from the visitor center. If you're in shape and acclimatized, climb toward the Lagunas Unidas on trail #4 (4.2 km) which crosses a polylepis forest—these twisted trees grow higher than any other tree on Earth. The lakes follow one another in total silence, broken only by birds. Bring warm, waterproof clothing: weather changes fast at this altitude.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

On the return from the hike, stop at the Tres Cruces viewpoint, the highest point on the Cuenca-Guayaquil road (4,167 m). On clear days, you can see the Pacific coast from this point—it's the only place in Ecuador where both slopes are visible simultaneously. Back in Cuenca, rest your legs in the Baños de Cuenca hot springs (8 km from downtown), fed by volcanic sources. Hot water at this altitude is pure luxury.

Guayaquil

★★★☆☆
1

Drive from Cuenca to Guayaquil (about 4h), a spectacular 2,500-meter descent from altitude to sea level. You'll pass through Cajas park (quick view if you skipped it yesterday) then cross banana and cacao plantations as you approach the coast. The tropical heat will hit you all at once—prepare for thermal shock after the Andes.

⏱ 30 min
2

Arriving in Guayaquil, head straight to the Las Peñas neighborhood, the city's oldest. Climb the 444 steps of Cerro Santa Ana through the brightly painted facades—each landing bears a number and an anecdote. At the top, the lighthouse and small chapel offer a panoramic view over the Guayas River, the Malecón, and the city rooftops. Go in late afternoon for the golden light and the atmosphere of the bars and art galleries lining the stairs.

⏱ 60 min
3

Walk down to the Malecón 2000, the 2.5-km promenade along the Guayas River. Renovated in the early 2000s, it connects the artisan market in the south to the Las Peñas neighborhood in the north. You'll find gardens, fountains, monuments, and Parque Seminario—don't miss it: its trees are populated by land iguanas basking in the sun by the dozens. Kids love them, but keep your distance: they can bite.

⏱ 50 min

Activities

Evening on the Malecón: the riverside restaurants serve shrimp ceviche (Guayaquil's specialty) and encebollado, a tuna and onion soup considered the country's best hangover cure. The nighttime atmosphere is lively, especially on weekends.

Puerto Lopez

★★☆☆☆
1

Drive from Guayaquil north along the Pacific coast (about 3h). You'll follow the Ruta del Spondylus, the coastal road named after a shell sacred to pre-Columbian civilizations. The landscape shifts dramatically: tropical dry forest, giant cacti, and small fishing villages. Arrive in Puerto López, a quiet town that serves as base for exploring Machalilla National Park.

⏱ 30 min
2

In the afternoon, head to Los Frailes beach, considered one of Ecuador's most beautiful mainland beaches. Located in Machalilla National Park, it forms a white sand crescent bordered by cliffs and turquoise water. Access is via a 20-minute trail through dry forest (free, registration required at the park entrance). There's no infrastructure on the beach—bring water and a picnic. The calm is total on weekdays.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

If you're traveling between June and September, watch for humpback whales from Puerto López's waterfront. They migrate each year from Antarctica to breed in the warm waters off Ecuador's coast. Boat tours leave every morning from the port—book the day before. The sight of these 15-meter giants breaching is stunning.

Montanita

★★☆☆☆
1

Coastal drive south (about 1h) to Montañita, Ecuador's most famous surf village. The vibe is radically different from the rest of the trip: this is a concentration of backpackers, surfers, and craft vendors. The main street is pedestrian, lined with bars, ceviche restaurants, and shell jewelry shops. If you're seeking calm, continue 5 km north to Olón beach, much quieter and just as beautiful.

⏱ 45 min
2

Before settling in Montañita, push 5 km north to Olón. This fishing village is the antithesis of Montañita: calm, family-friendly, with a huge beach nearly deserted on weekdays. On the cliff separating the two villages, the Santuario de Olón—a small white church perched above the ocean—offers a magnificent panorama of the coast. The trail leading there from the beach takes 15 minutes and is worth the detour for photos.

⏱ 45 min
3

Back in Montañita, the beach offers a long stretch of sand with consistent waves—ideal for surfing. Schools offer lessons for all levels, budget 2h for an introduction. Walking north from the center, a wilder spot at the base of the rocky point attracts locals away from the crowds. If surfing isn't your thing, swimming is pleasant but watch the current—stay in supervised areas.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Montañita is mostly known for its nightlife. Bars open in late afternoon with live music, and the party continues until late. If you prefer a more zen plan, sunset from the rocky point north of the beach is superb—bring a local beer and settle in. The shrimp cocktail sold by beach vendors is a classic end-of-day treat.

Activities
