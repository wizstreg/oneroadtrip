Saint Antoine L Abbaye

★★★☆☆
1

Saint-Antoine-l'Abbaye was voted France's Favorite Village in July 2025, and arriving from the plain makes it clear why: the Gothic abbey church rises at the end of an avenue of centuries-old linden trees like a vessel of stone. Its construction spanned from 1130 to the late 15th century—nearly four centuries of building. The structure measures 32 meters wide by 22 meters high, an unusually wider-than-tall format. Inside, don't miss the walnut choir stalls, Aubusson tapestries, and the 17th-18th century organ. Classified as a Historic Monument by Prosper Mérimée as early as 1840.

⏱ 90 min
2

The village's history began in 1070 when Geilin, a local lord, brought back the relics of Saint Anthony the Great from Constantinople—believed to cure "St. Anthony's fire," ergot poisoning that caused gangrene and hallucinations. The Antonine Order, these monk-physicians, eventually established a network of 300 commanderies across Europe. You'll find their treasury in the church sacristies: liturgical vestments, medieval surgical instruments, and an ivory Christ. Plan for a guided tour to access these pieces—schedules vary by season.

⏱ 60 min
3

The museum housed in the former novitiate offers two permanent exhibitions—"Chronicles of a Medieval Abbey" and "Healing Soul and Body"—and provides access to the medieval garden in the former stables. Medicinal plants, herbs, and fruit trees are cultivated according to Antonine pharmacopoeia. The garden is free; allow an hour for the complete visit.

⏱ 60 min
4

Wander through the goulets—narrow cobblestone alleys—between half-timbered houses and facades adorned with mullioned windows from the 14th and 15th centuries. The village is also a stop on the Way of St. James. Climb to the viewpoint above the abbey courtyard to take in the entire site with the Chambaran hills as backdrop.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The 2-3 km heritage loop within the village walls connects the abbey church, medieval alleys, market hall, and rampart remnants. Interpretive panels mark the route. Start from the abbey courtyard square in late afternoon when the light gilds the molasse facades—the local golden, slightly rosy stone.

⏱ 90 min

The trail to Saint-Jean-le-Fromental chapel follows the old Antonine pilgrim paths for about 7 km (2h45 round trip). Along the way, you'll pass walnut groves overlooked by the Vercors rim on the horizon. A map is available at the tourist office.

⏱ 165 min

Saint Marcellin

★★☆☆☆
1

In the 15th century, Saint-Marcellin was the stronghold hosting the region's largest market, held three times weekly. This market gave its name to the town's emblematic cheese—an 80-gram disc with soft paste and bloomy rind, first documented in King Louis XI's household accounts from 1461. Saved from a bear in 1447, Louis XI allegedly received bread and cheese—Saint-Marcellin—from two local woodcutters. According to legend, he promised them fortune and nobility. The cheese earned its PGI designation in 2013, covering 274 communes across Isère, Drôme, and Savoie.

⏱ 45 min
2

Saint-Marcellin's old center preserves a medieval layout around its church with arcades, narrow streets, and remnants of ramparts. Then descend to the Isère riverbanks: the tree-lined promenades along the river offer open views of the Royans hillsides.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The historic gates and suburbs circuit of Saint-Marcellin covers about 2 km on flat ground and reveals the different layers of occupation from the medieval town onward. If you're there Wednesday or Saturday morning, the weekly market is a perfect opportunity to taste Saint-Marcellin PGI in its two states: dry with blue rind, Dauphiné style; or soft and creamy, Lyon style.

⏱ 90 min

The riverside walk along the Isère, downstream from the town center, leads to the confluence with the Bourne. It's a bucolic route with no elevation gain, perfect for evening strolls with reflections on the water and Royans hillsides in the background.

⏱ 60 min

La Sone

★★☆☆☆
1

La Sône is a boating village perched on the right bank of the Isère, between Royans and Vercors. Its church and former watermen's houses recall the era when the river was an active commercial route connecting the Valence plain to Royans villages. Take time to walk the quays and observe the terraced gardens on the opposite bank—the village opens entirely onto the river.

⏱ 45 min
2

La Sône's mills long operated using the current's power. Though the industrial buildings have nearly all disappeared, their locations along the quays remain visible in the landscape. Sit on a waterside bench to gaze at the green Royans hillsides across the way: it's one of the valley's most peaceful views.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The quays and nearby hamlets loop—2-3 km with no elevation change—follows the Isère then climbs to the first hamlets perched on the hillside. You'll see the architectural variations between lowland houses and the first stone constructions typical of Royans.

⏱ 75 min

The path to riverside viewpoints upstream from the village reveals the river's geography: across the way, the limestone foothills of the Vercors begin to rise, heralding the Bourne gorges you'll reach in two days.

⏱ 60 min

Saint Nazaire En Royans

★★★☆☆
1

Upon entering Saint-Nazaire-en-Royans, the aqueduct greets you—without warning. This structure, 235 meters long and 35 meters high with 17 arches, was built between 1873 and 1876 to irrigate the Valence plain. It was presented at the 1878 Paris Universal Exposition as a masterpiece of hydraulic engineering. A panoramic elevator beside the artificial lake formed in 1958 by the EDF dam lifts you to the pedestrian walkway above the Bourne. From up there, the view extends to the entrances of the Bourne gorges on the right and Combe Laval on the left. Allow 1 hour.

⏱ 60 min
2

The medieval village below the aqueduct deserves a stroll: steep streets, old houses, and remnants of the 12th-century Dauphine tower recall that Saint-Nazaire was a Dauphin stronghold. During World War II, on June 29, 1944, the village was bombed by German aviation—it was a strategic crossroads between Valence, Grenoble, and the Vercors maquis. Some forty resistance fighters were executed in the castle park.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Thaïs cave at the village entrance is a prehistoric cavity inhabited during the Magdalenian period. An hour-long guided tour reveals galleries rich in archaeology. Don't confuse it with Choranche cave: Thaïs is smaller but older in terms of human occupation.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Local operators offer paddlewheel boat cruises on the Isère from the artificial lake's shores. The 1.5-hour outing circles the limestone cliffs of the Vercors foothills and travels upstream to Pont-en-Royans. A guide narrates the history of villages crossed and the birds nesting in the cliffs.

⏱ 90 min

The hike to the Vanille viewpoint (3-hour round trip) climbs the western hillside and offers a 360° panorama of the aqueduct, village, and three breaches in the Vercors cliff face. Yellow and red trail markers from the lakeside.

⏱ 180 min

Pont En Royans

★★★☆☆
1

Pont-en-Royans reveals itself from the left bank of the Bourne, from the Picard bridge: a row of ochre, pink, and yellow-fronted houses suspended from the limestone cliff face above the river's green waters. These structures date from the early 16th century—built overhanging to maximize space on this narrow site and facilitate timber trade descending by raft from the Vercors. Stendhal described them in his 1838 "Memoirs of a Tourist." Classified as Historic Monuments in 1944. The view from the bridge is the most photographed in Royans.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Water Museum has occupied a 4,000 m² building since 2002 that was successively a convent (13th-19th century), silk mill, then Compagnie Générale d'Électricité factory employing up to 400 people until 1990. Architect Andrea Bruno inserted a contemporary building into this historic fabric. You enter through a conduit shaped like a penstock—an allegory of the water droplet. The route leads to a glass balcony suspended above the Bourne. The mineral water bar with its approximately 2,000 selections is a curiosity not to be missed.

⏱ 90 min
3

The entrance to the Bourne gorges emerges at the village's east exit: limestone cliffs close in, the road clings to the cliff face, and the river roars below. Stroll the developed riverbanks—swimming possible in summer in calm areas—and observe the striated karst rock walls above you. This is the road that climbs to Choranche and Villard-de-Lans.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The pedestrian climb to the Three Castles feudal tower above the village takes 30-45 minutes round trip. It crosses the medieval quarter with its winding streets and terraced gardens before opening onto a panorama of Royans and the Bourne gorges. Moderate effort but rewarding.

⏱ 60 min

The viewpoints-quays-footbridges loop (3 km) connects the village's main viewpoints without ever leaving the banks or alleys. Begin with the Bourne quays downstream, climb to the Picard bridge for the view of suspended houses, then finish with the shaded upstream banks.

⏱ 75 min

Choranche

★★★☆☆
1

The Choranche cave is the most spectacular natural site in the northern Vercors. In 1871, drought drove villagers to follow a dry streambed; at the foot of the Presles cliffs (300 meters high), they entered a cavity from which water flowed. It wasn't until 1897 that Oscar Descombaz, speleology pioneer, conducted the first explorations. Open to the public since 1967. Choranche's distinctive feature: thousands of tubular stalactites—"calcite straws" exceeding 3 meters—cover the vaults. They've grown only 5 cm in 100 years. No site in Europe concentrates so many. Bring a sweater: it's permanently 10°C.

⏱ 75 min
2

Along two underground rivers, you'll encounter olms in their aquariums: blind, depigmented cave amphibians from Slovenia, capable of fasting for months and living up to 80 years. The visit concludes in the Cathedral Room—40 meters in diameter, 25 meters high—with a sound and light show projected onto millennia-old walls. Book ahead in high season.

⏱ 15 min
3

The cave's outdoor site offers exceptional views of the Presles cliffs and limestone cirque. Before or after the visit, walk the developed path at the foot of the walls: karst geology is visible to the naked eye, with Jurassic limestone strata exposed for hundreds of meters.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The balcony trail overlooking the Bourne valley starts from Choranche village and climbs 3-5 km in marked aerial passages along the cliffs. Across the way, the Presles walls with their climbing routes, and below, the river emerging from rock. One of the Vercors' most striking routes—bring hiking shoes.

⏱ 120 min

Rencurel

★★★☆☆
1

Rencurel sits at the outlet of the Bourne gorges, where the valley opens onto meadows and hamlets scattered through forests. The village preserves a chapel and rural architecture typical of the Vercors—steep roofs, gallery farms, and stone walls. It's a transition village between the limestone cliff Royans and the gentler plateau that heralds Villard-de-Lans.

⏱ 45 min
2

From the viewpoint above the village, you have a plunging view of the Bourne gorges you arrived through. The wooded ridges of the Quatre Montagnes extend to the east. It's a different framing from the cave: here, it's the overall geography of the Vercors that imposes itself.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The hamlets and clearings loop around Rencurel (4-6 km, minimal elevation gain) crosses meadows and forest edges between the commune's scattered hamlets. In May-June, meadows blooming with gentians and wild orchids are spectacular. This kind of pastoral mid-mountain landscape is exactly what justifies the designation "Vercors Regional Natural Park."

⏱ 120 min

The round trip to the gorges viewpoint from the village heights (about 45 minutes) follows a marked forest path. The view of the Bourne gorge cut between cliffs gives the measure of the relief you've just traversed from Pont-en-Royans.

⏱ 60 min

Villard-de-Lans is the capital of the Isère Vercors, at 1,050 meters altitude, dominated by the Grande Moucherolle peak rising to 2,285 meters. 2

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The urban loop and balcony path (3-4 km) starts from the center, follows the first alpine pastures, and climbs to a plateau viewpoint. In summer, ski runs become grassy trails that let you appreciate the domain's scale without major effort. The Grande Moucherolle closes the horizon to the east like a limestone wall.

⏱ 90 min

The forest-edge walk to alpine pastures above the village follows the Chemin des Clots for about 5 km. You'll encounter summer herds and the first firs rising on either side of the path. It's a good introduction to Vercors landscape before the more demanding days ahead.

Activities

⏱ 120 min

The heritage circuit of the center and old Autrans farms (2 km loop) shows different generations of plateau construction: large 19th-century farms with crenellated roofs, early 20th-century vacation homes, and contemporary resort buildings. Map available at the tourist office.

Vassieux En Vercors

★★★☆☆
1

Vassieux-en-Vercors is a martyred village and Companion of the Liberation: on July 21-22, 1944, Germans landed by glider on the airstrip built by resistance fighters. Within days, 73 of 430 inhabitants were massacred, 91 resistance fighters killed, and the village 97% destroyed. In total, the summer 1944 Vercors battles claimed 840 French casualties. The departmental Resistance museum, founded in 1973 by former resistance fighter Joseph La Picirella and completely renovated in 2010, houses over 1,000 period objects and interactive terminals. Allow 1h30.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Vercors Resistance Memorial, inaugurated in 1994 at Col de la Chau 5 minutes from the village, is a contemporary building "camouflaged" in the mountain—a citadel embedded in the terrain referencing the resistance fighters' clandestinity. The 1,200 m² immersive audio-visual journey blends participant testimonies, unpublished archives, and 3D models. The visit concludes on a viewpoint overlooking Vassieux village and the High Plateaus ridges: a moment of contemplation facing this landscape that witnessed everything.

⏱ 75 min
3

The national cemetery at the foot of the memorial road gathers the graves of resistance fighters and civilians killed in summer 1944. A sober place of remembrance, surrounded by the plateau's high-altitude grasslands. The white crosses contrast with the limestone ridges in the background.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

The plateau loop on white trails (6-8 km, very gentle slope) departs Vassieux village and crosses the vast high-altitude grasslands of the Drôme Vercors. In summer, the place is particularly quiet—the high plateaus remain little frequented outside season. The 1944 airstrip track (called "Taille-Crayon") is still visible in the landscape.

⏱ 150 min

Correncon En Vercors

★★★☆☆
1

Corrençon-en-Vercors separated from Villard-de-Lans in 1857 to form its own commune. It's the southernmost village of the Villard-Corrençon ski domain—the Vercors' largest alpine ski domain, linked since 1984, with 125 km of trails from 1,143 to 2,050 meters altitude. The alpine village is smaller and quieter than Villard, with its chalets, flagstone walls, and small squares. It's a good starting point for the High Plateaus, a Nature Reserve since 1985.

⏱ 30 min
2

Clearings and trail heads to high combes open just outside the village. The view of the Grande Moucherolle and Roc Cornafion (2,049 m) from the pastures above Corrençon is one of the most beautiful in the Isère Vercors. From these heights you understand this massif's uniqueness: a plateau closed on all sides by cliffs, a natural fortress that explains the 1944 maquis.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The panoramic loop at the High Plateaus edge (6-8 km) starts from Clos de la Balme, Corrençon's ski base, and climbs to the first ridges of the Nature Reserve. The path to Grand Veymont (Vercors' highest point at 2,341 m) is glimpsed in the distance. This walk is the perfect conclusion to a Royans-Vercors circuit: you look back toward the Isère plain you left 14 days earlier.

⏱ 180 min

The heritage walk through the village—flagstone and wood-shingle roofs, dry-stone walls, fountains, and squares—takes 30-45 minutes and reveals the vernacular architecture of the Isère Vercors before leaving the massif. A map is available at the Villard-de-Lans tourist office, 10 km away.
