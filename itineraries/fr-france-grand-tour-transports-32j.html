Total: 22 stopovers, 5 UNESCO sites, three coastlines (Mediterranean, Atlantic, English Channel), one domestic flight — everything else by train or bus.

1

Start at the Trocadéro on the Right Bank. This is where you get the best straight-on view of the Eiffel Tower, especially in the morning when the light hits the ironwork head-on. The terraced gardens descend to the fountains, and the esplanade of the Palais de Chaillot frames the classic postcard panorama you've seen a thousand times.

⏱ 45 min
2

Cross the Pont d'Iéna and head for the Eiffel Tower. Standing 330 metres including its antenna, it was built by Gustave Eiffel for the 1889 World's Fair and was originally meant to be dismantled after 20 years — it survived only because of its usefulness as a radio antenna. Book online several weeks ahead, or expect a two-hour queue. The second floor (115m) offers the best balance between views and vertigo.

⏱ 120 min
3

Take metro line 8 to Concorde. The square covers 8.6 hectares — the largest in Paris. The Luxor Obelisk at its centre dates from the 13th century BC (the reign of Ramesses II) and was gifted by Mehemet Ali, Viceroy of Egypt, in 1830 and erected on the square in 1836. Louis XVI was guillotined here in 1793. So was Marie-Antoinette.

⏱ 45 min
4

Walk up the Champs-Élysées to the Arc de Triomphe — 1.9 km of avenue. Napoleon commissioned the arch in 1806 after Austerlitz, but never saw it completed in 1836. There are 284 steps to the top. Under the vault, the Tomb of the Unknown Soldier and its flame, rekindled every evening at 6:30pm.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

In the evening, head back to the Eiffel Tower for the light show — every hour on the hour after dark, 20,000 LED bulbs flash for five minutes. Spread out on the Champ-de-Mars with a picnic: it's a proper Parisian ritual.

⏱ 90 min

For dinner, try Rue Cler in the 7th — a lively pedestrian market street with solid bistrots in the evenings. Budget €25–40 for a main and a glass of wine at a classic spot like Café Constant or Le Petit Cler.

1

Arrive at the Louvre the moment it opens at 9am sharp. Use the Carrousel entrance (the underground passage on the Rivoli side) rather than the pyramid — the queue is shorter. The world's largest museum holds 380,000 works, with 35,000 on display across 73,000 m². Prioritise the Mona Lisa (Denon wing, 1st floor, room 711), the Venus de Milo, the Winged Victory of Samothrace, and the Napoleon III apartments.

⏱ 210 min
2

Walk through the Tuileries Garden to Place de la Concorde — 25 hectares, created in 1564 for Catherine de' Medici, the oldest garden in Paris. The green metal chairs scattered around the central pond are one of the city's best-loved details.

⏱ 45 min
3

At the far end of the Tuileries, step into the Orangerie for Monet's Water Lilies. Eight monumental compositions totalling 91 metres, housed in two oval rooms designed by architect Camille Lefèvre following Monet's own instructions. He offered the gift to the French state on 12 November 1918, the day after the armistice; the deed was signed on 12 April 1922, and the rooms inaugurated in May 1927, a few months after his death. The Walter-Guillaume collection downstairs features Renoir, Cézanne, Utrillo, and Soutine.

⏱ 90 min
4

Head up towards the Palais-Royal via Rue de Rivoli. The inner courtyard contains Buren's Columns (1986) — 260 black and white marble cylinders. The rear gardens, lined with arcaded galleries of antique shops and cafés, are an unexpected haven of calm right in the city centre.

⏱ 60 min
5

Finish at the Opéra Garnier. Completed in 1875, 11,000 m², 2,000 seats. Its Italian-style red-and-gold auditorium, ceiling painted by Chagall in 1964, and grand staircase in 30 different marbles are among the great theatrical interiors of Europe. Self-guided visit €15.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Stop at Angelina, 226 Rue de Rivoli, for a L'Africain hot chocolate — the thickest in Paris since 1903, served with homemade whipped cream. €9. Proust and Coco Chanel were regulars.

⏱ 45 min

Dinner in the Palais-Royal neighbourhood — Le Grand Colbert (a grand 1900s brasserie), Le Gabriel, or Le Soufflé, a restaurant devoted to sweet and savoury soufflés since 1961. €40–60.

📍 Notre-Dame Cathedral Paris📍 Sainte-Chapelle Paris📍 Panthéon Paris📍 Luxembourg Gardens Paris📍 Musée d'Orsay Paris
1

Île de la Cité — the historic cradle of Paris. Notre-Dame reopened in December 2024 after five years of post-fire restoration following the blaze of 15 April 2019. The new spire is a faithful recreation of Viollet-le-Duc's 1859 design. Entry is free; booking a time slot is not mandatory but strongly recommended on notredamedeparis.fr to avoid the queue.

⏱ 75 min
2

Two hundred metres away, the Sainte-Chapelle. Saint Louis had it built in seven years (1242–1248) to house the Crown of Thorns. The upper chapel: 15 stained-glass windows standing 15 metres tall, depicting 1,113 biblical scenes, with 90% of the original 13th-century glass still in place. When the sun is out, the kaleidoscope effect is breathtaking. €13.

⏱ 60 min
3

Cross to the Latin Quarter. Shakespeare & Company, the English-language bookshop opened in 1951 by George Whitman — Hemingway, Fitzgerald, and Ginsberg all slept here. Climb to the Panthéon, a former church converted into a national mausoleum in 1791: Voltaire, Rousseau, Hugo, Zola, Curie, and Joséphine Baker (2021) are all interred here. Foucault's pendulum has been demonstrating the Earth's rotation since 1851.

⏱ 90 min
4

Descend to the Luxembourg Garden — 25 hectares, created by Marie de' Medici in 1612 on the model of the Boboli Garden in Florence. It is laid out around the Luxembourg Palace, seat of the French Senate since 1799. Children rent toy sailboats to race on the octagonal pond.

⏱ 60 min
5

The Musée d'Orsay, a former railway station built for the 1900 World's Fair and converted into a museum in 1986. Monumental clock, 138-metre nave. The world's finest Impressionist collection: Monet, Renoir, Van Gogh, Manet, Degas, Cézanne, Gauguin. Allow at least two hours. €16, closed Mondays.

⏱ 150 min

Activities

For lunch, try Polidor on Rue Monsieur-le-Prince — a workers' soup kitchen open since 1845, still serving honest food at honest prices (€15–20), with checked tablecloths and numbered cloth napkins. Hemingway, Joyce, and Verlaine all ate here.

⏱ 90 min

End the day on the Left Bank quays, UNESCO-listed since 1991. Between the Pont Neuf and the Pont des Arts, the bouquinistes have been setting up their 900 green boxes since the 16th century. The bridges are illuminated after dark.

Metro to Abbesses — the deepest station within Paris proper (36 metres), surpassed since January 2025 by Villejuif-Gustave Roussy on line 14. Original Art Nouveau Guimard canopy. Walk up to the Wall of Love: 612 declarations in 311 languages on blue ceramic tiles. Then Rue Lepic, where Amélie Poulain worked at the Café des 2 Moulins (no. 15).

⏱ 60 min
2

Climb via Rue Tholozé to the Moulin de la Galette, the last surviving original windmill of Montmartre (17th century). Renoir painted his Bal du moulin de la Galette here in 1876. Rue Girardon and Rue Norvins bring you to the Place du Tertre, the historic heart of the village.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Sacré-Coeur basilica, completed in 1914, crowns the top of the Butte at 130 metres. Its Château-Landon limestone actually whitens in the rain. The dome (€7) offers the second-highest viewpoint in Paris — but the view from the steps in front is already exceptional. The basilica itself is free.

⏱ 75 min
4

Descend on the north side past the Montmartre Vineyard — 2,000 vines planted on Rue des Saules in 1933, with a festive harvest in early October. Le Lapin Agile, a cabaret open since 1860 where Picasso, Modigliani, and Utrillo used to drink. The Moulin Rouge on the Boulevard de Clichy, open since 1889.

⏱ 75 min
5

Afternoon: metro to République for the Canal Saint-Martin. 4.5 km dug by Napoleon in 1825 to supply Paris with drinking water. Iron swing bridges painted dark green, 9 working locks, pedestrianised banks at weekends. This is the Paris of bobos and intellectuals — cocktail bars, vintage shops.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

In Montmartre, skip the Place du Tertre — it's a tourist trap. Head instead to Rue des Abbesses or Rue Lepic: Le Relais Gascon does a generous warm goat's cheese salad for €14; Pink Mamma is the go-to for trendy Italian.

⏱ 90 min

Evening on the Canal Saint-Martin. Le Verre Volé (a cult wine bar and bistrot in the 10th), Holybelly (brunch and coffee), or head to Place Sainte-Marthe — a small, almost secret cobblestoned square with a genuinely local feel.

1

Metro to Saint-Paul for the Marais, the best-preserved neighbourhood in old Paris — largely spared by Haussmann in the 19th century. The Place des Vosges, inaugurated in 1612 by Henri IV, was Paris's first royal square: 36 identical pavilions in red brick and white stone. Victor Hugo lived here for 16 years, at no. 6.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Musée Picasso, on Rue de Thorigny, occupies the Hôtel Salé — a private mansion from 1659. Some 5,000 works by Picasso: paintings, sculptures, ceramics, engravings. The collection came to the state as a donation from his heirs in lieu of inheritance tax. €16, closed Mondays.

⏱ 90 min
3

Lunch on Rue des Rosiers, the heart of the historic Jewish quarter. L'As du Fallafel (or Chez Hanna directly opposite) — €7–9 for a stuffed pitta. Lenny Kravitz and Scarlett Johansson both stop here whenever they're in Paris.

⏱ 60 min
4

The Centre Pompidou closed on 22 September 2025 for a five-year renovation, with reopening scheduled for 2030. The exterior architecture remains spectacular: the Renzo Piano and Richard Rogers building (1977) with its colour-coded pipework (blue=air, yellow=electricity, green=water, red=circulation). The Place Beaubourg attracts street performers and musicians. During construction, the collections are touring via the Constellation programme (Centre Pompidou-Metz, the Grand Palais, and the Tripostal in Lille for temporary shows); a new Centre Pompidou Francilien is due to open in Massy between late 2026 and 2027.

⏱ 45 min
5

Metro to Père-Lachaise. 44 hectares, Paris's largest cemetery, 70,000 graves since 1804. Notable tombs: Jim Morrison (division 6), Edith Piaf (97), Oscar Wilde (89, covered in lipstick kisses), Chopin (11), Molière (25), Apollinaire (86). Free map at the entrance.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

End of the day at Place de la Bastille. The July Column commemorates those who died during the Three Glorious Days (1830), not the storming of the Bastille in 1789. Have a drink on Rue de Lappe, or on the quieter Rue de la Roquette.

⏱ 90 min

Dinner in the Marais. Breizh Café for gourmet Breton crêpes (€16–22), Chez Janou for a Provençal bistrot with unlimited chocolate mousse, or Miznon on Rue des Écouffes for Israeli-style pittas. €35–50.

1

The Musée Rodin, on Rue de Varenne, is housed in the Hôtel Biron (1732), where Rodin had his studio at the end of his life. In 1916 he bequeathed to the state 6,600 sculptures, 8,000 drawings, and his personal collection including works by Van Gogh and Monet. The three-hectare garden holds The Thinker, The Burghers of Calais, and The Gates of Hell. €14, closed Mondays.

⏱ 120 min
2

Les Invalides. Commissioned by Louis XIV in 1670 to house wounded soldiers — still a functioning hospital today. The gilded dome rises to 107 metres and shelters the church of Saint-Louis and Napoleon's tomb, a sarcophagus of red porphyry placed here in 1861. The Army Museum covers 12 centuries of French military history. €15.

⏱ 120 min
3

Metro to Bercy (line 14). Bercy Village, a 19th-century wine warehouse complex converted into a cobblestoned shopping and dining promenade in 2001. The Parc de Bercy is right next door — 14 hectares with three themed gardens, and the original railway tracks preserved in the ground.

⏱ 75 min
4

Metro to Buttes-Chaumont (line 7bis). This 25-hectare park was inaugurated in 1867 by Napoleon III — one of the four great Haussmann parks. Built on a former gypsum quarry, it plays on the terrain with artificial cliffs, a stalactite cave, a lake with a central island topped by a Temple of the Sibyl, and a suspension bridge designed by Gustave Eiffel. Completely unlike any other park in Paris.

⏱ 90 min
5

Walk down towards Belleville and Ménilmontant. Belleville is Paris's second-highest point after Montmartre. Street art, world cuisine (the best Asian neighbourhood in Paris on Rue de Belleville). The Cité Fleurie, with its pedestrian lanes, offers a glimpse of the Paris that escaped Haussmann.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

If you still have energy: Parc de la Villette in the 19th — 55 hectares, Paris's largest park, designed by Bernard Tschumi in 1987. Cité des Sciences, the Géode, the Philharmonie de Paris, the Zénith. Free outdoor cinema in summer.

⏱ 120 min

Farewell dinner in Paris at a bistrot in the 11th or 10th. Le Chateaubriand on Rue Parmentier remains one of the best tables in the city at €75 (book a month ahead). Budget option: Bouillon Pigalle, with dishes from €4 to €15.

1

Leave early on RER C from Saint-Michel, Musée d'Orsay, Invalides, or Champ-de-Mars. Direction: Versailles Château Rive Gauche, 40 minutes, €8 return. Book your entry slot online — the 9am–10am windows are the least crowded.

⏱ 40 min
2

The Palace of Versailles has 2,300 rooms across 63,000 m². Louis XIV moved the court here in 1682, after 21 years of construction. Walk through the King's Grand Apartments (seven drawing rooms dedicated to the planets, ceilings by Le Brun) to the Hall of Mirrors. Seventy-three metres long, 357 mirrors opposite 17 arched windows, completed in 1684. The Treaty of Versailles was signed here on 28 June 1919.

⏱ 150 min
3

The Queen's Apartments are as impressive as the King's. Marie-Antoinette's restored four-poster bed, the Salon des Nobles, the Antichambre du Grand Couvert where the royal family ate in public. Don't miss the Royal Chapel completed in 1710, with Corinthian columns on two levels and a vaulted ceiling painted by Antoine Coypel.

⏱ 75 min
4

The gardens, designed by André Le Nôtre: 800 hectares in total, 93 of which are formal French gardens. Geometric parterres, hidden groves, 400 sculptures, and up to 1,400 fountains in Louis XIV's time (around 670 today, including 55 main basins). Tuesdays and weekends from April to October: the Grandes Eaux Musicales, fountains choreographed to music by Lully and Rameau. €10–12 supplement.

⏱ 90 min
5

Walk to the Grand Trianon (20 minutes on foot) or take the small train (€8). A pink marble palace built by Mansart in 1687. The Petit Trianon was given by Louis XVI to Marie-Antoinette in 1774. Behind it, the Queen's Hamlet: a fake Norman village built in 1783 with a farm, a mill, and a dairy — Marie-Antoinette's pastoral retreat.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Lunch at La Flottille in the gardens (on the edge of the Grand Canal, €30–40). Alternative: Angelina inside the palace, or head out to the Place du Marché Notre-Dame.

⏱ 75 min

Back in Paris by late afternoon. Dinner in an unexplored neighbourhood — the 9th arrondissement (South Pigalle) has been one of the city's top dining destinations for the past decade: Hôtel Amour, Buvette, Le Pantruche. €40–60.

1

TER train from Paris Montparnasse to Chartres, a direct 1h–1h15, around €21 full fare (from €7 with the Rémi card). Aim to arrive around 9:30–10am to see the cathedral in the morning light — its two mismatched spires dominate the Beauce plain and are visible from 30 kilometres away.

⏱ 70 min
2

The Notre-Dame Cathedral of Chartres, UNESCO-listed since 1979, was built almost entirely between 1194 and 1220 — a remarkable pace. Its 176 stained-glass windows from the 12th and 13th centuries cover 2,600 m², the majority original. The famous Chartres blue came from a manufacturing secret that has since been lost.

⏱ 90 min
3

In the middle of the nave, the Labyrinth — 13 metres in diameter, 261.5 metres of path, inlaid in the floor since 1205. Pilgrims once walked it on their knees. Visible on one Friday per month outside liturgical periods.

⏱ 20 min
4

Descend into the crypt, the third largest in Europe — 220 metres long. It houses the Veil of Our Lady, a relic gifted by Charles the Bald in 876. The Well of the Strong Saints dates from the Gallo-Roman era. Guided visit only, €3.

⏱ 45 min
5

Head down to the lower town via Rue des Écuyers. The banks of the Eure: half-timbered houses, stone bridges, medieval washhouses, converted mills. The Maison Picassiette is worth the detour — a house covered entirely in mosaic by a visionary worker between 1938 and 1962. €6.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Lunch in the old town — Le Saint-Hilaire on Rue du Pont-Saint-Hilaire at €25–35. Alternative: La Picoterie on Rue du Bourg, lunch menu at €16. Local specialities: pâté de Chartres, cochelins, mentchikoff.

⏱ 75 min

April to January: Chartres en Lumières after dark — 22 sites lit up with video mapping every evening until 1am. Completely free. Otherwise, catch the TER back to Paris (last train around 10pm).

1

Direct TER from Paris Austerlitz to Blois-Chambord, 1h25 to 1h40, €16–35 depending on how far ahead you book (OUIGO Train Classique from €16, full-fare TER/Intercités around €34). Trains every 1–2 hours. Blois town centre is a 10-minute walk from the station. Drop your bags near the Place Louis XII — you'll be right in the thick of things. Head straight for the royal château that towers over the town.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Royal Château of Blois is a rare thing: four châteaux in one, built around a single courtyard across different eras. Start with the Louis XII wing (1498–1501) in red brick and stone, flamboyant Gothic with Italian touches. Inside, the Fine Arts Museum displays works by Rubens, Ingres, and Boucher. €15, allow 2 hours.

⏱ 90 min
3

The François I wing (1515–1524) is the showpiece. Its monumental open spiral staircase, placed on the façade like a stage set, is a pure Renaissance invention. Look for crowned salamanders everywhere — the emblem of François I. It was at Blois that he launched his great architectural ambitions before Chambord.

⏱ 75 min
4

The Salle des États Généraux — the oldest part of the château, 13th century. A vast Gothic civic hall measuring 30 × 18 metres, one of the largest surviving in France. The Estates General met here in 1576 and 1588. On 23 December 1588, Henri III had the Duke of Guise assassinated in this very room for threatening his throne.

⏱ 45 min
5

Across the square, the Maison de la Magie Robert-Houdin. Every half hour, six giant mechanical dragons emerge from the windows and breathe steam. Jean-Eugène Robert-Houdin (1805–1871), a native of Blois, is considered the father of modern magic — Houdini took his stage name as a tribute. €12.

⏱ 60 min
6

Walk down towards the Loire. The Saint-Louis Cathedral, rebuilt after a hurricane in 1678, preserves a 10th-century Carolingian crypt. Behind it, the Jardins de l'Évêché — a formal terraced garden with a sweeping view over the town and the river. The Denis-Papin staircase, 120 steps, leads down to the square and its statue.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Dinner in the old town. L'Orangerie du Château, next to the monument, serves gastro cuisine at €45–70 in an 18th-century setting. More affordable: Le Castelet (Rue des Trois Marchands, €18–25). Try rillettes de Tours, Selles-sur-Cher AOP goat's cheese, and tarte Tatin — invented at Lamotte-Beuvron, 45 km away, in 1889.

⏱ 120 min

April to September: son et lumière show at the Royal Château after dark. 45 minutes of projections across the four façades of the inner courtyard — 1,500 projectors, original score. Off-season: an evening stroll along the illuminated Loire embankment and over the Pont Jacques-Gabriel (1724, listed).

1

Rémi bus (line 2) from Blois-Chambord station to Chambord in about 35 minutes, €2 per journey (Rémi fare). Arrive at opening time, 9am. Chambord reveals itself along a 4.5 km approach: a cascade of turrets, chimneys, and pinnacles. 426 rooms, 77 staircases, 282 fireplaces. UNESCO World Heritage Site. €16.

⏱ 45 min
2

François I began Chambord in 1519. The largest Loire château was originally a hunting lodge — he spent only 72 nights here in his entire life. The central double-helix staircase is attributed to Leonardo da Vinci, who died just a few months before construction began. Two interlocking spirals: two people can go up and down simultaneously without ever crossing paths.

⏱ 90 min
3

Climb to the rooftop terraces. The forest of 800 sculpted pinnacles evokes the skyline of an oriental city — historians have identified Leonardo's Italian influence throughout. The Greek cross floorplan was inspired by St Peter's Basilica in Rome. Views over the 5,440-hectare enclosed estate.

⏱ 60 min
4

Back to Blois around midday. Quick lunch, then TER to Chenonceaux — about 1h20 total with a change at St-Pierre-des-Corps (the TGV hub for the Tours area), €14–29. Chenonceaux station is 400m from the château, five minutes on foot.

⏱ 180 min

The Pont du Gard: 275 metres long, 48 metres tall (the highest Roman aqueduct bridge ever built), three tiers of arches in local limestone, some blocks weighing 6 tonnes, all assembled without mortar. Eight UNESCO-listed monuments in the Arles Roman and Romanesque Monuments ensemble, listed since 1981.

The Arles Amphitheatre, built around 90 AD under Domitian — 25,000 spectators, one of the largest in Gaul (136 × 107 m). The Ancient Theatre of Arles, built around 25 BC under Augustus, with 10,000 seats. The Vieux-Port, beating heart of the city for 2,600 years. Greek settlers from Phocaea landed here around 600 BC and founded Massalia. The Basilica of Saint-Sernin, the largest Romanesque church still standing in Europe. Built 1080–1220 for pilgrims on the Way of Saint James, UNESCO listed. Couvent des Jacobins (13th century, work begun in 1230). Place de la Bourse, an 18th-century jewel. Designed 1730–1755 by Jacques Gabriel then Ange-Jacques Gabriel — France's first open square in urban planning history, a revolution compared with the closed royal squares of the past. Three identical curved façades, Fountain of the Three Graces (1869). Saint-André Cathedral, begun in the 11th century, enlarged through the 15th — 124 metres long, 41 metres to the vault. Eleanor of Aquitaine, duchess of the entire south-west, married the future Louis VII here in 1137, a foundational union that brought the crown of France closer to the Duchy of Aquitaine. The 13th-century Royal Portal. Tour Pey-Berland alongside: 66 metres, 231 steps, €6. A detached bell tower from 1440. Panoramic views over the Bordeaux rooftops.

The Grand Théâtre on Place de la Comédie. Victor Louis, 1773–1780 — one of Europe's most beautiful theatres. Twelve monumental Corinthian columns, twelve statues of the Muses and goddesses. Hotel near the Grand'Place or in Vieux Lille. Lille is the capital of the Hauts-de-France region, 230,000 inhabitants, right on the Belgian border — warm, carnival-spirited Northern atmosphere.

2

The Grand'Place (officially Place du Général-de-Gaulle, but nobody calls it that). The Colonne de la Déesse (1845), commemorating Lille's successful resistance to the Austrian siege of 1792. Flemish façades from the 17th and 18th centuries in red brick and white stone with scroll gables. La Voix du Nord (the regional newspaper) occupying a 1936 Art Deco building. Christmas market in December — among the finest in France.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Vieille Bourse, an architectural gem. Built 1652–1653 during the Spanish era (Lille was Spanish until 1667, when Louis XIV annexed it). A masterpiece of Flemish architecture — 24 houses arranged as a quadrilateral around an inner courtyard. A sculpted façade: caryatids, garlands, grotesque masks, coats of arms. Second-hand booksellers fill the courtyard every afternoon (except Mondays). Chess and draughts players. Listed monument. Free.

⏱ 45 min
4

Vieux Lille — the quarter that escaped Haussmannian planning, entirely pedestrianised. Cobbled lanes, brick-and-stone façades, 17th-century Flemish mansions. Rue de la Monnaie, Rue Esquermoise, Rue Basse. Designer boutiques, concept stores, chocolate shops (Meert, since 1761 — a Lille institution, serving a waffle filled with Madagascar vanilla favoured by De Gaulle). Notre-Dame-de-la-Treille Cathedral with its contemporary translucent marble façade (2000).

⏱ 90 min
5

The Opéra de Lille (1913, monumental neo-classical) and the Chamber of Commerce with its 76-metre belfry flank Place du Théâtre. The Beffroi de l'Hôtel de Ville (1929–1932): France's tallest belfry at 104 metres, UNESCO since 2005 as part of the Belfries of Belgium and France. Lift then 100 steps (€7) for a 360° panorama over Lille, the Flemish plain and — on a clear day — Belgium. The legendary giant figures Lydéric and Phinaert carved into the belfry.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Dinner. Estaminet Chez la Vieille (Rue de Gand): carbonade flamande (beef stewed in beer), waterzooi, potjevleesch (a 3-to-5-meat terrine in aspic), moules-frites in season. La Chicorée (26 Rue de Béthune), open 24 hours, cult address. €25–40. Local beers: Goudale, Jenlain amber, Ch'ti blonde. Meert waffle for dessert.

⏱ 120 min

Rue Royale, Rue de la Barre, Place Louise-de-Bettignies for bars and cocktails. Le Barbue d'Anvers, an estaminet and live-music bar on Rue Saint-Étienne. Festive: Rue Solférino and Rue Masséna for students — Lille has 120,000 of them. First weekend of September: the Braderie de Lille, Europe's largest flea market since 1127, two million visitors, 10,000 sellers spread across 100 km of pavements.

Lille

★★★★☆

1

Palais des Beaux-Arts, Place de la République. The second-largest museum in France after the Louvre, inaugurated in 1892. A monumental neo-Renaissance building. Five centuries of collections: Donatello, Raphael, Rubens (Descent from the Cross), Van Dyck, Goya, David, Courbet, Monet, Renoir, Degas, Toulouse-Lautrec, Picasso. Basement: relief maps — 15 military scale models of French fortified towns commissioned by Louis XIV in the 17th century to plan sieges. €7, closed Tuesdays. Allow 2 hours.

⏱ 150 min
2

Sunday morning (or Wednesday/Friday/Saturday): the Wazemmes market — Lille's largest and most popular, an emblem of the cosmopolitan city. Covered 1869 market hall plus outdoor stalls. Three hundred vendors, produce from around the world. Music, florists, cheesemongers, rotisserie, Maghrebi, Italian and Portuguese specialities. Roast chicken with chips to take away, €9, a local institution. 7am–2pm. Metro Gambetta.

⏱ 90 min
3

Afternoon in Roubaix for La Piscine — one of the most memorable visits in France. Art and industry museum installed in a 1932 Art Deco municipal swimming pool, converted into a museum in 2001. The main pool with its original mosaics, zenithal lighting and a sunset stained-glass window serves as the main gallery — sculptures facing one another in the still, reflecting water. Paintings, sculptures and textiles (Roubaix was France's textile capital). Metro Gare Jean-Lebas then 10 minutes. €11, closed Mondays.

⏱ 120 min
4

The Vauban Citadel, a masterpiece of the great military architect. Built 1667–1670 after Louis XIV's annexation of Lille. Known as the Queen of Citadels for its perfect pentagonal shape with five bastions, it is still operational as a military barracks. The surrounding 70-hectare park is free and popular with Lille joggers. The Lille Zoo is inside (free, 70 species).

⏱ 60 min
5

The birthplace of Charles de Gaulle (9 Rue Princesse, Vieux Lille) — a museum in the house where the General was born on 22 November 1890. A late-19th-century bourgeois home, restored and furnished as it was. A biographical trail, 1,500 personal objects. €7, closed Mondays and Tuesdays. Worth visiting even for non-Gaullists, for the atmosphere of a Flemish Catholic bourgeois childhood.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Last dinner of the tour. Bloempot by Florent Ladeyn (Rue des Bouchers): a bistronomic table that is resolutely locavore, a single set menu at €48, 100% regional produce. Or T-Rex Café (Rue Saint-André) for a Northern burger and craft beer. Welsh rarebit is unmissable (bread, melted Cheddar, beer, fried egg) — caloric but iconic. Genièvre as digestif (a Flemish juniper berry spirit, 40%).

⏱ 120 min

Last night of the grand tour. A drink in a rooftop bar or a signature cocktail. Le Comptoir Volant (Rue Esquermoise), creative cocktails in a cosy room. Open Gallery (Place du Lion d'Or) for something trendier. Or go back to the hotel and get ready for tomorrow's return to Paris — 1 hour on the TGV, arriving at Gare du Nord in the morning.

2

Saint-Germain-des-Prés, which you may not have explored at the start of the trip. The Church of Saint-Germain-des-Prés, the oldest in Paris (6th century, rebuilt in the 11th), where Descartes is buried. Café de Flore and Les Deux Magots (Boulevard Saint-Germain) — the bastions of existentialism where Sartre, Beauvoir and Camus held court. Coffee €6–8, but the atmosphere is unchanged. Rue Jacob, Rue de Buci, Rue de Seine: galleries, boutiques, covered market.

⏱ 90 min
3

Alternative: Place Vendôme and Rue Saint-Honoré for the Paris luxury experience. The Vendôme Column (1810) erected by Napoleon from the bronze of cannon captured at Austerlitz, a copy of Trajan's Column in Rome. Classical façades by Mansart (1702). The Hôtel Ritz at no. 15 (Coco Chanel lived here for 37 years; Proust and Hemingway were regulars). Boutiques of Chanel, Dior, Cartier, Van Cleef & Arpels. Place de la Madeleine with the neo-classical Madeleine church (1842) and the luxury food shops (Fauchon, Hédiard, Maille).

⏱ 75 min
4

A final Parisian lunch to match the occasion. Le Grand Véfour (Palais-Royal, an institution since 1784, frequented by Napoleon, Victor Hugo and Colette; chef Guy Martin — the setting is a listed monument and still sumptuous, €75–135 at lunch). Bouillon Chartier (Rue du Faubourg-Montmartre, a popular institution since 1896, dishes from €5 to €15). Or Clamato (Rue de Charonne, oyster bar and small plates, €35–55). Or the simplest and most genuine: a baguette-tradition, Paris ham, a wedge of cheese and a glass of wine on a bench in the Tuileries.
