Guatemala City

★★★☆☆
1

Let's be honest: Guatemala City won't make you dream, but it's the country's unavoidable gateway. La Aurora Airport drops travelers at 1500 m elevation into a sprawling capital where most visitors spend just one night. If you arrive early, the Popol Vuh Museum in Zona 10 displays one of the country's finest collections of pre-Columbian Maya art. Book your Antigua transfer in advance—the route covers 45 km but takes a solid hour.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Take advantage of your arrival to exchange money at the airport (quetzals, decent rate at the official counter) and grab a local SIM card from Tigo or Claro. The terminal ATMs work with international cards. Don't linger downtown after dark—certain neighborhoods have a rough reputation.

⏱ 60 min
$$$★9.2Casa Carmel Bed & Breakfast
🚗Drive to next stop: 45 km · 1h10

Antigua Guatemala

★★★★★
1

The scent of roasted coffee hangs over Antigua from dawn, and that's a good thing: the town cultivates some of the country's finest beans on the neighboring slopes. Founded in 1543 as Santiago de los Caballeros, the former capital was partly destroyed by the 1773 earthquake, which paradoxically froze its baroque ruins in time. Stroll from Plaza Mayor in the morning before the sun blazes on the cobblestones.

⏱ 120 min
2

Founded in 1543, Antigua lost its capital status after the 1773 earthquake—residents fled to present-day Guatemala City. The Palace of the Captains General and the ruins of Santo Domingo Convent bear witness to what it once was. The convent now houses a museum and a five-star hotel; paid access to the historic section but worth every quetzal.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Santa Catalina Arch is Antigua's obligatory postcard shot—a bright yellow baroque passage with Agua volcano as backdrop. Go before 8am for the crowd-free photo, or late afternoon for golden light. The street leading to the arch, Calle del Arco, is also the town's most photogenic.

⏱ 30 min
4

The Capuchin Convent deserves a detour for its circular cloister, unique in Guatemala, built in 1736. Admission is modest, around 40 quetzals. The petal-shaped monastic cells and the torre del retiro offer a shortcut to understanding cloistered life in colonial times.

⏱ 60 min
5

If you climb Cerro de la Cruz at sunrise, you'll embrace all of Antigua—its red rooftops and Agua volcano towering at 3760 meters. The paved trail takes 15 to 20 minutes from the center. Go accompanied—incidents have been reported on the dawn climb; the tourist police sometimes offer free escorts.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Guatemalans call Pacaya 'the easy volcano,' but easy is relative: 2552 meters, two hours climbing on solidified lava flows. It's one of the country's most active volcanoes—you'll see fumaroles and recent flows. For the full experience, Acatenango (3976 m) demands two days, high-altitude camping, and views of neighboring Fuego's eruptions.

⏱ 360 min

Take a cooking class to learn pepián, the spiced stew considered the national dish. Several workshops also teach tamales and rellenitos made from plantain. La Tortilla cooking school is well-regarded and fits into two to three hours.

⏱ 180 min

Roasted beans crack under your fingers at Finca Filadelphia, a plantation accessible in fifteen minutes from downtown. The tour explains everything from planting to cupping. Café Azotea also offers a free coffee museum in an old hacienda.

⏱ 120 min
$$$★9.4San Rafael Hotel
🚗Drive to next stop: 90 km · 3h

Panajachel

★★★★☆
1

Touristy, yes, but Panajachel remains the best entry point to Lake Atitlán, and all shuttles from Antigua arrive here. The town serves as a logistics hub: agencies, restaurants, boat dock for lanchas to other villages. Book everything upon arrival—boats leave when they're full.

⏱ 60 min
2

Calle Santander pulses from morning to night—it's Pana's tourist backbone. Huipil textile shops, craft vendors, bohemian cafés, the works. Bargain without guilt—it's the local sport—but know that fifty quetzals make a real difference for vendors.

⏱ 90 min
3

Formed 84,000 years ago, Lake Atitlán is a caldera ringed by San Pedro, Tolimán, and Atitlán volcanoes. It's Central America's deepest lake, and Aldous Huxley wrote in 1934's Beyond the Mexique Bay that it surpassed Lake Como in beauty. Swimming is possible, but avoid areas near large villages due to periodic cyanobacteria.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Climb to Mirador de Panajachel or Cerro de la Cruz for panoramic views of all three volcanoes, especially late afternoon when light turns the lake purple. The trail is short but steep—thirty minutes uphill.

⏱ 90 min

The fresh morning air smells like rain even in dry season: Atitlán Nature Reserve, ten minutes from downtown, offers suspension bridges, a butterfly greenhouse, and cloud forest trails. Count on three hours for the full loop.

⏱ 180 min
$$$★9.5Villa de las aves with Lake Access
🚗Drive to next stop: 12 km · 30min

San Juan La Laguna

★★★☆☆
1

San Juan La Laguna's Tz'utujil weaving cooperatives are run by women who still use natural dyes: indigo, cochineal, tree bark. The workshops explain the entire process, from thread to finished huipil. The demonstration takes an hour and directly supports the community.

⏱ 90 min
2

San Juan La Laguna's alleys explode with murals—works by local naïve painters who exhibit on the street itself. It's the lake's most artistic village, peaceful, without Pana's chaos or San Pedro's backpacker vibe. An hour's stroll covers the whole place.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Visit the medicinal plant garden run by a local cooperative that makes soaps, shampoos, and candles from garden plants. You can combine it with a crafting workshop. Free admission, donations appreciated.

⏱ 60 min

Coffee cultivated on San Pedro volcano's slopes is a source of Tz'utujil pride. Several fincas offer the complete tour, from bean to cup, with tasting. Count on two hours and 100 to 150 quetzals depending on the finca.

⏱ 120 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 8 km · 25min

Santiago Atitlan

★★★☆☆
1

Santiago Atitlán residents move Maximón every year—this syncretic Maya deity, a dressed and hatted wooden statue, changes houses each May. Faithful ensure he always has a lit cigarette in his mouth and a full glass of alcohol. Ask your lancha captain or a tuk-tuk driver where he's staying this year—they know. Bring a few quetzals as an offering.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Santiago Apóstol church bears the marks of heavy events: the 1981 assassination of priest Stanley Rother and the massacre of fourteen villagers during the civil war. A plaque in the church commemorates these moments. It's also the lake's largest village and the Tz'utujil stronghold least oriented toward tourism.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Climb to Mirador del Valle above Santiago to see the entire lake open up and glimpse where it ends—a view impossible from other villages. Fifteen to twenty minutes on foot from downtown. The local market near the dock is worth a detour for its typical embroidery worn by women.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 65 km · 2h

Chichicastenango

★★★☆☆
1

Go to Chichicastenango on Sunday, not Thursday—the market is even denser, and vendors come from across the Altiplano. It's one of Central America's largest indigenous markets, though don't claim it's the oldest or largest without nuance: local sources make it a point of national pride—that's enough. Guard your bag and bargain with a cool head.

⏱ 180 min
2

Built in 1540, Santo Tomás church rises on the foundations of an ancient Maya temple. The eighteen steps leading to its entrance are still considered sacred by the K'iche', and shamans burn incense and copal there daily. This is where the Popol Vuh manuscript, sacred Maya book, was rediscovered in the 18th century. Don't photograph inside, and cover shoulders and knees.

⏱ 60 min
3

Chichicastenango's cemetery explodes with color: tombstones painted yellow, turquoise, magenta, Maya and Catholic rituals intertwined. Five minutes on foot from the market, the visit is discreet and respectful, especially on days of family offerings.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Climb Santo Tomás's steps to see the market from above—the labyrinthine sprawl of stalls becomes legible from up here. The Calvario chapel on the opposite side of the plaza offers the same kind of viewpoint but with fewer crowds.

⏱ 30 min

If you really want to bargain, divide the announced price by two and work your way up slowly. Embroidered huipiles and traditional dance masks are the most typical purchases. Avoid religious feast days like December 7 (Quema del Diablo), when the town closes for business.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 280 km · 1h30

Flores

★★★★☆
1

Flores's pink, yellow, and blue houses reflect in Lake Petén Itzá—the island barely covers 13 hectares and can be crossed on foot in twenty minutes. Connected to the mainland by a causeway, it serves as base for Tikal and Yaxha. The atmosphere shifts quickly: Caribbean, slow, hot.

⏱ 90 min
2

Before becoming Flores, the island was called Noj Petén, capital of the last independent Maya kingdom—the Itzá resisted the Spanish until 1697, more than 200 years after Columbus's arrival. The conquistadors razed the city, and the modern town rebuilt on its ruins in the 18th century.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Iglesia Nuestra Señora de los Remedios crowns the island's high point at 127 meters elevation. The colonial church was built with stones from a dismantled Maya pyramid. Climb up for panoramic views of the lake and Petén hills at sunset.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Cross by lancha to San Miguel on the north shore (10 quetzals), then climb to Mirador del Rey Canek—fifteen minutes' walk. The wooden platform offers the best photo of the island. Bring repellent—mosquitoes are plentiful.

⏱ 120 min

Sunset from a Flores rooftop is an institution. Several bars and hotels around the island's perimeter have west-facing terraces. A Guatemalan Gallo beer, the lake's shifting colors—it's the relaxation moment before Tikal's big day.

⏱ 60 min
$$$★9.7Las Lagunas Boutique Hotel
🚗Drive to next stop: 65 km · 1h20

Tikal

★★★★★
1

Temple IV rises 65 meters, Tikal's tallest pyramid (La Danta at El Mirador, even taller, demands five days of jungle trekking to reach). Built around 741 under the reign of Yik'in Chan K'awiil, it marked the western boundary of the ceremonial center. The wooden staircase leads to the summit where the canopy opens endlessly, pierced by other temple peaks. Sunrise bookings possible, 5:30am on site, sometimes foggy conditions.

⏱ 120 min
2

Howler monkeys wake Tikal's jungle before dawn—their deep roar carries for several kilometers. The national park covers 576 km² and shelters jaguars, coatis, toucans, over 400 bird species. You'll walk fifteen kilometers in the day—bring water and closed-toe shoes.

⏱ 180 min
3

Tikal's Gran Plaza aligns two pyramids face to face: Temple I (45 meters, Temple of the Great Jaguar, 682-734) and Temple II (38 meters). The North Acropolis and Central Acropolis border them—you can climb the latter for plunging views. The nobles' residential palace contains about a hundred rooms.

⏱ 120 min
4

El Mundo Perdido is worth the detour—Tikal's oldest complex, built in the Preclassic era and abandoned around 900 AD. The ceremonial pyramid rises 30 meters—you can climb it for 360° views far less crowded than the Gran Plaza.

⏱ 90 min
5

Local guides tell how George Lucas filmed the Rebel base scene on Yavin 4 here for Star Wars Episode IV in 1977. The park sometimes plays the film's music around the Gran Plaza—it's kitschy but makes you smile. The city's peak was between 600 and 800 AD, up to 60,000 inhabitants in the urban core.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

If you can handle a 4am wake-up, the 'sunrise' tour leaves before official opening and places visitors atop Temple IV for sunrise. Mist, howlers, first light on the pyramids—it's the trip's most memorable experience. Count on 250 to 350 quetzals with guide.

⏱ 240 min

Spot coatis, keel-billed toucans, and spider monkeys on trails between temple groups. Morning and late afternoon are best. A naturalist guide makes all the difference—otherwise you'll miss half the animals.

⏱ 180 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 75 km · 1h35

Yaxha

★★★★☆
1

Yaxha has neither Tikal's size nor its crowds, and that's exactly what makes it worthwhile. One of Petén's major sites, it counts about 500 structures including 9 temple-pyramids, all on the lagoon's edge. Do it before Tikal so you won't be disappointed, or do it for the sunset.

⏱ 120 min
2

Temple 216 rises 37 meters above the canopy and offers the signature view: Yaxha lagoon, the Topoxté site on its islet, and jungle stretching to the horizon. The wooden staircase replaces the stone stairway that became impassable. Yaxha means 'green water' in Maya, a name unchanged since the Classic era.

⏱ 90 min
3

Iguanas dart through the stones and howler monkeys settle in trees above the stelae. The minor astronomical complex, erected between 700 and 800 AD, aligns stelae and a four-step pyramid with the solstices. Stela 31 and its carved birds on Plaza F.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Stay until sunset atop Temple 216—it's the site's signature experience. Silence is broken only by howlers and toucans returning for the night. Bring water, headlamp for the descent, and strong repellent: mosquitoes emerge at 5pm.

⏱ 120 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 110 km · 3h

San Ignacio

★★★☆☆
1

The Hawkesworth Bridge, Belize's only suspension bridge, connects San Ignacio to its twin town Santa Elena over the Macal River. It's the classic arrival photo. Main street Burns Avenue concentrates cafés, tour agencies, and the town's lively center.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Saturday market smells of papaya, habanero pepper, and Marie Sharp's sauce being sampled at stalls. Producers come from across Cayo District: Mennonites, Maya, Creole. This is where you find crafts, fresh fruit, and the trip's best snacks.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Belize Iguana Project deserves a stop—a conservation program for endangered green iguanas (hunted for their meat). At San Ignacio Resort Hotel, you feed them, hold them, learn their whole life cycle. 18 Belize dollars, about an hour.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Paddle the Macal River by canoe to spot iguanas, herons, and sometimes agoutis. The ride lasts two to three hours from downtown. Barton Creek Cave, an hour's drive away, offers a canoe variant in a Maya underground river.

⏱ 180 min

Belizeans speak of their hot sauce 'Marie Sharp' like a religion. The factory makes sixteen different flavors, from mild to 'Beware.' Free tour with tasting at the factory near Dangriga, or direct purchase at all market stalls.

⏱ 60 min

If you like thrills, cave tubing at Nohoch Che'en (Caves Branch) involves drifting on an inner tube through an underground river lit by headlamp—45 minutes of descent. Can be combined with a zipline above the jungle.

⏱ 240 min

Try rice and beans with stew chicken—Belize's national dish, served everywhere with potato salad and plantain. Ko-Ox Han Nah on Burns Avenue is the safe bet. Also ask for 'one barrel rum,' the best local rum by popular vote.

⏱ 90 min
$$$★9.4Hidden Valley Wilderness Lodge
🚗Drive to next stop: 3 km · 10min

Cahal Pech

★★★★☆
1

Cahal Pech overlooks San Ignacio from its hilltop—modest-sized but well-preserved Maya ruins: 34 structures, two ball courts, several residential temples. The site was the elite residence of a ruling Maya family occupied from the Preclassic (1200 BC) to Late Classic. Admission costs about 5 US dollars and the visit takes an hour. Small museum at the entrance for context before the walk.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Go early morning, 7-8am—you'll have the site almost to yourself, agoutis and toucans are active in the trees. Ten minutes on foot from downtown San Ignacio, steep climb at the end. Good warm-up before the more impressive Xunantunich in the afternoon.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 10 km · 20min

Xunantunich

★★★★☆
1

The hand-crank ferry creaks across the Mopan River, operated by a ferryman who turns the wheel by arm strength—it's the only access to Xunantunich site. The crossing takes one minute, free of charge, tip welcome. Four cars maximum on board. The approach via this artisanal crossing is half the experience.

⏱ 30 min
2

El Castillo climbs to 40 meters, Belize's second tallest structure after Caracol. Built from 800 AD onward, it bears an elaborate frieze on its east façade depicting the sun god, moon, and Venus—the original is protected by a fiberglass replica. The west frieze was uncovered in 2016.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Climb to El Castillo's summit—the interior staircase leads to two vaulted chambers and the top, panoramic views over the Mopan valley into Guatemala (border 1 km west). Go early morning—the site opens at 8am—you'll avoid heat and the 10am tourist peak.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 30 km · 1h

Actun Tunichil Muknal

★★★★★
1

ATM Cave is Belize's most memorable experience according to nearly every traveler, and National Geographic ranked it #1 worldwide in its top sacred caves. Actun Tunichil Muknal means 'cave of the stone sepulcher' in Maya. Fourteen skeletons rest in the main chamber, including the Crystal Maiden, calcified and sparkling for over a thousand years. No photos allowed inside since 2012.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

The tour starts with 45 minutes walking through jungle, three river crossings on foot, then a swim to enter the cave through an hourglass mouth partially submerged. Three hours inside between swimming, light climbing, and walking in wet socks (shoes stay at the artifact chamber entrance). Helmet and headlamp provided. Mandatory guide, departure from San Ignacio.

⏱ 480 min

If you're claustrophobic or not very athletic, tell your guide beforehand: some passages are tight and several hours of effort are required. Guides are used to claustrophobes—it usually works out. Avoid with heart or back problems. Not for young children (12 years minimum).

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 130 km · 2h30

Hopkins

★★☆☆☆
1

The Garifuna of Hopkins descend from a singular history: a blend of shipwrecked African slaves and Caribbean Arawaks, exiled by the British in 1797 from Saint Vincent to Honduras, then migrated to Belize. Hopkins is considered the country's Garifuna cultural capital. Language, songs, percussion, hudut—everything is alive, not patrimonialized.

⏱ 60 min
2

Three kilometers of golden sand and wooden fishing boats—that's Hopkins beachside. No giant resort, small oceanfront hotels, thatched-roof bars, wooden piers reaching into turquoise water. The village was named 'Friendliest Village in Belize' by Belize First magazine, and you feel it.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Lebeha Drumming Center has trained village children in Garifuna percussion for twenty years. Evening workshops are open to visitors: three or four drums, one voice, sometimes a dancer in a full skirt. Paranda and punta rhythms can be learned in an hour—poorly, but you learn them.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Learn hudut during a Garifuna cooking class: fish cooked in coconut milk, plantain pounded in a mortar, spicy cassava root sauce. Several village houses offer the workshop by reservation, meal included, around 50 USD.

⏱ 180 min

If you're there on November 19, you'll catch Garifuna Settlement Day: reenactment of the canoe arrival from Honduras, parades, drums all night. Book accommodation six months in advance—prices double and everything fills up.

⏱ 480 min

Snorkeling at South Water Caye Marine Reserve, about 12 miles offshore, is the big outing from Hopkins. Reef less crowded than Caye Caulker, crystal water, rays and turtles. Count on 120 to 150 USD for the day with lunch on an island. Ask for Happy Go Luckie Tours.

⏱ 480 min

Bike along the village from north (Baila) to south (False Sittee)—three kilometers of sandy track by the sea. Most hotels rent bikes for 5 USD half-day. It's the best way to see how Hopkins really lives, away from the hotel circuit.

⏱ 120 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 35 km · 1h

Cockscomb Basin

★★★★☆
1

Cockscomb Basin was created in 1986 as the world's first reserve dedicated to jaguar protection, at the initiative of biologist Alan Rabinowitz. The sanctuary covers about 400 km² on the eastern slopes of the Maya Mountains. It shelters the world's highest concentration of jaguars, as well as pumas, ocelots, margays, and jaguarundis—all five of Belize's felines.

⏱ 60 min
2

Over 200 jaguars live here, and you probably won't see a single one—they're nocturnal and shy. That's the honest warning to give before visiting. On the other hand, over 290 bird species, howler monkeys, coatis, and sometimes fresh jaguar tracks on muddy trails—that compensates.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Walk the Ben's Bluff Trail to the waterfall and its swimmable basin—4 km round trip with 170 meters elevation gain. The Wari Loop, shorter and flat, is ideal for morning birds. Bring closed-toe shoes, water, strong repellent: Cockscomb receives over 100 inches of rain per year—it's one of Belize's wettest corners.

⏱ 240 min

Tubing on South Stann Creek is worth it after the hike: rent an inner tube at the visitor center for 7.5 USD and float an hour with the current. At the sanctuary's campground or dorms, you can sleep on-site (10 to 80 USD depending on comfort) and attempt a guided night walk to hear jaguars.

⏱ 180 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 60 km · 40min

Caye Caulker

★★★☆☆
1

The Split, a gap created by Hurricane Hattie in 1961, cuts Caye Caulker in two and becomes the island's social hub. Bar, turquoise water, tree jumpers, kayakers—this is where you spend late afternoons. The island's north side, little developed, is reached by ferry from The Split.

⏱ 90 min
2

Caye Caulker's sandy roads are traveled on foot or by golf cart—no cars on the island. The local motto 'Go Slow' is painted everywhere. Three main streets, brightly colored wooden houses, thatched-roof restaurants where you eat grilled lobster in season (July to February).

⏱ 60 min
3

Hol Chan, created in 1987, protects 18 km² of coral reef, seagrass beds, and mangroves at the island's southern tip. It's Belize's oldest marine reserve, part of the world's second-largest barrier reef. Depths vary from 1.5 m to 9 m, ideal for snorkeling.

⏱ 60 min
4

The local motto 'Go Slow' isn't a tourist slogan—it's what residents tell everyone, especially speeding golf carts. Caye Caulker has deliberately stayed small, without big hotels, unlike neighboring Ambergris Caye. The unanimous verdict: you plan two days, you stay five.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Dive at Shark Ray Alley to swim with nurse sharks and southern stingrays, calm and accustomed to swimmers. The combined Hol Chan + Shark Ray Alley tour lasts about 5.5 hours, several stops, lunch included. Count on 70 to 100 USD depending on operator. Book with Ragga Sailing, Black Hawk, or Salt Life.

⏱ 330 min

If you're a certified diver, the Great Blue Hole is reached from Caye Caulker in about 2.5 hours by boat. Three dives in the day, including the descent to 40 meters in the sinkhole formed 10,000 years ago. Count on 280 to 350 USD, full day, 6am-5pm.

⏱ 660 min

Sunset at The Split is an institution. Lazy Lizard serves fluorescent rum punch, Raggamuffin sailboats organize sunset cruises for about 50 USD, two and a half hours. For quiet, go to the island's west shore, much less crowded.

⏱ 150 min
$$$★9.8Caye Caulker Boutique Guesthouse 1
🚗Drive to next stop: 35 km · 20min

Belize City

★★★☆☆
1

Belize City has little to hold travelers—it's essentially the transit hub to Philip Goldson International Airport. The city suffered from repeated hurricanes (1931, Hattie in 1961) and the capital was moved to Belmopan in 1970. A few hours suffice before catching your return flight or the water taxi to reach the islands.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Make a stop at the Museum of Belize, installed in a former colonial red-brick prison. Small well-organized collection: Maya artifacts, British colonial history, local fauna, old bills and stamps. An hour suffices. Admission about 10 USD for foreigners.

⏱ 60 min
$$$★9.2St. George's Caye Resort
