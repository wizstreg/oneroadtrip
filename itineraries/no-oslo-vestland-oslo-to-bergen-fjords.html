Oslo

★★★★☆
1

Follow the Akerselva from Grünerløkka down to the waterfalls and old mills - 9.8 km of trail threading through Oslo's industrial history. Back in 1856, the river's 20 cascades powered around twenty textile factories and flour mills. Walk it north to south: the view of the Opera House and fjord comes as a grand finale.

⏱ 120 min
2

The waterfront at Aker Brygge and Tjuvholmen shows you two faces of Oslo along the Oslofjord: former shipyards converted into restaurants and galleries on one side, the contemporary jetty at Tjuvholmen on the other. Walk out to the end of the pier for views across the fjord islands.

⏱ 60 min
3

Climb onto the marble roof of Oslo Opera House (Operahuset) - it's free, open all the time, and the view over the fjord is the best in the city. The building, designed by Snøhetta and opened in 2008, is clad in Carrara marble and white granite. It won the Mies van der Rohe Prize in 2009. Pack slip-resistant shoes in winter: the roof gets treacherous.

⏱ 45 min
4

Take a turn through the tropical glasshouses at Oslo's Botanical Garden (Botanisk hage), in Tøyen. The garden has been around since 1814 and holds one of Europe's largest collections of Nordic plants. Free admission to the outdoor park.

⏱ 60 min
5

The Barcode Project, in the Bjørvika district, lines up a series of towers with striped facades designed by different architecture firms between 2005 and 2016. View them from the waterfront promenade - the barcode effect reads more clearly from a distance.

⏱ 30 min
6

Climb up to the Damstredet and Telthusbakken neighbourhood, a ten-minute walk from the centre: you'll find painted wooden houses from the 18th and 19th centuries that survived Oslo's successive rebuildings. A striking contrast with the glass towers down by the water.

⏱ 45 min
7

Sagene, Oslo's former industrial heartland, is now one of the city's most sought-after neighbourhoods. The name comes from Old Norwegian 'sager', meaning sawmills - the first date back to the 16th century. Wander past red-brick facades and former warehouses now converted into workshops and cafés.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Photo walk through Sentrum: the colourful facades and heritage buildings of the historic centre are particularly photogenic late in the day when raking light cuts through the narrow streets. Head from City Hall toward the Parliament for an hour of urban wandering.

⏱ 60 min

Oslo

★★★★☆
1

The Norsk Folkemuseum at Bygdøy gathers over 150 historic buildings relocated from across Norway - it's one of Europe's largest open-air museums. The centrepiece is the Gol Stave Church, built around 1200 in the Hallingdal valley. In 1880, the parish of Gol wanted to demolish it to build a new one. The Society for the Preservation of Ancient Monuments bought it, and King Oscar II funded its transfer to Bygdøy in 1884. On one of the nave pillars, a runic inscription reads: 'Kiss me, for I struggle.' Allow 2 hours to tour the whole museum.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Viking Ship Museum (Vikingskipshuset) houses three longships from the 9th and 10th centuries, including the Oseberg, discovered in 1904 in a burial mound near Tønsberg. It's one of the best-preserved medieval wooden artefacts in the world - the find also included sledges, tapestries, and everyday objects. Note that the museum reopened after renovation in 2026 as the Museum of the Vikings.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Round-trip ferry ride from Aker Brygge to Bygdøy - about 10 minutes across the Oslofjord. The public ferry (line 91) runs May to September. It's the most pleasant way to reach the Bygdøy peninsula and its museums, with fine views of the fjord islands.

⏱ 30 min

Oslo

★★★★☆
1

The Holmenkollen Ski Museum is the world's oldest ski museum - it opened in 1923 and traces 4,000 years of skiing history, from Stone Age rock carvings to the gear from Fridtjof Nansen and Roald Amundsen's polar expeditions. The facade was completely renovated by Snøhetta in 2023. The building is carved into the mountain, beneath the ski jump.

⏱ 90 min
2

From the viewing platform atop Holmenkollbakken - a ski jump built in 1892 and rebuilt for the 1952 Oslo Olympics - you get the panoramic view of the city and Oslofjord that every guidebook describes. It's deserved. Ride the elevator, then 114 steps to the top. Allow 30 to 40 minutes to explore the summit. Avoid competition days if you want the terrace to yourself.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Holmenkollen hosts annual biathlon and ski jumping competitions on the national arena - dates vary according to the FIS and IBU calendars. Outside competition season, the cross-country ski trails of Nordmarka start just behind the jump. Reach Holmenkollen by metro (line 1, Holmenkollen station, 25 minutes from the centre).

⏱ 60 min

Flam

★★★★☆
1

Leaving Oslo on the E16, stop at Sundvollen for views over Tyrifjorden - one of Norway's largest lakes, nestled between conifer forests and hills. The road then follows the Hallingdal valley on the RV7, with its red wooden farmhouses scattered across the pastures.

⏱ 45 min
2

On the E16 between Lærdal and Aurland, you enter the Lærdal Tunnel - 24.5 km long, making it the world's longest road tunnel, opened in 2000. To break the monotony, engineers carved out three large caverns lit in blue and yellow at regular intervals. Don't speed: the limit is 80 km/h.

⏱ 40 min
3

Before entering the tunnel, stop on the scenic Aurlandsfjellet road (RV243) if conditions allow: the plunging views over Aurlandsfjord from 1,300 metres elevation are spectacular. Warning - this road closes in winter.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Driving route Oslo-Flåm via E16: allow 4 to 5 hours total, including breaks. The road is passable year-round but winter conditions may apply from November to April. Check vegvesen.no for road conditions before departure.

Flam

★★★★☆
1

The Flåmsbana Museum, free admission at the foot of the station, retraces the 20 years of construction of the Flåm Railway (1923-1940). The 20 km line descends 866 metres between Myrdal and Flåm, with a maximum gradient of 5.5% - one of the steepest in the world for standard gauge. 18 of the 20 tunnels were carved by hand by teams of 120 to 220 workers.

⏱ 60 min
2

Otternes Bygdetun is a hamlet of historic farms perched on a promontory above Aurlandsfjord, at 300 metres elevation. Access on foot from Flåm in 45 minutes uphill (marked trail). The wooden houses, inhabited until the 1980s, have kept their original appearance. The elevated view of the fjord from the hamlet meadows is worth the effort.

⏱ 120 min
3

Aurlandsvangen, the main town of Aurland municipality 7 km from Flåm, has a wooden church from the 1600s and a quiet waterfront on Aurlandsfjord. Stop by late afternoon to watch the evening light on the calm fjord waters.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Hike or photo walk along the Flåm harbour and the shore of Aurlandsfjord. The village is tiny - a few hundred inhabitants - but the setting between fjord and towering mountains is arresting. In high season, dozens of cruise ships anchor in the bay: arrive early morning for peace and quiet.

⏱ 90 min

1

From the electric boat, the walls of Nærøyfjord rise 1,800 metres - and the fjord is only 250 metres wide at its narrowest. A UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2005, it has remained untouched because no road was ever built along its shores: you'll pass farms accessible only by boat. 2

Gudvangen, at the terminus of Nærøyfjord, is a village of a few dozen inhabitants squeezed between vertiginous cliffs. Its name means 'field of the gods at the water's edge' in Old Norse. Stop to see Kjelfossen waterfall, which plunges more than 800 metres from the heights.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Silent cruise by electric boat on Nærøyfjord between Flåm and Gudvangen - 2 hours one way. The boat 'Future of the Fjords', fully electric since 2018, glides soundlessly between the fjord walls. The absence of combustion engine is striking: you hear only waterfalls and eagle cries. Several departures daily, year-round.

Bergen

★★★★★
1

On the E16 between Gudvangen and Voss, the road climbs Nærøydalen valley through a series of tight hairpin bends - 21 serpentine turns in a few kilometres. Walls drop sheer on either side. Take your time: viewpoints over the valley and waterfalls are plentiful and accessible directly from the road.

⏱ 60 min
2

Upon arrival in Bergen, stroll the Hanseatic wharf of Bryggen, a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1979. In 1360, the Hanseatic League established its trading office here and controlled the dried fish trade for nearly 400 years - Bergen is the only non-League city whose original medieval structure still stands within the urban fabric. The current appearance dates from the reconstruction after the 1702 fire; 62 buildings survive.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Drive from Flåm to Bergen via E16 and RV13: allow 3 hours, including photo stops in Nærøydalen valley. The section between Gudvangen and Voss is the most spectacular - don't rush through it.

⏱ 210 min

Bergen

★★★★★
1

Explore the alleys and interior warehouses of Bryggen - the facade along the wharf is what everyone photographs, but the back of the rows of houses is far more interesting. You'll find craft galleries, jewellers' and potters' workshops set in spaces that haven't changed since the 18th century. In high season, arrive before 9am to have the alleys to yourself.

⏱ 90 min
2

Fisketorget fish market is one of Norway's oldest open-air markets. You'll find fresh salmon, langoustines and halibut - plus prepared food stalls. The adjacent indoor market, open year-round, is the better option outside summer.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Nordnes peninsula, at the western tip of Bergen's centre, offers a pleasant walk along the shore with views over Byfjord. Nordnesparken at the point is where Bergen locals come to swim in summer - the water is cold but the setting is beautiful.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Take the Fløibanen funicular from the city centre up to Mount Fløyen (320 m) - in service since 1918, 850 metres of track, 6 minutes uphill. The panoramic view over Bergen, its seven mountains and Byfjord is the city's essential postcard shot. At the summit, marked trails lead into the troll forest - allow 30 to 45 minutes for the loop around Lake Skomakerdiket. Book online to skip the queue in summer.

Bergen

★★★★★
1

Fantoft Stave Church, in Paradis south of Bergen, is a reconstruction. The original, a 12th-century stavkirke moved from Fortun in 1883, burned in arson in 1992. The identical reconstruction was completed in 1997. Visit the exterior and grounds - the carved doors with intertwined dragon motifs are particularly detailed.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Milde Arboretum, in southern Bergen, is one of Norway's largest botanical gardens with 50 hectares of rhododendron, conifer and coastal plant collections. Trails wind through coves and mossy woods - this is quiet, non-touristy Bergen, 15 km from the centre.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Hike through the coastal woods and rocky coves of Milde - the trails around the arboretum extend several kilometres toward the fjord. The terrain is easy, ideal for a morning walk in Norwegian nature without leaving Bergen.

Ulvik

★★★★☆
1

Steinsdalsfossen waterfall, 2 km from Norheimsund, is one of Norway's most visited for a simple reason: you can walk behind it on foot, staying dry. A paved path follows the riverbank, climbs behind the 46-metre curtain of water and emerges cliffsside. Kaiser Wilhelm II came here every summer for 25 years - until 1914; in May-June, the snowmelt flow peaks.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Hardanger suspension bridge (Hardangerbrua), opened in 2013, spans Hardangerfjord for 1,380 metres - one of the world's longest suspension bridges. It replaced a ferry service that connected Bruravik and Brimnes for decades. Stop at one of the panoramic viewpoints to see it in its entirety from the shore.

⏱ 30 min
3

Ulvik, a village of a few hundred inhabitants at the head of Ulvikafjord, is nicknamed 'Norway's orchard'. The slopes around the fjord are covered with thousands of apple, pear and cherry trees - English Cistercian monks introduced fruit cultivation here in the 13th century. In May, the blossoming orchards transform the village into an Impressionist painting. Stroll along the waterfront and fjord shore.

⏱ 60 min
4

The Ulvik Fruit and Cider Route is the only one of its kind in Norway: three family farms welcome you for cider and apple juice tastings. Hardanger cider has obtained Protected Geographical Indication - Norway's equivalent of an AOC. In autumn, harvests are at their peak.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Drive Bergen-Ulvik via Norheimsund and Hardangerfjord: allow 2h30. The scenic Alvikvegen road along the fjord is worth the detour - the road hugs the water a few metres up with constant views over the green waters of Hardangerfjord and the snow-capped peaks in the background.

⏱ 150 min

Ulvik

★★★★☆
1

The trails above Ulvik offer plunging views over Ulvikafjord and Osafjord - two quiet arms of Hardangerfjord encircled by orchards and birch forests. Marked local trails start directly from the village. Pack hiking shoes: some sections are steep and can be slippery in wet weather.

⏱ 120 min
2

Vøringsfossen waterfall, 20 km from Eidfjord, is Norway's most famous: 163 metres of free fall from the Hardangervidda plateau down into Måbødalen valley. In 1880, a hotel was built right at the edge - all materials carried up by pack horse from Eidfjord. A 47-metre bridge opened in 2020, with 99 steps above the gorge, offers a vertiginous view of the drop.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Scenic loop from Ulvik to Eidfjord and back via RV7 - allow half a day. The section of road that descends through the hairpin tunnels of Måbødalen valley is spectacular: the transition from lunar plateau at 1,250 m to green valley to fjord happens in a few kilometres.

Oslo

★★★★☆
1

On the RV7, the crossing of Hardangervidda gives you 67 km of lunar landscape - northern Europe's largest high-altitude plateau, at 1,100-1,200 metres. Lake after lake, open heath and infinite horizons. It's also one of the few places in Europe where wild reindeer migrate freely: several thousand animals cross the plateau with the seasons.

⏱ 60 min
2

At Hol, on the RV7, stop to see Hol Gamle Kyrkje (the old church of Hol), a medieval stone church dating from the 12th century. A brief 15-20 minute stop suffices to view the exterior and historic cemetery - it's one of the best-preserved old stones in the region.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Return drive Ulvik-Oslo via RV7 and E16: allow 4 to 5 hours total, including breaks. Pass through Geilo to refuel if needed - it's the only real stop on the plateau. Check conditions on vegvesen.no in autumn and spring: the RV7 can close during snowstorms on the plateau.

⏱ 300 min
