Classic China: Beijing, Xi'an & Shanghai

📅 11 days📍 10 stops🚗 2451 km⏱ 7h30

From the Forbidden City to the Terracotta Warriors, from centuries-old hutongs to Pudong's skyscrapers: this circuit connects China's three most sought-after cities. Beijing the imperial capital, Xi'an cradle of the Silk Road, Shanghai showcase of modern China.

✨ Highlights
  • Forbidden City and Temple of Heaven in Beijing
  • Mutianyu Great Wall with toboggan descent
  • Xi'an's Terracotta Army, 8,000 life-size warriors
  • Xi'an's Ming ramparts for a bike circuit
  • Shanghai's Bund and the Pudong skyline
  • Yu Garden, a Ming dynasty landscape masterpiece
  • Zhujiajiao water town and its peaceful canals
📆 Best period

April, May, September, October

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 9 and 13 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Spring (April-May): Pleasant temperatures, blooming flowers, ideal weather, moderate crowds, excellent for most regions, comfortable travel.

Fall (September-October): Perfect weather, clear skies, comfortable temperatures, peak tourist season, higher prices, autumn colors beautiful.

Summer (June-August): Hot and humid, rainy season, crowded tourist sites, highest prices, but best for Tibet and western regions.

Winter (November-March): Cold temperatures, fewer crowds, lower prices, perfect for southern regions, harsh in north, unique winter experiences.

Regional Variations: Vast climate diversity, southern subtropical, northern continental, western plateau, plan according to regions visited.

🌍 What to discover in CN?

Ancient civilization meets modern superpower with Great Wall, forbidden cities, and cutting-edge technology. Experience diverse landscapes, incredible cuisine, and 5000-year cultural heritage. The country offers bustling megacities, pristine nature, and traditional arts. China provides overwhelming cultural richness and geographical diversity in rapidly modernizing nation.

📋 Important notes

Domestic flights Beijing→Xi'an→Shanghai included in the route. Spring (April-May) and autumn (September-October) offer the best weather conditions. Online booking required for the Forbidden City and the Terracotta Army.

Day 1

Beijing

Imperial capital of 24 emperors, from the Forbidden City to the Great Wall

★★★★★
1

The Forbidden City, built between 1406 and 1420 under Emperor Yongle, sprawls across 178 acres and contains 980 buildings connected by courtyards paved with 12 million bricks. Home to 24 emperors of the Ming and Qing dynasties for five centuries, it now houses the Palace Museum and its vast imperial collections—paintings, bronzes, ceramics, lacquerware. The yellow roofs—a color reserved for the emperor—crown an ensemble that's been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1987. Start at the Gate of Heavenly Peace, the monumental entrance topped with Mao Zedong's portrait since 1949, then cross through the three great ceremonial halls of the outer court.

⏱ 180 min
2

Climb Jingshan (Coal Hill), an artificial mound rising 354 feet, built from earth excavated from the Forbidden City's moats. From the summit, you'll get a sweeping view over the entire expanse of golden palace roofs. This is where the last Ming emperor hanged himself in 1644 when an army of 400,000 peasants stormed Beijing.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Watch the changing of the guard at Tiananmen Gate and cross the world's largest public square (109 acres).

⏱ 30 min
Day 2

Beijing

Imperial capital of 24 emperors, from the Forbidden City to the Great Wall

★★★★★
1

The Temple of Heaven, built in 1420 under Emperor Yongle and expanded in the 16th century under Jiajing, covers 673 acres—four times the size of the Forbidden City. The Hall of Prayer for Good Harvests, a 125-foot-tall rotunda with a 98-foot diameter, rests on 28 wooden pillars assembled without a single nail or drop of cement. The four central pillars symbolize the seasons, the next twelve represent the months. The emperor came here twice a year to pray to Heaven for bountiful harvests. Struck by lightning in 1889, the hall was rebuilt identically the following year. It's been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1998.

⏱ 120 min
2

Step into the hutongs of the Nanluoguxiang quarter, one of Beijing's oldest alleyways with over 800 years of history. These narrow passages date back to the Yuan dynasty (1271-1368)—the word 'hutong' likely comes from the Mongolian 'hottog,' meaning 'well.' The courtyard houses (siheyuan) are arranged around small courtyards planted with pomegranate trees, a symbol of fertility. Beijing had 3,216 of them in 1944; fewer than 1,000 remain today.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Rise early to watch local residents at the Temple of Heaven Park practicing tai chi, dancing, singing, and playing cards beneath centuries-old cypresses—an unchanging morning ritual.

⏱ 45 min

Take a rickshaw ride through the Shichahai hutongs, Beijing's largest protected quarter, winding between lakes, weeping willows, and Qing dynasty mansions.

⏱ 60 min
Day 3

Beijing

Imperial capital of 24 emperors, from the Forbidden City to the Great Wall

★★★★★
1

The Mutianyu section of the Great Wall, 45 miles from Beijing, was first built in the mid-6th century under the Northern Qi dynasty, then reconstructed in granite in 1569 by General Qi Jiguang during the Ming period. The restored stretch runs 7,380 feet and features 22 watchtowers—an unusual density, with a tower roughly every 330 feet. The walls reach 23 to 26 feet in height and 13 to 16 feet in width at the top. Unique feature: crenellations line both sides of the walkway, which is exceptional—on most other sections, they only protect the outer side. The Mutianyu Pass, with its three interconnected watchtowers (Zhengguantai), forms a defensive structure found nowhere else on the Wall.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

Take the cable car up to Tower 14, then hike the ramparts between Towers 14 and 20 (western section) with panoramic views over forested hills—vegetation covers over 96% of the area. You can descend by toboggan from Tower 6, a slide that winds through the forest.

⏱ 120 min

Day 4

Beijing

Imperial capital of 24 emperors, from the Forbidden City to the Great Wall

★★★★★
1

The Summer Palace, designed in 1750 by Emperor Qianlong to celebrate his mother's 60th birthday, covers 716 acres—three-quarters of which are occupied by Kunming Lake. The emperor had 100,000 workers excavate and enlarge the lake, using the earth to raise Longevity Hill. Destroyed in 1860 by Franco-British troops during the Second Opium War, it was rebuilt in 1886 by Empress Cixi—who diverted funds meant for naval modernization. The Long Corridor, a covered gallery stretching 2,388 feet, is adorned with thousands of paintings on wood depicting landscapes, flowers, and scenes from Chinese literature. At the lake's edge, Cixi's marble boat, 118 feet long, remains moored on the north shore. It's been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1998.

⏱ 180 min
2

The 798 Art District occupies a former industrial complex of 6.9 million square feet built in the 1950s by East German architects in Bauhaus style. Factory 718, intended for manufacturing military electronic components, was divided into six units in the 1960s—including Unit 798, which gave the district its name. After subsidies dried up in the early 1990s, the large workshops with high ceilings and north-facing skylights attracted artists drawn by cheap rents. Today there are over 200 galleries, including UCCA (107,000 square feet of exhibition space) and Tang Contemporary Art.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Cross the Seventeen-Arch Bridge, built in 1750 under Qianlong: 490 feet long, it links the lakeshore to Nanhu Island and bears over 500 carved stone lions, each one different. From the bridge's midpoint, you can count nine arches on each side—the number nine symbolized imperial power.

⏱ 45 min

Day 5

Xian

Cradle of the Silk Road guarded by 8,000 clay warriors

★★★★★
1

Flight from Beijing to Xi'an (about 2 hours). The Terracotta Army, discovered on March 29, 1974, by farmers digging a well, lies 19 miles east of Xi'an. Commissioned by Emperor Qin Shi Huang—who unified China in 221 BC and launched construction of the Great Wall—this funerary army was meant to protect him in the afterlife. Pit 1, the largest (755 feet × 203 feet), contains over 6,000 warriors and horses arranged in a rectangular battle formation across 11 corridors. Each statue, standing 5.9 to 6.6 feet tall, has a unique face: expressions, hairstyles, armor, and ranks differ from soldier to soldier. The statues were fired at 1,650°F in kilns, then painted with mineral pigments to distinguish units. In total, excavations have uncovered about 8,000 warriors, 130 chariots, 520 horses, and over 10,000 bronze weapons. The site has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1987.

⏱ 180 min
2

Pit 2, to the northeast, contains about 1,000 warriors with greater variety: crossbowmen, infantry, cavalry, and war chariots in a combined unit. Display cases let you observe details up close—seven variants of Qin armor have been identified. Pit 3, the smallest (5,380 square feet), U-shaped, holds 68 high-ranking officers and a ceremonial chariot drawn by four horses: this is the underground army's headquarters. The emperor's tomb itself, covered by a 377-foot-high mound, has never been opened—archaeologists fear the artifacts would degrade upon contact with air, as happened with the first warriors' paint when they were unearthed.

⏱ 60 min

Day 6

Xian

Cradle of the Silk Road guarded by 8,000 clay warriors

★★★★★
1

The Xi'an City Wall, built between 1374 and 1378 under the first Ming emperor Zhu Yuanzhang, forms a rectangle with an 8.5-mile perimeter around the old town. First constructed of rammed earth mixed with lime and glutinous rice juice, then covered with bricks in 1568, it rises 39 feet high, 49 to 59 feet wide at the base, and 39 to 46 feet wide at the top. A moat 66 feet wide and 33 feet deep encircles the wall. Four monumental gates pierce the ramparts at the cardinal points; 98 watchtowers, spaced 394 feet apart—a distance calculated based on half an arrow's range—line the route. Enter through the South Gate (Yongning), the most ornately decorated, whose main tower rises 105 feet.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Muslim Quarter (Huimin Jie), at the foot of the Drum Tower, has been home to Xi'an's Hui community since the Tang dynasty (7th century), when Xi'an—then called Chang'an—was the starting point of the Silk Road. The alleys overflow with stalls selling hand-pulled noodles (biang biang mian), grilled lamb skewers, and flatbreads (roujiamo). The Great Mosque, founded in 742 during the Tang dynasty and rebuilt in the 14th century, blends traditional Chinese architecture with Islamic elements across a 129,000-square-foot garden.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Giant Wild Goose Pagoda (Dayan Ta), built in 652 during the Tang dynasty to house Buddhist scriptures brought from India by the monk Xuanzang after 17 years of travel. The current pagoda, renovated under Empress Wu Zetian, has 7 stories and stands 210 feet tall. It's part of an ensemble inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2014 as part of the Silk Road corridor sites.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Bike the city walls: rentals are available at each gate. Allow 1.5 to 2 hours to complete the 8.5-mile circuit, with contrasting views of the old town inside and modern towers outside. The walkway, 39 to 46 feet wide, is perfectly flat.

⏱ 120 min

Tasting in the Muslim Quarter: biang biang noodles hand-pulled before your eyes, cumin lamb skewers, pao mo lamb soup (bread crumbled into broth), sesame cakes, and fresh-pressed pomegranate juice.

⏱ 60 min

Day 7

Shanghai

Metropolis where the Bund's Art Deco façades face Pudong's towers

★★★★☆
1

Flight from Xi'an to Shanghai (about 2.5 hours). The Bund (Waitan), an Anglo-Urdu term meaning 'muddy embankment,' stretches for one mile along the west bank of the Huangpu River. Before the British arrived in 1845, there were only muddy shores here. The Opium Wars opened the port to Westerners, and 52 buildings in various styles—Romanesque, Gothic, Neoclassical, Beaux-Arts, Art Deco—gradually lined the avenue. The most imposing is the former HSBC Building (No. 12), completed in 1923, considered at the time the most sumptuous building between the Suez Canal and the Bering Strait. Next door, the Customs House (No. 13), built in 1927, sports a clock inspired by Big Ben. Across the Bund, on the opposite bank of the river, the Pudong skyline bristles with futuristic skyscrapers.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Twilight stroll along the Bund: when lights come on along both riverbanks, the contrast between the colonial architecture and the illuminated Pudong towers is striking.

⏱ 60 min
Day 8

Shanghai

Metropolis where the Bund's Art Deco façades face Pudong's towers

★★★★☆
1

Yu Garden (Yuyuan), built starting in 1559 by Pan Yunduan—a high-ranking Ming dynasty official—to offer his father a peaceful retreat. Construction took nearly 20 years. Across 5 acres, the garden distills the landscape art of Jiangnan: pavilions, rockeries, lotus ponds, and winding corridors, separated by 'dragon walls' whose ridges undulate and end in a dragon's head. The Great Rockery, designed by master gardener Zhang Nanyang, is a 39-foot-high artificial mountain composed of 2,000 tons of yellow Wukang stones—it's the only surviving work by this master. In front of the entrance, the Nine-Turn Bridge leads to the Huxinting Teahouse in the middle of a pond: its zigzags are meant to block evil spirits, who can only travel in straight lines.

⏱ 90 min
2

The former French Concession of Shanghai retains its plane trees planted in the early 20th century, Art Deco villas, and shikumen—stone-gate townhouses typical of Shanghai, which appeared in the 1860s and once represented over 60% of the city's housing stock. The Tianzifang quarter, a former residential complex of narrow lanes, has been transformed into art galleries, design studios, and cafés.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Wander through the Yuyuan Bazaar surrounding the garden, among souvenir shops, teahouses, and xiaolongbao restaurants (soup dumplings), a Shanghai specialty.

⏱ 60 min
Day 9

Shanghai

Metropolis where the Bund's Art Deco façades face Pudong's towers

★★★★☆
1

The Lujiazui district in Pudong, a special economic zone opened in 1990, concentrates the three towers that define Shanghai's silhouette: Jin Mao Tower (1,380 feet, 88 floors, completed 1999), Shanghai World Financial Center (1,614 feet, 101 floors, 2008), and Shanghai Tower (2,073 feet, 128 floors, 2015)—the world's second-tallest building. The latter's elevator reaches the 119th floor in 55 seconds. From the top, on a clear day, the view extends across the entire megalopolis of 26 million inhabitants.

⏱ 90 min
2

Nanjing Road (Nanjing Lu), a 3.4-mile shopping artery, links People's Square to the Bund. The pedestrianized western section, opened in 1999, concentrates department stores and brand-name shops along a half-mile stretch. At the street's end, People's Square houses the Shanghai Museum, a building shaped like an ancient bronze vessel displaying collections of Shang and Zhou bronzes, ceramics, and calligraphy.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Ascend to the Shanghai Tower observatory (119th floor) for a 360° panorama of the city, then walk down through the Lujiazui financial district.

⏱ 60 min
Day 10

Shanghai

Metropolis where the Bund's Art Deco façades face Pudong's towers

★★★★☆
1

Excursion to the water town of Zhujiajiao, 29 miles west of Shanghai (about 1 hour on metro line 17). Nicknamed 'Shanghai's Venice,' this 1,700-year-old town preserves lanes and buildings from the Ming and Qing dynasties. Thirty-six stone bridges span the canals, including Fangsheng Bridge—the largest five-arch stone arch bridge in the Shanghai region, built in 1571 by monk Xingchao of Cimen Temple in memory of his mother. Rising 19 feet, it's adorned with a relief of eight dragons surrounding a pearl on the central arch and stone lions at the ends. North Street (Bei Dajie), stretching half a mile, is lined with old buildings and craft shops.

⏱ 90 min
2

Kezhi Garden, Zhujiajiao's largest private garden, blends traditional Chinese styles with Western influences in an ensemble of pavilions, ponds, and greenery. Its five-story building topped with a pavilion is the old town's highest point.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Traditional boat ride on the canals: boatmen in local costume glide wooden craft under stone bridges and along white houses with black-tiled roofs.

⏱ 30 min

Sample local specialties: zongzi (sticky rice dumplings wrapped in bamboo leaves), braised pork trotters, and sesame cakes from the North Street stalls.

⏱ 45 min
