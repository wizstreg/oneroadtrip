Lisboa

★★★★★
1

Conquered in 1147 with the help of crusaders en route to Jerusalem, the Castelo de São Jorge fortress served as a royal residence until the 16th century. The current castle owes much to a restoration carried out between 1938 and 1944 - knowing this changes how you see the ramparts. From the top of the keep, there's a 360° view over the tiled roofs and the Tagus. Buy your ticket online to skip the queue.

⏱ 120 min
2

The smell of grilled sardines wafts up from the alleys of Alfama long before you reach the Miradouro de Santa Luzia: it's the only Lisbon neighborhood that survived the 1755 earthquake intact. Stop in front of the azulejo tiles at the viewpoint depicting the Praça do Comércio before its destruction. Avoid the descent on Sunday mornings: the miradouro crowds are at their peak.

⏱ 60 min
3

Austere, almost brutal: the Sé de Lisboa surprises after the Manueline exuberance of Belém. The 12th-century Romanesque cathedral is Lisbon's oldest - built over a mosque shortly after the 1147 reconquest. The Gothic cloister houses archaeological excavations that are still active. Free admission to the church.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Look for Tasca do Chico in Bairro Alto for an unstaged fado session: small room, amateur singers sometimes better than the professionals, no organized show. Reservations are almost mandatory even on weekdays - call or stop by during the day.

⏱ 120 min

Go up the Elevador de Santa Justa before 9am to enjoy the empty terrace - the view over the Baixa rooftops is worth the ticket. After 10am, the queue often exceeds 40 minutes. Walk down to Chiado through the Carmo alleyways.

⏱ 30 min
$$$9.5Hotel Britania Art Deco

Belem

★★★★★
1

Too beautiful to be true, yet everything is authentic: the Mosteiro dos Jerónimos is a masterpiece of Manueline style, financed by a 5% tax on the spice trade with India. Built from 1501 onwards by order of Manuel I, it houses Vasco da Gama's tomb. UNESCO-listed since 1983 along with the Torre de Belém. Closed Mondays.

⏱ 90 min
2

Standing on the Tagus since 1519, the Torre de Belém looks like a stone wedding cake. This Manueline river defense tower, built by Francisco de Arruda between 1514 and 1519, guarded the harbor entrance. A rhinoceros sculpture on the west façade recalls that a living rhino was gifted to King Manuel I - the animal drowned in a shipwreck before reaching Rome. Long queues in summer, arrive early.

⏱ 60 min
3

Cotton was spun here in 1846, since 2008 it's been home to designers and restaurants: LX Factory occupies 23,000 m² of former factories belonging to the Companhia de Fiação e Tecidos Lisbonense, under the 25 de Abril bridge. The Ler Devagar bookstore, in a former printing house, has shelves reaching to the ceiling and a bicycle suspended in mid-air. Sunday morning flea market.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Only a few master pastry chefs know the original recipe for Pastéis de Belém - kept secret since 1837 when monks from the neighboring monastery started selling their tarts to survive after the closure of convents. The Fábrica de Pastéis de Belém, Rua de Belém 84-92, is the only one authorized to use this name. Long queue, but it moves quickly. Order them sprinkled with cinnamon.

⏱ 30 min

Finish with the Padrão dos Descobrimentos in late afternoon: 52 meters of white concrete shaped like a ship's prow, inaugurated in 1960. Controversial, kitsch for some, but the view from the top over the Tagus and the Jerónimos justifies the €4.

⏱ 45 min
$$$9.4Olissippo Lapa Palace – The Leading Hotels of the World

Sintra

★★★★★
1

The Palácio da Pena is what a romantic king would have imagined if he could build his castle in a cartoon - garish colors, Moorish towers, grotesque sculptures. Ferdinand II, husband of Maria II, had it erected between 1842 and 1854 on the ruins of a Hieronymite monastery. UNESCO-listed since 1995 along with central Sintra. Buy your ticket online and arrive before 10am in summer.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Quinta da Regaleira is the work of one man - António Carvalho Monteiro, a Portuguese millionaire - and an Italian architect - Luigi Manini - from 1898 onwards. Freemason, alchemical, Templar symbolism: everything is coded, including the Poço Iniciático, a 27-meter well in a descending spiral that serves no purpose other than to exist. An absolute must-see.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Castelo dos Mouros, built in the 8th-9th century, occupies a ridge above Sintra. Christian reconquest in 1147, gradual abandonment, romantic restoration in the 19th century. The walk along the crenellated ramparts offers the best panoramic view of Sintra and the Cascais coast. Often less crowded than Pena.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Train from Rossio station in Lisbon, 40 minutes, departures every 30 minutes. Avoid the car: parking in Sintra is a nightmare in high season. Once there, bus 434 loops through Pena/Castelo/center, day ticket available.

⏱ 60 min

Taste the travesseiros de Sintra at Casa Piriquita, Rua das Padarias, on your way down from the palaces. This almond and egg yolk pastry has been emblematic of Sintra since 1862. Count on €1.50 each.

⏱ 20 min
$$$9.6Quinta da Maquia Hotel

Cabo Espichel

★★★★☆
1

The dinosaur footprints at Cabo Espichel weren't falsified by local devotion - they're perfectly real, dated to the Lower Cretaceous, about 145 million years ago. Several series of sauropod (giant herbivore) tracks are visible on the tilted slabs of Praia dos Lagosteiros and Baía dos Lagosteiros. The legend of Our Lady of the Cape, who allegedly appeared on the back of a mule, arose from medieval interpretation of these giant traces.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Ermida da Memória, a small chapel of medieval origin, marks the exact spot of the Marian apparition that gave birth to the cult of Nossa Senhora do Cabo Espichel. The current sanctuary - commissioned by Dom Pedro II at the turn of the 18th century - has been under restoration via the Portuguese tourism Revive program since 2014.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Walk to the northern tip of the cape to see the Baía dos Lagosteiros from above - 100-meter vertical cliffs dropping into a cove inaccessible by land. The wind here is constant and often violent. The trails near the edge aren't marked, keep your distance.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 12 km · 18min

Sesimbra

★★★★☆
1

Sesimbra is a fishing village that hasn't forgotten its trade: the auction operates every morning at the port, trawlers return loaded around 2pm, restaurant signs announce peixe espada preto and chocos with fresh numbers on chalkboards. The village slopes steeply down to a sheltered beach - calm in rough seas, packed in summer. Old town intact between ruas Cidade de Setúbal and 5 de Outubro.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Castelo de Sesimbra dominates the bay from 230 meters altitude. Moorish origins, reconquered in 1165 by Afonso Henriques, given to the Templars then to the Order of Santiago in 1186. The complete ramparts can be walked in 30 minutes, with a view extending to Sintra on clear days. Free access, parking at the summit.

⏱ 45 min
3

Porto de Sesimbra, north side of the beach: fishing boats pull up to the quay in mid-afternoon, unloading peixe espada preto and conger eels in front of local buyers. Fifteen minutes in this artisanal fishing port is enough to see that Sesimbra isn't a disguised beach resort.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Eat grilled fish at Sesimbra port: Ribamar, A Nova Estrela, or the simpler tascas towards rua Cândido dos Reis. Peixe espada preto (black scabbardfish) is the specialty, caught in deep water off Sesimbra. Count on €12-18 per dish.

⏱ 75 min

At Cabo Espichel, descend onto the rocky slabs near the lighthouse to see the Cretaceous dinosaur footprints - about 145 million years old - clearly visible to the naked eye on the tilted layers. The trail is marked from the sanctuary, allow 30 minutes round trip. Cliffs without barriers, caution with children.

⏱ 60 min
$$$9.6Beach House In Sesimbra
🚗Drive to next stop: 18 km · 25min

Serra Da Arrabida

★★★★☆
1

The Parque Natural da Arrábida covers about 17,000 hectares since 1976 - including over 5,000 ha of marine area - a limestone massif peaking at 501 meters that drops straight into the Atlantic. The N379-1 road, panoramic, follows the ridge then descends to hidden beaches. The Mediterranean vegetation here is one of the best preserved in the Iberian Peninsula according to ICNF. Careful driving: tight bends and cliffs without barriers.

⏱ 60 min
2

Praia de Galapinhos surprises with the contrast: turquoise water, white sand, limestone cliffs - 40 km from Lisbon. Access in summer season (July-August) only by shuttle from Portinho da Arrábida; off-season, parking on the N379-1 then 15 minutes downhill walk. No services on site. Bring water.

⏱ 120 min
3

The Convento de Nossa Senhora da Arrábida is a 16th-century Franciscan monastery perched mid-slope, designed for total isolation - two chapels, a refectory, a kitchen, a library, and 40 small cells scattered in the surrounding hills. Acquired in 1990 by Fundação Oriente. Guided tours Wednesday to Sunday, €5 by prior reservation.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

If you're into diving or snorkeling, Arrábida is one of the best zones in the Portuguese Atlantic - integral marine reserve, clear water, rich biodiversity. Several clubs in Setúbal offer half-day to full-day outings.

⏱ 180 min

In Azeitão, on the road between Setúbal and Arrábida, stop for the queijo de Azeitão DOP - creamy sheep's cheese, and the tortas and tartes d'Azeitão (egg and almond pastries). Several producers open to the public on rua Direita.

⏱ 45 min
$$$9.4Upon Harbor - Seixal Bay Hotel
🚗Drive to next stop: 80 km · 1h15

Setubal

★★★☆☆
1

The Igreja de Jesus de Setúbal is Portugal's first Manueline building - begun in 1490 by architect Diogo de Boitaca before he designed the Jerónimos. Twisted columns in local breschia marble, complex ribbed vaulting: the entire Manueline style vocabulary is born here. The pink stone floor contrasts with the green marble columns. Classified as a national monument.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Forte de São Filipe dominates Setúbal and the Sado estuary since 1582 - built by Philip II of Spain during the Iberian Union, on a four-bastion star plan. Now partially converted into a pousada, the ramparts remain accessible. View over Tróia, the sandy peninsula opposite, and the dolphins that still populate the estuary.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Mercado do Livramento dates from 1930: Setúbal's market has been selling its fresh fish here since 1930 under hand-painted azulejos depicting maritime scenes. Arrive before 10am for sardines, choco frito (fried cuttlefish, local specialty) and seafood.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Eat choco frito in a tasca on rua Antão Girão or around the market. Setúbal has made it its specialty: cuttlefish cut into strips, breaded, fried to order, served with lemon and fries. Count on €8-12 per dish.

⏱ 75 min

If you have time, cross by ferry to Tróia (20 min, €7 round trip on foot) to see the Roman fish salting remains - the Cetariae de Tróia, active from the 1st to 5th century, exported their garum widely in the western Empire. Site managed by the Tróia Resort foundation, ticket ~€3.

⏱ 120 min
$$$8.7B&B HOTEL Setúbal Bonfim
🚗Drive to next stop: 50 km · 50min

Santiago Do Cacem

★★☆☆☆
1

Three kilometers of hills separate Santiago do Cacém from the Miróbriga site - but the real shock is the medieval castle that dominates the coastal plain from a ridge. Founded in the 11th century with Moorish origins, restored by the Templars, it offers from its ramparts a view that extends to the coast. Free entrance, ten-minute climb from the center. Better before 11am, before the sun beats down.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Santiago do Cacém railway station doesn't look like much from the street. Go in anyway: the platform walls of the Beja-Sines line are covered with azulejos depicting plowing scenes and local monuments. A program of tiles created in the 20th century in the Pombaline tradition. Two minutes' detour, zero ticket.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Climb to the medieval castle in late afternoon when the raking light brings out the golden stones of the ramparts. The descent into the old town passes some well-preserved low houses. Allow 45 minutes round trip from the center.

⏱ 45 min
$$$10Monte De Santiago
🚗Drive to next stop: 3 km · 5min

Mirobriga

★★★★☆
1

Wild grass has reclaimed the tiers of Miróbriga's hippodrome - 360 meters long, traced into the hills. This 2nd-century Roman circus is one of the few identified in Portugal. Site managed by the Regional Directorate of Culture of Alentejo, open Tuesday to Sunday 9am to 12:30pm and 2pm to 5:30pm.

⏱ 60 min
2

A hippodrome in Portugal is rare - and Miróbriga has an almost intact one. But the rest of the site is just as solid: two distinct thermal complexes (one attributed to men, the other to women), a forum, tabernae and a single-arch Roman bridge. Founded in the Iron Age, the site remained inhabited until the 4th century. Ticket about €3, audio guide available at reception.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

If you have time, look for the single-arch Roman bridge below the main site: it's poorly signposted but intact. From the bridge, the path climbs back to the baths via a marked trail. Bring closed-toe shoes - the terrain is uneven.

⏱ 30 min
$$$9.4Comfortable Studio in Santiago do Cacém, private pool, 65 m²
🚗Drive to next stop: 22 km · 25min

Sines

★★★☆☆
1

In Sines, everything starts with Vasco da Gama - born here around 1469, son of the castle governor. The Castelo de Sines now houses the Museu de Sines and Casa de Vasco da Gama, inaugurated in 2008, with a multimedia reconstruction of his voyages to India. The navigator's statue on the ramparts faces the ocean to the south. Open Tuesday to Sunday 10am to 5pm, free admission.

⏱ 60 min
2

Pass by the Igreja Matriz where Vasco da Gama was baptized, then head to Largo dos Penedos da Índia for a panoramic view over the bay. The Centro Histórico de Sines consists of a few streets between the castle and the port - aimless wandering recommended. The old town can be done in 1h30 on foot.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

The wind smells of the sea at Largo dos Penedos da Índia, a natural terrace above the bay. It's also the best viewpoint over Sines' industrial port - striking contrast with the three streets of the old town. Since 2015, an elevator connects the center to the waterfront.

⏱ 30 min
$$$9.5Monte Xisto Country Charm Hotel
🚗Drive to next stop: 12 km · 15min

Porto Covo

★★★☆☆
1

Porto Covo is the most carefully preserved village on the Alentejo coast: white houses with blue trim, paved alleys, nothing superfluous. The Largo Marquês de Pombal is its heart - a rectangular square with Pombaline proportions, built after the 1755 earthquake according to the reconstruction's urban planning principles. Stop by in the evening when residents bring out their chairs.

⏱ 45 min
2

Built in 1603 by order of Philip II to defend the coast against pirate raids, the Forte da Ilha do Pessegueiro occupies the center of Pessegueiro islet, a few hundred meters offshore. Around it, the seabed reveals Roman garum salting vats - the island was an active port center since at least Carthaginian times.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

At low tide, walk across to Ilha do Pessegueiro following the rocks for a few hundred meters. In summer, boats offer crossings from the beach (about €10 round trip). The seabed around the island is rich in Roman amphorae - snorkeling possible but access to underwater ruins not marked.

⏱ 60 min
$$$9.3T Monte
🚗Drive to next stop: 18 km · 25min

Praia Do Malhao

★★★★☆
1

Dune sand as far as the eye can see, clean waves without beach infrastructure: Praia do Malhão remains wild because access is deliberately long - 2 km of sandy track from the N393, impassable for low-clearance cars. In the Parque Natural do Sudoeste Alentejano, camping outside official zones has been prohibited since 2021.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Trilho dos Pescadores from the Rota Vicentina follows the cliff edge between Malhão and Vila Nova de Milfontes for 7 km - the best section of the Alentejo coast for walking alone in the morning. Marked in blue and green, low elevation gain.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Surf or swim early: Praia do Malhão faces due west, direct Atlantic swell. No lifeguards or buoys. No shops on site - bring water and food for the day.

⏱ 120 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 8 km · 12min

Vila Nova De Milfontes

★★★☆☆
1

Privatized in 1903, the Forte de São Clemente is no longer open to visitors - but the Praça da Barbacã in front of it is public and offers a direct view of the Mira estuary. The fort itself, built in 1602 against North African pirates, dominates the foz from a rocky point. It remains Milfontes' postcard image, photographed by everyone from the square.

⏱ 30 min
2

The fresh water of the Mira estuary smells different from the ocean - more vegetal, calmer. Praia da Franquia extends into this estuary sheltered from Atlantic winds: calm water, ideal for paddleboarding or children's swimming. The rio Mira is the only Portuguese coastal river that has never been dammed - it remains navigable from Odemira.

⏱ 60 min
3

River side, the water is fresh and warm; ocean side, cold and rough: Praia da Franquia and Praia do Farol are 15 minutes apart on foot and offer two temperatures. Praia do Farol faces Atlantic waves and overlooks the lighthouse. Surfers prefer the beaches to the north, Malhão and Aivados, accessible by car via the N393.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Take the ferry boat in summer to Praia das Furnas, on the other side of the Mira. A few minutes' crossing to a wilder beach, less crowded than the village beaches. Off-season, car access via the bridge (10 minutes). The beach is in the Parque Natural protected zone.

⏱ 120 min

Rent a kayak or stand-up paddle on Praia da Franquia to go up the Mira estuary towards Odemira: a few kilometers of calm river between reed banks, far from the ocean. Several rental providers on the beach from June to September.

⏱ 90 min
$$$9.5Monte da Corça Nova
🚗Drive to next stop: 30 km · 35min

Cabo Sardao

★★★★☆
1

White storks nest twenty meters above the waves at Cabo Sardão - impassive beneath the gulls' cries, beaks turned seaward. Storks nesting on cliffs above the ocean: an extremely rare phenomenon, documented here since the 1980s. The nesting season runs from April to late August. Free access, parking at Cavaleiro village.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Cabo Sardão lighthouse entered service in 1915: 17-meter square tower, built backwards according to local tradition - the entrance door faces the sea rather than the land. Its current range is 42 km. It's the westernmost point of the Alentejo coast.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Park at Cavaleiro village and walk 20 minutes on the coastal trail to the lighthouse. The path follows the black schist cliffs and passes several stork nests visible to the naked eye. Bring binoculars in May - the chicks are in the nests.

⏱ 40 min
$$$9.8Casa da Courela
🚗Drive to next stop: 8 km · 12min

Zambujeira Do Mar

★★★☆☆
1

Praia da Zambujeira is more beautiful than famous: fine sand in a steep cove, black cliffs on each side, clear waters despite the Atlantic swell. At the top of the cliff, the Capela de Nossa Senhora do Mar watches over the inlet. Access via a steep staircase from the village - allow 10 minutes on foot.

⏱ 90 min
2

Perched on cliffs a hundred meters above the ocean, the village of Zambujeira do Mar hasn't budged since the 1980s. One main street, a few low houses, a restaurant terrace. What mass tourism spared here is rare at this latitude. Book accommodation well in advance - few rooms available.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Rota Vicentina passes through Zambujeira: two marked trails - the Trilho dos Pescadores follows the coast on cliffs to Odeceixe (7 km north), the Caminho Histórico crosses the schist hinterland. Maps and trail guides available at the Parque Natural reception in Vila Nova de Milfontes.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

If you walk 30 minutes south from the beach, Praias dos Alteirinhos and do Carvalhal open in successive coves - accessible only on foot, partly naturist, without infrastructure. The trail follows the cliff edge in the Parque Natural do Sudoeste Alentejano.

⏱ 90 min

Book in advance: few rooms in the village, and the only serious restaurant - O Sacas on the cliff - fills up in season. The cuisine is simple, the terrace exposed to the wind, the view over the cliffs is worth the wait.

⏱ 90 min
$$$10Casa Cisne - Selão da Eira
🚗Drive to next stop: 25 km · 30min

Odeceixe

★★☆☆☆
1

Odeceixe is the last Alentejo village before the Algarve - the administrative border runs through the middle of the beach. The perched village overlooks the valley from a ridge, with a restored windmill clearly visible from the N120. A few low houses, a bakery, a crossroads for Rota Vicentina hikers.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Ribeira de Seixe divides fresh and salt water 100 meters apart at Praia de Odeceixe. On one side the river, cool and calm; on the other the ocean, cold and rough. This phenomenon of mixed lagoon is rare in Portugal. The beach is protected by cliffs on both sides, ideal for families.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The mixed bathing at Praia de Odeceixe is unlike anything else: choose your water according to your mood, fresh water on the left or Atlantic on the right. Access from the village on foot in 20 minutes or by car via the EN120 road with paid parking in summer.

⏱ 90 min
$$$9.6AL-Odeceixe Verde
🚗Drive to next stop: 18 km · 25min

Aljezur

★★★☆☆
1

The Moorish castle of Aljezur fell in 1249, among the last Moorish strongholds conquered in Portugal - the Reconquista ended here very late. The ruins dominate the valley from a basalt peak. From the summit, view over the inland valley and the Atlantic in the distance. Free climb, path marked from the main square.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Museu Municipal of Aljezur is tiny - but the local Reconquista is carefully recounted there, with Islamic ceramics and campaign maps to support it. This is where you understand why this coast resisted longer than the rest of the country. Ticket a few euros, visit in 45 minutes.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walk the castle trail on foot: from Praça da Liberdade, the path climbs in 10 minutes to the ramparts. View over the Aljezur valley and the Bordeira dunes in the distance. Descent through the alleyways of the historic quarter.

⏱ 40 min
$$$9.7Amaria
🚗Drive to next stop: 10 km · 18min

Praia Da Arrifana

★★★★☆
1

Steep cove, 50-meter red cliffs, turquoise water between the rocks - Praia da Arrifana is more dramatic than beautiful, and that's what makes it memorable. A 17th-century watchtower still stands atop the northern cliff. Recognized surf spot, regular swell even in summer.

⏱ 60 min
2

The ruins of Forte da Arrifana dominate the cove from the northern promontory - built in the 17th century to monitor the coast, only the foundations and two wall sections remain. Plunging view over the surfers below.

⏱ 15 min

Activities

Descend to the beach before sunset via the staircase carved in the rock - 10 minutes walk from the parking. Allow 1h on site. The return is done with a headlamp if you stay late.

⏱ 60 min

Carrapateira

Praia da Bordeira smells of salt and dried seaweed long before you see it. A freshwater lagoon borders the north of the beach, where the Bordeira river meets the ocean - fine sand dunes, lagoon, schist cliffs. Less known than Amado but more visually spectacular. Praia do Amado is beautiful winter and summer, but the wind is constant and the swell frank - hence its inclusion on the world surf circuit in winter. Surf schools operate on the beach from September to June. In summer, families replace the surfers.

If you surf, Praia do Amado is on the WSL world circuit in winter (regular waves, sandy bottom). Surf lessons are available year-round via village schools. Off-season, count on €40-60 per 2h lesson.

Cabo De Sao Vicente

★★★★☆
1

Sailors called this cape the "end of the world" - the last land before the open Atlantic, America beyond. The Cabo de São Vicente lighthouse entered service in 1846, built on the site of a convent destroyed by the corsair Francis Drake in 1587. Its current range exceeds 60 km. Free parking, free access to the surroundings.

⏱ 45 min
2

The current lighthouse is one of Europe's most powerful. It stands on the ruins of a 16th-century fortress built to protect the Franciscan convent that stood there - destroyed in 1587 by Drake, rebuilt in 1606. A small museum inside traces the site's history. Open 10am to 5pm-6pm depending on season, adult ticket €1.50.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Arrive in late afternoon for the Atlantic sunset: the raking light on the 75-meter black cliffs is the simplest and most effective spectacle on the entire coast. Dozens of people converge here every evening - even in low season.

Sagres

★★★☆☆
1

The Fortaleza de Sagres doesn't impress from the outside - a large white wall across the promontory, without tower or keep. But pass through the gate: the Rosa dos Ventos awaits in the center of the courtyard, a 43-meter diameter stone circle unearthed in 1921, whose function is still debated - wind rose, sundial, navigation instrument. Uncertain origin, possibly from the 15th or 16th century.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Rosa dos Ventos measures 43 meters in diameter, it's the courtyard floor. Henry the Navigator allegedly established his navigation school here in the 15th century - but the existence of this "Escola de Sagres" remains debated by historians. What's certain: the fortress is a national monument, and the view over the cliffs from the ramparts is worth the ticket (€3).

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The wind whistles constantly at Praia do Tonel, 10 minutes walk from the fortaleza: long, regular waves, renowned surf spot in winter. In summer, families enjoy the sand. The coastal path between Sagres and Cape São Vicente (6 km) is among the most beautiful in the region.

Lagos

★★★★☆
1

The Igreja de Santo António is too gilded to be true - and yet. The baroque interior, completed around 1769, is entirely covered in talha dourada, from floor to ceiling, without a single bare square centimeter. It's one of the most complete examples of this style in Portugal. Access via the Museu Municipal de Lagos, shared ticket.

⏱ 30 min
2

Pass by the Mercado de Escravos de Lagos - Europe's oldest slave market, active from the 15th century on this same square. The building under the Gothic arches now houses a museum dedicated to this history. Admission a few euros, visit in 30-45 minutes.

⏱ 35 min

Activities

Lagos also lives from the sea: the fishing port still lines up its colorful boats at the end of Avenida dos Descobrimentos. Fish market in the morning on weekdays, fishmongers open until 1pm. A quick stop before heading back to Alentejo.

Castro Verde

★★★☆☆
1

The steppe stretches as far as the eye can see from Castro Verde: cereals, scattered cork oaks, immense sky. This Zona de Proteção Especial is classified by BirdLife International as one of Europe's most important areas for steppe birds - little bustards, pin-tailed sandgrouse, lesser kestrels. The LPN (Liga para a Protecção da Natureza) manages several reserves accessible to the public.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Basílica Real de Nossa Senhora da Conceição preserves an 18th-century azulejo cycle recounting the Battle of Ourique of 1139 - mythical battle of Portugal's founding, where Afonso Henriques allegedly saw Christ appear to him. The exact location of the battle remains contested by historians, but the building marks the traditional site.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

If you're here in March-April, little bustards parade at sunrise in the fields around Castro Verde. The LPN organizes guided birdwatching outings - information at the Castro Verde nature house. Binoculars essential.

Mertola

★★★☆☆
1

The Igreja Matriz de Mértola is a church that looks like a mosque - because it was one. The former 12th-century Almohad mosque was converted into a church in 1238 during the Reconquista, without being demolished. Its five naves, its original mihrab behind the main altar and its horseshoe arches are intact. Exceptional case in Portugal. Free admission.

⏱ 30 min
2

Few castles in Portugal offer such a clear view of two rivers at once: the Castelo de Mértola dominates the confluence of the Guadiana and Oeiras from a rocky spur. The 13th-century keep tower provides access to a circular panorama over the white village below. The Campo Arqueológico de Mértola manages a network of ten themed museums - group ticket available at reception.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The Campo Arqueológico offers a well-designed group ticket giving access to ten themed museums: Islamic, Roman, Visigothic, Roman house, early Christian basilica. Allow at least 2h to do four or five. The main reception is in the village center, Rua Dr. Afonso Costa.

Mina De Sao Domingos

★★★★☆
1

The red waters of the Corta da Mina sting your eyes at first sight: acidic, loaded with iron and copper, they've stained the bottom of the quarry a blood red that resembles nothing else. The São Domingos copper mine, exploited since Roman times then relaunched by a British company in 1855, closed in 1966. The landscape hasn't been decontaminated.

⏱ 45 min
2

A separate English quarter survives at the end of the village - British managers lived apart from Portuguese miners, with their separate Protestant cemetery, their football club, their theater. Today some houses are still inhabited, others are open to the winds. One of Europe's most singular industrial archaeology sites.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Walk through the ruins of the industrial complex: factory chimneys, mine headframe, disused power plant. The marked path starts from the central parking and loops around the site in 45 minutes. Caution: the Corta da Mina surroundings are signposted as a toxic zone - don't touch the water.

Serpa

★★★☆☆
1

Serpa smells of sheep's cheese from the town entrance - the queijo de Serpa DOP is one of Portugal's most renowned cheeses, creamy and slightly bitter, produced on surrounding farms. The castle ramparts, partially collapsed in an explosion in 1707, frame a perfectly preserved walled town. The aqueduct runs along the ramparts for several hundred meters - medieval in conception, still standing.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Museu do Relógio exhibits over 2,300 mechanical clocks in a 16th-century convent - from the oldest (1630) to the most recent, pendulum clocks, pocket watches, grandfather clocks, alarm clocks. António Tavares d'Almeida, a Serpa watchmaker, began collecting in the 1970s and opened the museum in 1995.

⏱ 60 min
3

The aqueduct runs along the ramparts as if it had always been there - and it almost has: its route dates from the Middle Ages. It opens onto the Porta de Beja, the old town's main gate. The inner square, Praça da República, brings together the Igreja de Santa Maria and the Manueline Pelourinho - granite pillory that served to expose the condemned.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The queijo de Serpa DOP flows at room temperature - literally. In season (January-March), local cheese dairies sell fresh queijos still runny. A typical old town adega serves carne de porco à alentejana with a slice of cheese as a starter. Order vinho tinto de Serpa with it.

⏱ 90 min

If you pass through in January-March, the cheese is at its production and runny peak. Off-season, Serpa cheese dairies still sell their production - ask for 3-4 week aged pieces, firmer but just as tasty. The Friday morning market on Praça da República sells it.

Moura

★★★☆☆
1

Moura takes its name from a Moorish princess legend - the Moura Saluquia, who allegedly threw herself from the castle upon seeing her fiancé killed by Christian soldiers. Legend or not, the town was Moorish until 1232. Its white alleyways near the castle preserve the trace of a little-documented medieval Jewish quarter but readable in the urban fabric.

2

Climb to Moura castle for the view over the Alentejo plain stretching to Spain - the border is 12 km away. The keep tower dates from the 14th century. Below, Praça Sacadura Cabral gathers the town's café terraces. Moura is also known for its olives and azeite, produced on surrounding estates.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The medieval Jewish quarter of Moura is discreet but readable: a few narrow streets behind the castle, without tourist signage. Ask the tourist office for the small Bairro Mourisco map - free and well done. 30-minute walk.

Beja

★★★☆☆
1

The Torre de Menagem of Beja rises to 40 meters, entirely built of local marble - one of Portugal's tallest medieval keep towers. The star vault at the top surprises after the narrow staircase. 360° view over the Baixo Alentejo plain. Beja was Roman (Pax Julia), Visigothic, Moorish - as many layers visible descending the hill.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Museu Rainha Dona Leonor houses a possibly invented love story. Installed in the former Convento da Conceição founded in 1459, this is where the nun Mariana Alcoforado allegedly wrote her Portuguese Letters in the 1660s - letters to a French officer, published in Paris in 1669. Authentic or fictional work by a Parisian publisher? The debate isn't settled. The collection of 15th-century Hispano-Moorish azulejos, however, is indisputable.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Also visit the Igreja de Santo Amaro de Beja, a few steps from the castle: it's one of the rare examples of Visigothic architecture preserved in Portugal. Sculpted capitals, reused Roman columns, single nave from the 6th or 7th century. The Visigothic nucleus of the Museu Regional de Beja is installed there.

Alcacer Do Sal

★★★☆☆
1

Alcácer do Sal comes from Arabic Al-Qasr - the fortress - and sal for the salt pans that made its fortune since Antiquity. The castle atop the hill was conquered by Afonso Henriques in 1158, then taken and retaken until 1217. Now converted into a pousada, the keep and ramparts remain visitable. Clear view over the Sado estuary and rice fields.

⏱ 45 min
2

The historic center of Alcácer descends gently from the castle to the river. White houses with blue or yellow trim, several baroque churches, an archaeology museum installed in the archaeological crypt of the Pelourinho. Natural lunch stop on the route between Beja and Lisbon.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Lunch on enguias fritas (fried eels) or açorda de sável (shad soup with garlic and coriander) in a riverside tasca. Local specialties linked to the river, to taste in the cool season (October-March).

Lisboa

1

Europe's largest private collection, they say: for once, you can believe it. The Museu Calouste Gulbenkian brings together works from ancient Greek to the 20th century - Roman lamps, Ottoman faience, Rembrandt, Lalique, Manet. Two hours is enough for the essentials. Open Wednesday to Sunday.
