Belfast

★★★☆☆
1

Painted on the gable end of Sinn Féin headquarters at the corner of Falls Road and Sevastopol Street, Bobby Sands' smiling face is the most photographed mural in Belfast. The artwork dates from 1998 and reproduces a photo taken at Long Kesh, before the hunger strike that killed him in 1981 at age 27. All around, the International Wall rotates through solidarity murals: Palestine, Cuba, American civil rights. Walk early morning when the light is better and the tourist pickpockets still asleep.

⏱ 60 min
2

One hundred metres on foot, world turned inside out. Shankill Road is the Protestant-loyalist artery running parallel to Falls Road. UVF and UDA flags, murals of armed paramilitaries, flowers on pavements for the dead from the other side. The frontier is invisible but it's everywhere: pubs, schools, shops, everything doubled. This is Belfast's most unsettling experience and the most useful for understanding the Good Friday Agreement.

⏱ 45 min
3

Eight metres of concrete, wire mesh on top to block projectiles. The Cupar Way Peace Wall was started by the British Army on 10 September 1969, after the August riots. It was meant to be temporary. Fifty-seven years later it's still here, gates still locked at night. Bring a marker if you want to add a word. Aim for late afternoon, low sun on the graffiti.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

To understand both sides, take a black cab tour with an ex-prisoner guide. Coiste runs pairings of loyalist and republican guides who tell the story of the same street from two angles. Expect £25 to £40 per person for two hours, tip customary. Book the day before in high season, the best guides go fast.

⏱ 120 min

Belfast

★★★☆☆
1

The tunnel still smells of damp limestone. Running beneath Crumlin Road, it links the Victorian prison to the courthouse opposite—prisoners crossed it in handcuffs until 1996. The Crum opened in 1846, closed a century and a half later. Seventeen men were hanged here between 1854 and 1961, the last being Robert McGladdery. C-wing held republican and loyalist prisoners during the Troubles. Guided tour runs 70 to 90 minutes, take the guided rather than the audio.

⏱ 90 min
2

Slightly pompous but classy Edwardian building, City Hall was completed in 1906 on Donegall Square. The dome is visible from all over the city centre. In the gardens east of the facade, the Titanic Memorial Garden lists the names of the 1517 victims of the 1912 sinking, many of them Belfast workers. Free guided tour at 11am and 2pm on weekdays, it's short and well done.

⏱ 60 min
3

If you're wary of tourist traps, forget your prejudices. Titanic Belfast sits on the old Harland & Wolff shipyards, right where the hull was launched on 31 May 1911 in exactly 62 seconds. Nine galleries, including a mini-ride that passes through a reconstruction of the shipyards. The museum opened in 2012 for the centenary of the sinking. Allow 2h30 and book online, morning slots go first.

⏱ 150 min
4

Red-and-black hull moored next to the museum, SS Nomadic is the last White Star Line vessel still afloat. Built in 1911 in the same yards, she ferried first-class passengers to Titanic at Cherbourg, the port being too small to accommodate the giant. Restored in 2013. Thirty minutes is enough, combined ticket with Titanic Belfast works out cheaper.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

For dinner, the Mourne Seafood Bar on Bank Street is a safe bet: Carlingford oysters, Donegal mussels, simplicity. Book two days ahead, especially Friday and Saturday. Expect £30 to £40 per person with a drink.

⏱ 90 min

Carrickfergus

★★☆☆☆
1

Built in 1177 by John de Courcy, a Norman knight who came north seeking fortune, Carrickfergus Castle is one of Ireland's best-preserved fortresses. Edward Bruce, brother of Scottish king Robert Bruce, besieged it from September 1315 to September 1316—a year of famine before surrender. The original polygonal keep still stands, six cannons on the east battery survivors of the twenty-two installed in 1811. Allow 1h30, closed Mondays outside summer.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The A2 follows Belfast Lough heading north before turning toward the Causeway Coast. The Carrickfergus-Larne stretch holds no major interest but opens onto the Antrim Coast Road, one of Europe's finest coastal drives. Stop at Glenarm, small port of grey stone, coffee at Steensons. No direct tunnel, road open year-round.

⏱ 60 min

Stranocum

★★☆☆☆
1

Marketed as Game of Thrones magic, it's really an avenue of beech trees planted around 1775 by the Stuart family to impress visitors arriving at Gracehill House. The branches have interlaced into a tunnel above the road. About 90 remain of the original 150, Storm Gertrude in 2016 brought down three. Bregagh Road is now closed to cars to protect the roots. Parking at the Hedges Hotel, five-minute walk.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Before 8am or at dusk, raking light does its work and the Belfast coaches are elsewhere. Avoid summer weekends and British school holidays. If the weather is miserable, even better: mist under the beeches delivers exactly the photo everyone's after. Count on thirty minutes on site excluding photo shoots.

⏱ 30 min

Bushmills

★★★☆☆
1

Forty thousand hexagonal columns thrust up from a volcanic eruption 60 million years ago, UNESCO-listed in 1986. Legend says Finn McCool built the causeway to go challenge a Scottish giant. The geological truth is more brutal: basalt that fractured as it cooled. Allow two hours including the National Trust visitor centre. Walk from Bushmills if you want to save the parking fee, it's 30 minutes on the red trail.

⏱ 150 min
2

Wind slaps the planks of the bridge, 30 metres above the Atlantic. Salmon fishermen have strung rope bridges to the islet for over 350 years, the first documented one dates from 1755. Fishing stopped in 2002, not enough fish left. The current structure was tested to ten tonnes in 2000, so breathe. Online booking mandatory, ticket £15 adult, 20-minute walk from the car park.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

World's oldest licensed distillery, Old Bushmills has held its licence since 1608. The tour ends with a tasting of three whiskeys, including Black Bush. Allow 1h, booking advised at weekends. If you're driving, the tasting can be taken as miniatures. Shop decently stocked for gifts.

⏱ 60 min
$$$★9.5Bushmills Townhouse

Derry

★★★☆☆
1

Ireland's only city with intact ramparts, Derry is best explored from the top. The walls were built between 1613 and 1619 to protect English and Scottish settlers during the Plantation of Ulster, financed by London guilds—hence the official name Londonderry. 1.5 kilometres of circuit, 12 to 35 feet high. They've never been breached despite three sieges. The complete walk takes 1h, views over the Bogside and the Foyle.

⏱ 60 min
2

On 5 January 1969, after a night of riots, Liam Hillen scrawls on the gable of 33 Lecky Road: 'You Are Now Entering Free Derry'. John 'Caker' Casey repaints it neatly in black letters a few months later. Slogan inspired by Berkeley sit-ins. The street was demolished in 1975 but the wall was kept in the middle of a junction. In front, the memorial to the 1981 hunger strikers. Site of the annual Bloody Sunday march on 30 January.

⏱ 30 min
3

Twelve giant murals, painted gable by gable by The Bogside Artists collective from 1994 onwards. Bloody Sunday is everywhere: Father Daly waving a white handkerchief, the portrait of Bernadette Devlin, the faces of the fourteen dead of 30 January 1972. British paras opened fire on civil rights marchers. The Petrol Bomber mural has become a global icon. Walk rather than cab, the neighbourhood is calm.

⏱ 75 min
4

No staging, no pathos. The Museum of Free Derry, in Glenfada Park, presents the archives of the nationalist neighbourhood from 1968 to 1972: October 1968 marches, Battle of the Bogside, Bloody Sunday, Operation Motorman. Objects, photos, testimony from victims' families. Allow 1h30, ticket £8. Open Sunday afternoons only June to October, check before.

⏱ 90 min
5

Volunteer guides tell you the story of locks and keys at the Chapter House Museum. St Columb's Cathedral was consecrated in 1633, the first Anglican cathedral built after the Reformation in the British Isles. During the 1689 siege, lead from the spire was melted for bullets. A mortar shell in the porch still contains the surrender terms refused by the besieged. Free entry, suggested donation £5.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

For dinner on Bishop Street, Browns in Town is a reliable local address, modern Irish cooking without fuss. Expect £25 to £35 per person. Peadar O'Donnell's pub for live trad music in the evening, free, ten-minute walk.

⏱ 120 min
$$$★9.8Larchmount House B&B
🚗Drive to next stop: 110 km · 1h45

Kesh

★★☆☆☆
1

Sculpture wrongly called Janus, because the Boa Island statue doesn't have two faces on one head but two complete figures back to back. Dated between the Iron Age and early Irish Christianity (400-800 AD). 73 cm tall, arms crossed, tongue stuck out. Archaeologist Dorothy Lowry-Corry publicised it in 1933. The island's name comes from Inis Badhbha, island of the war goddess. Mysterious and genuinely solitary, almost never anyone on site.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Waterlogged field, small rusty iron cross marks the entrance. Caldragh cemetery is still in use, keep your voice down. No information panel, no shop, no bin. Wear shoes that accept mud, especially in spring. Count time to and from the road, 15 minutes. Free, accessible year-round.

⏱ 30 min

Drumcliff

★★★★☆
1

Yeats wrote his own epitaph in 1938, four months before dying in Roquebrune-Cap-Martin. 'Cast a cold eye on life, on death. Horseman, pass by'. He'd asked for a simple stone, no marble, no conventional phrase. His remains returned to Drumcliff in 1948, beneath Ben Bulben which he sang so often. The 2015 controversy over the authenticity of the remains was never settled. Modest grave, accessible year-round, cafe and shop on site.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

At the north exit of the cemetery, look up: Ben Bulben cuts out in a broken plateau over the Atlantic, like an ocean liner of stone. Approach walk from Luke's Bridge if you have two hours and good boots, otherwise the viewpoint from the N15 suffices. Fickle weather even in summer, bring a fleece.

⏱ 30 min

Trim

★★★☆☆
1

More impressive than it looks from the road. Trim Castle covers 30,000 m², Ireland's largest Cambro-Norman fortress. Hugh de Lacy began the keep in 1174, his son Walter finished it in 1224. The twenty-angled cruciform keep is unique in Norman architecture. Mel Gibson turned the place into medieval York for Braveheart in 1995. Access to the keep is guided tour only for safety reasons, allow 1h.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Right next to the castle, the medieval bridge crosses the Boyne. Lunch break at Franzini's on the riverbank, decent terrace in summer. Expect €25 to €30 per person, simple food well done. Trim has a high concentration of medieval buildings, thirty-minute walk through the centre if weather cooperates.

⏱ 75 min

Dublin

★★★★★
1

Arrival in Dublin to return the car and catch the return flight. If you have the evening, Chapter One or Mr Fox for dinner, two Michelin-starred at reasonable prices (menu around €95). For something simpler, The Brazen Head claims to be Ireland's oldest pub (1198 according to them), atmosphere and trad music in the evening. DUB airport 20 minutes by taxi from the centre.
