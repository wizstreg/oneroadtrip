Tottori

★★★★☆
1

The Tottori Sand Dunes stretch 16 km from east to west and 2.4 km from north to south—they're the largest coastal dunes accessible to visitors in Japan, and the only ones designated a National Natural Monument. They were born over 100,000 years ago: sand from the Chugoku Mountains, carried by the Sendai River to the Sea of Japan, was pushed back to shore by currents and winds that sculpted this ever-shifting landscape. Climb up to Umanose—the 'horse's back'—a ridge that peaks at 47 meters and from which the sea and dunes extend in every direction. Bring a hat and water: in summer, the sand reaches 50°C.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Sand Museum, right on the edge of the dunes, is the world's only museum entirely devoted to sand sculptures—and it's been housed in a permanent building since 2012. Each year, world-renowned sculptors create monumental works on a different theme (in 2026, Spain). What makes the place fascinating is that everything will be destroyed in January to start over from scratch. Plan on 45 minutes to an hour to tour the exhibits.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Sandboarding and paragliding are the two flagship activities at the dunes—operators offer sessions on-site, including for beginners. Since the sand is soft, falls are harmless. If you prefer contemplation over action, take the chairlift to the Sakyu Center observation platform: the view of the coast and waves of sand is striking, especially in late afternoon when the raking light draws shadows along the ridges.

⏱ 90 min

In winter (from mid-December to March), snow covers the dunes and transforms them into a landscape of snow-covered ridges that's absolutely unique. There are very few visitors, and the undulations of sand beneath the snow create patterns you won't see anywhere else. If you're there during this period, don't miss the nighttime illuminations in mid-December.

⏱ 60 min

Kurayoshi

★★★☆☆
1

The Utsubuki-Tamagawa district of Kurayoshi is one of Japan's Important Preservation Districts for Groups of Traditional Buildings—about a hundred merchant houses and warehouses built between the Edo period and the Taisho era line the small Tamagawa River. The visual signature of the place: white walls on top, cedar planks blackened by the sun below, and red-brick sekishu tiles imported from Iwami for their resistance to frost. The main street Honmachi-dori and the warehouses along the river form two distinct atmospheres—plan at least an hour to get lost in both. Red and black carp glide under the small stone bridges.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Makita Residence—also known as Kurayoshi Yodoya—is the country house of one of Osaka's greatest merchants from the Edo period, the House of Yodoya. The fact that such a figure maintained a foothold in Kurayoshi says much about the town's commercial importance at the time. It's open to visitors free of charge.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The 15 red tile pavilions (Akagawara, numbered 1 to 15) now house local product shops, cafés, and artisan workshops—notably sake and soy sauce producers whose buildings haven't changed since the Meiji era. This is also where you'll find Kurayoshi Gasuri, a traditional patterned textile that artisans still weave by hand. A good reason to push open a few doors.

⏱ 60 min

If you pass through in spring, Utsubuki Park at the foot of Mount Utsubuki (204 m) is one of Japan's hundred most beautiful hanami sites, with more than 4,000 cherry trees and 40,000 azaleas. The rest of the year, the path that climbs to the summit offers a lovely view of the town and its red roofs.

⏱ 45 min

Yasugi

★★★☆☆
1

The Adachi Museum of Art, opened in 1970 by Yasugi-native entrepreneur Zenko Adachi, houses the world's largest collection of works by Yokoyama Taikan—120 pieces on permanent display—as well as works by Takeuchi Seiho, Kawai Gyokudo, and Uemura Shoen. But what makes this museum truly exceptional is that the founder considered his garden a living painting: 165,000 m² of gardens in six different styles (karesansui, pines and white gravel, moss, pond…) form a composition that you contemplate from the museum windows as you would look at a framed painting. Don't miss the Kikaku waterfall, built in 1978 to evoke Taikan's painting 'Nachi Falls.'

⏱ 120 min

Activities

The Adachi Museum garden has been ranked number 1 among Japanese gardens without interruption since 2003 by the American magazine Sukiya Living, which specializes in Japanese gardens—beating out Kyoto's famous Katsura Imperial Villa garden every single year. It also holds three stars in the Michelin Green Guide. What makes this ranking particularly striking: the Adachi gardens are not accessible on foot—they can only be viewed from inside the museum, like ever-changing paintings according to the hour and season. In autumn, the red maples transform the karesansui into something unreal.

⏱ 60 min

The free shuttle from Yasugi Station (every 30 minutes, 20-minute ride) eliminates parking concerns. If you have time after the museum, the town of Yasugi is also the region's ceramics center—a few workshops offer demonstrations.

Sakaiminato

★★☆☆☆
1

Mizuki Shigeru Road runs from Sakaiminato Station and extends 800 meters to the memorial museum—along this route, 177 bronze yokai statues welcome passersby from pedestals of all sizes. The road was inaugurated in 1993 with only 23 statues; the first year, 20,000 visitors came; the following year, 280,000. Mizuki Shigeru (1922-2015), a Sakaiminato native, lost his left arm during World War II and didn't achieve success as a manga artist until age 43 with GeGeGe no Kitaro (1965), adapted into anime six times. In the evening, the statues are illuminated and yokai silhouettes are projected onto the ground—the atmosphere changes dramatically.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Mizuki Shigeru Memorial Museum, opened in 2003, presents approximately 700 original works by the manga artist, rotated every six months. Permanent exhibitions mix original drawings, resin character statues, and video screens to immerse visitors in the world of yokai. Audio guides are available in five languages.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The yokai stamp rally is the town's game: pick up a booklet at the station tourist information center, and collect stamps at shops and sites all along Mizuki Shigeru Road. At the end, bring back the completed booklet to receive a gift. On-site, restaurants offer dishes with evocative names (Kitaro onigiri, Medama-Oyaji seafood bowl)—Sakaiminato is first and foremost a fishing port: the fresh fish here is remarkable.

Matsue

★★★★☆
1

Matsue Castle is one of the twelve original keeps still standing in Japan—and one of five designated National Treasures (along with Himeji, Matsumoto, Hikone, and Inuyama). Built between 1607 and 1611 by Lord Horio Yoshiharu, founder of Matsue, it's nicknamed Chidori-jo ('Plover Castle') for the shape of its eaves that recall the bird's outstretched wings. Its dark color comes from a dye made from unripe persimmons macerated for five years. From the Tengu-no-Ma at the top—a room open to the four winds—the 360° view over Lake Shinji and the city is the best in the region. The stairs are steep and the steps uneven: wear closed-toe shoes.

⏱ 90 min
2

Hinomisaki Lighthouse, a 20-minute drive west of Izumo Taisha, is Japan's tallest stone lighthouse: 43.65 meters high, or 63 meters above sea level. Built in 1903, it's still in service and appears on the IALA's list of the world's 100 historic lighthouses. From the 163 spiral steps, on clear days you'll glimpse the Oki Islands on the northern horizon—once a place of exile for political prisoners.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Horikawa moat boat cruise around the castle lasts about 50 minutes and passes under bridges so low that the boat's roof automatically lowers. In winter, kotatsu (heated tables) built into the boats transform the cruise into a particularly pleasant experience. Combined castle + boat tickets are available—ask at the castle entrance.

⏱ 60 min

Shiomi Nawate Street runs along the north side of the castle and preserves several samurai residences from the Edo period. This is also where you'll find the house of Lafcadio Hearn (Koizumi Yakumo), the Irish-Greek writer who settled in Matsue in 1890 and became one of the first Western authors to document Japanese culture and ghosts. The sunset over Lake Shinji from the banks is selected among Japan's 100 most beautiful sunsets.

⏱ 60 min

Izumo

★★★☆☆
1

Izumo Taisha is one of Japan's two great Shinto shrines along with Ise Jingu—and the only one whose exact founding date is unknown, so ancient is it. The Kojiki, Japan's oldest chronicle (712), already mentions the site. The principal deity, Okuninushi no Okami, is the god of marriage and destinies—which is why you clap four times here instead of two: twice for yourself, twice for the person you love or are seeking. The current prayer hall (Haiden) was rebuilt in 1981; the great main hall (Honden, 24 m high) dates from 1744 in the Taisha-zukuri style, the oldest Shinto architectural style in Japan. Take time to walk along the two rows of pines on the approach path: the central corridor is reserved for the gods.

⏱ 90 min
2

The giant shimenawa hanging in the Kaguraden (the sacred dance hall) is Japan's largest ritual rope: 13.5 meters long, 5.2 tons. It's handmade by artisans from Shimane Prefecture, and replaced approximately every six years. Its twist is reversed compared to all other shrines in Japan—a sign of Izumo's very ancient independence from the dominant Shinto system. The small rabbit statues scattered throughout the grounds recall the legend of the white rabbit of Inaba that Okuninushi saved, an act that earned him the love of Princess Yakamihime.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Every year in October (the 10th lunar month), all the gods of Japan are said to gather at Izumo for their annual assembly—which is why this month is called Kannazuki ('month without gods') everywhere in the country, except at Izumo where they say Kamiarizuki ('month with gods'). If you're there during this period, the atmosphere is particularly striking. Outside of October, the approach to the shrine—a 700-meter-long street—is lined with Izumo soba shops, the local noodles served stacked in three superimposed bowls: a specialty not to be missed.

⏱ 75 min

The Shimane Museum of Ancient Izumo, right next to the shrine, presents reconstructions of what the Honden was like in medieval times—it would have reached 48 meters high, making it the tallest wooden structure in Japan at the time. In 2000, excavations uncovered immense pillars formed from trios of logs bound by iron hoops, confirming the plausibility of these extraordinary dimensions. Note: the museum is closed for renovation until September 2026.

⏱ 60 min

Hamada

★★★☆☆
1

The Shimane Aquas Aquarium in Hamada is the only aquarium in western Japan to host belugas—these white cetaceans nicknamed 'sea canaries' for their varied vocalizations. The bubble ring shows, where belugas create and play with air rings, are the most renowned. The aquarium also presents some 400 marine species. Plan 1.5 to 2 hours on-site.

Okayama

Korakuen Garden, on the banks of the Asahi River facing Okayama Castle, is one of the three great Edo-period gardens in Japan, along with Kenrokuen in Kanazawa and Kairakuen in Mito. Bitchu Matsuyama Castle is the only mountain castle among the twelve original keeps still standing in Japan, and the highest: it perches atop Mount Gagyu at 430 meters altitude. Hiroshima

★★★★★
1

The Genbaku Dome is the only building still standing near the hypocenter of the August 6, 1945 bomb—detonated at 8:15 in the morning just 160 meters away. Designed by Czech architect Jan Letzel and inaugurated in 1915 as the Hiroshima Prefectural Commercial Exhibition Hall, the building wasn't destroyed because the explosion's pressure came directly from above, and its vertical columns resisted the downward force. The city decided in 1966 to preserve it indefinitely as a memorial. Inscribed as a UNESCO site in 1996, it's not open to the interior—but from the banks of the Motoyasu, its skeleton of metal and burnt brick produces a striking effect.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Peace Memorial Museum, at the other end of the park, opened in 1955. The 2019 renovation reoriented the exhibitions toward individual stories: personal effects of victims with their names, photos, and stories, a watch stopped at 8:15, a charred schoolboy's jacket. This is not an easy museum, but it's necessary. Allow at least 1.5 hours—audio guides are available in French.

⏱ 90 min
3

At the center of Peace Memorial Park, the Cenotaph—a stone arch sheltering the names of all known victims—is aligned with the Flame of Peace (lit August 1, 1964, it will not be extinguished until all nuclear weapons have been eliminated) and the Dome, so that all three are visible in enfilade. Take time to walk through the park rather than in a straight line.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In the evening, stroll along the Motoyasu—this river that borders the park was one of the only escape routes on August 6, 1945. Each August 6, hundreds of paper lanterns float on it at nightfall for the souls of the victims. Hiroshima is a resolutely lively city: try an okonomiyaki Hiroshima-style (layered in strata, very different from the Osaka version) in the Hondori covered arcades.

Miyajima

★★★★★
1

Itsukushima Shrine, built in 593 then reconstructed in its current form in 1168 by Lord Taira no Kiyomori, is both a National Treasure and a UNESCO World Heritage site (since 1996). Its 56 vermilion wooden structures, connected by corridors totaling 275 meters, are built on stilts above the water so as not to 'defile' the sacred island. At high tide, the whole appears to float. The corridor floors have slightly spaced slats to let tidal water pass through without warping the structure. Check tide times before coming: the scene is radically different depending on water level.

⏱ 90 min
2

The great torii of Itsukushima—16.6 meters high, 60 tons—is the current version built in 1875, the eighth in the shrine's history. It's made of camphor wood, chosen for its resistance to seawater, and stands upright solely by its own weight without cement. At low tide, you can walk to its base and touch the pillars. Tradition held that one should pass through it by boat to enter the shrine.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The hike to the summit of Mount Misen (535 m), the island's sacred mountain, is the best way to see Miyajima beyond the shrine crowds. From the summit, the view over the Seto Inland Sea and its islands is extraordinary. Allow 1.5 to 2 hours ascending the forest trails, or take the ropeway to mid-height. In autumn, the island's red foliage makes it one of western Japan's most beautiful koyo spots.

⏱ 180 min

Spending the night on the island is highly recommended—tour groups leave on the last afternoon ferry, and the island becomes peaceful. Very early in the morning, you'll have the shrine and the deer (hundreds roam freely, having done so for centuries) almost to yourself. The five-story pagoda of Senjokaku, built in 1587, merits a stop.

Iwakuni

1

Iwakuni's Kintaikyo Bridge is one of Japan's three famous bridges—built in 1673 by the region's third lord, Kikkawa Hiroyoshi, who sought to solve a recurring problem: floods of the Nishiki River regularly destroyed any ordinary bridge. A 3-minute ropeway ascends to Iwakuni Castle, rebuilt in 1962—the view of the bridge and valley from the summit is the day's best photo.

Yamaguchi

★★★☆☆
1

Rurikoji Temple's five-story pagoda is one of Japan's National Treasures and one of the country's three most beautiful pagodas—built in 1442 in memory of Lord Ouchi Yoshihiro. Its 31.2 meters are covered in hinoki cypress bark in an elegant style that marries Japanese aesthetics with some continental Buddhist touches. Yamaguchi calls itself the 'Kyoto of the West'—under the Ouchi family in the 15th and 16th centuries, the city was a cultural center rivaling Kyoto, and Francis Xavier preached here in 1550.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Akiyoshidai karst plateau, 45 minutes from Yamaguchi, is Japan's largest karst: 45 km² of grasslands dotted with white limestone rocks, remnants of coral reefs formed 300 million years ago. Below lie the Akiyoshido caves—with 10 km of known galleries, it's East Asia's largest cave. About 1 km of galleries is accessible to the public, with underground rivers, stalactites, and gigantic chambers.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

In the evening in Yamaguchi, the Yuda Onsen district offers street footbaths (ashiyu, free) and several traditional ryokans. Local legend tells that a white fox with injured paws came to heal in a natural spring—this is how Yuda's waters were supposedly discovered. The regional specialty is fugu (pufferfish) from Shimonoseki, 50 km away, which supplies the entire region.

⏱ 60 min

Nagato

★★☆☆☆
1

Motonosumi Shrine stands on the cliffs of Yamaguchi's north coast, facing the Sea of Japan. In 1955, a local fisherman dreamed that a white fox asked him to build a shrine on this promontory. The 123 vermilion torii that descend in a tunnel from the shrine to the cliff edge were erected progressively between 1987 and 1997. The contrast—brilliant red, cobalt sea, green cliff grasses—made the place world-famous, particularly after CNN ranked it among Japan's 31 most beautiful places in 2015. A unique feature: the offering box is fixed atop the first torii, 6 meters high. You must toss your coin so it falls inside—if it enters, your wish is granted. Arrive in the morning to avoid traffic jams on the narrow access road.

⏱ 75 min
2

Tsunoshima Bridge connects the mainland to Tsunoshima Island over 1,780 meters above the Sea of Japan—it's one of Japan's longest free bridges to an island, opened in 2000. Driving on this slightly curved bridge, with the turquoise sea on both sides, is one of the country's most photogenic experiences. The island itself has white sand beaches and a small white lighthouse at the end of a pine-lined path.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Just below Motonosumi Shrine, a fissure in the cliffs creates a striking natural phenomenon: the Ryugu no Shiofuki (the 'Dragon Palace's Spout'). Waves rush into the cavity and water sometimes shoots up to 30 meters high. The phenomenon is more spectacular in autumn and winter, during heavy seas. If you're lucky, a rainbow forms in the spray.

⏱ 45 min

The coastal region around Nagato is also known for its terraced rice fields facing the sea—from the Yuya terrace, the stepped plots descend to the shore. In the evening, squid fishing boats illuminate the sea offshore. The nearest airport is Yamaguchi-Ube (about 1.5 hours drive)—plan accordingly for the return trip.

⏱ 60 min
