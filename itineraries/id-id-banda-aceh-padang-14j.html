Medan

Indonesia's third-largest city, a colonial crossroads of Malay, Chinese and Indian cultures since the 19th century

★★★★☆
📍 Maimun Palace Medan📍 Masjid Raya Al Mashun Medan📍 Tjong A Fie Mansion Medan📍 Kesawan Street Medan
1

Maimun Palace (Istana Maimun) commands attention as soon as you reach central Medan with its bright yellow facade — this colour is not a decorative whim but the symbol of Malay royalty. Built between 1888 and 1891 on the orders of the Sultan of Deli, Ma'mun Al Rashid Perkasa Alamsyah, it covers 2,772 m² and contains 30 rooms spread over two floors. Its architect, the Dutch captain Theodoor van Erp, blended Mughal, Spanish and Italian styles — look for the Moorish arches, wooden terraces and 'bamboo shoot' motifs on the roof symbolising fertility. The throne room is the only section open to the public: you can try on traditional Malay costumes there.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Grand Mosque Al Mashun, just opposite Maimun Palace, was commissioned by the same sultan in August 1906 and completed in September 1909. Its plan is octagonal with wings facing the four cardinal points — a design not unlike Maghrebi mosques. What is less well known: its construction was partly funded by Tjong A Fie, Medan's wealthy Hakka merchant who was not Muslim. Enter before the midday prayer to admire the high ceilings and stained glass windows.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Tjong A Fie Mansion, a ten-minute walk away in the Kesawan district, is one of the best-preserved merchant houses in Southeast Asia. Tjong A Fie arrived from China in 1875 aged 18 with a few silver coins sewn into his belt. By 1911, he was the recognised leader of Medan's Chinese community, owner of plantations, banks and the Medan–Belawan railway company. His house, completed around 1900, blends Sino-Portuguese, Victorian and Malay styles: 35 rooms, hand-painted floor tiles imported from Italy, and a ballroom on the upper floor. A guided tour is recommended — the man's story is more fascinating than the architecture.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Kesawan district, Medan's historic main street, is the best place for dinner on your first evening. Nineteenth-century Chinese trading houses line the streets with their arcaded facades. Look for a warung serving mie goreng or bihun Medan — Medan's cuisine is one of the most renowned in Sumatra, shaped by the Malays, Batak, Chinese and Tamil Indians present since colonial times.

Medan

Indonesia's third-largest city, a colonial crossroads of Malay, Chinese and Indian cultures since the 19th century

★★★★☆
📍 Museum Negeri Sumatera Utara Medan📍 Sri Mariamman Temple Medan📍 Lapangan Merdeka Medan📍 Pos Bloc Medan
1

The Museum Negeri Sumatera Utara, housed in a building modelled on traditional Batak architecture, offers the best introduction to all the cultures you will encounter on this trip. The collections cover ancient Hindu civilisations of central Java, Batak Toba ulos textiles, gondang musical instruments, ritual kris daggers and archaeological pieces from the sultanate era. Allow 1h30 to see it properly.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Sri Mariamman Hindu temple, steps away from Kesawan, is Medan's oldest Hindu temple — dating from 1884. Its Dravidian style, typical of Tamil Nadu, with a gateway tower (gopuram) adorned with colourful deities, contrasts with the neighbouring mosques. It bears witness to the Tamil workers recruited from the nineteenth century by Dutch companies for their plantations. Ceremonies are held on Friday mornings.

⏱ 45 min
3

Medan's town hall (Pos Bloc), built in 1908 in an ornate neoclassical style, is one of the best-preserved colonial buildings in the city. Its clock tower was donated in 1913 by Tjong A Fie — him again. Stroll around Lapangan Merdeka, the large central park, to take in the Dutch colonial architecture that frames the city.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Explore Pasar Petisah market, Medan's liveliest covered market, to stock up on provisions and sample local specialities before heading into the forest: fresh durian (if in season), bika ambon (rice flour and coconut cake) and sirup marquisa, the passion fruit syrup typical of the Karo Highlands. It is also the cheapest place to shop before Bukit Lawang.

Bukit Lawang

Trekking village on the Bohorok River, gateway to the primary forests of Gunung Leuser

★★★☆☆
📍 Bukit Lawang village Bohorok River📍 Gunung Leuser National Park entrance Bukit Lawang📍 Bat Cave Bukit Lawang📍 Bohorok River Bukit Lawang
1

Bukit Lawang is a village of 3,000 people perched on the Bohorok River, 78 km west of Medan. The road passes through rubber and oil palm plantations — this parade of monoculture contrasts starkly with the primary forest that begins here. As soon as you arrive, settle in on the riverside: bamboo cabins perched on the banks offer a direct view into the jungle and its gibbon concerts in the late afternoon. Book your trek this evening through a park-certified operator.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Bat Cave, a twenty-minute walk from the village, houses a spectacular colony. Arrive around 5:30 pm for the fly-out: thousands of bats emerge in spiralling columns at dusk to hunt. It is the best foretaste of Gunung Leuser National Park's biodiversity — 7,927 km² of tropical forest sheltering the Sumatran orangutan, tiger, Sumatran rhinoceros and elephant.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Walk through the village in the evening to choose your trekking guide. Bukit Lawang has been an eco-tourism hub since two Swiss nationals founded an orangutan rehabilitation centre here in 1973 — the centre has since closed and the animals released. Prioritise guides who are members of the national park's official local association: they follow safety rules (stay at least 10 metres away, never feed the animals). A one-day trek is enough to see semi-wild orangutans. Two days with an overnight in the forest offers a significantly deeper experience.

⏱ 60 min

Bukit Lawang

Trekking village on the Bohorok River, gateway to the primary forests of Gunung Leuser

★★★☆☆
📍 Gunung Leuser National Park orangutan trek📍 Bohorok River tubing Bukit Lawang📍 Prevab Research Station Gunung Leuser📍 jungle camp Bukit Lawang overnight
1

Gunung Leuser National Park (TNGL), a UNESCO World Heritage Site along with Bukit Barisan Selatan and Kerinci Seblat parks under the 'Tropical Rainforest Heritage of Sumatra' designation, is one of only two remaining habitats of the Sumatran orangutan (Pongo abelii) on Earth. The park covers 7,927 km² — roughly 150 km long — with dipterocarp forests sheltering 130 mammal species. The park entrance is a five-minute walk from the village. Your guide teaches you to read the forest: orangutan nests in the trees, macaque tracks, gibbon calls announcing their territory in the early morning.

⏱ 360 min
2

The return trail follows the Bohorok River to a more accessible part of the park where semi-wild orangutans congregate — former residents of the rehabilitation centre founded in 1973, they are used to human presence but not domesticated. You can watch them building their nests in the trees in the late afternoon. Do not feed them and keep your bag closed: they are perfectly capable of opening it. Long-tailed macaques and Thomas leaf monkeys (endemic to Sumatra) are even easier to spot than orangutans.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

River tubing on the Bohorok is the post-trek tradition in Bukit Lawang — you sit on inflatable inner tubes tied together and ride 45 minutes of rapids between jungle cliffs. It is how the guides bring their groups back to the village after the trek.

Berastagi

Highland retreat at 1,300 m, at the foot of the active Sibayak, heart of Batak Karo culture

★★☆☆☆
📍 Mount Sibayak Berastagi📍 Mount Sinabung Berastagi viewpoint📍 Berastagi fruit market📍 Lau Debuk-Debuk hot springs Berastagi
1

Berastagi sits at 1,300 m altitude in the Karo Highlands — after the humid heat of Bukit Lawang, the change in air is immediate. The mountain road offers views of the cabbage, tomato and passion fruit plantations that have made the region's agricultural reputation. Settle in and head out to see Mount Sinabung from the viewpoints north of town: this 2,460 m volcano is one of Indonesia's most active and its plumes of smoke are visible in clear weather from Berastagi. It erupted in 2010 after 400 years of dormancy, then again in 2013–2014 with significant damage to nearby villages.

⏱ 75 min
2

Berastagi's fruit market, along the main street, is the best in the region: marquisa (passion fruit), mountain strawberries, pepino, salak and above all the apples grown on Sibayak's slopes. Batak Karo vendors wear the sarong and traditional woven hat. It is also the place to buy the fresh flowers that have made the town famous — a cabbage-shaped monument was even erected in 1972 to celebrate the area's agricultural wealth.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Plan your Sibayak ascent for tomorrow morning, and spend the afternoon soaking in the Lau Debuk-Debuk hot springs, 5 km north of Berastagi towards the volcano's geothermal sources. These small pools heated by Sibayak's underground activity are frequented by locals unwinding after work in the fields. The water smells faintly of sulphur but is perfect for recovering after the forest days.

Berastagi

Highland retreat at 1,300 m, at the foot of the active Sibayak, heart of Batak Karo culture

★★☆☆☆
📍 Gunung Sibayak crater trek Berastagi📍 Lingga Batak Karo village📍 Sipiso-Piso Waterfall Tongging📍 Taman Alam Lumbini Berastagi
1

Mount Sibayak (2,212 m) is Indonesia's most accessible volcano — its name comes from the Batak Karo word for 'King's Mountain'. It went extinct over a century ago (last eruption 1881) but remains geothermally active: as you approach the crater, you can hear the fumaroles hissing and smell the sulphur. The 3.5 km trail from the starting point north of Berastagi takes about 3 hours return. Set off between 5 and 6 am to reach the summit at sunrise — on a clear day you can see Sinabung smoking to the east and the Karo Highlands stretching to the horizon. Bring a jacket: it is chilly.

⏱ 210 min
2

Sipiso-Piso Waterfall, 24 km north of Berastagi near the village of Tongging, plunges 120 metres from the plateau down to Lake Toba stretching below. The lookout above the falls offers the first view of the lake — at this altitude, the immensity of the lake inside its ancient volcanic crater begins to reveal itself. Take the path down if you want to approach the base of the falls (allow 30 minutes for the descent).

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Detour to Lingga, a Batak Karo village a few kilometres northwest of Berastagi, to see traditional houses that are still inhabited. These large dwellings with curved saddle-back roofs, their facades decorated in red, black and white motifs representing sky, earth and the underworld, house several families. A local guide can explain how the space is organised and the social rules governing Karo communal life.

Tuk Tuk

Peninsula on Samosir Island in Lake Toba, centre of Christian Batak Toba culture

★★★☆☆
📍 Ferry Parapat Samosir Island Lake Toba📍 Tuk Tuk peninsula Samosir📍 Ambarita stone chairs Samosir📍 Tomok village Samosir
1

Lake Toba is the world's largest volcanic caldera — it formed during a cataclysmic eruption roughly 74,000 years ago that expelled 2,800 km³ of material and is thought to have caused a global cooling of several degrees. The lake measures 100 km long by 30 km wide, reaching a depth of 505 m. From Parapat, a ferry crosses in 30 minutes to Tuk-Tuk on the Samosir peninsula. Settle in on the lake side — guesthouses with direct lake access are plentiful and the view over the calm water ringed by forested hills is especially beautiful at sunset.

⏱ 60 min
2

The village of Ambarita, 2 km north of Tuk-Tuk, is famous for its stone chairs — an eighteenth-century Batak Toba court where kings delivered their verdicts. The accused sat on the small chair, the king on the larger one. According to tradition, those found guilty of serious crimes could be executed on the flat stones nearby. The staging is spectacular and local guides recount these stories with disarming matter-of-factness.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Rent a scooter for the evening and explore Samosir's east coast. The Batak Toba are predominantly Protestant Christians — a legacy of nineteenth-century German Lutheran missions. You will see churches in every village, amid Batak tombs painted white, red and yellow. Batak culture is one of Indonesia's most complex: patrilineal clans, marriage alliance systems, gondang music, hand-woven ulos cloth — an introduction tonight, and a deeper exploration tomorrow.

⏱ 90 min

Tuk Tuk

Peninsula on Samosir Island in Lake Toba, centre of Christian Batak Toba culture

★★★☆☆
📍 Pusuk Buhit Samosir sacred mountain📍 Bukit Holbung viewpoint Samosir📍 Pangururan Samosir island hot springs📍 Lake Toba swim kayak Tuk Tuk
1

Pusuk Buhit (1,978 m), on the isthmus connecting Samosir to the mainland, is the sacred mountain of the Batak Toba — according to their mythology, this is where the first ancestor of the Batak people lived. The climb on foot or by scooter to the first viewpoints is one of the most beautiful on Samosir: terraced rice paddies step down to the lake's edge, and on a clear day you can see the entire caldera. The sulphurous hot springs of Pangururan, at the foot of Pusuk Buhit, are heated by the caldera's volcanic activity.

⏱ 120 min
2

Bukit Holbung, a hill reachable by scooter from Tuk-Tuk, offers a 360° panorama of the lake, Samosir's hills and the mainland mountains. It is one of the island's least visited viewpoints — you will often find only a few grazing cows for company. Arrive in the early morning before the mist rises.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Swim in Lake Toba from your guesthouse or kayak to the small coves of the Tuk-Tuk peninsula. The water is fresh, calm and remarkably clean for such a large lake. Surface temperature is around 25–26°C. If you prefer to stay on land, most lakeside restaurants serve grilled ikan mas (golden carp) caught that morning — the local speciality to try with andaliman, a Sichuan pepper grown around the lake.

⏱ 120 min

Bukittinggi

Cultural capital of West Sumatra at 930 m, cradle of the Minangkabau people

★★★☆☆
📍 Jam Gadang clock tower Bukittinggi📍 Fort of Kock Bukittinggi📍 Ngarai Sianok canyon Bukittinggi📍 Japanese tunnels Lobang Jepang Bukittinggi
1

The day starts before dawn: catch the ferry from Tuk-Tuk back to Parapat (30 min) then allow 7 to 8 hours of driving to Bukittinggi via the Sumatran highlands. The road crosses landscapes of rice paddies, rubber plantations and roadside markets. Arrival in mid-afternoon. If you opt for an overnight bus from Parapat (departing around 8 pm), you arrive in Bukittinggi at about 6 am and gain a full day of sightseeing.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Jam Gadang has dominated Bukittinggi's central square since 1926 — it was a gift from the Dutch Queen Wilhelmina to her city secretary. This 26 m tower was built without any metal framework or cement: a mixture of limestone, egg whites and sand. Its four dials display the number 4 as 'IIII' rather than 'IV' — a quirk it shares with Big Ben in London, because the mechanisms were made by the same German manufacturer, Bernard Vortmann, in Recklinghausen. The roof has changed three times: European style during the Dutch era, Japanese style under the 1942–1945 occupation, Minangkabau style (the buffalo horns) after independence.

⏱ 60 min
3

Sianok Canyon (Ngarai Sianok), at the southwestern edge of town, plunges 100 to 120 m over 15 km. It is not a river-carved canyon but a tectonic fault — the same fault that runs the entire length of Sumatra. From the Panorama Park lookout, the view is vertiginous: sheer cliffs fall onto rice paddies crossed by a winding river, framed by forests and hills. Early morning, the mist filling the canyon is spectacular.

⏱ 75 min
4

The Japanese tunnels (Lobang Jepang), dug 40 m beneath Sianok Canyon by forced Indonesian labourers during the 1942–1945 occupation, extend for 1,470 m. This military network served as a headquarters, weapons depot and shelter for Japanese soldiers of the 25th Army based in Bukittinggi. The interior has explanatory panels on the occupation. It is dark and cool — bring a jacket.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Dine at the evening markets around the Jam Gadang for your first immersion in Minangkabau cuisine. Beef rendang (meat braised for hours in coconut milk and spices until caramelised), sate Padang (skewers with thick spicy sauce), gulai ayam (chicken curry with turmeric) — this cooking is the foundation of the nasi Padang you will find everywhere in the region.

Bukittinggi

Cultural capital of West Sumatra at 930 m, cradle of the Minangkabau people

★★★☆☆
📍 Koto Gadang silversmith village Bukittinggi📍 Pagaruyung Palace Batusangkar📍 Museum Rumah Kelahiran Bung Hatta Bukittinggi📍 Pasar Atas Bukittinggi market
1

Pagaruyung Palace, 40 km southeast of Bukittinggi in the Batusangkar plain, is the reconstruction of the Minangkabau royal palace that burned down in 2007 after being struck by lightning — the seventeenth-century original had itself already burned and been rebuilt. This monument is an architectural statement: three tiers of upswept roofs evoking buffalo horns, each level decorated with floral patterns painted in red, black and gold. The Minangkabau are matrilineal — property and family names pass through the women, a social organisation that is extremely rare at this scale.

⏱ 90 min
2

Koto Gadang, Bukittinggi's silversmith village, is reachable on foot from Sianok Canyon via a trail or by minibus. This village has produced filigree silver jewellery for generations — you can watch artisans at work in their family workshops. The technique of twisted and soldered silver wire is unique to this region. It is also the birthplace of several figures of Indonesian independence.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Explore Pasar Atas, Bukittinggi's large daily market, to buy batik textiles, embroidery, dried chillies and spices. It is one of the busiest markets in West Sumatra — vendors come from across the regency with their agricultural produce. Then head down to Pasar Bawah for artisanal souvenirs and Chinatown for traditional Chinese medicines.

⏱ 120 min

Stop 11

Harau Valley

300 m granite canyon dubbed Indonesia's Yosemite, with seven waterfalls and 300 climbing routes

★★★★☆
📍 Harau Valley Lembah Harau waterfalls📍 Sarasah Bunta Waterfall Harau📍 Harau Valley rock climbing cliffs📍 Harau Valley rice fields panorama
1

Harau Valley (Lembah Harau) reveals itself gradually as you leave Payakumbuh: granite and red sandstone cliffs surge up 80 to 300 m, encircling chessboard rice paddies of a luminous green. The rocks are 40 million years old according to geologists, and the valley is part of an official geopark. Dubbed 'Indonesia's Yosemite' by the global climbing community, Harau has 300 routes on its vertical walls. Even without climbing, the sight of the cliffs at the end of the day when the light turns them red is reason enough for the detour.

⏱ 90 min
2

Sarasah Bunta waterfall, the most spectacular on the site, tumbles directly from the edge of a cliff into a pool surrounded by moss-covered boulders. It was officially inaugurated in 1926 by a Dutch colonial administrator — a plaque still testifies to this in the rock. Four rivers cross the valley, feeding seven catalogued waterfalls. Allow 5 minutes' walk between each.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Walk or ride a scooter through the valley in the late afternoon to watch farmers at work in the rice paddies. Water buffaloes still plough the fields with their drivers — a scene of Minangkabau agricultural life barely touched by time. Rumah gadang houses with buffalo-horn roofs dot the valley floor. Macaques and gibbons still live in the forests along the cliff edges.

Padang

Capital of West Sumatra, birthplace of Nasi Padang and spicy Minangkabau cuisine exported worldwide

★★★☆☆
📍 Pantai Air Manis Padang Malin Kundang stone📍 Museum Adityawarman Padang📍 Muaro Port old town Padang📍 Siti Nurbaya Bridge Padang
1

Pantai Air Manis (Freshwater Beach), 20 minutes from central Padang, is the setting for West Sumatra's most famous legend: the tale of Malin Kundang, the ungrateful son who disowned his poor mother after becoming rich. Legend has it that his mother cursed him and his ship ran aground — the rock formation on the beach resembling a prostrate man is said to be his petrified remains. The beach itself is pleasant in the morning before the crowds arrive.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Museum Adityawarman, in a building modelled on a large-scale gadang house, brings together the ethnographic collections of the entire West Sumatra province: Minangkabau textiles, gold ornaments, musical instruments, traditional weapons. The museum is named after Adityawarman, a fourteenth-century ruler who reigned over West Sumatra and whose Sanskrit inscriptions are among the oldest on the island. Allow 1h30.

⏱ 90 min
3

The old port of Muaro and its colonial quarter are the most authentic spot in Padang to feel the city's trading history. Eighteenth-century Dutch warehouses line the Batang Arau — some are still used to store cinnamon and coffee awaiting shipment. The Siti Nurbaya Bridge, lit up at night, links the old port to the hill where the tomb of the heroine from the classic Indonesian novel of the same name is said to lie.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Lunch at a genuine rumah makan Padang to experience the hidang service in its place of origin: the moment you sit down, the waiter arrives with arms stacked with 12 to 14 plates which he spreads across the table. You only pay for what you touch. Rendang, gulai ikan, sate Padang, daun singkong — each dish is cooked from family recipes handed down for generations. This is the original version of what you find in Padang restaurants worldwide.

⏱ 90 min

Banda Aceh

City that survived the 9.3 earthquake and 2004 tsunami that killed 160,000 Acehnese

★★★☆☆
📍 Baiturrahman Grand Mosque Banda Aceh📍 Aceh Tsunami Museum Banda Aceh📍 PLTD Apung ship Banda Aceh📍 Gunongan Park Banda Aceh
1

The Baiturrahman Grand Mosque is the first place to see in Banda Aceh — and its condensed history tells that of the entire city. Burned by the Dutch during their 1873 attack (in which they lost their commander, General Köhler, killed by an Acehnese sniper), it was rebuilt from 1879 by the same occupiers as a gesture of reconciliation. Completed in 1881, with Indian-style black domes and 35 m white minarets, it remained standing during the 26 December 2004 tsunami while the buildings around it collapsed — hundreds of survivors took shelter by climbing onto the domes to escape the waves.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Aceh Tsunami Museum, designed by architect Ridwan Kamil (later governor of West Java), is a 2,500 m² structure over four levels whose long curved facades are covered in geometric low-reliefs. The entrance takes you through the Lorong Tsunami — a dark corridor where water streams down both sides producing a low rumble. Then comes the hall of names: 40,000 victims from Aceh province inscribed on the walls. The 26 December 2004 earthquake reached 9.3 on the Richter scale — one of the most powerful ever recorded. The total death toll across the Indian Ocean exceeded 230,000 in twelve countries.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

In the evening, visit the Gunongan, a set of pleasure baths built in the seventeenth century for the queen of Sultan Iskandar Muda — terraces stepping down in a flower shape on the banks of the Aceh River. It is one of the few remaining architectural traces of the Sultanate of Aceh, one of Southeast Asia's most powerful thalassocracies in the seventeenth century, which controlled the pepper trade and the entrance to the Strait of Malacca.

⏱ 60 min

Banda Aceh

City that survived the 9.3 earthquake and 2004 tsunami that killed 160,000 Acehnese

★★★☆☆
📍 PLTD Apung ship museum Banda Aceh📍 Lampuuk Beach Banda Aceh📍 Aceh State Museum Banda Aceh📍 Blang Padang Park Banda Aceh
1

The PLTD Apung 1 is a 2,600-tonne power-generating vessel that the 26 December 2004 tsunami tore from its dock and carried 3 km inland before depositing it on top of two houses in a residential neighbourhood — whose occupants are probably still underneath. This is not a conventional museum but an involuntary monument to chaos. You climb on deck to grasp the scale: this metal colossus sitting among ordinary houses is the best measure of the tsunami's hydraulic force. A semi-circular wall with bas-reliefs illustrating the scene surrounds the site.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Aceh State Museum (Museum Negeri Aceh) preserves the sultanate's historical collections: sets of weapons, royal costumes, musical instruments, gold jewellery. A section is devoted to inscriptions in Old Malay and Arabic that bear witness to Aceh's role as the first region of Indonesia to convert to Islam, as early as the thirteenth century.

⏱ 75 min
3

Lampuuk Beach, 15 km southwest of Banda Aceh, is the most beautiful on the Acehnese coast: 3 km of white sand facing the Indian Ocean. Before the tsunami, it was a popular beach. The 2004 wave destroyed everything — the site has been entirely rebuilt with international aid. Several mosques in the area survived, including the Rahmatullah Mosque in Lhoknga, often photographed standing amid the ruins — an iconic image of the disaster. On this final day, Banda Aceh is 20 km away and Sultan Iskandar Muda Airport is within easy reach.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Final free morning in Banda Aceh to buy local specialities: kopi Aceh, Indonesia's finest coffee roasted the old-fashioned way in wajan (iron pans) with butter and sugar, kasab embroidery (needlework on red and gold velvet) and spices. Cafés around the Baiturrahman Mosque open early — ordering a kopi sanger (coffee with condensed milk) is an Acehnese ritual.
