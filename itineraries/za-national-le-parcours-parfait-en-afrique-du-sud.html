☀️ What weather should you expect?

Autumn (March-May): Perfect weather with comfortable temperatures (18-26°C), minimal rainfall, and ideal conditions for safari and outdoor activities. Excellent wine harvest season, beautiful landscapes, moderate tourist numbers, reasonable accommodation rates.

Spring (September-November): Excellent weather with warming temperatures (16-24°C), blooming wildflowers, and perfect conditions for wildlife viewing. Whale watching season, jacaranda blooms, moderate prices, excellent photography opportunities.

Summer (December-February): Warm temperatures (20-30°C) with occasional rainfall, peak holiday season, and vibrant outdoor atmosphere. Beach season, highest accommodation rates, school holidays, excellent for coastal activities.

Winter (June-August): Cool, dry weather (8-20°C) perfect for safari with excellent wildlife viewing and clear skies. Peak safari season, whale watching, cozy wine estates, moderate accommodation rates.

Johannesburg

Where the wounds of apartheid are told without flinching — and a city reinvents itself

★★★★☆
📍 Apartheid Museum Johannesburg📍 Constitution Hill Johannesburg📍 Vilakazi Street Soweto📍 Mandela House Soweto📍 Hector Pieterson Memorial Soweto📍 Maboneng Precinct Johannesburg
1

Apartheid Museum — Opened in 2001 on a 7-hectare site at Gold Reef City, this museum traces the rise and fall of apartheid through 22 exhibition rooms. At the entrance, each visitor is handed a random racial classification card — 'white' or 'non-white' — and directed through a separate door, recreating the segregation system in an immediate, visceral way. In one small chamber, 131 nooses hang from the ceiling, one for each political opponent executed under the anti-terrorism laws. Allow a minimum of two hours.

⏱ 60 min
2

Constitution Hill — Built in 1893 by President Paul Kruger as a prison and military fort, this complex once held Nelson Mandela (in 1956 and 1962), Mahatma Gandhi and thousands of political prisoners. Number Four Prison, reserved for Black men, was notorious for its inhuman conditions. In 2004, South Africa's Constitutional Court was established here — built using bricks salvaged from the former awaiting-trial block. The constitutional law library, the largest in the southern hemisphere, houses more than 200 contemporary artworks.

⏱ 60 min
3

Soweto and Vilakazi Street — The only street in the world to have been home to two Nobel Peace Prize laureates: Nelson Mandela at number 8115 (he lived there from the 1940s; the house still bears bullet holes and fire damage) and Archbishop Desmond Tutu at 957A, where he settled in 1975. Nearby, the Hector Pieterson Memorial commemorates the student uprising of 16 June 1976, when police opened fire on schoolchildren protesting compulsory education in Afrikaans. For lunch, the restaurants along Vilakazi Street serve authentic local food — kota (township-style sandwiches), pap and chakalaka.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Sunset at the Maboneng Precinct — This former industrial district in east Johannesburg has reinvented itself as an arts hub, with galleries, rooftop bars and restaurants spread across repurposed warehouses covered in street art. A creative, buzzing way to end your first evening.

Graskop

Gateway to a canyon the size of a country and a road that stops you at every bend

★★★☆☆
📍 Maropeng Cradle of Humankind📍 Sterkfontein Caves Krugersdorp📍 Graskop Gorge Lift Graskop
1

Maropeng and the Cradle of Humankind — This 53,000-hectare UNESCO World Heritage Site holds around 40% of all human ancestor fossils ever discovered on Earth. The Maropeng visitor centre, shaped like a burial mound, takes you through an immersive journey of human evolution. Ten minutes away, the Sterkfontein Caves have yielded more than 500 hominid fossils over two million years old, including the famous Mrs Ples and Little Foot. The cave network spans 12 fossil-bearing sites. Allow 2 to 3 hours for the full experience.

⏱ 60 min
2

Drive to Mpumalanga — From Maropeng, take the N4 then the N12 toward Dullstroom (roughly 3.5 hours). Dullstroom, perched at 2,076 metres, is the highest town in South Africa and its trout-fishing capital. Stop for lunch at one of the village's cosy pubs. Continue to Graskop (around another 1.5 hours) via the R532 through pine plantations and Mpumalanga's indigenous forests.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Graskop Gorge Lift — If time allows on arrival, descend into the Graskop Gorge by glass elevator: the lift drops 51 metres into the forest, leading onto suspended walkways through the canopy with views down to the waterfalls below.

Graskop

Gateway to a canyon the size of a country and a road that stops you at every bend

★★★☆☆
📍 God's Window Mpumalanga📍 Pinnacle Rock Graskop📍 Bourke's Luck Potholes Moremela📍 Three Rondavels Blyde River Canyon📍 Blyde River Canyon Nature Reserve📍 Pilgrim's Rest Mpumalanga
1

God's Window — Perched at 1,902 metres, this viewpoint looks out over the Lowveld below, with visibility that can reach as far as Kruger National Park and, on exceptionally clear days, the Lebombo Mountains along the Mozambican border. A short trail climbs through afromontane forest — tree ferns and yellowwoods — to the main lookout. Just next to it, Pinnacle Rock rises like an isolated quartzite column above a thickly wooded valley.

⏱ 60 min
2

Bourke's Luck Potholes — At the confluence of the Blyde River (River of Joy) and the Treur River (River of Sorrow), millions of years of swirling water have carved cylindrical potholes into the sandstone. Distinct layers of rock give each cavity its own colour — yellows, browns and whites. Footbridges and walkways connect the viewpoints above the gorge. The site is named after Tom Bourke, a prospector who predicted there was gold here — he never found a single grain, though others who came after him had better luck.

⏱ 60 min
3

Three Rondavels and Blyde River Canyon — The Blyde River Canyon stretches 26 km long and 800 metres deep, making it the third largest canyon in the world — and the largest 'green canyon', because unlike the Grand Canyon, its walls are blanketed in vegetation. The Three Rondavels are three rounded dolomite peaks that resemble traditional African huts, looming over the dam far below. The panorama from the viewpoint takes in the full sweep of the canyon — the most photographed spot on the Panorama Route.

⏱ 60 min
4

Pilgrim's Rest — This entire village is a declared national monument, frozen in the Victorian era. Declared a gold field in 1873, it drew thousands of fortune-seekers. The corrugated-iron buildings and wooden verandas have been lovingly restored: the Royal Hotel still welcomes guests, and visitors can try their hand at gold panning in the streams running through the village. The 'Robber's Grave' cemetery tells the story of a thief buried face-down, turned toward the mountain by the miners as a form of punishment.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Panorama Route by car — The full loop covers roughly 160 km and fills a long day. Start early from Graskop, heading north on the R532 and R534. Recommended order: God's Window → Pinnacle Rock → Bourke's Luck Potholes → Three Rondavels → back via Pilgrim's Rest. Bring snacks and water.

Skukuza

The heart of Kruger — where lions own the riverbank and leopards sleep in the trees

★★★★☆
📍 Paul Kruger Gate Kruger National Park📍 Skukuza Rest Camp Kruger📍 Sabie River Kruger
1

Entering Kruger via Paul Kruger Gate — Kruger National Park covers nearly 20,000 km² — roughly the size of Israel — and supports the greatest diversity of large mammals of any African reserve. The Paul Kruger Gate, the park's busiest entrance, opens at 5:30am in summer and 6:00am in winter. The southern zone around Skukuza is the richest for wildlife: open grasslands, thorn thickets and riverine forest along the Sabie and Sand rivers concentrate lions, leopards, elephants, buffalo and rhino.

⏱ 60 min
2

Skukuza Loop — The 40-km road between Skukuza and Lower Sabie follows the Sabie River — the most consistently productive stretch in the park for predator sightings. Lion prides thrive here thanks to an abundance of prey: impala, zebra and wildebeest. Pull over at the waterhole sign posts marked on the park map: hippos, Nile crocodiles and giant kingfishers are virtually guaranteed.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Late-afternoon safari — Arrive at Skukuza camp in the early afternoon, settle in, then head out for the evening drive (gates close at sunset). The final hours of daylight are when predators are most active. For a guided night drive, book at the camp office.

Final morning safari in Kruger — Make the most of one last dawn drive before leaving the park through Paul Kruger Gate or Numbi Gate. Every safari is different: the bush in morning light, washed in gold, offers the best conditions for photography. Aim to exit by 10am to reach the airport on time.

Addo

★★★☆☆
📍 Addo Elephant National Park Main Camp📍 Hapoor Dam Addo📍 Spekboom Trail Addo📍 Sundays River Valley
1

Full day in Addo — Start early to cover the different sections of the park. The main road threads through spekboom (portulacaria) forest — an endemic shrub that absorbs more CO₂ per hectare than the Amazon rainforest. The park spans 5 of South Africa's 7 biomes. Look out for the flightless Addo dung beetle, a tiny endemic species so vital to the ecosystem that crushing its dung balls on the park roads is prohibited — the only sign of its kind in the world, displayed at the gate.

⏱ 60 min
2

Sundays River Valley — Heading out of the park through the southern exit, the Sundays River Valley stretches into the distance under citrus orchards as far as the eye can see — South Africa's largest citrus-growing basin. The area also offers golden sand dunes for sandboarding and peaceful kayaking on the river, a relaxed contrast to the safari pace.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Self-drive safari at sunrise — Addo gates open at 7am (winter) or 5:30am (summer). Follow the Spekboom-Rooidam-Hapoor loop to maximise sightings. Elephants move in herds of 20 to 50, often accompanied by buffalo and warthogs.

Storms River

A suspension bridge, a forest a thousand years old, and the world's highest bungee jump

★★★★☆
📍 Bloukrans Bridge Bungy Nature's Valley📍 Tsitsikamma National Park Storms River📍 Storms River Suspension Bridge📍 Storms River Mouth Rest Camp📍 Big Tree Tsitsikamma
1

Bloukrans Bridge Bungee — On the N2 between Addo and Storms River, the Bloukrans Bridge is impossible to miss: at 216 metres above the valley, it is the highest commercial bridge bungee jump in the world. Even if you're not jumping, the walkway leading to the jump platform (slung beneath the bridge deck) delivers genuinely vertiginous views. The bridge itself, completed in 1984, spans 451 metres.

⏱ 60 min
2

Storms River Mouth and suspension bridge — Tsitsikamma National Park protects 80 km of rugged coastline where ancient indigenous forest falls directly into the Indian Ocean. The trail to Storms River Mouth (1 km each way) descends through dense forest to a spectacular gorge where the river meets the sea. The suspension bridge spanning the mouth shudders as waves surge into the canyon below. This is the most iconic image of the Garden Route.

⏱ 60 min
3

Big Tree of Tsitsikamma — A yellowwood (Podocarpus falcatus) roughly 1,000 years old, with a trunk 9 metres in circumference and 36 metres tall. A short loop trail (500 m) winds through the surrounding afromontane forest — tree ferns, creepers and a canopy so thick that light barely filters through.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Kayaking through the Storms River gorge — Paddling from the river mouth, you work your way into the narrow gorge beneath the suspension bridge. Sheer rock walls rise on both sides, draped in moss and ferns. There are options to jump from rocks into the deep pools below. The water runs the colour of dark tea, stained by tannins from the forest roots.

Oudtshoorn

Limestone caves carved over millennia, ostrich farms and the road less travelled through the Karoo

★★★★☆
📍 Jeffreys Bay Beach📍 Cango Caves Oudtshoorn📍 Cango Wildlife Ranch Oudtshoorn
1

Jeffreys Bay — A morning stop in this laid-back surf town whose 'Super Tubes' wave is ranked among the finest in the world. When the south-westerly swell reaches 1.2 to 2.5 metres, the wave can peel for 300 metres without breaking. Even if surfing isn't on your agenda, Kabeljous Beach and the dolphins that cruise along the shore make it a thoroughly pleasant stop. A walk along the seafront and a coffee facing the ocean before heading inland.

⏱ 60 min
2

Cango Caves — Arriving in Oudtshoorn in the late afternoon, descend into the Cango Caves. This limestone cave system has been forming for millions of years. The main chamber (Van Zyl's Hall) measures 98 metres long, 49 wide and 15 high. Highlights include Cleopatra's Needle (a 9-metre stalactite), the Madonna and Child formation and the Leaning Tower of Pisa. Two tours are offered: the Heritage Tour (accessible to all, 1 hour) and the Adventure Tour (crawling through tight passages and climbing ladders — not recommended for claustrophobes).

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Drive from Jeffreys Bay to Oudtshoorn — Take the N2 westward then the N12 and R62. The Route 62, the inland alternative to the N2, cuts through the Klein Karoo: semi-arid landscapes, ostrich farms and quiet Karoo villages. The drive covers about 280 km (3.5 hours). Optional stops at Willowmore or De Rust for a coffee.

Cape

Table Mountain, Robben Island, vineyard valleys and the best sunset on the continent

★★★★★
📍 Swartberg Pass Prince Albert📍 Route 62 Klein Karoo📍 Montagu Western Cape📍 Camps Bay Cape Town
1

Swartberg Pass — An early start to cross one of South Africa's finest mountain passes. This 27-km gravel road, built between 1883 and 1888 by Thomas Bain using 250 convict labourers, climbs to 1,585 metres. The dry-stone retaining walls (including a single 2.4 km unbroken section on the southern approach) use no cement or mortar — an engineering feat awarded provincial heritage status. The hairpin bends have their own names: Skelmdraai ('the treacherous bend'), Die Stalletjie ('where the stagecoach horses were changed'). At the summit, the view spans the Great Karoo to the north and the Klein Karoo to the south. Along the way, the ruins of the Victoria Hotel and the old toll gate recall the era of horse-drawn coaches.

⏱ 60 min
2

Drive to Cape Town — From Oudtshoorn, take the R62 westward through Calitzdorp (a small wine village known for its South African port-style wines), Barrydale and Montagu. The R62 unfolds across 350 km of ever-changing scenery: dry Klein Karoo vineyards, rocky gorges, dried-fruit orchards. Stop for a wine tasting at Robertson or Montagu if time allows. Arrive in Cape Town in the late afternoon via the N1 or the coastal R44.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Sunset at Camps Bay — Your first evening in Cape Town: settle in at Camps Bay, framed by the Twelve Apostles mountain range. The restaurants along Victoria Road have terraces facing the Atlantic. Watching the sun sink behind Lion's Head is a proper Cape Town ritual.

Cape

Table Mountain, Robben Island, vineyard valleys and the best sunset on the continent

★★★★★
📍 Chapman's Peak Drive Hout Bay📍 Hout Bay Harbour Cape Town📍 Boulders Beach Simon's Town📍 Cape Point Nature Reserve📍 Cape of Good Hope
1

Chapman's Peak Drive — This 9-km corniche road with 114 bends, blasted into the cliff between 1915 and 1922, hugs the Atlantic face of the peninsula between Hout Bay and Noordhoek. Named after John Chapman, an officer aboard the English vessel Consent who anchored in Hout Bay in 1607, the cliffs plunge vertically to the ocean 593 metres below. Stop at the lookout points to photograph Hout Bay on one side and the unspoilt sweep of Noordhoek beach on the other. This is South Africa's most spectacular coastal drive — BMW filmed a now-legendary advertisement here in the 1990s.

⏱ 60 min
2

Boulders Beach and the penguins — In Simon's Town, a former naval base with considerable Victorian charm, Boulders Beach shelters a colony of around 3,000 African penguins, a globally endangered species. The 540-million-year-old granite boulders create sheltered coves where penguins nest between September and February. Wooden boardwalks let you get remarkably close without disturbing the nesting areas. Simon's Town itself is worth a wander: Georgian houses, antique shops and seafood restaurants overlooking the marina.

⏱ 60 min
3

Cape Point — The route continues to Cape Point, the south-western tip of the continent where the Atlantic meets the Indian Ocean (the true southernmost point of Africa is Cape Agulhas, further east). The original lighthouse, built in 1860 at 238 metres above sea level, was deemed too high — fog made it invisible — and a second one was built lower in 1919. The Flying Dutchman funicular carries you to the summit. The surrounding nature reserve is draped in fynbos and home to chacma baboon troops, ostriches and Cape mountain zebra. The waters below have claimed more than 26 shipwrecks.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Fish and chips at Hout Bay harbour — Stop on the way back from Chapman's Peak for the quintessential fish and chips, eaten watching the fishing boats. The harbour is also the departure point for boat trips to Duiker Island, a rocky outcrop colonised by Cape fur seals.

⏱ 60 min

Cape

Table Mountain, Robben Island, vineyard valleys and the best sunset on the continent

★★★★★
📍 Bo-Kaap Cape Town📍 Auwal Mosque Bo-Kaap📍 V&A Waterfront Cape Town📍 Nelson Mandela Gateway Robben Island📍 Robben Island Cape Town📍 Zeitz MOCAA Cape Town
1

Bo-Kaap — This neighbourhood of pastel-painted houses clinging to the slopes of Signal Hill is the cradle of Cape Malay culture. Its origins go back to the 1760s, when the Dutch East India Company built rental houses for enslaved people deported from Indonesia, Malaysia and East Africa. When slavery was abolished in 1834, residents were able to buy their homes — which had to be painted white under colonial rule — and celebrated their freedom by painting them in vivid colours. The Auwal Mosque, founded in 1794 by Tuan Guru (a political prisoner freed from Robben Island), is the oldest in South Africa. The Bo-Kaap Museum, housed in a 1760 Cape Dutch home, is the neighbourhood's oldest building. For a taste of Cape Malay food: bobotie, samoosas, daltjies and koeksisters.

⏱ 60 min
2

Robben Island — The ferry departs from the Nelson Mandela Gateway at the V&A Waterfront (advance booking essential; 3.5 hours including 30 minutes each way by sea). The island, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1999, has served variously as a prison, leper colony, psychiatric hospital and military base since the 17th century. Nelson Mandela spent 18 of his 27 years of imprisonment here, in a cell measuring 2 metres by 2 — visible during the tour. The guides are former political prisoners who recount their own stories. Three former Robben Island inmates went on to become President of South Africa: Mandela, Motlanthe and Zuma.

⏱ 60 min
3

V&A Waterfront and Zeitz MOCAA — The Victoria & Alfred Waterfront, developed within the old Victorian harbour, is the beating heart of Cape Town. The Zeitz Museum of Contemporary Art Africa, opened in 2017 inside a converted 1924 grain silo redesigned by architect Thomas Heatherwick, is the largest museum of contemporary African art in the world — 6,000 m² of gallery space across 9 floors, with the silo tubes carved out to create a cathedral-like atrium 27 metres high.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Dinner at the V&A Waterfront — In the evening, the waterfront comes alive: restaurants with harbour views and Table Mountain illuminated behind. The Silo District, clustered around Zeitz MOCAA, is where the most sought-after addresses are.

⏱ 60 min

Cape

Table Mountain, Robben Island, vineyard valleys and the best sunset on the continent

★★★★★
📍 Table Mountain Cableway Cape Town📍 Kirstenbosch National Botanical Garden📍 Tree Canopy Walkway Kirstenbosch📍 Signal Hill Cape Town📍 Lion's Head Cape Town
1

Table Mountain — The rotating cable car (the cabin completes a full 360° turn during the ascent) reaches the flat summit at 1,085 metres in five minutes. Table Mountain is part of a 60-km chain extending to Cape Point, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2004 within the Cape Floristic Region — the smallest of the world's six floral kingdoms, yet the richest in species per unit area. From the summit you can see two UNESCO sites simultaneously: the mountain itself and Robben Island offshore. Around 2,200 plant species grow on the mountain, many found nowhere else on Earth. The dassies (rock hyraxes — small mammals resembling stout marmots) are everywhere at the top; their closest living relative is the elephant.

⏱ 60 min
2

Kirstenbosch — This 528-hectare botanical garden, founded in 1913 on the eastern slopes of Table Mountain, was the first in the world devoted entirely to a country's indigenous flora. A UNESCO World Heritage Site, it displays more than 7,000 species — including a cycad garden (primitive plants contemporary with the dinosaurs) and the Peninsula Garden with 2,500 species found only in the Cape. The Tree Canopy Walkway ('Boomslang'), a curving 130-metre steel and timber bridge, rises 12 metres above the ground through the afromontane forest canopy, with panoramic views across the Cape Flats to the bay beyond.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Sunset from Signal Hill or Lion's Head — Signal Hill (350 m) is accessible by car and offers a complete panorama of the City Bowl, the bay and the mountain. For those up for a walk, the Lion's Head hike (669 m, about 1.5 hours up) ends with a 360° view that is quintessentially Cape Town. Chains and ladders on the final section, but manageable for any fit hiker.

Cape

★★★★★
📍 Stellenbosch Wine Route📍 Franschhoek Wine Tram📍 Babylonstoren Franschhoek📍 Groot Constantia Cape Town
1

Stellenbosch — South Africa's second oldest town, founded in 1679 by Governor Simon van der Stel on the banks of the Eerste River ('First River' in Dutch — the first one the settlers came across). Streets lined with three-hundred-year-old oaks give it its Afrikaans nickname 'Eikestad' (Town of Oaks). Cape Dutch whitewashed gables, Georgian and Victorian façades line the centre. The Schreuderhuis, a thatched-roof house built in 1709, is the oldest documented urban dwelling in the country. Stellenbosch accounts for nearly 20% of all South African vineyards and more than 150 estates open to visitors. This is where Pinotage was created in 1924 — a crossing of Pinot Noir and Cinsault, and South Africa's signature grape.

⏱ 60 min
2

Franschhoek — 'The French Corner' was founded by Huguenot refugees fleeing religious persecution in France in the late 17th century. They named their farms after their home towns — La Motte, L'Ormarins, Chamonix — and brought with them the art of winemaking. Today the village is considered South Africa's gastronomic capital, with some of the finest restaurants on the continent. The Babylonstoren estate, one of the best-preserved Cape Dutch farms in the country (buildings dating to 1690), has a 3.5-hectare garden inspired by the gardens of Babylon, growing more than 300 varieties of edible plants.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Franschhoek Wine Tram — This vintage tram (with bus relays) serves the valley's wine estates on a hop-on hop-off basis. Several lines wind through the vineyards nestled between the Drakenstein and Franschhoek mountains. Tastings of Chardonnay, Cabernet Sauvignon, Chenin Blanc and the superb Méthode Cap Classique (South Africa's answer to Champagne). The scenery — golden vines, blue mountains, white-gabled estates — gives Tuscany a run for its money.
