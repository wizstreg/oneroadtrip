Balikpapan

East Kalimantan port city and gateway to Borneo's orangutan forests

★★★☆☆
📍 Samboja Lestari Orangutan Rehabilitation Center📍 Bukit Bangkirai Canopy Bridge📍 Sungai Hitam Black River Balikpapan📍 Samboja Lodge Balikpapan
1

The Samboja Lestari reserve lies about 45 km from Balikpapan — allow 1.5 hours by 4×4. This rehabilitation centre, run by the Borneo Orangutan Survival Foundation (BOSF), takes in orangutans and sun bears rescued from deforested areas. Since its founding in 2001, over one million trees representing 1,000 species have been replanted across 2,000 hectares. Visits run at 8 am and 1 pm, each lasting about 4 hours: don't miss feeding time on the floating islands designed for orangutans that cannot be released into the wild.

⏱ 240 min
2

Bukit Bangkirai, about 1.5 hours' drive from Samboja, is home to a 1,500-hectare protected tropical forest dominated by bangkirai — hardwood trees standing 40 to 50 m tall, some 150 years old and up to 2.3 m in diameter. The canopy bridge connecting five bangkirai trees hangs at a height of 30 to 40 m and stretches 64 m: the view over Borneo's emerald jungle from the top is worth every step. Arrive early — gibbons, hornbills and red langurs are most active in the morning.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

In the late afternoon, board a motorised canoe on the Sungai Hitam (Black River) to cruise along the mangroves of Balikpapan Bay. This is your best chance of spotting proboscis monkeys in their natural habitat. These Borneo-endemic primates with their prominent noses are most visible at sunset, when they return to the riverside trees for the night.

The drive from Balikpapan to Sangata takes roughly 6 to 7 hours — you'll cross the equator (marked by a monument), pass through palm-oil plantations and Dayak hamlets. In the evening in Sangata, walk down to the banks of the Sangatta River for a short sunset stroll. The estuaries and riverbanks are transit zones for freshwater crocodiles and macaques. Activities

Sangata

Gateway to Kutai National Park, last refuge of Borneo's wild orangutans

★★★★☆

1

Very early morning departure by motorised canoe from Sangata's port — allow 20 minutes of navigation to reach the park's research station. The Prevab station, one of Indonesia's oldest orangutan research centres, is the starting point for guided forest treks with the rangers. The forest is lowland dipterocarp tropical — trees soaring 30 to 40 m, vines, and an incredible acoustic backdrop of bird calls and gibbon song. The orangutans living here are completely wild, with no guarantee of a sighting — that's precisely what makes the experience so rare.

⏱ 240 min
2

The Sangkima area, on the park's eastern side, is a flora reserve containing one of Borneo's oldest trees — an ulin (ironwood), an endemic species that is virtually rot-proof. The mangrove forest at the mouth of the Sangatta River delta is a zone for canoe excursions to spot proboscis monkeys, false gharial crocodiles and around a hundred bird species, including 8 species of hornbill.

⏱ 90 min
3

Sangata's market and the riverbanks in the evening offer a window into the daily life of a frontier town caught between the industrial world (Pertamina oil operations) and the forest. Local operators run sunset canoe trips on the Sangatta River to observe false gharial crocodiles and giant kingfishers — a transition between jungle and coast that sums up East Kalimantan nicely.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Guided forest trek in Kutai Park in search of wild orangutans. The best sightings happen early in the morning (around 7 am) and late afternoon (around 3 pm), when orangutans build their nests in the trees. If you still have energy in the evening, a short night walk with a headlamp lets you look for tarsiers — tiny nocturnal primates with enormous eyes, endemic to Borneo.

Yogyakarta

Java's cultural heartland, home to the UNESCO-listed temples of Borobudur and Prambanan

★★★★★
📍 Borobudur Temple Magelang📍 Keraton Yogyakarta Sultan Palace📍 Taman Sari Water Castle Yogyakarta📍 Malioboro Street Yogyakarta
1

Borobudur lies about 40 km northwest of Yogyakarta — leave early in the morning to beat the heat and the tour buses that arrive after 9 am. This Mahayana Buddhist temple, built between 778 and 850 AD under the Sailendra dynasty, is the largest Buddhist monument in the world: 2 million stones assembled without mortar to form a 33.5-m-high pyramid on a 120-m-wide base. Its 2,672 carved relief panels and 504 Buddha statues depict the path to nirvana across three symbolic levels. The temple was buried under volcanic ash around the year 1000 and rediscovered in 1814 by Raffles. It was listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1991 after a 10-year restoration carried out with the organisation's support.

⏱ 180 min
2

The Kraton of Yogyakarta, founded in 1755 by Sultan Hamengkubuwono I after the Treaty of Giyanti split the Mataram kingdom into two sultanates, is the only still-inhabited royal palace in Indonesia. The 184-hectare complex is laid out according to Javanese cosmological principles — the Kraton, the commemorative column and Mount Merapi align perfectly on a north–south axis. Inside: collections of royal carriages, gamelan instruments, kris daggers and wayang puppets. Javanese dance performances are held in the open pavilions on certain mornings — check at the entrance.

⏱ 90 min
3

Taman Sari, the water castle built in 1758 by Hamengkubuwono I west of the Kraton, was the royal pleasure garden. The complex originally included bathing pools, underground passages and a maze of corridors used, according to tradition, for the sultan's meditations. The central courtyard with its filled pools has been restored — the surrounding ruins carry a romantic charm that the children of Hamengkubuwono must still haunt.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Jalan Malioboro, the philosophical axis of Yogyakarta linking the Tugu Golong Giling to the Kraton, is the city's busiest commercial artery. It's lined with batik shops, street vendors and souvenir sellers. If you're after authentic batik, head for the workshops tucked into the side streets behind the main road — the prices are more honest. In the evening, street musicians play gamelan and keroncong (a genre with Portuguese influences).

Yogyakarta

Java's cultural heartland, home to the UNESCO-listed temples of Borobudur and Prambanan

★★★★★
📍 Prambanan Temple Complex Yogyakarta📍 Mount Merapi Volcano Yogyakarta📍 Museum Lava Merapi Yogyakarta📍 Ratu Boko Palace Yogyakarta
1

Prambanan, 17 km northeast of Yogyakarta on the road to Solo, is the largest Hindu temple complex in Indonesia. Built in the 9th century by the Sanjaya dynasty of the Mataram kingdom, the site is UNESCO-listed. The central temple dedicated to Shiva rises to 47 m — its walls are covered with bas-reliefs narrating the Ramayana, some episodes of which are re-enacted every evening in the open-air amphitheatre at sunset (the Ramayana Ballet, a magnificent show with the illuminated temple as a backdrop). If you have time, the Sewu and Ratu Boko temples (a ruined palatial site on a hilltop with sweeping views of Merapi and Prambanan) are well worth the detour.

⏱ 150 min
2

Mount Merapi (2,930 m) is the most active volcano in Indonesia — its last major eruption in 2010 claimed 353 lives. Jeeps run excursions up the northern slopes from the village of Kaliurang. You'll see fossilised lava flows and houses partly buried by the pyroclastic surge. The Lava Museum, built at the foot of the volcano, preserves testimony from the eruption and a car twisted by the heat, left in place as a memorial.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Jeep excursion on the slopes of Merapi departing from Kaliurang — local operators offer 2-to-3-hour circuits at dawn or in the evening. The view of the caldera from the slopes is striking, and the landscape of ash and regrowing vegetation tells the story of the eruptions' violence and the resilience of the villages that rebuild in the volcano's shadow better than any text could.

⏱ 150 min

The Ramayana Ballet at sunset on Prambanan's outdoor stage is an experience not to be missed if you're there on a Tuesday, Thursday or Saturday — dancers in golden costumes bring the Hindu epic to life with the illuminated temple as a backdrop. Book in advance; seats facing the stage sell out quickly.

Yogyakarta

Java's cultural heartland, home to the UNESCO-listed temples of Borobudur and Prambanan

★★★★★
📍 Kraton Yogyakarta batik workshop📍 Museum Sonobudoyo Yogyakarta📍 Pasar Beringharjo Yogyakarta📍 Fort Vredeburg Yogyakarta
1

The Sonobudoyo Museum, steps from the Kraton, is the best place to understand Javanese arts and history before leaving Yogyakarta: wayang kulit (shadow puppets), kris (ritual daggers, each believed to carry its own character depending on how it was forged), batik textiles and archaeological collections from Central Java. In the evening, wayang kulit performances are held in the museum courtyard.

⏱ 90 min
2

Fort Vredeburg, built by the Dutch in 1760 at the request of Sultan Hamengkubuwono I to keep watch over the Kraton, has been turned into a museum of the Indonesian independence struggle. The dioramas cover the Japanese occupation, the 1945 declaration of independence and the revolutionary period when Yogyakarta briefly served as the capital of the Republic. The irony: the fort designed to control the sultan became the memorial to Javanese resistance against the colonisers.

⏱ 75 min
3

Beringharjo Market, at the entrance to Malioboro, is one of the largest traditional markets on Java. The upper floors overflow with batik by the metre, kebayas and traditional fabrics — this is where Yogyakartans shop for ceremonial cloth. Bring someone who speaks Bahasa to negotiate, or accept that listed prices are often double the real price.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Yogyakarta is the world capital of batik — the hot-wax resist technique applied with a canting (a small copper stylus) has been inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list since 2009. Numerous workshops around Taman Sari offer half-day batik classes where you can create your own piece. A far more original souvenir than a t-shirt.

Cemoro Lawang

Village perched on the Tengger caldera rim, the launchpad for sunrise over Mount Bromo

★★★☆☆
📍 Cemoro Lawang village Bromo📍 Mount Penanjakan viewpoint Bromo📍 Kawah Bromo crater📍 Pura Luhur Poten Hindu Temple Bromo
1

The drive from Yogyakarta to Cemoro Lawang takes about 5 hours via Probolinggo. Arrive before sunset for a first glimpse of the Tengger caldera from the village rim. Cemoro Lawang (2,217 m) sits perched on the edge of this caldera — below stretches the Sea of Sand (Segara Wedi), an 11-km-long ash plain protected as a nature reserve since 1919. Mount Bromo (2,329 m) smokes continuously in the middle of the caldera — its name comes from the Javanese pronunciation of Brahma, the Hindu god of creation.

⏱ 90 min
2

Pura Luhur Poten, at the foot of Bromo on the Sea of Sand, is the Hindu temple of the Tenggerese — one of the last Hindu peoples on Java, believed to be descendants of a princess from the Majapahit kingdom. Every year between June and July, they celebrate the Yadnya Kasada festival by throwing offerings into the Bromo crater from the caldera rim. If you happen upon a ceremony, show respect.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Wake-up at 3 am to climb to the Penanjakan viewpoint (2,770 m) — the highest point on the caldera's northern rim — before dawn. 4×4 jeeps shuttle from the village, but if you're a strong walker with a headlamp, the hike takes about 1 h 45 min from Cemoro Lawang via the Seruni viewpoint (about 1 h) to the summit. Sunrise falls between 5 and 5:30 am depending on the month. On a clear day, you'll see Bromo smoking inside the caldera with Mount Semeru (3,676 m, Java's tallest volcano) in the background — a scene found nowhere else on earth. Bring a warm jacket: temperatures drop to 5–10 °C at this altitude before dawn.

⏱ 180 min

After sunrise, descend into the Sea of Sand and walk up to the rim of Bromo's crater (250 steps). The crater floor smokes continuously — the sulphur smell is noticeable but bearable when there's wind. Avoid days with no wind or volcanic alerts. The climb from the Sea of Sand car park takes about 45 minutes on foot.

Ubud

Bali's artistic capital, surrounded by terraced rice paddies and Hindu temples

1

From Cemoro Lawang, the drive to Banyuwangi (Java's eastern port) takes about 3–4 hours. The Banyuwangi–Gilimanuk ferry crosses the Bali Strait — less than a kilometre wide at this point — in roughly 45 minutes. Ferries run all day with frequent departures. The Sacred Monkey Forest Sanctuary in Ubud spans 12 hectares right in the heart of town. It's home to over 700 long-tailed macaques and three ancient temples dating from the 14th century: Pura Dalem Agung, Pura Beji and Pura Prajapati, all still used for ceremonies. The monkeys are sacred to the locals — they're believed to protect the temple from negative influences. Keep a firm grip on your glasses and camera: these macaques are light-fingered.

3

If you take the north coast road from Gilimanuk, make a stop at Pura Pulaki — a cliff temple suspended between sea and mountain, known for its colony of semi-wild macaques occupying the stairways. Continuing east, Lovina is a quiet beach town where wild dolphins can be spotted by boat early in the morning. The north coast road is noticeably more scenic and far less busy than the central route.

Activities

Arrival in Ubud after a long transit day: treat yourself to a Balinese massage on your first evening. The local technique combines deep kneading, frangipani oil and acupressure — an hour is enough to recover from a night on Mount Bromo. Ubud's spas are plentiful and considerably cheaper than those in the south of Bali.

Ubud

Bali's artistic capital, surrounded by terraced rice paddies and Hindu temples

★★★★★
📍 Tegalalang Rice Terraces Ubud Bali📍 Tirta Empul Temple Tampaksiring📍 Goa Gajah Elephant Cave Ubud📍 Campuhan Ridge Walk Ubud📍 Ubud Art Market Bali
1

The Tegalalang Rice Terraces, 8 km north of Ubud, are the symbol of the subak irrigation technique — a system of hydraulic cooperatives between Balinese villages inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2012. The terraces cascade in tiers down the valley, forming a landscape of green waterfalls that photographers chase in the golden morning or late-afternoon light. Arrive before 8 am to beat the tour buses.

⏱ 60 min
2

Tirta Empul, 15 km from Ubud towards Tampaksiring, is a purification temple built in 962 AD around a sacred spring whose water, according to legend, was created by the god Indra to resurrect his soldiers. You can watch — or take part, if you respect the dress code — in the ritual baths in the purification pools. This is a living practice, not a performance: Balinese families come here to purify themselves for deeply personal spiritual reasons.

⏱ 75 min
3

Goa Gajah (Elephant Cave) dates from the 11th century — a life-size monstrous mouth sculpted into the rock marks the entrance to the cave sanctuary. Inside, the chamber houses statues of Ganesh and Shiva lingams. In front of the entrance, two carved fountains were used for ritual ablutions. The site is surprisingly little-visited despite its proximity to Ubud.

⏱ 45 min
4

The Campuhan Ridge Walk, a 15-minute stroll on foot from central Ubud, follows a green ridge between two rivers — the Sungai Wos to the east and the Sungai Cebok to the west. The paved path passes rice paddies, small temples and open views over the wooded hills. Ideal at dawn or late afternoon. Allow about 1.5 hours for the return walk.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Ubud is the centre of Balinese art — ever since the 1930s, when European artists such as Walter Spies and Rudolf Bonnet settled here and stimulated local creativity. The craft market and the Museum Puri Lukisan (Balinese painting) or the Agung Rai Museum of Art show how that dialogue transformed the Balinese pictorial style. In the evening, Legong, Barong or Kecak performances are staged at several venues in the village — the most beautiful setting remains the Puri Saren (Ubud Royal Palace).

Seminyak, on Bali's southwest coast, is the island's upscale neighbourhood — Balinese designer boutiques, fine-dining restaurants and beach clubs line the lanes of Jl Kayu Aya and Jl Petitenget. Tanah Lot, 20 km north of Seminyak, is one of Bali's seven sea temples — a rocky platform jutting out of the ocean with a 16th-century temple perched on top. According to Balinese tradition, each coastal temple should be visible from the next to form a chain of protection. The sunset light on the temple is photographed by millions of visitors — arrive an hour before sunset to find a good angle before the crowds.

Activities

Canggu, the bohemian neighbourhood 5 km north of Seminyak, has become the playground of surfers, creatives and digital nomads. In Canggu's lanes, street-art murals, trendy cafés and vintage shops create an atmosphere radically different from the rest of Bali.

Sunset from Petitenget or Double Six beach, drink in hand at one of the beach clubs lining the Seminyak coast. The evening light is particularly beautiful here because the coast faces due west over the Indian Ocean. It's the daily ritual for Balinese residents and foreign expats alike — sundowners with a view of the horizon.

Uluwatu

Sacred cliffs of southern Bali with the Pura Luhur temple and sunset Kecak dance

★★★★☆
📍 Pura Luhur Uluwatu Temple Bali📍 Padang Padang Beach Uluwatu📍 Bingin Beach Uluwatu Bali📍 Kecak Dance Uluwatu sunset
1

Pura Luhur Uluwatu is perched 70 m above the Indian Ocean on a limestone cliff on the Bukit peninsula. The temple dates back to the 9th century — an inscription from 886 AD confirms it — and was expanded in the 11th century by the sage Mpu Kuturan, then embellished in the 15th century by Danghyang Nirartha, a Brahmin priest who according to tradition achieved moksha (spiritual liberation) here — his cloak is said to have turned to stone, forming the very foundation of the temple. It is one of the six directional temples (Sad Kahyangan) believed to protect Bali from evil forces coming from the sea. Be warned: the temple's macaques are expert at snatching sunglasses, phones and open bags.

Padang Padang Beach, reached by a staircase carved into the cliff below Uluwatu, is a cove of turquoise water framed by rocks. It's one of Bali's best surf spots for experienced surfers (reef break — not for beginners). For non-surfers, swimming is pleasant at low tide. The beach was made world-famous by the film Eat Pray Love.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Kecak dance at Uluwatu is performed every evening at 6 pm in the temple's open-air amphitheatre, with the ocean and the sunset as a backdrop. Around 75 male dancers form a circle chanting the rhythmic 'cak-cak-cak' chorus without any musical instruments — a technique born in the 1930s when Balinese dancer Wayan Limbak and German painter Walter Spies adapted the Sanghyang trance ritual into a theatrical narrative based on the Ramayana. The scene with the illuminated cliffs in the background, the fire and the chant building to a crescendo is one of the most powerful cultural experiences in Bali. Arrive 30 minutes before the show for a good spot. Ngurah Rai Airport in Denpasar is a 30-minute drive from Uluwatu.

⏱ 90 min

Bingin Beach, reached via a steep 10-minute path from the main road, is a small white-sand beach at the foot of Uluwatu's cliffs. The water is clear and the beach rarely crowded on weekdays. Bamboo warungs on the sand serve grilled fish and fresh juices — the perfect lunch to round off this Indonesian expedition.
