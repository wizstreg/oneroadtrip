Dublin

Where the ghosts of 1916 still haunt the prison yard.

★★★★★
1

On May 12, 1916, James Connolly awaited dawn in this courtyard, unable to stand—he was shot tied to a chair. Kilmainham Gaol, opened in 1796, witnessed the execution of 14 leaders of the Easter Rising between May 3 and 12, 1916. The mandatory guided tour takes you through the cells, the chapel where Joseph Plunkett married Grace Gifford the night before his execution, and the Stonebreakers' Yard. Book several weeks ahead—slots fill fast.

⏱ 90 min
2

Wet grass, crows overhead, and beneath your feet more than 1.5 million people—more than Dublin's current living population. Glasnevin, founded in 1832 by Daniel O'Connell so Catholics and Protestants could finally share a cemetery, brings together Collins, Parnell, de Valera, and a mass grave containing victims of the Great Famine. The guided tour is short but dense; the cemetery museum—the world's first of its kind—deserves an extra hour.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Allow 1.5 hours. Combine with dinner on Merrion Row—Etto is a solid choice, reservation recommended.

Newgrange

Older than the pyramids, lit by solstice sun for seventeen minutes.

1

Built around 3200 BC, before the pyramids of Giza and Stonehenge—yet unknown until 1699. Newgrange's inner chamber, 19 meters long, is illuminated for 17 minutes on winter solstice mornings by a beam passing through a roof box. In 2020, a study published in Nature revealed that a man buried at the center was the product of an incestuous union, likely indicating a hereditary dynastic elite as early as the Neolithic. Access only via Brú na Bóinne Visitor Centre—online tickets mandatory, especially July–August.

Knowth

More megalithic art than all of Britain combined, carved in spirals and mysteries.

1

Archaeologists call it the greatest concentration of megalithic art in Western Europe: Knowth has over 300 stones carved with spirals, lozenges, and undulating patterns—more than all of Great Britain combined. The main mound, larger than Newgrange, contains two back-to-back passages; 18 small satellite tombs surround it. Combined ticket with Newgrange via the Visitor Centre—allow the morning for both sites.

Hill Of Tara

Where High Kings were crowned and a roaring stone confirmed their fate.

1

Wind sweeps across the treeless, rampart-less hill—only grassy mounds remain, and the Lia Fáil, the standing stone that, according to legend, roared to confirm the true king. For centuries this was the ceremonial center of the High Kings of Ireland, in use since Neolithic times. The Mound of the Hostages is a passage tomb built around 3000 BC. The visitor center in the former St Patrick's Church explains the site's layered history in 20 minutes.

Clonmacnoise

Scholarship and sanctity on the Shannon, raided by Vikings, revered for centuries.

1

In 544, Saint Ciarán planted the first posts of Clonmacnoise at the exact junction of the Shannon and the Esker Riada, a royal road crossing Ireland from east to west. He died a few months later, but the monastery became one of Western Europe's greatest centers of learning, plundered by Vikings more than eight times between the 9th and 12th centuries. Seven ruined churches, two round towers, and the Cross of the Scriptures preserved indoors in the site museum. Allow 1.5 hours to see everything.

The Burren

Limestone wilderness where arctic flowers bloom beside Mediterranean kin.

1

Bare rock, yet over 70% of Ireland's flowering plant species grow in its crevices—arctic and Mediterranean, separated by mere inches. The Burren is a limestone karst of roughly 250 km² where deep fissures (grikes) create opposing microclimates: cool north-facing for mountain avens, warm south-facing for bloody cranesbill. In May and June, 23 orchid species bloom simultaneously. Free admission, free parking.

⏱ 60 min
2

Erected between 3800 and 3200 BC, this portal tomb raises two upright stones and a capstone in the middle of the Burren's limestone pavement. Excavations in the 1980s uncovered remains of twenty to thirty individuals in the chamber, adults and children. Photogenic at dawn or sunset—the low light brings out the textures. Free access from the road.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Musicians arrive unannounced, set down their cases, and the session begins. In Doolin, Gus O'Connor's Pub is one of the most reliable spots for live traditional music on weeknights. No reservations for sessions, but come early for a seat—some nights the room fills before 9 PM.

If the sky clears, the climb to Diamond Hill rewards in under 2 hours. This 442-meter summit in Connemara National Park offers 360° views of the Twelve Bens, Kylemore, and the Atlantic. Two marked trails depart from the Visitor Centre in Letterfrack: lower loop (3 km, easy) and upper loop (7 km, sustained climb). Free admission and parking.

Cong

Final refuge of Ireland's last High King, now only ruins and river whispers.

★★★☆☆
1

Here in 1198 the last High King of Ireland withdrew: Rory O'Connor (Ruaidhrí Ua Conchobair) spent his final fifteen years as a monk at Cong Abbey after abdicating in the face of the Norman invasion. He died within these walls, was briefly buried here before being reinterred at Clonmacnoise. The Augustinian abbey founded around 1138 preserves remarkable carved Romanesque doorways. The Monk's Fishing House, a medieval appendage on the river, is 50 meters away. Free access year-round.

⏱ 45 min
2

Five hundred meters from the abbey, a cave and a legend: the Pigeon Hole is a limestone passage open in the ground where, according to tradition, an enchanted salmon lived in underground darkness. Short descent on a few steps, naturally Gothic atmosphere. Free and often empty.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Take the Cong Forest Nature Trail—a 3 km loop through woods bordering the Ashford estate. The path follows an unfinished canal, remnant of a Great Famine project (1845–1848) meant to connect the two lakes but never worked because water seeps into the porous limestone. Centuries-old trees, ferns, and underground rivers create an enchanted-forest atmosphere.

⏱ 60 min

Lough Corrib, Ireland's second-largest lake, is renowned for fly fishing—brown trout and salmon. Local operators offer guided boat trips. Even if you don't fish, a walk along the shores at sunset is worth the detour.
