Istanbul

★★★★★
1

Hagia Sophia is the logical starting point for any Istanbul visit. Built in the 6th century under Emperor Justinian, it served as the world's largest cathedral for nine centuries, became a mosque after 1453, a museum in 1935, and returned to mosque status in 2020. Inside, the 31-meter central dome appears to float above a forest of marble columns, while Byzantine mosaics share space with Ottoman calligraphy. Arrive at opening time to beat the security lines, which can exceed an hour during peak season. Women must cover their heads - complimentary scarves are available at the entrance.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Blue Mosque (Sultan Ahmed, 1616) rises across from Hagia Sophia, recognizable by its six minarets - a record that scandalized the sultan's contemporaries. The interior is lined with over 20,000 İznik tiles in blue-and-white floral patterns that bathe the prayer hall in cool, calming light. Entry is free; respect prayer times (the mosque closes to visitors five times daily). Plan to cover shoulders and knees, and remove your shoes.

⏱ 60 min
3

Descend beneath the streets of Sultanahmet: the Basilica Cistern (Yerebatan Sarnıcı) is a 6th-century underground chamber supported by 336 marble columns bathed in amber light. At the far end, two columns rest on carved Medusa head bases - one positioned sideways, the other upside down. According to legend, this orientation neutralized the creature's petrifying gaze. Buy tickets online to skip the queue.

⏱ 60 min
4

The Hippodrome of Constantinople was the civic and sporting heart of Byzantium, capable of holding 100,000 spectators. Only a few monuments remain: the Obelisk of Theodosius (imported from Egypt in 390 AD, in perfect condition), the Serpent Column (brought from Delphi), and the German Fountain gifted by Wilhelm II in 1901. The square makes a pleasant break between visits.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

End your day in Sultanahmet with a çay (Turkish black tea) on one of the terraces facing Hagia Sophia. The sight of illuminated minarets at sunset, with the call to prayer echoing simultaneously from multiple mosques, creates an experience that's hard to forget.

⏱ 45 min

Wander through the Ottoman alleyways between the Hippodrome and the Sea of Marmara - the Arasta Bazaar neighborhood, just behind the Blue Mosque, shelters artisan shops in a covered 17th-century gallery. Less crowded than the Grand Bazaar, it offers a glimpse of what Ottoman markets must have been like.

⏱ 40 min

Istanbul

★★★★★
1

Topkapı Palace served as the administrative and residential heart of the Ottoman Empire for nearly 400 years. This isn't a palace in the European sense - it's a sprawling complex of pavilions, courtyards, and gardens extending over 70 hectares overlooking the Bosphorus. Don't miss the Treasury (86-carat diamond, Suleiman the Magnificent's sword) and the Harem if you're curious about Ottoman court intrigue. The palace is closed on Tuesdays. Allow at least 3 hours.

⏱ 180 min
2

The Süleymaniye Mosque dominates Istanbul from its hilltop perch, a masterwork by architect Mimar Sinan commissioned by Suleiman the Magnificent in 1550. Less crowded than the Blue Mosque, experts consider it a masterpiece of proportion. The adjacent cemetery holds the tombs of Suleiman and his wife Roxelana. The terrace offers one of the finest views over the Golden Horn.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Spice Bazaar (Mısır Çarşısı), built in 1663 near the New Mosque, is an L-shaped covered gallery where bags of sumac, Turkish tea packets, Turkish delight, and dried figs jostle for space. It's considerably more compact than the Grand Bazaar - allow 30 to 45 minutes to explore. Shops near the Eminönü entrance offer the most reasonable prices.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Get lost in the 4,000 shops of the Grand Bazaar (Kapalıçarşı), one of the world's oldest covered markets, operating since the 15th century. Each street specializes: gold jewelry, carpets, ceramics, leather. The golden rule: if a price interests you, negotiate - it's expected. The bazaar opens at 8:30 AM and closes at 7 PM, closed Sundays.

⏱ 90 min

Eat a grilled fish sandwich (balık ekmek) on the Galata Bridge, steps from the Spice Bazaar. Street vendors cook fresh mackerel on their boats moored along the Golden Horn. It's Istanbul's most popular lunch and costs just a handful of lira.

⏱ 30 min

Istanbul

★★★★★
1

Galata Tower (Galata Kulesi) was erected in 1348 by the Genoese at the highest point of their merchant quarter. From the summit (paid access), the panorama encompasses the Golden Horn, Sultanahmet with its minarets, the Bosphorus, and the Asian hills. It's currently partially closed for renovation - check status before visiting. If closed, the nearby Anemon restaurant has a terrace with identical views open to the public.

⏱ 60 min
2

Istiklal Avenue is Beyoğlu's pedestrian artery, stretching 1.5 km between Galata Tower and Taksim Square. From independent bookshops to art-house cinemas, 19th-century covered passages to contemporary galleries - this is secular, cosmopolitan Istanbul revealing itself. The historic red tram (nostaljik tramvay) runs the length several times daily, often crowded but photogenic.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

The public Bosphorus ferry cruise (Şehir Hatları) is the best way to see Istanbul from the water. The short ferry (2 hours round trip) departs Eminönü in early afternoon and passes Dolmabahçe Palace, Rumeli Fortress, Ottoman wooden mansions (yalı), and Ortaköy Mosque. Sit on the left side (European shore) for the best views on the outbound journey.

⏱ 120 min

Explore Karaköy and the Galata neighborhood on foot in late afternoon: third-wave coffee shops, art galleries, vinyl and used bookstores pack the alleys around the tower. This is where Istanbul's alternative cultural scene concentrates, far removed from Sultanahmet's tourist atmosphere.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 340 km · 5h

Bergama

★★★★☆
1

Troy (Truva) lies 30 km south of Çanakkale on the Aegean coast. The archaeological site reveals ten layers of civilizations stacked atop one another, from the Bronze Age to the 4th century AD. Heinrich Schliemann dug his famous trench here in 1871, convinced he'd found Homer's Troy. Today, detailed explanatory panels guide visitors, and a reconstruction of the Trojan Horse stands at the entrance. Allow 1.5 hours to tour the site.

⏱ 90 min
2

Perched on a rocky outcrop dominating the Bakırçay plain, the Acropolis of Pergamon ranks among Turkey's most spectacular ancient sites. The Hellenistic theater, carved into the hillside with 80 rows of seats at a vertiginous angle, accommodates 10,000 spectators and remains in perfect condition. Nearby stand the foundations of the Altar of Zeus (whose friezes now reside in Berlin's Pergamon Museum) and the partially restored Temple of Trajan. Take the cable car up from the lower town (5 minutes) and walk down through the ruins - that's the recommended sequence.

⏱ 150 min
3

The Asclepion, 3 km northwest of the Acropolis, was antiquity's most famous medical center. Treatments included mud baths, music therapy, dream interpretation, and radioactive thermal springs - an approach practiced here in the 2nd century by Galen, personal physician to Marcus Aurelius. The underground tunnel connecting the baths to the main building remains intact: enter and stand silent for a few seconds to experience the acoustic effect.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

After exploring the ruins, head down to Bergama's old Ottoman quarter (Kale Mahallesi) for a meal at one of the family-run restaurants on the main street. Local cuisine revolves around olive oil, fresh figs, köfte, and meze - a welcome contrast after a long day walking exposed archaeological sites.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 100 km · 1h30

Selcuk

★★★★☆
1

Ephesus (Efes) is the best-preserved Roman city in the eastern Mediterranean. The 500-meter Marble Street leads from the Gate of Hercules to the Library of Celsus - a two-story 2nd-century façade that housed 12,000 scrolls and served as a mausoleum for Senator Celsus. The Great Theatre, carved into Mount Panayır, seats 25,000 and possesses acoustics so perfect that a whisper on stage carries to the last row. Plan 4 to 5 hours. Arrive at opening - cruise ships from Kuşadası start disgorging passengers from 9:30 AM.

⏱ 240 min
2

The Terrace Houses of Ephesus are an open-air museum space where excavations continue: wealthy Roman citizens' villas from the 1st-7th centuries have preserved their mosaics, frescoes, and underfloor heating systems. Access costs extra beyond the general ticket, but it's one of the rare places worldwide where you watch archaeologists working overhead from walkways.

⏱ 60 min
3

Şirince, 8 km east of Selçuk in the hills, is a Greek Orthodox village founded in the 1st century. After the 1924 population exchange, Greeks departed and Anatolian refugees took over the wooden-balconied houses. Today, the village is known for its fruit wines (fig, peach, cherry, pomegranate) sold in shops lining the cobbled streets. It's quiet, charming, and 20 minutes' drive from Ephesus.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Taste Şirince's wines directly from village producers. Fig wine (incir şarabı) and pomegranate wine (nar şarabı) are local specialties - closer to sweet nectar than dry wine, but highly representative of Aegean artisan production.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 190 km · 2h30

Pamukkale

★★★★★
1

The travertines of Pamukkale - 'cotton castle' in Turkish - are white limestone terraces formed over 14,000 years by 17 hot springs at 36°C. Water loaded with calcite overflows from basin to basin, creating a frozen cascade of blinding white visible for miles. You can walk barefoot through the pools open to the public (shoes are forbidden to preserve the formations). Enter through the South Gate in early morning - it opens at 6 AM and gives direct access to the terraces before the crowds arrive.

⏱ 90 min
2

Hierapolis, the Greco-Roman city founded in the 2nd century BC above the thermal springs, is included in the same ticket. The Roman theater (2nd century AD) is remarkably well preserved - its marble tiers overlook the Denizli plain with unobstructed views for miles. The necropolis north of the site is one of Anatolia's largest, with tumuli, sarcophagi, and carved-façade tombs extending 2 km.

⏱ 120 min
3

Cleopatra's Pool (Antique Pool) is a 36°C thermal pool nestled at the heart of the archaeological site, its bottom strewn with columns and Roman capitals that collapsed during a 7th-century earthquake. Swimming surrounded by ancient ruins in clear, carbonated water (minerals create natural bubbles) is a rather strange and memorable experience. Access costs extra beyond the general ticket.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Stay on site until sunset: the travertines shift from white to pinkish orange in the late afternoon light, offering dramatically different photos from morning shots. The South Gate remains open until 11 PM in high season, giving you time to enjoy the evening crowd-free.

⏱ 60 min

The Hierapolis Archaeological Museum (inside the site, in the Roman baths) displays statues, sarcophagi, and mosaics from excavations. It's closed Mondays. Beyond the welcome air conditioning in summer, it presents exquisitely carved marble funeral sculptures that contextualize the ancient city.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 200 km · 2h30

Fethiye

★★★★☆
1

The Tombs of Amintas are carved directly into the cliff overlooking Fethiye, a 7-minute walk from the town center. The main tomb - Ionic temple façade style, 4th century BC - is the best-preserved in the Lycian region. A Greek inscription identifies the owner: 'Amintas, son of Hermapias.' The view from the steps over Fethiye Bay and the wooded hills is worth as much as the monument itself. Allow 30 minutes.

⏱ 30 min
2

Ölüdeniz Lagoon is a nearly enclosed bay whose water shifts from turquoise to deep indigo depending on depth, protected by a sand spit classified as a nature park. The inner part of the lagoon (paid access) is calmer and more photogenic; the outer beach is longer but more crowded. Arrive early morning in summer - minibuses from Fethiye fill the site from 10 AM onward.

⏱ 120 min
3

Kayaköy is a Greek village abandoned in 1923 during the population exchange between Greece and Turkey. The former town of Levissi had 6,000 inhabitants; today, 500 stone houses and two churches stand on the slopes, roofless, windowless, overgrown with vegetation. This isn't a reconstructed site - it's a landscape of exile frozen in time. The site is open 8:30 AM to 8 PM. Avoid midday: no shade, intense summer heat.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Paragliding from the summit of Babadağ (1,936 m) above Ölüdeniz is one of the world's most renowned tandem flights. Launch via minibus from Ölüdeniz, the flight lasts 25 to 35 minutes and ends on the beach. The view of the lagoon from the air - deep blue surrounded by mountains - is radically different from the ground-level perspective.

⏱ 120 min

Dine in Fethiye's old quarter (Paspatur), a network of Ottoman alleyways between the covered market and the harbor. Fish restaurants lining the old fish market (former hamam converted to dining space) serve the catch of the day at reasonable prices - grouper, sea bream, grilled prawns with meze starters.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 110 km · 1h40

Kas

★★★★☆
1

Kaş is a small fishing town turned diving and excursion base, with a Hellenistic theater carved into the hillside above the harbor. This 4th-century BC theater seats 4,000 spectators and retains its tiers and part of its stage. Entry is free, and the view over the sea from the upper tiers is particularly beautiful in late afternoon.

⏱ 45 min
2

The sunken city of Kekova is a protected island whose shallow waters reveal foundations, staircases, and façades of a Lycian port submerged during a 2nd-century earthquake. Boat excursions from Kaş (or from Uçağız, 45 minutes by minibus) cruise close to the ruins - diving is prohibited in the protected zone, but in clear water you see structures from the deck. The stop at Simena village (Kaleköy), accessible only by boat, with its medieval castle perched on the hill and Lycian sarcophagi emerging from the water, is the day's other highlight.

⏱ 360 min

Activities

The Lion Tomb (Aslan Mezarı), a two-minute walk from Kaş harbor, is a 4th-century BC Lycian sarcophagus placed on a carved pedestal amid houses - a testament to how Lycians integrated their dead into daily life. Other sarcophagi dot the town's alleyways.

⏱ 15 min

Book the Kekova boat excursion the evening before directly at Kaş harbor. Most operators depart around 9-10 AM and return late afternoon. Prefer small glass-bottom boats (max 12 people) over crowded gulets - you'll see the underwater ruins better and swimming stops are more enjoyable.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 70 km · 1h15

Olympos

★★★★☆
1

The ruins of Olympos extend through a wooded valley opening directly onto the sea. The Lycian city, founded in the 4th century BC, was abandoned in the 14th century. Today, remnants - Roman baths, theater, ancient pier, Byzantine basilica - emerge from Mediterranean vegetation along a riverbed. The atmosphere is profoundly different from exposed rocky sites like Ephesus or Pergamon: here everything is green, shaded, slightly wild. The beach at the trail's end is a protected Caretta caretta turtle nesting site.

⏱ 120 min
2

Yanartaş - the 'burning rock' - is a wind-swept mountainside where flames fed by natural methane and hydrogen emissions have burned for over 2,500 years. A dozen fissures in the rock burn continuously across more than 5,000 m². The site is 3.5 km from Çıralı; the forest trail ascent takes 20 to 30 minutes. The flames are barely visible in daylight - reserve this visit for after sunset, when the glowing lights dance in darkness. Directly beneath the fires lie ruins of a temple to Hephaestus, god of fire.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Spend the morning on Olympos beach before visiting the ruins. The beach is pebbles and sand, framed by wooded cliffs - considerably less crowded than neighboring resorts. Between May and October, signs mark turtle nests identified by park rangers: don't walk on them.

⏱ 90 min

The nighttime Yanartaş visit is simpler in a group: shuttles depart from Olympos accommodations in the evening (typically around 9 PM). Bring a headlamp for the trail. Local tradition involves boiling water for tea directly on the natural flames - you'll likely see other visitors doing this.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 80 km · 1h10

Antalya

★★★★☆
1

Kaleiçi is Antalya's historic heart, a maze of Ottoman alleyways squeezed between ancient Roman and Byzantine walls descending to a marina. Timber-framed 18th-century houses, Seljuk minarets, and Roman remnants succeed one another across a few hundred meters. The best way to discover Kaleiçi is to get lost: start at Hadrian's Gate and walk toward the harbor without a map, turning randomly down alleyways.

⏱ 90 min
2

Hadrian's Gate is a three-arched triumphal arch built in 130 AD to commemorate Emperor Hadrian's visit. It's the only Roman gate still standing in Antalya and marks the entrance to Kaleiçi from the modern city. Look at the joints in the floor slabs: you can still discern ruts carved by chariots over more than 1,900 years of passage. Entry is free.

⏱ 20 min
3

The Antalya Museum is one of Turkey's richest for ancient sculpture. It displays large-scale Greco-Roman deity statues discovered at Perge, a few kilometers from the city - Artemis, Hermes, Adonis, Apollo - plus an Ottoman ethnography hall and a gallery of exceptional sarcophagi. Allow 2 hours. Closed Mondays.

⏱ 120 min
4

The Düden Waterfalls are two separate sites east of the city. The Upper Düden (12 km from center) is a 15-meter fall surrounded by a wooded park where you can walk behind the water curtain. The Lower Düden - best viewed by boat from the marina or from the Düden Park cliff - plunges directly into the Mediterranean from a 30-meter cliff, offering a rare spectacle: a waterfall diving into the sea.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Take a boat from Kaleiçi marina to observe the Lower Düden from the sea. Most marina tour boats make this coastal circuit in 2 hours with swimming stops in coves. It's the most spectacular way to see the waterfall plunging into the sea - and a good introduction to Antalya's coastline.

⏱ 120 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 300 km · 4h

Konya

★★★☆☆
1

The Mevlana Museum is the mausoleum of Jalal al-Din Rumi (1207-1273), Sufi poet and founder of the whirling dervish order. Its turquoise-tiled conical dome is one of Turkey's most recognizable symbols. Inside, the hall beneath the dome houses 66 sarcophagi draped in gold-embroidered velvet, including Rumi's surmounted by a large turban. Adjacent rooms display his clothing, the original Mathnawi manuscript (25,000 verses), Sufi musical instruments, and a Quran copy so small its author went blind writing it. Entry is free. Arrive before noon to avoid pilgrim buses.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Alaeddin Mosque crowns the eponymous hill in central Konya, built by Seljuk sultans between the 12th and 13th centuries. It's one of Turkey's oldest mosques, with a forest of ancient columns salvaged from Roman and Byzantine sites. The terrace around the mosque offers circular views over the city and its minarets. Rumi is said to have delivered sermons here.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

If you're in Konya on a Saturday evening, attend the Sema ceremony at the Mevlana Cultural Center, a 15-minute walk from the museum. The whirling dervishes spin for 30 to 45 minutes in absolute silence, arms open - right hand toward heaven to receive divine grace, left hand toward earth to transmit it. This isn't a tourist show: it's an active spiritual practice.

⏱ 90 min

Sample Konya's cuisine: the city is known for its fırın kebabı (lamb cooked in an earthenware bread oven, local specialty), etli ekmek (thin, elongated minced lamb pizza cooked over wood fire), and honey pastries. The neighborhood around Mevlana Museum abounds with family restaurants serving these dishes at reasonable prices.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 240 km · 3h

Goreme

★★★★★
1

The Sultanhanı caravanserai, 40 km east of Konya, is one of the best-preserved Silk Road stops. Built in 1229 by Seljuk Sultan Alaeddin Keykubat, it could accommodate several hundred merchants, their camels, and their goods. The plan is classic: a large open courtyard surrounded by porticos, extended by a vaulted covered hall whose entrance is adorned with an exquisitely carved Seljuk portal. Caravanserais were spaced a day's march apart - about 30 to 40 km - along the entire route between Persia and Anatolia.

⏱ 60 min
2

Ihlara Valley is a 14-km canyon carved by the Melendiz River through Cappadocian volcanic tuff. The vertical walls reach 100 meters high and shelter over 100 Byzantine rock churches carved by monks between the 4th and 13th centuries. The most popular hike runs from Ihlara village to Belisirma village (4 km, 2 hours), with lunch at one of the restaurants on stilted terraces above the river.

⏱ 150 min
3

Selime Monastery closes Ihlara Valley to the north. It's Cappadocia's largest rock-cut religious complex: a cathedral the size of a church nave, a pyramid-shaped kitchen, monk dormitories, stables for Silk Road caravans' camels - all carved into the cliff across multiple floors. The 9th-13th centuries left partially legible frescoes. The ascent from the road is short but steep.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Arrival in Göreme in late afternoon. Climb to the Esentepe viewpoint (2 km from center) to see the classic Cappadocia panorama: fairy chimneys, Pigeon Valley, and - if you're lucky - the last hot air balloons landing at dusk.

⏱ 45 min

Goreme

★★★★★
1

The Göreme Open-Air Museum is a complex of 14 rock-cut chapels and monasteries from the 4th to 13th centuries, UNESCO World Heritage since 1985. The 11th-12th century Byzantine frescoes here are among the world's best preserved - life-of-Christ scenes in the Sandal Church (Çarıklı Kilise), saint portraits in the Snake Church (Yılanlı Kilise), and especially the extraordinarily vivid colors of the Dark Church (Karanlık Kilise, paid extra access), preserved thanks to lack of light that blocked pigment oxidation for centuries. Arrive at opening (8 AM) to tour before the buses.

⏱ 120 min
2

Uçhisar is a village built around a tuff pinnacle pierced with hundreds of caves - the region's highest point. From the summit (paid access), the view encompasses all Cappadocia's valleys within a 20-km radius: Göreme, Pigeon Valley, the Three Beauties, and distant Mount Erciyes. The walk down to Göreme through Pigeon Valley takes 45 minutes.

⏱ 60 min
3

Pigeon Valley (Güvercinlik Vadisi) takes its name from hundreds of niches carved into walls to house pigeons - whose droppings fertilized vineyards and orchards below. The hiking trail between Uçhisar and Göreme (3 km) crosses the valley at the canyon bottom, among rocks sculpted by erosion into organic shapes.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Sunset at Red Valley (Kızılçukur): the tuff rocks shift from bright red to deep orange in the last 30 minutes of light. It's Cappadocia's most photographed viewpoint in late afternoon. Several cliffside cafés rent lounge chairs.

⏱ 60 min

Book your hot air balloon flight for tomorrow morning tonight. Reputable Göreme companies post their departures on signs along the main street. The flight lasts 60 to 90 minutes at sunrise, with champagne on landing. Weather conditions can cause cancellation - operators contact you the evening before or at 5 AM.

⏱ 30 min

Goreme

★★★★★
1

Sunrise hot air balloon flight: launch occurs between 5:30 and 6 AM depending on season. For 60 to 90 minutes, the basket drifts silently above valleys - fairy chimneys, troglodyte villages, carpets of vineyards and orchards. At dawn, dozens of balloons rise simultaneously into the same sky, creating the spectacle that has become Cappadocia's most recognizable image. It's not cheap, but it's one of the rare activities that truly delivers on its promise.

⏱ 180 min
2

The underground city of Derinkuyu descends 8 levels to 85 meters depth and could shelter 20,000 people with their livestock, food stores, and wine presses. Christians took refuge here during Arab invasions between the 8th and 10th centuries. The most striking element is the 'millstone door': 500-kg stone discs that inhabitants could roll from inside to seal the tunnels. Derinkuyu is open April to October. The tunnels are narrow and low - not recommended for claustrophobes.

⏱ 120 min
3

Paşabağ (Monks Valley) hosts Cappadocia's most photographed multi-headed fairy chimneys. These tuff columns capped with harder basalt blocks were hollowed out by Byzantine hermits who carved multi-story cells and chapels. The surrounding vegetation - vines, apricot trees, and poplars - contrasts with the rocks' minerality. Allow 45 minutes.

⏱ 45 min
4

Devrent Valley (Imagination Valley) is a chaos of eroded rock formations shaped like animals that visitors enjoy identifying: camel, dolphin, snake, human figure. No paid entry, no archaeological site - just bizarre geology in a lunar landscape. 30 minutes suffices. It's often the day's last stop before returning to Göreme.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Stop in Avanos, on the banks of the Kızılırmak River (Red River), Cappadocia's pottery capital since Hittite times. Potters work with local red clay on foot-powered wheels, a technique transmitted for 4,000 years. Several workshops offer demonstrations and let you try.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 550 km · 1h

Gaziantep

★★★☆☆
1

The Zeugma Mosaic Museum is the world's largest mosaic museum, with 30,000 m² of space and a collection of 1,700 m² of Roman mosaics rescued urgently in 2000, just before the Birecik Dam submerged the ancient city of Zeugma beneath Euphrates waters. The centerpiece - the Gypsy Girl of Zeugma, a 2nd-century portrait of a woman with piercing, expressive eyes - has become Gaziantep's face. Allow at minimum two hours for the ground floor alone. English explanatory panels are detailed.

⏱ 120 min
2

Gaziantep Citadel is a fortress built on Hittite foundations, remodeled in Roman, Byzantine, then Ottoman times. The interior museum recounts the city's resistance against French forces during the 1920 War of Independence - the episode that earned the city its 'Gazi' prefix (Warrior for the faith). From the ramparts, the view over the old town and its limestone roofs is excellent.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Get lost in the Coppersmiths Bazaar (Bakırcılar Çarşısı), where artisans have hand-hammered copper since the 16th century. The sound of hammers on metal, pyramids of engraved trays, and polished copper coffeepots make this one of Turkey's most authentic bazaars. Mornings are livelier.

⏱ 60 min

Gaziantep is the world capital of baklava - the city counts over 180 registered pastry makers. The best baklava is eaten early morning, fresh from the oven: the phyllo layers stay crisp for just a few hours. Antep pistachio, grown locally, is fattier and more fragrant than imported varieties.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 150 km · 2h

Sanl Urfa

★★★★☆
1

Göbekli Tepe is the world's oldest known temple - 11,500 years old, 6,000 years older than Stonehenge. Hunter-gatherers erected T-shaped limestone pillars 5 meters high, carved with animals (foxes, snakes, boars, vultures) without any metal tools, without agriculture, without cities. The discovery completely overturned the idea that civilization precedes religion - here, it's the reverse. The site is 25 km from Şanlıurfa, accessible by taxi (25 min). A modern visitor center with models and videos stands at the entrance.

⏱ 150 min
2

The Pool of Abraham (Balıklıgöl) is a sacred body of water in the heart of old Şanlıurfa, surrounded by mosques and gardens. According to Islamic tradition, this is where Abraham was thrown into the fire by King Nimrod, and God transformed the flames into water and the embers into fish - the pool's sacred carp have been fed by the faithful for centuries. The atmosphere is peaceful and surprising, quite different from the region's archaeological sites.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Şanlıurfa Archaeological Museum is one of Turkey's most modern. It displays the Urfa Man - a 9th-millennium BC Neolithic statue, the oldest known life-size human statue - plus mosaics and objects from the region, including pieces linked to Göbekli Tepe and recently discovered satellite sites like Karahan Tepe.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Explore the covered Gümrük Hanı bazaar, a 16th-century Ottoman caravanserai converted to a market. You'll find spices, carpets, copper, and regional textiles. Şanlıurfa's cuisine differs from Gaziantep's: spicier, featuring Urfa kebab (smoked red pepper), lahmacun with fresh herbs, and şıra, a fermented grape drink.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 190 km · 3h

Adiyaman

★★★☆☆
1

Mount Nemrut (2,134 m) is a 1st-century BC royal mausoleum: King Antiochus I of Commagene erected 8-to-9-meter statues representing Zeus, Apollo, Heracles, and himself on two terraces facing east and west. All heads have been decapitated - they lie on the ground, arranged by size and surrounded by eagle and lion heads. Sunrise on the east terrace, when light pierces through the statues, is the most powerful moment. The hike from the parking lot takes 20 to 30 minutes. Bring warm clothes: even in summer, the wind is freezing at the summit before dawn.

⏱ 180 min
2

On the descent, stop at the Roman bridge of Cendere, built in the 2nd century AD for Emperor Septimius Severus, spanning a Kahta Çay gorge on a single 34-meter arch. The bridge remains in perfect condition and still usable on foot. Along the road, the Karakuş tumulus, a royal mausoleum of Antiochus's family, is visible from the road.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

To see sunrise on Nemrut, depart Adiyaman around 2:30-3 AM. The drive up to the parking lot takes about 1.5 hours. Hotels in Kahta or Karadut (villages at the mountain's foot) offer night shuttles and are better positioned - consider this if you want to avoid waking before 3 AM from Adiyaman.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 200 km · 3h30

Mardin

★★★★☆
1

Mardin's old town is built on a rocky ridge overlooking the Mesopotamian plain as far as the eye can see. All houses, madrasas, and mosques are constructed from local golden limestone, giving the city a warm, uniform hue quite different from the rest of Turkey. It's a labyrinth of covered alleys where 12th-century mosques, Syriac churches, Ottoman hammams, and mansions with tall windows succeed one another. The city is undergoing UNESCO inscription.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Deyrulzafaran Monastery (Mor Hananyo) is 7 km east of Mardin, by taxi. Founded in the 5th century on ruins of a pre-Christian Assyrian sun temple, it served as seat of the Syriac Orthodox patriarchate for 640 years until 1932. You visit in guided groups: the main church (6th century), the former patriarchal throne room, the sun temple crypt whose ceiling has held without mortar for centuries. The monastery is still active - monks live, teach, and worship here.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Settle in for the evening on a terrace overlooking the plain to watch the sunset. Late afternoon's oblique light transforms Mardin's golden stones to deep ochre, while shadows stretch across the Syrian plain to the horizon. Several restaurants offer local dishes - kaburga dolması (slow-cooked stuffed lamb), Kurdish meze, and wines from the Midyat region.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 380 km · 6h

Van

★★☆☆☆
1

Akdamar Island houses the Church of the Holy Cross, built in 921 AD by Armenian King Gagik Artzruni. Its entire exterior façade is sculpted with biblical reliefs - Adam and Eve, Jonah and the whale, David and Goliath, Abraham and Isaac - of remarkable finesse and preservation for an eleven-century edifice. The interior preserves fresco fragments. The island is 3 km from shore; boats depart Akdamar port (40 km west of Van city) once a group forms (minimum 10 people).

⏱ 120 min
2

Van Castle (Van Kalesi) is a 9th-century BC Urartian fortress built on a 1,200-meter-long rock dominating the lake. The Urartians carved their cuneiform inscriptions directly into the rock - some remain legible. At the rock's base, remnants of old Van are visible: three mosques isolated in a grass field, all that survives of a city destroyed in 1915.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Van breakfast (Van kahvaltısı) is a local institution: about twenty dishes spread across the table - local cheeses, fresh herbs, mountain honey, cream, buttered eggs, kavut (sweetened toasted flour), and otlu peynir (wild herb cheese). It's one of Turkey's most copious and flavorful breakfasts. Several lakeside restaurants serve it.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 380 km · 5h30

Kars

★★★☆☆
1

Ani is a medieval ghost city on a wind-swept plateau, 44 km east of Kars, meters from the Armenian border. Capital of the Armenian Bagratid kingdom in the 10th century, it rivaled Constantinople and Cairo. In 1001, Ani Cathedral was completed - its pointed arches preceded European Gothic architecture by a century. The city was ravaged by Mongols in 1237, devastated by an earthquake in 1319, then abandoned in the 18th century. UNESCO since 2016. The public bus departs Kars at 9 AM, returns at 12:45 PM - verify locally.

⏱ 180 min
2

The Church of Tigran Honents (1215) is the jewel of Ani's ruins: its interior walls are entirely covered with frescoes depicting the life of Christ and Saint Gregory the Illuminator, in astonishingly good condition for a building exposed to the elements for 800 years. Perched on the edge of the Arpaçay canyon with views over Armenia as backdrop, its location further intensifies the site's power.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Back in Kars, taste Kars gravyeri cheese, a hard cheese produced in the region since the Russian era (19th century). Kars aging cellars produce a local gruyère recognizable by its slightly nutty flavor. Several artisan cheese makers in the city sell directly.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 280 km · 1h

Trabzon

★★★☆☆
1

Sümela Monastery is built into a cliff face 300 meters above the Altındere Valley, in the national park of the same name. Founded in 386 AD by two Greek monks who reportedly found an icon of the Virgin in a natural cave, it developed until the 19th century before abandonment in 1923. The main façade - the massive monks' building cantilevered on black rock - is the Black Sea Turkish coast's most recognizable image. The interior includes the rock church with its 14th-18th century frescoes. Access: Kars → Trabzon flight in the morning, then shuttle (45 min from Trabzon).

⏱ 150 min
2

Trabzon's Hagia Sophia is a 13th-century Byzantine church built during the Empire of Trebizond, converted to a mosque. Its interior mosaics and frescoes (partially visible) and slender bell tower contrast with the surrounding Ottoman architecture. It's located 3 km west of the city center.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Climb Boztepe in late afternoon, the hill overlooking Trabzon at 350 meters. The hill's cafés serve Turkish tea with panoramic views over the Black Sea, wooded hills, and the city. It's Trabzon's classic viewpoint at sunset - and an opportunity to taste Trabzon hazelnuts, as the region is the world's leading producer.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 380 km · 4h

Amasya

★★★☆☆
1

Amasya is an extraordinary city squeezed into a narrow gorge: timber-framed Ottoman houses line the banks of the Yeşilırmak, and just above, carved into the cliff 30 meters high, five tombs of the Pontic kings (3rd-2nd centuries BC) gaze over the city. These monumental sepulchers belong to the founders of the Pontic kingdom - Mithridates I, Ariobarzanes, Mithridates II, Mithridates III, and Pharnaces I. The climb from the riverside takes 20 minutes. The view over the valley from the tombs matches the effort. At night, the tombs are illuminated - the effect is striking from the water's edge.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Yalıboyu district (waterside houses) is a succession of Ottoman konak - three-story houses with wooden overhangs, brown-tiled roofs, built between the 17th and 19th centuries directly on the Yeşilırmak's edge. Several have been restored as boutique hotels or cafés. A slow walk along the north bank quay - or better, from the opposite bank - gives the fullest view of the architectural ensemble.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Stay in Amasya for dinner and the evening walk: when the tombs light up and their reflections dance on the green Yeşilırmak waters, the city takes on an atmosphere few Turkish cities can rival. Several restaurants on pontoons at water's edge serve local trout, sheep's cheese, and manti (Turkish ravioli).

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 85 km · 1h30

Bogazkale

★★★★☆
1

Hattusa is the capital of the Hittite Empire, founded around 1650 BC and World Heritage since 1986. The site covers several square kilometers on a mountainous plateau: reconstructed ramparts, Temple of the Storm God, Lion Gate (two sculpted lions flanking the entrance), Sphinx Gate, and the King's Gate with its relief of a warrior deity. The Lion Gate is the site's symbol - its gaping-mouthed lions still announce the city after 3,500 years. The circuit by car (or on foot, 2-3 hours) runs about 7 km.

⏱ 150 min
2

Yazılıkaya is Hattusa's rock sanctuary, 2 km from the main entrance. Two natural rock galleries were decorated in the 13th century BC with processions of Hittite deities sculpted in relief. Gallery A shows the complete pantheon - 65 gods and goddesses marching. Gallery B, smaller and better preserved, depicts King Tudhaliya IV surrounded by his protective god. It's the world's best-preserved ensemble of Hittite reliefs. Don't skip Yazılıkaya, even if pressed for time.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Boğazkale Museum, in the village below the site, presents originals from the site: cuneiform tablet fragments, royal seals, ceramics, and figurines. Most major pieces are at Ankara's Museum of Anatolian Civilizations, but the local museum effectively complements the site visit in 30 minutes.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 190 km · 2h30

Safranbolu

★★★★☆
1

Safranbolu is Turkey's best-preserved Ottoman town, UNESCO-listed since 1994. Over 2,000 timber-framed houses from the 17th-19th centuries line the slopes of a narrow gorge: carved wooden overhangs, brown-tiled roofs, windowless street-side façades opening onto bright interior courtyards. Ottoman urbanism here remains intact - no concrete, no modern buildings visible from the cobbled alleys. The town was built on its position as a Silk Road caravanserai, and this mercantile prosperity reads in the quality of the carpentry and painted ceilings.

⏱ 150 min
2

The Cinci Han caravanserai (17th century) is Safranbolu's largest building - two stories of merchant cells, a central courtyard, ground-floor stables. It's been converted to a hotel and remains the best place to sleep at the heart of the historic site. Even if you don't stay there, the interior courtyard is freely visited.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Kaymakamlar House-Museum (19th century) is one of the town's best-preserved Ottoman mansions, open to visitors. The interior shows the typical layout of a konak: men's hall with central sofa, bridal chamber, kitchen, built-in cupboards, carved coffered ceiling. The costumed mannequins are kitschy but effective for understanding Ottoman domestic spatial organization.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Climb Hıdırlık Hill in late afternoon to see Safranbolu from above: a sea of brown-tiled roofs, minarets, and chimneys extending through the green gorge. It's the town's classic viewpoint. Several hilltop cafés serve tea and saffron-flavored lokum - a local specialty for centuries.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 420 km · 5h

Istanbul

★★★★★
1

Return to Istanbul by the Safranbolu → Istanbul route (420 km, about 4.5 hours via highway). This final leg crosses the wooded hills of northern Anatolia before descending toward the Sea of Marmara and the first silhouettes of Istanbul's minarets. Arrival in the afternoon. The 25-day loop is complete.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Closing evening on the Bosphorus: take a public ferry from Eminönü to see Istanbul one last time from the water. The 20-minute journey to Üsküdar and back costs a few lira and offers the same view as the Day 3 cruise - but with the feeling of having traveled all across Turkey between the two.

⏱ 60 min
