Istanbul

★★★★★
1

Hagia Sophia is the logical starting point for any Istanbul visit. Built in the 6th century under Emperor Justinian, it served as the world's largest cathedral for nine centuries, became a mosque after 1453, a museum in 1935, and returned to mosque status in 2020. Inside, the 31-meter central dome appears to float above a forest of marble columns, while Byzantine mosaics share space with Ottoman calligraphy. Arrive at opening time to beat the security lines, which can exceed an hour during peak season. Women must cover their heads - complimentary scarves are available at the entrance.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Blue Mosque (Sultan Ahmed, 1616) rises across from Hagia Sophia, recognizable by its six minarets - a record that scandalized the sultan's contemporaries. The interior is lined with over 20,000 İznik tiles in blue-and-white floral patterns that bathe the prayer hall in cool, calming light. Entry is free; respect prayer times (the mosque closes to visitors five times daily). Plan to cover shoulders and knees, and remove your shoes.

⏱ 60 min
3

Descend beneath the streets of Sultanahmet: the Basilica Cistern (Yerebatan Sarnıcı) is a 6th-century underground chamber supported by 336 marble columns bathed in amber light. At the far end, two columns rest on carved Medusa head bases - one positioned sideways, the other upside down. According to legend, this orientation neutralized the creature's petrifying gaze. Buy tickets online to skip the queue.

⏱ 60 min
4

The Hippodrome of Constantinople was the civic and sporting heart of Byzantium, capable of holding 100,000 spectators. Only a few monuments remain: the Obelisk of Theodosius (imported from Egypt in 390 AD, in perfect condition), the Serpent Column (brought from Delphi), and the German Fountain gifted by Wilhelm II in 1901. The square makes a pleasant break between visits.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

End your day in Sultanahmet with a çay (Turkish black tea) on one of the terraces facing Hagia Sophia. The sight of illuminated minarets at sunset, with the call to prayer echoing simultaneously from multiple mosques, creates an experience that's hard to forget.

⏱ 45 min

Wander through the Ottoman alleyways between the Hippodrome and the Sea of Marmara - the Arasta Bazaar neighborhood, just behind the Blue Mosque, shelters artisan shops in a covered 17th-century gallery. Less crowded than the Grand Bazaar, it offers a glimpse of what Ottoman markets must have been like.

⏱ 40 min

Istanbul

★★★★★
1

Topkapı Palace served as the administrative and residential heart of the Ottoman Empire for nearly 400 years. This isn't a palace in the European sense - it's a sprawling complex of pavilions, courtyards, and gardens extending over 70 hectares overlooking the Bosphorus. Don't miss the Treasury (86-carat diamond, Suleiman the Magnificent's sword) and the Harem if you're curious about Ottoman court intrigue. The palace is closed on Tuesdays. Allow at least 3 hours.

⏱ 180 min
2

The Süleymaniye Mosque dominates Istanbul from its hilltop perch, a masterwork by architect Mimar Sinan commissioned by Suleiman the Magnificent in 1550. Less crowded than the Blue Mosque, experts consider it a masterpiece of proportion. The adjacent cemetery holds the tombs of Suleiman and his wife Roxelana. The terrace offers one of the finest views over the Golden Horn.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Spice Bazaar (Mısır Çarşısı), built in 1663 near the New Mosque, is an L-shaped covered gallery where bags of sumac, Turkish tea packets, Turkish delight, and dried figs jostle for space. It's considerably more compact than the Grand Bazaar - allow 30 to 45 minutes to explore. Shops near the Eminönü entrance offer the most reasonable prices.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Get lost in the 4,000 shops of the Grand Bazaar (Kapalıçarşı), one of the world's oldest covered markets, operating since the 15th century. Each street specializes: gold jewelry, carpets, ceramics, leather. The golden rule: if a price interests you, negotiate - it's expected. The bazaar opens at 8:30 AM and closes at 7 PM, closed Sundays.

⏱ 90 min

Eat a grilled fish sandwich (balık ekmek) on the Galata Bridge, steps from the Spice Bazaar. Street vendors cook fresh mackerel on their boats moored along the Golden Horn. It's Istanbul's most popular lunch and costs just a handful of lira.

⏱ 30 min

Istanbul

★★★★★
1

Galata Tower (Galata Kulesi) was erected in 1348 by the Genoese at the highest point of their merchant quarter. From the summit (paid access), the panorama encompasses the Golden Horn, Sultanahmet with its minarets, the Bosphorus, and the Asian hills. It's currently partially closed for renovation - check status before visiting. If closed, the nearby Anemon restaurant has a terrace with identical views open to the public.

⏱ 60 min
2

Istiklal Avenue is Beyoğlu's pedestrian artery, stretching 1.5 km between Galata Tower and Taksim Square. From independent bookshops to art-house cinemas, 19th-century covered passages to contemporary galleries - this is secular, cosmopolitan Istanbul revealing itself. The historic red tram (nostaljik tramvay) runs the length several times daily, often crowded but photogenic.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

The public Bosphorus ferry cruise (Şehir Hatları) is the best way to see Istanbul from the water. The short ferry (2 hours round trip) departs Eminönü in early afternoon and passes Dolmabahçe Palace, Rumeli Fortress, Ottoman wooden mansions (yalı), and Ortaköy Mosque. Sit on the left side (European shore) for the best views on the outbound journey.

⏱ 120 min

Explore Karaköy and the Galata neighborhood on foot in late afternoon: third-wave coffee shops, art galleries, vinyl and used bookstores pack the alleys around the tower. This is where Istanbul's alternative cultural scene concentrates, far removed from Sultanahmet's tourist atmosphere.

Bergama

★★☆☆☆
1

Troy (Truva) lies 30 km south of Çanakkale on the Aegean coast. The archaeological site reveals ten layers of civilizations stacked atop one another, from the Bronze Age to the 4th century AD. Heinrich Schliemann dug his famous trench here in 1871, convinced he'd found Homer's Troy. Today, detailed explanatory panels guide visitors, and a reconstruction of the Trojan Horse stands at the entrance. Allow 1.5 hours to tour the site.

⏱ 90 min
2

Perched on a rocky outcrop dominating the Bakırçay plain, the Acropolis of Pergamon ranks among Turkey's most spectacular ancient sites. The Hellenistic theater, carved into the hillside with 80 rows of seats at a vertiginous angle, accommodates 10,000 spectators and remains in perfect condition. Nearby stand the foundations of the Altar of Zeus (whose friezes now reside in Berlin's Pergamon Museum) and the partially restored Temple of Trajan. Take the cable car up from the lower town (5 minutes) and walk down through the ruins - that's the recommended sequence.

⏱ 150 min
3

The Asclepion, 3 km northwest of the Acropolis, was antiquity's most famous medical center. Treatments included mud baths, music therapy, dream interpretation, and radioactive thermal springs - an approach practiced here in the 2nd century by Galen, personal physician to Marcus Aurelius. The underground tunnel connecting the baths to the main building remains intact: enter and stand silent for a few seconds to experience the acoustic effect.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

After exploring the ruins, head down to Bergama's old Ottoman quarter (Kale Mahallesi) for a meal at one of the family-run restaurants on the main street. Local cuisine revolves around olive oil, fresh figs, köfte, and meze - a welcome contrast after a long day walking exposed archaeological sites.

⏱ 60 min

Selcuk

★★★★☆
1

Ephesus (Efes) is the best-preserved Roman city in the eastern Mediterranean. The 500-meter Marble Street leads from the Gate of Hercules to the Library of Celsus - a two-story 2nd-century façade that housed 12,000 scrolls and served as a mausoleum for Senator Celsus. The Great Theatre, carved into Mount Panayır, seats 25,000 and possesses acoustics so perfect that a whisper on stage carries to the last row. Plan 4 to 5 hours. Arrive at opening - cruise ships from Kuşadası start disgorging passengers from 9:30 AM.

⏱ 240 min
2

The Terrace Houses of Ephesus are an open-air museum space where excavations continue: wealthy Roman citizens' villas from the 1st-7th centuries have preserved their mosaics, frescoes, and underfloor heating systems. Access costs extra beyond the general ticket, but it's one of the rare places worldwide where you watch archaeologists working overhead from walkways.

⏱ 60 min
3

Şirince, 8 km east of Selçuk in the hills, is a Greek Orthodox village founded in the 1st century. After the 1924 population exchange, Greeks departed and Anatolian refugees took over the wooden-balconied houses. Today, the village is known for its fruit wines (fig, peach, cherry, pomegranate) sold in shops lining the cobbled streets. It's quiet, charming, and 20 minutes' drive from Ephesus.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Taste Şirince's wines directly from village producers. Fig wine (incir şarabı) and pomegranate wine (nar şarabı) are local specialties - closer to sweet nectar than dry wine, but highly representative of Aegean artisan production.

⏱ 45 min

Pamukkale

★★★☆☆
1

The travertines of Pamukkale - 'cotton castle' in Turkish - are white limestone terraces formed over 14,000 years by 17 hot springs at 36°C. Water loaded with calcite overflows from basin to basin, creating a frozen cascade of blinding white visible for miles. You can walk barefoot through the pools open to the public (shoes are forbidden to preserve the formations). Enter through the South Gate in early morning - it opens at 6 AM and gives direct access to the terraces before the crowds arrive.

⏱ 90 min
2

Hierapolis, the Greco-Roman city founded in the 2nd century BC above the thermal springs, is included in the same ticket. The Roman theater (2nd century AD) is remarkably well preserved - its marble tiers overlook the Denizli plain with unobstructed views for miles. The necropolis north of the site is one of Anatolia's largest, with tumuli, sarcophagi, and carved-façade tombs extending 2 km.

⏱ 120 min
3

Cleopatra's Pool (Antique Pool) is a 36°C thermal pool nestled at the heart of the archaeological site, its bottom strewn with columns and Roman capitals that collapsed during a 7th-century earthquake. Swimming surrounded by ancient ruins in clear, carbonated water (minerals create natural bubbles) is a rather strange and memorable experience. Access costs extra beyond the general ticket.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Stay on site until sunset: the travertines shift from white to pinkish orange in the late afternoon light, offering dramatically different photos from morning shots. The South Gate remains open until 11 PM in high season, giving you time to enjoy the evening crowd-free.

⏱ 60 min

The Hierapolis Archaeological Museum (inside the site, in the Roman baths) displays statues, sarcophagi, and mosaics from excavations. It's closed Mondays. Beyond the welcome air conditioning in summer, it presents exquisitely carved marble funeral sculptures that contextualize the ancient city.

1

The ruins of Olympos extend through a wooded valley opening directly onto the sea. The Lycian city, founded in the 4th century BC, was abandoned in the 14th century. Activities

⏱ 90 min

The nighttime Yanartaş visit is simpler in a group: shuttles depart from Olympos accommodations in the evening (typically around 9 PM). Bring a headlamp for the trail. Local tradition involves boiling water for tea directly on the natural flames - you'll likely see other visitors doing this.

The Mevlana Museum is the mausoleum of Jalal al-Din Rumi (1207-1273), Sufi poet and founder of the whirling dervish order. The Sultanhanı caravanserai, 40 km east of Konya, is one of the best-preserved Silk Road stops. The 9th-13th centuries left partially legible frescoes. The ascent from the road is short but steep.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

★★★★☆
1

Sunrise hot air balloon flight: launch occurs between 5:30 and 6 AM depending on season. For 60 to 90 minutes, the basket drifts silently above valleys - fairy chimneys, troglodyte villages, carpets of vineyards and orchards. The underground city of Derinkuyu descends 8 levels to 85 meters depth and could shelter 20,000 people with their livestock, food stores, and wine presses. Christians took refuge here during Arab invasions between the 8th and 10th centuries. Activities

Sanl Urfa

Göbekli Tepe is the world's oldest known temple - 11,500 years old, 6,000 years older than Stonehenge. Hunter-gatherers erected T-shaped limestone pillars 5 meters high, carved with animals (foxes, snakes, boars, vultures) without any metal tools, without agriculture, without cities. The Pool of Abraham (Balıklıgöl) is a sacred body of water in the heart of old Şanlıurfa, surrounded by mosques and gardens. The atmosphere is peaceful and surprising, quite different from the region's archaeological sites.

Amasya

★★★★☆
1

Amasya is an extraordinary city squeezed into a narrow gorge: timber-framed Ottoman houses line the banks of the Yeşilırmak, and just above, carved into the cliff 30 meters high, five tombs of the Pontic kings (3rd-2nd centuries BC) gaze over the city. The climb from the riverside takes 20 minutes. The view over the valley from the tombs matches the effort. At night, the tombs are illuminated - the effect is striking from the water's edge.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Yalıboyu district (waterside houses) is a succession of Ottoman konak - three-story houses with wooden overhangs, brown-tiled roofs, built between the 17th and 19th centuries directly on the Yeşilırmak's edge. Bogazkale

★★★★☆
1

Hattusa is the capital of the Hittite Empire, founded around 1650 BC and World Heritage since 1986. The site covers several square kilometers on a mountainous plateau: reconstructed ramparts, Temple of the Storm God, Lion Gate (two sculpted lions flanking the entrance), Sphinx Gate, and the King's Gate with its relief of a warrior deity. The Lion Gate is the site's symbol - its gaping-mouthed lions still announce the city after 3,500 years. The circuit by car (or on foot, 2-3 hours) runs about 7 km.

⏱ 150 min
2

Yazılıkaya is Hattusa's rock sanctuary, 2 km from the main entrance. Two natural rock galleries were decorated in the 13th century BC with processions of Hittite deities sculpted in relief. Gallery A shows the complete pantheon - 65 gods and goddesses marching. Gallery B, smaller and better preserved, depicts King Tudhaliya IV surrounded by his protective god. It's the world's best-preserved ensemble of Hittite reliefs. Don't skip Yazılıkaya, even if pressed for time.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Boğazkale Museum, in the village below the site, presents originals from the site: cuneiform tablet fragments, royal seals, ceramics, and figurines. Most major pieces are at Ankara's Museum of Anatolian Civilizations, but the local museum effectively complements the site visit in 30 minutes.

Safranbolu

★★★★☆
1

Safranbolu is Turkey's best-preserved Ottoman town, UNESCO-listed since 1994. Over 2,000 timber-framed houses from the 17th-19th centuries line the slopes of a narrow gorge: carved wooden overhangs, brown-tiled roofs, windowless street-side façades opening onto bright interior courtyards. Ottoman urbanism here remains intact - no concrete, no modern buildings visible from the cobbled alleys. The town was built on its position as a Silk Road caravanserai, and this mercantile prosperity reads in the quality of the carpentry and painted ceilings.

⏱ 150 min
2

The Cinci Han caravanserai (17th century) is Safranbolu's largest building - two stories of merchant cells, a central courtyard, ground-floor stables. It's been converted to a hotel and remains the best place to sleep at the heart of the historic site. Even if you don't stay there, the interior courtyard is freely visited.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Kaymakamlar House-Museum (19th century) is one of the town's best-preserved Ottoman mansions, open to visitors. The interior shows the typical layout of a konak: men's hall with central sofa, bridal chamber, kitchen, built-in cupboards, carved coffered ceiling. The costumed mannequins are kitschy but effective for understanding Ottoman domestic spatial organization.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Climb Hıdırlık Hill in late afternoon to see Safranbolu from above: a sea of brown-tiled roofs, minarets, and chimneys extending through the green gorge. It's the town's classic viewpoint. Several hilltop cafés serve tea and saffron-flavored lokum - a local specialty for centuries.
