Ireland Road Trip: Capitals, Coasts and Mountains

📅 16 days📍 15 stops🚗 1335 km⏱ 19h09

Dublin → Laragh → Kilkenny → Cork → Kenmare → Dingle → Killarney → Doolin → Galway → Clifden → Westport → Sligo → Donegal → Belfast

Wander through Dublin between Georgian facades and thousand-year-old manuscripts, then plunge into the misty valleys of Glendalough around its 6th-century monastic ruins. Lose yourself in Kilkenny's medieval lanes, breathe Cork's creative energy and let the Dingle Peninsula blend sheer mountains with untamed ocean. From wild Connemara to the Cliffs of Moher, from Galway's pubs to Belfast's murals, this 15-day road trip circles the entire Emerald Isle.

✨ Highlights
  • Book of Kells at Trinity College: illuminated manuscript from the year 800 in a 65-meter library
  • Cliffs of Moher: 214 meters above the Atlantic along an 8km stretch
  • Slea Head Drive: 47km of coastal road at Europe's western edge
  • Titanic Belfast: world-class museum on the original shipyard site
📆 Best period

May, June, September

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 21 and 25 days.

Summer (June-August): Warmest weather, longest days, peak tourist season, highest prices, crowded attractions, festival season, vibrant atmosphere, advance booking essential.

Shoulder Season (April-May & September-October): Mild weather, fewer crowds, good value, comfortable temperatures, pleasant conditions, excellent photography, ideal balance.

Spring (March-May): Mild temperatures, blooming landscapes, fewer tourists, reasonable prices, longer days, fresh atmosphere, excellent touring.

Fall (September-November): Comfortable weather, harvest season, beautiful colors, fewer crowds, good value, cozy atmosphere, cultural events.

Winter (December-February): Cool wet weather, shortest days, lowest prices, minimal crowds, cozy pubs, indoor attractions, festive atmosphere.

Emerald Isle with stunning landscapes, rich literary heritage, and warm hospitality. Experience traditional music, beautiful countryside, and authentic Irish culture. The country offers cultural depth with natural beauty and excellent storytelling traditions. Ireland provides cultural richness with natural wonders and warm hospitality.

Day 1

Dublin

where literary giants, musical pubs and thousand-year manuscripts collide

★★★★★
📍 Trinity College Dublin📍 Book of Kells Old Library Dublin📍 Temple Bar Dublin📍 Chester Beatty Dublin Castle📍 Christ Church Cathedral Dublin
1

Start at Trinity College, founded in 1592 by Elizabeth I — it's Ireland's oldest university. You'll see the Book of Kells, an illuminated manuscript created around the year 800 by monks on the island of Iona in Scotland. The displayed pages change every twelve weeks, so each visit offers something different. The Long Room, a magnificent vaulted hall stretching 65 meters, ranks among the world's most beautiful libraries — George Lucas drew inspiration from it for the Jedi archives in Star Wars. Book your tickets online in advance — it's essential. Allow about 90 minutes for the full experience.

⏱ 90 min
2

Head down to Temple Bar, Dublin's liveliest quarter. Cobbled lanes brim with colourful pubs where live music kicks off as early as 3pm. If you want atmosphere without the crush, visit midweek in early afternoon. Evenings pack out but the energy's infectious. Don't limit yourself to the main drag — the parallel side streets hide art galleries and vintage shops worth discovering.

⏱ 60 min
3

Step inside the Chester Beatty, tucked within Dublin Castle's grounds. This free museum houses one of the world's finest manuscript collections, assembled by American mining magnate Alfred Chester Beatty. You'll find some of Christianity's oldest biblical papyri, Chinese jade books, Persian miniatures and even the Gospel of Mani. Lonely Planet ranked it among Europe's best museums. Stop by the Silk Road Café for lunch with Middle Eastern flavours.

⏱ 75 min
4

Finish your day at Christ Church Cathedral, founded around 1030 by the Viking king Sitric Silkenbeard. Its medieval crypt, stretching 63 meters, is the largest in Ireland and Britain. You'll spot a mummified cat and rat trapped in an organ pipe — James Joyce mentioned them in Finnegans Wake. Head down to admire the 14th-century copy of Magna Carta. The cathedral holds a world record with its 19 swinging bells.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Treat yourself to a late-afternoon stroll along the Liffey. From the Ha'penny Bridge — an elegant 1816 iron footbridge — you'll get perfect views of the illuminated quays. Continue to the Samuel Beckett Bridge, shaped like a harp lying on its side, Ireland's national symbol. It's the best time to feel Dublin's pulse among joggers, street musicians and quayside terraces.

⏱ 60 min

In the evening, dive into a musical pub crawl. Dublin's the world capital of traditional music sessions — fiddle, bodhrán, tin whistle. Skip the overly touristy Temple Bar pubs and head instead for Stoneybatter or the pubs around Camden Street for a more local vibe. Guinness tastes different here, the Irish will tell you.

⏱ 90 min

Day 2

Laragh

gateway village to the ancient monastic valley of Glendalough

★★★★★
📍 Powerscourt Estate Enniskerry📍 Powerscourt Waterfall Wicklow📍 Sally Gap Wicklow Mountains📍 Glendalough Monastic Site📍 Round Tower Glendalough
1

First stop: Powerscourt Estate gardens in Enniskerry, ranked the world's third most beautiful gardens by National Geographic. The Palladian mansion was designed in 1731 by German architect Richard Cassels. From the Italian terraces, the view towards Sugar Loaf is spectacular. Don't miss the 1908 Japanese garden, the Pepperpot tower and the pet cemetery — the epitaphs are touching. Allow 2 to 3 hours to see everything properly. Powerscourt Waterfall, 6km away, is a separate site — at 121 meters, it's Ireland's highest waterfall.

⏱ 120 min
2

Next, reach the monastic site of Glendalough, founded in the 6th century by Saint Kevin. This hermit monk first settled in a cave carved into the cliff above the upper lake — you can spot it from the opposite shore. The 30-meter round tower, built during the Viking raids, served as both bell tower and refuge. Wander among the ruins — the little St Kevin's Kitchen with its stone roof is the most photogenic. The site survived Vikings, Normans and the English who destroyed it in 1398.

⏱ 90 min
3

Take the detour through Glendalough valley to the upper lake. This is where Saint Kevin lived as a hermit for seven years in a rocky cavity perched 10 meters above the water. Legend tells he prayed so long, motionless in the icy waters, that birds came to nest in his outstretched hands — the poet Seamus Heaney, Nobel Prize winner, wrote a famous poem about it. The setting is magical, especially early morning when mist drifts between the two lakes.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

If you have time, walk a section of the Wicklow Way, Ireland's oldest long-distance trail. The route around Glendalough's two lakes suits everyone — allow about 90 minutes for the loop. For the more energetic, the trail climbing towards Poulanass Waterfall offers a rewarding payoff en route.

⏱ 90 min

Driving from Dublin, take the scenic Sally Gap road that crosses the Wicklow Mountains. This deserted route snakes between ochre peatlands and deep valleys — you'd think you were on the moon. Stop at the Lough Tay viewpoint, nicknamed the 'Guinness lake' because of its black waters bordered by white sand. It's one of Wicklow's most photographed panoramas.

⏱ 60 min

Day 3

Kilkenny

medieval city whose Norman castle sold for fifty pounds

★★★★☆
📍 Kilkenny Castle📍 Jerpoint Abbey Thomastown📍 Medieval Mile Kilkenny📍 St Canice Cathedral Kilkenny📍 Kilkenny Design Centre
1

Kilkenny Castle has dominated the River Nore since 1260. Built by the Normans, it served as the Butler family seat for over 600 years. In 1967, the last Marquess of Ormonde sold it to the town for the symbolic sum of £50. Now restored to its 1830s appearance, you'll tour the portrait gallery, library and Victorian bedrooms. Fun fact: Anne Boleyn's grandmother, Lady Margaret Butler, was born here. The 20-hectare park is freely accessible — perfect for a picnic.

⏱ 75 min
2

Twenty minutes south, Jerpoint Abbey is one of Ireland's finest Cistercian monasteries, founded in 1180. The cloister preserves remarkable Romanesque sculptures — look for knights and dragons on the pillars. The Norman lords' tombs are impressive. The neighbouring village of Thomastown also merits a look for its pretty streets lined with colourful houses.

⏱ 60 min
3

Walk up Kilkenny's Medieval Mile, a kilometre of medieval streets between the castle and St Canice's Cathedral. This 13th-century cathedral has a round tower you can climb — the view over the city from the top is the best around. En route, you'll pass Rothe House, a perfectly preserved 1594 Tudor merchant's house with its recreated garden.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Kilkenny has been brewing beer since the 14th century. Visit the Smithwick's Experience that traces this brewing tradition in the former St Francis Abbey. You'll learn that the Smithwick family started brewing here in 1710 on the site of an ancient Franciscan monastery. The final tasting lets you compare different ales — the Blonde is the most surprising.

⏱ 75 min

Late afternoon, stroll through the castle park along the Nore. Kilkenny locals come here to jog and walk their dogs at sunset. If you've still got energy, head to the Kilkenny Design Centre in the castle's former stables — it's one of Ireland's best spots for contemporary craft.

⏱ 45 min

Day 4

Cork

Ireland's rebel city between covered markets and church spires

★★★★☆
📍 English Market Cork📍 Saint Fin Barre's Cathedral Cork📍 Blarney Castle Cork📍 Shandon Bells Cork📍 Crawford Art Gallery Cork
1

Start at the English Market, open since 1788 — it's one of the world's oldest municipal covered markets. The ornamental Princes Street entrance dates from 1862. You'll find spiced beef (Cork's speciality), drisheen (local blood pudding) and farmhouse cheeses. Head upstairs to the Farmgate Café to watch the action from the balcony — it's the market's best table. A fire destroyed the building in 1980, but it was rebuilt identically.

⏱ 60 min
2

Saint Fin Barre's Cathedral, completed in 1879, is a neo-Gothic masterpiece by architect William Burges. Its three spires dominate the city's south side. The interior dazzles with the richness of its mosaics, stained glass and sculptures — Burges designed everything, down to the door handles. The site has been sacred since the 7th century, when Saint Fin Barre founded a monastery here.

⏱ 45 min
3

Twenty minutes northwest of Cork, Blarney Castle is a medieval tower from 1446 built by Cormac MacCarthy, King of Munster. The visit's highlight: kissing the Blarney Stone, wedged high in the battlements. You have to lean backwards over the void — safety bars hold you, but it's still impressive. Legend has it that Queen Elizabeth I, exasperated by Lord Blarney's verbal evasions, invented the word 'blarney' to mean flattery. The gardens around the castle also deserve exploring, especially the Poison Garden with its toxic plants.

⏱ 120 min
4

Head up to Shandon quarter on Cork's north hill. St Anne's Church and its octagonal 18th-century tower are the city's symbol. Climb the 132 steps and you can ring the famous Shandon Bells — locals say a true Corkonian must be born within earshot of their sound. The 360° view over Cork and the River Lee is the city's best.

⏱ 45 min
5

Stop by the Crawford Art Gallery in the city centre. Entry's free and the Irish paintings collection is worth seeing — works by Jack B. Yeats, the poet's brother, through to contemporary artists. The building itself, a former 18th-century customs house, has character. The attached café serves carefully prepared local cuisine — Corkonians consider it one of the city's best canteens.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Late afternoon, walk along the River Lee which divides into two arms around the city centre — Cork is actually built on an island. The south quay offers lovely perspectives on the colourful facades. If you have time, operators offer short cruises on the Lee to see the city from the water.

⏱ 60 min
Ballea Castle9.6

Day 5

Kenmare

colourful nest tucked between Kerry's mountains and bay

★★★☆☆
📍 Kenmare Stone Circle Kerry📍 Kenmare Town Centre📍 Moll's Gap Kerry📍 Ladies View Killarney📍 Ring of Kerry scenic route
1

Leaving Cork, you'll join the Wild Atlantic Way, that 2,500km coastal route hugging the Atlantic. The stretch between Cork and Kenmare gives you a taste of what's ahead — carved bays, emerald pastures and sheep indifferent to your passing. Stop at Moll's Gap for views over the MacGillycuddy's Reeks, Ireland's highest mountain range.

⏱ 30 min
2

Kenmare — 'Neidín' in Irish, the 'little nest' — is one of Kerry's most charming villages. Laid out in 1775 by the Marquess of Lansdowne, the X-shaped plan with two main streets and triangular square is a rare example of a planned town in Ireland. The brightly painted facades and gourmet restaurants make it a perfect base for exploring the Ring of Kerry. Wander Henry Street and William Street to scout your evening spots.

⏱ 45 min
3

Five minutes' walk from the centre, don't miss Kenmare stone circle — 15 standing stones dating from the Bronze Age, over 3,000 years ago. It's one of the largest megalithic circles in southwest Ireland. Its exact purpose remains mysterious — burial site or solar cult place, nobody knows. Access is free and the site is rarely crowded.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

If you arrive early enough, treat yourself to a scenic drive along part of the Ring of Kerry. This 179km loop around the Iveragh Peninsula is one of Ireland's most famous routes. Drive anti-clockwise to avoid the tour buses travelling the other direction. Stop at Ladies View — this viewpoint owes its name to Queen Victoria's ladies-in-waiting, amazed by the vista in 1861.

⏱ 120 min

Late afternoon, operators offer rides in traditional jaunting cars (horse-drawn carriages) through Killarney National Park. These horse carriages have been a local institution since the 19th century. The driver will tell you stories — true or invented — throughout the ride between the lakes and rhododendrons. Allow about an hour for the classic circuit.

⏱ 60 min
Bridge Street Townhouse9.6

Day 6

Dingle

Gaelic-speaking harbour facing the Blasket Islands and wild Atlantic

★★★★☆
📍 Slea Head Drive Dingle📍 Dunmore Head Dingle📍 Gallarus Oratory Kerry📍 Inch Beach Kerry📍 Dingle Town Harbour
1

The Slea Head Drive is a 47km loop that circles the western tip of the Dingle Peninsula — it's Ireland's westernmost point. Drive clockwise to avoid the buses. The cliffs plunge into the Atlantic facing the Blasket Islands, uninhabited since 1953. Stop at Dunmore Head for the most spectacular view, then at Coumeenoole Beach where scenes from the film 'Ryan's Daughter' were shot. Allow at least half a day.

⏱ 180 min
2

Dingle is a colourful fishing port where Irish is still spoken daily. Signs are bilingual and traditional music echoes from the pubs by early evening. The compact harbour is walkable in an hour — painted facades, craft shops, seafood restaurants. It's one of the rare places in Ireland where you feel Gaelic is a living language, not a relic.

⏱ 60 min
3

Gallarus Oratory, 10km from Dingle, is a small dry-stone chapel probably dating from the 11th or 12th century. Its corbelled construction — stones slot together without mortar and have stayed watertight for centuries — is a technical feat. The interior measures just 4.8m long by 3m wide, lit by a single window. Local legend says whoever passes through that window is guaranteed heaven. Site access is free if you avoid the paid visitor centre.

⏱ 30 min
4

En route, stop at Inch Beach, a massive 5km sand spit jutting into Dingle Bay. It's one of Kerry's most beautiful beaches — surfers come for the waves and walkers for the space. Even in grey weather, the light is exceptional.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

From Dingle harbour, operators offer boat trips to watch dolphins in the bay. The Dingle Peninsula is one of Ireland's best spots for cetaceans — bottlenose dolphins, common dolphins and sometimes basking sharks. Trips last about 2 hours. Bring a windbreaker, even in summer.

⏱ 120 min

In the evening, step into a local distillery producing artisan gin from wild plants gathered on the peninsula. The guided tour explains the distillation process and ends with a tasting. It's a good way to finish the day before dinner at one of the harbour's seafood restaurants.

⏱ 60 min
Country Setting in the Middle of Dingle Town.9.8

Day 7

Killarney

lakeside town wrapped in Ireland's most stunning national park

★★★★☆
📍 Muckross House Killarney📍 Muckross Abbey Killarney📍 Gap of Dunloe Kerry📍 Torc Waterfall Killarney📍 Killarney National Park
1

Muckross House is an 1843 Victorian mansion set beside Muckross Lake in Killarney National Park. Queen Victoria stayed three nights in 1861 — she slept on the ground floor for fear of fires. Period furniture remains in place and the guided tour traces daily life in a grand Irish estate. The Italianate gardens and rhododendrons that explode with colour in May-June are magnificent. Right next door, the traditional farm recreates Irish rural life from the 1920s to 1950s.

⏱ 90 min
2

Five hundred metres from Muckross House, the ruins of Muckross Abbey date from 1448. The cloister is invaded by a centuries-old yew tree growing right in the centre — the tree has become the place's symbol. Gaelic chieftains and poets are buried here. The site is freely accessible and rarely crowded in the morning.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Gap of Dunloe is a narrow 11km glacial valley between the MacGillycuddy's Reeks and Purple Mountain. The road snakes between dark lakes and moss-covered boulders — it's one of Kerry's wildest landscapes. You can traverse it by car, on foot or by jaunting car. The gap is so narrow in places that two cars struggle to pass. Arrive early morning to avoid the traffic.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Operators offer boat tours on Killarney's three lakes — Lough Leane, Muckross Lake and Upper Lake. The crossing takes about an hour and gives you a unique perspective on the mountains and ancient forests bordering the shores. Some circuits combine the boat with a walk through the Gap of Dunloe — an excellent full day.

⏱ 60 min

Don't leave Killarney without climbing to Torc Waterfall, 7km from the town centre on the N71 road. The 20-meter fall is nestled in a forest of centuries-old oaks. The trail from the car park takes just 5 minutes, but if you continue climbing the stone steps to the summit, the view over Killarney's lakes from up there amply rewards the effort.

⏱ 45 min

Day 8

Doolin

three-pub village where traditional music never stops

★★★☆☆
📍 Cliffs of Moher Clare📍 Burren National Park Clare📍 Aillwee Cave Ballyvaughan📍 Poulnabrone Dolmen Burren📍 Doolin Village Clare
1

The Cliffs of Moher rise 214 meters above the Atlantic along an 8km stretch — it's Ireland's most visited natural site. On clear days, you'll see the Aran Islands and Galway Bay. The visitor centre, embedded in the hillside, presents an exhibition on the geology and wildlife. Tip: arrive before 10am or after 4pm to avoid the crowds. The coastal path heading south towards Hag's Head is less frequented and offers equally spectacular views.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Burren is a lunar landscape of cracked limestone covering 250 square kilometres of County Clare. These grey rock slabs, called 'clints' and 'grikes', harbour unique flora — arctic orchids grow alongside Mediterranean plants, a botanical paradox that fascinates scientists. Walk on the pavements and observe the wildflowers bursting from the fissures. In spring, the display is particularly colourful.

⏱ 60 min
3

Aillwee Caves, carved into the Burren's limestone, extend over a kilometre underground. You'll see stalactites, an underground waterfall and the remains of a hibernating brown bear — bones several thousand years old were found here. The guided tour lasts 35 minutes and the underground temperature stays constant at 10°C — bring a jumper.

⏱ 60 min
4

Poulnabrone Dolmen stands in the middle of the Burren like a stone portal 5,800 years old. This megalithic tomb, built between 3800 and 3200 BC, contained the remains of 33 people. The horizontal slab balanced on two upright stones weighs several tonnes. It's one of Ireland's most photographed prehistoric monuments — and freely accessible.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Doolin is the self-proclaimed capital of Irish traditional music. In the evening, the village's three historic pubs host live music sessions almost nightly — fiddle, concertina, bodhrán. The atmosphere is authentic, far from tourist folklore. Arrive early to get a seat as the rooms are small.

⏱ 90 min

For walkers, the coastal path between Doolin and the Cliffs of Moher runs along the cliff edge for about 6km. It's one of Ireland's most beautiful coastal hikes — vertigo is guaranteed, but the trail is well marked. Allow 2 to 3 hours return.

⏱ 150 min

Day 9

Galway

bohemian capital of the west between buskers and fresh oysters

★★★★☆
📍 Galway Latin Quarter📍 Spanish Arch Galway📍 Galway City Museum📍 Eyre Square Galway📍 Galway Cathedral
1

Galway is the west of Ireland's liveliest city — the Irish call it the 'cultural capital'. Start in the Latin Quarter around Quay Street and Shop Street, lined with colourful pubs, buskers (street musicians) and shops. The bohemian atmosphere recalls Paris's Latin Quarter, only more laid-back. The pedestrian streets bustle day and night.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Spanish Arch is one of the last remnants of Galway's medieval walls, dating from 1584. It owes its name to maritime trade with Spain — Spanish ships came to load salted fish here. The surrounding quays are Galwegians' favourite spot for catching sun when it deigns to appear. Right next door, Galway City Museum traces the city's history from prehistory — entry is free.

⏱ 60 min
3

Cross the bridge over the Corrib to see Galway Cathedral, completed in 1965 — it's one of the last great stone religious buildings constructed in Europe. Its copper dome and interior mosaics merit a 15-minute detour.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Galway lives to the rhythm of music — practically every city-centre pub offers live Celtic music sessions, especially weekends. Push open the doors of pubs on Shop Street or Dominick Street and let yourself be carried along. The atmosphere differs from Dublin — more intimate, more spontaneous.

⏱ 90 min

Saturday mornings, Galway Market sets up around St Nicholas Church. You'll find local oysters, farmhouse cheeses, artisan bread and Breton crêpes (yes, a Frenchman has settled here). It's the best spot for brunch before hitting the road again. On weekdays, the market is smaller but still pleasant.

⏱ 60 min

Day 10

Clifden

colourful gateway town to the wild heart of Connemara

★★★★☆
📍 Kylemore Abbey Connemara📍 Connemara National Park Letterfrack📍 Clifden Town Connemara📍 Sky Road Clifden📍 Diamond Hill Connemara
1

Kylemore Abbey is an 1868 neo-Gothic castle set beside a lake in the heart of Connemara — the image is so perfect it seems unreal. Mitchell Henry, a wealthy London doctor, built it for his wife Margaret. After her death in Egypt, he had a miniature cathedral built in her memory in the grounds. Since 1920, Benedictine nuns have occupied the place. Also visit the walled Victorian garden, restored identically — it's a 15-minute walk from the abbey.

⏱ 90 min
2

Connemara National Park in Letterfrack covers 2,000 hectares of bogs, heathland and mountains. The visitor centre explains the geology and ecology of this wild region. It's the starting point for the Diamond Hill hike — the climb rewards the effort with coastal views (see activities).

⏱ 30 min
3

Clifden, the 'capital of Connemara', is a lively small town nestled between the Twelve Bens and the Atlantic. The twin spires of its churches can be spotted from afar. Wander Main Street for its traditional pubs and wool shops. At the town's edge, a monument commemorates the first successful non-stop transatlantic flight by Alcock and Brown in 1919 — they landed in a bog right next to it.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The Sky Road is a 12km scenic loop from Clifden that takes you to cliff tops with breathtaking views over the Atlantic and islands. The road climbs in hairpins above the coast — at every bend, the panorama changes. It's one of Ireland's most beautiful coastal roads and often deserted. Allow 30 to 45 minutes by car, more if you stop (and you will stop).

⏱ 45 min

The Diamond Hill hike in Connemara National Park is the region's most popular. The marked trail climbs 3km to the summit at 442 meters. From the top, the view embraces the jagged coast, the islands of Inishbofin and the Connemara bogs. Allow 2h30 return. The trail is well constructed but the last 500 meters are rocky — good shoes are essential.

⏱ 150 min
Clifden Wildflower Cottage - Clifden Countryside Lettings9.7

Day 11

Westport

Georgian gem at the foot of Ireland's sacred mountain

★★☆☆☆
📍 Croagh Patrick Westport📍 Westport House Mayo📍 Westport Town Centre📍 Great Western Greenway Mayo📍 Clew Bay Mayo
1

Croagh Patrick is Ireland's sacred mountain — 764 meters above Clew Bay. According to tradition, Saint Patrick fasted there for 40 days in 441 and drove out the snakes. Every last Sunday of July, thousands of pilgrims climb to the summit, some barefoot. You don't have to go all the way to the top — the first third of the trail already offers magnificent views over Clew Bay's 365 islands. The start is from Murrisk car park.

⏱ 60 min
2

Westport House is a 1730 Georgian mansion built on the foundations of a castle belonging to the family of 'pirate queen' Grace O'Malley. The interior preserves period furniture, family portraits and an impressive stucco-decorated hall. The gardens and surrounding park are pleasant for a stroll.

⏱ 60 min
3

Westport town centre, designed by architect James Wyatt in the 18th century, is one of the rare examples of a planned town in Ireland. The Octagon (central square) and the tree-lined Mall along the Carrowbeg River give particular charm to this small town. The pubs on Bridge Street are renowned for their live music sessions.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The Great Western Greenway is a 42km cycling trail between Westport and Achill Island, built on a former railway line. You don't have to do it all — the Westport-Newport section (11km) is flat, easy and hugs the Clew Bay coast. Bike rental shops offer their services in Westport. It's one of Ireland's most beautiful greenways.

⏱ 120 min

If you prefer something calmer, farms around Westport offer visits where you'll discover sheep farming, shearing and sheepdog work. It's a very popular activity for families and a lovely way to understand rural life in the west of Ireland.

⏱ 60 min

Day 12

Sligo

Yeats country between ancient megaliths and table-top mountains

★★★☆☆
📍 Carrowmore Megalithic Cemetery Sligo📍 Sligo Abbey📍 Benbulben Mountain Sligo📍 Yeats Memorial Building Sligo📍 Strandhill Beach Sligo
1

Carrowmore megalithic cemetery is Ireland's largest — over 30 passage tombs and stone circles dating from 5,500 years ago, some older than Egypt's pyramids. The visitor centre explains Neolithic burial rituals. The central tomb, Listoghil, is the most impressive with its burial chamber covered by a cairn. The site sits in open countryside, with Knocknarea as a backdrop — they say the mythical Queen Maeve is buried at the summit.

⏱ 60 min
2

Sligo Abbey, founded in 1253 by the Dominicans, preserves fine Gothic remains — notably an intact cloister and Renaissance sculptures on the altar. It's one of the best-preserved medieval buildings in northwest Ireland.

⏱ 40 min
3

Benbulben is Sligo's emblematic mountain — its table-top silhouette, resembling an anvil or a ship's prow, can be spotted from miles away. This limestone formation sculpted by glaciers reaches 526 meters. This is where poet W.B. Yeats asked to be buried — his grave is in Drumcliff churchyard, at the mountain's foot. The epitaph he chose himself says: 'Cast a cold eye on life, on death. Horseman, pass by.'

⏱ 45 min
4

The museum dedicated to Yeats, housed in the Yeats Memorial Building in the town centre, traces the life and work of Ireland's greatest poet, Nobel Prize winner for Literature in 1923. Even if you're unfamiliar with his poetry, the exhibition captures well the mystical atmosphere that nourished his work — Sligo was his land of inspiration.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Strandhill Beach, 8km from Sligo, is the region's surf spot — Atlantic waves are powerful and consistent. Even if you don't surf, the walk on this wind-swept beach is invigorating. Operators offer lessons for beginners. After your session, head to the village's seaweed baths — a hot bath in water filled with algae, a centuries-old local tradition.

⏱ 90 min

Follow the Yeats Trail, a marked literary route linking the places that inspired the poet — Lough Gill lake, Innisfree island (which he made famous in a poem), Lissadell manor. It's a beautiful way to discover the Sligo landscape through an artist's eyes. You can do part of it by car and the most striking sections on foot.

Day 13

Donegal

world's-edge county of giant cliffs and hand-woven tweed

★★☆☆☆
📍 Donegal Castle📍 Slieve League Cliffs Donegal📍 Glenveagh National Park Donegal📍 Donegal Town Diamond📍 Donegal Tweed
1

Donegal Castle, in the town centre, was built in the 15th century by the O'Donnell clan, then remodelled by an English settler in the 17th century — the result is a unique blend of Gaelic tower and Jacobean manor. The guided tour recounts the turbulent history of the O'Donnells, who burned their own castle in 1607 rather than leave it to the English. The Diamond, the triangular square in front of the castle, is the small town's lively heart.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Slieve League cliffs, 50km west of Donegal, rise to 601 meters — nearly three times the height of the Cliffs of Moher, making them Europe's highest sea cliffs. Yet they're far less frequented. The main viewpoint is accessible by car, but if you're a confident walker, the One Man's Path trail along the ridge is extraordinary — narrow, vertiginous and spectacular. In clear weather, you can see all the way to Connemara.

⏱ 90 min
3

Warning: Slieve League (west) and Glenveagh (north) lie in opposite directions from Donegal Town — allow 1h30 driving between the two. If you want to do both, leave very early morning for Slieve League, then continue to Glenveagh in the afternoon. Otherwise, choose one or the other. Glenveagh National Park protects 16,000 hectares of wild glacial valley. The 1870 Victorian castle, set beside a lake ringed by mountains, is accessible by shuttle from the visitor centre. The gardens, created in the 1960s, harbour exotic plants that shouldn't survive at this latitude — the valley's microclimate protects them.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Donegal's coast is part of the Wild Atlantic Way and offers some of Ireland's wildest coastal walking trails. Even a short 30-minute walk along the cliffs around Slieve League will give you the feeling of being at the world's edge. The wind is often fierce — dress accordingly.

⏱ 60 min

Donegal is renowned for its tweed, hand-woven for generations. Visit one of the region's weaving workshops to see craftspeople working on traditional looms. The fabric colours — greens, browns, blues — are inspired directly by the surrounding landscape. It's a good place to take home an authentic souvenir.

Day 14

Belfast

northern capital reborn between Titanic heritage and street art

★★★★☆
📍 Ulster American Folk Park Omagh📍 Titanic Belfast Museum📍 Belfast Cathedral Quarter📍 Peace Walls Belfast📍 Ulster Museum Belfast📍 St George's Market Belfast
1

On the route between Donegal and Belfast (218km, about 3 hours), stop at the Ulster American Folk Park, an open-air museum in Omagh, right on your route. Over 30 reconstructed buildings trace three centuries of Irish emigration to America — from Ulster thatched cottages to Pennsylvania log cabins. You'll even board a full-scale replica of an emigrant ship to feel the cramped conditions of the crossing. The museum is built around the birthplace of Thomas Mellon, founder of the Mellon banking dynasty of Pittsburgh. Costumed guides bake bread on griddles and recount daily life. Allow 2 hours for the visit.

⏱ 120 min
2

Titanic Belfast is the city's unmissable museum — a spectacular building shaped like a ship's prow, built on the very site of the Harland & Wolff shipyards where the Titanic was assembled. The exhibition across nine galleries traces the liner's construction, voyage and sinking. The original dry dock, visible outside, is impressive — this is where the ship was launched in 1911. Book your tickets online. Allow at least 2 hours.

⏱ 120 min
3

Belfast's political murals are both street art and scars of history. The 'Peace Walls' — these walls that still today separate Catholic and Protestant neighbourhoods — reach up to 8 meters high. The murals in Falls Road (republican side) and Shankill Road (loyalist side) each tell their version of the Troubles. Operators offer black taxi tours with former residents from both communities as guides — it's the best way to understand this recent and complex history.

⏱ 90 min
4

The Ulster Museum, at the entrance to the Botanic Gardens, is Northern Ireland's main museum. Entry is free. Collections cover art, archaeology and natural sciences. Among the most remarkable pieces: treasures recovered from the Spanish Armada wrecked off the Donegal coast in 1588, and an Egyptian mummy. Allow 1 to 2 hours depending on your interests.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

St George's Market, open Friday, Saturday and Sunday, is Belfast's last Victorian covered market. The 1896 building is worth seeing in itself. Saturday mornings, stalls overflow with local produce, street food and crafts. Live musicians play between the aisles — the atmosphere is joyous and Belfastians come with their families.

⏱ 60 min

The Cathedral Quarter is Belfast's trendy district — art galleries, cocktail bars, street art and fashionable restaurants. In the evening, it's the city's liveliest spot. Wander Hill Street and Commercial Court for the atmosphere. If you're looking for a traditional pub, the Duke of York in a cobbled alley is an institution.

Day 15

Dublin

where literary giants, musical pubs and thousand-year manuscripts collide

★★★★★
📍 Kilmainham Gaol Dublin📍 Dublin Castle📍 Glasnevin Cemetery Dublin📍 Jameson Distillery Bow Street Dublin📍 Grafton Street Dublin
1

Kilmainham Gaol is Dublin's most moving visit. This 1796 prison held the great names of Ireland's independence struggle. In May 1916, fourteen leaders of the Easter Rising were shot in the stonebreakers' yard. James Connolly, too wounded to stand, was strapped to a chair for his execution. Joseph Plunkett married his fiancée Grace in the prison chapel the night before his execution. The guided tour lasts an hour and the queue can be long — book online at least 28 days in advance.

⏱ 90 min
2

Dublin Castle, in the city centre, was the seat of English power in Ireland for over 700 years, from its construction in 1204 until independence in 1922. The State Apartments — the ceremonial rooms — are sumptuously decorated. The neo-Gothic Royal Chapel and the 13th-century Record Tower are the oldest parts of the complex. Today, the castle hosts Irish presidential ceremonies.

⏱ 60 min
3

End your stay at the Jameson Distillery on Bow Street in the Smithfield quarter. Irish whiskey differs from its Scottish cousin through triple distillation that makes it smoother. The guided tour explains the process and ends with a comparative tasting — you'll learn to distinguish a Jameson from a bourbon and a scotch. The brick building is the original production site, founded in 1780.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

If you have time in the morning, Glasnevin Cemetery is worth the detour. Founded in 1832, it's Ireland's largest cemetery — 1.5 million people are buried here, including Daniel O'Connell, Michael Collins and Éamon de Valera. The 51-meter round tower built in O'Connell's honour dominates the site. The adjacent museum offers fascinating guided tours that trace Ireland's history through its dead.

⏱ 90 min

For your last hours in Dublin, walk down Grafton Street, the main pedestrian shopping street. Buskers — street musicians — are a tradition here. Ed Sheeran made his debut here. Continue to St Stephen's Green park for a final peaceful stroll in the heart of Dublin before completing your Irish circuit.
