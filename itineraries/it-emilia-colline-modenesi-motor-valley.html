Nonantola

★★☆☆☆
1

Sixty-four columns await you in the crypt of Nonantola Abbey, a true stone forest underground. Founded in 752 by Abbot Anselmo, brother-in-law to the Lombard king Astolfo, it still houses the relics of Pope Saint Sylvester I. The sculpted portal by the school of Wiligelmo tells the story of the monastery's foundation and Christ's nativity. Closed Monday mornings; entry is free.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Museo Benedettino preserves parchments bearing Charlemagne's monogram from 797. The abbey archive holds over 4,500 parchments, with selected pieces displayed on rotation. Allow 30 minutes for the circuit, longer if the display cases pull you in. Combined ticket available with the abbey.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Take advantage of your walk through the village to spot the remnants of the Partecipanza Agraria, a medieval land-reform system still active today. Residents have collectively managed these lands since monastic times. A quick 20-minute loop on foot through the alleyways around the piazza.

⏱ 25 min

Modena

★★★★☆
1

The Duomo di Modena deserves a full hour. Begun in 1099 by architect Lanfranco, it displays Wiligelmo's reliefs on its façade, classified as UNESCO World Heritage alongside the Torre Ghirlandina and Piazza Grande since 1997. In the crypt: the tomb of San Geminiano and Guido Mazzoni's Madonna della Pappa from 1480. Avoid mass times.

⏱ 60 min
2

Climb the stairs of the Ghirlandina: the tower, completed in 1319, offers a bird's-eye view over Modena's rooftops and the plain stretching to the Apennines. The ascent is steep but short. Separate entrance ticket; closed during thunderstorms.

⏱ 30 min
3

If you book for Saturday or Sunday, the Palazzo Ducale opens for a one-hour guided tour. Built from 1634 onward by Bartolomeo Avanzini for the Este family, it now houses the Accademia Militare. The Salottino d'Oro, Duke Francesco III's private study, is covered in gilded panels that the Modenese hid in the cellars during wartime occupations. Photo ID required at entry; no photography inside.

⏱ 60 min
4

Opened in 1894, the Galleria Estense brings together the Este dukes' collections: Velázquez, Tintoretto, Correggio. You can cover it in about an hour. Closed Mondays. Combined ticket available with Palazzo Ducale in Sassuolo (€10).

⏱ 60 min
5

The roar of a V12 engine greets you at the Museo Enzo Ferrari, set inside the founder's birthplace. The exhibition rotates regularly, but the futuristic yellow hangar is worth the visit on its own. Allow an hour. Combined Maranello + Modena pass available.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Touristy, yes, but Mercato Albinelli remains Modena's beating heart. Opened in 1931, Art Nouveau style, central fountain by Graziosi. About 65 stalls of Parmigiano, mortadella, and fresh pasta. Arrive before 10 a.m. to cross paths with local chefs. Closed Sundays.

⏱ 45 min

Book a visit to the municipal acetaia to understand the process behind traditional balsamic vinegar. You'll touch the barrels and taste the 12-year and 25-year versions. The acetaia sits in the town hall attic. Available Friday through Sunday.

⏱ 45 min

The late-afternoon passeggiata through the historic center is worth the time: Via Emilia, Piazza Grande bathed in golden light, then aperitivo under the arcades. Modena is a walkable city; you can loop the center in 40 minutes on foot.

⏱ 40 min

Sassuolo

★★★☆☆
1

Beneath its industrial-town façade, Sassuolo conceals a stunning Baroque palace. The Palazzo Ducale, transformed in 1634 for Francesco I d'Este, was designed by Avanzini with advice from Bernini himself. The Galleria di Bacco and the stuccoed apartments are covered in frescoes. Open Tuesday through Sunday, 10 a.m.–6 p.m., €8 entry.

⏱ 90 min
2

Behind the palace, the Peschiera spreads its arcaded basin across a terraced garden. This is the former Este fishpond, where the court came to fish and feast. The spot is peaceful, in total contrast to the town around it. Access included in the palace ticket.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Take time to stroll through Sassuolo's center: Piazza Garibaldi, a few painted façades, and an espresso at the bar. The town lives on ceramics, not tourism, which gives it an authentic edge. Easy parking at Piazza Martiri Partigiani.

⏱ 30 min

Salse Di Nirano

★★★★☆
1

You could swear you're in Iceland, but you're 15 minutes from Maranello. The Salse di Nirano are cold mud volcanoes: methane rises from the depths, pierces through clay, and forms little gray cones that gurgle in silence. Emilia-Romagna's first nature reserve, created in 1982, covers 200 hectares. Free access year-round.

⏱ 60 min
2

Stop by the Cà Tassi visitor center to grasp the geology behind the phenomenon. Panels explain how an ancient seabed trapped beneath clay produces these cold eruptions. Small free museum; useful before your walk.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

The gurgle of methane breaking through clay is audible from six feet away. Trail No. 1, on wooden boardwalks, loops around the active cones in 45 minutes. Flat terrain, accessible to all. In summer, avoid the hottest hours: the central zone has no shade.

⏱ 50 min

Maranello

★★★★☆
1

Ferrari red everywhere, from floor to ceiling. The Museo Ferrari in Maranello lines up F1 single-seaters, road-going GTs, and trophies in a circuit that traces the story from 1947 onward. Open daily; allow 90 minutes. €22 ticket, combined Modena + Maranello €26.

⏱ 90 min
2

If the calendar cooperates, you'll spot red prototypes running on the Pista di Fiorano from the road. Ferrari's private test track sits 500 meters from the museum. No public access, but the roar carries far. Panoramic bus shuttle tour available (€20, 12:30 or 1:30 p.m. departures).

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Try the F1 driving simulators at the end of the museum circuit. Sensation of piloting around Monza, vibrations included. A few euros extra; queues on weekends.

⏱ 20 min

Founded in 1990 as a simple Galleria Ferrari, the museum took its current form in 2011. The official shop at the exit sells high-end merchandise. Prices steep, but quality to match. Allow 15 minutes for window shopping.

⏱ 15 min

Spilamberto

★★★★☆
1

The Modenese call it black gold—not oil, but traditional balsamic vinegar. The Museo dell'Aceto Balsamico in Spilamberto, opened in 2002, is housed in Villa Fabriani. The first room is shaped like a giant barrel. You follow the mosto cotto from vine to cask, then through 25 years of waiting. Open Saturday and Sunday, 9 a.m.–1 p.m. / 3–6 p.m.

⏱ 45 min
2

Villa Fabriani is worth the visit for its social acetaia in the attic alone: the Consorteria's battery of casks has been aging there since 1967. Created by Rolando Simonini, the Consorteria also organizes the Palio di San Giovanni, an annual competition for tasters.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

If you come on the second Saturday of October, witness the cottura dei mosti in the open air: 16 to 17 copper cauldrons over wood fires, from morning till evening, right in the piazza. The smell of caramelized must fills the village. Outside this date, tastings are possible by reservation.

⏱ 30 min

Castelvetro Di Modena

★★★★☆
1

Piazza Roma in Castelvetro has a black-and-white checkerboard floor. Every two years (even years), the residents stage a living checkers match in Renaissance costume. The Torre dell'Orologio, slightly leaning, has watched over the scene for centuries. Touring Club Bandiera Arancione; the village is impeccably maintained.

⏱ 30 min
2

Climb the steps of the Torre delle Prigioni, over 20 meters tall, for a panorama of the Grasparossa-planted hills. The old medieval prison reminds you this fortified village had character. Free access during the village's opening hours.

⏱ 20 min
3

From the Santuario di Puianello, the view extends over vineyards, the plain, and the Apennines. The Capuchin church on its hilltop can be reached by car or on foot (20-minute walk). Open 8 a.m.–noon / 2:30–7 p.m.; free-will offering at entry.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Lambrusco Grasparossa tastes better here than anywhere. Local wineries offer tours with tastings by reservation. The sparkling red wine pairs with mortadella and gnocco fritto like nowhere else. Allow an hour for a winery visit.

⏱ 60 min

Walk among the vine rows at sunset. In autumn, the Grasparossa leaves turn red, and the landscape looks like a painting. Start from Via Cialdini parking; easy 45-minute loop.

⏱ 45 min

1

Activities

⏱ 30 min

Head down to the center for coffee on Piazza dei Contrari. Vignola also thrives on cherry production, and the local pastry shops put them to good use. Torta di ciliegie to try if it's the season.

Savignano Sul Panaro

★★☆☆☆
1

Enclosed by a double medieval wall, the Borgo Alto of Savignano sul Panaro seems detached from the modern world below. Cobbled streets, stone houses, and legend says Matilde di Canossa stayed here. The 41-meter bell tower, built with stones from five towers demolished under Napoleon, dominates the landscape.

⏱ 30 min
2

A 22-centimeter statuette, 25,000 years old, found in 1925: the Venere di Savignano is one of Europe's oldest female representations. The original is in Rome's Pigorini Museum, but the copy displayed in the Museo della Venere is worth seeing. Small museum; free entry.

⏱ 20 min
3

The Museo dell'Elefante surprises with its centerpiece: a Pliocene elephant skeleton found on the banks of the Panaro in 1980, roughly 2 million years old. Modest museum, but the fossil is impressive. Same hours as the Museo della Venere.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Cross the Borgo Alto following its three levels: the lower piazza, the Oratorio San Rocco (built in 1631 against the plague), and the church at the top. Allow 30 minutes for a leisurely stroll. Every September, the Spada dei Contrari brings the village alive with a fifteenth-century costume reenactment.

⏱ 30 min

Rocca Malatina

★★★★☆
1

Two sandstone spires over 70 meters tall rise from the hills like geological totems. The Sassi di Roccamalatina, heart of a 2,300-hectare regional park, served as natural fortresses from Byzantine times onward. Today, peregrine falcons nest there. Free access, 12 marked trails.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Pieve di Trebbio, Romanesque and stocky, probably dates from the eleventh or twelfth century. It marks the starting point for several park trails. Small, sober building; no entrance fee; often closed but handsome from the outside with its golden stones.

⏱ 15 min

Activities

Wear good shoes: trail No. 3, the Sassi loop, circles the spires on all sides. Allow 3 to 4 hours with the climb to Sasso della Croce (567 m). The panoramic view from up there justifies the effort. Short variant: trail No. 1, 90 minutes, family-friendly.

⏱ 180 min

Here, you eat borlengo, a crêpe thin as paper, stuffed with lard and Parmigiano. The Museo del Borlengo in Guiglia tells the story of this hyper-local specialty. Try it at autumn sagre or at park trattorias.

⏱ 30 min

Zocca

★☆☆☆☆
1

Zocca owes its fame to a rocker, not a monument. Vasco Rossi was born here in 1952, at 759 meters elevation, among chestnut trees and mist. His house, on the outskirts, has become a pilgrimage site covered in stickers and dedications. No interior visits; just a wall that speaks volumes.

⏱ 15 min
2

The Chiesa del Sacro Cuore, neo-Romanesque in stone, rises in Zocca's center with its gabled façade. Sober, not large, but a nice stopping point. Right next door, the Antica Filmeria Roma, a cinema open since 1957, holds on in a village of 5,000 inhabitants.

⏱ 15 min

Activities

Stop at a trattoria on the main corso for a crescentina, the flatbread of the Modenese Apennines, served hot with melted lard and Parmigiano. It's Zocca's signature dish, once cooked on chestnut leaves over the fireplace.

⏱ 45 min
