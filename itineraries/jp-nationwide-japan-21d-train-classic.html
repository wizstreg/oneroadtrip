Grand Japan Rail Journey: Tokyo to Fukuoka by Shinkansen

📅 21 days📍 21 stops🚗 982 km⏱ 6h20

Step into Tokyo's luminous districts, then slow down in Kyoto among gardens and alleyways. Savor Osaka's street food flavors, discover Hiroshima's quiet strength, approach Miyajima from the sea. End in Fukuoka amid the yatai atmosphere where ramen steam draws passersby. Three weeks aboard the Shinkansen, from temple to temple, bowl to bowl.

✨ Highlights
  • 30 million annual visitors to Sensō-ji, Tokyo's oldest temple (628)
  • 10,000 vermilion torii at Fushimi Inari, 4 km trail up Mount Inari
  • Himeji Castle, Japan's only never-destroyed medieval castle, UNESCO-listed
  • Itsukushima's floating torii on Miyajima, shrine founded in 593
📆 Best period

March, April, May, October, November

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 16 and 20 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Spring (March-May): Cherry blossom season, perfect weather, mild temperatures, iconic sakura viewing, peak tourist season, advance booking essential, magical atmosphere.

Fall (September-November): Autumn colors, comfortable weather, clear skies, beautiful foliage, excellent touring, fewer crowds, ideal photography, pleasant conditions.

Summer (June-August): Hot humid weather, rainy season, festival season, vibrant atmosphere, mountain hiking, beach activities, air conditioning essential.

Winter (December-February): Cold weather, snow sports, illuminations, onsen season, fewer crowds, winter festivals, cozy atmosphere, skiing opportunities.

🌍 What to discover in JP?

Unique culture with ancient traditions, modern innovation, and incredible attention to detail. Experience temples, technology, and authentic Japanese lifestyle. The country offers cultural immersion with natural beauty and culinary excellence. Japan provides transformative cultural experiences with historical depth and modern sophistication.

📋 Important notes

Itinerary designed for the Japan Rail Pass (21 days). Long-distance transfers are via Shinkansen. Book your JR Pass before departure. In Kyoto and Osaka, use local buses and metro.

Day 1

Tokyo

sprawling metropolis where 7th-century temples meet Akihabara's neon blaze

★★★★★
1

Sensō-ji is Tokyo's oldest temple—founded in 628 after two fishermen legendarily pulled a Kannon statue from their nets in the Sumida River. You'll first pass through Kaminarimon, the Thunder Gate, recognizable by its massive 700 kg red lantern guarded by the gods of wind and thunder. Beyond it, Nakamise-dōri stretches for 200 meters—one of Tokyo's oldest shopping streets, active since the Edo period. Arrive before 8:30 am: after 10 am, you'll feel the weight of 30 million annual visitors. Don't miss drawing an omikuji fortune—if it's bad luck, tie the paper strip to the designated racks.

⏱ 90 min
2

Climb to the top floor of the Asakusa tourist information center, right across from Kaminarimon—the view of the temple and Skytree is free. Continue to the Sumida riverbanks to board at Azumabashi Bridge. The cruise to Hinode Pier takes about 40 minutes and passes under 13 bridges. You'll glide past Tokyo Skytree (634 meters, opened in 2012), the futuristic Asahi headquarters, and Hama-Rikyū Gardens. If you can, board just before sunset—the light on the Sumida is magnificent.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

In the evening, return to Asakusa for a twilight photo walk. Sensō-ji lit up at night has a different atmosphere—red lanterns glow in the dusk and the crowds have vanished. Push on to the Sumida River Walk, the pedestrian bridge attached to the Tobu railway bridge, for an unobstructed view of the illuminated Skytree.

⏱ 60 min
Day 2

Tokyo

sprawling metropolis where 7th-century temples meet Akihabara's neon blaze

★★★★★
1

Akihabara, nicknamed "Electric Town," was born from the postwar black market when students resold radios cobbled together from military surplus. Today the district packs multi-story arcades, figurine shops, manga stores, and retro game boutiques. Exit through the Electric Town Exit of JR station. On Chūō-dōri, the main artery, the street is closed to cars on Sunday afternoons. If you like digging, dive into the side streets where independent shops hide the best finds.

⏱ 90 min
2

Five minutes from Akihabara, don't miss Kanda Myōjin shrine, founded in 730. It hosts the Kanda Matsuri every two years, one of Tokyo's three greatest festivals. You'll see votive tablets illustrated with anime characters—a quintessentially Akihabara blend of tradition and pop culture.

⏱ 40 min
3

Ginza is the district born in 1612 on the site of the former Mint—"ginza" means "seat of silver." After an 1872 fire, the Meiji government rebuilt it with brick buildings and Parisian-style boulevards. Stroll down Chūō-dōri, where luxury brand facades compete in audacity—Hermès in glass blocks, Mikimoto by Toyo Ito. In front of the Wako clock tower (former Seiko headquarters), note that in the 1954 Godzilla film, the monster reduces it to rubble when the clock strikes.

⏱ 75 min
4

Step through the doors of Kabuki-za, Ginza's kabuki theater, open since 1889. Even without attending a full performance, you can buy a single-act ticket—an accessible way to discover this dramatic art without committing four hours.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In the evening, treat yourself to a karaoke session in a private booth in Ginza or Shimbashi. The rooms are immaculate, often equipped with tambourines, and the catalog offers thousands of songs in multiple languages. Plan for one to two hours.

⏱ 75 min

Head to Tsukiji Outer Market (the inner market moved to Toyosu in 2018, but the outer stalls remain). It's the perfect place to taste ultra-fresh sushi, tamagoyaki (sweet-savory omelets), and grilled tuna skewers. Arrive hungry.

⏱ 75 min
Day 3

Tokyo

sprawling metropolis where 7th-century temples meet Akihabara's neon blaze

★★★★★
1

Takeshita-dōri in Harajuku is a 400-meter pedestrian avenue where Japanese street fashion expresses itself without filter—cosplay, lolita, streetwear. The street is narrow and packed on weekends. Pass through on a weekday morning if you want to circulate. You'll find stuffed crepes, kawaii boutiques, and multi-story sock shops.

⏱ 60 min
2

Yoyogi Park, one of Tokyo's largest green spaces, transforms on weekends into an open-air stage: musicians, rockabilly dancers, cosplayers. On weekdays, it's calmer—perfect for catching your breath after Harajuku's effervescence. In spring, cherry blossoms draw crowds for hanami.

⏱ 45 min
3

From Yoyogi, walk five minutes to Meiji-jingū shrine, dedicated to Emperor Meiji (died 1912) and Empress Shōken. Built in 1920, destroyed in 1945, and rebuilt in 1958 in cedar wood, it's nestled in a 70-hectare forest planted with 100,000 trees from across Japan. As soon as you pass under the 12-meter wooden torii, the city noise vanishes. It's the most visited shrine at New Year's—over 3 million worshippers. Curious detail: facing the sake barrels, you'll see wine casks from Burgundy, symbolizing openness to the West during the Meiji era.

⏱ 75 min
4

Finish at Shibuya Crossing, the world's busiest pedestrian intersection—about 2,500 people cross at each green light, up to 3,000 during rush hour. Five crossings including one diagonal. To see it from above, climb to Shibuya Sky (230 meters, atop Scramble Square completed in 2019). Before crossing, stop at the Hachikō statue, the Akita dog who waited for his deceased master at the station for nine years (1925-1935). The current statue dates from 1948—the first, unveiled in 1934 with the dog himself present, was melted down during the war.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Treat yourself to a break at one of Harajuku or Shibuya's theme cafés—cat cafés, hedgehog cafés, owl cafés, or anime-themed cafés. Each café has its own rules (duration, mandatory drink), but the experience is worth it. Plan for about an hour.

⏱ 60 min
Day 4

Tokyo

sprawling metropolis where 7th-century temples meet Akihabara's neon blaze

★★★★★
1

Shimokitazawa—"Shimokita" to regulars—is Tokyo's bohemian quarter, three minutes from Shibuya by train. A former writers' refuge in the 1920s, it's transformed into a vintage fashion haven. Its pedestrian alleys overflow with impeccably organized thrift stores, record shops, independent cafés, and over 15 small theaters. Don't go before 11 am—most shops are still closed. Afternoon and evening is when the neighborhood comes alive.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Tokyo Metropolitan Government Building in Shinjuku, designed by Kenzō Tange and completed in 1991, rises to 243 meters. Its design supposedly draws inspiration from Notre-Dame de Paris and a computer chip. Each twin tower houses a free observatory on the 45th floor, at 202 meters. On clear days, Mount Fuji stands out on the horizon. The north observatory stays open until 10 pm—go in late afternoon to watch Tokyo light up.

⏱ 60 min
3

Midday, detour through Shinjuku Gyoen, one of Tokyo's most beautiful gardens—58 hectares combining French garden, English garden, and traditional Japanese garden. It's one of the best hanami spots in spring, with over 1,000 cherry trees.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

In the evening, plunge into Omoide Yokochō (Memory Lane), a maze of tiny izakayas under the train tracks at Shinjuku's west exit. These few-seat counter establishments serve yakitori, gyōza, and beer in a postwar Tokyo atmosphere—the place has barely changed since the 1950s.

⏱ 75 min
Day 5

Tokyo

sprawling metropolis where 7th-century temples meet Akihabara's neon blaze

★★★★★
1

Day at Tokyo Disney Resort in Urayasu (Chiba), about 20 minutes on JR Keiyō from Tokyo Station to Maihama. Two parks to choose from: Tokyo Disneyland (classic, ideal with children) or Tokyo DisneySea (unique in the world, more adult, inspired by maritime myths). DisneySea is often considered the most beautiful Disney park—its Mediterranean port setting, central volcano, and thrilling attractions deserve a full day. Plan to arrive at opening—lines explode after 11 am.

⏱ 480 min

Activities

If the park offers late opening (check the calendar), stay for the nighttime show on the lagoon—water projections, fireworks, and special effects. Otherwise, swing by Ikspiari shopping center at the park exit for dinner and exclusive souvenirs.

⏱ 90 min
Day 6

Tokyo

sprawling metropolis where 7th-century temples meet Akihabara's neon blaze

★★★★★
1

Day trip to Hakone from Shinjuku—take the Odakyu Romancecar (about 80 minutes) and get the Hakone Free Pass covering train, bus, boat, and ropeway. Lake Ashi, nestled in a caldera at 720 meters altitude, crosses by pirate ship in 30 minutes. On clear days, you'll see Mount Fuji reflecting in its waters. Disembark at Moto-Hakone to reach Hakone-jinja shrine, hidden in the forest—its Heiwa torii, erected in 1951 to celebrate the peace treaty, stands at the water's edge. Expect a bit of patience for the photo—there's often a queue.

⏱ 120 min
2

Take the ropeway to Owakudani, a volcanic valley born from Mount Hakone's last eruption 3,000 years ago. The landscape is almost lunar—yellowish ground, sulfur fumaroles, gray rocks. This is where they cook the famous kuro-tamago, eggs whose shells blacken in contact with 80°C sulfurous water. Legend says eating one extends your life by seven years. If the volcano is calm, you'll also have a spectacular view of Mount Fuji from the station.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

If you have time before returning to Tokyo, detour to the Hakone Open-Air Museum, a contemporary sculpture park nestled in the hills, with a Picasso pavilion and an outdoor foot bath.

⏱ 60 min

Day 7

Kyoto

former imperial capital of 2,000 temples, between bamboo groves and golden pavilions

★★★★★
1

Transfer Tokyo → Kyoto via Nozomi Shinkansen from Tokyo Station (about 2h15, 476 km). Sit on the right side (seat E) facing forward—on clear days, Mount Fuji appears between Shin-Yokohama and Shizuoka, about 40 minutes after departure. It's a brief moment—keep your eyes open.

⏱ 140 min
2

Once settled in Kyoto, head to Nishiki Market, nicknamed "Kyoto's kitchen." This 400-meter covered arcade, active for over 400 years, lines up more than 100 stalls of fish, pickled vegetables (tsukemono), tofu, matcha tea, and skewers. Taste yuba (soy milk skin, local specialty), fresh mochi, and tako tamago (small octopus stuffed with quail egg). The market closes around 5 pm—don't arrive too late.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

In the evening, stroll through Ponto-chō, a narrow alley parallel to the Kamo River, lined with restaurants and bars, some with terraces over the water (yuka) in summer. The atmosphere is hushed—paper lanterns, wooden facades. If you glimpse a kimono-clad figure slipping between doors, it might be a maiko (apprentice geisha) heading to an engagement.

⏱ 60 min
Day 8

Kyoto

former imperial capital of 2,000 temples, between bamboo groves and golden pavilions

★★★★★
1

Kinkaku-ji (Golden Pavilion) has gleamed amid its mirror pond since 1397, when shōgun Ashikaga Yoshimitsu built it as a retirement villa. Its upper two stories are entirely covered in gold leaf—the ground floor, in natural wood, remains sober. Each floor represents a different style: Heian palace, samurai house, Zen temple. On July 2, 1950, a monk set fire to the pavilion—this episode inspired Yukio Mishima's novel. The current building is a faithful 1955 reconstruction. Arrive at opening (9 am) to avoid tour buses.

⏱ 60 min
2

Nijō Castle, built in 1603 for first shōgun Tokugawa Ieyasu, served as his residence during Kyoto visits. Its "nightingale" floors (uguisubari) intentionally creak underfoot to alert against intruders. The sliding panel paintings of Ninomaru Palace, executed by the Kanō school, are sumptuous. The gardens surrounding the castle deserve a leisurely stroll. Allow 1.5 hours for everything.

⏱ 90 min
3

Gion, the historic geisha district, is explored on foot along Hanamikōji street. Teahouses (ochaya) with wooden facades line up their bamboo blinds—this is where maiko and geiko practice their art. The best time to glimpse a maiko hurrying to an engagement: between 5:30 and 6:30 pm. Stay discreet—don't chase them for photos.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Participate in a tea ceremony (chanoyu) at one of the traditional teahouses in the district. The ceremony lasts about 45 minutes—they'll explain the ritual gestures, the meaning of whisked matcha, and the choice of wagashi (confection) accompanying the tea. Book in advance, especially during high season.

⏱ 60 min
Day 9

Kyoto

former imperial capital of 2,000 temples, between bamboo groves and golden pavilions

★★★★★
1

Kiyomizu-dera (Pure Water Temple), founded in 778, perches on Otowa Hill. Its wooden terrace, supported by 139 thirteen-meter pillars assembled without a single nail, overlooks the valley and offers a panoramic view of Kyoto. The Japanese expression "jumping from Kiyomizu-dera" (making a bold decision) comes from the belief that surviving the jump guaranteed wish fulfillment. UNESCO-listed since 1994. To reach it, climb the shopping lanes of Ninenzaka and Sannenzaka—they're photogenic but very crowded after 10 am.

⏱ 75 min
2

Heian-jingū shrine, built in 1895 to commemorate the 1,100th anniversary of the capital's transfer to Kyoto, is a two-thirds scale reproduction of the Heian period imperial palace. Its 24-meter vermilion torii is one of Japan's largest. Behind the shrine, the garden (paid) harbors a pond crossed by a covered bridge and over 3,000 plants—the weeping cherry trees are spectacular in April.

⏱ 60 min
3

Fushimi Inari Taisha, founded in 711, is the head shrine of Japan's 30,000 Inari shrines. What makes it unique: roughly 10,000 vermilion torii donated by businesses and individuals form a tunnel along 4 km of trails climbing to the summit of Mount Inari (233 m). The most famous section, Senbon Torii, is at the bottom—but the higher you climb, the fewer people. The full round trip takes about 2-3 hours. The shrine never closes—going early morning or at nightfall, when the lanterns light up, is the best option.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Late in the day, wander around Yasaka-jinja shrine at the entrance to Gion. Its paper lanterns light up at dusk and the atmosphere becomes hushed. If you have energy, push on to Maruyama Park just behind—it's Kyoto's most popular hanami spot in spring, with a century-old weeping cherry tree illuminated at night.

⏱ 45 min
Day 10

Kyoto

former imperial capital of 2,000 temples, between bamboo groves and golden pavilions

★★★★★
1

Arashiyama Bamboo Grove is one of Kyoto's most iconic images—a path several hundred meters long bordered by giant bamboo whose stalks crack and whistle in the wind. To enjoy it without crowds, be there before 8 am. The path is short (10-15 minute walk), but the atmosphere is striking. Exiting, push through the gate of Tenryū-ji temple, UNESCO-listed, whose Zen garden facing the Arashiyama mountains is a masterpiece by landscape architect Musō Soseki (14th century).

⏱ 75 min
2

Iwatayama Monkey Park shelters a colony of free-roaming Japanese macaques. The ascent takes about 20 minutes—the slope is steep but the path well-maintained. At the top, monkeys roam freely around you (don't look them in the eye, don't show food). The view over Kyoto from the summit is one of the city's finest.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Descend toward Togetsukyō Bridge (the bridge that crosses the moon), Arashiyama's emblem. From the Katsura riverbank, the view of the bridge with mountains as backdrop is superb, especially in autumn when maples redden. If you have time, rent a bike to explore the surroundings at your pace—Gio-ji temple and its moss gardens are 15 minutes by bike.

⏱ 60 min

Day 11

Osaka

kuidaore capital where you eat till you drop, between glowing canals and Hideyoshi's castle

★★★★★
1

Excursion to Nara (45 min by JR train from Kyoto or Osaka). Tōdai-ji, founded in 728, houses the Daibutsu—a 15-meter-tall, 500-ton bronze Buddha, the world's largest covered bronze Buddha. The building that houses it, Daibutsuden, is one of the planet's largest wooden structures (57 m wide, 50 m high), even though the current version (rebuilt in 1709) is 30% smaller than the original. At the building's rear, try sliding through the hole in a pillar—according to belief, those who succeed attain enlightenment.

⏱ 75 min
2

Kasuga Taisha shrine, founded in 768, is recognizable by its 3,000 lanterns—2,000 stone ones along the pathways and 1,000 bronze ones suspended in the buildings. They're all lit only twice a year, during the Setsubun (February) and Obon (August) festivals. The moss-lined lantern path leading to the shrine through Kasugayama forest is one of Nara's most beautiful.

⏱ 60 min
3

If you have energy, climb Mount Wakakusa (342 m), a dome-shaped grassy hill from which the view over Nara and its temples is panoramic. The ascent takes 30-40 minutes. Every January, the slopes are burned during the Yamayaki festival—an impressive fire spectacle.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Stroll through Nara Park where over 1,200 deer (shika) roam freely. These cervids are considered divine messengers in Shinto tradition. You can buy shika-senbei (deer crackers) from park vendors—beware, the deer can be insistent when they see food.

⏱ 45 min
Day 12

Osaka

kuidaore capital where you eat till you drop, between glowing canals and Hideyoshi's castle

★★★★★
1

Dōtonbori is Osaka's beating heart—a canal bordered by giant neon signs including the famous Glico Man, that runner with arms raised who's been shining since 1935 (the current version is the sixth). The district is paradise for kuidaore—"eat till you drop," Osaka's culinary philosophy. Walk along the canal and on Ebisu Bridge to snap the classic photo with Glico Man in the background.

⏱ 75 min
2

Osaka Castle (Osaka-jō), initially built in 1583 by Toyotomi Hideyoshi to unify Japan, has been destroyed and rebuilt several times. The current tower dates from 1931 (reinforced concrete) and houses a museum on Hideyoshi's history and the castle's siege battles. Climb to the 8th floor for a 360° city view. The castle park, with its moats and cherry trees, is a favorite picnic spot for Osakans.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Impossible to leave Osaka without a food tour of its specialties. Okonomiyaki (savory pancake filled with cabbage, meat, and sauce), takoyaki (octopus balls crispy outside and melting inside), and kushikatsu (fried breaded skewers) are the must-tries. The Shinsekai district, with its Tsūtenkaku tower and kushikatsu restaurants, is a good spot for dinner in a popular, retro atmosphere.

⏱ 90 min
Day 13

Osaka

kuidaore capital where you eat till you drop, between glowing canals and Hideyoshi's castle

★★★★★
1

Minoh Park (Minoo), north of Osaka, offers an easy 2.7 km hike along a stream to Minoh Falls, a 33-meter waterfall surrounded by forest. The trail is flat and well-developed—count about 40 minutes to reach it. In autumn, flaming maples frame the falls—it's one of the region's best momiji (autumn leaves) spots. On the return, taste fried maple leaves (momiji tempura), a local specialty sold in shops along the path.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

In the afternoon, explore America-mura (American Village), Osaka's young, alternative district with its streetwear boutiques, record shops, and trendy cafés. Continue on Shinsaibashi-suji, a 600-meter covered shopping arcade mixing international brands and local boutiques. It's Osaka's longest shopping street.

⏱ 90 min
Day 14

Osaka

kuidaore capital where you eat till you drop, between glowing canals and Hideyoshi's castle

★★★★★
1

Day at Universal Studios Japan (USJ), Osaka's theme park. The Wizarding World zone, reproducing Hogsmeade village and Hogwarts castle with meticulous attention to detail, is the park's star. Other zones (Jurassic World, Super Nintendo World with its life-sized Mario sets, Minion Park) are also worth exploring. Plan to arrive before opening and buy tickets online in advance—on high-attendance days, the park may refuse entry.

⏱ 480 min

Activities

If the calendar permits, stay for the nighttime show or parade. Times vary by season—check USJ's website in advance. During Halloween and Christmas periods, special events transform the park.

⏱ 60 min
Day 15

Hiroshima

reborn city rebuilt around seven rivers and its peace memorial

★★★★★
1

En route to Hiroshima, stop in Himeji (30 min by Shinkansen from Shin-Osaka). Himeji Castle, nicknamed the White Heron, is Japan's most beautiful medieval castle—a 46-meter keep completed in 1609, never destroyed by war or earthquakes. UNESCO-listed and a national treasure, it was restored between 2009 and 2015 to recover its dazzling whiteness. The interior is austere (wooden walls, steep stairs) but climb to the 6th floor for the view and to understand the ingenious defensive system—loopholes, machicolations, labyrinthine passages. Allow at least 1.5 hours.

⏱ 90 min
2

Right next to the castle, Kōkō-en garden, laid out in 1992 on the former grounds of samurai residences, brings together nine themed gardens connected by beaten-earth pathways. It's a quiet place to breathe after the castle visit. You can have matcha at the teahouse overlooking the garden.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

If you have time before catching the Shinkansen to Hiroshima (40 min), take the cable car up Mount Shosha to visit Engyō-ji, a Buddhist temple complex founded in 966 and used as a setting in the film The Last Samurai. The surrounding forest is magnificent.

⏱ 90 min

Day 16

Miyajima

sacred island of the Seto Inland Sea, floating shrine and free-roaming deer

★★★★★
1

The Hiroshima Peace Memorial Museum, renovated in 2019, traces with sobriety and unflinching honesty the events of August 6, 1945, when the atomic bomb destroyed 90% of the city in an instant. Victims' personal effects, testimonies, and models are deeply moving. Allow at least 1.5 hours—and prepare yourself emotionally.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Genbaku Dome (A-Bomb Dome), the only building left standing near the hypocenter, has been preserved as-is as a memorial. It's the former Prefectural Industrial Promotion Hall, designed by Czech architect Jan Letzel and completed in 1915. UNESCO-listed in 1996. The surrounding Peace Memorial Park, with its cenotaph, peace flame, and peace bell, should be visited in silence—it's a place of reflection.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

After this intense visit, rent a bike to explore central Hiroshima, rebuilt around its seven rivers. The city has a pleasant rhythm, calmer than the major metropolises. Detour through Hondōri district to taste Hiroshima okonomiyaki—different from Osaka's: here the layers (noodles, cabbage, egg, sauce) are stacked instead of mixed.

⏱ 75 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 26 km · 50min
Day 17

Miyajima

sacred island of the Seto Inland Sea, floating shrine and free-roaming deer

★★★★★
1

Itsukushima Shrine, founded in 593 and rebuilt in its current form in 1168 by Taira no Kiyomori, seems to float on water at high tide. Its great 16-meter vermilion torii, planted in the sea 200 meters from shore, is one of Japan's symbols. At low tide, you can walk to the torii's base—check tide times before planning your visit. The entire shrine is UNESCO-listed.

⏱ 75 min
2

Daishō-in temple, on the slopes of Mount Misen, is often overshadowed by Itsukushima but well worth the detour. It's the island's main Buddhist temple—its staircases lined with hundreds of copper prayer wheels, its statues covered with knitted caps, and its sea view are enchanting. Less crowded than the shrine, it offers a more intimate atmosphere.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Ascending Mount Misen (535 m), Miyajima's highest point, can be done on foot (about 1.5 hours of sustained climbing) or by combining cable car and walk (20 min walk from the arrival station). At the summit, the view embraces the Seto Inland Sea and its dozens of islands. According to tradition, Kōbō Daishi (founder of Shingon Buddhism) lit a flame there 1,200 years ago—it supposedly still burns in one of the summit temples.

⏱ 120 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 200 km · 2h
Day 18

Fukuoka

gateway to Kyushu, tonkotsu ramen capital served under yatai canvas roofs

★★★★★
1

Transfer Miyajima → Fukuoka: ferry from Miyajima to Miyajimaguchi (10 min), then JR to Hiroshima (30 min), then Nozomi or Sakura Shinkansen to Hakata (about 1h). Allow 2 to 2.5 hours for the entire journey.

⏱ 150 min
2

Tenjin is Fukuoka's main shopping district—underground shopping arcades (Tenjin Chika), department stores, and boutiques spread across several city blocks. Calmer and more spacious than Tokyo or Osaka shopping districts, it's a good place to wander and buy souvenirs.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

In the evening, head to the yatai of Nakasu—these street stalls set up along the Naka River and around Tenjin are a Fukuoka institution. You sit at the counter (6-8 seats maximum per yatai), order a bowl of Hakata ramen (milky pork broth, thin noodles), and chat with the cook and neighbors. Yatai open around 6 pm and close around 1 am. Arrive early to get a seat—they don't take reservations.

⏱ 75 min
Day 19

Fukuoka

gateway to Kyushu, tonkotsu ramen capital served under yatai canvas roofs

★★★★★
1

Ohori Park, laid out around an artificial lake inspired by Hangzhou's West Lake in China, is Fukuoka's green lung. A 2 km promenade circles the lake—it's locals' favorite jogging circuit. The traditional Japanese garden (paid) on the south shore is an oasis of calm. Just next door, Fukuoka Castle ruins (Maizuru Park) offer a panoramic city view from the former stone terraces of the keep.

⏱ 75 min
2

Canal City Hakata is a shopping and entertainment complex built around an artificial canal—fountains come alive every 30 minutes with a water and light show. The curved architecture by American Jon Jerde is spectacular. It's also a good eating spot: "Ramen Stadium" on the 5th floor brings together ramen restaurants from across Japan.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Take advantage of your time in Fukuoka to taste different variants of Hakata ramen—creamy tonkotsu (pork bone) broth, ultra-thin noodles you can order extra-firm (kata-men or bari-kata). Most restaurants offer kaedama, an extra portion of noodles to add to your bowl to extend the pleasure.

⏱ 60 min
Day 20

Fukuoka

gateway to Kyushu, tonkotsu ramen capital served under yatai canvas roofs

★★★★★
1

Excursion to Dazaifu (30 min by Nishitetsu train from Tenjin). Dazaifu Tenmangū shrine, founded in 905, is dedicated to Sugawara no Michizane, deified as Tenjin, the kami of study and literature. Thousands of students come to pray before exams. In front of the shrine, the first plum blossom (ume) supposedly flew from Kyoto to follow its exiled master—the legend of the "flying plum." The access avenue is lined with shops selling umegae-mochi, rice cakes filled with red bean paste and stamped with a plum blossom.

⏱ 60 min

Just behind the shrine, the Kyushu National Museum, designed by Kikkawa Kiyonori, is a massive glass and steel building nestled in the hill. It traces the history of cultural exchanges between Japan, China, and Korea. The architecture alone is worth the trip—the forest reflections on the curved facade are striking.

Before catching the train back, stroll through Dazaifu's historic lanes. This small town was the seat of Kyushu's regional government in the 8th century—it has a tranquil pace and streets lined with traditional houses that contrast with Fukuoka's energy.

Fukuoka

gateway to Kyushu, tonkotsu ramen capital served under yatai canvas roofs

★★★★★

Nanzōin temple in Sasaguri (20 min by JR from Hakata) houses one of the world's largest reclining bronze Buddhas—41 meters long, 11 meters high, 300 tons. Built in 1995, it's less ancient than its Southeast Asian rivals but its size impresses. The complex is nestled in forest and little frequented by tourists—it's a peaceful place to end your circuit. Take time to visit the 88 small shrines of the miniature Shikoku pilgrimage, scattered in the hills around the temple.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Last afternoon in Japan—return to Hakata and Tenjin districts for final shopping. Department store basements (depachika) are the best place to buy boxes of Japanese confections to bring home. Hakata Tōrimon (white bean paste filled cakes) and mentaiko (marinated cod roe) are Fukuoka specialties not to miss.
