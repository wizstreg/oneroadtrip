☀️ Weather

Spring (March-May): Perfect weather with comfortable temperatures (18-28°C), minimal rainfall, and ideal conditions for exploring archaeological sites and desert regions. Excellent balance of pleasant weather and moderate tourist numbers with wildflower blooms in desert areas.

Autumn (September-November): Excellent temperatures (20-30°C) with clear skies, perfect outdoor conditions, and comfortable hiking weather. Second-best season with beautiful weather for Petra visits, reasonable prices, excellent photography lighting.

Winter (December-February): Cool temperatures (8-18°C) with occasional rainfall, fewer crowds, and budget-friendly rates. Some sites may close early, but excellent for cultural exploration and avoiding summer heat.

Summer (June-August): Very hot temperatures (30-45°C) making outdoor activities challenging during midday hours. Early morning and evening activities recommended, highest accommodation rates, extreme heat limits desert exploration.

Amman

★★★★☆
1

Jerash welcomes you through Hadrian's Arch, built in 129 CE for the emperor's visit—a 25-by-21-meter gateway that opens onto one of the best-preserved Roman cities in the Middle East, sometimes called the 'Pompeii of the East'. Pass the hippodrome and emerge at the Oval Plaza, likely the largest forum in the Roman Empire (90 by 80 meters) and the only one with an oval shape. Its 1st-century Ionic colonnade frames a space that served both as marketplace and gathering place. From here unfolds the Cardo Maximus, 800 meters lined with 200 Corinthian columns—you'll still see the chariot grooves carved into the flagstones. Allow 2 to 3 hours for the site and bring a hat; there's virtually no shade.

⏱ 150 min
2

Don't leave Jerash without pushing on to the Temple of Artemis, dedicated to the city's patron goddess. Its 12-meter Corinthian columns still rise against the sky. The South Theatre, built around 90 CE under Domitian, seated 5,000 spectators—climb to the upper tiers and test the acoustics by clapping your hands at the center of the stage. It still serves as a performance venue during the Jerash Festival each July.

⏱ 45 min
3

Back in Amman (~1 hour drive), climb to the Citadel on Jebel al-Qala'a hill at 850 meters elevation. The Temple of Hercules, commissioned under Marcus Aurelius between 162 and 166 CE, preserves two 10-meter columns and three stone fingers from a colossal statue—the scale becomes dizzying when you imagine the original size. Just behind it, the Umayyad Palace, built around 720 CE and destroyed by the 749 earthquake, shows a vaulted audience hall designed to impress the governor's visitors. Go late afternoon: the panorama across Amman's thousands of beige houses and down to the Roman theatre is best at sunset.

⏱ 90 min
4

Walk down to the Roman Theatre, carved into the rock in the 2nd century with capacity for 6,000 spectators. From there, plunge into the Downtown souk where animated streets connect the theatre to the al-Husseini Mosque. It's the perfect spot to taste your first mansaf (lamb stew in fermented yogurt, Jordan's national dish) or a hot falafel fresh from the oil.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Evening stroll through the Rainbow Street quarter—cafés, art galleries and terraces with views over the lower city. On summer Fridays, the Souk Jara market sets up here with local crafts and street food.

⏱ 60 min

Dead

★★★★★
1

Start the day at Mount Nebo, 8 km from Madaba. This promontory at 817 meters elevation is where, according to Deuteronomy, Moses gazed upon the Promised Land before dying at age 120. On clear days you'll see the Dead Sea, the Jordan Valley and sometimes Jerusalem in the distance. Before the restored basilica (4th-century foundations, rebuilt in 597 CE), admire the Brazen Serpent, a bronze sculpture by Gian Paolo Fantoni that intertwines Christ's cross with Moses' serpent. Inside, the mosaics dated to 530 CE show hunting and grape-harvest scenes on a 9-by-3-meter panel.

⏱ 45 min
2

Push open the door to St. George's Church in Madaba, a modest Orthodox building constructed in 1896 atop the ruins of a 6th-century Byzantine basilica. Here's where the Madaba Map is preserved—the oldest cartographic representation of the Holy Land, with over 2 million stone tesserae assembled around 550-560 CE, originally measuring 21 by 7 meters (16 by 5 meters survive). You'll recognize Jerusalem at the center, the Dead Sea with two boats, the Jordan River with its fish. Arrive early in the morning—tour groups quickly crowd this small church.

⏱ 40 min
3

Stay in Madaba for the archaeological park, a few streets from St. George's. You'll see more Byzantine mosaics, including those from the Hall of Hippolytus and the Church of the Virgin. Madaba is nicknamed the 'City of Mosaics'—if the subject captivates you, you can join an introductory workshop at one of the artisan studios that perpetuate the tradition.

⏱ 45 min
4

Late in the day, descend toward the Dead Sea—the lowest point on the planet, roughly 430 meters below sea level. The water contains nearly 30% salt (versus 3-4% in the oceans), making floating inevitable. Coat yourself with the black mud rich in magnesium and potassium, let it dry for 20 minutes, then rinse in the salty water. Don't exceed 20 minutes of bathing per session, and above all never put your head underwater. Bring water shoes because the salt crystals on the bottom are sharp.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Sunset from the Dead Sea shores—the low-angle light on the calm waters and the mountains of the West Bank across. Bring a book for the classic souvenir photo: reading while floating.

⏱ 30 min

Ma In Hot Springs

★★☆☆☆
1

Start early for Wadi Mujib, just 15 minutes' drive from the Dead Sea shores. The Siq Trail, nicknamed the 'Grand Canyon of Jordan', is a 3-km round-trip water hike: you wade upstream between sandstone walls rising dozens of meters high, with water waist-deep in places. The trail ends at a waterfall. Count on 2 to 3 hours. Warning: restricted to those 18 and over, open only April through October, closed if it rains. Bring a waterproof pouch for your phone and water shoes. Arrive at 8 AM opening to avoid meeting everyone on the way back.

⏱ 150 min
2

After Mujib, head up to the Ma'in hot springs (Hammamat Ma'in), 15-20 minutes' drive. The waterfalls cascade down the wadi cliffs at temperatures reaching 63°C. The water's charged with sulfur, calcium and magnesium—Jordanians have bathed here for centuries, and tradition holds that King Herod the Great made the journey from Jerusalem to treat his rheumatism. The natural pools at the foot of the waterfalls are accessible to all. After the canyon's coolness, the springs' heat creates a striking contrast.

⏱ 90 min
3

On the route between Ma'in and the Dead Sea, detour to the Dead Sea Panoramic Complex. This viewpoint perched 400 meters above the shores offers a plunging view across the entire blue expanse of the Dead Sea and the mountains opposite. A small museum traces the geological history and biodiversity of the region.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Short hike in Wadi Zarqa Ma'in—a trail descending toward the waterfalls through a landscape of rock gorges and quasi-tropical vegetation, surprising in the middle of the desert.

⏱ 60 min

Wadi Musa

★★★☆☆
1

Enter Petra through the Siq, a 1.2-km canyon whose multicolored sandstone walls narrow to 3-4 meters wide. After a final turn, Al-Khazneh (the Treasury) appears: a 39-meter-high by 25-meter-wide façade carved from rose sandstone, probably a tomb for the Nabataean king Aretas IV (1st century BCE). The Bedouins believed an urn at the summit held treasure and shot at it—the bullet impacts are still visible on this 3.5-meter urn carved from solid rock. Be there at 6-7 AM opening when the sun illuminates the façade and the stone turns bright pink—and especially before the groups arrive.

⏱ 90 min
2

Continue beyond the Treasury to the Street of Facades, where about forty cave tombs are aligned in the cliff face, then to the Nabataean theatre carved from the sandstone, capable of seating around 3,000 spectators. Opposite, on the east cliff, the Royal Tombs merit the climb: the Urn Tomb offers a plunging view across all of Petra's valley. Estimates suggest only 10% of the site has been excavated to date.

⏱ 90 min
3

Push on to the High Place of Sacrifice, accessible by a steep but short trail (20-30 min ascent). At the summit, a Nabataean altar carved from rock and a 360° view across Petra's mountains. The descent via Wadi Farasa lets you see less-frequented tombs and façades barely touched by time.

⏱ 90 min
4

End the day with the Colonnaded Street and Qasr al-Bint, one of Petra's rare built structures (not carved from rock)—probably a temple dedicated to the Nabataeans' principal deity. This is where French CNRS archaeologists conducted excavations that highlighted the city's urban character.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Petra by Night: the Treasury illuminated by 1,500 candles, accompanied by Bedouin music and sage tea. Offered Mondays, Wednesdays and Thursdays—inquire at the visitor center.

⏱ 90 min

Wadi Rum

★★★★☆
1

Second day at Petra—head for the Monastery (Ad Deir). Climb the 800 steps from the central basin: the ascent takes 45 minutes to an hour, but at the summit you discover a façade 47 meters wide by 48 meters high, even more massive than the Treasury but in a far wilder setting. Bedouins run a small tea house directly opposite—take a break with a view. Descend by the same path or explore the trail to the viewpoint overlooking the valley.

⏱ 150 min
2

On the way down toward the exit, stop at the Obelisk Tomb at the Siq entrance. It stacks two monuments: above, four obelisks representing the deceased, and below, a triclinium (funerary banquet hall). If you have a bit of time, make a detour to Little Petra (Siq al-Barid), 10 minutes by car north of Wadi Musa—a mini-canyon with Nabataean façades and far fewer people.

⏱ 60 min
3

In the afternoon, take the road to Wadi Rum (~1.5 hours from Wadi Musa). At the visitor center, board a 4x4 driven by a Bedouin guide. Wadi Rum, UNESCO-listed since 2011 (mixed natural and cultural site), covers 74,000 hectares of red sand and sandstone towers sometimes exceeding 1,800 meters. The circuit passes through Khazali Canyon, where 12,000-year-old petroglyphs show hunting scenes, then by the Um Fruth arch, easily accessible on foot. The desert has served as backdrop for Lawrence of Arabia, The Martian and other films.

⏱ 60 min
4

Finish the 4x4 tour with a stop at sunset on a southern red dune. When the low-angle light strikes the sandstone monoliths, the palette shifts from golden yellow to deep red in just minutes. Complete silence, not a car, not a sound—it's the strongest moment of the day.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Night at a Bedouin camp in Wadi Rum—traditional tents or sleeping under the stars depending on the season. The zarb dinner (chicken and vegetables slow-cooked under the sand) is followed by sweet tea around the fire. Far from any light pollution, the celestial vault is spectacular—you'll make out the Milky Way with the naked eye.

⏱ 120 min

Aqaba

★★★☆☆
1

Arrival in Aqaba, Jordan's only coastal city, perched at the country's extreme south on the Gulf of Aqaba. Don't expect a paradise beach downtown—the charm lies underwater. Start with the seafront (Corniche) and the Mamluk fort, built in the 14th century and reinforced by the Ottomans, which controlled the trade routes between Asia and Africa.

⏱ 45 min
2

Head toward South Beach, 10 km south of downtown, at the heart of the Aqaba Marine Park which stretches 7 km of coastline. With mask and snorkel, you access a coral reef directly from shore—no boat needed. The Red Sea harbors here over 200 species of coral and more than 1,000 fish species: clownfish in their anemones, yellow tang surgeonfish, lionfish, moray eels. A few meters from shore, you can spot an M42 military tank deliberately submerged at 6 meters depth to create an artificial reef.

⏱ 120 min
3

Push 5 minutes north of South Beach to Japanese Garden, considered by many the best snorkeling spot in Jordan. Its hard and soft corals with rounded forms recall a Japanese garden. The depth doesn't exceed 12 meters and visibility often reaches 30 to 40 meters.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Grilled fish dinner on Aqaba's seafront—souk or Corniche restaurants serve fresh Red Sea fish, accompanied by hummus and warm bread.

⏱ 60 min

Take advantage of the morning for a final snorkeling session on Aqaba's southern reefs—King Abdullah Reef, at the far south of the Marine Park near the Saudi border, shelters corals in excellent condition and hawksbill turtles. It's one of the least crowded and most beautiful spots.

Take the road back to Amman via the Dead Sea Highway which follows the Jordan Valley (~4 hours drive). The road itself is a spectacle: to the left the cliffs of Wadi Araba, to the right the desert, then the blue surface of the Dead Sea appearing below. If you have time, a stop at the Dana Reserve viewpoint offers a panorama across the gorges and mountains before plunging back down to the valley.

Amman

★★★★☆
1

Before heading to the airport, devote 2 hours to the Jordan Museum in the Ras al-Ain quarter. It's the country's largest museum (10,000 m²), with collections spanning prehistory to the present. The masterpieces: fragments of the Dead Sea Scrolls, discovered in the Qumran caves between 1947 and 1956—including the famous Copper Scroll that describes a hidden treasure—and the Ain Ghazal statues, 9,000 years old, among the world's oldest human statues.

⏱ 120 min
2

If time permits, take a final turn through Jabal al-Weibdeh, right next to the museum. The Darat al Funun gallery occupies several restored traditional houses overlooking downtown, with contemporary Arab art exhibitions and pleasant gardens. Entry is free.
