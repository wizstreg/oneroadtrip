Chester

★★★☆☆
1

Two thousand years of defence still standing strong: Chester's walls form the only complete Roman-medieval circuit in Britain, nearly 3 km of elevated walkway along red sandstone ramparts. Begun around 70 AD by the XXth Legion, they've survived Saxons, Normans, and civil war. Climb King Charles Tower: Charles I reportedly watched his troops defeated at Rowton Moor from here in 1645. Avoid July-August weekends when crowds compress the narrow sections.

⏱ 75 min
2

Built around 70 AD for the Roman legion Deva Victrix, Chester's amphitheatre is the largest in Britain. Half the site still sleeps beneath neighbouring buildings, giving it an odd iceberg quality. Two minutes' walk from the walls; free admission.

⏱ 30 min
$$$★9.6No. 23 at The Moorings, Chester

Llangollen

★★★☆☆
1

A canal suspended 38 metres above the Dee valley, with no guardrails on the water side, and boats still crossing it today. Pontcysyllte is Britain's highest and longest navigable aqueduct, designed by Thomas Telford and completed in 1805, inscribed as a UNESCO site in 2009. The 18 hollow masonry pillars support a cast-iron trough whose mortar was, according to local tradition, strengthened with lime and ox blood. Walk the towpath: it's free and the vertigo is guaranteed.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Board one of the motorboats departing from Trevor's Canal Wharf, opposite the aqueduct. The 45-minute return crossing offers a perspective the towpath walk cannot: finding yourself suspended above the void, level with the crows. Departures at regular intervals in season; no reservations needed for small groups.

⏱ 60 min
$$$★9.6Hafod
🚗Drive to next stop: 45 km · 1h

Betws Y Coed

★★★☆☆
1

Locals simply call it 'the gateway to Snowdonia' and the name says it all: Betws-y-Coed (pronounced 'bett-us-uh-koyd') is the slate-roofed village nestled at the confluence of four rivers where every hiker ends up sooner or later. The Welsh name means 'prayer house in the forest'. The Pont-y-Pair bridge, built around 1500, withstands the Llugwy floods with almost arrogant presence. Visit the Railway Museum for children and scout hiking routes at the information centre.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Take the A5 west for 3 miles to reach Swallow Falls, where the River Llugwy tumbles 42 metres down a narrow limestone gorge. The falls are among the highest in North Wales; the swifts that inspired their name swoop in June and July. Access to the viewing platform costs a few pounds at the turnstile. Waterproof shoes strongly recommended.

⏱ 45 min
$$$★9.8Y Bwthyn
🚗Drive to next stop: 25 km · 35min

Llanberis

★★★★☆
1

The climb isn't easy, but at the summit of Yr Wyddfa at 1,085 metres you'll stand at the highest point in Wales and England—with Ireland and the Isle of Man sometimes visible on clear days. Six different paths lead to the top; the most accessible is the Llanberis Path from the village. Count on 6 to 8 hours round-trip on foot. Weather changes brutally: set off with waterproofs even in fine weather.

⏱ 360 min

Activities

Opened in 1896, this rack-and-pinion railway is the only one of its kind still operating in the UK, climbing 7.6 km from Llanberis to the summit. For those who don't fancy walking 6 hours—or who want to descend without shattered knees—it solves the problem elegantly. Hafod Eryri cafe at the summit is open when the train runs. Book online: places sell out fast in July-August.

⏱ 150 min
$$$★9.5Marchlyn
🚗Drive to next stop: 25 km · 35min

Caernarfon

★★★★☆
1

Oppressive by design, fascinating nonetheless: Caernarfon was conceived by Edward I from 1283 as a symbol of absolute conquest over Wales. Its polygonal towers were inspired by the walls of Constantinople—not the usual discretion of medieval fortresses. Inscribed as a UNESCO site in 1986 alongside Beaumaris, Harlech, and Conwy, it hosted Prince Charles's investiture in 1969. The Eagle Tower with its stone eagles and the King's Gate with six portcullises deserve your time.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Photograph the castle from the town streets where medieval towers frame views of shops and everyday Welsh life

⏱ 60 min
$$$★9.5Traditional Welsh cottage in Llanberis

Beaumaris

★★★☆☆
1

If an unfinished castle can be a masterpiece, it's Beaumaris. Edward I and his architect James of St George laid out their most ambitious fortress here in 1295, the last of the iron ring, designed from an empty marsh without the constraints of a pre-existing site. Result: near-perfect concentric symmetry, 300 arrow slits total, moats with their own dock. The money ran out, Scotland called, the towers were never completed—and UNESCO inscribed it anyway in 1986.

⏱ 90 min
$$$★9.2Lluest
🚗Drive to next stop: 40 km · 55min

Holyhead

★★★☆☆
1

Wind lashes the cliffs, puffins huddle in their burrows, and South Stack's white lighthouse seems to float above the Irish Sea 100 metres from shore. The RSPB reserve shelters several thousand seabirds—guillemots, razorbills, puffins, choughs—between April and July. Ellin's Tower viewing station provides access to the reserve's binoculars. Visit between April and July for puffins; off-season the site remains striking but the nests are empty.

⏱ 90 min
$$$★9.3Upper Harbour Watch
🚗Drive to next stop: 25 km · 35min

Newborough

★★☆☆☆
1

The Welsh call it the island of the lovers' saint: Llanddwyn is dedicated to Saint Dwynwen, Welsh patron saint of lovers since the 5th century, whose feast day on 25th January corresponds to the 'Welsh Valentine's Day'. The peninsula only becomes an island at the highest tides. You reach it on foot from Newborough car park, through the dunes and forest of the national reserve—about 30 to 45 minutes. The ruins of the medieval chapel, the lighthouse, and the views across to Snowdonia reward the effort.

⏱ 90 min
$$$★10Gull Cottage
🚗Drive to next stop: 75 km · 1h40

Portmeirion

★★★★☆
1

Too kitsch? Perhaps. But still: this Italianate fantasy village built between 1925 and 1975 by Welsh architect Sir Clough Williams-Ellis is one of Britain's most singular architectural works. Frank Lloyd Wright visited in 1956—his only visit to the British Isles. Noël Coward wrote a play here. The television series The Prisoner was filmed here in 1967-68. Admission covers the entire village; arrive early to avoid coach tours.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Take a picturesque coastal walk through the wooded gardens with views across the estuary to Snowdonia

⏱ 60 min
$$$★9.6Castle Cottage Inn
🚗Drive to next stop: 35 km · 50min

Harlech

★★★★☆
1

Built between 1283 and 1289 on a 61-metre crag overlooking Cardigan Bay, Harlech inspired the song 'Men of Harlech' after withstanding a seven-year siege by Yorkist forces during the Wars of the Roses. It's one of Edward I's four UNESCO castles in North Wales. The battlements have no guardrails—UNESCO protection obliges—and the view across to Snowdonia from the wall-walk is vertiginous. The 'Way from the Sea', 108 steps cut into the rock, once allowed defenders to be resupplied by boat.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Capture the perfect castle photograph from the road below, framing it with the Snowdonia mountains in the background

⏱ 60 min
$$$★10Carreg Wen
🚗Drive to next stop: 70 km · 1h35

Aberystwyth

★★★☆☆
1

Take the 1896 electric cliff railway to the summit of Constitution Hill: it's the longest in Britain, and from the top the panoramic view embraces Cardigan Bay and the northern peaks on clear days. At the summit stands a large Camera Obscura. Below, the kilometre-long Victorian promenade follows a dark sand beach with the 1864 pier—the oldest in Wales.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Stroll the Victorian promenade and watch the sunset over Cardigan Bay from the seafront

⏱ 60 min

Devils Bridge

★★☆☆☆
1

Three stacked bridges and a devilish legend: according to tradition, the first bridge—probably dating from the 12th century—was built by the devil in exchange for a soul, and an old woman outwitted him by sending her dog across first. The Mynach Falls plunge 91 metres total in five successive cascades into the gorge. Wordsworth wrote about this place in 1824. The lower path (Jacob's Ladder) is steep; bring waterproof shoes.

⏱ 60 min
$$$★9.2Caban Glas
🚗Drive to next stop: 85 km · 1h55

St Davids

★★★☆☆
1

The purple stone of Cambrian sandstone glows in the evening light, and the cathedral rises from a hollow where it hides until the last moment—the effect is striking. St Davids is Britain's smallest city, barely 1,600 inhabitants, but its status comes from here: the cathedral founded in the 6th century on the site of Dewi Sant's monastery, patron saint of Wales. Pope Callixtus II decreed in the 12th century that two pilgrimages here equalled one to Rome. Arrive early for the light in the carved Welsh oak nave.

⏱ 60 min
2

The ruins of the 14th-century Bishop's Palace, across the River Alun, form one of the most spectacular Gothic ensembles in Wales. Bishop Henry de Gower had a great banqueting hall and a gallery built here in a distinctive arcaded style. Separate Cadw admission; allow 45 minutes. Open-air theatre performances take place here in summer.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Book a boat trip with Thousand Island Expeditions to Skomer Island, home to southern Britain's largest Atlantic puffin colony

⏱ 60 min
$$$★10Ty Rhosyn
🚗Drive to next stop: 45 km · 1h

Tenby

★★★★☆
1

Touristy, yes, but Tenby's multicoloured harbour—its Georgian houses stacked on the cliff, its four beaches around a medieval promontory—fully deserves its reputation. The Welsh name 'Dinbych-y-Pysgod' means 'little fortress of fish'. The Five Arches, the only surviving gate in the 13th-century walls, gives access to the old town. Avoid July-August if possible; parking becomes a nightmare. Off-season, the town regains its character.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The hike to the tip of Worm's Head (about 1.5 km from the causeway) gives access to a natural arch, a blowhole, and seabird colonies. Allow at least 2h30 on the island to have time to return before the tide cuts off the passage. Don't attempt the crossing if the sea is rough.

⏱ 60 min

The sunset at Rhossili is renowned throughout the Gower Peninsula. From the cliff edge above the beach, you watch the sun drop directly into the sea with Worm's Head in the foreground. Bring a picnic and wait—it's worth the patience.

⏱ 60 min

The boat crossing to Caldey Island takes 20 minutes from Tenby harbour. This private island inhabited by Cistercian monks since 1929 produces its own chocolate and herb-based perfumes. Access is seasonal (generally April to October). Outside ferry times, tour Tenby's three main beaches (North, South, and Castle Beach) following the cliff path.

⏱ 60 min

The Bosherston-Broad Haven South-Barafundle Bay coastal loop is the best circuit of the day: 10 km from the Stackpole Quay or Bosherston car park, alternating pond-side trails and clifftop coastal paths. The section between Broad Haven South and Barafundle passes Stackpole Head, a limestone promontory with sea stacks offshore. Allow 3 hours. In June-July, the lily ponds are in flower—don't miss this moment.

⏱ 60 min
$$$★9.3The Dunes
🚗Drive to next stop: 18 km · 25min

Stackpole

★★★★★
1

If you accept an 800-metre walk from Stackpole Quay car park, you'll reach one of the purest beaches in Wales—and in Britain. Barafundle was the private beach of the Earls Cawdor, and the access wall still betrays this use. Country Life ranked it the best beach in the UK in 2004. No facilities on the beach: bring water, food, and sunscreen. Pay car park at the Quay (National Trust).

⏱ 120 min
$$$★10Chapel Hill House
🚗Drive to next stop: 85 km · 1h55

Rhossili

★★★★☆
1

Three miles of white sand facing the Atlantic, a tidal island shaped like a dragon—Worm's Head—and Dylan Thomas, who got trapped here by the tide one night and wrote a short story about it. Rhossili is the iconic beach of the Gower Peninsula, Britain's first designated Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty in 1956. The sun descends directly onto the horizon from the cliffs of Rhossili Down: arrive late afternoon. Check tide times before attempting Worm's Head: the rocky crossing is only passable for 2h30 either side of low tide.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

The coastal loop from Southgate around Three Cliffs Bay and Pennard Burrows is one of Gower's best walking circuits: 8 km, minimal elevation gain, scenery changing every ten minutes between cliffs, dunes, estuaries, and beaches. Pass behind Pennard Castle for the plunging view over Three Cliffs from behind.

⏱ 60 min
$$$★9.7The Caswell bay hide out
🚗Drive to next stop: 20 km · 30min

Three Cliffs Bay

★★★★☆
1

Hard to reach, and that's precisely why Three Cliffs Bay is Gower's most beautiful beach. The River Pennard Pill winds through the dunes before meeting the sea, the three limestone cliffs that give it its name loom over the beach, and the Norman ruins of 12th-century Pennard Castle crown the ridge. Park at Southgate and walk 30 minutes on the coastal path. Low tide only for beach access. Strong currents: swimming not advised.

⏱ 75 min
$$$★10Cariad Cwtch
🚗Drive to next stop: 110 km · 2h25

Hay On Wye

★★★★☆
1

Richard Booth, the founder, proclaimed himself King of Hay and appointed his horse as Prime Minister in 1977 to attract world attention—and it worked. This town of fewer than 2,000 inhabitants on the Welsh border counts more than 20 secondhand and antiquarian bookshops, making it the world's first book town since the 1960s. Richard Booth's Bookshop on Lion Street occupies three floors. The Hay Festival of literature takes place in late May; the rest of the year the town is quieter.

⏱ 120 min
$$$★9.4Foyles of Glasbury
🚗Drive to next stop: 35 km · 50min

Brecon

★★★☆☆
1

Pen y Fan is the highest summit in southern Britain—886 metres. From its flat ridge, on clear days, the view embraces the entire Gower Peninsula and the Bristol Channel. Its Welsh name literally means 'the summit of the peak' and it was once known as Cadair Arthur—King Arthur's Chair. The path from Pont ar Daf car park (on the A470) is the most direct: 3.2 km one way, 450 metres of elevation gain. It's the 'motorway' that regulars like to deride, but the summit view is worth any crowd. Arrive before 9am in summer if you want the ridge to yourself.

⏱ 180 min
2

If you do the horseshoe (Pen y Fan Horseshoe), the summit of Cribyn (795 m) naturally joins the programme: it dominates Pen y Fan by its eastern ridge and offers a different angle of view—more open towards Neuadd Reservoir and the central hills. The ridge between the two summits is narrow in places; watch out for gusts of wind. Pack waterproofs and good boots whatever the weather forecast in Brecon below—conditions change quickly at 800 metres.

⏱ 90 min
3

Brecon is a pleasant Georgian market town, sitting in the Usk valley between the Brecon Beacons and the Black Mountains. Stroll around the 11th-century cathedral and the Monmouth & Brecon Canal—one of the best-preserved in Wales, where you can hire a day boat.

⏱ 60 min
4

Waterfall Country, between Pontneddfechan and Ystradfellte, concentrates more waterfalls per square kilometre than anywhere else in Britain. The Rivers Mellte and Hepste tumble down wooded gorges cut through red sandstone, creating a succession of falls each with its own personality. Pontneddfechan is the best entry point for walkers coming from the south—the Elidir Trail starts there, and the toilets and cafe at the Waterfall Centre are in the village.

⏱ 60 min
5

Sgwd yr Eira—'the snow waterfall' in Welsh—is the undisputed star of the Four Waterfalls Walk. Nine metres wide, it falls in an almost horizontal curtain over the River Hepste, and a narrow path cut into the rock allows you to walk literally behind the water veil. This path was used by shepherds to drive their flocks across. Behind the falls, the noise is deafening, the ground gleaming and slippery, the air cold and saturated with droplets—you see daylight filtered through the water curtain as through frosted glass. Prepare to get soaked even in fine weather, put your phone in a waterproof pocket before approaching, and wear grippy-soled shoes. The descent from the main path counts about 170 steep steps.

⏱ 90 min
6

The village of Ystradfellte is the ideal starting point for exploring the eastern part of Waterfall Country. Tiny—a handful of farms and an inn—but well positioned between Sgwd Clun-Gwyn, Sgwd Isaf Clun-Gwyn, and Sgwd yr Eira. Cwm Porth car park (2 km south) provides access to the Four Waterfalls loop.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

At 886 metres, it's the highest point in South Wales and in all of Britain south of Snowdonia. The ascent via the Storey Arms / Pont ar Daf Path takes about 3 hours round-trip and remains accessible to fit walkers. The British Army selects its special forces here during the 'Fan Dance', a 24-km night march with loaded pack. In fine weather, the flat-topped silhouette of Pen y Fan with its neighbour Corn Du (873 m) is recognisable from 30 km around.

⏱ 240 min
$$$★9.9Pantybeiliau House Bed & Breakfast
🚗Drive to next stop: 45 km · 1h

Coelbren

★☆☆☆☆
1

An ordinary waterfall? Not at all: here's the highest waterfall in South Wales, a 27-metre plunge into a wooded gorge of oaks and ash trees. Henrhyd is also the only place in Britain where you can walk behind a major waterfall—Christopher Nolan used it as the Batcave entrance in The Dark Knight Rises in 2012. National Trust property, free parking at the start. The 20-30 minute descent to the bottom of the gorge is steep and slippery in wet weather.

⏱ 60 min
$$$★10Suite 5 - Sleeping Giant Hotel - Pen Y Cae Inn
🚗Drive to next stop: 45 km · 1h

Cardiff

★★★★☆
1

Twenty centuries of history wrapped in a Victorian neo-Gothic dream: Cardiff Castle rests on 1st-century Roman foundations, but what you see today is the extravagant work of architect William Burges for the Marquess of Bute in the 19th century. Inside, rooms whose decor rivals in ostentation any European palace. During the Second World War, the tunnels beneath the walls sheltered nearly 2,000 people. Admission includes the Firing Line Museum of the Welsh Soldier.

⏱ 90 min
2

The fresh grass beneath the century-old trees of Bute Park stretches across 56 hectares along the River Taff—that's 75 football pitches in the heart of the capital. Designed by Andrew Pettigrew between 1873 and 1903 as the private garden of the Marquess of Bute, the park was gifted to the city in 1947 on the death of the 4th Marquess. The champion tree trail, sculpture path, and riverside cafes are worth the walk. Grade I listed by Cadw.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Principality Stadium—74,500 capacity, retractable roof in 20 minutes, the UK's first retractable-roof stadium—offers tours on non-match days. The guided tour takes you into the changing rooms, through the players' tunnel, and right to the pitch edge. Fun fact: the pitch is grown outside then rolled inside for matches. Opened in June 1999, it hosted the Rugby World Cup in its first year, then the 2017 Champions League final (Real Madrid-Juventus) and concerts by Beyoncé, Ed Sheeran, and Oasis. Book your tour in advance.

⏱ 60 min

Cardiff Bay is worth visiting whenever the sun deigns to appear. The old coal docks—once the world's largest coal-exporting port—were transformed into a 200-hectare freshwater lake thanks to construction of a barrage completed in 1999. Stroll around Mermaid Quay, peek into the Senedd (the Welsh Parliament, a glazed building by Richard Rogers), then walk the barrage to Penarth—2 km walk with sea views. Doctor Who fans will recognise Roald Dahl Plass: it's where the fictional Torchwood Hub entrance was located.

⏱ 60 min

The coastal path between Dunraven Bay and Nash Point (about 7 km round-trip) is one of the finest walks on the Glamorgan coast. The path follows a grassy limestone plateau with constant views across the Bristol Channel and, on clear days, to the Devon coast. Pack waterproof boots—the path can be muddy after rain—and a waterproof jacket, even in summer.

⏱ 60 min

If the coast has whetted your appetite for culture, St Fagans National Museum of History, 6 km west of Cardiff, is one of the UK's best open-air museums—and it's free. More than 40 historic buildings have been dismantled and rebuilt on this 40-hectare site, from 15th-century Welsh cottages to industrial revolution workers' houses. Worth seeing if you have a free evening.

⏱ 60 min
$$$★9.5Sophisticated Cardiff Apartment Free Parking

Cardiff

★★★★☆
1

Opened in 2004, the Wales Millennium Centre displays giant bilingual letters in steel and bronze on its facade: 'In These Stones Horizons Sing / Creu Gwir Fel Gwydr O Ffwrnais Awen'. The entrance hall is free; it hosts daily concerts. Then move on to the Senedd next door—the Welsh Parliament, in glass and cedar, is open to the public on weekdays.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Aquabus links the city centre to Cardiff Bay hourly from the foot of the castle. The Bay, a former industrial port area converted into a cultural quarter, concentrates the Senedd, Wales Millennium Centre, Mermaid Quay, and Pierhead Museum. An evening on the quays watching the sun set over the Bay barrage makes a fitting conclusion to twelve days driving through Wales.
