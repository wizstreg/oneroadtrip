Chester

★★★☆☆
1

Two thousand years of defence still standing strong: Chester's walls form the only complete Roman-medieval circuit in Britain, nearly 3 km of elevated walkway along red sandstone ramparts. Begun around 70 AD by the XXth Legion, they've survived Saxons, Normans, and civil war. Climb King Charles Tower: Charles I reportedly watched his troops defeated at Rowton Moor from here in 1645. Avoid July-August weekends when crowds compress the narrow sections.

⏱ 75 min
2

Built around 70 AD for the Roman legion Deva Victrix, Chester's amphitheatre is the largest in Britain. Half the site still sleeps beneath neighbouring buildings, giving it an odd iceberg quality. Two minutes' walk from the walls; free admission.

Llangollen

★★★☆☆
1

A canal suspended 38 metres above the Dee valley, with no guardrails on the water side, and boats still crossing it today. Pontcysyllte is Britain's highest and longest navigable aqueduct, designed by Thomas Telford and completed in 1805, inscribed as a UNESCO site in 2009. The 18 hollow masonry pillars support a cast-iron trough whose mortar was, according to local tradition, strengthened with lime and ox blood. Walk the towpath: it's free and the vertigo is guaranteed.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Board one of the motorboats departing from Trevor's Canal Wharf, opposite the aqueduct. The 45-minute return crossing offers a perspective the towpath walk cannot: finding yourself suspended above the void, level with the crows. Departures at regular intervals in season; no reservations needed for small groups.

Betws Y Coed

★★★☆☆
1

Locals simply call it 'the gateway to Snowdonia' and the name says it all: Betws-y-Coed (pronounced 'bett-us-uh-koyd') is the slate-roofed village nestled at the confluence of four rivers where every hiker ends up sooner or later. The Welsh name means 'prayer house in the forest'. The Pont-y-Pair bridge, built around 1500, withstands the Llugwy floods with almost arrogant presence. Visit the Railway Museum for children and scout hiking routes at the information centre.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Take the A5 west for 3 miles to reach Swallow Falls, where the River Llugwy tumbles 42 metres down a narrow limestone gorge. The falls are among the highest in North Wales; the swifts that inspired their name swoop in June and July. Access to the viewing platform costs a few pounds at the turnstile. Waterproof shoes strongly recommended.

Llanberis

★★★★☆
1

The climb isn't easy, but at the summit of Yr Wyddfa at 1,085 metres you'll stand at the highest point in Wales and England—with Ireland and the Isle of Man sometimes visible on clear days. Six different paths lead to the top; the most accessible is the Llanberis Path from the village. Count on 6 to 8 hours round-trip on foot. Weather changes brutally: set off with waterproofs even in fine weather.

⏱ 360 min

Activities

Opened in 1896, this rack-and-pinion railway is the only one of its kind still operating in the UK, climbing 7.6 km from Llanberis to the summit. For those who don't fancy walking 6 hours—or who want to descend without shattered knees—it solves the problem elegantly. Hafod Eryri cafe at the summit is open when the train runs. Book online: places sell out fast in July-August.

Caernarfon

★★★★☆
1

Oppressive by design, fascinating nonetheless: Caernarfon was conceived by Edward I from 1283 as a symbol of absolute conquest over Wales. Its polygonal towers were inspired by the walls of Constantinople—not the usual discretion of medieval fortresses. Inscribed as a UNESCO site in 1986 alongside Beaumaris, Harlech, and Conwy, it hosted Prince Charles's investiture in 1969. The Eagle Tower with its stone eagles and the King's Gate with six portcullises deserve your time.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Beaumaris

★★★☆☆
1

If an unfinished castle can be a masterpiece, it's Beaumaris. Edward I and his architect James of St George laid out their most ambitious fortress here in 1295, the last of the iron ring, designed from an empty marsh without the constraints of a pre-existing site. Result: near-perfect concentric symmetry, 300 arrow slits total, moats with their own dock. The money ran out, Scotland called, the towers were never completed—and UNESCO inscribed it anyway in 1986.

1

Wind lashes the cliffs, puffins huddle in their burrows, and South Stack's white lighthouse seems to float above the Irish Sea 100 metres from shore. Visit between April and July for puffins; off-season the site remains striking but the nests are empty.

Brecon

★★★☆☆
1

Pen y Fan is the highest summit in southern Britain—886 metres. From its flat ridge, on clear days, the view embraces the entire Gower Peninsula and the Bristol Channel. Its Welsh name literally means 'the summit of the peak' and it was once known as Cadair Arthur—King Arthur's Chair. The path from Pont ar Daf car park (on the A470) is the most direct: 3.2 km one way, 450 metres of elevation gain. It's the 'motorway' that regulars like to deride, but the summit view is worth any crowd. Arrive before 9am in summer if you want the ridge to yourself.

⏱ 180 min
2

If you do the horseshoe (Pen y Fan Horseshoe), the summit of Cribyn (795 m) naturally joins the programme: it dominates Pen y Fan by its eastern ridge and offers a different angle of view—more open towards Neuadd Reservoir and the central hills. The ridge between the two summits is narrow in places; watch out for gusts of wind. Pack waterproofs and good boots whatever the weather forecast in Brecon below—conditions change quickly at 800 metres.

⏱ 90 min
3

Brecon is a pleasant Georgian market town, sitting in the Usk valley between the Brecon Beacons and the Black Mountains. Stroll around the 11th-century cathedral and the Monmouth & Brecon Canal—one of the best-preserved in Wales, where you can hire a day boat.

⏱ 60 min
4

Waterfall Country, between Pontneddfechan and Ystradfellte, concentrates more waterfalls per square kilometre than anywhere else in Britain. The Rivers Mellte and Hepste tumble down wooded gorges cut through red sandstone, creating a succession of falls each with its own personality. Pontneddfechan is the best entry point for walkers coming from the south—the Elidir Trail starts there, and the toilets and cafe at the Waterfall Centre are in the village.

⏱ 60 min
5

Sgwd yr Eira—'the snow waterfall' in Welsh—is the undisputed star of the Four Waterfalls Walk. Nine metres wide, it falls in an almost horizontal curtain over the River Hepste, and a narrow path cut into the rock allows you to walk literally behind the water veil. This path was used by shepherds to drive their flocks across. Behind the falls, the noise is deafening, the ground gleaming and slippery, the air cold and saturated with droplets—you see daylight filtered through the water curtain as through frosted glass. Prepare to get soaked even in fine weather, put your phone in a waterproof pocket before approaching, and wear grippy-soled shoes. The descent from the main path counts about 170 steep steps.

⏱ 90 min
6

Henrhyd Falls, about fifteen kilometres further west near Coelbren, is the highest waterfall in South Wales—27 metres. It's less visited than the Four Waterfalls and worth the detour if you have time. Access from the National Trust car park requires a 20-minute walk.

⏱ 60 min
7

The village of Ystradfellte is the ideal starting point for exploring the eastern part of Waterfall Country. Tiny—a handful of farms and an inn—but well positioned between Sgwd Clun-Gwyn, Sgwd Isaf Clun-Gwyn, and Sgwd yr Eira. Cwm Porth car park (2 km south) provides access to the Four Waterfalls loop.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

At 886 metres, it's the highest point in South Wales and in all of Britain south of Snowdonia. The ascent via the Storey Arms / Pont ar Daf Path takes about 3 hours round-trip and remains accessible to fit walkers. The British Army selects its special forces here during the 'Fan Dance', a 24-km night march with loaded pack. In fine weather, the flat-topped silhouette of Pen y Fan with its neighbour Corn Du (873 m) is recognisable from 30 km around.

Cardiff

★★★★☆
1

Twenty centuries of history wrapped in a Victorian neo-Gothic dream: Cardiff Castle rests on 1st-century Roman foundations, but what you see today is the extravagant work of architect William Burges for the Marquess of Bute in the 19th century. Inside, rooms whose decor rivals in ostentation any European palace. During the Second World War, the tunnels beneath the walls sheltered nearly 2,000 people. Admission includes the Firing Line Museum of the Welsh Soldier.

⏱ 90 min
2

The fresh grass beneath the century-old trees of Bute Park stretches across 56 hectares along the River Taff—that's 75 football pitches in the heart of the capital. Designed by Andrew Pettigrew between 1873 and 1903 as the private garden of the Marquess of Bute, the park was gifted to the city in 1947 on the death of the 4th Marquess. The champion tree trail, sculpture path, and riverside cafes are worth the walk. Grade I listed by Cadw.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Principality Stadium—74,500 capacity, retractable roof in 20 minutes, the UK's first retractable-roof stadium—offers tours on non-match days. The guided tour takes you into the changing rooms, through the players' tunnel, and right to the pitch edge. Fun fact: the pitch is grown outside then rolled inside for matches. Opened in June 1999, it hosted the Rugby World Cup in its first year, then the 2017 Champions League final (Real Madrid-Juventus) and concerts by Beyoncé, Ed Sheeran, and Oasis. Book your tour in advance.

⏱ 60 min

Cardiff Bay is worth visiting whenever the sun deigns to appear. The old coal docks—once the world's largest coal-exporting port—were transformed into a 200-hectare freshwater lake thanks to construction of a barrage completed in 1999. Stroll around Mermaid Quay, peek into the Senedd (the Welsh Parliament, a glazed building by Richard Rogers), then walk the barrage to Penarth—2 km walk with sea views. Doctor Who fans will recognise Roald Dahl Plass: it's where the fictional Torchwood Hub entrance was located.

⏱ 60 min

The coastal path between Dunraven Bay and Nash Point (about 7 km round-trip) is one of the finest walks on the Glamorgan coast. The path follows a grassy limestone plateau with constant views across the Bristol Channel and, on clear days, to the Devon coast. Pack waterproof boots—the path can be muddy after rain—and a waterproof jacket, even in summer.

⏱ 60 min

If the coast has whetted your appetite for culture, St Fagans National Museum of History, 6 km west of Cardiff, is one of the UK's best open-air museums—and it's free. More than 40 historic buildings have been dismantled and rebuilt on this 40-hectare site, from 15th-century Welsh cottages to industrial revolution workers' houses. Worth seeing if you have a free evening.

⏱ 60 min
