Dalkey

Where Dublin's medieval merchants built seven castles to guard their fortunes
Dalkey Castle Castle StreetColiemore Harbour DalkeyDalkey IslandKilliney Hill Park
✈️ Book a flight🚗 Rent a car
1

Dalkey was once Dublin's main port—in medieval times, the Liffey was too silted for large ships, so merchants built seven fortified castles to store their goods. Only two remain standing on Castle Street. Wander the lanes of this coastal village dubbed Ireland's 'Amalfi Coast'—you'll pass bookshops, delis and pubs where local celebrities are regulars. If you're here on a Tuesday, it's market day, a tradition running since 1482.

⏱ 60 min
2

Step inside Dalkey Castle, the only one of the seven medieval fortifications still standing and open to visitors. Built in the 14th century, it was nicknamed 'Goat Castle' by the Cheever family in the 1600s. The guided tour is theatrical—costumed actors demonstrate the 'Murder Hole' (a defensive trapdrop), archery techniques, and Tudor barber-surgeon remedies. Climb the battlements for sweeping views over Dublin Bay. Book online—places fill fast in summer.

⏱ 75 min
3

From Coliemore Harbour, catch a boat to Dalkey Island—25 hectares of uninhabited land 300 metres offshore. The island has been occupied for over 6,000 years, predating Stonehenge. You'll see the ruins of St. Begnet's Church (7th century), a Napoleonic Martello tower, and wild goats who rule the rocks. Vikings ran a slave trading post here in the 10th century—coins from King Edgar's reign (959-975) were found during Victorian excavations. Allow 75 minutes return including the crossing.

⏱ 90 min
4

Don't miss Sorrento Park, a discreet promontory tucked between Dalkey and Killiney—the entrance on Coliemore Road is easy to overlook. From its benches, you'll gaze down at Dalkey Island and across to the Wicklow Mountains. It's the kind of view that local residents contemplate each morning from their villas hidden behind the palm trees.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Walk up Killiney Hill—20 minutes from the car park brings you to the summit. The hill rises to 140 metres and offers 360° views: Dublin to the north, the Wicklow Mountains to the south, and on clear days, Snowdon in Wales to the east. At the top you'll find a 1742 obelisk, built as a famine relief project, and the 'Pyramid of Dublin' (1852)—legend says if you walk around it uphill, then make a wish facing Dalkey Island, it'll come true. Arrive early on weekends when the car park fills quickly.

⏱ 60 min

Head down for a swim at the Forty Foot, the historic bathing spot in Sandycove. This natural rock pool, once men-only, now welcomes everyone—year-round regulars plunge in even in winter. The water is bracing (10-15°C in summer), but the experience is worth the thermal shock. James Joyce set the opening of Ulysses here. Bring shoes that can handle the rocks.

⏱ 45 min

Enniskerry

Gateway to Wicklow beneath Ireland's highest waterfall
Enniskerry Village WicklowPowerscourt Waterfall EnniskerryGreat Sugar Loaf KilmacanogueDjouce Mountain Wicklow
✈️ Book a flight🚗 Rent a car
1

Enniskerry is one of Wicklow's most photographed villages—a handful of colourful houses clustered around a triangle of green, with St. Patrick's Church watching over the scene. The village serves as the gateway to the Powerscourt estate and the Wicklow Mountains. Walk around it in twenty minutes, but take time to stop in one of the cafés for a homemade scone before tackling the day ahead.

⏱ 45 min
2

Six kilometres from Enniskerry, Powerscourt Waterfall plunges 121 metres down granite rock—it's Ireland's highest waterfall. The valley was carved by a glacier, and the River Dargle cascades down in a fan year-round. In 1821, Viscount Powerscourt had the waterfall dammed to impress King George IV—the king never came, and when the water was released, it swept away the bridge. Visit after rain when the flow is at its peak. Warning: no phone signal at the car park, so if you're coming by taxi, arrange your return in advance.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Great Sugar Loaf rises to 501 metres—its conical silhouette looks volcanic, but it's actually a Cambrian quartzite dome. The ascent is short (about 1 hour return) but steep, with a rocky scramble at the summit. The view sweeps from the coast at Bray to Greystones, across the Wicklow Mountains and Dublin Bay. On clear days, you'll spot Wales. Don't leave valuables in your car—thefts have been reported at the car park.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

If you're an experienced walker, tackle Djouce Mountain (725m). The trail starts from Crone Woods and climbs through forest before emerging onto heather moorland with plunging views down to Powerscourt Waterfall below. Allow 3-4 hours for the full loop. The terrain can be muddy and exposed to wind—bring hiking boots and a windproof layer.

⏱ 180 min

Detour to the National Botanic Gardens at Kilmacurragh, east of Wicklow. This 21-hectare estate, founded in 1712 by the Acton family, houses 180 rhododendron species and over 420 varieties—it's the national collection. The acidic soil and mild microclimate allow Himalayan and southern hemisphere species to thrive where they'd fail elsewhere in Ireland. You'll also see giant sequoias and a dawn redwood once thought extinct. The ruins of the Queen Anne house (1697) add a romantic touch. Free entry, guided tours at noon and 3pm from April to October.

⏱ 90 min

Cashel

Where limestone towers bear witness to Ireland's sacred kings
Rock of Cashel TipperaryCormac's Chapel Rock of CashelHore Abbey CashelFethard Medieval Town Walls Tipperary
✈️ Book a flight🚗 Rent a car
1

The Rock of Cashel is visible for miles—this 109-metre limestone outcrop dominates the golden plain of Tipperary with the silhouette of a phantom cathedral. It's here that St. Patrick supposedly converted King Aengus in the 5th century, accidentally piercing the king's foot with his crosier—the king said nothing, believing it was part of the ritual. Brian Boru was crowned High King here in 978. Arrive early morning or late afternoon to avoid the tour buses—after 10am it gets crowded. Tickets for Cormac's Chapel (guided tour only) are sold at the entrance and sell out quickly.

⏱ 90 min
2

Cormac's Chapel, consecrated in 1134, is the Rock's jewel—it's the only Romanesque building in Ireland to preserve its original frescoes, nearly 900 years old. Two carpenters sent by the Abbot of Regensburg contributed to its construction, hence the Germanic influences. Beside it, the 28-metre round tower (circa 1100) is the oldest monument on the site—its entrance is 4 metres above ground because its foundations only go down one metre. The Gothic cathedral (1235-1270) lost its roof in 1749, when Archbishop Price had it removed—a decision that still rankles.

⏱ 60 min
3

Five hundred metres below the Rock, cross the field (sometimes muddy, sometimes with cows) to reach Hore Abbey—the last Cistercian abbey founded in Ireland before the Reformation, in 1272. Legend says the archbishop banished the Benedictines after dreaming they wanted to behead him. In reality, he replaced them with Cistercians from Mellifont and gifted them land, mills, and two jugs of beer from each brew of Cashel's 38 breweries. The abbey is freely accessible—you'll often have it to yourself.

⏱ 60 min
4

Push on to Fethard, 16km east of Cashel—this Norman village has Ireland's best-preserved medieval walls, with over 90% of the original ramparts still standing. The walls date from 1292 and rise to 7.8 metres in places. Follow the Heritage Trail along the River Clashawley—you'll pass Holy Trinity Church (13th century, still active), the Augustinian abbey and three Sheela-na-gigs, those mysterious medieval carvings whose exact meaning nobody knows. In 1650, Cromwell marched on Fethard, but the town surrendered—which is what saved its walls.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

If you have time, detour through the Glen of Aherlow, a valley nestled between the Galtee Mountains and the Slievenamuck hills. Waymarked trails wind through the forests—the Christ the King loop offers open views across the entire valley. You'll encounter only sheep and blackbirds.

⏱ 90 min

Before leaving Cashel, walk around the town—the lanes off Main Street hide 15th-century merchant houses and remnants of the town walls (built between 1319 and 1324). The BrúBorú Cultural Centre, at the foot of the Rock, hosts traditional music evenings in season.

⏱ 45 min

Kinvara Harbour GalwayDunguaire Castle KinvaraPoulnabrone Dolmen BurrenFlaggy Shore New Quay Clare

1

Kinvara is a small fishing port on the south shore of Galway Bay—the kind of village where colourful houses reflect in the harbour water at high tide. The town comes alive in summer during Cruinniú na mBád, a gathering of traditional Galway hooker boats. Stroll along the quay and through the lanes—you'll find artisan galleries and pocket-sized pubs.

⏱ 60 min
2

Dunguaire Castle sits on a rocky spur at the water's edge, 300 metres from the village. Built in 1520 by the O'Hynes clan, this 23-metre tower takes its name from King Guaire, 7th-century ruler of Connacht, famed for his generosity—legend says dishes flew from his table to feed the poor. In the 20th century, poet Oliver St John Gogarty bought it and made it a meeting place for Yeats, Shaw and Synge. Climb to the roof for views over Kinvara and the bay—mind the narrow passages. Medieval banquets are held in the evenings from April to October—book ahead.

⏱ 75 min
3

Head into the Burren—twenty minutes from Kinvara, you enter a lunar landscape of fissured limestone where Arctic and Mediterranean orchids grow side by side. Stop at Poulnabrone Dolmen—this 5,800-year-old portal tomb stands alone amid the stone slabs. Excavations revealed the bones of 33 people and a newborn. The site is freely accessible, but arrive before 10am or after 4pm to avoid the crowds.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walk the Flaggy Shore between Kinvara and New Quay—Nobel laureate Seamus Heaney wrote a poem about it. The walk follows limestone slabs that slope into the sea. You'll see swans, herons and cormorants. Perfect late in the day when the only sound is the wind.

⏱ 60 min

Explore the quieter eastern Burren. The turloughs (seasonal lakes) and clints-and-grikes (fissured pavements) shelter rare flora—from May to July, wild orchids, gentians and thyme bloom between the stones. Local naturalists offer guided walks.

Ballyvaughan

Where fishing boats shelter at the edge of a lunar world
Ballyvaughan Village ClareCaherconnell Stone Fort BurrenGreen Road Burren Way Black HeadDoolin Cave Clare

1

Ballyvaughan is a pocket-sized village perched on the edge of Galway Bay, at the northern fringe of the Burren. A handful of houses, a quay, two pubs and an end-of-the-world atmosphere. It's your base for exploring the Burren—everything's within 20 minutes. Stop at the little harbour to watch the fishing boats come in with the day's lobster catch.

⏱ 40 min
2

Fifteen minutes south, Caherconnell Stone Fort is one of the Burren's best-preserved—a 1,000-year-old stone ring, inhabited until the 16th century. Excavations unearthed objects dating back to the Bronze Age. The Davoren family, who've farmed the neighbouring land for five generations, offer guided tours of the fort and sheepdog demonstrations—border collies herd sheep to whistle commands in the Burren landscape. Book ahead, it's one of Clare's most popular activities.

⏱ 90 min
3

Head to Doolin Cave, 25 minutes from Ballyvaughan. You'll descend 125 steps to reach, 20 metres underground, the Great Stalactite—7.3 metres long, roughly 10 tonnes, the longest free-hanging stalactite in Europe. Discovered in 1952 by two cavers, it remained hidden for millennia. The guided tour lasts 45 minutes and the temperature stays at 11°C year-round—bring a jumper. The pottery made on-site uses glacial clay containing pollen from prehistoric flowers.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Walk the Green Road, an old track linking Ballyvaughan to Black Head along the Burren coast. This section of the Burren Way winds between dry-stone walls and limestone terraces—you'll overlook Galway Bay with the Aran Islands on the horizon. The path follows the ancient route locals took before the coastal road was built. Allow 2-3 hours one-way—turn back when you like.

⏱ 120 min

If you're into botany, join a guided Burren walk—from May to July, the limestone pavements burst with orchids, gentians and pimpernels. The Burren holds 75% of Ireland's wildflower species on just 1% of its land area. The limestone absorbs daytime heat and releases it at night, creating near-Mediterranean conditions in the heart of the Atlantic.

Cong sits on an isthmus between Lough Corrib and Lough Mask—a village of 900 souls threaded by underground rivers that bubble up here and there between the stones. The Augustinian abbey, founded in the 12th century by High King Turlough O'Connor, stands at the village heart. Ruaidrí Ua Conchobair, the last High King of Ireland, spent his final years here before dying at the end of the 12th century. Wander among the carved arches and cloisters—centuries of erosion have given the stone an almost organic texture.

⏱ 60 min
2

From the abbey, a footbridge crosses the river to the Monk's Fishing House—a small 15th- or 16th-century stone hut built on stilts over the water. The monks fished through a trapdoor in the floor, and a rope linked to the monastery kitchen rang a bell when a fish bit. Continue to the grounds of Ashford Castle—built in 1228 by the Anglo-Norman de Burgos, transformed into a neo-Gothic castle by the Guinness family in the 19th century. The 140-hectare estate is open to the public (except during private events)—the Kinlough woods lead down to the shores of Lough Corrib.

⏱ 75 min
3

Film fans will recognise Cong as the village where John Ford shot 'The Quiet Man' in 1951 with John Wayne and Maureen O'Hara. The Quiet Man Museum, in a cottage that recreates the film's sets, immerses you in 1920s rural Ireland. On the square, the statue of Wayne carrying O'Hara is the village's photo spot. Even if the film means nothing to you, the museum offers a fascinating glimpse of daily life in post-war Ireland.

⏱ 60 min
4

A kilometre and a half west of the village, in a pine forest, the Pigeon Hole caves are reached by a waymarked trail from the abbey. Stone steps (steep and slippery) descend into a karst network where underground rivers link Lough Mask to Lough Corrib. No equipment needed, but proper footwear is essential.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Follow the Cong Forest Nature Trail—a 3km loop through woods bordering the Ashford estate. The path runs alongside an unfinished canal, remnant of a Great Famine project (1845-1848) meant to link the two lakes but never worked because the water seeps through the porous limestone. The ancient trees, ferns and underground streams create an enchanted-forest atmosphere.

⏱ 75 min

Lough Corrib, Ireland's second-largest lake, is renowned for fly fishing—brown trout and salmon. Local operators offer guided boat outings. Even if you don't fish, a walk along the shores at sunset is worthwhile.

Achill Island

Ireland's largest island, where cliffs plunge into Atlantic storms
Deserted Village Slievemore AchillKeem Bay Achill IslandAtlantic Drive AchillCroaghaun Cliffs Achill

1

On the flanks of Slievemore (671m), the Deserted Village stretches in a line of 80 to 100 abandoned stone houses—mortarless walls, a single room per dwelling. The village was abandoned during the Great Famine of 1845, when families migrated to Dooagh by the sea. But young people continued to bring cattle up here each summer ('booley-ing') until the 1940s. Walk among the ruins and the 'lazy beds' (potato cultivation ridges) still visible—it's a silent place that tells the story of harsh rural Irish life better than any museum.

⏱ 75 min
2

Keem Bay, at the end of a vertiginous switchback road, is a horseshoe beach wedged between the cliffs of Benmore and the Croaghaun massif. The sand is golden, the water crystalline (and cold). Basking sharks swim here each summer—you might spot one from the shore. In the 1950s, they were fished here for the oil in their livers, exported to the aerospace industry. Above the beach, archaeological digs revealed a mysterious 40-house village, inhabited until 1838 and completely forgotten. Arrive early in summer—the car park is chaos after 9am.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Atlantic Drive loops around the southern part of Achill on a narrow, spectacular coastal road. You'll hug cliffs battered by waves, pass deserted beaches and isolated cottages with views over Clew Bay. Allow an hour without stops, but plan for double—you'll stop at every bend for photos.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

If you're an experienced hiker, climb to the Croaghaun cliffs—at 688 metres, they're Europe's third-highest sea cliffs, after Hornelen in Norway and Cap Enniberg in the Faroes. The trail starts from Keem Bay and climbs steeply for 2-3 hours. There's no marked path on the summit—it's a traverse across moorland and boulders. The wind can be fierce and the cliff edges are dangerous. Don't attempt this hike in bad weather or alone.

⏱ 180 min

Achill is a good spot for seabird watching—gannets, guillemots and puffins nest on the west coast cliffs between April and July. From Keem Bay or Saddle Head, you can also spot grey seals on the rocks below. Bring binoculars.

⏱ 60 min

Day 8

Mullaghmore

Where surfers chase Europe's biggest waves beneath a fairytale castle
Mullaghmore Harbour SligoClassiebawn Castle MullaghmoreMullaghmore Head WalkBenbulben Mountain Sligo
✈️ Book a flight🚗 Rent a car
1

Mullaghmore is a hamlet clinging to a wild Sligo peninsula, facing Donegal Bay. The small stone harbour, built between 1822 and 1841 by Lord Palmerston (British Prime Minister), is lined by nearly 2km of sandy beach. From the quay, the view sweeps across to Benbulben, that flat-topped mountain formed during the Ice Age that dominates the Sligo landscape.

⏱ 60 min
2

Classiebawn Castle stands on a grassy knoll a hundred metres from the sea—with its conical turret, it looks like something out of Disney. Built from 1860 in Donegal sandstone for Lord Palmerston, it was completed in 1874. The castle was Lord Mountbatten's summer residence until his assassination in 1979, when an IRA bomb blew up his fishing boat in the harbour. The castle is privately owned and closed to the public, but you'll see it close-up from the peninsula road—it's one of Ireland's most photographed castles, especially with Benbulben in the background.

⏱ 45 min
3

Stop at Drumcliff churchyard, 20 minutes south—this is where W.B. Yeats, 1923 Nobel laureate in literature, is buried. His grave bears the epitaph he chose himself: 'Cast a cold eye on life, on death. Horseman, pass by!' The 6th-century church and the High Medieval Celtic cross complete the scene, with Benbulben rising just behind.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The Mullaghmore Head loop is one of the northwest's finest coastal walks—a 4.5km circuit on the cliffs facing the Atlantic. In big swells (October to March), waves can exceed 15 metres here—in March 2012, surfers from around the world rode 15-metre waves. Even without surf, the spectacle of the thrashing ocean from the path is gripping.

⏱ 75 min

If weather permits, explore the flanks of Benbulben—this 526-metre table mountain is said in mythology to shelter the Fianna warriors and the tomb of the lovers Diarmuid and Gráinne. The summit is a wind-battered limestone plateau. Approach trails start from the north side. Warning: the faces are sheer and the terrain can be dangerous in wet conditions—check locally before ascending.

Day 9

Carrick On Shannon

The Shannon's capital, where houseboats drift through emerald floodplains
Carrick-on-Shannon LeitrimCostello Memorial Chapel CarrickShannon River Cruise CarrickLough Key Forest Park Boyle
✈️ Book a flight🚗 Rent a car
1

Carrick-on-Shannon is the capital of Leitrim—a small riverside town of 4,500 perched on the Shannon, the longest river in the British Isles (360km). The lively quay is the departure point for cruises and houseboats heading upriver. In the evenings, the pubs along Bridge Street and Main Street come alive with live music—it's a good place for a traditional session.

⏱ 60 min
2

Take a cruise on the Shannon—boats offer one- to two-hour outings that wind upriver between reeds and floodplain meadows. You'll pass under stone bridges and alongside wooded islets where grey herons nest. It's the best way to understand why this river has shaped all of central Ireland's life for millennia.

⏱ 90 min
3

Don't walk past Costello Chapel without stopping—squeezed between two buildings on Main Street, this tiny 5m by 3.5m chapel is Europe's smallest and the world's second-smallest. Edward Costello had it built in 1879 as a mausoleum for his wife Mary Josephine, who died aged 47. He'd had her body embalmed and kept it with the nuns while waiting for the building to be finished. The two coffins, covered in thick glass plates, are visible beneath the floor. It's Leitrim's most touching love story—the Irish 'Taj Mahal' in miniature.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Operators offer houseboat rentals for half-day or longer trips on the Shannon and its connected lakes. No licence required—they explain the navigation basics before you set off. It's a unique way to see the Irish countryside from the water, at your own pace.

⏱ 120 min

Thirty minutes north, Lough Key Forest Park offers forest trails, suspension bridges in the canopy and a tree canopy walk 9 metres above the ground. The 350-hectare park stretches around the ruins of Rockingham Castle, on the shores of Lough Key. It's also a good pike fishing spot if that tempts you.

⏱ 90 min

Day 10

Dublin

Return via the passage tomb that predates the pyramids
Newgrange Brú na Bóinne MeathTrim Castle MeathSmithfield DublinIveagh Gardens Dublin
✈️ Book a flight🚗 Rent a car
1

On your way back to Dublin, stop at Brú na Bóinne to visit Newgrange—this 5,200-year-old passage tomb is older than the pyramids of Giza and Stonehenge. The 85-metre-diameter cairn is faced with white quartz stones, and its 19-metre interior passage is aligned so that at the winter solstice, a beam of sunlight illuminates the burial chamber for exactly 17 minutes. Visits are guided tours only from the Visitor Centre—book online, slots sell out weeks ahead in summer.

⏱ 120 min
2

Detour through Trim, 15 minutes from Newgrange—Trim Castle is Ireland's largest Anglo-Norman castle, built from 1176 by Hugh de Lacy. Its 20-metre keep dominates the River Boyne and several scenes from 'Braveheart' were filmed here in 1995. The town also preserves medieval walls, a 14th-century Yellow Steeple and the ruins of Newtown Trim abbey—all walkable in an hour.

⏱ 75 min
3

For your last evening, skip Temple Bar and head to Smithfield—this neighbourhood north of the Liffey is the Dubliners' Dublin. The old horse market square (the last sale was held just a few years ago) is surrounded by converted distilleries, independent cafés and galleries. The Lighthouse Cinema screens arthouse films in a former industrial building. It's the kind of neighbourhood where you won't run into other tourists.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Iveagh Gardens, steps from St Stephen's Green, are Dublin's secret garden—most tourists don't even know they exist. Designed in 1865, these formal gardens hide a waterfall, a box maze, a rose garden and lawns where Dubliners come to read at lunchtime. Entry is free and discreet—look for the gate on Clonmel Street.

⏱ 45 min

End your trip with an exploration of Dublin's vintage markets—the Dublin Flea Market and weekend neighbourhood markets offer vinyl, crafts, street food and improbable finds. It's the best way to bring home a souvenir that doesn't come from a tourist shop.

⏱ 60 min
