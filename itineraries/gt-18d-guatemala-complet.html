Guatemala Grand Tour: 18 Days From South to North

📅 18 days📍 18 stops🚗 1784 km⏱ 35h05

Guatemala → Antigua → Panajachel → Rio Dulce → Livingston → Coban → Lanquin → Flores

Eighteen days to cross Guatemala end to end: Antigua and its Baroque convents at the foot of three volcanoes, Lake Atitlán and its Tz'utujil Maya villages, the tropical gorges of the Río Dulce, the Garífuna enclave of Livingston reachable only by boat, the turquoise pools of Semuc Champey hidden in the Alta Verapaz jungle, and the pyramids of Tikal piercing the Petén canopy. Two radically different cultures, five ecosystems, one single circuit.

✨ Highlights
  • Antigua: UNESCO colonial Baroque architecture, three volcanoes including the permanently erupting Fuego
  • Lake Atitlán: volcanic caldera 335 m deep, 12 Tz'utujil Maya villages with living traditions
  • Livingston: Guatemala's only Garífuna town, accessible only by boat from Río Dulce
  • Tikal: 3,000 structures across 16 km², dual UNESCO World Heritage Site (cultural and natural) since 1979
  • Semuc Champey: 300-meter natural limestone bridge where the Río Cahabón disappears underground
  • Bocas del Polochic: 20,760 ha of Ramsar-listed wetlands, Guatemala's main manatee sanctuary
📆 Best period

November, December, January, February, March, April

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 5 and 9 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Dry Season (November-April): Perfect weather, minimal rainfall, ideal temperatures, clear skies, peak tourist season, excellent hiking conditions, advance booking recommended.

Shoulder Season (May & October): Good weather, fewer crowds, moderate temperatures, reasonable prices, occasional showers, excellent value, transitional seasons.

Wet Season (June-September): Heavy afternoon rains, lush landscapes, fewer tourists, lowest prices, muddy roads, challenging travel conditions, morning activities best.

Highland Climate: Year-round spring temperatures, cool evenings, dry season ideal, comfortable conditions, minimal seasonal variation, pleasant climate.

🌍 What to discover in GT?

Maya heritage with colorful markets, volcanic landscapes, and authentic indigenous culture. Experience ancient ruins, traditional textiles, and incredible biodiversity. The country offers cultural authenticity with natural beauty and archaeological treasures. Guatemala provides deep cultural immersion with historical significance and natural wonders.

📋 Important notes

Livingston is only accessible by boat from Río Dulce (1h15 by lancha). The Cobán–Lanquín stretch includes 18 km of unpaved road. The Lanquín–Semuc Champey stretch is done by pickup truck (40 min on dirt track). The Chichicastenango market only runs on Thursdays and Sundays — plan Day 6 accordingly. Stock up on cash before Lanquín, ATMs there are unreliable. Best overall period: November–April.

Guatemala

An overlooked capital where colonial grandeur meets world-class Maya collections

★★★★☆
📍 Palacio Nacional de la Cultura Guatemala City📍 Plaza de la Constitución Guatemala City📍 Catedral Metropolitana Guatemala City📍 Museo Popol Vuh Guatemala City📍 Torre del Reformador Guatemala City
1

Arrival at La Aurora International Airport and your first encounter with the capital. Guatemala City is the largest metropolis in Central America — don't let its reputation put you off. Zona 1, the historic center, is well worth half a day. Everything revolves around the Plaza de la Constitución, where the Palacio Nacional de la Cultura showcases early 20th-century official architecture. Allow about 45 minutes to explore its halls.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Museo Popol Vuh, housed on the campus of Universidad Francisco Marroquín, holds one of the continent's largest collections of Maya art. Inside you'll find pre-Hispanic ceramics, funerary masks, and a reproduction of the Popol Vuh — the Maya creation text discovered in Chichicastenango in the 18th century. Set aside at least 90 minutes to take it all in without rushing.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Dive into the pedestrian 6th Avenue in Zona 1 — this is where Guatemala City shows its true colors: street vendors, open-air markets, locals heading home from work. The Mercado Central, just behind the cathedral, is the perfect spot to try your first local meal (chicken pepián, tamales) and soak up everyday life without a tourist filter.

⏱ 75 min

In the evening, head to Paseo de la Reforma in Zona 10 for a drink on a terrace. This upscale neighborhood is a world apart from the historic center — skyscrapers, contemporary art galleries, restaurants. It's a good place to catch your breath before the three weeks ahead.

Day 2

Antigua

A former royal capital in Baroque ruins, with three volcanoes as its backdrop since 1543

★★★★★
📍 Arco de Santa Catalina Antigua Guatemala📍 Parque Central Antigua Guatemala📍 Catedral de Santiago Antigua Guatemala📍 Iglesia La Merced Antigua Guatemala📍 Convento Las Capuchinas Antigua Guatemala📍 Cerro de la Cruz Antigua Guatemala
1

Antigua was founded in 1543 under the name Santiago de los Caballeros de Guatemala. A UNESCO World Heritage Site, the city sits in the Panchoy Valley, framed by three volcanoes: Agua (3,760 m, dormant), Fuego (3,763 m, active), and Acatenango (3,976 m). Start at the Parque Central: surrounding it you'll find the Catedral de Santiago (only its grand façade survived the 1773 earthquakes), the Palace of the Captains General, and the City Hall.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Arco de Santa Catalina is the city's most iconic landmark: this saffron-yellow arch was built in 1694 to allow cloistered nuns to cross the street unseen. The clock on top is a Lamy & Lacroix timepiece made in France, added in the 1830s — it's still wound by hand every three days. Get there before 8 AM to avoid tour groups and enjoy the Agua volcano as a backdrop.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Iglesia La Merced, recognizable by its bright yellow and white façade in the Spanish Baroque style, is one of the best-preserved churches in Antigua. Its cloister houses a fountain 27 meters in diameter — the largest in Central America. Climb to the roof for a sweeping view over the fountain and the city's rooftops.

⏱ 40 min
4

The Convento de Las Capuchinas, founded in 1736, is one of the best-preserved convent ruins in the city. Its circular galleries of cells arranged around a central tower are unique in colonial American architecture. Sunlight streams through the collapsed vaults — the atmosphere is striking.

⏱ 45 min
5

Head up to Cerro de la Cruz in the late afternoon: this lookout point north of the city offers the best panoramic view of Antigua and the three volcanoes. The walk from the center takes about 20 minutes. Time it for golden hour — the volcanoes glow orange against the red-tiled rooftops below.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Wander the cobblestone streets between ruined convents: Santa Clara (founded in 1700, its collapsed arches frame the sky like perfect oculi) and Santo Domingo (destroyed in the 1773 earthquake, its courtyards have been partially transformed into gardens). Both sites can be explored in about ninety minutes total.

⏱ 90 min

Local operators offer Guatemalan cooking classes and food tours through the streets of Antigua — a great way to learn the basics of local cuisine (pepián, kak'ik, hand-pressed tortillas) alongside locals.

⏱ 120 min

★★★★★
📍 Volcan Pacaya Parque Nacional Guatemala📍 San Francisco de Sales Pacaya Guatemala📍 Hobbitenango Antigua Guatemala📍 Convento Santo Domingo Antigua Guatemala
1

Hobbitenango is an eco-park perched on the hills above Antigua, designed with a nod to Tolkien's universe — round houses, gentle winding paths through pine trees, and wide-open views of the three volcanoes and the valley. It's mostly a good excuse to slow down and take in the scenery without any effort. Allow about an hour on site.

⏱ 60 min
2

On your way back from Pacaya in the late afternoon, make a detour to the Convento Santo Domingo — Antigua's largest convent, built by the Dominicans who arrived in Guatemala in 1538. Destroyed in the 1773 earthquake, its ruins now house a museum and interior gardens. The collapsed nave is one of the most photographed spots in the city: the open ceiling lets light pour in as if it were an open-air cathedral.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

The Pacaya ascent is the most accessible volcano hike in Guatemala. The volcano reaches 2,552 meters and remains one of the country's three active volcanoes along with Fuego and Santiaguito. From the entrance of Parque Nacional Volcán Pacaya (at San Francisco de Sales, one hour from Antigua), the trail loops for 5.6 km with 453 meters of elevation gain — allow 2.5 to 3 hours round trip. At the summit, cracks in the earth release sulfurous heat — guides will offer to roast marshmallows over them. Leave early in the morning (the cone is often shrouded in clouds after 11 AM). A guide is mandatory on site.

⏱ 240 min

Back in Antigua in the evening, a nighttime stroll through the lamplit cobblestone streets reveals a different city: the colorful façades take on coppery tones under the lantern light. A terrace at one of the cafés around the Parque Central is the perfect spot for a cold beer before heading to the lake the next day.

Day 4

Panajachel

Gateway to Lake Atitlán, where volcanic shores meet living Tz'utujil Maya traditions

★★☆☆☆
📍 Indian Nose Rostro Maya San Juan La Laguna📍 Lago Atitlán Panajachel📍 Calle Santander Panajachel📍 Santa Cruz La Laguna Lago Atitlán
1

Lake Atitlán formed roughly 84,000 years ago when a volcanic explosion carved out a caldera several kilometers wide. Today, at 335 meters deep, it is the deepest lake in Central America. Three volcanoes tower over its shores: San Pedro (3,020 m), Tolimán (3,158 m), and Atitlán (3,537 m). Panajachel is the main gateway — the pedestrian Calle Santander, lined with craft shops, leads straight down to the boat dock.

⏱ 45 min
2

Santa Cruz La Laguna is reachable by lancha from Panajachel (15 minutes) and offers one of the lake's most tranquil panoramas — a small village perched on a cliff, accessible only by water or a steep trail. No paved road, no cars: just stone houses, terraces overlooking the lake, and lush vegetation. Plan about ninety minutes on site.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Wake up before dawn for the Indian Nose (Rostro Maya) — this is Lake Atitlán's signature experience. This 2,093-meter summit above San Juan La Laguna offers a 360° view of the lake and its volcanoes at sunrise. Leave at 4 AM to reach the top by golden hour. A guide is strongly recommended — the path crosses private land.

⏱ 240 min

Return to the lake mid-morning, hop on a lancha from the Panajachel dock. A free afternoon by the water is well-deserved after the sunrise hike — kayaks and paddleboards can be rented in most lakeside villages.

⏱ 120 min

Day 5

Panajachel

Gateway to Lake Atitlán, where volcanic shores meet living Tz'utujil Maya traditions

★★☆☆☆
📍 Santiago Atitlán Lago Atitlán Guatemala📍 Iglesia Parroquial Santiago Apóstol Santiago Atitlán📍 San Juan La Laguna Guatemala📍 Cooperativa tejedoras San Juan La Laguna
1

Santiago Atitlán is the capital of the Tz'utujil Maya. Take the lancha from Panajachel (25 minutes). As soon as you step off the dock, children will offer to guide you to Maximón: say yes — it's the best way to find him. Maximón is a syncretic Maya-Catholic deity whose coralwood effigy, roughly one meter tall, is dressed in costumes and 36 neckties. The figure is moved to a different family home every year. Devotees bring alcohol and cigarettes as offerings in exchange for protection.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Iglesia Parroquial Santiago Apóstol, built by the Franciscans in 1541, is one of Guatemala's oldest churches. On the entrance walls, plaques commemorate the Martyrs of Santiago, including Father Stanley Rother, assassinated in 1981 — his heart was buried here at the request of the Tz'utujil community.

⏱ 40 min
3

San Juan La Laguna, a 15-minute lancha ride from Santiago, is the lake's artisan village: its alleyways are covered in murals depicting Tz'utujil cosmology, and several women's cooperatives keep the tradition of backstrap-loom weaving alive using natural dyes. Step into one of the workshops to see a live demonstration.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

In San Juan La Laguna, an artisanal chocolate workshop lets you follow the entire process: from cacao pod to finished bar. Cacao cultivation in this region is ancestral among the Tz'utujil Maya. Allow 30 to 45 minutes for the tour and tasting.

⏱ 45 min

Head back to Panajachel by lancha in the late afternoon. Catch the sunset from the dock or from one of the rooftop terraces along Calle Santander — the volcanoes stand in silhouette against the last orange glow of the sky.

⏱ 45 min

Day 6

Antigua

A former royal capital in Baroque ruins, with three volcanoes as its backdrop since 1543

★★★★★
📍 Mercado de Chichicastenango Guatemala📍 Iglesia Santo Tomás Chichicastenango📍 Pascual Abaj Chichicastenango📍 Cementerio Chichicastenango
1

Chichicastenango sits at 1,965 meters in the mountains of Quiché. Its market, one of the largest in Central America, runs on Thursdays and Sundays — traders from across the region converge at dawn to sell textiles, pottery, ceremonial dance masks, medicinal plants, and copal incense. Arrive at opening (around 6 AM) to watch the stalls being set up before the tour buses roll in.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Iglesia de Santo Tomás, built around 1545 on the platform of a Maya temple, is the most striking example of religious syncretism in Guatemala. Its 18 steps represent the 18 months of the Maya calendar — K'iche' shamans burn copal incense on them throughout the day. Inside, the floor is strewn with flower petals and candles. Photography inside is strictly forbidden.

⏱ 45 min
3

The cemetery of Chichicastenango is one of the country's most unexpected sights: its mausoleums are painted in turquoise (for mothers), white (for fathers), and yellow (for grandparents), creating an explosion of color cascading down the hillside.

⏱ 40 min
4

Pascual Abaj is a Maya shrine on a hilltop about 20 minutes on foot south of town. At the summit, a black stone idol represents Huyup Tak'ah, the Earth god — K'iche' shamans still perform daily ceremonies here with offerings, candles, and incense.

⏱ 50 min

Activities

Delve into the market aisles to hunt for the best finds: huipils (intricately embroidered blouses) and ceremonial dance masks are Chichicastenango's specialties. Vendors on the northern side of the plaza tend to carry more authentic artisanal goods.

⏱ 90 min

Shuttle back to Antigua (about 3 hours via Chimaltenango). Overnight in Antigua to set up an easy departure east the following morning.

Day 8

Rio Dulce

A tropical river guarded by a 1651 fortress, gateway to Guatemala's Caribbean coast

★★★★★
📍 Castillo de San Felipe de Lara Río Dulce📍 Río Dulce Guatemala Fronteras📍 Parque Nacional Río Dulce Guatemala📍 Lago de Izabal Guatemala
1

The Castillo de San Felipe de Lara deserves half a day on its own. Built in 1651 by the Spanish at the mouth of Lake Izabal to block English pirates sailing up the river, it was stormed and occupied in 1686, then rebuilt in 1956. Inside you'll find small vaulted rooms, period cannons, and ramparts with a direct view over the lake. If you can, circle it by lancha — the view from the water is even better than on foot.

⏱ 75 min
2

From the Fronteras dock, board a lancha to cruise through the gorges of the Río Dulce National Park: limestone cliffs rise over 100 meters on each side, cloaked in vegetation so dense it nearly touches the water's surface. Grey herons, pelicans, and kingfishers nest in the rock walls. Manatees are regular visitors to these calm waters.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Kayak or canoe through the mangroves on the outskirts of town. Local operators offer half-day rentals. The atmosphere here is completely different from the main river — stilted huts disappear into the vegetation, and fishing pirogues cross your path.

⏱ 120 min

In the late afternoon, make your way to the Finca El Paraíso hot waterfall, about 20 kilometers along the northern shore of the lake. A 12-meter cascade of hot water (between 30 and 40°C) plunges into a cold river pool — the contrast feels like a natural spa in the middle of the jungle.

⏱ 90 min

Rio Dulce

A tropical river guarded by a 1651 fortress, gateway to Guatemala's Caribbean coast

★★★★★
📍 Refugio Bocas del Polochic El Estor Guatemala📍 Cañón El Boquerón Guatemala📍 Lago de Izabal Guatemala
1

Rise before dawn to reach the Bocas del Polochic Reserve by lancha, at the western end of Lake Izabal. This 20,760-hectare wetland, designated a Ramsar site in 1996, is formed by the Río Polochic delta. It is Guatemala's main manatee sanctuary — 17th-century Spaniards mistook them for mermaids. The reserve is also home to 250 bird species. Leave early: manatees are more likely to surface in the early morning.

⏱ 180 min
2

On the way back toward Río Dulce, stop at Cañón El Boquerón. This narrow limestone canyon is navigated by rowboat. The water is clear, the walls rise about ten meters high, draped in orchids and mosses. If you continue on foot wading through the current, you'll reach a small inland beach.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Sunrise birdwatching on Lake Izabal — keel-billed toucans, cormorants, and egrets share the skies. Bring binoculars.

⏱ 90 min

Evening swim in the river from your lodge's dock. Río Dulce has several stilted eco-lodges built directly over the water — some have no road access at all and can only be reached by boat.

Day 10

Livingston

A boat-access-only Garífuna village where coconut, drums, and the Caribbean collide

★★★★★
📍 Livingston Guatemala Garífuna📍 Playa Blanca Livingston Guatemala📍 Siete Altares Livingston Guatemala📍 Embarcadero Livingston Guatemala
1

Livingston can only be reached by boat — no road connects this town to the rest of Guatemala. The lancha from Río Dulce takes about 1 hour 15 minutes. Arriving by water, facing this village perched on a limestone cliff, is unforgettable. Livingston is the country's main Garífuna settlement. This people, descended from a mix of shipwrecked Africans and indigenous Caribs, were deported by the British to Roatán before gradually migrating to Guatemala in the early 19th century.

⏱ 60 min
2

Follow the coastal trail to the Siete Altares, a series of seven waterfalls and natural pools formed by a creek tumbling down the hillside to the sea. The walk from the center of town takes about 40 minutes. Go in the morning — in the afternoon, the water can rise quickly after upstream rain.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Stroll through the streets of Livingston: Garífuna murals, the scent of coconut, and the rhythms of primero and segunda drums are a stark contrast to the rest of Guatemala. Order a tapado — a rich broth of shrimp, crab, and fish cooked in spicy coconut milk.

⏱ 90 min

Take a lancha to Playa Blanca, a white-sand beach accessible only by boat from Livingston, about twenty minutes away. It's one of the few beaches on Guatemala's Caribbean coast with genuine white sand and translucent water.

⏱ 180 min

Day 11

Coban

A mist-wrapped city founded by Dominicans, where orchids bloom and coffee ripens in the clouds

★★★★☆
📍 Cobán Alta Verapaz Guatemala📍 Vivero Verapaz Cobán orquídeas📍 Finca Santa Margarita Cobán café📍 El Calvario Cobán vista panorámica📍 Museo Príncipe Maya Cobán
1

Take the lancha back to Río Dulce (1h15), then drive to Cobán — allow 4 to 5 hours of travel in total. The shift is dramatic: you leave behind the Caribbean heat and climb into the misty mountains of Alta Verapaz, above 1,300 meters. Founded in 1543 by Dominican monks, Cobán was nicknamed the city among the clouds by the Q'eqchi' people.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Vivero Verapaz, a former coffee estate turned orchid conservatory, shelters some 700 different species including the Monja Blanca — Guatemala's national orchid. Between November and January, the grounds are blanketed in an impressive carpet of multicolored blooms.

⏱ 90 min
3

El Calvario, the neoclassical chapel overlooking Cobán from a hilltop, offers a panoramic view of the city and surrounding mountains. It remains an active place of worship: locals climb to light candles and leave offerings that blend Catholic traditions with Maya rites.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Visit Finca Santa Margarita to understand where Cobán's coffee comes from. The guided tour walks you through the entire process, from picking to roasting. The chipi-chipi — an almost constant drizzle — creates ideal conditions for the slow maturation of the beans.

⏱ 90 min

Try kak'ik at a local restaurant: this spicy turkey stew, served with tamalitos, is a traditional Q'eqchi' dish recognized as intangible cultural heritage.

Coban

A mist-wrapped city founded by Dominicans, where orchids bloom and coffee ripens in the clouds

★★★★☆
📍 Biotopo del Quetzal Alta Verapaz Guatemala📍 Orquigonia jardín orquídeas Guatemala📍 Parque Las Victorias Cobán Guatemala📍 Mercado artesanal Cobán Guatemala
1

The Biotopo del Quetzal, about an hour south of Cobán, is one of the few reserves where you can still spot the quetzal in the wild — this bird with iridescent green plumage and a long red tail was sacred to the Maya. The site features two cloud forest trails (2 km and 4 km). Arrive before 9 AM to maximize your chances.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Museo El Príncipe Maya, tucked away off the central plaza, houses a private collection of ceramics, jewelry, and pre-Columbian artifacts from the Q'eqchi' region. It's a useful primer before the Maya sites of Tikal coming up in a few days.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Cobán's artisan market: Q'eqchi' textiles, silver jewelry, and cardamom-based products are sold directly by producers. Cobán is one of the world's cardamom capitals — you'll catch the scent on every corner.

⏱ 60 min

Wind down in the late afternoon at a café on the central plaza. The light chipi-chipi drizzle that wraps around Cobán in the evening gives the city a soft, melancholy atmosphere all its own.

Lanquin

A Q'eqchi' village at the foot of bat-filled caves, doorstep to Semuc Champey's turquoise pools

★★☆☆☆
📍 Lanquín village Alta Verapaz Guatemala📍 Grutas de Lanquín Guatemala📍 Río Cahabón Lanquín
1

The village of Lanquín is a quiet Q'eqchi' settlement with a lovely colonial church and hillsides blanketed in coffee and cardamom. The last 18 kilometers before Lanquín are on a badly rutted track — hold on tight.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Lanquín caves open right at the edge of the village — you can walk there from the center. The Río Cahabón originates here and plunges underground for several kilometers before resurfacing in the open air. Inside, a network of stalactites and stalagmites fills successive chambers teeming with bats. The marked path runs about 500 to 700 meters.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Arrive in Lanquín late morning from Cobán (2.5 hours of driving). Settle into one of the lodges along the banks of the Cahabón — most offer hammocks and terraces right over the river. Use this evening to sort out your trip to Semuc Champey the next day.

Day 14

Lanquin

A Q'eqchi' village at the foot of bat-filled caves, doorstep to Semuc Champey's turquoise pools

★★☆☆☆
📍 Semuc Champey piscinas naturales Guatemala📍 Mirador Semuc Champey📍 Grutas K'anba Lanquín Guatemala📍 Puente calcáreo Semuc Champey📍 Río Cahabón tubing Guatemala
1

Semuc Champey, 11 km south of Lanquín, is what happens when the Río Cahabón literally vanishes beneath a massive 300-meter-long limestone bridge. Above it, a series of tiered natural pools have formed, their water shifting from turquoise to emerald green with the light. The name means "where the water hides in the stone" in Q'eqchi'. Arrive before 9 AM to enjoy the pools before organized groups show up.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Semuc Champey mirador is reached after a 20- to 30-minute climb through the jungle. From the top, the downward view over the chain of turquoise pools framed by tropical forest is Guatemala's most famous photograph. Wear proper shoes — not flip-flops.

⏱ 90 min
3

The K'anba caves, near Semuc Champey, offer the most hands-on experience of the entire itinerary: you enter in a swimsuit with a candle in hand. The tour involves swimming and wading through water, rope climbing, and natural rock slides. Fair warning if you're claustrophobic.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

River tubing on the Cahabón: sitting in an inner tube, you let the current carry you for anywhere between 30 minutes and 2 hours depending on the water level. It's the ideal late-afternoon activity to wrap up a day of adventure.

⏱ 90 min

Free swim in the natural pools: groups tend to cluster in the first pools — keep going further down to find smaller cascades over the limestone, often nearly deserted.

Day 15

Flores

A painted island on Lake Petén Itzá — the last Maya stronghold to fall, in 1697

★★★★☆
📍 Isla de Flores Petén Guatemala📍 Parque Central Flores Guatemala📍 Malecón Flores Lago Petén Itzá📍 Mirador Rey Canek San Miguel Guatemala
1

The island of Flores, once the capital of the last independent Maya state, held out against the conquistadors until 1697 — a full 150 years after the fall of the rest of the empire. Cross the causeway linking the mainland to the island and find yourself in a labyrinth of cobblestone alleys and colorful façades set on Lake Petén Itzá. Walk the malecón in the late afternoon: the light on the lake at that hour is remarkable.

⏱ 60 min
2

From the island's dock, take a lancha to the village of San Miguel (5 to 10 minutes). The Mirador del Rey Canek offers the finest panoramic view of Flores island and Lake Petén Itzá — you'll understand why the Itzáes chose this island stronghold.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Long day on the road from Lanquín to Flores (7 to 8 hours via Cobán and the CA-13). Leave early in the morning. The drive crosses shifting landscapes — Alta Verapaz forest, the Petén lowlands. Arrival in Flores by evening, dinner of street food from the stalls along the lakeshore.

Flores

A painted island on Lake Petén Itzá — the last Maya stronghold to fall, in 1697

★★★★☆
📍 Parque Nacional Tikal Guatemala📍 Templo I Gran Jaguar Tikal📍 Templo IV Serpiente Bicéfala Tikal📍 Gran Plaza Tikal📍 Mundo Perdido Tikal📍 Yaxhá sitio arqueológico Petén📍 Templo 216 Yaxhá puesta de sol

Tikal is worth the early start — ideally before 6 AM. The city, inhabited from 600 BC until 900 AD, was the seat of one of the most powerful Maya kingdoms in Central America. Its monumental core stretches over 16 km² with nearly 3,000 recorded structures. On the Gran Plaza, Temple I (47 m tall) holds the tomb of King Jasaw Chan K'awiil and faces Temple II (38 m). Arrive early and wait for the howler monkeys to kick off their morning chorus.

⏱ 90 min
2

Temple IV, at 65 meters, is the tallest pre-Columbian structure in the Americas. From its platform, the tops of other pyramids pierce the canopy — this is the very setting that inspired the Yavin moon scene in Star Wars (Episode IV, 1977).

⏱ 60 min
3

In the afternoon, head to Yaxhá, about 30 km southeast of Tikal. With roughly 500 identified structures including 9 temple-pyramids, it is Guatemala's third-largest archaeological site. Unlike Tikal, you can still freely climb all the pyramids.

⏱ 60 min
4

Temple 216 at Yaxhá is the must-visit spot for sunset. From the platform, you can see the jungle stretching endlessly, the two lagoons flanking the ancient city, and on a clear day, the pyramids of Tikal on the horizon. Far fewer crowds than Tikal.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Wildlife spotting in Tikal National Park: howler and spider monkeys in the trees, toucans, macaws, coatis. Local guides are very skilled at picking out the more elusive animals — hire one for the day.

⏱ 60 min

Organized shuttles from Flores combine Tikal and Yaxhá in a single day. Be warned: it's a packed schedule (4 to 5 hours at Tikal in the morning, 1.5-hour drive, 2 to 3 hours at Yaxhá in the afternoon). Entrance fees for both parks are paid on site.

Last long day on the road: Flores to Guatemala City, roughly 8 hours via the CA-13 and then the CA-9. Leave early. If you arrive by mid-afternoon, the Museo Nacional de Arqueología y Etnología in Zona 13 is the fitting finale for an itinerary steeped in the Maya world — stelae, ceramics, jade funerary masks, and scale models of Tikal.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Evening in the Zona Viva (Zona 10): this is the liveliest neighborhood in the capital, packed with restaurants and cafés open late. After three weeks in villages and jungle, it's a welcome transition before departure.
