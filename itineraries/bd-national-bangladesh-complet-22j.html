Day 1

Dhaka

1

Baitul Mukarram, Bangladesh's national mosque: a striking modernist structure in white marble inspired by Mecca's Kaaba, built from 1960 onwards. Its vast prayer hall accommodates up to 40,000 worshippers. Non-Muslims may visit outside prayer times. The religious goods markets encircling the building buzz with activity throughout the day.

⏱ 60 min
2

Sadarghat, South Asia's largest river terminal on the banks of the Buriganga: by late afternoon, dozens of multi-deck launches moor here in organized chaos. Stevedores, street vendors and passengers weave between vessels in a scene untouched by time. The waterfront itself—with its brightly painted wooden boats and rudimentary shipyards—ranks among Bangladesh's most arresting urban spectacles.

⏱ 75 min
3

Ahsan Manzil, the Pink Palace of Dhaka's Nawabs on the Buriganga riverfront: built between 1859 and 1872 in Indo-Saracenic style blending Mughal and European influences. Its 23 galleries trace the opulent lifestyle of Bengali aristocracy under the British Raj—period furniture, portraits and personal artifacts. Closed Thursdays.

⏱ 90 min
4

Lalbagh Fort, an unfinished Mughal fortress from 1678: begun by Prince Muhammad Azam, son of Aurangzeb, and abandoned after the death of Pari Bibi. Spanning 18 acres, the complex comprises Pari Bibi's marble and basalt mausoleum, a three-domed mosque still in active use, and the Diwan-i-Aam. Museum of Mughal artifacts on site.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Arrival at Hazrat Shahjalal International Airport (DAC). Visa-on-arrival available for most European nationalities. Purchase a local Grameenphone SIM card at the Arrivals hall.

⏱ 90 min

Navigate Old Dhaka's alleyways by CNG auto-rickshaw or cycle rickshaw between sites—the street markets, artisan workshops and riverfront ghats are a spectacle in themselves.

⏱ 60 min

Day 2

Dhaka

1

Panam Nagar, the ghost town of Sonargaon's Hindu merchants: a single 600-meter street lined with 52 abandoned mansions built between the 19th and early 20th centuries by wealthy muslin traders. Their Indo-Saracenic façades blend European arches, wrought-iron balustrades imported from Calcutta, mosaic tilework and sculpted stucco. The town was deserted after the riots of 1947 and 1964 when the Hindu community fled to India. Listed among the World Monuments Fund's 100 Most Endangered Sites since 2006.

⏱ 90 min
2

Goaldi Mosque, west of the Sonargaon museum: built under Sultan Hussain Shah in the 16th century, it's one of the best-preserved monuments from the Bengal Sultanate era. Its terracotta brick façade adorned with floral and geometric motifs exemplifies pre-Mughal Bengali style. Still in active use.

⏱ 60 min
3

Bangladesh Folk Arts and Crafts Foundation, housed in the Bara Sardar Bari from 1901—a zamindar mansion in colonial architecture restored by painter Zainul Abedin in 1975. Two museums: the first devoted to folk arts (textiles, pottery, tools, boat models), the second dedicated to jamdani and nakshi kantha, traditional embroideries inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Heritage list.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Day trip from Dhaka to Sonargaon (28 km, ~1h). Take a CNG from Mograpara Bazar off the Gulistan road.

⏱ 60 min

Walk between Panam Nagar, Goaldi Mosque and the museum—all three sites lie within 15 minutes' walk of each other.

⏱ 30 min

Day 3

Barishal

1

The Rocket Steamer docks at Barisal in early morning. These historic paddle-wheel steamers have plied the delta since 1884—the PS Mahsud, after restoration, resumed service in November 2025 on the Dhaka-Barisal route. A journey through living Bengali railway history.

⏱ 30 min
2

Guthia Mosque, a few kilometers from the center, rises from the greenery like a miniature Mughal palace. Its white domes and minarets reflect in a perfectly still rectangular pool.

⏱ 60 min
3

Durgasagar Dighi is the district's largest pond—14 hectares of water ringed by century-old trees. Dug in the 18th century by Raja Shiv Narayan to supply the town, it remains a peaceful promenade.

⏱ 45 min
4

The Kirtonkhola River quays come alive in late afternoon: dozens of boats laden with rice, coconuts and guavas dock or cast off in unceasing ballet.

Activities

In evening, settle on the steps of the Kirtonkhola ghat to watch boats light up one by one. Barisal earns its nickname as the Venice of Bengal.

Barishal

Day 5

Khulna

1

Shat Gombuj Mosque (Sixty Domes) in Bagerhat, masterpiece of the Bengal Sultanate inscribed as UNESCO World Heritage in 1985: built between 1442 and 1459 by general-saint Khan Jahan Ali, governor of the Sundarbans. Despite its name, the building has 77 low brick domes supported by 60 stone pillars—medieval Bangladesh's largest mosque. Its 11 arches on the eastern façade, 2-meter-thick walls and undecorated terracotta brick facing reflect the unique 'Khan Jahan' style in Islamic architectural history. Archaeological museum on site.

⏱ 75 min
2

Khan Jahan Ali Mausoleum facing the Shat Gombuj mosque: tomb of Bagerhat's founder (died 1459), an animated pilgrimage site. The brick mausoleum is surrounded by a pond inhabited by venerated crocodiles—a local tradition going back centuries.

⏱ 45 min
3

Nine Dome Mosque (Noy Gombuj Masjid) in Bagerhat: a well-preserved edifice showcasing the terracotta brick floral decoration of Bengali style. Three rows of three domes on a square plan.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Morning excursion to Bagerhat: the Sixty Dome mosque (Shait Gumbad) is the jewel of Sultan Khan Jahan Ali's 15th-century empire. Its brick vaults rest on 77 stone pillars—the interior acoustics are striking.

⏱ 90 min

Evening cruise on the Rupsha River from Khulna. Fishing boats return laden with giant prawns—the city smells of iodine and spices, and warehouse lights reflect on black water.

⏱ 75 min

Day 6

Khulna

1

The Sundarbans, the world's largest mangrove forest (approximately 10,000 km², 6,000 km² on the Bangladesh side): classified UNESCO World Heritage for its exceptional natural value. The Ganges-Brahmaputra delta fragments here into hundreds of channels between islands covered with sundari mangrove trees. Habitat of the Bengal tiger (114 individuals estimated on the Bangladesh side), Irrawaddy dolphin, saltwater crocodile and 300 bird species.

⏱ 180 min
2

Karamjal Eco-tourism Center in Mongla: the official entry point to Bangladesh's Sundarbans, with a care center for crocodiles and turtles, panoramic observation tower and walking trails along the forest edge. Spotted deer visible in large numbers. Boardwalks on stilts.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Navigate in silent wooden boats from Khulna Jahaj Ghat or Mongla into the Sundarbans' secondary channels: early morning departure, 3-hour cruise through the mangrove. Bengal tigers, monitor lizards and Irrawaddy dolphins can be observed from the deck. Best season November-March.

⏱ 180 min

Birdwatching in the creeks: Brown-winged Kingfisher, White-bellied Sea Eagle, Oriental Pied Hornbill, Masked Finfoot (critically endangered species). Binoculars essential.

Day 7

Sundarbans Launch Overnight

1

Kotka, in the heart of the Sundarbans, is one of the few places to observe Bengal tigers from watchtowers. Forest guides point out fresh tracks in tidal mud—a paw print as wide as a dinner plate.

2

Harbaria Sanctuary shelters particularly dense mangrove. The Sundari mangroves (Heritiera fomes) that give the forest its name reach remarkable heights here—their aerial roots form labyrinths beneath which crocodiles glide.

Day 8

Kushtia

1

The mazar of Fakir Lalon Shah in Chheuria, 2 km from Kushtia station, is the heart of Baul philosophy. This 18th-century mystic rejected all religious boundaries between Hindus and Muslims—his songs still challenge orthodoxy.

⏱ 75 min
2

Shilaidaha Kuthibari, 20 km from town in Kumarkhali, was Rabindranath Tagore's residence from 1890 to 1901. Here, facing the Padma, he wrote the poems translated into English as Gitanjali, which earned him the 1913 Nobel Prize.

⏱ 75 min
3

Hardinge Bridge, 1.8 km long, has spanned the Padma since 1915. This masterpiece of British railway engineering—15 steel spans resting on masonry piers—is listed among Bangladesh's historic bridges.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In evening, Lalon Shah's disciples gather in the akhara (meditation hall) around the mazar and chant baul songs to dotara and khamak. They've sung the same melodies for generations.

⏱ 90 min

Stroll the Padma riverbanks at sunset from Shilaidaha. The Kuthibari terrace overlooks the river Tagore contemplated while composing poems—the view remains almost identical to what he immortalized in his writings.

⏱ 60 min

Kumarkhali village produces artisanal silk (Kushtia silk) on traditional looms. Visit weavers directly to watch sari-making and purchase the finest pieces at their source.

⏱ 60 min

Day 9

Rajshahi

1

Puthia Temple Complex, 23 km east of Rajshahi: Bangladesh's largest assemblage of historic Hindu temples—14 classified structures erected by the Raj family of Puthia between the 17th and 19th centuries. The temples cluster around Lake Shyam Sagar. Terracotta architecture in Pancharatna, Jor-Bangla and Char-Chala styles, with façades covered in panels illustrating the Ramayana, Mahabharata and Radha-Krishna love stories.

⏱ 90 min
2

Bhubaneshwar Shiva Temple in Puthia, Bangladesh's largest Shiva temple: built in 1823 by Rani Bhubonmoyee Devi in five-spire Pancharatna style. The central spire accumulates 108 sub-spires. Inside, a colossal black basalt lingam that the Pakistani army tried and failed to destroy in 1971.

⏱ 60 min
3

Varendra Research Museum in Rajshahi, founded in 1910: one of the subcontinent's oldest museums. Collections of stone sculptures from the Pala kings' reign (9th-12th centuries), Buddhist and Hindu bronzes, Mughal coins and terracotta pieces. Remarkable Pala-period basalt carved heads.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Drive from Kushtia to Rajshahi (~1h30 by car, 97 km). The West Bengal plain unfolds—rice paddies, mango orchards and red brick villages to the banks of the Padma.

⏱ 360 min

Day 10

Bogura

1

Mahasthangarh is Bangladesh's oldest urban archaeological site, dated to at least the 3rd century BCE. The brick ramparts of ancient Pundranagara still rise 6 meters above the river level.

⏱ 90 min
2

Govinda Bhita temple contains remains of two superimposed Buddhist sanctuaries. Franco-Bangladeshi excavations uncovered 18 successive construction levels spanning over 1,500 years of uninterrupted occupation.

⏱ 60 min
3

Mahasthangarh Museum displays a collection of terracotta plaques, silver Maurya coins, Brahmi inscriptions and Pala bronzes. The limestone plaque discovered in 1931, bearing six lines in Prakrit, is Bangladesh's oldest known written historical evidence.

⏱ 60 min
4

Vasu Vihara, 3 km north of the citadel, is a large Buddhist monastery whose excavations reveal stupas, monks' cells and decorative elements from the Gupta-Pala period (4th-12th century).

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walk the fortified perimeter of the citadel. The ramparts measuring 1.5 km long by 1.4 km wide once surrounded an entire town—tall grass and ongoing excavations lend the site an authentic exploration feel.

⏱ 90 min

Walk through Mahasthan village along the Karatoya. Inhabitants literally live above archaeological layers—some gardens still yield Maurya pottery shards on the surface.

⏱ 60 min

In evening, Bogura town offers an animated night market where local confections—notably Bogura doi (thick fermented yogurt) renowned throughout Bangladesh—are served fresh.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 210 km · 4h12
Day 11

Dhaka

1

Star Mosque (Tara Masjid) in Old Dhaka is covered in blue faience mosaics inlaid with stars—Japanese artisans contributed to its decoration in the 19th century. One of Bangladesh's most photographed mosques.

⏱ 45 min
2

Armenian Church of Saint Thomas, built in 1781, testifies to a prosperous Armenian merchant community in Dhaka during the 17th and 18th centuries. Its adjacent cemetery contains epitaphs of traders who came from Isfahan.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Lunch break in Gulshan district after the journey from Bogura. The capital shows a modern face here—cafés, restaurants and shopping centers for a respite before the late afternoon departure to Sylhet.

⏱ 90 min

Bus or overnight train Dhaka-Sylhet (4-5h). Parabat Express and Upaban Express connect Kamalapur to Sylhet several times daily—advance booking strongly advised.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 240 km · 5h
Day 12

Sylhet

1

The mazar of Hazrat Shah Jalal, 14th-century Sufi saint who Islamized the Sylhet region, welcomes thousands of pilgrims daily. Its turquoise domes and faience tiles gleam in morning sun. An adjacent pond is populated by catfish (gazar mach) that the faithful have fed for centuries—legend says they accompanied the saint.

⏱ 75 min
2

Malnicherra Tea Estate, founded in 1854, is Bangladesh's oldest tea estate. Its rows of dome-trimmed bushes extend across thousands of hectares around Sylhet—sari-clad pluckers traverse the rows with baskets on their backs.

⏱ 75 min
3

Osmani Museum honors General M.A.G. Osmani, commander-in-chief of Bangladesh liberation forces in 1971. Its collections of uniforms, battle maps and photographs document the independence war.

⏱ 45 min
4

Ratargul Swamp Forest, 26 km from Sylhet, is one of the world's few freshwater flooded forests. In rainy season, Koroi trunks emerge from transparent emerald-green water.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Navigate in a flat-bottomed boat through Ratargul Swamp Forest. Oars glide between submerged roots, kingfishers dive inches from the bow. It's one of Bangladesh's most singular experiences.

⏱ 90 min

Evening stroll along the Surma riverbanks from Keane Bridge. The river divides Sylhet into two distinct banks—the Shah Jalal mosque bank and the more commercial Zindabazar bank—animated until late at night.

⏱ 60 min

Zindabazar streets offer Sylhet's culinary specialties: coconut pitha (rice cake), Srimangal seven-layer tea if you haven't yet visited the plantations, and fish curry with Khasi herbs.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 55 km · 1h06
Day 13

Sreemangal

1

Srimangal, Bangladesh's tea capital: the town is encircled by over 150 tea gardens spreading across gentle hills with rows of tea bushes trimmed to picking height. Finlay Tea Estate on the road to Lawachara is one of the most accessible. Sari-clad pluckers still hand-harvest two leaves and a bud at a time. Main plucking season runs from March to November.

⏱ 90 min
2

Manipuri village of Srimangal: Bangladesh's Manipuri community is renowned for hand-woven textiles and traditional dances. The Manipuri craft market offers fabrics with distinctive geometric patterns and silver jewelry. A village visit lets you watch women working at domestic looms.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Morning journey Sylhet → Srimangal by bus or CNG (~1h30, 55 km). The road crosses green hills and passes the first tea plantations from Srimangal's outskirts.

⏱ 270 min

Taste the seven-layer tea at Nilkantha Tea Cabin—a local invention by Romesh Ram Gour, each layer using different tea (milk, ginger, cinnamon, lemon, mint...) without mixing.

⏱ 45 min

Bicycle ride on rented bike through secondary roads between plantations in late afternoon.

⏱ 90 min
Day 14

Sreemangal

1

Lawachara National Park, a 1,250-hectare semi-evergreen tropical forest established as a British plantation program in 1922: 400 recorded species of plants, mammals and reptiles. Its most famous residents are western Hoolock gibbons—Bangladesh's only great apes, listed as critically endangered. The park also shelters capped langurs, pig-tailed macaques and mouse deer. Two Khasi villages live inside the forest.

⏱ 120 min
2

Khasi village of Magurchara Punji inside Lawachara: 40 households from one of the world's rare matriarchal tribes. Bangladesh's Khasi people live by cultivating betel leaves and speak an Austro-Asiatic language distinct from Bengali. They are predominantly Christian in a 90% Muslim country.

⏱ 60 min
3

Baikka Beel, a 100-hectare wetland sanctuary on the eastern edge of Hail Haor, 18 km from Srimangal: declared a fish reserve in 2003. In winter (November-February), thousands of migratory birds from Central Asia and Siberia land here—ducks, grebes, herons, kingfishers. Panoramic observation tower on stilts. Boat rides available.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Guided trek through Lawachara in early morning—gibbons are active between 7am and 10am and their calls echo throughout the forest at sunrise.

⏱ 120 min

Boat ride on Baikka Beel in late afternoon—the low light on water lilies and silhouettes of herons in flight is one of Bangladesh's most peaceful images.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 330 km · 6h
Day 15

Chattogram

1

Chandanpura Masjid in Chittagong's old commercial quarter: an 18th-century mosque with façades adorned with polychrome blue and white faience tiles—a decoration exceedingly rare in Bangladeshi religious architecture.

⏱ 60 min
2

Patenga Sea Beach at the mouth of the Karnaphuli, facing the Bay of Bengal. View of cargo ships entering Chittagong port—Bangladesh's second export port. Popular promenade at sunset.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Early train from Srimangal to Chittagong (~6h journey). Recommended departure around 6-7am to arrive early afternoon.

⏱ 360 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 77 km · 2h
Day 16

Cox S Bazar

1

Cox's Bazar beach stretches 120 km uninterrupted—the world's longest unbroken natural beach. The Laboni segment at the town's heart is the epicenter of activity: strollers, coconut vendors and fishermen hauling nets onto sand.

⏱ 90 min
2

Himchari National Park, 12 km south, blends tropical jungle, waterfalls and sea panoramas. From the hilltop—accessible by 200 steps—you watch the beach curve vanish into northern haze.

⏱ 75 min
3

Aggmeda Khyang Monastery, founded centuries ago by the Rakhine community, preserves Buddhist manuscripts, bronze statues and murals depicting the Buddha's life. An enclave of serenity a few hundred meters from the beach tumult.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Wake before dawn for sunrise on the beach. Cox's Bazar is one of the world's rare places where you simultaneously watch the sun rise over the sea and green hills blaze on the horizon.

⏱ 60 min

The Burmese Market offers Rakhine crafts: traditional Longyi weavings, shell jewelry, local spices and dried fish. The vendors, descendants of the Rakhine community settled here for centuries, speak a dialect distinct from Bengali.

⏱ 90 min

In late afternoon, walk or rickshaw along Marine Drive, the coastal road running several dozen kilometers along the beach. Sea to the right, forested hills to the left—the panorama is particularly striking at golden hour.

⏱ 75 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 33 km · 45min
Day 17

Cox S Bazar

1

Inani Beach, 33 km south of the center, is dotted with coral rocks and wave-sculpted limestone formations. Less crowded than Laboni, its waters are more transparent—you can make out the seabed several meters deep.

⏱ 90 min
2

Ramu holds the region's largest concentration of Buddhist temples. The village, inhabited by the Rakhine community for centuries, centers on a huge gilded Buddha statue visible from the national highway.

⏱ 60 min
3

Maheshkhali Island, accessible in 30 minutes by boat from Cox's Bazar, bears on its highest point the Adinath temple dedicated to Shiva—a major Hindu pilgrimage site in Bangladesh. The view over the delta and sea from the summit is unparalleled.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Boat crossing to Maheshkhali (30 min from Cox's Bazar ghat). The island produces betel, salt and dried shrimp—its markets smell of iodine and spices. Fishermen mend nets on the shore while singing.

⏱ 180 min

Snorkeling around Inani Beach's coral formations. Rocky breakers protect a calm zone where parrotfish and starfish frolic. Vendors rent masks and snorkels on the beach.

⏱ 90 min

Sunset on Inani Beach from the flat coral rocks. Low light transforms tide pools into golden mirrors—a panorama photographers come to capture from across South Asia.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 105 km · 2h06
Day 18

Rangamati

1

Kaptai Lake, Bangladesh's largest artificial lake, created in 1960 for a hydroelectric dam on the Karnaphuli: its 777 km² of emerald water stretch between hills covered with dense forests dotted with islands and Chakma stilt villages. By boat, you pass fishermen hauling nets, women washing laundry on shores and buffaloes bathing in coves.

⏱ 120 min
2

Rangamati Hanging Bridge (Jhulonto Bridge), built in 1984 by Bangladesh Parjatan Corporation on Kaptai Lake shores: 335 feet of red metal walkway connecting two hills above the lake. An iconic Rangamati symbol. Warning: the bridge is submerged in full rainy season (August-September) when lake levels rise.

⏱ 60 min
3

Rajban Vihar, Bangladesh's largest Buddhist temple on Kaptai Lake shores in Rangamati: a wooden and concrete complex sheltering a large gilded Buddha statue. Spiritual center of the predominantly Theravada Buddhist Chakma community. Peaceful atmosphere, direct lake view.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Drive Chittagong → Rangamati in morning (77 km, ~2h on winding hill road). The journey itself is spectacular: hairpin turns on wooded ridges with plunging valley views.

⏱ 120 min

Boat cruise chartered at Reserve Bazar ghat to Shuvolong Waterfall (~1h30 one-way): crossing the main lake, passing between banana-covered islands and arriving in a canyon with steep walls where the waterfall tumbles.

⏱ 180 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 180 km · 4h
Day 19

Bandarban

1

Buddha Dhatu Jadi—the Golden Temple—is Bangladesh's largest Theravada Buddhist temple. Perched on a 60-meter hill 4 km from the center, it houses a Buddha relic gifted by Myanmar and the country's second-largest Buddha statue.

⏱ 75 min
2

Nilgiri, 46 km south of town, rises to 1,066 meters—one of Bangladesh's highest points. Clouds roll beneath the ridge in morning. The slopes are home to the Mro, one of the Chittagong Hill Tracts' most ancient tribal peoples.

⏱ 120 min
3

Chimbuk Hill, Bangladesh's third-highest summit at 762 meters, is reached by a winding road carved through tropical forest. Marma villages cling to slopes—their stilt bamboo houses defy the gradient.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Morning trek on ridges around Nilgiri with a local Mro guide. Trails cross giant bamboo forests and terraced rice cultivation—at a bend, you glimpse the Chittagong plain 1,000 meters below.

⏱ 180 min

Descend by Chander Gari jeep on the Chimbuk road, windows open to the valleys. These open vehicles are the traditional hill transport—locals pile onto wooden benches singing.

⏱ 120 min

Cruise the Sangu River at sunset from Bandarban Sadar. The river rises in Burma and crosses spectacular gorges before reaching the plains—Marma fishermen cast their circular nets from frail dugouts.

⏱ 75 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 77 km · 1h40
Day 20

Chattogram

1

Patenga Beach at the mouth of the Karnaphuli offers views of giant container ships entering Chittagong port—Bangladesh's most important, handling 90% of the country's foreign trade.

⏱ 60 min
2

Chittagong's Ethnological Museum is Bangladesh's only one of its kind. Its collections document the lifestyles, costumes, tools and beliefs of the country's 27 tribal communities, including the Chakma, Marma and Mro recently visited in Bandarban.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Night stroll through Sadarghat district along the Karnaphuli. Vessels of all sizes navigate under illuminated bridges—a final maritime image before returning to Dhaka.

⏱ 75 min

Foys Lake, 8 km from the center, is an artificial lake framed by forested hills. In evening, Chittagong residents gather to stroll and dine at stilt restaurants.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 265 km · 5h20
Day 21

Dhaka

1

Jatiya Sangsad Bhaban, Dhaka's National Parliament, masterwork by American architect Louis I. Kahn designed between 1962 and 1982: one of the 20th century's most important buildings according to architectural history. Nine geometric concrete blocks—circles, triangles and hollowed-out trapezoids—articulate around a grand central hall. Striking exterior view from the artificial lake shores.

⏱ 60 min
2

Kawran Bazar market, Dhaka's largest wholesale vegetable and fish market: active from 4am, a striking spectacle of the food logistics for a 22-million-person megacity. Mounds of red chilies, boatloads of Ganges catfish, towers of yellow melons.

⏱ 60 min
3

Liberation War Museum in central Dhaka: opened in 1996, it documents the 1971 genocide perpetrated by the Pakistani army. Photographs, testimonies and personal objects constitute a sober, moving account of nine months of war. Essential for understanding the Bangladeshi nation.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Train or bus from Chittagong to Dhaka in morning (~5-6h). Subarna Express and Turna Nishita connect Chittagong to Dhaka several times daily from Chittagong station.

⏱ 330 min

Final evening in Old Dhaka: dinner at a Nawabpur or Bangshal street restaurant specializing in Bengali cuisine—goat biryani, mustard hilsa or Sylhet shatkora.

⏱ 90 min
Day 22

Dhaka

1

Bangladesh National Museum at Shahbag: opened in 1913, it brings together 83,000 objects on four floors—ancient sculpture and architecture, Islamic art, ethnology and natural history. Pala sculpture gallery and jamdani textile collections are particularly remarkable.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Free day before return flight. Shopping at New Market or Bashundhara City—jamdani fabrics, nakshi kantha (embroidery), bamboo baskets and spices.

⏱ 120 min

Transfer to Hazrat Shahjalal International Airport (DAC). Allow 3h before international departure for formalities.

⏱ 180 min
