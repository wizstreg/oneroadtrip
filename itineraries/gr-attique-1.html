Athenes

★★★★☆
1

The Parthenon is why you came. Built between 447 and 432 BC by architects Ictinus and Callicrates under the direction of sculptor Phidias, it took 22,000 tons of marble quarried from Mount Pentelicus, 11 miles away, to raise it. Notice that the marble contains traces of iron that have oxidized over time—at sunrise or sunset, it turns a warm honey-gold. Arrive as early as possible, right at opening: the crowds become dense after 9:30am.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Erechtheion, built between 421 and 406 BC in the Ionic style, houses the famous Porch of the Caryatids. These six statues of young women serving as columns are copies—the originals are in the Acropolis Museum. Take time to walk around the entire building: the north facade, the most ornate, is the best preserved.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Propylaea forms the monumental entrance to the Acropolis, designed by architect Mnesicles starting in 437 BC, in Pentelic marble. This gateway with five openings was so imposing that ancient travelers described it as rivaling the Parthenon itself. Look up at the ceiling of the central hall—the coffered panels painted in blue and gold are still partially visible.

⏱ 20 min
4

The Temple of Athena Nike, perched to the right of the entrance, was built between 427 and 424 BC to celebrate victory over the Persians. It's the oldest Ionic temple on the Acropolis, and also the smallest: only 26 feet long. It was completely dismantled by the Ottomans in 1686 to build a cannon battery, then reconstructed stone by stone in the 19th century.

⏱ 20 min
5

The Theatre of Dionysus, carved into the southern slope of the Acropolis in the 6th century BC, is considered the birthplace of Western theater. This is where Aeschylus, Sophocles, and Aristophanes premiered their plays before 17,000 spectators. The marble seats you see date from the Roman renovation, but the original wooden stage goes back to the great Dionysia festivals.

⏱ 30 min
6

The Acropolis Museum, opened in 2009 and designed by Swiss architect Bernard Tschumi, houses 4,000 pieces from the sacred hill exclusively. Don't miss the third floor, oriented exactly like the Parthenon: you'll see 525 feet of friezes presented according to their original arrangement. The empty spaces, marked 'BM', remind visitors that two-thirds of the marbles are still in the British Museum. Book your ticket in advance during high season.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Climb up to the Acropolis right at opening (8am in summer). The low morning light gilds the Parthenon's marble and the crowds are still sparse—it's the best window of the day to photograph the site without throngs.

⏱ 30 min

After the Acropolis, descend toward Plaka, the historic quarter that sprawls at the foot of the rock. Its stepped cobblestone lanes, neoclassical houses, and bougainvillea make it one of the most pleasant spots in Athens to wander in the shade. Skip the restaurants directly on the tourist thoroughfares—venture into the side streets instead.

⏱ 75 min

Moussaka is the dish you must try in Athens: silky eggplant, spiced ground meat, and golden béchamel gratinéed in the oven. The best versions are found in Plaka or Monastiraki tavernas, not in terrace restaurants with laminated photo menus.

⏱ 60 min

In the evening, head back up toward the Acropolis rock at sunset from the Hill of the Muses or from Filopappou—free view, no crowds, of the Parthenon turning orange. It's the show of the day.

⏱ 45 min

Athenes

★★★★☆
1

If the Acropolis is Athens' symbol, the Ancient Agora is its brain. It was here, on these 62 acres northwest of the sacred rock, that democracy took shape: the Bouleuterion housed the Council of 500 members, the Tholos the standing committee. Socrates walked these very porticos. Plan 1.5 to 2 hours to tour the entire site with the museum—don't skip the latter, which preserves the first voting ballots in history.

⏱ 60 min
2

Take time to climb up to the Temple of Hephaestus, perched at the northwest corner of the Agora: it's the best-preserved Doric temple in Greece, built between 460 and 415 BC. Its survival is a lucky accident—converted into a Christian church in the 7th century, its walls and roof were preserved. The north and south friezes depict the exploits of Theseus and Heracles. Athenians themselves often call it the 'Theseion' by mistake.

⏱ 30 min
3

Entering the Stoa of Attalos, you're walking into history's first shopping mall. Built around 150 BC by Attalus II, king of Pergamon, as thanks to the city that had welcomed him during his studies, this 380-foot portico on two levels now houses the Agora Museum. Reconstructed identically between 1953 and 1956 by the American School of Archaeology, it preserves the Doric columns on the ground floor and Ionic above. Look for the ostraka—these pottery shards on which Athenians inscribed the name of the citizen to be banished from the city.

⏱ 45 min
4

Right next to the Roman Agora, don't miss Hadrian's Library: built in 132 AD by the Roman emperor, it was far more than a library—a vast 400-by-270-foot complex with reading rooms, gardens, and a central pool. Today only the facade with Corinthian columns is clearly visible from the street, but the site merits a brief stop. Admission is included in the combined ticket for Athens' seven archaeological sites.

⏱ 25 min
5

The National Archaeological Museum, founded in 1829, is Greece's largest museum. If you only have time for one room, go straight to the Mycenaean collection: the gold mask known as 'Agamemnon's,' discovered by Schliemann in 1876 at Mycenae, is one of the most striking pieces of Greek antiquity. Reserve at least 2 hours if you want to cover the Cycladic collection and the bronzes.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Monastiraki, right next to the Agora, is the flea market district, full of shops of every kind and local bustle. On Fridays and Sundays, the flea market spills over into the surrounding alleys—you can find everything from Byzantine icons to old Greek coins.

⏱ 60 min

Push into the Psyrri neighborhood, just north of Monastiraki. It's the haunt of Athenian artists and night owls: walls covered in graffiti, secondhand bookstores, cafes where people play cards until 3am. The neighborhood doesn't really come alive until after 8pm.

⏱ 60 min

Spanakopita—spinach and feta phyllo pastry—is the ideal snack to keep you going between visits. You'll find it in all the market bakeries, still warm in the morning. Pastitsio is the main dish of the day: baked pasta, ground meat, tomato sauce and gratinéed béchamel—a pasta version of moussaka.

⏱ 45 min

In the evening, try ouzo with a few mezze on a Psyrri terrace: cephalopods, olives, taramosalata. Ouzo is drunk diluted with cold water—it turns cloudy white and the anise flavor mellows. It's been the aperitif ritual of Athenians for generations.

⏱ 60 min

Athenes

★★★★☆
1

The Panathenaic Stadium—Kallimarmaro in Greek, meaning 'of beautiful marble'—is the only stadium in the world built entirely of white marble. Founded in 330 BC by statesman Lycurgus for the great Panathenaia, it was completely rebuilt in Pentelic marble under Herodes Atticus in the 2nd century AD (capacity: 70,000 spectators). Fallen into ruin and then pillaged for its stones in the Middle Ages, it was restored to host the first modern Olympic Games in 1896. You can run on the track: it's included in the ticket. Warning: in summer, the marble gets hot—visit in the morning or late afternoon.

⏱ 60 min
2

Lycabettus Hill rises to 908 feet, the highest point in Athens. From the summit, you have a 360° view over the Acropolis, Piraeus, and, on clear days, as far as the island of Aegina. Climb on foot from Kolonaki (30 minutes) or take the funicular from Aristippou Street. In the evening, it's the best spot in the city to watch Athens light up.

⏱ 60 min
3

The National Garden of Athens stretches over 37 acres in the heart of the city, planted by Queen Amalia in the 19th century. It's the green oasis of Athenians—ducks, tortoises, shaded pergolas—a welcome break between visits. At the southern exit stands the Zappeion, a neoclassical palace from 1888 designed by Theophil Hansen, used for official ceremonies and exhibitions.

⏱ 45 min
4

The Academy of Athens, the University, and the National Library form the Neoclassical Trilogy of Panepistimiou Street, all three designed by Theophil Hansen between 1859 and 1902. The Academy is the most impressive: Ionic columns, colored friezes, statues of Apollo and Athena on their pedestals. It's the iconic photo of neoclassical Athens—and often overlooked by tourists rushing off to the Acropolis.

⏱ 25 min
5

If you're experiencing a bit of ancient site fatigue, the Benaki Museum is an excellent antidote. Set in a beautiful neoclassical mansion in Kolonaki, it traces Greek history from prehistory to the present day—Mycenaean, Byzantine, Ottoman, modern—in one fluid journey. It's also one of the best-curated museums in Athens. Go up to the rooftop cafe terrace: the view over the gardens and city is worth the trip.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Kolonaki is Athens' chic neighborhood, at the foot of Lycabettus. Its cafes on Kolonaki Square are perfect for a midday break and people-watching affluent Athenians—it's a local sport. Greek designer boutiques cluster in the streets around the square.

⏱ 60 min

Plan your Lycabettus ascent for late afternoon, timing it to reach the summit an hour before sunset. When the light shifts over the Acropolis and the city begins to glow beneath your feet, it's one of the most powerful moments of an Athens stay. The little cafe at the top is open until nightfall.

⏱ 60 min

Dolmades—vine leaves stuffed with rice, fresh herbs, and lemon juice—are often served as mezze in Kolonaki or Plaka tavernas. Greek salad is always better here than anywhere else: thick tomatoes, cucumbers, Kalamata olives, a block of feta, and dried oregano sprinkled over the top.

⏱ 60 min

If you enjoy Greek wines, look for an Assyrtiko from Santorini in the evening restaurants: crisp white, very mineral, with a cutting acidity that comes from the island's volcanic soils. It's the most internationally known Greek white wine, and one of the best for pairing with seafood.

⏱ 45 min

Athenes

★★★★☆
1

After three days of ancient Greece, take a break at the Byzantine and Christian Museum—you'll discover another Greece, equally rich. Icons, mosaics, frescoes, liturgical embroideries: the collection covers fourteen centuries, from the 4th to the 15th. Some icons in gold and tempera are breathtakingly delicate. Plan about 1.5 hours to enjoy it at your own pace.

⏱ 75 min
2

Don't miss Kerameikos: it's one of Athens' most moving sites, and one of the least crowded. This ancient potters' quarter was also the city's main necropolis, used from the 12th century BC until the Roman era. You'll find sculpted funerary stelae, marble lions, and the Sacred Way that led from here to Eleusis. The on-site museum holds the most beautiful stelae. On weekdays, you'll often have it to yourself.

⏱ 60 min
3

Kapnikarea Church, nestled right in the middle of Ermou Street since the 11th century, is one of Athens' oldest Byzantine churches. Its central brick dome and gilded mosaics inside contrast dramatically with the commercial clamor of the street. Push the door—it's often open in the morning.

⏱ 20 min
4

Syntagma Square is the heart of modern Athens, dominated by the Parliament housed in the former Royal Palace of 1843. In front, the Tomb of the Unknown Soldier is permanently guarded by the Evzones, the presidential guards in traditional costume—white pleated fustanella with 400 pleats, white stockings, and shoes with black pompoms. The changing of the guard happens every hour, and on Sundays at 11am it's done in full dress uniform with music.

⏱ 30 min
5

The Museum of Cycladic Art houses one of the world's most important collections of Cycladic art, dating from 3200 to 2000 BC. These white marble figurines with streamlined forms—barely sketched silhouettes, smooth faces—fascinated Picasso and Modigliani in the 20th century. The effect is striking: you'd swear you were looking at contemporary sculptures.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

For breakfast or a midday break, look for a cafe on Syntagma Square or the adjacent alleys for a bougatsa—warm phyllo pastry filled with custard cream or fresh cheese, dusted with cinnamon and powdered sugar. It's the traditional breakfast of Thessaloniki residents that became popular throughout Greece.

⏱ 30 min

Ermou Street, pedestrianized between Syntagma and Monastiraki, is Athens' main shopping artery. This is where you'll encounter locals doing their shopping—not just tourists. Kapnikarea Church cuts it in two, a symbol of two millennia layered on top of each other.

⏱ 45 min

If you have the energy for an evening out, Exarcheia is Athens' most alternative neighborhood—bookstores, politicized bars, walls entirely covered in political graffiti, cafes open until dawn. It's a very different Athens from Kolonaki or Plaka, and far more authentic.

⏱ 60 min

For dinner, try keftedes—meatballs flavored with mint and cumin, fried and served as mezze. Mavrodaphne from Patras, a sweet red wine from Greece, is the ideal companion to finish the meal with honey-based desserts.

⏱ 60 min

Cape Sounion

★★★★☆
1

The Temple of Poseidon raises its 15 surviving Doric columns on a promontory 200 feet above the sea, at the southern tip of Attica. Built between 444 and 440 BC—at the same time as the Parthenon, as part of Pericles' grand program—it served both as a sanctuary to the god of the seas and as a landmark for sailors returning to Piraeus from the Mediterranean. On clear days, you'll make out the islands of Kea, Kythnos, and in good weather, as far as Serifos in the distance. Time your arrival for sunset: the orange light on the white marble is spectacular.

⏱ 90 min
2

On one of the temple columns, look for Lord Byron's carved name, who visited the site in 1810. The English poet was so captivated by Sounion that he recorded his reaction in verses from Don Juan (1819)—an ode to this marble promontory battered by waves that he described as the ideal place to listen to the sea in silence. This kind of graffiti would be a crime today—at the time, Byron was simply an enthusiastic Romantic tourist.

⏱ 20 min
3

Take time to explore the site beyond the main temple: the cape also encompasses the remains of ancient fortifications and, on the adjacent hill, the foundations of a Temple of Athena Sounion destroyed in the 1st century AD. Less documented, this second sanctuary confirms that Sounion was a major cult site—not just a navigation point for sailors.

⏱ 30 min
4

As you walk the promontory, keep in mind that you're standing on one of Greece's most mythologically charged places: according to legend, this is where King Aegeus threw himself into the sea, believing his son Theseus dead after the expedition against the Minotaur. Theseus had forgotten to change his black sails to white ones to signal his victory. The sea has borne the king's name ever since. Homer himself mentions Sounion in the Odyssey as 'the sacred promontory of Athens.'

⏱ 15 min

Activities

The coastal road from Athens hugs the Athenian Riviera for 44 miles—a succession of beaches, coves, and seaside resorts. Plan to leave in the morning to enjoy the water before the summer crowds arrive. Vouliagmeni and Varkiza beaches are the most popular with Athenians.

⏱ 60 min

At Vouliagmeni, make a stop at the thermal lake of the same name: a turquoise body of water fed by underground hot springs, set in a partially open cave. The water hovers around 72-84°F year-round. There's an entrance fee but the swim is worth it, especially if you've spent several days at archaeological sites.

⏱ 90 min

Arrive at Cape Sounion about an hour before sunset. The site stays open until sunset—check the exact time depending on the season. Warning: there's no shade at the site, bring a hat and water. The terrain is rocky, avoid light sandals.

⏱ 60 min

After sunset, the tavernas along the Sounion coast serve ultra-fresh locally caught fish: grilled octopus with oregano and lemon, Mediterranean fish soup, calamari and scampi. An ouzo or a Greek Alfa beer while watching the last light fade over the Aegean Sea: hard to beat as an end to the day.

⏱ 90 min
