Istanbul

★★★★★
1

Hagia Sophia plays both sides at once: active mosque on the ground floor, museum of Byzantine mosaics in the upper gallery. Former Christian cathedral from 537 AD, converted to a mosque in 1453, reopened for worship in 2020. The upper gallery visit costs €32.50 and requires advance booking—queues stretch long from 10 AM onward.

⏱ 90 min
2

Built in 532 under Justinian I, the Basilica Cistern lines up 336 columns across 138 meters beneath the city. This underground reservoir has held its silence for fifteen centuries, indifferent to Istanbul's noise overhead. No dress code, unlike the mosques—perfect between visits.

⏱ 60 min
3

The scent of incense and gold floods Topkapi from the second courtyard onward. Residence of the Ottoman sultans from 1465 to 1853, the palace covers an entire peninsula between the Golden Horn and Bosphorus. Closed Tuesdays—and this is where unlicensed guides swarm the thickest: only follow official badges.

⏱ 120 min
4

Crowded, touristy, and yet the Blue Mosque delivers on its promise: 20,000 Iznik tiles line the interior in deep blue. Its six minarets once scandalized Mecca, which itself had only six. Free entry outside prayer times, headscarf required.

⏱ 45 min
5

Take tram T1 to Sultanahmet and walk to the Hippodrome, the ancient Byzantine chariot-racing track. Three monuments survive on the square: the Obelisk of Theodosius (erected in Egypt c. 1450 BC, re-erected here by Theodosius in 390 AD), the Serpent Column, and the Constantine Column. Free access.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

If you've got your sea legs, a 90-minute Bosphorus cruise frames the entrance to the Black Sea: Dolmabahçe Palace, Anadolu and Rumeli fortresses, Ortaköy Mosque beneath its bridge. Frequent departures from Eminönü, reasonable fare.

⏱ 90 min

Istanbulites call it simply 'the Hippodrome'—but it's Sultanahmet Square that serves as headquarters for watching local life between the monuments. Weekday mornings before 9 AM, the square is nearly deserted and the light on Hagia Sophia is exceptional.

⏱ 40 min

End the evening in Karaköy for the strait's best fish: the boat sandwich (balık ekmek) opposite Galata Bridge is an Istanbul institution for under €3. The neighborhood comes alive from 7 PM onward.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 235 km · 2h30

Edirne

★★★★☆
1

Sinan outdid himself in Edirne, and he knew it. The Selimiye Mosque, built between 1568 and 1574, boasts a dome 31 meters in diameter—slightly wider than Hagia Sophia's—and four 71-meter minarets. UNESCO-listed in 2011, set within a külliye complex with a museum of medicine. Free entry.

⏱ 60 min
2

Built between 1402 and 1414, the Eski Cami ('old mosque') is Edirne's oldest surviving religious building, with grand Arabic calligraphy on the pillars. Contemplative atmosphere and rarely crowded. Open freely outside prayer times.

⏱ 30 min
3

Light slants obliquely into the Bayezid II Külliye Museum, housed in the former Bayezid II mosque complex—Quranic school and medieval hospital converted. Dedicated to Ottoman medical history, it displays surgical instruments and reconstructed treatment rooms. Modest fee, few visitors.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Wander the Ottoman arasta below the Selimiye: this 17th-century market gallery sells jewelry, textiles, and local olive oil. It's also the best angle to photograph the minarets in alignment.

⏱ 30 min

As locals say: 'Edirne has to be earned.' A provincial Turkish town... that was once the capital of an empire: Edirne preceded Istanbul as the Ottoman seat from 1365 to 1453. Almost no international tourists know this, which explains the preserved atmosphere. In June, wrestlers compete at the Kırkpınar Oil Wrestling Festival, ongoing since the 14th century.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 20 km · 25min

Svilengrad

1

The Mustafa Pasha Bridge spans the Maritsa River for 295 meters with 20 stone arches—built in 1529 on the orders of Vizier Çoban Mustafa Pasha, close confidant of Suleiman the Magnificent. Wikipedia and Structurae attribute the design to Mimar Sinan, though the question remains debated among historians. Declared a national monument in 2003, restored in 2016.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Take time to walk the bridge's 295-meter pedestrian zone: benches allow you to pause above the current. Local legend claims the mortar was reinforced with eggs collected from all across Thrace.

⏱ 25 min

The village of Mezek, 7 km away, holds one of the finest Mycenaean tombs outside Greece and the ruins of the medieval Neutzikon fortress. A 30-minute detour from the bridge if you have time.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 90 km · 1h30

Kardzhali

★★☆☆☆
1

The turquoise waters of Kardzhali Lake shimmer between the hills of the Eastern Rhodopes. First of three dams on the Arda River, the Kardzhali reservoir offers a striking panorama from the heights of Moniak fortress. Barge-mounted restaurants serve fish caught from the lake.

⏱ 45 min
2

Three millennia of Thracian, Greek, Roman, and Ottoman civilizations layer atop one another in the collections of the Kardzhali Regional History Museum—an hour well spent. The town remains mixed Bulgarian-Turkish, both languages heard in the central markets.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Try the local Turkish börek in one of the covered market cafés—here it's called bürek, filled with white cheese or minced meat. Bulgarian coffee is ordered simply as 'кафе' (kafe).

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 18 km · 25min

Beli Plast

1

Giant mushrooms carved in pink zeolite point skyward in a lunar landscape: the Stone Mushrooms of Beli Plast, natural formations unique to Bulgaria, reach several meters in height. The site has free access with a small car park. Expect a 10-minute walk from the road.

⏱ 40 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 18 km · 25min

Kardzhali

★★☆☆☆
1

Legend says Alexander the Great came here to consult the oracle before his Persian campaign—and left convinced he would conquer the world. Perperikon, the largest megalithic city in the Balkans, crowns a 470-meter Rhodope rock 15 km from Kardzhali. Excavations ongoing since 2000 by archaeologist Nikolay Ovcharov.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Wear closed shoes: the climb up the rock is steep and stones are slippery. Bring water—there's no shop at the top. Guided tours are available in English from Kardzhali and provide essential context on the Dionysus sanctuary.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 50 km · 55min

Haskovo

1

Inaugurated September 8, 2003, the Virgin Mary and Child statue in Haskovo is recognized as the world's tallest in this representation: 14 meters of concrete-polymer on a 17-meter pedestal, totaling 32.8 meters. Guinness World Records certified it in 2005 in the Religion category. A chapel is built into the base.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

From Yamacha Hill, the view sweeps across the entire city and the Rhodope hills in the distance. A bell tower built in 2010 offers an additional panorama from its summit. Free access, pleasant park for a break.

⏱ 25 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 80 km · 1h10

Asenovgrad

1

Asen's Fortress catches the eye before you even reach town: perched on a Rhodope cliff, 2 km south of Asenovgrad. Built in the 13th century under Ivan Asen II, it controlled the routes to Greece and has been held by Thracians, Romans, Byzantines, Bulgarians, and Ottomans. Entry around 5 BGN.

⏱ 60 min
2

In ruins, yet the Church of Saint Mary of Petrich still stands at the fortress's heart. Two-story, with restored medieval frescoes—including an Assumption on the west wall. The only preserved building on site, still active as a place of worship.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Follow the marked trail from the parking lot to the ramparts: about 2 km through woods with views of the Asenitsa River and the Rhodopes. Moderate climb. A path links the fortress to Bachkovo Monastery, 10 km by road.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 12 km · 20min

Bachkovo

1

Founded in 1083 by two Georgian brothers in Byzantine service—the nobles Gregory and Abasius Bakuriani—Bachkovo has survived the Ottomans, the centuries, and earthquakes. It's Bulgaria's second-largest monastery after Rila. Most of the complex dates to the 1601 reconstruction. Open daily.

⏱ 60 min
2

Zahari Zograf's frescoes cover the Church of Saint Nicholas and the Archangels' narthex—19th-century works in striking colors, among the painter's most celebrated. The Dormition Church (1604), meanwhile, is adorned with frescoes attributed to Moschos of Edirne. The icon of the Virgin, traditionally attributed to Saint Luke, is kept in the Dormition Church.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Ask the custodian to open the 11th-century ossuary, 50 meters from the monastery entrance: its medieval frescoes are among the oldest preserved on site. Only staff can unlock the doors—be courteous and patient.

⏱ 20 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 30 km · 35min

Plovdiv

★★★★☆
1

Philippopolis, Trimontium, Plovdiv: this city has changed names more often than ruling civilizations. Built on seven hills (six remain), it's one of Europe's oldest continuously inhabited cities. The old town layers 19th-century Bulgarian Revival houses, Roman remains, and an Ottoman mosque—all within a twenty-minute walk.

⏱ 60 min
2

Plovdiv's Roman theatre is still alive, not fossilized: built in the 1st or 2nd century AD, it still hosts concerts and open-air operas in summer. Check the program before visiting—if an evening show is scheduled, daytime access to the tiers may be restricted.

⏱ 45 min
3

If you only have time to visit one Old Plovdiv house, choose the Hindliyan House: decorated for a 19th-century merchant, it alternates carved-ceiling salons with fountain courtyards. The neighboring Balabanova House hosts concerts.

⏱ 45 min
4

Get lost in the Kapana quarter among the street-art murals and artisan workshops. This artists' district occupies the old craftsmen's quarter of the center. This is where Plovdiv earned its title as European Capital of Culture 2019.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

The aroma of roasted coffee drifts down Knyaz Alexander Street, Plovdiv's main pedestrian thoroughfare. This is where the 2nd-century Roman stadium is literally buried beneath the paving stones—floor windows reveal the tiers. Entry included in a combined ticket with the theatre.

⏱ 30 min

Join one of the free English-language tours offered each morning in front of the national theatre: two hours with a passionate local guide to understand why Plovdiv is complex and proud. Contribution appreciated.

⏱ 120 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 45 km · 50min

Hisarya

1

Diocletianopolis: the Romans were already coming here for treatment in the 2nd century AD, then the army sent its wounded. The ancient town preserves imposing 4th-century Roman walls and the Camel Gate, an arch that guarded the entrance to the spa city. Hisarya is a member of the European Historic Thermal Towns Association (EHTTA).

⏱ 40 min
2

The 4th-century Roman walls cut the town into two still-visible sections. At the center, a Roman bath complex with floor mosaics was uncovered during recent excavations. The Virgin of the Baths Park (Momina Banya) runs alongside the ruins—pleasant for a break.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Fill your bottle at one of the town's 22 free mineral-water fountains—each has a different chemical composition. Locals come each morning to stock up on several liters.

⏱ 20 min

An hour in the thermal baths here beats three days at a city spa: Hisarya's hotels offer balneotherapy treatments by the day, no overnight stay required. Open-air pools with mineral water.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 25 km · 30min

Starosel

1

Hard to believe a Thracian tomb of this scale remained unknown until 2000: the Chetinyova mound conceals a circular cupola 4.80 meters across, adorned with columns and colored decorations—the largest Thracian royal complex ever discovered in Southeast Europe. Dated to the late 5th century BC. Modest entry fee.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Climb to the temple to see the wine vats carved into the rock behind the mound: they served ceremonies honoring the Great Mother Goddess. Hiking shoes recommended for the climb.

⏱ 30 min

The small restored church adjacent to the Horizon Tomb deserves a stop: its flowered garden is maintained with unusual care for such a little-visited site. Take time to chat with the custodian—his English is limited but he understands enthusiasm.

⏱ 15 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 2 km · 5min

Starosel

1

Less known than its Chetinyova neighbor, the Horizon Tomb is no less striking: a flower-covered tumulus surrounded by a tended garden, with a small restored church adjacent. Free entry. An elderly man has looked after the site alone for years—he speaks of it with quiet pride.

⏱ 20 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 60 km · 1h15

Koprivshtitsa

1

Koprivshtitsa is a museum town that doesn't look like one: residents still live in their colored corbelled houses, the cobbled lanes serve daily life. Nestled in the Sredna Gora mountains at around 1,000 meters, it preserves over 200 19th-century buildings classified as architectural monuments.

⏱ 30 min
2

This is where the first shot of the April Uprising was fired on April 20, 1876, a Bulgarian revolt against Ottoman rule. The event is commemorated in six house-museums scattered across the historic center, each linked to a different revolutionary.

⏱ 75 min
3

Geraniums overflow from every courtyard, cobbled lanes connect small arched bridges over the Topolnitsa River. The whole ensemble resembles an Alsatian village reimagined through baroque Orientalism: facades in blue, red, and ochre, roofs of brown tile.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Buy the combined ticket for the six house-museums at the Koprivshtitsa Museums Directorate: it's significantly cheaper than separate tickets. Explanations are in Bulgarian; ask for an English map at reception.

⏱ 30 min

If visiting in summer, check the dates for the Koprivshtitsa National Folklore Festival: held every few years since 1965, it gathers musicians and artisans from across the country. The next date will be announced on official sites.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 90 km · 1h35

Samokov

★★☆☆☆
1

Samokov seems ordinary. It's not: set at 950 meters altitude at the foot of the Rila Mountains, this town is the birthplace of the Samokov School of painting, which supplied the frescoes for Rila, Bachkovo, and Troyan. An Ottoman fountain from 1660 stands on the central square.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Belyova Church was buried in the 17th century to escape the Ottomans—a century underground preserved it. Originally built around 1389 on the foundations of a 4th-5th century early Christian basilica, it hides brightly colored frescoes painted by masters of the Samokov School in 1869.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Make a detour to the Samokov History Museum to see the icons and tools of the painting school: works by Zahari Zograf and his father Hristo Dimitrov. The museum is housed in a typical Bulgarian Revival building.

⏱ 40 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 12 km · 20min

Borovets

★★★☆☆
1

Ferdinand I chose the Rila pines to build his hunting retreat between 1898 and 1914: Tsarska Bistritsa is Bulgaria's only mountain royal pavilion, with a Siemens power plant from 1912 that still runs in its original form. Open Thursday–Sunday, 9 AM–5 PM.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Stroll through the century-old park around the palace: Ferdinand I brought back rare tree species from his travels around the world—some century-old trees are labeled. 1 km from the resort center, 15-minute walk.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 50 km · 1h05

Sapareva Banya

1

Water shoots up at 103°C right in the town center, a stone's throw from café terraces—the Sapareva Banya geyser is the only one on the Balkan Peninsula and the hottest in continental Europe. Discovered during hydrological drilling in 1957, it surges from 73 meters underground and reaches 18 meters in height. On winter nights, the steam forms ice sculptures.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Enjoy the open-air mineral pools, open year-round in Sapareva Banya's spa hotels. The town is considered Bulgaria's first organized cure center, with traces of thermal activity dating to the late 19th century. Day spa access without overnight stay, by reservation at most establishments.

⏱ 90 min

If the season allows, Resilovo Monastery can be visited in 30 minutes from town—an active women's monastery founded in the 1930s, 7 km southeast. Road passable by car.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 25 km · 45min

Panichishte

1

Seven glacial lakes in an amphitheater between 2,100 and 2,500 meters: each basin is linked to the next by a stream, forming a natural cascade descending the flank of the Rila Mountains. The Seven Lakes cirque is Bulgaria's most visited natural site. Accessible in summer by chairlift from Panichishte.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

The chairlift from Panichishte drops you in a glacial cirque between 2,100 and 2,500 meters altitude. Seven glacial lakes are stacked in a cascade: each has a Bulgarian nickname—the Tear, the Eye, the Kidney, the Twins, the Clover, the Fish, the Lower Lake. The chairlift operates in summer only.

⏱ 30 min

Climb to Salzata Lake, the highest, to see all seven lakes in one sweep from the 2,500-meter viewpoint. The hike from the chairlift's upper station takes about two hours round-trip on a well-marked trail.

⏱ 120 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 40 km · 1h10

Rila Manastir

1

Rila Monastery is Bulgaria's most impressive building, locals say. Founded in the 10th century by Saint Ivan of Rila, destroyed, rebuilt between 1834 and 1862 in its current form. UNESCO since 1983. The central church is covered in polychrome frescoes on all interior and exterior surfaces—including those by Zahari Zograf.

⏱ 90 min
2

Hrelyu's Tower, the only medieval remnant rising above the roofs, was built in 1334–1335 by Protosebastos Hrelyu. It serves as both keep and bell tower in the inner courtyard. Photography forbidden inside the church and monastery museum.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Arrive before 9 AM: organized tour groups land at 10 AM and the inner courtyard fills within minutes. Early mornings, only monks and individual pilgrims occupy the arcades—the atmosphere is entirely different.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 120 km · 2h10

Dragalevtsi

1

Dragalevtsi Monastery dates to 1345 and hides among the pines at the entrance to Vitosha Park. Active, not touristy, with medieval frescoes in the main chapel. It's the natural starting point for the hike to Aleko.

⏱ 25 min

Activities

Vitosha's stone rivers crack underfoot: these formations of granite blocks, hauled by ancient glaciers, cover the mountain's flanks. Vitosha Nature Park, the Balkans' first (created in 1934), extends to 2,290 meters at Cherni Vrah.

⏱ 120 min

Take the chairlift from Dragalevtsi to Aleko to cut the two-hour climb and reach the plateau at 1,820 meters directly. Panoramic view of Sofia and the Balkan mountains in clear weather. In winter, ski slopes from Aleko.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 10 km · 25min

Boyana

1

Small, almost plain on the outside, Boyana Church conceals inside some of Europe's earliest pre-Renaissance frescoes. Dated to 1259, they represent portraits of stunning modernity for the era: Sebastocrator Kaloyan and his wife painted from life. UNESCO since 1979.

⏱ 15 min

Activities

Book your time slot online: only 9 people are allowed inside at once, for a maximum of 10 minutes. The official site doesn't yet offer a complete online system—arrive early or come on weekdays to avoid groups.

⏱ 20 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 8 km · 20min

Sofia

★★★★☆
1

Alexander Nevsky Cathedral is more complex than it appears: it's not a Russian gift, it's a Bulgarian memorial for Russian soldiers who died in the 1877–1878 war of liberation. Designed by Russian architect Aleksandr Pomerantsev, inaugurated in 1924. Floor area of 3,170 m², gilded central dome at 45 meters.

⏱ 40 min
2

Built from the late 3rd century onward, the Saint George Rotunda is Sofia's oldest standing building. It houses medieval frescoes in three superimposed layers—Byzantine, Ottoman, modern Bulgarian. It hides in a luxury hotel courtyard, hemmed in by communist-era buildings.

⏱ 30 min
3

Finish with the Museum of Socialist Art for an unexpected note: life-size sculptures of Lenin and Georgi Dimitrov in a quiet garden on the periphery of the center. A blend of nostalgia and unapologetic irony. Modest entry fee.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

If you're passing on a Saturday or Sunday morning, the antiques market in front of Alexander Nevsky Cathedral starts at 8 AM: polished wood, Soviet books, recent icons, and communist-era antiques all mixed—hard to tell them apart. It's Sofia's most photographed spot. Bargain.

⏱ 60 min

Sofians say it: 'Sofia has to be earned.' Two hours of a free English-language tour with a local guide reveals what paper guides don't show—the unapologetic communist architecture, the 19th-century Sephardic synagogue, the Ottoman Banya Bashi mosque. Departure from the Serdika bridge.

⏱ 120 min
