Deep South Road Trip: New Orleans and the Natchez Trace

📅 8 days📍 7 stops🚗 799 km⏱ 9h35

New Orleans → Baton Rouge → Natchez → Vicksburg → Jackson

New Orleans → Baton Rouge → Natchez → Vicksburg → Jackson

This 7-day loop from New Orleans takes you into the heart of the Deep South. You'll travel up River Road past sugar cane plantations, discover Natchez's antebellum mansions perched above the Mississippi, cross the Vicksburg battlefield, and return via Jackson and its civil rights museums. Between live jazz, Cajun cuisine, and the complex history of the South, it's a journey into the depths of America.

✨ Highlights
  • The Baton Rouge Capitol, 450 feet of Art Deco grandeur, where you can still see bullet holes from Senator Long's 1935 assassination
  • Longwood in Natchez, an octagonal mansion whose construction stopped dead when the Civil War began - the tools are still in the attic
  • The USS Cairo at Vicksburg, an 1862 ironclad raised from the river bottom after 102 years underwater
  • Rosedown Plantation's gardens in St. Francisville, 28 acres maintained according to a gardening journal kept from 1836 to 1895
📆 Best period

March, April, May, October, November

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 8 and 12 days.

Day 1

New Orleans

Where jazz was born beneath cast-iron balconies and Creole kitchens

★★★★★
1

Start at Jackson Square, the beating heart of the French Quarter. St. Louis Cathedral, consecrated in 1794, is the oldest continuously active Catholic cathedral in the United States - its three white steeples dominate the square. On either side, the Cabildo (where the Louisiana Purchase was signed in 1803) and the Presbytère form a rare colonial ensemble. Street musicians and artists set up around the square from early morning.

⏱ 60 min
2

Wander through the French Quarter streets along Royal Street - this is where the art galleries, antique shops, and most beautiful cast-iron balconies in the city are concentrated. Each balcony tells its own story: the cast-iron molded patterns date from the American era (after 1803), while the hand-wrought ironwork is of Spanish or French origin.

⏱ 45 min
3

Late in the day, head down to the Moonwalk, the riverfront promenade behind Jackson Square. You'll be just feet from the Mississippi - the tugboats and barges passing by give you a sense of the commercial activity that has kept this waterway alive for three centuries. It's also one of the best spots to watch the sun set over the river.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Stop to taste the beignets and chicory coffee with milk - it's been a New Orleans ritual since 1862. The beignets arrive piping hot, covered in powdered sugar that flies everywhere. Grab a table on the patio and watch the world go by.

⏱ 30 min

In the evening, head to Frenchmen Street for live jazz. This is where locals come to hear music, far from the tourist chaos of Bourbon Street. The clubs are small, admission often free or just a few dollars. If you prefer the traditional format, try Preservation Hall - the 45-minute sets in this no-frills venue (no bar, no air conditioning) are pure concentrated jazz. Arrive 30 minutes before opening as lines form quickly.

⏱ 90 min

Day 2

Baton Rouge

Where America's tallest state capitol has towered over the Mississippi since 1932

★★★★☆
1

On the way to Baton Rouge, stop along River Road to visit a plantation. Oak Alley in Vacherie is famous for its alley of 28 bicentennial oak trees leading to a Greek Revival mansion from 1839. But don't limit yourself to the beauty of the place: the exhibition in the reconstructed slave quarters tells the stories of the 110 to 120 enslaved people who lived here. Allow 2 hours on site.

⏱ 120 min
2

In Baton Rouge, head up to the Louisiana State Capitol. At 450 feet and 34 stories, it's the tallest state capitol in the United States - inaugurated in 1932, one year after the Empire State Building. The entrance steps bear the names of all 49 states carved into the granite. Inside, the Memorial Hall and its Art Deco frescoes are worth seeing. In the back corridor, you'll see bullet holes in the marble - this is where Senator Huey Long was shot in 1935. The observation deck on the 27th floor offers views of the Mississippi and the city. Free admission.

⏱ 60 min
3

If you have time, make a detour to the Old State Capitol, a neo-Gothic red brick castle on the Mississippi riverbank. Rebuilt in 1882 after a fire, it houses a spectacular spiral staircase and a colored stained-glass dome. Mark Twain found it "monstrously ugly" - you be the judge.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

In the evening, sample Baton Rouge's Cajun cuisine. Crawfish étouffée (crawfish in a thick roux with bell peppers and onions) is the region's signature dish. Smoked andouille, a spicy local sausage, accompanies most plates. It's more rustic than in New Orleans - and that's what makes it charming.

⏱ 60 min

Day 3

Natchez

Over a thousand antebellum mansions line the oldest city on the lower Mississippi

★★☆☆☆
1

Heading north from Baton Rouge, stop in St. Francisville, a village perched on hills above the Mississippi. Visit Rosedown Plantation, built in 1835 - it's one of the best-preserved plantations in the South. The 28-acre gardens, maintained according to Martha Turnbull's journal (kept from 1836 to 1895), feature camellias and azaleas imported from Asia in the nineteenth century. The 200-meter oak alley leading to the house is magnificent.

⏱ 75 min
2

Nearby, The Myrtles Plantation (1796) is nicknamed "America's most haunted house." Whether you believe in ghosts or not, the mansion is worth visiting for its 130-kilogram Baccarat crystal chandelier, its cantilevered staircase, and its hand-painted original French stained glass. The back veranda, with rocking chairs beneath oak trees draped in Spanish moss, is the essence of the Deep South.

⏱ 60 min
3

Continue north to Natchez (1.5 hours away). Founded in 1716 by the French, it's the oldest settlement on the lower Mississippi. Head up to Bluff Park late in the day: from this cliff, the Mississippi spreads out below and the view extends far across the Louisiana shore. It's the best spot in town for sunset.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Stroll through Natchez's historic downtown along Main Street. Before the Civil War, Natchez had more millionaires per capita than any other American city. Result: over 1,000 buildings listed on the National Register of Historic Places. You'll see Greek Revival, Federal, and Victorian facades at every corner. Antique shops and cafés occupy former cotton warehouses.

⏱ 60 min

Day 4

Natchez

Over a thousand antebellum mansions line the oldest city on the lower Mississippi

★★☆☆☆
1

Dedicate the morning to Natchez's mansions. Don't miss Longwood - it's the only octagonal house in the United States, and it was never finished. Construction was interrupted by the Civil War in 1861, and the Northern workers' tools are still in the attic, left there over 160 years ago. Only the ground floor is furnished; the upper floors remain a construction site frozen in time.

⏱ 60 min
2

Also visit Melrose Estate, managed by the National Park Service as part of Natchez National Historical Park. The 1845 mansion and its outbuildings - including original slave quarters - are among the best preserved in the South. Rangers give free guided tours that tell the daily life stories from both sides of the divide.

⏱ 60 min
3

If a third house appeals to you, Rosalie Mansion overlooks the Mississippi from the bluff. Built in 1820 on the site of French Fort Rosalie (1716), it served as Union Army headquarters during the war. The interior is furnished with period pieces.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In the afternoon, walk the Bluff Trail, an easy hiking path that follows the cliff above the Mississippi. The view changes at every turn - barges below, cypress forests on the opposite shore. In March-April and September-October, the "Pilgrimage" opens about twenty additional private homes: it's the best time to discover Natchez.

⏱ 60 min

Day 5

Vicksburg

Where a 47-day siege changed the course of the Civil War

★★★☆☆
1

Head to Vicksburg via the Natchez Trace Parkway - this old Native American trading path, which became a federal road in 1801, is now a scenic route with no billboards or trucks. The first 100 kilometers from Natchez traverse cypress forests and swamps. Stop at Emerald Mound, one of the largest ceremonial mounds in North America (dating from 1250), just north of Natchez.

⏱ 30 min
2

Vicksburg National Military Park commemorates the 47-day siege (May-July 1863) that gave the Union control of the Mississippi. The 16-mile scenic drive winds past more than 1,300 monuments and markers. Stop at the Illinois Memorial, inspired by Rome's Pantheon, and at the Kentucky Monument where statues of Lincoln and Jefferson Davis face each other - both were born in the same state. Allow 2 to 3 hours for the driving loop.

⏱ 150 min
3

In the park, don't miss the USS Cairo, a 175-foot Union ironclad gunboat sunk in the Yazoo River in 1862 by a Confederate mine - the first ship in history sunk by a mine. It remained on the bottom for 102 years before being raised in 1964. You can walk on the deck and see the breach from the explosion. The adjacent museum displays objects recovered from the ship, including a bottle of hot sauce that was still edible when opened.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Late in the day, visit the Old Court House Museum in downtown Vicksburg. This neo-Greek building from 1858 houses Civil War artifacts and tells the story of river commerce. From the cupola, the view of the Yazoo Canal and boat silhouettes at twilight is beautiful.

⏱ 45 min

Day 6

Jackson

Where civil rights museums preserve the memory of Mississippi's hardest struggles

★★★★☆
1

Jackson is an hour east of Vicksburg via I-20. Start at the Mississippi Civil Rights Museum, opened in 2017. The interactive galleries trace the civil rights movement in the state that was the scene of some of its most violent episodes - the Freedom Rides of 1961, the forced integration of Ole Miss in 1962, the assassination of Medgar Evers in 1963. It's a dense and moving museum; plan at least 1.5 hours.

⏱ 90 min
2

Right next door, the Old Capitol Museum occupies the former neo-Greek capitol from 1839. This is where Mississippi voted for secession in 1861. Admission is free and the exhibits cover the state's history from Native American nations onward. The building itself, with its columned portico, is one of the most beautiful downtown.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Explore the Fondren neighborhood, Jackson's bohemian corner. This small district is packed with independent bookstores, galleries, and Southern restaurants. This is where you'll find the best fried catfish and fried chicken in the city - Mississippi cuisine is simple, generous, and often served on paper plates.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 295 km · 2h55
Day 7

New Orleans

Where jazz was born beneath cast-iron balconies and Creole kitchens

★★★★★
1

On the way back to New Orleans (about 3 hours from Jackson), stop at Barataria Preserve in Jean Lafitte National Historical Park. Wooden boardwalks cross cypress swamps and bayous - you'll likely see alligators, great blue herons, and turtles. The Palmetto Trail (1.2 km) is short and accessible. Free admission.

⏱ 60 min
2

Back in the city, take the St. Charles Avenue streetcar - in service since 1835, it's the oldest continuously operating streetcar line in the world. It drops you in the heart of the Garden District. This neighborhood, developed in the 1830s-1860s by wealthy Americans, features monumental Victorian mansions beneath oak trees draped with Spanish moss.

⏱ 60 min
3

Pass by Lafayette Cemetery No. 1 (1833), with its above-ground tombs - the marshy ground doesn't allow for below-ground burials. Admission is free but hours are limited.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

End this road trip with a final evening on Frenchmen Street or in the Marigny. Order a fried shrimp po'boy at a neighborhood bar, listen to one last brass band, and let New Orleans do what it does best - make you want to come back.

⏱ 90 min
