  • Yinxu in Anyang: Shang dynasty capital 3,300 years ago, where the first Chinese inscriptions on oracle bones were discovered in 1899
  • Kaifeng Iron Pagoda, built in 1049 under the Song, standing 56 m tall, survivor of 38 earthquakes and 6 Yellow River floods

April, May, September, October

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 4 and 8 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Spring (April-May): Pleasant temperatures, blooming flowers, ideal weather, moderate crowds, excellent for most regions, comfortable travel.

Fall (September-October): Perfect weather, clear skies, comfortable temperatures, peak tourist season, higher prices, autumn colors beautiful.

Summer (June-August): Hot and humid, rainy season, crowded tourist sites, highest prices, but best for Tibet and western regions.

Winter (November-March): Cold temperatures, fewer crowds, lower prices, perfect for southern regions, harsh in north, unique winter experiences.

Regional Variations: Vast climate diversity, southern subtropical, northern continental, western plateau, plan according to regions visited.

🌍 What to discover in CN?

Ancient civilization meets modern superpower with Great Wall, forbidden cities, and cutting-edge technology. Experience diverse landscapes, incredible cuisine, and 5000-year cultural heritage. The country offers bustling megacities, pristine nature, and traditional arts. China provides overwhelming cultural richness and geographical diversity in rapidly modernizing nation.

📋 Important notes

Intense cultural and spiritual itinerary, with few dramatic landscapes outside Longmen and Songshan. High-speed rail along the entire Longhai axis (30 min to 1.5 hrs between cities). Private car or taxi essential for Songshan/Shaolin (1.5 hrs from Luoyang or Zhengzhou). Shaolin is very touristy: avoid Chinese weekends and public holidays. Peony Festival in Luoyang in April if you shift seasons.

Stop 1

Xian

Ancient Tang imperial capital, guardian of the Terracotta Army and the Ming ramparts

★★★★★
📍 Terracotta Army Xi'an📍 Xi'an City Wall📍 Big Wild Goose Pagoda Xi'an📍 Muslim Quarter Xi'an📍 Bell Tower Xi'an
1

Your journey begins with the Terracotta Army, 35 km east of Xi'an. Discovered accidentally in 1974 by farmers digging a well, Pit No. 1 houses more than 6,000 life-size soldiers, each with a unique face, who have guarded Emperor Qin Shi Huang's tomb since 210 BC. Arrive right at opening, around 8:30 am: tour buses roll in around 10 am and the pit becomes unbearable.

⏱ 150 min
2

Xi'an's city wall forms the largest and best-preserved urban fortification system in China. Built during the Ming dynasty in 1370 on the foundations of the ancient Tang capital, it traces a perfect rectangle measuring 13.7 km, standing 12 meters high and 15 meters wide at the top. Rent a bike atop the wall (enter at the south gate): count on 1.5 to 2 hours for the full circuit—it's faster than walking, and the late-afternoon light is glorious.

⏱ 120 min
3

The Big Wild Goose Pagoda, in the southern part of the city, was built in 652 during the Tang dynasty to house Buddhist sutras brought back from India by the monk Xuanzang after his sixteen-year pilgrimage. Seven stories, 64 meters tall: you can climb to the top for sweeping views over Xi'an. In the evening, the north plaza comes alive with water and light shows—it's free and one of the largest musical fountains in Asia.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Dive into the Muslim Quarter after dark, around Beiyuanmen Street. Xi'an's Hui people, descendants of Silk Road merchants, have run their stalls here for over a thousand years. Try yangrou paomo (lamb soup with crumbled flatbread), charcoal-grilled lamb skewers, and biangbiang noodles as wide as a belt. Avoid the main tourist-packed streets and venture into side alleys: the food is better and cheaper.

⏱ 90 min

If you still have energy in the late afternoon, climb the Bell Tower at the heart of the central crossroads. Built in 1384 during the Ming dynasty, it marked the exact geographic center of the imperial city. The combined ticket with the Drum Tower, 200 meters away, lets you watch a traditional drum performance every hour on the hour.

⏱ 45 min

Xian

Ancient Tang imperial capital, guardian of the Terracotta Army and the Ming ramparts

★★★★★
📍 Mount Hua Huashan📍 Huashan West Peak📍 Huashan Plank Walk📍 Huashan North Peak📍 Huashan North Railway Station📍 Xi'an North Railway Station
1

Head to Mount Hua on a day trip from Xi'an: 40 minutes by high-speed train to Huashan North, then a shuttle to the cable car. One of the five sacred Taoist mountains, with five peaks soaring above 2,000 meters—the South Peak reaching 2,154 meters. The paths carved into the rock have existed for over 2,000 years: this is where emperors came to make offerings to heaven. The north cable car costs about 80 yuan one-way and takes 20 minutes, versus 4 to 5 hours on foot up the historic stone stairs.

⏱ 240 min
2

At the West Peak summit, the Taoist Cui Yun Temple clings to the cliffs, its roof dating back to the 7th century. Monks still live there; you can buy incense and observe the morning rituals. The East Peak is more exposed—avoid it if you're afraid of heights.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Plank Walk in the Sky, on the south flank, is a wooden plank nailed to the cliff face at over 2,000 meters elevation. You walk it harnessed to a cable, roughly 100 meters out, 100 meters back. It's short, but the drop beneath your feet is absolute. Budget 30 extra yuan and prepare to wait an hour in high season.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Return to Xi'an in the evening by high-speed train from Huashan North station (40 minutes, around 55 yuan). Book ahead via the 12306 app or at the station, especially in high season when return trains around 6–8 pm fill up fast.

⏱ 60 min

Once back in Xi'an, grab a light dinner near the North Railway Station or head back to the city center for liangpi (cold spicy noodles) in the Muslim Quarter—perfect after a day of mountain trekking.

⏱ 45 min

Stop 3

Luoyang

Capital of thirteen dynasties, sanctuary of the Longmen Buddhist grottoes

📍 Longmen Grottoes Luoyang📍 Fengxian Temple Longmen📍 Vairocana Buddha Longmen📍 Yi River Luoyang
1

The Xi'an–Luoyang train takes just 1.5 hours and drops you at Luoyang Longmen, ten minutes from the grottoes. Head straight there in the morning: 2,345 niches carved into the limestone cliffs along the Yi River over a 1 km stretch, with nearly 110,000 Buddhist statues sculpted between 493 and the mid-8th century, inscribed as a UNESCO site in 2000. This is China's most breathtaking stone sculpture ensemble, rivaling Mogao and Yungang.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Fengxian Temple, on the west bank, is the crown jewel of the site. The central statue of Vairocana Buddha stands 17.14 meters tall, carved between 672 and 675 during the reign of Empress Wu Zetian, who is said to have lent her own features to the face. The heads of secondary statues were decapitated in the 1920s–1930s by Western collectors or during the Cultural Revolution. Many now reside at the Met in New York or the Atkinson Museum in Kansas City.

⏱ 60 min
3

Cross the bridge to the east bank, far less crowded. The Xiangshan Temple, rebuilt in 1707 under the Qing, and the garden of Bai Juyi (a Tang poet who spent his final days in Luoyang) offer panoramic views of the western cliffs. If you can, stay for the evening visit: the site reopens from 7 pm to 11 pm April–October, separate ticket at 90 yuan, far fewer people, and the lighting brings the Buddha to life.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

In the evening, stroll through Luoyang's old town (Laocheng), around Lijing Men Street. It's less touristy than Xi'an, more authentic, and you can sample the famous Luoyang Shuixi (the twenty-four water-based dishes), an imperial cuisine descended from the Tang court. The illuminated Lijing Men gate is stunning after dark.

⏱ 90 min

If you're here between April 5 and 25, don't miss the Peony Festival—the flower has been Luoyang's emblem since the Tang dynasty. Wangcheng Park (National Peony Garden) showcases over 1,200 varieties. It's become a national event, so plan extra time for the crowds.

⏱ 90 min
Stop 4

Luoyang

Capital of thirteen dynasties, sanctuary of the Longmen Buddhist grottoes

📍 White Horse Temple Luoyang📍 Qiyun Pagoda Luoyang📍 Luoyang Museum📍 Guanlin Temple Luoyang
1

According to tradition, the White Horse Temple is China's first Buddhist temple, founded in 68 AD under Emperor Ming of the Eastern Han, 12 km east of Luoyang. The emperor is said to have dreamed of a golden being and sent emissaries to India; they returned with two monks—Kasyapa Matanga and Dharmaratna—and sutras carried on a white horse, hence the name. The current buildings date mainly from the Ming and Qing, but the aura remains intact.

⏱ 90 min
2

Southeast of the temple, Qiyun Pagoda is one of China's oldest brick pagodas. Rebuilt in 1175 during the Jin dynasty, it rises 35 meters. Clap your hands at the south side of the pagoda: the echo sounds uncannily like croaking frogs—an unexplained acoustic phenomenon that has delighted visitors for centuries.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Luoyang Museum, opened in its new building in 2011, showcases one of the country's finest collections of Shang and Zhou bronzes, along with Tang tricolor (sancai) ceramics emblematic of the city. Plan on two unhurried hours—it's free with your passport.

⏱ 120 min
4

Guanlin Temple, south of the city, houses the presumed tomb of General Guan Yu's head. A hero of the Three Kingdoms era (3rd century), he was deified as Guandi. This is one of the three major Guan Yu worship sites in China, full of soldiers and police officers coming to pray before his statue.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Try a full Luoyang Shuixi meal at a traditional restaurant like Zhen Bu Tong on Zhongzhou Road. Twenty-four successive water- or broth-based dishes derived from Tang imperial cuisine: it's copious, fascinating, and best enjoyed with a group. Budget 150–300 yuan per person.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 75 km · 1h30
Stop 5

Dengfeng

Gateway town to Mount Song, portal to the Shaolin Temple and Chan Buddhism

📍 Shaolin Temple Songshan📍 Pagoda Forest Shaolin📍 Damo Cave Songshan📍 Shaolin Wushu Training Center
1

The Shaolin Temple was founded in 495 under Emperor Xiaowen of the Northern Wei to welcome the Indian monk Batuo. In 527, Bodhidharma arrived, reputedly meditating nine years facing a cliff in a cave on Mount Song and founding Chan (Zen) Buddhism along with the foundations of Shaolin martial arts. Inscribed as a UNESCO site in 2010, the complex covers 57,600 m² but remains highly touristic: arrive at opening (8 am) to visit before the tour buses.

⏱ 90 min
2

300 meters west of the main temple, the Pagoda Forest (Talin) gathers over 240 funerary stupas of eminent monks, dating from the 8th century (Tang) to the 19th (Qing). It's China's largest pagoda cemetery, peaceful and photogenic. Each stupa tells the life of the interred monk: the number of tiers reflects his rank, calligraphy adorns the base.

⏱ 60 min
3

High up at 1,360 meters elevation, the Damo (Bodhidharma) Cave must be earned: 30 minutes on foot or 5 minutes by cable car from Shifang Temple. The cave is small, empty, but this is where it all began, according to tradition. The view over the Shaoshi Valley is splendid on clear days.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Watch a kung fu demonstration at the Shaolin Wushu Training Center, right at the complex entrance. Three performances daily (10 am, 11:30 am, 2 pm), 30 minutes each. The techniques are choreographed for tourists, but the young monk-students (ages 8–15) are genuinely talented. Avoid weekends and Chinese holidays if you want to see anything.

⏱ 45 min

For those who want to go deeper, several schools in the Dengfeng valley (Tagou, Shaolin Epo) offer 1- to 3-hour kung fu introductory classes with a senior monk, for around 200–400 yuan. It's touristic but sincere, and far more instructive than the performances.

⏱ 90 min
Stop 6

Dengfeng

Gateway town to Mount Song, portal to the Shaolin Temple and Chan Buddhism

📍 Songyang Academy Dengfeng📍 Zhongyue Temple Songshan📍 Gaocheng Astronomical Observatory📍 Songyue Pagoda Dengfeng
1

Songyang Academy, at the southern foot of Mount Song, is one of the four great Confucian academies of the Song dynasty. Founded in 484 under the Northern Wei, it hosted the brothers Cheng Hao and Cheng Yi, founders of Neo-Confucianism. But its true stars are two giant cypress trees: according to tradition, they were named "Great Generals" by Emperor Han Wudi himself over 2,000 years ago—among the oldest trees in China.

⏱ 75 min
2

Zhongyue Temple, 4 km east of Dengfeng, is the largest and best-preserved of the five imperial temples dedicated to sacred mountains. Rebuilt mainly under the Qing, it retains its original Han layout with a succession of 11 courtyards spanning nearly a kilometer. It's a birthplace of Chinese Taoism, still vibrant: the monks practice here.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Gaocheng Astronomical Observatory, 15 km southeast of Dengfeng, is China's oldest preserved observatory. Built in 1276 under the Yuan by astronomer Guo Shoujing on Kublai Khan's orders, it was used to measure the shadow of the gnomon: standing 12.62 meters tall, it was one of 27 observatories in the imperial network. The Shoushi calendar computed here remained in use until the end of the Ming.

⏱ 60 min
4

On the way, make a quick stop at Songyue Pagoda (520), the oldest brick pagoda in China. Twelve sides, fifteen tiers, 40 meters tall, in the middle of the forest. It's stood for 1,500 years. Many miss it because it's off the standard circuits.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

If you're up for a hike, climb Junji Peak on Mount Song (1,491 m), starting from Zhongyue Temple on foot. Count on 4 to 5 hours round-trip, marked trail, quiet on weekdays. It's one of the five sacred Taoist mountains, and the least known: you'll cross paths with Chinese pilgrims rather than foreign tourists.

⏱ 240 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 95 km · 1h30
Stop 7

Kaifeng

Refined Song capital, city of seven dynasties layered atop five buried cities

📍 Iron Pagoda Kaifeng📍 Millennium City Park Kaifeng📍 Daxiangguo Temple Kaifeng📍 Kaifeng City Wall📍 Kaifeng Drum Tower Night Market
1

Kaifeng served as the capital of the Northern Song dynasty (960–1127), a city of one million inhabitants—the largest in the world at the time. The Iron Pagoda of Youguo Temple, built in 1049 under Emperor Renzong, stands as its symbol. Octagonal, thirteen stories, 56.88 meters: it's not made of iron but glazed brown bricks that mimic rust from a distance. Over 1,600 sculptures of Buddhas, dancers, and flowers adorn its walls. It has survived 38 earthquakes and 6 Yellow River floods.

⏱ 75 min
2

Millennium City Park (Qingming Riverside Landscape Garden) recreates life in 12th-century Kaifeng on a grand scale, based on the famous scroll painting "Along the River During the Qingming Festival" by Zhang Zeduan. It's highly theatrical: actors in Song costumes, horse races, cockfights, merchant streets, puppetry. For kids and enthusiasts of material culture alike, it's an excellent gateway.

⏱ 150 min
3

Daxiangguo Temple, right in downtown Kaifeng, was founded in 555 and played a pivotal role in the development of Chinese Buddhism under the Song. Its Hall of a Thousand Arms houses a statue of Guanyin carved from a single ginkgo trunk, dated to the 18th century—truly spectacular.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

In the evening, dive into the Drum Tower night market (Gulou). Kaifeng ranks among China's great culinary cities: try the Kaifeng-style xiaolongbao (juicy steamed dumplings), hu la tang (spicy morning soup), and sesame flatbreads with stuffing. It's lively until 11 pm, with a warm Chinese family atmosphere.

⏱ 90 min

Rent a bike and circle the Kaifeng city walls (12 km), rebuilt under the Qing. The remarkable part: beneath your feet lie five successive layers of buried cities, including Song-era Bianliang, buried under Yellow River silt. At dusk, a 3D show is projected onto the walls.

⏱ 120 min
Stop 8

Kaifeng

Refined Song capital, city of seven dynasties layered atop five buried cities

📍 Kaifeng Jewish Memorial📍 Longting Park Kaifeng📍 Kaifeng Fu📍 Yuwangtai Park Kaifeng📍 Kaifeng Bao Gong Temple
1

Kaifeng's former Jewish quarter is one of the most surprising chapters in Chinese history. A Jewish community settled here around the 10th–11th century, likely arriving from Persia via the Silk Road. For 800 years, they kept the Torah, spoke Hebrew, and built a synagogue (destroyed in the 19th century). The current memorial, modest, displays a few steles and an exhibition. You need a local guide to truly understand—the site itself is poorly curated.

⏱ 60 min
2

Longting Park (Dragon Pavilion), north of the city, occupies the site of the former Song imperial palace. The current pavilion dates to the Qing, but it evokes the vanished grandeur. In October–November, it's the heart of Kaifeng's Chrysanthemum Festival (held since the Song dynasty), with over 1,000 varieties on display.

⏱ 90 min
3

Kaifeng Fu recreates the tribunal of Judge Bao Zheng, an upright 11th-century magistrate who became a folk hero (Bao the Righteous). The visit is highly theatrical, but it's a chance to discover the Song judicial system: prison, interrogation room, instruments. There's a trial reenactment every two hours—in Chinese, but comedic and expressive.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

For authentic street food, venture off the tourist path to Madao Street (马道街) or Shudian Street (书店街, Bookshop Street). Local Kaifeng residents eat xiaolongbao from Diyilou (the city's historic brand, founded in 1922) and drink iced chrysanthemum tea in summer.

⏱ 75 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 215 km · 1h30
Stop 9

Anyang

Birthplace of Chinese writing and last capital of the Shang dynasty

📍 Yinxu Anyang📍 Tomb of Fu Hao Anyang📍 Yinxu Museum Anyang📍 National Museum of Chinese Writing Anyang
1

Yinxu, in Anyang, is one of the world's most significant archaeological sites. It was the last capital of the Shang dynasty, occupied around 1300–1046 BC, rediscovered in 1899 thanks to oracle bones being sold as "dragon bones" to pharmacies. Over 160,000 bone fragments have been unearthed, bearing the earliest known Chinese inscriptions. Inscribed as a UNESCO site in 2006. Budget 3 hours to truly grasp it, ideally with an audio guide.

⏱ 180 min
2

The Tomb of Fu Hao, within the palace compound, is the only intact Shang royal tomb ever found. Discovered in 1976, it belonged to the consort of King Wu Ding (around 1200 BC). Fu Hao was a queen, priestess, and general: oracle bones record that she led 13,000 soldiers into battle—the largest force ever documented. Nearly 1,928 objects were excavated: 468 bronzes, 755 jades, cowrie-shell currency, and human sacrifices.

⏱ 90 min
3

The new Yinxu Museum, opened February 26, 2024, is a revelation. 1.8 million visitors in one year. It assembles exceptional Shang bronzes (including a replica of the Houmuwu Ding, the world's largest ancient bronze at 832 kg), original oracle bones, and war chariots. If you see only one thing in Anyang, make it this.

⏱ 150 min
4

Right next door, the National Museum of Chinese Writing traces the evolution of characters, from oracle bones to Zhou bronzes to the Tang. Of the 4,300 characters identified on oracle bones, 1,600 have been deciphered so far. It's fascinating if you read or are learning Chinese—more arid otherwise.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

At the Yinxu Museum restaurant, they serve noodles decorated with oracle-bone characters printed in edible squid ink. Tourist gimmick, sure, but it's fun and unexpected after a morning of archaeology.

⏱ 45 min

Stop 10

Zhengzhou

Modern capital of Henan and archaeological hub of the Central Plains

📍 Henan Provincial Museum Zhengzhou📍 Erlitou Site Museum📍 Yellow River Scenic Area Zhengzhou📍 Shang Dynasty Wall Zhengzhou
1

The Henan Provincial Museum, in Zhengzhou, is one of China's great museums. Reopened in its new building in 2020, it brings together over 170,000 objects, including exceptional Shang collections (the Lian He You, the miniature Mu Wu Ding) and Zhou bronzes. Since Henan served as the cradle of nine dynasties, it offers a chronological reading of Chinese civilization. Budget 3 to 4 hours—it's free with your passport.

⏱ 210 min
2

If archaeology has captured you, make a detour to the Erlitou Site Museum, 1 hour by car from Zhengzhou. This is where the remains of the Xia dynasty (circa 1750–1530 BC) were allegedly identified—the first Chinese dynasty according to ancient texts. The building is new (2019), spectacular, but the most prestigious pieces are… in Zhengzhou.

⏱ 120 min
3

The ramparts of Shang-era Zhengzhou, dated to the 16th century BC, are the oldest surviving city walls in China. You can walk along them—it's free—and you'd never guess you're treading on 3,600 years of urban history. Several sections remain in the southeast of the modern city.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

In the late afternoon, visit the Yellow River 30 km north of Zhengzhou (Yellow River Scenic Area). The "cradle of the Chinese nation" flows here wide and muddy, and you can see the colossal statue of the two mythical emperors Yan and Huang carved into the cliff (106 m tall, one of the world's largest statues, completed in 2007). It's a bit kitschy, but the river is awe-inspiring.

⏱ 120 min
Stop 11

Zhengzhou

Modern capital of Henan and archaeological hub of the Central Plains

📍 Zhengzhou Xinzheng International Airport📍 Erqi Memorial Tower Zhengzhou📍 Zhengzhou East Railway Station
1

For your last morning, save time for the February 7th Memorial Tower (Erqi), at the heart of Zhengzhou. Built in 1971 to commemorate the great railway workers' strike of 1923, violently suppressed by warlords, it's a twin pagoda of 14 stories that has become the modern symbol of the city. Budget 30 minutes, mainly for the view from the top.

⏱ 45 min
2

Before your flight, make one last stop at Zhengzhou's jade market (Henan Yu City), one of China's largest for Nanyang jade. Even without buying, it's instructive to see the polishing techniques and stone varieties.

⏱ 60 min
3

Head to Zhengzhou Xinzheng International Airport (CGO), 35 km southeast. Direct flights to Beijing, Shanghai, Hong Kong, and several international destinations including Paris, Frankfurt, and Bangkok. Budget 45 minutes by taxi, 1 hour by metro (airport line from Zhengzhou East station).

⏱ 30 min

Activities

If your flight leaves in the evening, taste Henan cuisine one last time: hui mian (thick mutton noodles), Zhengzhou's specialty, at a traditional spot like Hesheng or Xiao Ji. It's rustic, generous, and far better than airport food.

⏱ 60 min
