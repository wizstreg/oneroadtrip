Tokyo

★★★★★
1

Step through the Kaminarimon Gate by 7:30 AM to see Senso-ji nearly empty. Founded in 628 after two fisherman brothers discovered a statue of Kannon in the Sumida River, it's Tokyo's oldest Buddhist temple. The giant red lantern at the entrance weighs about 700 kg. The current building dates from the 1950s—the March 1945 bombings destroyed everything except the residents' determination to rebuild it identically.

⏱ 90 min
2

Walking toward Senso-ji, Nakamise-dori draws you in. This 250-meter shopping arcade lines up about 90 shops that have been selling souvenirs, fans, ningyo-yaki (small red bean cakes), and grilled senbei since the late 17th century. Just beside it, the covered Shin-Nakamise-dori continues with cafés, restaurants, and artisans. Try a warm melon pan or dango while strolling.

⏱ 45 min
3

Head to Tokyo Skytree: at 634 meters, it's Japan's tallest tower and the world's second-tallest structure since its 2012 opening. Two observation decks at 350m and 450m. On clear days, you can spot Mount Fuji. Tickets cost around 2,100 ¥ for the first level. Book online the day before to skip 1-2 hour lines.

⏱ 90 min
4

Ueno Park spans 53 hectares since opening in 1873—it's Japan's first public park. At its heart, Ueno Toshogu Shrine founded in 1627 is also dedicated to Tokugawa Ieyasu. This jewel covered in gold leaf survived major earthquakes and the 1945 bombings. The grounds also house a lantern gallery of 50 stone and bronze lanterns offered by daimyo lords.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Explore Ameyoko, the market street running along the tracks between Ueno and Okachimachi. Its name comes from "Ameya Yokocho" (confectioners' alley) and evokes its post-war black market past. Today 400 stalls sell dried fish, exotic fruits, sneakers, and spices. It's one of the rare Tokyo spots where vendors shout their prices—a sonic shock after the city's polite silence.

⏱ 60 min

Finish at a traditional izakaya in Asakusa's back streets. Order yakitori (grilled skewers), edamame, and an Asahi beer or warm sake. The vibe is unpretentious, prices reasonable (2,000 to 3,500 ¥ per person). One rule: shoes stay on but silence at the table is earned by the clink of glasses as you say "kanpai!"

⏱ 90 min

Tokyo

★★★★★
1

Meiji Jingu Shrine was inaugurated in 1920 to honor Emperor Meiji and Empress Shoken. Bombed in 1945, it was rebuilt in 1958. The most striking feature is its forest: 100,000 trees donated from all over Japan and hand-planted, now grown into a mature 70-hectare forest in the heart of Tokyo. Pass through the great 12-meter cypress torii and let Harajuku's din disappear within steps.

⏱ 90 min
2

Yoyogi Park adjoins the shrine, offering 54 hectares of lawns, ponds, and paths. Created in 1967 on the site of the 1964 Olympic Village, it's where Tokyoites picnic, rehearse choreography, or play guitar on Sundays. Walk through it to experience the city differently, far from crowded sidewalks.

⏱ 45 min
3

Takeshita-dori—400 meters of packed sidewalk—concentrates kawaii fashion, giant crepes, gothic-lolita boutiques, and Japanese streetwear. Since the 1980s it's been the laboratory of Tokyo youth trends. Try a Marion crepe (strawberry-whipped cream) or Totti Candy Factory's rainbow cotton candy. Avoid Saturday afternoons when crowds become unbearable.

⏱ 45 min
4

Shibuya Crossing sees up to 3,000 pedestrians per light cycle during rush hour—it's the world's busiest intersection. Three free or near-free viewpoints: 11th floor of Shibuya Hikarie (free), Starbucks Tsutaya facing the crossing (crowded), or Magnet by Shibuya 109 rooftop (about 1,800 ¥ with a drink). The Hachiko statue nearby honors the Akita dog who waited for his deceased master for 9 years until his own death in 1935.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Dine in Omoide Yokocho in Shinjuku, the "memory lane" also nicknamed Piss Alley. These 60 tiny stalls (5-10 stools each) have served yakitori and ramen since the post-war era, in a smoky, convivial atmosphere. Budget 2,000 to 3,000 ¥. Continue with a stroll through Kabukicho, the red-light district: you can wander safely, but avoid touts offering "clubs"—tourist scams are common.

⏱ 150 min

To extend the evening, head up the Tokyo Metropolitan Government Building in Shinjuku. Two observatories at 202 meters, free entry until 10 PM. 360° views of the illuminated skyline and, on clear nights, Fuji's silhouette on the horizon. Less touristy than Skytree, and no lines.

⏱ 60 min

Tokyo

★★★★★
1

Yanaka is one of the rare Tokyo neighborhoods to have survived the great 1923 earthquake and 1945 bombings. Result: narrow alleys, period wooden houses, and a shitamachi (low city) atmosphere found nowhere else. Yanaka Ginza street, 170 meters lined with 70 small shops, remains run by families who've kept shop for three generations—fishmongers, tofu vendors, senbei makers.

⏱ 75 min
2

Yanaka Cemetery spans 10 hectares and holds over 7,000 graves, including that of the last shogun Tokugawa Yoshinobu. The central avenue lined with cherry trees becomes a pink tunnel in spring. In November, maples take over. It's a living cemetery: joggers, stray cats (Yanaka has hundreds), and residents cross it daily.

⏱ 45 min
3

Nezu Shrine is one of Tokyo's oldest—founded nearly 1,900 years ago according to tradition, with current buildings dating from 1706 on orders of shogun Tsunayoshi. Its tunnel of miniature torii rivals Fushimi Inari in miniature. In late April, 3,000 azaleas of 100 varieties color the hillside. Entry is free—it's a well-kept secret.

⏱ 60 min
4

Akihabara is the kingdom of electronics and otaku culture. The nickname "Electric Town" dates to the 1950s when the black market for radios and components exploded here. Today, entire buildings dedicated to manga, figurines, retro games, maid cafés. Yodobashi Camera Akiba on 9 floors remains a must-see even for non-geeks.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Sample a taiyaki (fish-shaped waffle filled with red bean paste) at Yanaka Shippoya, one of the street's iconic shops. Or try matcha ice cream from Himitsudo, artisan since 1997. These tasting stops punctuate the walk and immerse you in local habits.

⏱ 45 min

Step into a maid café in Akihabara for the experience—@home cafe is the most famous. The waitresses greet you shouting "okaerinasai goshujin-sama!" (welcome home, master). You eat decorated omurice, clap in rhythm. It's kitsch, unapologetic, and a fascinating window into an entire slice of Japanese pop culture. Budget 1,500 to 3,000 ¥.

⏱ 60 min

Nikko

★★★★★
1

Nikko Toshogu is the mausoleum of Tokugawa Ieyasu, founder of the shogunate that ruled Japan for 250 years. Built in 1617 by his son Hidetada, then massively expanded in 1636 by his grandson Iemitsu, it brings together 55 buildings covered in gilding and carvings—completely atypical in a culture where Shinto simplicity dominates. The famous Nemuri Neko (sleeping cat) carving and the three monkeys "see no evil, speak no evil, hear no evil" are here. UNESCO-listed since 1999, entry 1,600 ¥.

⏱ 150 min
2

Cross the Shinkyo, the vermillion "sacred bridge" spanning 28 meters over the Daiya River. Built in 1636, it was rebuilt identically in 1904 after a flood. Legend tells that priest Shodo Shonin managed to cross the river in the 8th century thanks to two divine serpents who served as a bridge. Paid access: 300 ¥.

⏱ 20 min
3

Rinno-ji is Nikko's major Buddhist temple, founded in the 8th century by the same monk Shodo Shonin. The Sanbutsudo (Hall of Three Buddhas) houses three 8-meter-tall gilded statues. Just beside it, the Sanbutsu-ji path winds through centuries-old cedars to various shrines. Sanbutsudo entry costs 400 ¥.

⏱ 60 min
4

Kegon Falls drops 97 meters—one of Japan's three most famous waterfalls. It forms the outlet of Lake Chuzenji and you can admire it from a free platform or from the lower observatory, accessible by elevator (570 ¥). In November, the cliff blazes with reds and golds.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Circle Lake Chuzenji, formed 20,000 years ago by Mount Nantai's eruption. At 1,269 meters altitude, its shores take on extraordinary colors in November. Pleasure boat (1,400 ¥ round-trip) or hiking trail around the lake—allow 2-3 hours for a partial loop.

⏱ 120 min

Taste yuba, Nikko's local specialty: it's the skin of soy milk, skimmed from the surface during tofu production. High in protein, unique texture, served as sashimi, in soup, or fried. Several restaurants in town make it their signature—try Hippari Dako or Meiji no Yakata (heritage addresses, about 2,500 ¥ for a set meal).

⏱ 75 min

Tokyo

★★★★★
1

Yudaki Falls pours 70 meters of water from Lake Yunoko. Less monumental than Kegon but wilder, cascading over volcanic rocks. Easy access from the Yudaki-iri bus stop. Its ochres and golds in November are unforgettable.

⏱ 45 min
2

Senjogahara Marshland spans 400 hectares at 1,400 meters altitude, formed by the gradual drying of an ancient lake. A 2-km wooden walkway crosses this protected wetland. In November, golden grasses wave in the wind with Mount Nantai as backdrop. Twin marsh Odashirogahara nearby, more intimate.

⏱ 90 min
3

Return train to Tokyo in late afternoon from Tobu-Nikko Station. The direct Revaty Kegon or Spacia takes 1h50. Enjoy the scenery rolling by: terraced rice paddies, traditional villages, then gradually the megalopolis densifying.

⏱ 110 min

Activities

Hike the trail linking Yudaki to Senjogahara: 4 km, 1h30, gentle elevation gain. It's Nikko National Park's most accessible hike, perfect for breathing fresh air before diving back into Tokyo. Dress warmly in November—temperatures drop quickly at altitude.

⏱ 90 min

Before the train, treat yourself to an onsen (thermal bath) at Yumoto Onsen, historic hot spring at 1,500 meters altitude. The whitish sulfurous water soothes muscles after hiking. Public baths from 700 ¥.

⏱ 60 min

Kawaguchiko

★★★☆☆
1

Lake Kawaguchi is one of five lakes surrounding Mount Fuji, formed 1,000 years ago by lava flows that dammed the valley. Its north shore offers the classic postcard view: Fuji reflecting in the water. On calm mornings, the symmetry is perfect (phenomenon called "sakasa Fuji"). The lake is 21 km in circumference and 14 meters maximum depth.

⏱ 60 min
2

Oishi Park on the north shore is THE photo spot for Fuji. In season, lavender (June-July), red kochia (October-November), or cosmos create a colorful foreground. In November, red maples frame the volcano. Free entry. An on-site café serves matcha latte decorated in Fuji shape.

⏱ 60 min
3

Mount Fuji stands at 3,776 meters—an active stratovolcano whose last eruption was in 1707. A sacred site in Shintoism, it's been UNESCO-listed since 2013 as an "object of artistic inspiration." From November to February, its summit remains snow-capped. Best viewing: morning (before 10 AM) to avoid afternoon clouds.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Rent an electric bike at Sazanami (about 1,500 ¥ for 4h) and circle the lake: 20 km, 3 hours with photo stops. The circuit is flat and well-marked, alternating touristy south shore and wild north side. Multiple Fuji viewpoints—few hikes are this photogenic.

⏱ 180 min

Take the Kachi Kachi ropeway (Mount Tenjo Ropeway) from the lake's east shore. In 3 minutes, you climb to 1,075 meters for stunning views of Fuji and the lake. Round-trip 1,000 ¥. The site tells the Kachi-Kachi Yama legend, a 19th-century Japanese tale. Walk down if you prefer (45-min path).

⏱ 75 min

Taste hoto, Yamanashi Prefecture's specialty: thick soup of flat noodles in miso broth with pumpkin, leek, and mushrooms. Created to feed warriors of the Takeda clan in the 16th century. Hoto Fudo has 5 branches around the lake, budget 1,500 ¥.

⏱ 60 min

Matsumoto

★★☆☆☆
1

Chureito Pagoda is part of Arakura Sengen Shrine, built in 1958 in memory of Fujiyoshida soldiers fallen in war. But it's the viewpoint that made it famous: climb 398 steps and you overlook the five-story red pagoda with Mount Fuji behind. One of Japan's most shared photos. Free entry.

⏱ 75 min
2

Matsumoto Castle is one of five Japanese castles classified as "National Treasure" and the country's oldest five-story, six-floor wooden keep. Built in the late 16th century (keep completed around 1594), it's the only flatland castle to have preserved its original wooden keep—others burned or were rebuilt in concrete. Nicknamed "Karasu-jo" (crow castle) for its black lacquered exterior. Entry 700 ¥.

⏱ 120 min
3

Downtown Matsumoto has preserved its Edo charm in the Nakamachi and Nawate-dori districts. Nawate, nicknamed "frog street" (referencing the nearby river), lines up craft shops, street food stalls, and amphibian statues. Nakamachi is renowned for its white warehouses with black roofs, housing galleries and cafés.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Take the Keio bus from Kawaguchiko to Matsumoto: about 3 hours, via the Japan Alps. The route crosses spectacular mountain landscapes, and toward Nagano you see snow-capped peaks appear. Book the day before (about 4,500 ¥).

⏱ 180 min

Enjoy a soba meal in Matsumoto. These buckwheat noodles are regional pride: the city and surroundings have produced renowned buckwheat since the Edo era. Try zaru soba (cold, dipped in dashi) at Kobayashi Soba—family address since 1910, about 1,500 ¥.

⏱ 60 min

Kamikochi

★★★★★
1

Kappa Bridge (Kappa-bashi) is Kamikochi's emblem—a 36.6-meter wooden footbridge spanning the icy turquoise Azusa River. The Hotaka peaks (3,190 m) tower above. The kappa is an aquatic creature from Japanese folklore said to inhabit these waters. The current bridge dates from 1997, built identically to the 1927 model.

⏱ 45 min
2

Taisho Pond formed in 1915 during Mount Yake's eruption, which dammed the Azusa River. Hence its name, marking the Taisho era. Dead tree trunks emerging from clear waters with the Alps as backdrop compose an almost ghostly landscape. 20 min walk from Kappa-bashi. Striking reflections in early hours.

⏱ 45 min
3

Myojin Pond is a natural mirror nestled at the foot of Mount Myojin (2,931 m). Hotaka-jinja Okumiya shrine nearby is dedicated to the mountain god. Access on foot from Kappa-bashi: about 1h along the Azusa, flat and well-maintained trail. Entry 500 ¥ around the pond.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Classic hike Kappa-bashi → Myojin → Taisho Pond: 15 km round-trip on flat trail along the Azusa River. Allow 4-5 hours with breaks. Wildlife to observe: Japanese macaques, snow monkeys, and with luck, rock ptarmigan. Protected area—stay on marked trails.

⏱ 300 min

Kamikochi access: local train from Matsumoto to Shin-Shimashima (30 min), then Alpico bus (60 min, about 2,450 ¥). Note that private cars have been banned in the valley since the 1970s to protect the site. JR Pass doesn't cover this segment.

⏱ 90 min

Takayama

★★★☆☆
1

Takayama Jinya is the only Edo-era regional government building still standing in Japan. Built in 1615, it served as the shogunate's administrative headquarters for 176 years. Tatami mat rooms, period kitchens, prison, and interrogation room are open to visit. Every morning on the esplanade: Jinya-mae Asaichi market (local products, crafts). Entry 440 ¥.

⏱ 75 min
2

Sanmachi-suji is Takayama's old merchant quarter. Three parallel streets preserved since the Edo era, with dark wooden houses, window grilles, and lanterns. Sake breweries are numerous—when you see a cedar ball (sugidama) hanging, there's fresh sake. Free tastings or 200-500 ¥.

⏱ 75 min
3

Hida Folk Village brings together 30 traditional houses transported from surrounding mountain villages then reconstructed here. Some are over 500 years old, with their gassho-zukuri thatched roofs (hands folded in prayer). Entry 700 ¥. 10 min by bus from Takayama Station.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Savor Hida beef, a wagyu breed raised locally for over 14 generations of selected cattle. Marbled fat, melts in the mouth. Several preparations: yakiniku (grilled at table), sushi (raw), korokke (croquette). Maison Kojima Ryori serves a lunch set for 3,000 ¥, dinner for 6,000 ¥.

⏱ 90 min

Take the Higashiyama trail: 3.5 km, about 2 hours, connecting 15 temples and shrines on the city's eastern hills. Monks' favorite meditation path, with panoramic views over the old town. Ancient cedar forest, old cemeteries, few tourists.

⏱ 120 min

Kanazawa

★★★☆☆
1

Kenrokuen is considered one of Japan's three most beautiful gardens along with Korakuen and Kairakuen. Designed over 11.4 hectares between the 17th and 19th centuries by the Maeda lords, its name means "garden of six qualities" (spacious, secluded, artificial, venerable, water-rich, panoramic). Its Karasaki maple is 170 years old. In November, yukitsuri (pyramidal rope supports) protect pines from snow weight. Entry 320 ¥.

⏱ 120 min
2

Kanazawa Castle was the residence of the Maeda clan for nearly three centuries, from 1583 to 1868. Several fires ravaged it; the current reconstruction uses only traditional techniques—not a single nail. The Ishikawa-mon gate (1788) is original. Park entry free, 320 ¥ for main buildings.

⏱ 90 min
3

Higashi Chaya was Kanazawa's largest geisha district since 1820. Three cobbled streets lined with red-façaded houses with kimusuko wooden lattices. Still active today: five geishas (geiko) practice here. Visit Shima house (500 ¥), opened to public since 2003, or Kaikaro which offers tea with maiko.

⏱ 75 min
4

Nagamachi, the samurai district, preserves its yellow earthen walls (dobei) and narrow alleys. The Nomura house, high-ranking samurai of the Maeda clan, offers an intimate glimpse: tatami room, miniature garden with koi carp. Entry 550 ¥. In winter, walls are protected by straw mats (komomaki).

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Explore Omicho Market, "Kanazawa's kitchen" since 1721. 170 stalls: fishmongers, snow crabs (local specialty November-March), sea urchin, vegetables, takeout snacks. Try a kaisen-don (ultra-fresh sashimi rice bowl) for 2,000-3,000 ¥. Open 9 AM-5 PM.

⏱ 90 min

Try Kanazawa's edible gold leaf, which produces 99% of Japan's leaf gold since the 16th century. Vanilla ice cream entirely covered in gold (Hakuichi, about 900 ¥) or golden matcha cake. There are also workshops for applying gold leaf to objects.

⏱ 45 min
$$$★9.9Onsen and Garden Nana

Kanazawa

★★★☆☆
1

The 21st Century Museum of Contemporary Art, nicknamed "Maru Bijutsukan" (the round museum), was designed by SANAA agency in 2004. Circular glass building with no back façade, inviting entry from all sides. Permanent installation "Swimming Pool" by Leandro Erlich creates the illusion of a traversable pool. Won Golden Lion at Venice Biennale. Free entry for open zones, 450 ¥ for exhibitions.

⏱ 120 min
2

Myoryuji, nicknamed "ninja temple," is a Nichiren temple built in 1643 under cover of a Buddhist sanctuary—actually a disguised fortress for Maeda clan samurai. Seven apparent floors hide 23 rooms, 29 staircases, trapdoors, traps. Guided tour mandatory (in Japanese, French booklet provided). Phone reservation essential. 1,200 ¥.

⏱ 75 min
3

Nishi Chaya, the third geisha district, is the smallest and most discreet. A single cobbled street, but still alive with active teahouses. Nishi Chayagai Museum (free) tells a geisha's daily life a century ago. Less touristy than Higashi Chaya.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Try a gold leaf workshop at Hakuichi or Kanazawa Yasue-yunuki: apply a leaf yourself to a small lacquered box or pair of chopsticks. 1,500 to 3,500 ¥ depending on object. The artisan explains the process: gold is hammered here until reaching 0.0001 mm thickness.

⏱ 75 min

Learn tea ceremony at Shima teahouse in Higashi Chaya, dating from 1820. A master guides you through ritual gestures: whisking matcha, serving, drinking in three sips. Service in kimono, accompanied by seasonal wagashi. About 1,500 ¥.

⏱ 45 min

Oyama Shrine, built in 1873, honors Maeda Toshiie, clan founder. Its gate (Shinmon) surprises: designed in 1875 by a Dutch architect, it mixes Japanese, Chinese, and European styles, with a colorful stained-glass window at the top. Totally atypical in Japan.

⏱ 45 min

Kyoto

★★★★★
1

Nijo Castle was built in 1603 as the first shogun Tokugawa Ieyasu's official residence in Kyoto. UNESCO-listed since 1994. Its Ninomaru Palace impresses with "nightingale floors" (uguisubari) that sing at every step—revolutionary anti-ninja system. Paradoxically, this is where the last shogun Yoshinobu officially returned power to the emperor in 1867. Entry 1,300 ¥.

⏱ 120 min
2

Nishiki Market, nicknamed "Kyoto's kitchen," stretches 400 meters over 5 blocks with 130 stalls. It has existed since the 1310s as a fish market. Today: fresh tofu, tsukemono (fermented vegetables), yuba, tamago-yaki (sweet omelets), sashimi, wagashi sweets. Free entry, but budget 1,500-2,500 ¥ for snacking.

⏱ 75 min
3

To-ji Temple was founded in 796, just after Kyoto's founding. Its five-story pagoda stands 55 meters—Japan's tallest wooden structure. Destroyed several times, rebuilt in 1644. Entry 800 ¥ for main buildings. On the 21st of each month, Kobo-san flea market with 1,200 stalls.

⏱ 90 min
4

Kyoto Tower, 131 meters high, was inaugurated in 1964. Its observatory at 100m offers complete city views and, on clear days, out to the Higashiyama and western mountains. Entry 900 ¥. Illuminated in white at night, it was long controversial for its modern look facing millennial temples.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Sample snacks at Nishiki Market: tako-tamago (octopus with quail egg head, 500 ¥), fried tofu sticks, glazed soy, iced matcha tea. Some shops have existed over 400 years and deserve a look inside—Aritsugu for instance has forged knives since 1560.

⏱ 75 min

In the Karasuma-Shijo-Kawaramachi district, Kyoto's commercial and nightlife heart, stroll covered Teramachi and Shinkyogoku galleries, then a light kaiseki dinner or obanzai (Kyoto home cooking) in one of Pontocho-dori's izakaya. 3,000-6,000 ¥.

⏱ 120 min
$$$★9.8Hotel Chourakukan Kyoto Gion

Kyoto

★★★★★
1

Fushimi Inari Taisha, founded in 711 by the Hata clan, is the head shrine of Inari deity (rice, fertility, commerce). About 10,000 vermillion torii form a tunnel climbing Mount Inari (233 m). Each gate was donated by a company or individual—current price: 175,000 to 1.3 million yen. Full loop: 2-3 hours. Open 24/7, free.

⏱ 150 min
2

Kiyomizu-dera, founded in 778, is UNESCO-listed. Its main hall sits on a gigantic 13-meter-high wooden terrace without a single nail. 139 zelkova pillars support it. The expression "jump from Kiyomizu's veranda" (launch into something risky) comes from here: 234 people actually did it in the Edo era (85% survived). Entry 500 ¥.

⏱ 90 min
3

Sannen-zaka and Ninen-zaka are two steep cobbled streets leading to Kiyomizu-dera. Built in the 8th century, preserved in their Edo state. Wooden façades, lanterns, and ceramic shops (Kiyomizu-yaki is local pottery). Legend says whoever stumbles on these pavements dies within 3 years—stay careful!

⏱ 60 min
4

Yasaka Pagoda (Hokan-ji) towers over the district at 46 meters. Built in 1440, it's one of Kyoto's oldest standing pagodas. Photogenic anytime: framed by narrow alleys it creates the postcard silhouette of Gion. Its interior occasionally opens (400 ¥).

⏱ 30 min
5

Gion is the geisha district since the 17th century. Its Hanamikoji and Shirakawa alleys preserve machiya (narrow, deep houses) and okiya (geisha houses). Still 80 geishas and maiko active. You might spot them around 6 PM, en route to a banquet—don't photograph them without asking.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Discover Chion-in, headquarters of the Jodo school (Pure Land Buddhism), founded in 1234. Its 24-meter-high Sanmon gate is Japan's largest temple wooden gate (1621). Its 74-ton bell is struck 108 times every December 31st. Free entry, 300 ¥ for gardens.

⏱ 60 min

Dine in Pontocho, a 500-meter cobbled alley parallel to the Kamo River. 100 restaurants of all ranges line up their red lanterns. The fanciest have a yuka: wooden terrace over the river (May-September). Budget 4,000-15,000 ¥ for kaiseki.

Kyoto

★★★★★
1

Kinkaku-ji (officially Rokuon-ji), the Golden Pavilion, was built in 1397 as shogun Ashikaga Yoshimitsu's retirement villa, then converted to a Zen temple upon his death in 1408. The upper two floors are covered in pure gold leaf (0.5 μm thick). Burned by a monk in 1950 (Mishima's novel "The Golden Pavilion"), the current building dates from 1955. UNESCO since 1994. Entry 500 ¥.

⏱ 75 min
2

A 10-minute walk away, Ryoan-ji houses Japan's most famous dry Zen garden (karesansui), created around 1500. Fifteen stones arranged on raked gravel, but only 14 visible simultaneously from any angle—only wisdom allows seeing them all. UNESCO-listed. Entry 600 ¥.

⏱ 75 min
3

Tenryu-ji is Arashiyama's main Zen temple, founded in 1339 in memory of Emperor Go-Daigo. UNESCO-listed. Its Sogenchi garden, designed by monk Muso Soseki, has remained virtually unchanged for 700 years—one of Japan's first "strolling gardens" using Arashiyama's mountains as natural backdrop (shakkei). Garden entry 500 ¥.

⏱ 75 min
4

Arashiyama's bamboo grove covers 16 hectares. Its stalks reach 20 meters. The sound of wind in the leaves was officially classified as "one of 100 soundscapes of Japan to preserve." Very touristy during the day—come before 9 AM or after 5 PM to experience the true atmosphere.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walk Togetsukyo bridge ("moon-crossing bridge"), 155 meters long over the Katsura River. Built in the 9th century, rebuilt several times, current version in wood-painted concrete dates from 1934. Arashiyama's photo meeting point, with mountains in background. Traditional yakatabune boats offer river rides (1,500 ¥, 30 min).

⏱ 45 min

Stop at a tofu artisan in Arashiyama—the district is renowned for it. Sagano Yudofu Sanjuku offers boiled tofu (yudofu) served in several preparations, in a traditional building beside a garden. 3,800 ¥ for the set.

⏱ 75 min

Kyoto

★★★★★
1

Mount Kurama, north of Kyoto, is a sacred site linked to tengu (mythological creatures). Kurama-dera temple, founded in 770, nestles on the mountainside. Paved ascent path through ancient cedars, 1 hour climb. Perched sanctuary, valley views. Cosmic energy said to be particularly intense here according to shugendo.

⏱ 120 min
2

Descend on foot to Kibune village—30 min hike. Kibune-jinja, founded 1,600 years ago, is dedicated to the water god. Its entrance stairway lined with red lanterns is iconic. In November, flaming maples compose an extraordinary setting. The village is famous for its kawadoko restaurants (terraces over the river, in summer).

⏱ 75 min
3

Ginkaku-ji, the Silver Pavilion, was built in 1482 by shogun Ashikaga Yoshimasa as a retreat, inspired by his grandfather's Golden Pavilion. The silver was never applied—project abandoned for lack of funds during the Onin War. Its white sand garden "sea of silver" (Ginshadan) with its "Kogetsudai" cone imitating Mount Fuji is unique. UNESCO. Entry 500 ¥.

⏱ 75 min
4

Heian Shrine was built in 1895 for Kyoto's 1,100th anniversary. It reproduces at reduced scale (5/8) the original imperial palace of 794. Its vermillion-painted concrete torii at 24 meters is one of Japan's largest. Its 30,000 m² gardens (600 ¥) line up 1,000 cherry trees. The building is recent but the architectural feat is debated.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Take the Philosopher's Path (Tetsugaku no Michi): 2 km along a canal lined with 450 cherry trees, connecting Ginkaku-ji to Nanzen-ji. Named because philosopher Nishida Kitaro walked it daily. In November, maples dominate. Several small temples en route (free Honen-in worth the detour).

⏱ 75 min

Enjoy a shojin-ryori meal (Buddhist vegetarian cuisine) at Nanzen-ji or a nearby temple. Based on 5 colors, 5 flavors, 5 preparation methods—strictly vegan and codified since the 13th century. 3,000-8,000 ¥ for set meal. Reservation essential.

⏱ 90 min

Nara

★★★★★
1

Todai-ji houses the Daibutsu, a 15-meter-tall, 500-ton bronze Buddha statue cast in 752. Its Daibutsuden is the world's largest ancient wooden structure—even though rebuilt in 1709 at only two-thirds its original size. The pillar with a hole at the base: if you pass through (size of the Buddha's nostril), you're promised enlightenment. Entry 600 ¥.

⏱ 90 min
2

Nara Park is home to 1,200 sika deer roaming freely. Considered divine messengers since the 8th century, they're protected by law—killing a deer was punished by death until 1637. You buy special crackers (shika senbei, 200 ¥) from approved vendors. Deer bow to get them—learned behavior by tradition.

⏱ 60 min
3

Kasuga Taisha, founded in 768, is the Fujiwara clan's main shrine. Famous for its 3,000 stone lanterns lining the access path and 1,000 bronze lanterns suspended in the grounds. Illuminated twice yearly (February and August). The Man'yo secondary grounds are planted with 250 plants mentioned in the Manyoshu (8th-century poetic anthology). Free grounds entry, 500 ¥ for inner sanctuary.

⏱ 75 min
4

Kofuku-ji was founded in 669. Its five-story pagoda stands 50 meters and dates from 1426—Japan's second-tallest. Its National Treasures Museum houses exceptional wooden Buddhist statues, including the three-faced, six-armed Ashura. UNESCO. Museum entry 700 ¥.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Stroll Isuien Garden, a traditional landscape garden in two parts (17th and 19th centuries) using Mount Wakakusa and Todai-ji's gate as background ("shakkei" technique). Soba restaurant on-site. Entry 1,200 ¥. Its twin Yoshikien is free for foreigners.

⏱ 75 min

Taste freshly pounded mochi at Nakatanidou: artisans pound glutinous rice with mochitsuki (huge wooden mallet) at spectacular speed, 150 strikes in two minutes. Yomogi (mugwort) ball stuffed with red bean paste, still warm. 200 ¥ piece, eat immediately.

⏱ 30 min

Osaka

★★★★☆
1

Osaka Castle was built by Toyotomi Hideyoshi in 1583, unifying Japan after a century of civil wars. The current keep is a 1931 concrete reconstruction furnished as a museum. Panoramic view from the 8th floor. The surrounding 106-hectare park is magnificent in November. Keep entry 600 ¥.

⏱ 120 min
2

Dotonbori is Osaka's neon heart for 400 years. Its eponymous canal was dug in 1612. The Glico running man sign (1935, current 6th version) and the giant Kani Doraku crab are icons. It's the temple of kuidaore ("eat till you drop"): takoyaki, okonomiyaki, kushikatsu, fugu.

⏱ 90 min
3

Shinsekai, "new world," was built in 1912 inspired by Paris (north) and New York (south). Its 103-meter Tsutenkaku Tower, rebuilt in 1956, dominates the district. Once a seedy area, now a popular neighborhood renowned for kushikatsu skewers. Quick visit but unique Osaka atmosphere.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Try okonomiyaki, Osaka's iconic "Japanese pancake": batter of cabbage, egg, bacon, sauce, mayonnaise, bonito flakes dancing from the heat. At Mizuno in Dotonbori (since 1945) or Kiji, budget 1,200-2,000 ¥. The Hiroshima version is layered, Osaka version mixed—don't confuse them.

⏱ 75 min

Explore Kuromon Market (600 meters, 150 shops since 1902), nicknamed "Osaka's kitchen." Grilled seafood on-site, Kobe beef sashimi, sea urchin, fresh wasabi. Budget 2,000-4,000 ¥ for a complete tasting. Open 9 AM-6 PM.

⏱ 90 min

End the evening with a drink atop Umeda Sky Building, free observatory (entry 1,500 ¥) at 173 meters with floating walkway connecting two towers. Signature Hiroshi Hara architecture (1993). 360° views of nighttime Osaka, lights as far as the eye can see.

⏱ 90 min

Miyajima

★★★★★
1

The Genbaku Dome is the only building left standing near the hypocenter of the atomic bomb dropped on August 6, 1945 at 8:15 AM. Built in 1915 as the Industrial Promotion Hall, it stood 150 meters from the explosion's vertical point—the downward force spared its frame. UNESCO-listed in 1996. Preserved in its August 6th state. You can't enter, but walking around outside is deeply moving.

⏱ 45 min
2

Hiroshima Peace Memorial Park was designed by Kenzo Tange and inaugurated in 1954. The central Cenotaph holds the names of all identified victims (now over 340,000). The Flame of Peace has burned since 1964 and won't be extinguished until all nuclear weapons are abolished. The Children's Monument honors Sadako Sasaki, who died of leukemia at 12 after folding over 1,000 origami cranes.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Peace Memorial Museum is one of the world's most harrowing museums. It assembles victims' personal items, photos, testimonies. The east wing (2019 renovation) recounts August 6th minute by minute. No sensationalist images, but an emotional depth that marks you for life. Allow at least 2 hours. Entry 200 ¥.

⏱ 120 min
4

Ferry across (10 min) to Miyajima, "island of gods." Itsukushima Shrine, founded in the 6th century, was built on stilts at the sea's edge because the entire island was considered too sacred to set foot on. At high tide, the 16-meter-tall floating torii (camphor wood, 60 tons) seems to drift. UNESCO since 1996. Entry 300 ¥.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Osaka-Hiroshima journey: Shinkansen Sakura or Nozomi, 1h30, about 10,000 ¥ (covered by JR Pass except Nozomi). Numbered seats, wifi, perfect cleanliness.

⏱ 90 min

Enjoy Miyajima's sacred deer, which roam freely like in Nara but are more discreet. Regular street walkers, they sometimes steal tourist maps from distracted visitors. Also taste the giant oysters (kaki), produced in the bay and island specialty—grilled with lemon, 400 ¥ each.

⏱ 60 min

Hiroshima

★★★★★
1

Daisho-in is Miyajima's oldest temple, founded in 806 by Kukai, founder of Shingon Buddhism. The access stairway is lined with 500 rakan statues (Buddha's disciples), all different. Mantra prayer wheels to spin. Mystical atmosphere, much less crowded than Itsukushima. Free entry.

⏱ 75 min
2

Mount Misen peaks at 535 meters and offers extraordinary panoramas of the Seto Inland Sea and its islands. Miyajima Ropeway from Momijidani, in two sections, 30 min journey (2,000 ¥ round-trip). At the summit, 30-min trail to the true peak with Reikado temple housing an "eternal flame" lit by Kukai 1,200 years ago.

⏱ 150 min
3

Return to Hiroshima and castle visit. Built in 1589 by lord Mori Terumoto, completely destroyed by the 1945 atomic bomb. Rebuilt identically in 1958, it now houses a samurai history museum. Entry 370 ¥. Free park, pleasant for a late afternoon stroll.

⏱ 90 min
4

Mitaki-dera, perched on Mount Mitaki's flanks in northwest Hiroshima, was founded in 809. Its name comes from the site's three waterfalls. Three-story pagoda transported here in 1951 from central Hiroshima to commemorate victims. Mossy forest path, rare oasis of peace in the city. Free.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Try Hiroshima-style okonomiyaki: "layered" version with a thin crepe, cabbage, noodles (yakisoba or udon), egg, sauce. Created in the 1950s to feed the post-war population cheaply. Okonomi-mura brings together 25 counters on 4 floors. 1,000-1,500 ¥.

⏱ 75 min

Return to Hiroshima by ferry + train from Miyajima (40 min total). Head to your hotel in the city center, near the station or Peace Park to simplify tomorrow.

Hiroshima

★★★★★
1

Shukkei-en, "miniature scenery garden," was created in 1620 by lord Asano. It miniaturizes 60 famous Chinese landscapes, notably West Lake in Hangzhou. Destroyed by the bomb in 1945, then rebuilt identically. 4 hectares, central pond, bridges, teahouses. Entry 260 ¥. Peaceful stroll before departure.

⏱ 75 min
2

Hondori, a 500-meter covered shopping street in Hiroshima's heart, gathers nearly 200 shops and restaurants. Perfect for last-minute shopping or okonomiyaki before the train. Very local atmosphere, few tourists despite the size.

⏱ 60 min
3

Hiroshima Station: catch Shinkansen Nozomi or Hikari to Tokyo. 4-5 hours depending on train, about 19,000 ¥. Cross Kansai, pass within kilometers of Mount Fuji on the north side. Last chance to see Japanese landscapes rolling by at 300 km/h.

⏱ 240 min

Activities

Final ekiben to buy at the station before the train. These regional lunch boxes are a tradition: each station offers its own. In Hiroshima: anaguni-meshi (grilled eel on rice), oysters, or lunchbox inspired by local baseball. 1,000-2,000 ¥. Perfect ritual before leaving the region.

⏱ 45 min

Night in Tokyo, ideally in a hotel near the airport (Narita or Haneda depending on your flight). Pack, rest. Return flight scheduled tomorrow.

Tokyo

★★★★★
1

Last Tokyo morning, adapt to flight time. If you have time, stop at Tokyo Station whose Marunouchi façade (1914) was restored in 2012 in its Belle Époque style. Two steps away, the Imperial Palace and its outer gardens (free) offer a final calm look at the capital.

2

Ginza shopping district for a last souvenir—high-end boutiques but also Uniqlo Ginza (12 floors!), Itoya for Japanese stationery, or Don Quijote for pop goodies. The main street is pedestrian on weekend afternoons.
