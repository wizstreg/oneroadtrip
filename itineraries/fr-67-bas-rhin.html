Strasbourg

★★★★☆
1

Strasbourg Cathedral deserves at least an hour outside alone. Its 142-meter spire in pink Vosges sandstone, completed in 1439 by Jean Hültz of Cologne, made this the tallest building in Christendom for over two centuries—from 1647 to 1874. Victor Hugo called it a 'prodigy of the gigantic and the delicate'; Goethe fell in love here for the first time. Inside, don't miss the 1574 astronomical clock and the 14-meter rose window. If you want to climb to the 66-meter platform (330 steps), go in the morning—lines are short and the light on the rooftops is beautiful.

⏱ 90 min
2

From Place de la Cathédrale, dive into La Petite France. This medieval tanners' quarter—one of the best-preserved in Europe—concentrates 16th-century half-timbered houses that reflect in the arms of the Ill River. The Grande Île as a whole has been a UNESCO World Heritage site since 1988: it was the first historic city center in France to earn this designation. Wander aimlessly toward the Ponts Couverts—three 13th-century towers that once guarded the entrance to the quarter.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Palais Rohan, built between 1731 and 1742 for Cardinal Armand de Rohan-Soubise, houses three museums within its rococo salons: the Decorative Arts Museum (with royal apartments where Marie-Antoinette stayed), the Fine Arts Museum, and the Archaeological Museum. Budget at least 90 minutes if you visit all three, 45 if you only tour the apartments. The riverfront façade alone is worth the detour.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

In the evening, step into the Alsatian Museum, housed in three 17th-century houses connected by wooden galleries. The reconstructions of Alsatian interiors from the 18th to 20th centuries—kitchens, bedrooms, shops—are strikingly precise. The atmosphere feels like a giant family attic, warmly lit.

⏱ 75 min

End the day at Place Kléber, Strasbourg's beating heart. General Kléber's statue stands at the center of this grand paved square. The brasserie terraces around the square are ideal for catching your breath and watching Strasbourg life unfold. If you come in December, the Christmas market held here since 1570 is one of Europe's oldest—the first records date precisely to that year.

⏱ 60 min

Strasbourg

★★★★☆
1

The Neustadt quarter holds a surprise: this extension of Strasbourg built between 1871 and 1918, when the city was German, has been a UNESCO World Heritage site since 2017. The Wilhelminian architecture—imposing, ornamental, designed to manifest the power of the Second Reich—contrasts radically with yesterday's medieval core. Place de la République with its Rhine Palace (1889) and the University (1884) are the centerpieces. Take time to walk along Boulevard de la Victoire.

⏱ 75 min
2

Climb to the terrace of the Vauban Dam for one of Strasbourg's finest panoramas. This late-17th-century military work, designed by Vauban on Louis XIV's orders, could flood the entire lower town within hours to stop an invading army. From the terrace, you take in the Ponts Couverts, La Petite France, and the city's steeples—it's free and worth the climb.

⏱ 45 min
3

Parc de l'Orangerie stretches across 26 hectares in the heart of the European quarter. Created in the 17th century then redesigned for Empress Joséphine in 1804 (a pavilion is dedicated to her), it's Strasbourg's oldest public park. Storks nest here every spring—the symbolic bird of Alsace. It's the perfect spot to unwind after two intensive days in the city.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Board a boat for a cruise on the Ill. Several operators offer 70-minute circuits that follow the Grande Île then the Neustadt, with multilingual commentary on board. It's the best way to see the cathedral, La Petite France, and the Palais Rohan from the water—the angles are completely different. Book ahead in summer.

⏱ 75 min

Make the detour to the European quarter, a 20-minute walk from the center. The glass-and-steel European Parliament, inaugurated in 1999, symbolizes Strasbourg's role as the capital of Europe—the only city in the world hosting a permanent European institution without being a national capital. The European Court of Human Rights sits just across. The architectural ensemble is striking.

⏱ 60 min

Obernai

★★★★☆
1

Obernai concentrates the best of medieval Alsace on a single square. At Place du Marché—where the weekly market has been documented since 1301—the Renaissance Town Hall (1523, remodeled in 1610), the 1554 Corn Exchange (originally a butcher shop and salt store), the 1579 Six-Bucket Well, and the Kappellturm belfry form a remarkably cohesive ensemble. Arrive in the morning for the light and before the tour buses descend.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Kappellturm belfry dominates the square at 60 meters tall. It's what remains of a chapel built in the late 13th century—the chapel was destroyed in 1873 but the bell tower survived, with its six bells, the two oldest dating from 1429 and 1474. The gallery with its watchtowers was added in 1596-97. On the northeast buttress, you'll find the official measurements of the ell and dagger carved into the stone—medieval measurement standards.

⏱ 30 min
3

Obernai is the legendary birthplace of Saint Odile, patroness of Alsace, born blind in the 7th century and said to have regained her sight on the day of her baptism. The medieval ramparts that still encircle the center—1,400 meters of walls with towers and moats—testify to the town's strategic importance. In 1354, Obernai joined nine other cities to found the Decapolis, the league of free imperial cities of Alsace. A walk along the rampart path gives you a good sense of the scale of the work.

⏱ 45 min
4

Saints Peter and Paul Church, built between 1867 and 1872 in monumental neo-Gothic style, preserves two classified treasures: the 1882 Merklin organ and the 1504 Holy Sepulchre altar, which depicts the risen Christ surrounded by the Holy Women with striking realism for its time.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Climb to Mont Sainte-Odile, 15 km from Obernai, Alsace's foremost pilgrimage site since the 7th century. The Abbey of Hohenbourg perches at 764 meters and offers views on clear days as far as the Bernese Alps and the Black Forest. Around the abbey runs the Pagan Wall—a 10 km enceinte of cyclopean stones whose origin remains debated, probably Celtic. An 11 km hiking circuit allows you to walk its full perimeter.

⏱ 150 min

In the evening, linger in Obernai's lanes to taste Alsatian specialties in a winstub: tarte flambée, baeckeoffe, choucroute garnie, and sweet kouglof for dessert. The town is the Bas-Rhin's second tourist destination after Strasbourg—restaurants are numerous and quality is generally good.

⏱ 90 min

Selestat

★★★★☆
1

The Humanist Library of Sélestat is one of the Bas-Rhin's finest surprises. Housed since 1889 in a former neo-Romanesque corn exchange built by Gustave Klotz (1843-1845), entirely restructured by Rudy Ricciotti and reopened in 2018, it preserves one of Europe's most precious collections. Its treasure: the 423 volumes of the personal library of Beatus Rhenanus (1485-1547), friend of Erasmus born in Sélestat, bequeathed to his native city at his death. The collection has been inscribed in UNESCO's Memory of the World Register since 2011. Notable items include a 7th-century Merovingian lectionary—the oldest book preserved in Alsace—and a copy of the 1507 Cosmographiae Introductio where the name 'America' appears for the first time.

⏱ 90 min
2

Sélestat's Romanesque church of Sainte-Foy dates from 1094 and is part of the Alsatian Romanesque Route. Its three-aisle nave in pink Vosges sandstone and octagonal tower are perfect examples of Rhenish Romanesque art. Before it, visit the Gothic church of Saint-Georges—built between the 13th and 15th centuries—which once housed the parish library of Sélestat's Latin school, with some volumes chained to lecterns to protect them from theft.

⏱ 60 min
3

Wander through Sélestat's historic center—its Renaissance houses, Vauban ramparts, and fountains tell of a time when it was one of the ten free cities of the Alsatian Decapolis. An amusing detail: the city archives preserve the world's first written mention of a Christmas tree, dated 1521, where the city notes the purchase of wood to 'make a Christmas tree'.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

From Sélestat, admire the silhouette of Haut-Koenigsbourg castle perched at 757 meters on its rocky outcrop—it dominates the entire Alsace plain and can be seen from many points in town. You'll visit it in detail tomorrow, but seeing it from the plain already gives you a good sense of its imposing character and the political dimension of its restoration.

⏱ 30 min

In summer, Sélestat's Corso Fleuri transforms the town with giant flower-covered floats. The city is also known for the Fête du Feu festival in July, which transforms the castle park into a pyrotechnic spectacle. Check local programming according to your visit period.

⏱ 60 min

Chateau Du Haut Koenigsbourg

★★★☆☆
1

Haut-Koenigsbourg castle is history within history. First mentioned in 1147, razed by Swedish troops after a 52-day siege in 1633, it lay in ruins for two and a half centuries before the city of Sélestat offered it in 1899 to Emperor Wilhelm II of Hohenzollern. He saw in it a symbol of Alsace's Germanic identity and entrusted the restoration to architect Bodo Ebhardt—a project from 1900 to 1908 that resurrected the castle as it was around 1500, in the Thierstein era. Pass through the high gate: two-thirds of the walls are medieval, the rest reconstructed using documented methods. Nearly 550,000 visitors per year.

⏱ 90 min
2

The castle's lower court houses a forge, water mill, and reconstructed inn—a village within the village, which functioned self-sufficiently during sieges. The ramparts extend 270 meters. Two artillery towers flank the great bastion. Climb to the bastion terrace for the panorama: the Alsace plain, the Black Forest to the east, the Vosges to the west, and on exceptional days the Swiss Alps. Bring warm clothing—even in summer, it's often 5°C cooler than in the plain.

⏱ 60 min
3

The banquet hall—called the 'Kaiser's Hall'—is the castle's most overtly political room. Wilhelm II had it enlarged beyond its original dimensions (a gallery indicates the initial height) and covered with heraldic frescoes by Léo Schnug illustrating Alsace's Germanic character. The imperial eagle dominates the coats of arms of Alsatian cities. Propaganda in stone worth seeing for what it reveals about the restoration's political ambition.

⏱ 45 min
4

The medieval garden arranged in the lower enceinte cultivates aromatic and medicinal plants according to medieval methods—squares bordered by woven branches called 'plessis,' which protected against wild animals. Jean Renoir filmed a scene from 'La Grande Illusion' (1937) here, and illustrator John Howe, who worked on 'The Lord of the Rings,' says he was inspired by the castle.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Theatrical tours animate the castle in season, with costumed actors reenacting scenes from medieval life. Check the castle's website program before your visit—time slots are often limited and animations vary by day and season.

⏱ 90 min

Riquewihr

★★★☆☆
1

Riquewihr had the fortune to pass through both world wars without a single bombing—its relative geographic isolation earned it this miraculous preservation. The result: 300 half-timbered houses from the 13th to 18th centuries, intact, in a village of 1,200 inhabitants surrounded by 368 hectares of vineyards. Listed among France's Most Beautiful Villages since 1982, it attracts over 1.5 million visitors per year. Arrive at opening time or late afternoon—during the day, the main street is packed.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Dolder, a 25-meter bell tower erected in 1291 along with the first fortified enceinte, is Riquewihr's symbol. Its Alsatian name means 'the highest point.' On the exterior side, it's austere and defensive; on the town side, it presents a half-timbered façade. Climb to the museum at the top for views over the rooftops and vineyard—from up there you understand better why the two grand crus Schoenenbourg and Sporen surround the village.

⏱ 45 min
3

The 13th-century Thieves' Tower—former prison of the Wurtemberg seigneury—preserves its medieval torture instruments and the interior of a 16th-century winemaker's house. The atmosphere is dark and authentic, very different from the picturesque main street. Not to be confused with the castle of the counts of Wurtemberg-Montbéliard, a 16th-century Renaissance building that today houses a museum of communication in Alsace.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Push through the doorways into interior courtyards—behind the main street façades lie winemakers' cellars that welcome visitors for tastings. About fifteen family estates still produce the village's two grand crus: Schoenenbourg, renowned since the 16th century for its rieslings of remarkable minerality and longevity, and Sporen, more clay-and-marl, the preferred terrain of gewürztraminer.

⏱ 90 min

The vineyard trail of the Grands Crus starts from rue de la Piscine and winds through the vines of Schoenenbourg and Sporen. In summer, winemakers lead guided tours followed by free tastings. Allow 90 minutes for the complete circuit. It's the best way to understand the soils and topography that make Riquewihr wines distinctive.

⏱ 90 min

Make the 5 km detour to Ribeauvillé, at the foot of the Vosges. A marked 7 km circuit (2h30) connects its three ruined medieval castles perched on the ridges: Saint-Ulrich castle, Girsberg, and Haut-Ribeaupierre. The panorama from each embraces the Alsace plain and the vineyards below.

⏱ 150 min

Kaysersberg

★★★☆☆
1

Cross the fortified bridge of 1514 in pink sandstone that spans the Weiss—unique in Alsace, it's equipped with arrow slits and a shrine housing an 18th-century statue of the Virgin. Formerly, it also served as a 'temporary prison' for petty offenders exposed to passersby's mockery. From there, the view of 15th and 16th-century half-timbered houses and the castle ruins on the hill is one of Alsace's most photographed. Kaysersberg, meaning 'Emperor's Mountain,' was elected France's Favorite Village in 2017.

⏱ 30 min
2

Climb to the medieval castle ruins that dominate the town by 50 meters. The castle was built from the 13th century onward to control the passage between Alsace and Lorraine via the Col du Bonhomme. Its circular keep, with walls 4.42 meters thick, withstood the Thirty Years' War. From the summit, the panorama extends over the Weiss valley, terraced vineyards, the Alsace plain, and on clear days the Black Forest.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Church of the Holy Cross preserves a late-15th-century carved altarpiece, considered one of the masterpieces of Alsatian wood sculpture. The scenes of Christ's Passion are treated with striking finesse and realism. The Romanesque bell tower dates from the 12th century.

⏱ 45 min
4

The birthplace of Albert Schweitzer, 1952 Nobel Peace Prize laureate, has become the Schweitzer Center. Schweitzer was born here in 1875—physician, theologian, musician, and philosopher, he founded the Lambaréné hospital in Gabon and devoted most of his life to it. The museum traces his exceptional journey with personal documents and objects. An interactive exhibition has been presented since a new extension opened.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Local operators offer tastings in the cellars of wine estates surrounding Kaysersberg. The Grand Cru Schlossberg (its name directly evokes the castle—'Schloss'—and the mountain—'Berg') is one of the most prestigious appellations. Independent winemakers receive visitors directly in their cellars.

⏱ 90 min

Kaysersberg's Christmas market, the four weekends of Advent, is considered among Alsace's most authentic. Wooden chalets settle in the central square and lanes, local artisans present their work, and the scents of bredele (small Alsatian cookies), mulled wine, and gingerbread fill the town.

⏱ 90 min

Saverne

★★★☆☆
1

Saverne's Château des Rohan astonishes with its proportions. Nicknamed the 'Little Alsatian Versailles,' its neoclassical pink sandstone façade extends exactly 140 meters—no accident: it's the equivalent, horizontally, of the height of Strasbourg Cathedral. Built in the late 18th century by architect Nicolas Salins de Montfort for Cardinal Louis René de Rohan-Guéméné, it's the fourth building erected on this site since the Middle Ages. Its museums (Gallo-Roman collections, art and history, Louise Weiss archives) deserve 90 minutes.

⏱ 90 min
2

A few kilometers from Saverne, Haut-Barr castle merits the detour. Nicknamed 'the Eye of Alsace,' this 12th-century medieval castle perched at 470 meters on three sandstone rocks offers a panorama over the entire Alsatian plain as far as Strasbourg Cathedral on clear days. The Devil's Bridge footbridge, suspended between two rocks, is a highlight of the visit.

⏱ 75 min
3

Saverne's rose garden extends at the foot of the pass—France's second-oldest rose garden, it assembles nearly 5,000 rose bushes from 800 varieties. Open from late May through late August, it's particularly spectacular in June during the Rose Festival.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The Saint-Louis-Arzviller inclined plane, about ten kilometers from Saverne, is a unique engineering feat in Europe. Opened in 1969 to replace 17 locks over 3.8 km, this transverse inclined plane allows barges to cross 44 meters of elevation on the Marne-Rhine Canal in a single 35-minute operation. Operators offer tourist boat rides on the inclined plane in season.

⏱ 90 min

Stroll along the Marne-Rhine Canal facing the Château des Rohan. The 70-berth marina, installed in the large basin, gives the town particular animation. Operators offer bike rides along the canal toward Arzviller or eastward. The greenway is pleasant and easy.

⏱ 60 min

La Petite Pierre

★★★☆☆
1

La Petite-Pierre occupies a pink sandstone outcrop that overlooks in one sweep the Northern Vosges forests. Its castle—first traces dating to the 13th century—was considerably remodeled by Vauban in 1684 for Louis XIV, who made it a strategic control point between Alsace and Lorraine. Today headquarters of the Northern Vosges Regional Nature Park, it houses a permanent exhibition on the history of fortifications and the park. Entry to the courtyard is free.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Staedtel—the old fortified town—rises in tiers on the sandstone outcrop with its ramparts, underground cisterns carved directly into the rock, and pink sandstone houses. The church preserves remarkably well-preserved 15th-century painted frescoes depicting biblical figures in ochre and blue tones. One of Alsace's least-known treasures.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Alsatian Seal Museum and the Museum of Popular Arts and Traditions of La Petite-Pierre respectively exhibit the heraldic history of Alsatian families and local popular traditions, notably Springerle—these little white cookies molded in shapes of flowers and animals that are part of Alsatian Christmas bredele.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Northern Vosges Regional Nature Park has been a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve since 1989—one of northeastern France's most extensive protected natural areas with 135,000 hectares of pine and sandstone forests, streams, and ruined castles. Over 2,600 km of marked trails cross the park. From La Petite-Pierre, several hiking circuits reach directly to spectacular sandstone rocks and medieval castle ruins buried in the forest.

⏱ 180 min

The rampart path circles La Petite-Pierre's outcrop and follows the fortifications for about 250 meters long by 80 wide. The underground cisterns—carved into the sandstone to supply the garrison during sieges—open for guided visits at certain times. Inquire at the Park House.

⏱ 60 min

Wissembourg

★★★☆☆
1

The Abbey Church of Saints Peter and Paul in Wissembourg is one of Alsace's great Gothic edifices—the second after Strasbourg Cathedral. Founded around 660, rebuilt in Gothic style from 1262 onward, it possesses a curiosity unique in France: a 14th-century mural painting depicting Saint Christopher carrying the Christ child, 11 meters tall—the largest preserved medieval full-length figure in both countries. The north transept stained glass windows, some dating to the 12th century, are among Alsace's oldest.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Maison du Sel, an imposing 1448 edifice in the center of Wissembourg, was originally a bourgeois hospital then a salt warehouse—a lucrative monopoly in medieval times. Its monumental two-pitched roof and massive proportions make it one of the Bas-Rhin's most remarkable civic buildings. It can be visited from the outside.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Bruch quarter runs along the Lauter, the river that today marks the Franco-German border. The 16th-century Maison de l'Ami Fritz reflects in the water—this picturesque image is worth the detour, especially in late afternoon when the light is warm. Heading back toward the ramparts, the medieval towers that still survive recall that Wissembourg was a Decapolis member since 1354.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Walk Wissembourg's green belt along its ramparts. The promenade on the former moats, now public gardens, passes several medieval watchtowers still standing. It's a pleasant 45-minute walk that circles the old town.

⏱ 60 min

About twenty kilometers from Wissembourg, the Schoenenbourg fortification is the largest Maginot Line fortification open to the public. This underground fort, built between 1931 and 1935, could shelter 630 men and protect 17 km of front. Its galleries extend 3 km underground—1,500 meters accessible to visitors. Absolutely bring warm clothing: the interior temperature is 12°C year-round. Open seasonally only.

⏱ 120 min

The German border and Palatinate vineyards are a few kilometers away. Local operators organize tastings of Alsatian crémants and wines from the Wissembourg vineyard, which has produced for decades as an extension of Alsatian vines.

⏱ 60 min
