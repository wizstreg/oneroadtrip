Epirus & Zagori: Gorges, Stone Villages and the River of the Underworld

📅 8 days📍 8 stops🚗 200 km⏱ 4h20

Ioannina → Monodendri → Papigo → Konitsa → Preveza

Epirus is the Greece nobody knows: no islands, no postcard beaches, but breathtaking gorges, UNESCO-listed stone villages, a lake haunted by Ali Pasha, and the real River of the Underworld from Greek mythology. This 8-day route connects Ioannina to Preveza via Zagori and the Vikos Gorge—listed in the Guinness Book as the world's deepest canyon relative to its width—before ending at the ruins of Nikopolis, the city founded by Augustus after his victory over Mark Antony.

✨ Highlights
  • Vikos Gorge: listed in the Guinness Book as the world's deepest canyon relative to its width, with walls reaching 1,000 m
  • Ioannina: the only city in Greece with three mosques, tied to Ali Pasha who was assassinated on the lake island in 1822
  • Zagori stone bridges: 60 surviving arched bridges out of 160 built in the 18th-19th centuries by Epirote master masons
  • Acheron River: mythological river identified in antiquity as the entrance to Hades, which actually rises in Epirus
  • Nikopolis: founded in 31 BC by Augustus after his victory at Actium over Mark Antony and Cleopatra, Greece's largest ancient city
📆 Best period

April, May, June, September, October

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 2 and 6 days.

🌍 What to discover in GR?

Ancient civilization birthplace with stunning islands, incredible history, and Mediterranean lifestyle. Experience mythology sites, beautiful beaches, and authentic Greek culture. The country offers cultural depth with natural beauty and culinary excellence. Greece provides historical significance with island paradise and cultural authenticity in Mediterranean setting.

📋 Important notes

Zagori is a mountain region: winding roads, snow possible from October to May. Some villages (Vradeto, Papigo) are isolated—check road conditions in winter. Vikos Gorge is accessible from April to November.

Day 1

Ioannina

Where Byzantine ramparts meet Ottoman minarets on the shores of a lake haunted by Ali Pasha's ghost

★★★☆☆
📍 Kastro Ioannina fortress📍 Aslan Pasha Mosque Ioannina📍 Its Kale citadel Ioannina📍 Nissi island Lake Pamvotis Ioannina📍 Byzantine Museum Ioannina
1

The Kastro of Ioannina is one of the few Byzantine fortresses in Greece still inhabited today. Originally built by Emperor Justinian in the 6th century on a rocky promontory overlooking Lake Pamvotis, it has been extensively remodeled by successive rulers—Byzantines, Normans, Serbs, Venetians, and Ottomans. Enter through the Agios Georgios gate, redesigned by the Ottomans in 1843; to your left stands Bohemond of Taranto's tower, built by the Norman crusader in 1082. Allow at least two hours to lose yourself in the cobbled lanes.

⏱ 120 min
2

Inside the Its Kale citadel, the Fethiye Mosque ('of the Conquest') was built when the Ottomans captured Ioannina in 1430, then renovated by Ali Pasha in 1795. Next to it stands the tomb of Ali Pasha himself, a tyrant as charismatic as he was ruthless, who ruled a quasi-independent state covering much of Greece and Albania from 1787 to 1822—before being assassinated by the sultan. Macabre detail: only his body is buried here; his severed head was sent to Constantinople as proof of death.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Aslan Pasha Mosque, built in 1618 by an Albanian pasha, has housed the Ethnographic Museum of Ioannina since 1933. For centuries, Jews, Christians, and Muslims coexisted in Ioannina—the city's Romaniote Jewish community was one of the oldest in Greece. The museum presents artifacts from these communities, including liturgical objects from the nearby synagogue, almost entirely destroyed during the Occupation.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

From the quay at Mavili Square, ferries depart every 15 minutes to Nissi, the lake island (10-minute crossing). Walk through its peaceful lanes lined with lake trout tavernas, then visit the Agios Panteleimon monastery where Ali Pasha met his death in 1822—the monks had hidden him in a ground-floor room when the sultan's troops surrounded the island. The adjacent museum reconstructs his life and final moments.

⏱ 120 min

In the evening, stroll through the shopping streets of the old town around the Kastro: Ioannina has been the capital of Epirote silversmithing for centuries—this is where Sotirios Voulgaris, founder of Bulgari, was born. Silver shops still line the alleyways. End the evening on the lakeside terraces, with views of the illuminated Kastro and its reflections in the water.

⏱ 90 min

Day 2

Ioannina

Where Byzantine ramparts meet Ottoman minarets on the shores of a lake haunted by Ali Pasha's ghost

★★★☆☆
📍 Archaeological site Dodona Ioannina📍 Ancient theater Dodona📍 Perama Cave Ioannina📍 Archaeological Museum Ioannina
1

The site of Dodona lies 22 km south of Ioannina and is well worth the drive. It's one of the oldest oracular sanctuaries in Greece, dedicated to Zeus and the mother goddess Dione—its oracle was renowned throughout the Greek world as early as the 6th century BC, long before Delphi. Priests interpreted the will of the gods through the rustling leaves of a sacred oak tree. The theater, which could seat 25,000 spectators, is one of the largest in Greece. The site is remarkably uncrowded—you'll likely have it to yourself outside July-August.

⏱ 90 min
2

Perama Cave, 4 km from central Ioannina, was discovered by chance in 1940 by locals seeking shelter during the bombings. It extends for 1 km in length over an area of 14,800 m², with chambers whose ceilings are covered in stalactites and stalagmites formed over 1.5 million years. The bones of a cave bear from the Pleistocene era were found inside.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Archaeological Museum of Ioannina, housed in Litharitsia Park, presents a collection spanning prehistory to the Roman era for all of Epirus. Don't miss the objects from Dodona—bronze tablets engraved with questions posed to the oracle, votive figurines, and coins from the time of Pyrrhus, the king of Epirus whose costly victories against Rome gave us the expression 'Pyrrhic victory.'

⏱ 60 min

In the evening, enjoy the buzz of Ioannina: the city is home to several universities, including medical and humanities faculties ranked among the best in Greece. The streets of Anexartisias and Kallari, between the old Bazaar quarter and the lake shores, come alive after 9pm. Sample Ioannina's baklava—the city is one of the Greek capitals of this phyllo pastry layered with pistachios.

⏱ 90 min

Day 3

Monodendri

A stone village perched at the precipice of the world's deepest canyon

★★★★☆
📍 Agia Paraskevi Monastery Monodendri📍 Oxia viewpoint Monodendri📍 Vikos Gorge from Monodendri📍 Monodendri village Zagori📍 Stone forest Oxia Zagori
1

Monodendri perches at 1,060 meters altitude at the southern entrance to the Vikos Gorge. The Agia Paraskevi monastery, clinging to the cliff face 800 m from the village via a paved path, was built in 1412 by local lord Michael Therianos in gratitude to the saint for curing his daughter. From its terraces, the gorge floor—900 meters below—stares straight back at you. Take care: there's no guardrail at the precipice edge.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Oxia viewpoint, 7.5 km north of the village via a drivable track, offers the best view of the gorge from the Monodendri side. On the way, you pass through the 'stone forest'—a field of limestone columns eroded over millennia, some reaching several meters high. Once at the viewpoint, you gaze straight down into the vertiginous chasm: the narrowest point of the gorge is barely a few hundred meters wide and over 1,000 m deep.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

For a first taste of the gorge without crossing it completely, descend the trail from Monodendri to the Voidomatis riverbed via the old kalderimi (paved path). The descent takes 1h30, the river is at the bottom—ice-cold water even in August (rarely above 8°C). The return via the same path is more demanding. The turquoise karst springs halfway down make a perfect rest stop.

⏱ 180 min

Late in the day, wander the lanes of Monodendri and neighboring villages Vitsa and Ano Pedina. All houses are built from local stone and roofed with stone slabs—Zagori's designation as a national historical monument prohibits any other materials. Tavernas serve pites (savory pies) with wild mountain mushrooms and grilled local lamb.

⏱ 90 min

Day 4

Monodendri

A stone village perched at the precipice of the world's deepest canyon

★★★★☆
📍 Vikos Gorge hiking Monodendri📍 Voidomatis springs Zagori📍 Vikos village Zagori📍 Kapesovo Zagori📍 Vradeto steps Kapesovo
1

The Vikos Gorge is listed in the Guinness Book of Records as the world's deepest canyon relative to its width: 20 km long, walls rising to 1,000 meters, and a width sometimes reduced to just a few hundred meters. The classic trail descends from Monodendri to the village of Vikos along the Voidomatis River—allow 5 to 7 hours depending on pace. Vikos-Aoos National Park, which encompasses the gorge, is a UNESCO Geopark hosting over 1,800 plant species and 186 bird species, including golden eagles and griffon vultures.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Vradeto steps, starting from Kapesovo, are a masterpiece of engineering: roughly 1,100 steps carved into the cliff face of the Mezaria canyon over a 400-meter elevation gain. Until 1970, this was the only access to the village of Vradeto, perched at 1,340 meters—the highest village in Zagori. The ascent takes 45 minutes to an hour and offers increasingly spectacular views of the gorge.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The complete traverse of Vikos Gorge from Monodendri to the village of Vikos is one of Greece's great hikes. The O12 trail is marked but requires good fitness and hiking boots. Once at the bottom, you follow the Voidomatis between limestone walls covered with forests. Stop at the Voidomatis karst springs: water surges from the limestone in an unreal turquoise blue, perfect for an ice-cold swim after the effort. It's possible to arrange a return transfer from Vikos village.

⏱ 360 min

Day 5

Papigo

Twin hamlets in the shadow of limestone towers, where mountain rivers carve turquoise pools

★★★★☆
📍 Megalo Papigo village Zagori📍 Mikro Papigo village Zagori📍 Ovires natural pools Papigo📍 Astraka Towers Papigo📍 Kipi bridge Kalogeriko Zagori
1

Megalo Papigo and Mikro Papigo are the two most photographed villages in Zagori, separated by 3 km via a hiking trail. Their cobbled lanes, stone-and-slate houses, and centuries-old plane trees on the central square are perfect examples of the Epirote architecture classified as a UNESCO World Heritage site. Look up and you'll see the Astraka Towers dominating the landscape—vertical limestone columns connecting the summit of Mount Tymfi (2,497 m) to the Vikos Gorge.

⏱ 60 min
2

Near Kipi, the Kalogeriko (or Plakidas) bridge is the iconic three-arched bridge of Zagori. Built in 1814 by Abbot Seraphim from the local monastery to replace a wooden bridge, it spans the Voidomatis River at 56 meters long and 6.7 meters high. Viewed from the surrounding hills, its three successive arches give it the appearance of a caterpillar in motion—a nickname that stuck. A few minutes away, the Kokkoris bridge, built in 1750, is framed by two sheer rock walls: the most spectacular setting in the region.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Between Megalo and Mikro Papigo, the trail connecting the two villages passes by the Ovires (or Kolymbithres)—natural pools carved into the limestone rock by the Rogovo River. Legend has it that Ali Pasha himself came here to bathe. In summer, the water is cool and the cascading pools offer a unique swim. The trail easily loops the two villages in 2 hours.

⏱ 120 min

On the return route, do the Kipi bridges loop on foot (10 km, 3 hours) to discover six single-arch stone bridges. The Kipi and Koukouli area has the densest concentration of stone bridges in all of Zagori—every configuration is represented: one, two, and three arches. These bridges were the only link between villages until the first paved roads arrived in the 1950s.

⏱ 180 min

Day 6

Konitsa

Where the wildest rivers in Europe converge beneath a single-arch bridge built to defy gravity

★★★★★
📍 Stone bridge Konitsa Aoos river📍 Panagia Stomiou Monastery Konitsa📍 Aoos Gorge Konitsa📍 Konitsa village Epirus
1

Konitsa is built amphitheater-style on a Pindus slope, overlooking the confluence of the Aoos and Voidomatis rivers. Its stone bridge is one of the largest single-arch bridges in the Balkans: 20 meters high, nearly 40 meters span, built between 1823 and 1871 by master mason Zioga Frontzo from the village of Pyrsoyianni. Cross it on foot—the wind blows hard at the arch's summit, and the masons hung a small bell underneath to keep rhythm during construction.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Panagia Stomiou monastery sits 5 km downstream from Konitsa bridge, clinging to the rock wall of the Aoos Gorge at 650 meters altitude. Built in 1774, it was inhabited for several decades by monk Paisios, who lived there as a hermit before joining Mount Athos. The trail leading to it follows the turquoise river in a grandiose setting—swimmers bathe there in summer, though the water is ice-cold and the current strong.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Aoos River below Konitsa is one of Europe's last wild rivers: clear waters, pebble beds, steep gorges. In summer, local operators offer rafting on the rapids downstream from the bridge—a 2 to 3-hour descent suitable for beginners. If you prefer a calmer approach, kayaking on the peaceful stretches lets you observe the cliffs and riverside vegetation from the water.

⏱ 150 min

Day 7

Preveza

An Ionian harbor town seven kilometers from the ruins of Augustus's greatest triumph

★★☆☆☆
📍 Acheron River Glyki Epirus📍 Necromanteion Ephyra Mesopotamos📍 Acheron gorge hiking📍 Acheron rafting Epirus
1

The Acheron is one of the five rivers of Hades in Greek mythology—and the only one that actually rises in the world of the living, in Epirus. For the ancient Greeks, this river that plunged into karst chasms before reappearing near the Ionian Sea could only be the boundary between the world of the living and the dead. Homer sends Odysseus to consult it in the Odyssey to question the seer Tiresias. The village of Glyki is the starting point for activities on the river.

⏱ 30 min
2

A few kilometers from Mesopotamos, a hill bears the ruins of the Necromanteion of Ephyra—identified since 1958 as the oracle of the dead described by Homer in the Odyssey, though this identification remains debated among archaeologists. According to the site's interpretation, pilgrims descended into an underground labyrinth after purification and sacrifice rituals to 'receive' the souls of their dead. The 4th-century BC ruins are worth the visit, whatever interpretation you give them.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The hike through the Acheron Gorge from Glyki is one of the most beautiful water hikes in Greece. The trail follows the river for 8 km round trip between limestone walls covered with plane trees and oleander. The water is transparent and cool—swimming possible in the natural pools along the way. Operators also offer canoe descent from Glyki, physically easier and equally spectacular.

⏱ 180 min

Day 8

Preveza

An Ionian harbor town seven kilometers from the ruins of Augustus's greatest triumph

★★☆☆☆
📍 Archaeological site Nikopolis Preveza📍 Monument of Augustus Actium Nikopolis📍 Archaeological Museum Nikopolis📍 Roman ramparts Nikopolis📍 Byzantine basilica Nikopolis📍 Preveza port Epirus
1

Nikopolis—'the city of victory'—was founded in 31 BC by Octavian Augustus on the very hill where he had pitched his camp during the naval Battle of Actium against Mark Antony and Cleopatra. It's the largest ancient city in Greece at 900 hectares. Its Victory Monument still bears anchor-shaped notches where bronze rams from ships captured from the enemy were sealed—it had 36 in all. This is also where Epictetus, the slave-philosopher founder of Stoicism, taught until 125 AD.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Archaeological Museum of Nikopolis, inaugurated in 2009, is an essential introduction before exploring the site. It presents collections chronologically: from the Battle of Actium to the city's abandonment. Don't miss the early Christian mosaics from the basilicas—some are of exceptional quality and preservation—and fragments of Augustus's votive inscription, 54 meters long.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Preveza, 7 km south of Nikopolis, is a quietly charming Ionian port town. In the evening, the pedestrian Spiliadou street comes alive around its fish and seafood tavernas—the Ambracian Gulf before you is the same one where the fate of the Roman world was decided on September 2, 31 BC. Aktion airport is 20 km away, across the strait.

⏱ 120 min
