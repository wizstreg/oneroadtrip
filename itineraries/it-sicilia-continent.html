Complete Tour of Sicily

📅 14 days📍 14 stops🚗 639 km⏱ 11h25

Palermo → Monreale → Trapani → Agrigento → Piazza Armerina → Ragusa → Noto → Siracusa → Catania → Taormina → Messina

Palermo → Monreale → Trapani → Agrigento → Piazza Armerina → Ragusa → Noto → Siracusa → Catania → Taormina → Messina

Begin in Palermo with its Arab-Norman Byzantine mosaics, then journey south to Agrigento's Greek temples—the finest preserved outside Greece. The Baroque interior of Ragusa, Noto, and Piazza Armerina precedes Syracuse, founded in 734 BC. Mount Etna, Europe's highest active volcano, and Taormina's sea-view terrace complete this grand island circuit.

Day 1

Palermo

1

Palermo Cathedral layers five architectural styles across seven centuries: Norman, Arab, Catalan Gothic, Baroque, and Neoclassical. Its southwest tower bears a Koranic inscription carved into marble—a remnant from the Arab period (827-1072). The imperial tombs house the remains of Frederick II of Hohenstaufen and Roger II of Sicily.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Quattro Canti, an octagonal crossroads built between 1608 and 1620 by the Spanish viceroy, divides Palermo into four mandamenti (districts). Each façade features a fountain, one of the four seasons, a Spanish king, and a neighborhood patron saint.

⏱ 20 min
3

The Pretoria Fountain, commissioned in 1554 for a Florentine villa then reassembled in Palermo in 1573, contains 644 sculpted elements depicting river deities and sea monsters. Local clergy nicknamed it the "Fountain of Shame" due to its nude figures.

⏱ 20 min
4

La Martorana, founded in 1143 by Admiral George of Antioch, preserves Sicily's oldest Norman mosaics, including one depicting Roger II crowned by Christ—a unique image of a Norman king in Byzantine imperial dress.

⏱ 45 min
5

Ballarò Market, Palermo's largest street market, sprawls through several alleys of the Albergheria quarter and dates back to Arab times. Vendors shout their wares in Sicilian-Arabic dialect while stalls overflow with local produce and North African spices.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Palermo street food tasting at Ballarò Market: panelle (chickpea fritters), crocchè (potato croquettes), sfincione (thick anchovy pizza), and arancini.

⏱ 60 min

Evening stroll through La Vucciria, Palermo's oldest market (14th century), which transforms at night into a buzzing scene of street food vendors and wine bars.

⏱ 90 min
Day 2

Palermo

1

The Norman Palace, seat of the Sicilian Parliament since 1947, was erected by Arab emirs in the 9th century then expanded by Roger II in 1130. Its Palatine Chapel gleams with 6,340 square meters of Byzantine mosaics on gold ground—the most accomplished synthesis of Arab-Norman-Byzantine art in Europe.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Capuchin Catacombs, excavated beneath the convent in 1599, display 8,000 mummies dressed in period clothing along underground corridors. Little Rosalia Lombardo, who died in 1920, remains so perfectly preserved that biochemists studied her until 2009 to unlock the embalming formula.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Teatro Massimo, inaugurated in 1897, is Italy's largest opera house and Europe's third largest. Its neoclassical Doric façade serves as the final scene in Coppola's Godfather III. Its acoustics, among the world's finest, result from the elliptical shape of the hall.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Visit Palazzo Abatellis, a 15th-century Catalan-Gothic palace converted into Sicily's regional art museum. It houses the Triumph of Death, an anonymous 15th-century fresco depicting a skeletal horseman mowing down nobility—one of medieval Italy's most powerful works.

⏱ 75 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 8 km · 20min
Day 3

Monreale

1

Monreale Cathedral, built by William II between 1172 and 1189, shelters 6,340 square meters of Byzantine mosaics—the largest mosaic surface in the world after Istanbul's Hagia Sophia. The 130 scenes from the Old and New Testaments were created by Constantinople craftsmen under the direction of Benedictine monks.

⏱ 90 min
2

Monreale's cloister, formed by 228 twin columns—no two identical—blends mosaics, Arab interlacing, lion heads, and Apocalypse scenes. The southwest corner houses an Arab fountain with a twisted column, the sole remnant of the original Norman royal palace's Islamic architecture.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Lunch at the trattorias surrounding the cathedral with views over the Conca d'Oro—the golden basin of orange and lemon groves encircling Palermo—before driving to Trapani (100 km, ~1h40).

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 100 km · 1h40
Day 4

Trapani

1

Trapani stretches along a sickle-shaped promontory—its name derives from the Greek Drepanon (sickle). Its old town preserves Arab streets aligned parallel to the prevailing wind and 17th-century Baroque palaces. The port, 160 km from North Africa, served for centuries as the maritime gateway to Tunisia.

⏱ 60 min
2

Trapani's salt pans have operated continuously since Phoenician antiquity. The 18th-century windmills, now national monuments, pumped seawater through the evaporation basins. Trapani salt has been protected by European PGI status since 2018.

⏱ 60 min
3

Erice, a medieval town perched at 751 meters, was founded by the Elymians—a pre-Greek people of Anatolian origin according to Thucydides—then successively occupied by Carthaginians, Romans, Arabs, Normans, and Spanish. Its 70 churches for 700 inhabitants make it Sicily's most church-dense town.

⏱ 120 min
4

Marsala, 30 km south, is renowned for its fortified wine discovered by Englishman John Woodhouse in 1773. It's also where Garibaldi landed on May 11, 1860 with his Thousand to conquer the Kingdom of the Two Sicilies.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Marsala DOC wine tasting at Florio or Pellegrino cellars, the two historic producers founded in the 19th century—available in dry, semi-dry, or sweet styles.

⏱ 90 min

Sunset over the salt pans from Nubia's viewpoint: the low-angle light turns the basins pink and orange, with windmills silhouetted against the sky.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 126 km · 1h50
Day 5

Agrigento

1

Agrigento's Valley of the Temples, founded in 580 BC as Akragas, contains the largest ensemble of Doric temples outside Greece. Pindar described it as "the most beautiful of mortal cities." The UNESCO site sprawls across 1,300 hectares.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Temple of Concordia, built around 430 BC, ranks among the world's best-preserved Greek temples. Its survival stems from its conversion into a Christian church in the 6th century. All 34 columns, standing 6.70 meters tall, retain their original golden hue.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Temple of Hera Lacinia, at the ridge's eastern end, dominates the valley from its 25 partially standing columns. The charring marks on its stones date from the fire set by Carthaginians during the city's sack in 406 BC.

⏱ 45 min
4

The Temple of Olympian Zeus, erected after the victory over Carthage in 480 BC, was the largest Doric temple ever attempted: 113 by 56 meters, never completed. One of its telamones—7.65-meter atlas figures—lies reconstructed on the ground.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Lunch on Via Atenea with macco di fave—dried fava bean soup with olive oil and wild fennel, an Agrigento dish of Greek origin.

⏱ 75 min
Day 6

Agrigento

1

Agrigento's Regional Archaeological Museum houses the reconstructed telamon from the Temple of Zeus (7.65 m tall) and an exceptional collection of Greek vases, coins, and sculptures. Its topographical section illuminates the complete urban layout of ancient Akragas.

⏱ 75 min
2

Agrigento's Hellenistic-Roman quarter, discovered in 1950, reveals a perfectly gridded street plan with perpendicular roads 6 meters wide, underground drainage systems, and the foundations of atrium houses from the 2nd and 1st centuries BC.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Excursion to Caltagirone (40 km): its 142 steps of the Santa Maria del Monte Staircase are covered in ceramic tiles illustrating each century of the town's history. Caltagirone ceramics are classified as UNESCO intangible heritage.

⏱ 180 min

Night visit to the Valley of the Temples on Mondays and Fridays from May to October: the illuminated temples create an extraordinary atmosphere.

⏱ 120 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 88 km · 1h30
Day 7

Piazza Armerina

1

The Villa Romana del Casale, 5 km south of Piazza Armerina, shelters 3,500 square meters of in-situ 4th-century Roman mosaics—the world's largest surface. Its hunting scenes, athletes, and the famous "bikini girls"—ten women exercising—represent a unique depiction in ancient art. UNESCO-listed since 1997.

⏱ 120 min
2

Piazza Armerina, founded by Normans in the 11th century, preserves a Baroque center with its blue-domed cathedral dominating the hill. The town counted 40 noble families in the 17th century for just 5,000 inhabitants—hence its nickname "city of one hundred thousand."

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Lunch in the upper town with pasta al ragù di maiale nero dei Nebrodi—black pig from the Nebrodi mountains, an Iberian breed raised semi-wild in Sicily's interior forests.

⏱ 75 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 75 km · 1h25
Day 8

Ragusa

1

Ragusa Ibla, rebuilt after the 1693 earthquake, is one of eight Val di Noto towns inscribed on the UNESCO list in 2002. Its golden limestone Baroque palaces and sloping alleys form one of Italy's most cohesive urban ensembles.

⏱ 60 min
2

Ibla's San Giorgio Cathedral, Rosario Gagliardi's masterpiece completed in 1775, soars in three superimposed orders across 43 meters above a monumental staircase. Gagliardi, self-taught, knew Roman Baroque only through engravings.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Ponte dei Cappuccini offers plunging views over Ibla's brown tile roofs and wooded ravines. This bridge provides the vantage point for the town's iconic photographs.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Evening stroll to the Giardini Iblei, a public garden suspended at the ravine's edge with views of the town's three Baroque bell towers.

⏱ 75 min

Tasting of Modica chocolate—prepared cold without butter or milk according to the Aztec recipe transmitted by the Spanish in the 16th century—and Ragusano DOP cheese.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 50 km · 55min
Day 9

Noto

1

Noto, rebuilt from scratch after the 1693 earthquake, represents Sicilian Baroque's crowning achievement. Its Corso Vittorio Emanuele, a 700-meter artery oriented east-west to capture evening light, is lined with palaces and churches whose local limestone façades turn honey-gold at sunset.

⏱ 90 min
2

Palazzo Nicolaci di Villadorata, built in 1737, boasts Sicily's most ornate façade: its six balconies rest on consoles sculpted as horses, lions, mermaids, and chimeras.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Visit to the Vendicari Nature Reserve, 12 km away, with its salt lagoons, ruins of a 9th-century Arab tonnara (tuna fishery), and fine sandy beaches accessible on foot.

⏱ 180 min

Lunch in Marzamemi, a tuna-fishing village founded by Arabs in the 10th century, with its low lava-stone houses surrounding the piazza.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 32 km · 40min
Day 10

Siracusa

1

Ortygia, a 1-square-kilometer island linked to Sicily by two bridges, forms the ancient heart of Syracuse, founded by Corinthian colonists in 734 BC. Cicero described it as "the greatest and most beautiful of all Greek cities." Its alleyways layer Greek foundations, Arab arches, Baroque façades, and 18th-century balconies.

⏱ 90 min
2

Syracuse Cathedral, Baroque since 1728, incorporates the Doric columns of the former 5th-century BC Temple of Athena: twelve 8.70-meter columns remain visible within the nave walls. One of the world's rare cases of an ancient temple converted into a mosque then a church without destruction.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Fountain of Arethusa, a freshwater spring bubbling meters from the sea on Ortygia, is surrounded by papyrus—the only papyrus growing naturally in Europe outside Egypt.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Morning wander through Ortygia Market, then lunch with arancini al ragù and granita di mandorla (almond granita) at a historic bar on Via Roma.

⏱ 90 min

Swimming at Punta Castelluccio beach south of Ortygia, a 15-minute walk, with views of the old town walls.

⏱ 120 min
Day 11

Siracusa

1

Syracuse's Greek Theatre, carved from the Temenite rock in the 5th century BC, seats 15,000 spectators. Aeschylus premiered The Persians here in 476 BC in the presence of tyrant Hieron I. Each spring it hosts a festival of ancient plays performed in Greek—one of the few worldwide staged in an intact ancient theatre.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Ear of Dionysius, a 5th-century BC quarry, is an S-shaped cave 65 meters deep with extraordinary acoustics. Caravaggio, visiting in 1608, gave it this name, imagining the tyrant imprisoned captives here to eavesdrop on their amplified conversations.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Altar of Hieron II, 198 meters long, is the largest altar in the ancient world, built in the 3rd century BC. During the Eleutheria festival, 450 oxen were sacrificed here in a single day.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Visit to the Paolo Orsi Archaeological Museum, Sicily's richest museum of prehistory and archaeology, featuring the Venus Anadyomene of Syracuse—a 5th-century BC marble fragment considered one of the finest preserved Greek sculptures.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 75 km · 1h20
Day 12

Catania

1

Catania, rebuilt in black Etna lava after the 1693 earthquake, is Sicily's second city. Its Baroque center in white limestone contrasts with black basalt paving—a chromatic pairing unique in Europe. Vaccarini's Piazza del Duomo ranks as Sicily's finest Baroque square.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Fountain of the Elephant, the city's symbol since 1736, depicts an 8th-century BC basalt elephant bearing a 2nd-century Egyptian obelisk. Legend claims it protects the city from Etna's eruptions.

⏱ 20 min
3

La Pescheria market, behind the cathedral, is Sicily's most animated fish market. From dawn, whole tuna, swordfish, sea urchins, clams, and octopus sprawl across ice-laden stalls. The vocal and olfactory assault makes it one of the Mediterranean's most intense market experiences.

⏱ 60 min
4

The 2nd-century AD Roman theatre, partially buried within the Baroque houses of the Cruyllas quarter, presents a unique case of an ancient theatre absorbed by the modern city. Its adjacent odeon served for rehearsals and lectures.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Lunch at La Pescheria with spaghetti alle vongole or fried calamari, followed by granita di limone e brioche—a typical Sicilian breakfast also enjoyed as dessert in Catania.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 30 km · 45min
Day 13

Taormina

1

Mount Etna, at 3,357 meters, is Europe's highest active volcano. Its eruptive activity has been documented since 425 BC. Parco dell'Etna, created in 1987 across 59,000 hectares, encompasses over 300 secondary cones.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Funivia dell'Etna cable car ascends from Rifugio Sapienza (1,923 m) to 2,500 meters in 15 minutes. From there, the summit craters are accessible on foot in 2 hours round-trip, with a guide mandatory above 2,900 meters.

⏱ 240 min
3

Taormina's Greek Theatre, built in the 3rd century BC and remodeled by the Romans, is Sicily's second-largest ancient theatre. Its unique feature worldwide: the stage frames smoking Mount Etna 30 km away and the Bay of Naxos, Greece's first Sicilian colony.

⏱ 75 min
4

Isola Bella, a rock connected to the coast by a natural sand bar, remained private property until 1990. Its botanical garden shelters rare Mediterranean species and its waters form a nature reserve.

⏱ 90 min
5

Corso Umberto I, a 500-meter medieval artery, preserves 14th-century Gothic palaces. Piazza IX Aprile with its terrace overlooking the sea and Etna constitutes one of Sicily's most celebrated panoramas.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hike in the Valle del Bove, a collapsed caldera measuring 7 by 5 km at 1,800 meters altitude, ringed by 1,000-meter walls. Historic lava flows layered here read like geological strata.

⏱ 240 min

Tasting of Etna DOC wines (red Nerello Mascalese, white Carricante) at cantine in Randazzo or Linguaglossa—vineyards at 800-1,000 meters on volcanic soil.

⏱ 90 min

Swimming at Mazzarò beach, accessible by cable car from Taormina (5 minutes, ~€3 round-trip). Pebble beach nestled in a cove with views of Isola Bella.

⏱ 150 min

Excursion to Giardini Naxos, 4 km downhill, site of Sicily's first Greek foundation (734 BC) with a museum of Corinthian ceramics.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 55 km · 1h
Day 14

Messina

1

Messina Cathedral, rebuilt after the 1908 earthquake and 1943 bombings, preserves its bell tower housing the world's largest mechanical astronomical clock, built by Strasbourg's Ungerer in 1933. Each day at noon, its automatons—the lion of Messina, the rooster, death, the Three Kings—perform for 15 minutes.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Strait of Messina, 3 km wide at its narrowest point between Sicily and Calabria, ranks among the world's busiest maritime passages with 200,000 vessels annually. Its opposing currents create the ancient Charybdis—a whirlpool identified by Homer in the Odyssey. The Calabrian coast is visible to the naked eye from the quays.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Regional Museum of Messina preserves two Caravaggios painted during the artist's 1609 Sicilian sojourn—The Adoration of the Shepherds and The Raising of Lazarus—commissioned by local confraternities. These works rank among the master's final paintings, created one year before his death.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Seafood lunch featuring fish caught in the strait: pesce spada (swordfish) alla messinese—with capers, olives, tomatoes, and pine nuts—a specialty exclusive to the strait where swordfish migrate from May to August.

⏱ 90 min
