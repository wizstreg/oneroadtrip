Sevilla

★★★★★
1

Built in 1401 on the site of the former Almohad grand mosque, Seville's Gothic cathedral is the world's largest according to UNESCO. La Giralda, its former minaret, rises 98 meters—you climb it via a ramp, not stairs, so the muezzins could ride up on horseback. Book tickets two days ahead: queues without reservations will eat up an entire morning.

⏱ 120 min
2

Book before you unpack: the Alcázar often sells out. Built in the 10th-11th centuries by the Umayyads, rebuilt by Peter I in 1364, it's one of Europe's oldest royal palaces still in active use, UNESCO-listed in 1987. The 7-hectare gardens were still the Spanish royal family's summer residence until recently. Go early morning for crowd-free photos.

⏱ 90 min
3

Squeezed between the cathedral and the Alcázar, the General Archive of the Indies (1785) houses 80 million pages of original colonial documents—Magellan's handwritten maps, Columbus's letters, the founding charters of American cities. Entry is free. Non-historians spend 30 minutes: for the building alone (Herrera, same architect as El Escorial), it's worth the detour.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The scent of azahar (orange blossom) floods Santa Cruz as evening falls. This is where the best flamenco tablaos hold their performances, and where tapas bars fire up around 9 p.m. Then cross the Triana bridge for drinks facing the Guadalquivir—a more local, less touristy neighborhood than Santa Cruz.

⏱ 120 min

The Torre del Oro (13th century) reflects in the Guadalquivir at sunset—the naval museum inside is small but honest, €3. It's mainly an excuse for a stroll along the river to the Triana market, where Andalusian ceramics can be bought directly from artisans. Skip the tourist shops on the quayside.

⏱ 60 min

Jerez De La Frontera

★★★★☆
1

Locals simply call it 'la bodega'. González Byass, founded in 1835, has been producing Tío Pepe continuously since 1844. The bodega complex covers 374,000 m² visible from several points in the city. La Concha's dome, built for Queen Isabella's visit in 1862 by Gustave Eiffel's firm, is a detail guides rarely mention—ask. Allow 1.5 hours, tasting included, around €20.

⏱ 90 min
2

Fifty Andalusian horses that dance: kitsch on paper, stunning in practice. The Royal Andalusian School of Equestrian Art presents 'How the Andalusian Horses Dance' on Tuesdays and Thursdays (sometimes Fridays), from €21. Morning rehearsals (on non-performance days) are cheaper and just as impressive. Book several weeks ahead essential.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Alcázar of Jerez, built by the Almohads in the 12th century, conceals within its walls an almost intact octagonal mosque converted into a chapel. The castle's camera obscura—installed in the watchtower—projects a live view of the city, like in Cádiz. Entry around €6, not crowded: you can spend an hour virtually alone.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

If you hit a Thursday evening, the barrio Santiago—birthplace of Jerez's gypsy flamenco—comes alive in local peñas. The tablao Puro Arte (in a 1739 bodega) offers the staged version, intense and honest. For flamenco not prepared for tourists, ask at your hotel about weekend peñas in Santiago: some nights are open to the public.

⏱ 90 min

Tabancos are Jerez's sherry bars—standing room, glass of fino at the counter, half-cellar half-bistro atmosphere. El Pasaje, open since 1925, is considered the oldest still in business. Fino by the glass for €2, served cold. Nothing to order, nothing to choose: the barman knows.

⏱ 45 min

Cadiz

★★★★☆
1

The golden dome blazes in the sun from any lookout in the city. Cádiz's Baroque cathedral, built from 1722 to 1838, watches over a peninsula surrounded by the Atlantic on three sides. Cádiz claims the title of the oldest city in the West—founded around 1100 BC by the Phoenicians under the name Gadir. On Mondays, several museums close: avoid.

⏱ 60 min
2

Opened in 1994, Spain's first camera obscura in the Torre Tavira (18th century). The device projects a live 360° view of the city onto a concave disc in a darkened room—striking effect. The terrace offers the most complete panorama of the rooftops and bay. Allow 1 hour, around €7. Guides available in French.

⏱ 60 min
3

Beneath everyone's feet, 3,000 years of history: the Museo de Cádiz houses two Phoenician anthropoid marble sarcophagi (5th-4th century BC), the only examples of their kind in Spain. Upstairs, a Zurbarán collection. Free entry for EU citizens. The rooms are quiet—tourists walk past without stopping.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Better than restaurant lunch: Cádiz's Central Market, beneath its neoclassical Doric portico from 1838. Tortillitas de camarones (tiny shrimp fritters) are eaten standing, scalding hot, with a glass of fino. Pescaíto frito (fried fish) is ordered by weight. Arrive before 1 p.m. to avoid weekend crowds.

⏱ 60 min

Head down to La Caleta for sunset. This cove nestled between two castles (Santa Catalina and San Sebastián) is the Gaditanos' historic beach—and backdrop for several scenes in the James Bond film Die Another Day (2002). Swimming possible outside summer, little frequented in the morning.

⏱ 60 min

Vejer De La Frontera

★★★☆☆
1

Two hundred meters of elevation to see Africa: on clear days from Vejer's ramparts, the Moroccan coastline appears. The village, listed as a historic site in 1976, is a defensive Moorish maze—the barred windows and blind interior courtyards betray its Arab heritage more clearly than any explanatory panel. Don't confuse it with Vejer Playa, 10 km below.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Gothic-Mudéjar church was built on the site of the village's former mosque, retaining its minaret converted into a bell tower. The Plaza de España, with its fountain covered in Sevillian azulejos (1955 tilework), is the village's center of gravity. The old Jewish quarter (judería), accessed through the Arco de la Puerta Cerrada, deserves 30 minutes of wandering.

⏱ 60 min
3

Four restored windmills dominate the ridge above the village—remnants of an era when Vejer processed wheat from the La Janda plain. The path along the ramparts to the mills takes 20 minutes and offers the best view of the sea and countryside. Early morning, the light is poster-worthy.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Almadraba bluefin tuna melts differently than any other tuna. Caught between April and June off Zahara and Barbate using a 3,000-year-old Phoenician technique, it arrives fresh in Vejer's restaurants. El Jardín del Califa (Arab-Andalusian cuisine) offers the refined version. Simpler: bars on Plaza de España serve the tuna as tartare or plancha-grilled.

⏱ 90 min

The San Francisco food market—a converted former market hall, regional tiles, high stools—serves local products without pretense. Ibérico, payoyo goat cheese, olive oil from Campo de Gibraltar. Open mornings until around 2 p.m. Vendors speak little English and lots of local pride.

⏱ 60 min

Zahara De Los Atunes

★★★★☆
1

Walk the 5 km of sand facing Africa and stop at the Castillo de las Almadrabas, a ruined watchtower that reminds you Zahara lived exclusively from tuna fishing before tourism. The village is small, with no monuments to speak of—its beauty lies in the wide, preserved beach, the white houses, and the off-season silence. In high season, the population jumps from 2,000 to 20,000.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Iglesia de Nuestra Señora del Carmen, built between the 16th and 17th centuries, is Zahara's patron. Small, unadorned, it sums up this village's relationship with the sea: sober and direct. Calle Marqués de Montemar opposite concentrates the few local craft shops—mojama (salt-cured dried tuna) vacuum-packed, worth taking home.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

A simple fishing village, but tuna is treated like religion here. The almadraba—a maze of nets fixed to the seabed to trap tuna migrating toward the Mediterranean—has been practiced on these coasts for over 3,000 years, a Phoenician technique perpetuated by the Arabs. The season runs from April to June. El Campero in Barbate (20 minutes) is the regional reference for gastronomic tuna cuisine. In Zahara itself, beachfront chiringuitos offer the simpler, equally honest version.

⏱ 90 min

Bolonia

★★★★☆
1

Rome on the Atlantic: that's what Baelo Claudia is. Founded in the 2nd century BC on a Phoenician settlement, it received Roman municipium status under Emperor Claudius—hence the name. Forum, basilica, theater, Temple of Isis, aqueducts, and salting workshops for garum (fish sauce exported throughout the Empire): it's all there, almost intact. Listed as an archaeological site in 1989. Free entry for EU citizens. Allow 2 hours with the museum.

⏱ 120 min
2

Bolonia beach measures 3.8 km long, 70 m wide, and ends at the west with a 30-meter dune. Not a hotel in sight, not a development—the Strait Natural Park froze construction. The village of Lentiscal at the end of the beach has three chiringuitos; Sirocco is the most reputed for grilled fish. Ideal for lunch between Baelo Claudia and the dune.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Cows walk down to Bolonia beach in the morning—nobody explained to them it's a beach. The 30-meter dune, declared a natural monument in 2001, takes 20 minutes to climb from the parking lot: from the summit, the African coast appears on clear days. In summer, the sand burns bare feet. In the afternoon, continue 22 km toward Tarifa for the next stop.

⏱ 60 min

Tarifa

★★★★☆
1

In 1294, Guzmán chose to throw down his own knife rather than surrender the city to the besiegers: his captive son was executed, Tarifa held. The episode built a Spanish national myth. His castle, built in the 10th century by Caliph Abd al-Rahman III, is open Wednesday through Sunday. From the ramparts, Africa is 14 km away (Punta Cires)—on clear days, you can make out Tangier's buildings at 32 km.

⏱ 60 min
2

The white alleyways smell alternately of sea and jasmine. The Arab-origin walled old town can be explored on foot in 1 hour: the Puerta de Jerez, San Mateo church, the vaulted passages. Tarifa is small, intense, young—the town has lived off windsurfing since the 1980s and it shows in the old town bars.

⏱ 60 min
3

Six kilometers from the center via the coastal road, the Mirador del Estrecho is the best viewpoint over the strait. Two continents, two seas, tankers in single file: maritime traffic through the Strait of Gibraltar is among the world's densest. Free parking, 10-minute walk from the road. Best in late afternoon when raking light catches the waves.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Tarifa's best show is free: Playa de Los Lances unfurls 7 km of sand with dozens of colorful kites constantly in the air. Two winds, Levante (east) and Poniente (west), make this strait the European kitesurfing capital. Don't come here expecting flat calm—if the Levante blows hard for several days, ferries to Tangier are canceled and the beach becomes unmanageable.

⏱ 90 min

If the Levante is calm, take the ferry to Tangier: 35-minute crossing, from €37 round-trip with FRS or Inter Shipping. Passport required (EU ID card accepted). A day is enough for the medina, Souk market, and a tagine lunch. Return in the afternoon to enjoy the evening in Tarifa.

⏱ 420 min
