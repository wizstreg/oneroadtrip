Hungary Grand Tour: Thermal Baths, Lake Balaton and UNESCO Villages

📅 9 days📍 8 stops🚗 817 km⏱ 9h45

Budapest and its neo-Baroque thermal baths, Eger and its castle that held off 40,000 Ottomans, Lillafüred with its waterfall and beech forests, Lake Balaton and the mystical Tihany peninsula, then Pécs and its mosques turned churches: this grand tour spans eight centuries of Hungarian history in eight days. Every stop layers cultures — Roman, Ottoman, baroque, Art Nouveau — in a country that has been soothing travellers in thermal waters since antiquity.

✨ Highlights
  • Budapest's Parliament, built between 1885 and 1902, is one of the three largest parliament buildings in the world, with 691 rooms and a 96-metre dome
  • Eger Castle withstood a 38-day siege in 1552 against an Ottoman army 20 times its size — the most celebrated episode in Hungarian history
  • Tihany Abbey, founded in 1055, contains the only medieval royal tomb still in its original location in Hungary: that of King Andrew I
  • The Pasha Qasim Mosque in Pécs, built between 1543 and 1546, is the largest surviving Ottoman structure in Hungary, now converted into a Catholic church
📆 Best period

May, June, September, October

Day 1

Budapest

A Danube capital where neo-Gothic grandeur and century-old thermal waters meet under UNESCO skies

★★★★★
1

Start at the 'Shoes on the Danube Bank' memorial (Cipők a Duna-parton), between the Chain Bridge and the Parliament. In 2005, artists Can Togay and Gyula Pauer fixed 60 pairs of metal shoes along 45 metres of riverbank — men's, women's, children's — to remember the hundreds of Jews forced to remove their shoes before being shot and thrown into the river by Arrow Cross militiamen in 1944–1945. The site is free and accessible at any hour. Pause for a moment: the simplicity of the installation makes the memorial all the more powerful.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Hungarian Parliament is one of the most extravagant buildings in Central Europe. Built between 1885 and 1902 to the designs of Imre Steindl, it stretches 268 metres long, contains 691 rooms, 29 staircases and a dome rising to 96 metres — a height chosen to mark the millennium of the Hungarian nation in 1896. You can't miss it from the riverbank: its neo-Gothic façade lit up at night is Budapest's defining image. Book the interior tour in advance during high season — slots fill up fast.

⏱ 75 min
3

St Stephen's Basilica (Szent István-bazilika) took more than 50 years to build — from 1851 to 1905 — and its dome measures exactly 96 metres, the same height as the Parliament: by law, no building was allowed to stand taller. Inside, look for the Chapel of the Holy Right, which houses the mummified right hand of King Stephen I, Hungary's foremost relic; a coin in the slot lights up the reliquary. Climb to the dome viewing platform via the lift or 364 steps for a 360° panorama over Budapest.

⏱ 75 min
4

Vörösmarty Square and Váci Street, just next door, are the busy pedestrian arteries at the heart of Pest. Váci Street is a touch touristy but unmissable for the atmosphere and the shop fronts; walk all the way down to the Great Market Hall (Nagy Vásárcsarnok) at the southern end if you have time.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Walk up Andrássy Avenue — a UNESCO World Heritage Site — to Heroes' Square (Hősök tere). This 2.3-kilometre boulevard was laid out in the late 19th century on the model of the Champs-Élysées. At the far end, the colossal statues of the seven Magyar tribal chieftains and Hungarian kings form an imposing tableau. Allow 45 minutes for the round-trip walk, longer if you duck into a museum.

⏱ 60 min

End the day at the Széchenyi Thermal Baths (Széchenyi gyógyfürdő), inaugurated in 1913 inside a neo-Baroque palace in the Városliget park. These are Budapest's largest baths — 18 pools including three outdoor ones open even in winter — fed by an artesian spring at 74°C from more than 1,000 metres underground. Bring swimwear and a towel; book online in high season to skip the queue.

⏱ 120 min
Day 2

Budapest

A Danube capital where neo-Gothic grandeur and century-old thermal waters meet under UNESCO skies

★★★★★
1

Fő tér is the baroque heart of Szentendre, a small town perched on the Danube bank 20 kilometres from Budapest. In the centre stands the Plague Cross, erected in the 18th century by Serbian merchants to celebrate the town being spared by the epidemic. All around, colourfully painted houses recall the heritage of Serbian refugees who settled here from the 17th century onward, fleeing the Ottoman advance. Step inside the Blagovestenska Orthodox Church (18th century): its gilded icons are well worth a look.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Margit Kovács Museum is one of Hungary's most visited: it brings together the work of this Hungarian ceramicist (1902–1977) whose sculptures blend folk art and symbolism with striking originality. Don't miss it if you have even the slightest interest in Hungarian craftsmanship. Right next door, some thirty galleries and artists' studios line the lanes — Szentendre has been an artists' colony since 1926.

⏱ 60 min
3

A walk along the Danube embankment offers a different perspective on the town and the wooded hills across the water. If you have a half-day to spare, the Skanzen — an open-air museum on the outskirts — reconstructs a Hungarian village with 340 authentic period buildings and live craft demonstrations. Open from April to October.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The most pleasant way to reach Szentendre from Budapest is by riverboat from April to October (around 1h30 from the Vigadó quay). Outside the season or to save time, the HÉV suburban rail line H5 from Batthyány tér gets you there in 40 minutes. The return by boat, with the lights of Budapest growing closer, is worth the trip in itself.

⏱ 90 min

Day 3

Eger

A baroque hillside town famed for its heroic fortress and its cellars of Bull's Blood wine

★★☆☆☆
1

Eger Castle commands the town from its hilltop and owes its fame to one of the most celebrated episodes in Hungarian history: in 1552, Captain István Dobó and roughly 2,000 defenders — women and children included — held out for 38 days against an Ottoman army estimated at 40,000. The victory became a national symbol, immortalised in Géza Gárdonyi's novel 'Eclipse of the Crescent Moon.' The Ottomans eventually took the town in 1596 and held it for 91 years. Allow two hours to explore the ramparts, the underground casemates and the exhibitions.

⏱ 120 min
2

Dobó István Square is Eger's lively hub, lined with terraces, a statue of Captain Dobó and the 18th-century baroque Franciscan church. This is where the town comes alive in the evening — take a seat on a terrace and watch it unfold.

⏱ 45 min
3

Eger's minaret is the only intact minaret in Hungary and the northernmost surviving Ottoman structure in Europe. Standing 40 metres tall and built in the early 17th century, you need to climb 97 steps up an extremely narrow spiral staircase to reach the top. The views over the town and the castle are worth the effort, but the ascent is not recommended for anyone with claustrophobia. Open from April to October.

⏱ 30 min
4

Eight kilometres from Eger, the travertine terraces of Egerszalók form white limestone shelves shaped by thermal waters cascading down the hillside — a geological formation unique in Hungary. The view from the overlook is spectacular, especially in winter when steam rises into the cold air.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In the late afternoon, head down to the Valley of the Beautiful Women (Szépasszony-völgy), two kilometres from the centre. More than 200 wine cellars carved into the volcanic tuff line a sun-drenched south-facing slope — ideal conditions for ageing wine. This is where you'll taste Egri Bikavér, 'Bull's Blood,' a full-bodied red blend named after a legend: the defenders of 1552 supposedly fought with such ferocity after drinking this wine that the Ottomans believed they'd been fortified by actual bull's blood. Allow an hour for a leisurely tasting in one of the cellars.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 60 km · 1h
Day 4

Lillafured

A mountain retreat in the Bükk, where a waterfall, a mirror lake and a forest railway weave through beech groves

★★★☆☆
1

The Szinva waterfall is Hungary's tallest man-made cascade — 20 metres — created in the 1920s during the construction of the Palace Hotel, when the stream was diverted at the initiative of Count István Bethlen. Artificial or not, it's beautiful: in summer the water plunges through terraced gardens planted with rare species; in winter it freezes into spectacular columns of ice. Walk down to the hanging gardens for the closest view.

⏱ 45 min
2

Lake Hámori stretches 1.5 kilometres and was formed in the early 19th century when the Szinva and Garadna streams were dammed to supply the nearby blast furnaces. Today it's a perfect spot for a stroll: the trail along the northern shore loops around in about 45 minutes, with the neo-Renaissance Palace Hotel (built between 1927 and 1930) reflected in the water as a focal point. In summer, pedal boats can be hired on the spot.

⏱ 60 min
3

Don't leave without a look at Anna Cave and St Stephen's Cave, both within walking distance of the hotel. The first is a 1,043-metre-long stalactite cave of which visitors see only a portion; the second lies 500 metres along the road toward Eger.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The Lillafüred narrow-gauge forest railway (Lillafüredi Állami Erdei Vasút) is one of Hungary's finest scenic lines. Built in the 1920s to haul timber from the Bükk Mountains, it now runs from Miskolc to Garadna through beech forests, gorges, bridges and tunnels. The wooden carriages are open on the sides — bring a jacket. In service mainly from May to September; check timetables beforehand.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 230 km · 2h30
Day 5

Siofok

The sun-soaked summer capital on Lake Balaton's southern shore

★★★☆☆
1

Make a stop in Székesfehérvár, Hungary's former royal capital where 37 kings were crowned — and 17 buried — before the Ottoman invasion. The pedestrianised centre holds the Garden of Ruins (Romkert), where the apse and a few pillars of King Stephen I's medieval basilica, founder of the kingdom, still stand. Don't miss the fresco by Vilmos Aba-Novák that depicts the history of Hungary in a small neighbouring chapel.

⏱ 60 min
2

Bory Castle (Bory-vár) is one of Hungary's most eccentric curiosities: architect and sculptor Jenő Bory (1879–1959) built it single-handedly over 40 consecutive summers starting in 1923 — with no formal plans, guided only by his imagination and his love for his painter wife Ilona Komócsin. Seven towers, thirty rooms, courtyards and galleries rise from quartz concrete in a wild mix of Romanesque, Gothic and Scottish styles. The building entered the Guinness Book of Records as the largest structure ever erected by one person. Allow an hour to lose yourself in it.

⏱ 75 min
3

In Siófok, the 45-metre Water Tower (Víztorony) is the symbol of the town. Designed in 1912 by architects Árpád Guth and Jenő Gergely, it now houses a viewing gallery and the tourist office. Climb up for views over Lake Balaton and the marina.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In the evening, head to the Petőfi sétány promenade along the lake's biggest beach, the Nagystrand. Siófok is Hungary's 'summer capital' — buzzing, festive, with a well-known nightlife scene. Jókai Park (4.6 hectares), with its Japanese garden and pond, is the perfect spot for a quiet stroll before dinner.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 50 km · 45min
Day 6

Siofok

The sun-soaked summer capital on Lake Balaton's southern shore

★★★☆☆
1

Take the ferry from Szántódrév (south shore, Siófok side) to Tihanyrév (north shore): ten minutes on the water to reach the Tihany peninsula where the lake narrows to just 1.4 kilometres. Ferries run frequently from April to October; outside the season, plan to drive around. Views of the hills from the deck.

⏱ 30 min
2

Tihany's Benedictine Abbey was founded in 1055 by King Andrew I of Hungary and is one of the most historically significant sites in the country. Its Romanesque crypt houses the only medieval royal tomb still in situ in Hungary. The current twin-towered church was rebuilt between 1719 and 1754 in the baroque style; inside, the carved woodwork and frescoes by Lajos Deák Ébner reward a lingering look. From the forecourt, the view over Lake Balaton is the finest on the entire peninsula.

⏱ 75 min
3

Echo Hill (Visszhang-domb, Pisky promenade) has been famous since the 18th century for its peculiar acoustics: depending on atmospheric conditions, a voice could once reverberate up to 15 times. Celebrated Hungarian poems reference this echo. A statue marks the spot of best resonance.

⏱ 30 min
4

The lanes of Tihany village, with their thatched-roof houses and lavender shops, are worth a wander. The Lavender House tells the story of lavender cultivation on the peninsula (introduced in the early 20th century) and its unique volcanic geology. Try the lavender ice cream — it's the essential local treat.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The coastal trail circling the peninsula takes two to three hours on foot and delivers constantly changing views of the lake. Tihany was Hungary's first protected nature reserve (designated in 1952); its two inland lakes attract numerous rare bird species. Walking shoes recommended — some sections are steep.

⏱ 120 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 140 km · 1h40
Day 7

Pecs

Where a Roman necropolis, an Ottoman mosque and Art Nouveau ceramics share the same sunlit streets

★★★★☆
1

On Széchenyi tér, the main square of Pécs, you'll find one of the best-preserved Ottoman monuments in Europe: the Mosque of Pasha Gazi Qasim, built between 1543 and 1546 using materials salvaged from demolished medieval churches. After the Habsburg reconquest in 1686, the minaret was torn down and the building was converted into a Catholic church. Step inside: the interior layers an Ottoman dome with Christian frescoes on the same walls, with verses from the Quran still visible. One building, two centuries of faith.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Zsolnay Cultural Quarter grew out of the conversion of the former Zsolnay ceramics factory, founded in 1853 and world-renowned for its iridescent eosin glazes — a technology patented in 1893. Those golden-green ceramics still adorn numerous Art Nouveau buildings in Budapest and across Europe. The Zsolnay Museum and galleries display exceptional pieces from the early 20th century. The quarter also hosts a light festival in summer (Zsolnay Light Festival).

⏱ 75 min
3

The Cathedral of Saints Peter and Paul in Pécs is one of Hungary's most important Romanesque monuments. The original crypt dates from the 11th and 12th centuries; the building was heavily remodelled in the baroque era. Just below it, the Early Christian Necropolis of Sopianae has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2000: 4th-century mausolea with frescoes of the Virgin and of Adam and Eve lie buried beneath the modern streets.

⏱ 75 min
4

Climb to the Tettye quarter, on the heights above Pécs, to see the ruins of the Ottoman episcopal palace and enjoy a fine viewpoint over the city. The surrounding green spaces are a local favourite at weekends.

⏱ 45 min
5

If you still have energy, the Pécs TV Tower (Misina antenna, 534 m altitude) offers the widest panorama in the region — all the way to the southern plains and the Villány vineyards. Reachable on foot from the centre in 45 minutes or by road.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Pécs has one main pedestrian street, Király utca, packed with cafés, restaurants and shops. Stroll along it in the late afternoon, have a glass of Villány wine — the neighbouring wine region produces some of Hungary's finest reds — and finish at Lake Balokány and its green spaces as the sun goes down.

⏱ 60 min

Day 8

Budapest

A Danube capital where neo-Gothic grandeur and century-old thermal waters meet under UNESCO skies

★★★★★
1

The Fisherman's Bastion (Halászbástya) is Budapest's most photographed monument — and with good reason: its seven neo-Romanesque turrets, built between 1895 and 1902 by architect Frigyes Schulek, frame the most spectacular panorama over the Danube, the Parliament and the rooftops of Pest. The seven towers represent the seven Magyar tribes who founded Hungary in the 9th century. Arrive early in the morning to beat the crowds; at night, access to the terraces is often free. Matthias Church just behind, with its multicoloured tiled roof, was the coronation site of several Hungarian kings — including Franz Joseph I in 1867.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Buda Castle Quarter (Várnegyed) surrounds the rebuilt Royal Palace, home to the Hungarian National Gallery and the Budapest History Museum. The esplanades and medieval lanes are worth an hour's wander: the views across to Pest from the eastern ramparts are consistently stunning.

⏱ 75 min
3

Margaret Island (Margit-sziget) takes its name from the daughter of King Béla IV, Margaret, who lived in a Dominican convent on the island until her death in 1271. Stretching 2.5 kilometres long and 500 metres wide in the heart of the Danube, it packs in rose gardens, Japanese gardens, medieval ruins and the musical dancing fountain — a free show every hour with synchronised water jets. Perfect for a relaxed stroll at the end of the trip.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Round off the trip with a twilight cruise on the Danube (1 hour). As the current carries you along, the illuminated Parliament, the Fisherman's Bastion, the historic bridges and Buda Castle will glide past in golden light — the sequence of monuments that earned the site its UNESCO listing. Several companies depart from the Vigadó quay on the Pest side; the evening departures are often the most beautiful.

⏱ 60 min
