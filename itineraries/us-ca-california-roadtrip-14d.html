San Francisco, CA

★★★★★
1

Start with Haight-Ashbury, the neighborhood that embodied the Summer of Love in 1967—Janis Joplin, the Grateful Dead, and Jefferson Airplane all had homes here. Take a detour to 710-720 Steiner Street, facing Alamo Square: this is where the Painted Ladies stand, seven Victorian houses built in the 1890s and painted in three or more colors. San Francisco once had 48,000 houses in this style between 1849 and 1915—only about 10,000 remain today. Arrive before 9 AM to catch them without the tour buses.

⏱ 90 min
2

Fisherman's Wharf and Pier 39 form the liveliest stretch of waterfront in the city. Don't miss the sea lions that have colonized the K-Dock pontoons since 1989—there can be several hundred depending on the season. If you're hungry, this is where you'll find San Francisco's best clam chowder served in a sourdough bread bowl.

⏱ 75 min
3

The cable car is one of the only forms of public transport designated a National Historic Monument in the United States. The Powell-Hyde line climbs up Russian Hill before descending toward Fisherman's Wharf; the Powell-Mason line connects the Wharf to Union Square. Expect 15 to 20 minutes for the ride, and be patient during peak hours—lines can get long.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Alcatraz deserves half a day. The 22-acre island, 1.5 miles offshore, houses the former federal prison that operated from 1934 to 1963—Al Capone was incarcerated here as prisoner number 85. Book your ferry from Pier 33 well in advance: tickets sell out fast, especially in summer. Plan for 2.5 to 3 hours on the island, including the audio tour.

⏱ 180 min

End the day at the Palace of Fine Arts, a neoclassical rotunda built for the 1915 Panama-Pacific Exposition. The original structure was made of plaster and cardboard—it was rebuilt in concrete between 1964 and 1975 to preserve it. In late afternoon, the golden light on the dome and the reflecting lagoon is particularly stunning.

⏱ 60 min

San Francisco, CA

★★★★★
1

Cross the Golden Gate Bridge on foot or by bike to grasp its true scale: 1.7 miles long, towers rising 746 feet above the water, and a central span of 4,200 feet. Built between 1933 and 1937 for $35 million—well below the initial $100 million estimate. Go early in the morning for optimal light and to avoid the sometimes fierce midday wind.

⏱ 90 min
2

San Francisco's Chinatown is the oldest in the United States, founded in 1848. Its 24 blocks contain the city's highest population density. Wander along Grant Avenue, the main artery, and duck into the perpendicular alleyways to find the grocery stores, herbalists, and temples that tourists usually miss.

⏱ 75 min
3

City Lights Books, in the heart of North Beach, is the bookstore founded in 1953 by poet Lawrence Ferlinghetti—the first American publisher of Allen Ginsberg. It stays open until midnight every day. Next door, Vesuvio Café was Jack Kerouac's favorite bar. The entire North Beach neighborhood was the birthplace of the Beat Generation—an hour's stroll covers the essential sites.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

In the evening, head to the Castro for dinner. This has been the heart of San Francisco's LGBT+ community since the 1970s—Harvey Milk opened his camera shop here in 1973 before being elected to the city council. The restaurants are numerous and varied, the atmosphere relaxed and welcoming.

⏱ 90 min

Oakhurst, CA

★★★★☆
1

The drive from San Francisco to Yosemite passes through Mariposa, a small town that was one of the most active during the 1850s Gold Rush. Its wooden courthouse, built in 1854, is the oldest still in operation in California. Stop for 30 minutes to walk the main street—the period architecture is remarkably well preserved.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Mariposa Museum and History Center traces the region's history from the Miwok people to the Gold Rush. Small but well documented—allow 45 minutes to an hour to see everything.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Near Oakhurst, the Yosemite Mountain Sugar Pine Railroad offers steam train rides on narrow-gauge tracks that were used from 1899 to 1931 to haul timber from the sequoia forests. The 4-mile journey winds through pine forests and offers a glimpse of Sierra Nevada country before entering the park. Check seasonal schedules.

Oakhurst, CA

★★★★☆
1

As you enter the valley, Bridalveil Fall greets you: a 620-foot waterfall that flows year-round—one of the few in the park that never completely dries up. In strong wind, the water is blown sideways, which earned it its English name. The Ahwahneechee people, the valley's Native Americans, called it 'Pohono,' the spirit of the puffing wind. The access trail is half a mile round-trip from the parking lot, reachable in 15 minutes.

⏱ 60 min
2

El Capitan is one of the world's largest granite walls: 3,000 feet of vertical rock rising from the valley floor. The first free climb was completed in 1993 by Lynn Hill—a performance that stunned the global climbing community. From Tunnel View as you enter the valley, you get the classic frame with El Capitan on the left, Half Dome in the distance, and Bridalveil Fall on the right.

⏱ 60 min
3

Yosemite Falls is the tallest waterfall in North America: 2,425 feet total in three sections. The Lower Yosemite Falls trail is an easy 1-mile loop leading to the base. In spring during snowmelt, the flow is spectacular—by summer, the falls can be nearly dry.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Drive up to Glacier Point for sunset—it's the park's most famous viewpoint. The overlook sits at 7,214 feet elevation, 3,200 feet above the valley floor. From here, you have Half Dome directly in front of you, Vernal Fall and Nevada Fall below, and the High Sierra peaks stretching to the horizon. Ansel Adams captured some of his most iconic photographs here. Plan for an hour's drive from the valley and arrive 45 minutes before sunset to secure a spot.

⏱ 120 min

★★★★☆
1

Tioga Road (Highway 120) is the only road that crosses Yosemite from east to west at high elevation. It climbs to 9,945 feet at Tioga Pass—the highest highway pass in California—before reaching Tuolumne Meadows at 8,600 feet. The road is typically closed from late October to late May depending on snow. Along the way, stop at Olmsted Point: a granite overlook offering a sweeping view down into Tenaya Canyon with Half Dome in the background. Tuolumne Meadows is the largest subalpine meadow in the Sierra Nevada—nearly 2 miles of open grassland crossed by the Tuolumne River, framed by granite domes. A quiet place with a completely different atmosphere from the main valley.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

Return to Yosemite Valley for stargazing. Yosemite Valley is recognized as one of California's premier stargazing sites thanks to minimal light pollution. In summer months, the Milky Way is visible to the naked eye from the valley meadows. Set up at Cook's Meadow or the Swinging Bridge Picnic Area for clear skies.

Lone Pine, CA

★★★☆☆
1

The route from Oakhurst to Death Valley passes through Mammoth Lakes, a summer ski resort at 7,900 feet on the Sierra Nevada slopes, then Bishop, a small ranching town in the Long Valley. Take a break in Bishop—it's the last real town before the desert expanses. After Bishop, the road hugs the eastern face of the Sierra Nevada before dropping into Death Valley. The landscape transition is striking: pine forests then bare granite, high-altitude desert and finally scorching plains.

⏱ 60 min
2

At Lone Pine, stop at the Alabama Hills, a field of orange boulders sculpted into bizarre shapes at the foot of Mount Whitney. These landscapes have served as backdrops for hundreds of Hollywood westerns since the 1920s—from John Ford films to recent television series. Movie Road lets you drive among the most spectacular formations. Look for natural arches that frame the 14,505-foot Mount Whitney in the background.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Badwater Basin is the lowest point in North America: 282 feet below sea level. The salt flats covering the basin floor form hexagonal crystals—a phenomenon caused by repeated evaporation cycles in an area that receives only 1.9 inches of rain per year. Look up at the cliff face across from you and find the 'Sea Level' sign mounted 282 feet above your head. Avoid walking on the salt between 10 AM and 4 PM in summer—temperatures regularly exceed 113°F. Zabriskie Point, 19 miles north, offers a viewpoint over the ochre and gold badlands at sunset—these eroded hills provided the setting for a 1970 Michelangelo Antonioni film.

⏱ 120 min

Lone Pine, CA

★★★☆☆
1

Artist's Drive is a 9-mile loop through the multicolored badlands on the valley's eastern flank. The highlight is Artist's Palette, a geological formation where exposed rocks display manganese oxides (purple), iron silicates (green), and iron oxides (red and orange). The colors are most vivid in mid-morning. The road is one-way—plan your route accordingly.

⏱ 75 min
2

Dante's View is a viewpoint at 5,475 feet elevation that looks directly down on Badwater Basin—the elevation difference between the two points is 5,755 feet, one of the greatest in the United States over such a short horizontal distance. On clear days, you can simultaneously see the lowest point in the United States (Badwater) and Mount Whitney, the highest point in the continental U.S., 85 miles away as the crow flies. Arrive at sunrise to watch the shadows of the Panamint Mountains settle over the salt flats.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Mesquite Flat Sand Dunes, near Stovepipe Wells, are the most accessible and most photographed dunes in the park. They reach about 100 feet high. Go early in the morning or late afternoon—at these times the long shadows reveal the undulations of the sand and the tracks of nocturnal animals.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Set out at sunrise from Lone Pine for a photography session on Route 190. At this hour, the raking light transforms every sand ripple, every salt polygon, and every rock face. Death Valley is one of the places on earth where light changes most dramatically throughout the day.

⏱ 90 min

Joshua Tree, CA

The drive from Death Valley to Joshua Tree passes through Barstow in the Mojave. Once in the park, stop at Hidden Valley, a basin surrounded by quartz monzonite rock formations—the characteristic rock type of the park, formed 100 million years ago and sculpted by erosion ever since. The Hidden Valley loop trail is 1 mile, easy. Continuing east, Skull Rock is a formation whose two cavities carved by erosion suggest eye sockets—the associated trail is 1.7 miles.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

The quartz monzonite boulders turn pink, orange, and red depending on the angle of light, and the Joshua Trees—those tree yuccas that grow only at this elevation and in this precise geographic zone—take on the appearance of silhouettes cut from paper. Set up at Keys View, at 5,185 feet elevation, for a panorama embracing the Coachella Valley and, on clear days, Mexico 90 miles away.

Joshua Tree, CA

★★★★☆
1

The Barker Dam trail is a 1.7-mile loop deep in the rock formations. It leads to a small man-made reservoir built by ranchers in the early 20th century to water their cattle—today it's one of the few permanent water sources in the park and a prime spot for observing local wildlife. Along the way, you'll pass pictographs painted directly on the rock by Serrano and Cahuilla peoples.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Cholla Cactus Garden is one of the park's most peculiar spots: 10 acres covered with teddy bear cholla, also called 'jumping cactus' because its spiny segments detach and attach at the slightest contact—a fearsome dispersal mechanism. The loop is a quarter mile and crosses a transition zone between the Mojave Desert and the Colorado Desert. Don't touch anything, wear closed-toe shoes, and go at sunrise to see the chollas glow orange in the raking light.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

In the evening, head to a local bar in the town of Joshua Tree for live music. The town has long attracted artists and musicians—U2 recorded the album 'The Joshua Tree' here in 1987, and the area continues to nurture an active music scene blending desert blues, country, and indie rock.

Los Angeles, CA

★★★★☆
1

Enter Los Angeles via Santa Monica. The pier, built in 1909, extends 1,600 feet into the Pacific Ocean and hosts an amusement park with a Ferris wheel. This is where Route 66 ends—a sign at the end of the pier marks it. A few steps away, Venice Beach and its boardwalk are unmissable: skaters, musicians, street artists, and bodybuilders at Muscle Beach on the sand create a perpetual spectacle.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Getty Center perches on a Brentwood hilltop, 110 acres above the city. Designed by architect Richard Meier and opened in 1997 after 13 years of construction at a cost of one billion dollars, the campus is clad with 16,000 tons of travertine quarried near Tivoli, Italy. Admission is free—only parking has a fee. The permanent collection spans European paintings from the Middle Ages to the 19th century, including Van Gogh's Irises painted in 1889. The view of Los Angeles from the terraces alone is worth the visit.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Stroll along the Hollywood Walk of Fame, the 1.6 miles of star-studded sidewalks lining Hollywood Boulevard and Vine Street. More than 2,700 stars have been embedded since 1960, each bearing the name of a film, television, music, or circus artist. Grauman's Chinese Theatre, opened in 1927, is where you'll find star hand and footprints set in concrete.

Monterey, CA

★★★★☆
1

Start with the Piedras Blancas elephant seal rookery, 7 miles north of San Simeon. This is the only free, year-round, no-reservation elephant seal viewing site in the world. Males can reach 15 feet long and weigh up to 4,400 pounds. More than 25,000 elephant seals frequent this 7-mile stretch of beach depending on the season—the peak is between December and March for pupping. Wheelchair-accessible viewing platforms line Highway 1.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Big Sur crossing on Highway 1 is one of the world's most spectacular coastal roads: 80 miles of cliffs and redwood forests plunging into the Pacific Ocean. Bixby Bridge, built in 1932, is the iconic viewpoint: a concrete arch 260 feet high and 700 feet long. Stop at Julia Pfeiffer Burns State Park for the short hike to McWay Falls—an 80-foot waterfall tumbling directly onto an inaccessible beach. The round-trip trail is half a mile.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

The Monterey Bay Aquarium, opened in 1984 on the former Cannery Row, is one of the world's premier aquariums for marine conservation. The main tank holds 1.2 million gallons of seawater pumped directly from the bay. Don't miss the jellyfish exhibit—their toroidal tanks were specially designed to prevent wall damage—and the two-story kelp forest visible from outside the building.

⏱ 150 min
