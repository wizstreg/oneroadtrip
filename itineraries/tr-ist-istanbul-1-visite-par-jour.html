Istanbul Hagia Sophia

★★★★★
1

Push through the Imperial Gate of Hagia Sophia and look up: the dome, 31 metres in diameter, soars 56 metres above the floor, carried by four pendentives. Built between 532 and 537 under Justinian I by Anthemius of Tralles and Isidore of Miletus, this engineering masterpiece defined monumental architecture for a millennium.

⏱ 90 min
2

In the upper gallery, Byzantine mosaics from the 9th to 12th centuries stand alongside Ottoman calligraphic medallions 7.5 metres in diameter. The 13th-century Deësis, depicting Christ flanked by the Virgin and St John the Baptist, remains one of the peaks of late Byzantine art.

⏱ 45 min
3

Orthodox cathedral for 916 years, mosque for 482 years, museum from 1934 to 2020, then mosque again: the building encapsulates Istanbul's history in stone. Its 40 windows at the dome's base create a floating-light effect that impressed medieval visitors just as it does today.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Slide your thumb into the copper-ringed hole of the Weeping Column and rotate your finger a full circle without pulling it out—tradition promises your wish will be granted.

⏱ 10 min

On your way out, turn back to study the Ottoman ablutions fountain and the buttresses Sinan added in the 16th century to prevent the structure from collapsing.

⏱ 15 min

Settle down with a çay on a bench in Sultanahmet Park, facing the façade: at sunset, the stone takes on a golden hue that transforms the building's appearance.

⏱ 20 min

Istanbul Blue Mosque

★★★★★
1

Six minarets pierce the sky above the Blue Mosque (Sultanahmet Camii), commissioned by Sultan Ahmed I in 1609 and completed in 1616. The count sparked controversy: only Mecca had as many, forcing the sultan to fund a seventh there to restore the hierarchy.

⏱ 60 min
2

Enter through the north gate and let your eyes adjust: over 20,000 blue-and-white Iznik tiles line the walls, patterned with tulips, carnations and cypresses. The 260 windows bathe the prayer hall in soft light that makes the shades of blue shimmer—hence the nickname.

⏱ 30 min
3

On the neighbouring Hippodrome, three ancient monuments still stand: the Obelisk of Theodosius (15th century BC, brought from Egypt in 390), the bronze Serpent Column (5th century BC, from Delphi) and the Walled Obelisk of Constantine VII. This 480-metre esplanade hosted chariot races before crowds of 100,000.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Pause at the Fountain of Wilhelm II, gifted in 1901 by the German emperor: octagonal, crowned with a neo-Byzantine dome and adorned with golden mosaics inside.

⏱ 15 min

Cross the square to enter the Museum of Turkish and Islamic Arts, housed in the former palace of Ibrahim Pasha (16th century). The collection of calligraphy and Anatolian carpets is one of the world's finest.

⏱ 60 min

Istanbul Topkap

★★★★★
1

Pass through the Imperial Gate (Bab-ı Hümayun) and cross four successive courtyards: Topkapı Palace served as the residence of Ottoman sultans from 1465 to 1856. This 70-hectare complex housed up to 4,000 people—pages, eunuchs and women of the Imperial Harem.

⏱ 60 min
2

In the Treasury, the 86-carat Spoonmaker's Diamond gleams behind its armoured case, beside the Topkapı Dagger encrusted with three massive emeralds and the golden throne of Murad III. The Sacred Relics chamber preserves the cloak and sword attributed to the Prophet Muhammad.

⏱ 60 min
3

Take the corridor to the Harem: over 400 rooms decorated with Iznik tiles in floral patterns. The apartments of the Valide Sultan (the sultan's mother) and the domed Imperial Hall illustrate the power women wielded in Ottoman politics.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Descend into Gülhane Park, the palace's former garden planted with centuries-old plane trees, for an unobstructed view over the Golden Horn and the Sea of Marmara.

⏱ 30 min

Head to the terrace of the fourth courtyard to photograph the confluence of the Bosphorus, the Golden Horn and the Sea of Marmara—all three visible in a single panorama.

⏱ 20 min

Istanbul Basilica Cistern

★★★★★
1

Descend the 52 steps and let your eyes adjust to the gloom: 336 columns, nine metres tall, support this subterranean space of 9,800 m². The Basilica Cistern (Yerebatan Sarnıcı), built by Justinian in 532, supplied drinking water to the Great Imperial Palace.

⏱ 45 min
2

At the far end of the cistern, two columns rest on carved Medusa heads—one laid sideways, the other upside down. Their origin remains disputed. The cistern could hold 80,000 m³ of water piped from the Belgrade Forest via the Valens Aqueduct.

⏱ 30 min
3

On your way up, note the Weeping Column (peacock-eye column), carved with teardrop motifs. It recalls a style similar to Trajan's Forum in Rome and may have been salvaged from a Roman monument during construction.

⏱ 15 min

Activities

Walk the wooden platforms between the columns bathed in shifting lights and reflections in the residual water—an excellent moment for tripod photography.

⏱ 20 min

Back on the surface, walk up Divanyolu Caddesi, the ancient Roman triumphal route (Mese) linking the forum to the Great Palace. The Column of Constantine (Çemberlitaş), blackened by a 1779 fire, still stands midway, 35 metres tall.

⏱ 30 min

Istanbul Archaeological Museum

★★★★☆
1

Founded in 1891 by Osman Hamdi Bey to halt the dispersion of Ottoman antiquities, the Istanbul Archaeological Museum holds over one million objects. The centrepiece: the Alexander Sarcophagus (4th century BC) discovered at Sidon, carved with battle and hunting scenes with traces of original polychromy.

⏱ 90 min
2

Cross the courtyard to enter the Tiled Kiosk (Çinili Köşk), built in 1472 by Mehmed II: it's the oldest surviving civilian Ottoman building in Istanbul. It exhibits Seljuk and Ottoman faience, including 16th-century floral panels from imperial mosques.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Museum of the Ancient Orient, in the third building of the complex, displays cuneiform tablets including the Treaty of Kadesh (1259 BC)—the oldest known peace treaty, signed between Ramesses II and the Hittites—alongside a copy of the Code of Hammurabi.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Step into the Museum of the History of Science and Technology in Islam, set in Gülhane Park: astronomical instruments, water clocks and mechanical devices from the 9th to 16th centuries are reconstructed at full scale.

⏱ 45 min

Take a break in the archaeological museum's inner courtyard: the sarcophagi and ancient columns arranged in the open air form a free, peaceful sculpture garden.

⏱ 20 min

Istanbul Grand Bazaar

★★★★★
1

Get lost in the 66 covered streets of the Grand Bazaar (Kapalıçarşı), founded in 1461 under Mehmed II. Over 4,000 shops line painted vaults and stone domes across 30,000 m²: it's the world's oldest and one of the largest covered markets.

⏱ 90 min
2

Each street bears the name of its trade guild: Kalpakçılar for jewellers, Perdahçılar for coppersmiths, Halıcılar for carpet merchants. The Beyazıt and Nuruosmaniye gates are the main entrances to this labyrinth where haggling is a codified art.

⏱ 45 min
3

Just beyond the west exit, the Book Market of Sahaflar (Sahaflar Çarşısı) occupies an open courtyard used by papermakers since Byzantine times. You can still find antiquarian books, Ottoman calligraphy and Persian miniatures.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Step into Beyazıt Mosque (1506), the oldest imperial mosque still in use in Istanbul: its 20-column portico of granite and porphyry leads to a peaceful courtyard.

⏱ 30 min

Scout by theme without purchase pressure: hammered copper, Iznik ceramics, mosaic lamps, leather, spices. Bargaining always begins with a çay offered by the seller.

⏱ 30 min

Istanbul Suleymaniye

★★★★☆
1

Completed in 1557 by Sinan for Suleiman the Magnificent, Süleymaniye Mosque crowns one of Istanbul's seven hills. Its four minarets with ten balconies encode the sultan's place in Ottoman history: fourth sultan of Istanbul, tenth of the dynasty.

⏱ 60 min
2

The complex (külliye) extends far beyond the mosque: tombs of Suleiman and his wife Roxelana (Hürrem Sultan), four madrasas, a hospital, a caravanserai, a hamam and a public kitchen (imaret). Sinan himself is buried in a modest tomb in the north-east corner of the garden.

⏱ 45 min
3

Approach Sinan's tomb (türbe), a small open-air stone enclosure, modest compared to the scale of his oeuvre: over 300 buildings across the Ottoman Empire, including 84 mosques. An inscription records that he lived almost a century (1489–1588).

⏱ 15 min

Activities

From the terrace of the funerary garden, the view plunges over the Golden Horn and the Galata shore—one of the city's best free viewpoints.

⏱ 20 min

In the street below, try the white beans (kuru fasulye) and rice pilavı at the traditional restaurants backed against the mosque wall. These canteens haven't changed their recipes in decades.

⏱ 30 min

Istanbul Spice Bazaar

★★★★☆
1

Built in 1660, the Spice Bazaar (Mısır Çarşısı) is Istanbul's second-largest covered market. Its L-shaped plan shelters 85 shops piled with spices, Turkish delight, teas, dried fruits and caviar. It owes its name (Egyptian Bazaar) to the taxes on Egyptian goods that funded the neighbouring mosque.

⏱ 60 min
2

Completed in 1665 after 66 years of construction interrupted by wars and unrest, the New Mosque (Yeni Cami) dominates Eminönü Square. Its silhouette—two minarets and a cascade of semi-domes—has become the defining image of Istanbul's waterfront.

⏱ 30 min
3

Climb the narrow staircase to the tiny Rüstem Pasha Mosque (1563), signed by Sinan. Every interior surface is covered with Istanbul's finest Iznik tiles: crimson tulips, carnations and geometric patterns on white-and-blue ground.

Istanbul Uskudar

★★★★☆
1

Set on a raised terrace facing the jetty, Mihrimah Sultan Mosque in Üsküdar (completed 1548) is Sinan's first work to use the concept of the floating dome. Commissioned by Princess Mihrimah, daughter of Suleiman and Roxelana, its central dome flanked by three semi-domes once rose directly above the water, before the coastal road was built.

⏱ 45 min
2

Take the boat from Salacak to reach the Maiden's Tower (Kız Kulesi), on its islet at the entrance to the Bosphorus. The first tower was erected in 408 BC by the Athenian general Alcibiades. Successively lighthouse, customs post, lazaretto and fortification, it now houses a museum and restaurant.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Çinili Mosque (1640), commissioned by Kösem Sultan, one of the most powerful women in Ottoman history, is lined with blue-and-turquoise Iznik tiles in floral patterns. The adjacent Çinili Hamam still operates as a traditional Turkish bath.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Stroll along the Salacak waterfront: the view over Sultanahmet, Hagia Sophia and the Blue Mosque from the Asian shore is Istanbul's most photographed.

⏱ 20 min

Walk up to the garden of Fethipaşa Korusu, a wooded park overlooking the Bosphorus where Üsküdar families come to drink çay under the trees.

⏱ 25 min

Explore Kuzguncuk, a former cosmopolitan village where synagogue, Armenian church, Orthodox church and mosque coexist on the same street—a microcosm of Istanbul's multicultural past.

⏱ 30 min

1

Kadıköy is ancient Chalcedon, founded in 685 BC by the Megarians, seventeen years before Byzantium. It was nicknamed the 'city of the blind' because its founders supposedly failed to see the superior site of the Golden Horn opposite. Today it's the liveliest neighbourhood on the Asian shore.

2

Dive into the Fish Market (Kadıköy Balıkçılar Pazarı), a maze of alleys lined with stalls selling fish, olives, cheese, spices and dried fruit. ⏱ 30 min

Activities

Head down to Moda and walk the coastal promenade to Moda Park: the view stretches over the Sea of Marmara and the silhouette of Sultanahmet opposite.

⏱ 40 min

Explore Kadife Sokak (Bar Street), the heart of Kadıköy's alternative nightlife, lined with live bars and independent clubs like Arkaoda, open since 1999.

⏱ 25 min

Order a Turkish coffee at Fazıl Bey, a neighbourhood institution for decades, in Kadıköy market.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

⏱ 60 min

Settle into a Çamlıca tea garden at sunset: the Bosphorus turns gold between two continents, with the minarets of the European shore in silhouette.

Istanbul Princes Islands

★★★★☆
1

Büyükada (5.4 km²), the largest of the nine Princes' Islands (Adalar), is a car-free archipelago in the Sea of Marmara. The islands served as a place of exile for disgraced Byzantine princes and aristocrats. Trotsky lived here four years (1929–1933) in a 950 m² mansion now in ruins, where he wrote his History of the Russian Revolution.

⏱ 30 min
2

Climb to the Greek Orthodox monastery of Aya Yorgi (St George), perched atop Büyükada's southern hill at 188 metres altitude. An active pilgrimage site since 1751, the path is dotted with ribbons tied to trees by pilgrims making a wish on the way up.

⏱ 60 min
3

Heybeliada, the archipelago's second island, houses the Orthodox Halki Seminary (1844), closed since 1971 by Turkish authorities but still symbolically important to 300 million Orthodox Christians. Its pine forests cover the hills and run down to the coves on the southern coast.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Rent a bike at the Büyükada jetty and cycle round the island (about 1h30) via the beaches, Victorian wooden villas and pine groves.

⏱ 90 min

Lunch at a fish restaurant in Büyükada harbour, trying the stuffed mussels (midye dolma) and balık ekmek, island specialities.

⏱ 45 min

Visit the Princes' Islands Museum (Adalar Müzesi), housed in a former warehouse east of Büyükada, to understand the archipelago's multicultural history—Byzantine, Ottoman, Greek, Jewish, Armenian.

⏱ 30 min

⏱ 30 min
