Dominican Republic Circuit: East Coast & Samaná

📅 6 days📍 5 stops🚗 450 km⏱ 8h40

Punta Cana → Miches → Las Terrenas → Las Galeras → Santo Domingo

Punta Cana and its 48 km of white sand, the unspoiled coves of Miches, the "French village" of Las Terrenas, the authenticity of Las Galeras, and Santo Domingo's cobbled streets—this circuit crosses the east and the Samaná Peninsula, the Dominican Republic's most diverse region.

✨ Highlights
  • Playa Rincón and its 3 km of white sand at the tip of Samaná
  • Humpback whale watching in Samaná Bay (January-March)
  • Santo Domingo's Zona Colonial, the Americas' first colonial city
  • Montaña Redonda and its swings suspended at 300 m altitude
📆 Best period

December, January, February, March, April

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 5 and 9 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Peak Season (December-April): Perfect weather, minimal rainfall, ideal beach conditions, highest prices, most crowded, advance booking essential.

Shoulder Season (May-June, November): Good weather with occasional showers, fewer crowds, moderate prices, excellent value.

Hurricane Season (July-October): Higher humidity, potential storms, lowest prices, lush landscapes, September-October peak risk.

Baseball Season (October-February): Winter baseball league, cultural experience, passionate fans, authentic Dominican atmosphere.

🌍 What to discover in DO?

Caribbean's most diverse destination with pristine beaches, merengue music, and colonial heritage. Experience Santo Domingo's history, Punta Cana resorts, and authentic Dominican culture. The country offers excellent value with diverse landscapes and warm hospitality. Dominican Republic provides complete Caribbean experiences with cultural authenticity and beach paradise.

Day 1

Punta Cana

Where 48 kilometers of Caribbean powder meet turquoise shallows

★★★★☆
📍 Playa Bavaro Punta Cana📍 Playa Macao Punta Cana📍 El Cortecito Bavaro Punta Cana📍 Arena Gorda Beach Punta Cana📍 Hoyo Azul Cap Cana
1

The Costa del Coco stretches for 48 kilometers of white sand along the eastern shore—one of the longest unbroken coastlines in the Caribbean. Playa Bávaro is its most famous and developed section. What's remarkable is that this entire coastline was nothing but roadless, powerless tropical jungle as recently as the 1960s. A group of North American investors spotted the potential in 1969 and built the first hotel, which opened in 1971. The offshore coral reef naturally shields the coast from Atlantic swells—the water stays calm and shallow, perfect for swimming. If you're not staying at a resort, four public access points are available: Bibijagua, Jellyfish, Corales, and Cortecito.

⏱ 90 min
2

Make the detour to Playa Macao, thirty minutes north of Bávaro. The contrast hits immediately: you're standing at the boundary between the Caribbean Sea and the Atlantic Ocean, where winter waves can reach 2 to 3 meters, and local fishermen dry their nets next to surfboards. Before Punta Cana became a resort zone, Macao was already the Dominicans' beach—a tradition that endures. The beach runs 4 km, remains public and undeveloped. Be cautious with children: there are no lifeguards and currents can be strong.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

The catamaran excursion to Saona Island is Punta Cana's most popular outing—and it earns the reputation. The island measures 20 km long by 5 km wide (110 km² total) and forms part of Cotubanamá National Park. Christopher Columbus discovered it in May 1494 during his second voyage and named it "Saona" to honor Michele da Cuneo, a friend from Savona in Liguria. The two cities are still officially twinned today. The natural pool—a sandbar emerging in open water where you wade waist-deep—is the day's highlight: you splash around surrounded by starfish in translucent water. Choose a small-group tour: large excursions all arrive simultaneously and the crowds diminish the experience.

⏱ 480 min

Surf lesson or free session at Playa Macao. This is Punta Cana's only real surf spot: a north-facing beach break that produces consistent waves from autumn through spring. Local schools have welcomed beginners since the 2000s and provide equipment on-site.

Day 2

Miches

A forgotten fishing village beneath a round mountain and twin lagoons

★★☆☆☆
📍 Montana Redonda Miches📍 Laguna Redonda Miches📍 Laguna Limon Miches📍 Playa Esmeralda Miches📍 Miches El Seibo Dominican Republic
1

Montaña Redonda—"round mountain"—rises 300 meters above sea level between Laguna Redonda and Laguna Limón. From the summit, you get a 360-degree panorama over the ocean, both lagoons, the Cordillera Oriental, and the still-virgin beaches of the coast. The site had existed for over twenty years, but Instagram put Montaña Redonda on the global map with photos of swings suspended at the edge of the precipice. Access is by 4x4 or local pickup truck from below—the red dirt track often turns muddy after rain. Arrive early morning: the light is better and you'll avoid the tour groups arriving from Punta Cana.

⏱ 90 min
2

Then head down to Playa Esmeralda, the Miches beach that mass tourism hasn't touched. You won't find parasols for rent, no vendors, no resorts in sight—just a long ribbon of white sand and coconut palms. Miches resembles what Punta Cana looked like before the 1980s, and that's precisely its appeal.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Climb onto the Montaña Redonda swings for the signature photo: the site's guides position you at the summit's edge and trigger the camera at the perfect moment—at the right split-second, you appear to float in mid-air above the valley. There's also a broomstick prop to vary the angles. These installations cost extra on-site, beyond the entrance fee.

⏱ 45 min

Boat or quad excursion on Laguna Redonda and Laguna Limón, two freshwater nature reserves with calm, verdant waters bordering the base of Montaña Redonda. Local tours often combine both lagoons with a visit to a coconut plantation—a hands-on way to see how locals extract oil artisanally.

⏱ 120 min

Day 3

Las Terrenas

Where French expats and Dominican fishermen share the wildest coast

★★★★☆
📍 Playa Coson Las Terrenas📍 Playa Bonita Las Terrenas📍 Playa Las Ballenas Las Terrenas📍 Pueblo de los Pescadores Las Terrenas📍 Playa Punta Popy Las Terrenas
1

Playa Cosón, west of Las Terrenas, offers 4 km of sand lined with coconut palms and not a single hotel directly on the beach. It's one of the most untouched expressions of the Samaná coastline. Las Terrenas itself has a surprising history: founded in 1946 on President Trujillo's orders, who sent poor families from Santo Domingo to settle here as farmers and fishermen, the village had neither paved roads (until 1989) nor electricity (until 1994). This prolonged inaccessibility is what preserved the beaches and attracted the first Europeans—mainly French—starting in the 1970s.

⏱ 90 min
2

Stroll through central Las Terrenas, nicknamed "the French village": some 1,700 French nationals now live among the town's 6,000 expats. The Pueblo de los Pescadores, the old fishermen's quarter on the waterfront, has become the lively evening promenade—restaurants, bars, fishing boats, and travelers mingle in a deeply laid-back atmosphere. Just behind it, Playa Las Ballenas takes its name from three rocks emerging from the sea that evoke a whale family—the male, female, and calf.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Swimming at Playa Bonita in late afternoon and sunset from the rocks that close off the beach to the west. Playa Bonita is considered the best swimming spot in Las Terrenas: the water is calm, transparent, and the beach less crowded than the town center.

⏱ 90 min

If you're here between mid-January and mid-March, don't miss the humpback whale watching excursion in Samaná Bay under any circumstances. Thousands of whales come to breed and give birth each year in these protected waters—one of the species' largest gatherings in the Atlantic. You embark from the town of Samaná, about 45 minutes' drive away. Book early: good guides fill up quickly in high season.

⏱ 240 min

Day 4

Las Galeras

The last village before the point, gateway to Rincón's endless sand

★★☆☆☆
📍 Playa Rincon Las Galeras Samana📍 Playa Fronton Las Galeras📍 La Playita Las Galeras📍 Las Galeras village Samana📍 Playa Madama Las Galeras
1

Playa Rincón regularly ranks among the Dominican Republic's most beautiful beaches—and its 3 km of white sand framed by palm trees justify the reputation. What makes this beach truly distinctive is its dual personality: the northern end forms a perfectly calm turquoise pool, ideal for swimming; the southern end, exposed to the Atlantic, produces livelier waves. Between the two, the Caño Frío river flows directly into the sea—after a day in the sun, bathing in its cool waters is pure pleasure. Some historians actually believe this, not Playa las Flechas, is where Columbus landed. Access is by boat from Las Galeras or via a track passable outside rainy season.

⏱ 180 min
2

Explore Las Galeras village in late afternoon, when fishermen return with their catch. Its name comes from two galleons sent in the 16th century by the King of Spain to combat pirates and smuggling in Rincón Bay—the ships anchored here so long that the place kept the name. A few colorful houses backed by palm trees, boats on the beach, food shacks serving the day's catch: it's the antithesis of Punta Cana, and that's deliberate.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Boat trip to Playa Frontón, accessible only by sea from Las Galeras. This beach framed by a 300-meter black cliff is one of the peninsula's most spectacular. The well-preserved coral bottoms also make it one of Samaná's best snorkeling spots.

⏱ 180 min

Optional boat excursion to Cayo Levantado, the small forested island in Samaná Bay nicknamed "Bacardi Island" by locals for its immaculate sand and turquoise waters. You embark from the town of Samaná. It's a very touristy outing but the island's beauty is undeniable.

⏱ 360 min

Day 5

Santo Domingo

Where the New World began, cobblestone by cobblestone, in 1498

★★★★★
📍 Zona Colonial Santo Domingo📍 Catedral Primada de America Santo Domingo📍 Alcazar de Colon Santo Domingo📍 Fortaleza Ozama Santo Domingo📍 Parque Colon Santo Domingo📍 Calle Las Damas Santo Domingo📍 Museo de las Casas Reales Santo Domingo
1

Santo Domingo's Zona Colonial has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1990, and it thoroughly deserves the designation: here, founded in 1498 by Bartolomé Columbus, rose the Americas' first cathedral, first hospital, first university, and first customs house. In 1502, a hurricane destroyed the wooden city. The new governor Nicolás de Ovando rebuilt it on the opposite bank of the Ozama River with a grid plan that would later serve as the model for nearly every colonial city in Latin America. Today, the quarter covers less than 5 km² and is entirely walkable—the cobbled lanes are frequented by locals and visitors alike.

⏱ 60 min
2

At Parque Colón, pause before the Catedral Primada de América. The first stone was laid in 1514—but construction dragged because workers left to seek gold in Mexico. Bishop Geraldini finally resumed the project in 1519 and completed it around 1540. Pope Paul III declared it the New World's first cathedral in 1546 at Charles V's request. The facade blends Gothic, Plateresque, and Renaissance styles. Inside, 14 chapels and—an often-overlooked fact—Christopher Columbus's remains were kept here before their transfer to the Columbus Lighthouse, a move still disputed between the Dominican Republic and Spain.

⏱ 60 min
3

From Plaza de España, enter the Alcázar de Colón. This palace was built between 1510 and 1514 for Diego Columbus, Viceroy of the Indies and Christopher Columbus's son, who lived here with his family until 1577. The structure is entirely coral stone, without a single nail in the joints, and originally had 55 rooms. In 1586, Francis Drake invaded Santo Domingo and carried off much of the palace's artwork. Today, 22 rooms are restored and furnished with pieces from the 14th to 16th centuries.

⏱ 75 min
4

Finish at Fortaleza Ozama, at the end of Calle Las Damas—the oldest paved street in the Americas, laid in 1502. The fortress is the continent's oldest European military construction: built between 1502 and 1508 by Nicolás de Ovando to defend the city against English, French, and Dutch pirates, it has flown the flags of Spain, England, France, Haiti, Gran Colombia, and the United States in turn. The torre del Homenaje rises 18 meters with 2-meter-thick walls—and bullet holes from the 1965 Dominican Civil War are still visible in the doors.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In the evening, Plaza de España and Calle El Conde come alive: street musicians, restaurants in old colonial houses, salsa bars where Dominicans and travelers mix in a warm atmosphere. Sit on a terrace, order local rum, and watch the quarter animate. On weekends, Parque Duarte becomes the local youth hangout—a very different vibe from postcard tourism.

⏱ 120 min

Visit the Museo de las Casas Reales, housed in two connected colonial palaces on Calle Las Damas. The museum traces Dominican colonial history from conquest to independence, with artifacts, documents, and models that put the Zona Colonial's monuments into historical context. Allow 1.5 to 2 hours to see it all.

⏱ 100 min
