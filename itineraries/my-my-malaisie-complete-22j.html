Grand Malaysia Circuit: Peninsula & Borneo in 22 Days

📅 23 days📍 22 stops🚗 3554 km⏱ 37h25

KL and its Petronas, the tea plantations of Cameron Highlands, UNESCO George Town, the reefs of the Perhentians, Malacca and its five centuries of colonization - then fly to Borneo for Kuching, the proboscis of Bako, the giant caves of Mulu, the orangutans of Sepilok, the pygmy elephants of Kinabatangan, and Mount Kinabalu.

✨ Highlights
  • Perhentians closed October to March - plan accordingly
  • Flight KL → Kuching: 1h50, multiple daily flights Malaysia Airlines and AirAsia
  • Semenggoh: avoid November to January (fruiting season)
  • Mulu: accessible only by air, book MASWings early
  • Kinabatangan: minimum 2 nights to maximize elephant sightings
  • Mount Kinabalu: permits and lodges limited to 135 people/day, book months ahead
📆 Best period

March, April, May, June, July, August, September

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 12 and 16 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Dry Season (March-September): Best weather for most regions with minimal rainfall, comfortable temperatures (24-32°C), and ideal conditions for outdoor activities and island visits.

Shoulder Season (February, October): Good weather with occasional showers, moderate hotel rates, and fewer crowds. Excellent balance of conditions and value for most destinations.

Monsoon Season (October-February): Regional variations with east coast affected by northeast monsoon, while west coast remains largely dry. Lower prices but weather-dependent planning required.

Year-Round Destination: Tropical climate means travel possible anytime, though regional monsoon patterns affect specific areas differently throughout the year.

🌍 What to discover in MY?

Multicultural paradise with incredible diversity, pristine rainforests, and vibrant cities. Experience cultural fusion, delicious cuisine, and natural beauty. The country offers cultural diversity with natural wonders and modern amenities. Malaysia provides diverse experiences with cultural authenticity and natural preservation.

📋 Important notes

Combines my-peninsule-10j (D1-D10) + flight KL→Kuching + my-borneo-sarawak-sabah-12j (D11-D22).

Day 1

Kuala Lumpur

Where multicultural Asia rises beneath the world's tallest twin towers

★★★★★
1

Head up to the Petronas skybridge in the evening - these towers held the world's tallest title for six years from 1998 to 2004, and the bridge connecting them at the 41st floor gently slides in the wind rather than being welded solid. You don't notice details like that from ground level. Slots sell out fast, so book online before you arrive.

⏱ 90 min
2

Descend toward Chinatown and its alleys covered by the green canopy of Jalan Petaling. Two blocks away, step through the entrance of Sri Mahamariamman Temple - founded in 1873 by Tamil immigrants, with its five-tiered gopuram carved by artisans from South India. The monkeys around the entrance will steal anything they can grab, consider yourself warned.

⏱ 75 min
3

Continue toward Little India around Jalan Masjid India: a twenty-minute walk from Chinatown, and it's another city entirely. Spice aromas, saris stacked in shop windows, temples squeezed between mobile phone stores. KL fits into these three neighborhoods - Malay, Chinese, Tamil - ten minutes apart from each other.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In the evening, hit Jalan Alor for street food. Tables spill onto the sidewalk at nightfall, and the smells of charcoal and grilled satay flood the street. Arrive after 7 PM when everything's in full swing - before that, it's half empty.

⏱ 90 min
Day 2

Kuala Lumpur

Where multicultural Asia rises beneath the world's tallest twin towers

★★★★★
1

Take the train to Batu Caves - 30 minutes from downtown, and the Murugan statue hits you the moment you step off the platform. 42 meters of golden concrete. After that, 272 colorful steps to reach the main cave. Keep your bag zipped tight - the macaques work as a team.

⏱ 90 min
2

On your way back, detour through Masjid Negara. The National Mosque from 1965 is open to non-Muslims outside prayer times - robes and headscarves provided at the entrance. The fountain gardens surrounding it are peaceful, a world away from the noise of downtown.

⏱ 45 min
3

KLCC Park at the foot of the Petronas offers the best free view of the towers. Come back in the evening when they're lit up and reflected in the artificial lake - it's more stunning than from the skybridge.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Dinner at the revolving restaurant atop KL Tower - the building rotates slowly during the meal, you don't. Order your main course facing the Petronas, dessert will arrive with the gardens as backdrop.

⏱ 120 min

Day 3

Cameron Highlands

Mist-wrapped highlands where Malaysia's largest tea estate has flourished since 1929

★★☆☆☆
1

After four hours of switchback driving from KL, Cameron Highlands emerges through the mist - and Malaysian heat vanishes instantly. Start with BOH's Sungai Palas plantation, founded in 1929 by a Scotsman who switched from rubber to tea. Its 1,200 hectares still produce 70% of Malaysia's tea today. The café terrace overlooks rows of tea bushes cascading endlessly into the valley. Arrive before 10 AM - tour buses flood in afterward.

⏱ 90 min
2

Then climb to the Mossy Forest at the summit of Gunung Brinchang at 2,032 meters. Everything is covered in moss - trunks, branches, the ground between roots. A wooden boardwalk winds through to avoid crushing it. Early morning in the mist, the atmosphere is genuinely eerie, like walking through a fairy tale. Afternoons it's often closed due to fog.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

After the plantation, settle into a tea house for tastings. The difference between lowland tea and high-altitude tea is immediately noticeable - smoother, less tannic. The scones are there by pure colonial tradition - the British had recreated their teatime ritual in the middle of the tropical jungle.

⏱ 60 min

Forest hike around Tanah Rata. Not all trails are marked - grab a map from your accommodation and check conditions before heading out.

⏱ 150 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 250 km · 5h
Day 4

Penang Georgetown

Where street art transformed colonial shophouses into the world's most Instagrammed open-air gallery

★★★★☆
1

George Town has been UNESCO-listed since 2008 for its 12,000 heritage buildings. Following that, the local government launched a competition to give the city a visual identity - 52 wire sculptures appeared in the streets. Then in 2012, a Lithuanian artist painted his first murals with an actual rusty scooter embedded in the wall. It went viral worldwide. Set out to hunt for murals on foot before 8 AM - after that it's queues in front of every piece.

⏱ 120 min
2

Grab a taxi to Kek Lok Si Temple on Ayer Itam hill - the largest Buddhist complex in Southeast Asia. Start from the bottom and climb on foot through the levels. The commercial sprawl at the base doesn't prepare you at all for what awaits at the top.

⏱ 90 min
3

Head back down to the Clan Jetties, stilt villages founded by Chinese clans who arrived in the 18th and 19th centuries. The Chew Jetty is the largest - 25 families still live there, a street the width of a scooter, residents living their lives among tourists' cameras.

⏱ 60 min
4

The Pinang Peranakan Mansion - residence of a late-19th-century clan chief. The interior tells the complete story of Baba-Nyonya culture, descendants of Chinese men who married Malay women: European furniture, English porcelain, Portuguese tiles, Chinese embroidery. Four cultures in a single house.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

In the evening, eat at Gurney Drive or New Lane. George Town has Malaysia's best street cuisine - assam laksa, char kway teow, cendol. Go on an empty stomach and order in waves.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 400 km · 6h
Day 5

Perhentian Islands

Car-free islands where blacktip reef sharks and sea turtles swim alongside snorkelers

★★★★★
1

The boat from Kuala Besut drops you on two islands with no roads and no cars. You move on foot through jungle or by water taxi. Long Beach on Kecil: white sand, a line of bungalows, campfires in the evening. Turtle Beach on Besar: no infrastructure whatsoever, accessible only by boat - turtles come ashore to nest at night.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Head out on a snorkeling tour in the afternoon - stops at Shark Point (five-foot blacktip reef sharks, perfectly harmless), Turtle Point where turtles surface for air six feet away from you, and Coral Garden. The sharks are sometimes visible from the beach edge without even putting on a mask.

⏱ 180 min

Beach barbecue in the evening. No street lighting, no light pollution - the night sky here is serious. On moonless nights, bioluminescent plankton glows in the water when you move your hands - thousands of blue dots lighting up on contact.

⏱ 120 min
Day 6

Perhentian Islands

Car-free islands where blacktip reef sharks and sea turtles swim alongside snorkelers

★★★★★
1

Jungle hike on Kecil to the Windmill Viewpoint - 30 minutes climbing from Long Beach through dense vegetation, and the panorama over the bay and cliffs makes every drop of sweat worthwhile. An old metal staircase descends to an abandoned pier above turquoise water, but the steps are rusted and some are missing - stay at the top for the view. Bring proper shoes, mosquito repellent, and water.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Rent a kayak from Long Beach to reach D'Lagoon and Adam & Eve Beach on Kecil's northwest coast. These coves are only accessible by water or via an hour-long jungle trail. D'Lagoon has a colorful reef just meters from shore - park the kayak on the sand and put on your mask right there.

⏱ 180 min

End the day at Coral Bay, on the other side of the island - 15 minutes on foot from Long Beach via the trail through the village. Sunset drops straight into the sea from this beach. Baby blacktip reef sharks sometimes swim in the shallow waters around the pier.

⏱ 90 min
Day 7

Perhentian Islands

Car-free islands where blacktip reef sharks and sea turtles swim alongside snorkelers

★★★★★
1

Take a water taxi to Perhentian Besar - the larger and quieter island. Tuna Bay is one of the best beaches for snorkeling directly from shore, no boat needed. The reef in front of Coral View and the Perhentian Island Resort is rich with green turtles, blue-spotted stingrays, and giant parrotfish.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

Jungle hike on Besar - the trail leads from Tuna Bay to Teluk Keke, then cuts through forest to the viewpoint overlooking Perhentian Island Resort beach. Count on two hours with stops. Dusky leaf monkeys live in this part of the island and can be observed from the trail.

⏱ 150 min

Final snorkeling session at Teluk Keke - a small rocky islet facing the beach is surrounded by a shallow reef with clownfish, triggerfish, and, with luck, turtles. Make the most of your last evening on the island before tomorrow's departure.

⏱ 120 min

Day 9

Malacca

Where five centuries of Portuguese, Dutch, and British rule left layers of colonial architecture on every corner

★★★☆☆
1

Start with the Stadthuys, the red building by the river - the oldest Dutch building in Asia, built in 1650. The Dutch left it white; it was a 20th-century renovation that painted it red. Behind it, climb up to the ruins of A Famosa: only the Porta de Santiago gate remains of a Portuguese fortress that controlled the entire strait. Malacca was founded around 1400, conquered by the Portuguese in 1511, the Dutch in 1641, the British in 1795. Each layer left something behind.

⏱ 75 min
2

Head down to Jonker Street, the main artery of Chinatown lined with 19th-century shophouses. Baba-Nyonya family antiques were sold off here after the Great Depression when rubber collapsed. Today it's souvenirs and street food, but the architecture still holds. The Friday and Saturday night markets take over the entire street.

⏱ 60 min
3

Take a look at Christ Church from 1753 while passing by - the Dutch brought the bricks from Zeeland, on the other side of the world. St. Paul's Hill behind offers a view over the Strait of Malacca.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

End the day with a river cruise on the Malacca River - 45 minutes along the brightly painted buildings lining the old commercial artery. In the evening from the water, the facades light up.

⏱ 45 min

Day 11

Kuching

The riverfront capital founded by a British adventurer who became the White Rajah of a jungle kingdom

★★★☆☆
1

Start with Kuching's Waterfront in late afternoon - the promenade runs along the Sarawak River facing Fort Margherita, built in 1879 by the White Rajah James Brooke to protect the city from pirates. Kuching means "cat" in Malay, and feline sculptures have lined the streets since colonial times. The old town behind the wharf concentrates perfectly preserved 19th-century Chinese shophouses.

⏱ 60 min
2

Head up to the Sarawak Museum - one of the best museums in Southeast Asia according to Alfred Russel Wallace who stayed here in 1854. The section on Dayak, Iban, and Penan peoples traces the cultures of Borneo's forests better than any book.

⏱ 75 min
3

In the evening, wander Carpenter Street and Main Bazaar for street food. Kuching has its own local specialties: kolo mee, dry noodles with char siu, and umai, Dayak ceviche with raw fish marinated in lime juice with onions and chili.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Sunset boat cruise on the Sarawak River. Boats depart from the Waterfront and pass the illuminated Fort Margherita. It's the easiest way to see both sides of Kuching in an hour.

⏱ 60 min

Day 12

Kuching

The riverfront capital founded by a British adventurer who became the White Rajah of a jungle kingdom

★★★☆☆
1

Leave early for Bako National Park - 40 minutes' drive to Kampung Bako, then a 30-minute boat ride along the coast to park headquarters. Bako is Sarawak's oldest park, gazetted in 1957, and it's the world's best place to observe proboscis monkeys on foot. About 300 individuals live here in 27 km² of forest protected for 68 years - they're so habituated to humans they sometimes come down to the cafeteria.

⏱ 60 min
2

Take the Telok Paku or Telok Delima trail to see the proboscis - the dominant male with his ten-centimeter pendulous nose and swollen belly weighs up to 20 kg. The potbelly isn't fat: it's a multi-chambered stomach that digests toxic mangrove leaves other primates can't handle. Early morning or at 5 PM, they descend to the river for the night. The macaques around headquarters steal anything they can grab - keep your bag closed.

⏱ 150 min
3

After the hike, stay for sunset on the sculpted coastline - red sandstone cliffs carved by erosion, sea arches, isolated beaches. The park contains seven complete ecosystems within 27 km², from peat swamp forest to cliff vegetation. Warning: saltwater crocodiles have been reported in the bays in recent years; swimming is not recommended.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Dinner on the banks of the Sarawak River

⏱ 60 min

Shopping for souvenirs and batik textiles

⏱ 60 min

Return to Kuala Lumpur for international flight

Day 13

Kuching

The riverfront capital founded by a British adventurer who became the White Rajah of a jungle kingdom

★★★☆☆
1

Grab a ride to Semenggoh Wildlife Centre, 24 km south of Kuching. Founded in 1975 as a sanctuary for orphaned or confiscated orangutans, the center now hosts about thirty semi-wild individuals who roam freely in the 653-hectare reserve and return when the forest lacks fruit. Arrive before 9 AM for the morning session - rangers call each orangutan by name and distribute bananas, papayas, and hard-boiled eggs on the feeding platform.

⏱ 120 min
2

After the session, stay in the reserve for a hike around the center. If you visit between April and October, chances of seeing several individuals are excellent - during fruiting season from November to January, the forest is their buffet and they don't bother showing up. It's a sign of successful rehabilitation, but you come away empty-handed.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Return to Kuching for a final evening in Chinatown. The evening market around Carpenter Street is quieter than the previous night - enjoy the temples open in the evening and the last Iban and Penan craft shops before leaving.

⏱ 90 min
Day 14

Mulu

Where the world's largest cave passage and underground chamber hide beneath UNESCO rainforest

★★★★★
1

Mulu is accessible only by air - MASWings operates flights from Kuching, Miri, and Kota Kinabalu in 64-seat ATRs. There's no road. Since 1974, the Sarawak government has chosen not to build one to preserve the park's ecological integrity. Check into the park lodge then take the 3 km boardwalk from headquarters to Deer Cave in the afternoon.

⏱ 45 min
2

Enter Deer Cave - the world's largest cave passage at 120 to 150 meters in diameter and 197 meters high. It held the world record until the discovery of Hang Son Doong in Vietnam in 2009. The floor is covered with guano accumulated by the 3 million bats living overhead. Follow with Lang Cave right next door, discovered in 1977 by a Berawan local named Lang Belarek for whom the cave is named - stalactites lit to perfection, total silence.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Stay at the observation platform in front of Deer Cave from 5:30 PM for the Bat Exodus. Every evening, the 3 million bats emerge in spiral columns - a giant helix swirling for several kilometers above the forest for 45 minutes. No two evenings are identical depending on weather and predators.

⏱ 75 min
Day 15

Mulu

Where the world's largest cave passage and underground chamber hide beneath UNESCO rainforest

★★★★★
1

Take a longboat on the Melinau River in the morning to reach Clearwater Cave and Wind Cave - they're only accessible by boat or via a 4 km forest trail. Clearwater is Asia's longest cave network with 227 km of mapped passages. Wind Cave gets its name from the air currents flowing through its corridors - the King's Room inside is a chamber where floor and ceiling bristle with joined stone columns.

⏱ 180 min
2

In the afternoon, take the park's canopy walkway - one of the longest in the world, suspended several dozen meters above the ground. Mulu has been UNESCO-listed since 2000 for its 52,864 hectares covering 17 vegetation zones. Sarawak Chamber, inaccessible to the public, is the world's largest natural underground chamber - 600 meters long by 415 wide, enough for 40 Boeing 747s end to end.

⏱ 90 min
Day 16

Sandakan

Gateway to Sepilok, the world's first orangutan rehabilitation center established in 1964

★★★★★
1

Arrive in Sandakan and head straight to Sepilok Orangutan Rehabilitation Centre, 25 km west of the city. Founded in 1964, it's the world's first orangutan rehabilitation center - Guinness World Record for the oldest still-active program. Since its creation, more than 300 orphaned or trafficking-confiscated individuals have been released into the wild. Arrive before 10 AM for the first feeding session from the forest platform.

⏱ 120 min
2

Continue to the outdoor nursery - young orangutans in final-stage rehabilitation learn to climb and swing before being released into the 43 km² forest reserve. You see adolescents playing while rangers monitor from a distance. The difference from Semenggoh: here there's also a nursery for younger ones, off-limits to visitors to prevent transmission of human diseases.

⏱ 60 min
3

Follow up with the Bornean Sun Bear Conservation Centre right next door - the Malayan sun bear is the world's smallest bear and the least studied of all. Rescued from the pet trade, they learn here to recover wild behaviors in forest enclosures before being released.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 80 km · 2h
Day 17

Kinabatangan

The only river sanctuary on Earth where wild orangutans, pygmy elephants, and proboscis monkeys share the same forest corridor

★★★★★
1

Check into a lodge in Sukau on the Kinabatangan River - the only way to properly see wildlife here is from a boat, and cruises are included in all lodge packages. The river runs 560 km from the Crocker Range to the Sulu Sea, and its protected forest corridor is the only place in the world where you can encounter wild orangutans, Bornean pygmy elephants, and proboscis monkeys simultaneously.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Sunset cruise between 4 PM and 6 PM - the time when proboscis descend from trees to settle by the riverbank for the night. Harems with their dominant males gather on tributaries like the Menanggul River. Saltwater crocodiles are regularly visible from the boat.

⏱ 120 min

Night cruise after dinner with powerful torches. The light makes animals' eyes shine in the darkness - civets, slow lorises, owls. Crocodiles with only their eyes visible at water level are particularly impressive at night.

⏱ 90 min
Day 18

Kinabatangan

The only river sanctuary on Earth where wild orangutans, pygmy elephants, and proboscis monkeys share the same forest corridor

★★★★★

Activities

Dawn cruise from 6 AM - chances of encountering pygmy elephants are best early morning when they come to drink by the riverbank. Only 1,500 remain on all of Borneo, classified as critically endangered. Endemic to Borneo, they're the smallest elephants in Asia. Also look up at the trees above the water: wild orangutans sleep in nests built every evening and are visible at sunrise.

⏱ 120 min

Forest hike with a local guide in the afternoon. Oil palm plantations encircle the river on both sides - the protected forest corridor is only a few hundred meters wide in places. The guide shows what tourist signs don't.

⏱ 150 min
Day 19

Kinabatangan

The only river sanctuary on Earth where wild orangutans, pygmy elephants, and proboscis monkeys share the same forest corridor

★★★★★

Activities

Final morning cruise on the oxbow lakes set back from the main river - these cutoff channels are the quietest spots for bird watching. Eight hornbill species live along the Kinabatangan, along with several kingfisher species. Some guides combine this outing with a detour to Gomantong Caves, Sabah's most important source of edible bird's nests.

⏱ 150 min

Day 20

Kota Kinabalu

Where coral islands float minutes offshore and Southeast Asia's highest peak rises from the jungle behind

★★★★☆
1

Arrive in Kota Kinabalu - capital of Sabah, nicknamed KK. The city was nearly razed by 1945 bombing and rebuilt almost entirely after the war. Climb to Signal Hill Observatory for an overview of the bay and the islands of Tunku Abdul Rahman Marine Park.

⏱ 45 min
2

Head down to the Filipino Market by the waterfront for lunch - Sabah's largest fresh seafood market. Choose your fish, crab, or prawns at the counter, and one of the stalls across the way cooks it to order. No menu, no set list.

⏱ 90 min
3

Continue to the City Mosque on the heights - nicknamed the floating mosque because it's built on an artificial lagoon that makes it appear suspended on water at sunset. Open to non-Muslims outside prayer times.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Sunset from the waterfront - KK is one of the rare cities in Southeast Asia where you see the sun drop directly into the sea. The terraces of bars and restaurants on the Waterfront fill up from 5:30 PM onward.

⏱ 60 min

Kota Kinabalu

★★★★☆

Activities

Take the ferry from KK port to the islands of Tunku Abdul Rahman Marine Park - 15 minutes crossing to Sapi or Manukan. The coral reefs around Sapi shelter green turtles, clownfish, and moray eels. The marine park covers five islands less than 10 km from the city center.

Day 22

Kota Kinabalu

Where coral islands float minutes offshore and Southeast Asia's highest peak rises from the jungle behind

★★★★☆
1

Day trip to Kinabalu Park - UNESCO-listed for its exceptional biodiversity. Without climbing to the summit, the park is worth visiting for its carnivorous plant gardens, its giant Rafflesia - the world's largest flower, which can reach a meter in diameter - and its montane forest trails. Mount Kinabalu at 4,095 meters is the highest mountain in Southeast Asia.

⏱ 180 min
2

Then descend to Poring Hot Springs, 40 km from the park. Natural pools at 40°C in the middle of tropical rainforest - the contrast after a morning at altitude is striking.
