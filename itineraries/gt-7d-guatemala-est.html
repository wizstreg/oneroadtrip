Rio Dulce

A tropical river guarded by a 17th-century fortress, gateway to the Caribbean Sea

★★☆☆☆
📍 Castillo de San Felipe de Lara Río Dulce📍 Rio Dulce Guatemala Fronteras📍 Parque Nacional Rio Dulce Guatemala📍 Lago de Izabal Guatemala
1

Catch a bus from Guatemala City heading towards Río Dulce — expect roughly 4 to 5 hours on the road northeast. Along the way, the landscape gradually shifts from the capital's urban sprawl to lush tropical lowlands. Río Dulce is technically the town of Fronteras, nestled on the banks of the river that connects Lake Izabal to the Caribbean Sea. The suspension bridge spanning the río is one of the longest in Central America.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Castillo de San Felipe de Lara deserves half a day on its own. Built in 1651 by the Spanish at the mouth of Lake Izabal to block English pirates sailing upriver, it was stormed and occupied in 1686, then rebuilt in 1956. Inside you'll find small vaulted rooms, period cannons, and ramparts with unobstructed views over the lake. The site is open daily from 8 AM to 5 PM. If you can, circle it by lancha — the view from the water is even better than from land.

⏱ 75 min
3

Río Dulce National Park has protected this river corridor since 1955. From the dock at Fronteras, board a lancha and head upstream through the gorge: limestone cliffs rise over 100 metres on either side, draped in vegetation so thick it nearly touches the water's surface. Keep your eyes peeled — grey herons, pelicans, and kingfishers nest in the rock face. Manatees are regular visitors to these calm waters, though spotting one is largely a matter of luck.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Paddle through the mangroves on the outskirts of town by kayak or canoe. Local operators rent them out for half-day trips. The atmosphere here is completely different from the main river channel — stilt houses disappear into the vegetation, and fishing dugouts cross your path.

⏱ 120 min

In the late afternoon, head to the hot waterfall at Finca El Paraíso, about twenty kilometres along the northern shore of the lake. A 12-metre cascade of hot water (between 30 and 40°C) plunges into a cold river pool — the contrast feels like a natural jungle spa. The spot is popular with locals on weekends, so try to go on a weekday if you can.

Rio Dulce

A tropical river guarded by a 17th-century fortress, gateway to the Caribbean Sea

★★☆☆☆
📍 Refugio Bocas del Polochic El Estor Guatemala📍 El Estor Lago Izabal Guatemala📍 Canon El Boqueron Guatemala📍 Lago de Izabal Guatemala
1

Rise before dawn and take a lancha to the Bocas del Polochic Reserve at the western end of Lake Izabal. This 20,760-hectare wetland, designated a Ramsar site in 1996, is formed by the Río Polochic delta. It's Guatemala's main manatee sanctuary — 17th-century Spanish explorers famously mistook them for mermaids. The reserve is also home to 250 bird species (both migratory and resident), river otters, howler monkeys, and, in theory, all five of the country's wild cat species. Leave early: manatees are most likely to surface in the first hours of the morning.

⏱ 180 min
2

On the way back to Río Dulce along the lake's northern shore, stop at Cañón El Boquerón. This narrow limestone canyon is navigated by rowboat — local guides will drop you off at the entrance. The water is clear, the walls rise about ten metres and are covered in orchids and moss. If you continue on foot through the current, you'll reach a small interior beach. The atmosphere feels almost otherworldly.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Birdwatching at sunrise on Lake Izabal — the 50 km-long, 20 km-wide lake sees few visitors, and the conditions for birdlife are exceptional. Keel-billed toucans, cormorants, and egrets share the waters. Bring binoculars.

⏱ 90 min

Evening swim in the river from the dock of your lodge. Río Dulce is home to several eco-lodges on stilts built directly over the water — some have no road access at all and can only be reached by boat, which gives the whole experience a timeless, off-the-grid feel.

Stop 3

Livingston

A boat-only Garifuna village where African drums and coconut stews replace Maya markets

★★★★★
📍 Livingston Guatemala Garifuna📍 Playa Blanca Livingston Guatemala📍 Siete Altares Livingston Guatemala📍 Embarcadero Livingston Guatemala
1

Livingston can only be reached by boat — no road connects this town to the rest of Guatemala. The lancha from Río Dulce takes about 1h15, heading downriver before rounding the coast to the Caribbean side. Arriving by water, facing this village perched on a limestone bluff, is an unforgettable moment. On land, you won't find Maya markets or embroidered traditional dress: here it's reggaeton and dreadlocks. Livingston is the country's main Garifuna heartland. This people emerged from the intermarriage of shipwrecked Africans (a Nigerian vessel ran aground off Saint Vincent in 1635) and indigenous Caribs, and were later deported by the British to Roatán (Honduras) before gradually migrating to Guatemala in the early 19th century.

⏱ 60 min
2

Wander through the streets of Livingston to take in the cultural contrast. Garifuna murals on the walls, the scent of coconut in the air, and the beat of primero and segunda drums are a world away from the rest of Guatemala. Be sure to order a tapado, the local signature dish: a stew of shrimp, crab, and fish simmered in spiced coconut milk — the same dish locals have been serving arriving travellers here for the past two centuries.

⏱ 90 min
3

Follow the coastal trail to the Siete Altares, a series of seven waterfalls and natural pools carved by a creek tumbling downhill to the sea. Allow about 40 minutes on foot from the town centre. The last pool is swimmable and surrounded by dense tropical vegetation. Go in the morning — in the afternoon, the water can rise quickly after upstream rainfall.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Catch a live Garifuna music session at one of the waterfront bars in the evening. Punta music, with its drums and polyphonic singing, was originally played at funerals — today it's the soundtrack to every celebration along the Caribbean coast of Central America. The Feast of San Isidro Labrador on May 15 is the town's main annual event.

⏱ 90 min

Take a lancha out to Playa Blanca, a white-sand beach accessible only by boat from Livingston, about twenty minutes away. It's one of the few beaches on Guatemala's Caribbean coast with genuine white sand and translucent water. Local operators arrange full-day trips.

Stop 4

Coban

A city cradled in clouds, founded by Dominicans in 1543, capital of coffee and orchids

★★★★☆
📍 Cobán Alta Verapaz Guatemala📍 Parque Central La Paz Cobán📍 Vivero Verapaz Cobán orchidées📍 Finca Santa Margarita Cobán café
1

Head back to Río Dulce by lancha (1h15), then drive to Cobán via the northern shore of Lake Izabal — expect 4 to 5 hours of travel in total. The transition is striking: you leave the Caribbean heat behind and climb into the misty mountains of Alta Verapaz, above 1,300 metres. Founded in 1543 by Dominican friars, Cobán was dubbed 'the city among the clouds' by the Q'eqchi' people. In the 19th century, a wave of German settlers arrived to develop the coffee plantations — their influence is still visible in the architecture.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Vivero Verapaz, a 30-minute walk from the centre, is a former coffee estate converted into an orchid conservatory. It houses around 650 different species. The best time to visit is between November and January, when the grounds erupt in a spectacular carpet of colour during peak blooming season. Outside this window, it's still a remarkable botanical garden.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Museo El Príncipe Maya, tucked away on the main square, holds a private collection of ceramics, jewellery, and pre-Columbian artefacts from the Q'eqchi' region. It's a useful introduction before heading to Semuc Champey — the site was considered sacred by the Maya, and the museum provides valuable context.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Visit Finca Santa Margarita to learn where Cobán's coffee comes from — widely regarded as some of Guatemala's finest. The guided tour of the plantation walks you through the entire process, from picking to roasting. The cool, humid climate of Alta Verapaz, with its persistent local drizzle known as chipi-chipi, creates ideal conditions for the slow maturation of the beans.

⏱ 90 min

Try kak'ik at a local restaurant in the town centre. This spicy turkey stew, served with tamalitos (corn dough wrapped in dried banana leaves), is a traditional Q'eqchi' dish recognised as intangible cultural heritage. It's Cobán's quintessential speciality — don't leave without trying it at least once.

★★★★☆
📍 Semuc Champey Guatemala piscines naturelles📍 Mirador Semuc Champey📍 Kanba Caves Semuc Champey Guatemala📍 Lanquín Guatemala village
1

Full day at Semuc Champey, 65 km from Cobán — allow 1h30 of driving, the last 20 kilometres on a bumpy dirt track. Join a shuttle from your accommodation or hire a pick-up. Semuc Champey means 'where the water hides in the stone' in Q'eqchi': the Cahabón River vanishes beneath a 300-metre natural limestone bridge, and above it a series of cascading pools has formed, their colour shifting from azure to emerald depending on the light. Leave by 8 AM — organised tour groups typically don't arrive until after 1 PM.

⏱ 45 min
2

Hike up to the Semuc Champey viewpoint: allow 30 to 45 minutes of steep climbing through the jungle from the site entrance. The bird's-eye view over the entire chain of cascading pools, framed by tropical forest, is Guatemala's most iconic photograph. The trail is tough when it's wet — proper walking shoes beat sandals every time. Head straight down to the pools for a swim afterwards.

⏱ 90 min
3

Explore the Kamba (Kanba) Caves, adjacent to the pools. The tour is done by candlelight, wading through waist-deep water in places, squeezing through narrow passages, and sliding down natural rock chutes. A local guide is essential — they'll adapt to your level. Not for the claustrophobic, but for everyone else, it's an unforgettable experience.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Swim in the natural pools at Semuc Champey. The water is refreshing — some shallow pools let you stretch out on the limestone rocks, while others reach two to three metres deep. Head down to the lower pools, which see far fewer visitors: you'll often have the small cascades trickling over the stone all to yourself.

⏱ 120 min

Tubing on the Cahabón River on the way back from Semuc Champey: you float downstream on an inner tube through a stretch of fast-moving rapids covering several hundred metres. Local operators run this activity from the village of Lanquín, 11 km from the site. Bring a change of clothes.

1

The Biotopo del Quetzal, an hour south of Cobán on the road to Guatemala City, is one of the few reserves where you can still spot a quetzal in the wild — this iridescent green bird with a long red tail was sacred to the Maya. The site offers two trails through the cloud forest (2 km and 4 km). Arrive early, before 9 AM, to maximise your chances. Even without a quetzal sighting, the moss-draped, epiphyte-laden forest is worth the trip on its own.

⏱ 120 min
2

Orquigonia is a botanical garden dedicated to orchid conservation, and can be visited in about an hour and a half with a guide. The trail runs roughly 1 km through grounds planted with hundreds of orchid species and epiphytes. The guides are excellent educators — they'll teach you how to tell species apart and lend you a magnifying glass. The view from the garden's mirador over the valley is an added bonus.

⏱ 90 min
3

Las Victorias National Park sits right within the city of Cobán, northwest of the centre — a pocket of primary forest woven into the urban fabric, crisscrossed by marked trails. You can picnic, watch birds, and stroll away from the bustle. Entry is free.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Browse Cobán's artisan market in the morning: Q'eqchi' textiles, silver jewellery, and cardamom-based products (the region's top export crop) are sold directly by the producers. Cobán is also one of the world's cardamom capitals — you'll catch its scent on every street corner.

⏱ 60 min

In the late afternoon, settle into a café on the main square and savour some locally grown Alta Verapaz coffee. The light mist — the famous chipi-chipi — that often wraps around Cobán in the evening gives the town a soft, melancholic atmosphere unlike anywhere else. A fine excuse to linger over a hot cup.

Guatemala

The capital and launchpad for Guatemala's wild east, home to La Aurora international airport

★★★★☆
📍 Aeropuerto La Aurora Guatemala City📍 Zona Viva Guatemala City📍 Museo Nacional de Arqueología y Etnología Guatemala City📍 Mercado Central Guatemala City
1

Drive back from Cobán to Guatemala City — about 5 hours via the CA-9 Norte and the CA-14. The route crosses the highlands before descending into the capital's basin. If you have an evening flight, leave first thing in the morning. If you have time, stop at La Cumbre, the crossroads between Cobán and Guatemala City, for a lunch with views over both slopes.

⏱ 60 min
2

In Guatemala City, the Museum of Archaeology and Ethnology in Parque Aurora showcases the country's finest pre-Hispanic pieces, including Maya stelae, scale models of Tikal, and collections of polychrome ceramics. It's a fitting way to close a week in the east by putting everything you've seen into perspective. The museum is closed on Mondays.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

If you have a few hours before your flight from La Aurora airport, the Zona Viva (Zone 10) is the liveliest neighbourhood in the capital for a final meal. Restaurants in the area serve specialities from every region of the country — a solid gastronomic wrap-up before departure.
