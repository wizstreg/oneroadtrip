Sevilla

★★★★★
1

Beneath the stalls of Triana Market lie the ruins of a castle that made Europe tremble: the Castillo de San Jorge, seat of the Spanish Holy Office from 1481 to 1785—with an interruption from 1626 to 1639 when the tribunal had to relocate to the Casa de los Tavera due to flooding from the Guadalquivir. Spain's first Inquisition tribunal, the most active, the most brutal. Three hundred years of interrogations, torture, and trials unfolded in these cellars until its definitive transfer in 1785, when the walls were buried beneath the present-day market. Today it's a free basement museum: visible cells, torture instruments, nominal lists of the condemned. Chilling and necessary. Closed Mondays.

⏱ 60 min
2

Plaza de San Francisco, right in the heart of downtown, is the visible flip side of the Inquisition: here stood Seville's main quemadero, the stake where the Holy Office's condemned were publicly burned. Six pyres in the first year, 1481, which consumed primarily Jewish conversos and later Moriscos. The square today serves as the starting point for Holy Week processions—the pasos emerging with hooded Nazarenos have been passing through since 1521. No plaque mentions the pyres: it's this silence that hits hardest.

⏱ 30 min
3

Triana, the former gypsy and sailor suburb across the Guadalquivir, deserves more than a detour: this is where those expelled from Seville after the 1391 pogroms lived, and where the historic rivalry with 'los de Sevilla' was born. The Iglesia de Santa Ana, the city's first Gothic-Mudéjar church (begun in 1276), houses the Virgin of Hope—Triana's pride during the Madrugá on Good Friday. Calle Betis, along the river, is the place for a fino facing the sunset over La Giralda across the way.

⏱ 75 min
4

The Puente de Triana (Puente de Isabel II), built in 1852 on the model of Paris's Carrousel Bridge, connects Triana to downtown. The city's first fixed bridge—before that, you crossed via a pontoon bridge. At the foot on the Triana side, the Carmen chapel and the Altozano, where statues of bullfighter Juan Belmonte and the flamenco singer stand. Obligatory landmark for Sevillians at aperitif hour.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Dinner at Casa Morales (calle García de Vinuesa), a taberna open since 1850. Classic atmosphere with barrels of manzanilla lined against the wall, slates for chalking up your tab, hand-sliced Iberian ham. Classic tapas: croquetas, espinacas con garbanzos, montadito de pringá. Gentle prices (15-20 euros per person). No reservations, elbow your way to the bar.

⏱ 90 min

Flamenco tablao at La Casa de la Memoria (Cuna 6), a small venue in a 15th-century patio. Daily shows at 7:30 PM and 9 PM, one hour, 22 euros. Unlike the big tourist tablaos, here the palo (style) varies each evening and the artists are references—Patricia Guerrero, Antonio Rey have performed here. Book 2-3 days ahead.

⏱ 60 min

Sevilla

★★★★★
1

Seville Cathedral is the world's largest Gothic cathedral—11,520 m², more than 50 chapels, central nave 42 m high. Built from 1402 on the site of the great Almohad mosque, whose architects reportedly said: 'Let us build a church so large that those who see it finished will take us for madmen.' Mission accomplished. Inside, Christopher Columbus's tomb is borne by four crowned statues representing the kingdoms of Castile, Aragon, León, and Navarre. DNA analysis in 2006 showed that the Seville remains are at least related to Columbus, without total authentication; Santo Domingo, which also claims to hold the remains, refuses testing. La Giralda, a 1198 Almohad minaret, now serves as the bell tower: climb via 35 ramps (no stairs, designed for horses).

⏱ 150 min
2

The Hospital de la Caridad, 300 m from the Cathedral, preserves one of Spain's most brutal pictorial cycles. Founded in 1674 by Miguel de Mañara—a historical figure often associated with the Don Juan myth (wrongly: Tirso de Molina had published his play in 1630, three years after Mañara's birth). According to legend, he repented after a macabre vision in which he saw himself dead; in reality, his wife Jerónima's death in 1661 likely turned him toward charity. He commissioned Valdés Leal to create two funereal paintings for his hospital for the poor: 'In Ictu Oculi' (a bishop struck down dying) and 'Finis Gloriae Mundi' (a decomposing knight, worms included, with Christ weighing his sins on a scale). The Golden Age's most brutal memento mori. Murillo painted eight softer canvases alongside to balance it out. 6 euros.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Reales Alcázares, right next to the Cathedral, are Europe's oldest royal palace still in use. Built by Peter the Cruel in the 14th century on Almohad foundations, with Morisco artisans from Toledo—it's the archetype of Mudéjar style. The terraced gardens, dating in part to the 12th-century Almohads, became famous as the filming location for Game of Thrones (the gardens of Dorne). Book online; the shared line with the Cathedral is unbearable in season.

⏱ 120 min
4

Barrio Santa Cruz, the former Jewish quarter transformed into a picturesque labyrinth, holds the memory of an ancient presence erased by the 1492 expulsion. A few commemorative plaques, a street still called 'calle de la Judería,' but mainly the compressed alleys climbing toward the Cathedral. Touristy, certainly, but after 8 PM when the groups have left, it becomes manageable again.

⏱ 60 min
5

The Torre del Oro on the Guadalquivir, a 1220 Almohad watchtower, controlled the port entrance and marked the end of a chain stretched across the river. This is where the galleons returned from the Americas loaded with gold docked, and where they were unloaded directly at the Casa de Contratación. Small naval museum inside, interesting if you have time. 3 euros.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Dinner at Eslava (calle Eslava, San Lorenzo), 15 minutes' walk from downtown. Creative tapas: egg on boletus-truffle biscuit (2010 Spanish champion, still served), pork ribs with 'purr-purr' sauce. Packed every evening, lines from 7:30 PM. Count on 25-30 euros per person with wine. Reservations only for the restaurant's table side—bar is direct queue.

⏱ 90 min

Nighttime stroll along the Guadalquivir from Torre del Oro to Puente de Triana, then crossing and drinks on Calle Betis. View of the illuminated Giralda and the Hospital de la Caridad across the way. Allow 1.5 hours, golden light at dusk.

⏱ 90 min

Cordoba

★★★★★
1

The Mezquita-Cathedral is Andalusia's architectural shock. 856 columns in granite, jasper, marble, and porphyry, topped by red brick and white checkered arches—each composed of four alternating voussoirs. Begun in 786 by Abd al-Rahman I on the ruins of a Visigothic basilica itself built on a Roman temple. Expanded four times until the 10th century, it became one of the medieval world's largest mosques. After the 1236 reconquest, the bishop planted a Gothic-Renaissance cathedral right in the middle in the 16th century. When Charles V discovered the result, he reportedly told the workers: 'You have destroyed something unique to build something commonplace.' Book online, long lines in season.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Judería, former Jewish quarter inscribed as UNESCO in 1994, bears traces of a decimated community. Córdoba housed Al-Andalus's largest aljama in the 10th century—Maimonides was born here in 1138. The pogrom of June 6, 1391, killed much of the Jewish population in a few hours, burned houses, forced survivors to convert or flee. The 1315 synagogue, on calle de los Judíos, is one of three medieval synagogues preserved in Spain (along with two in Toledo). Small (7×7 m), Mudéjar, stuccos carved with Hebrew verses. Free entry for EU citizens.

⏱ 75 min
3

Casa de Sefarad, 50 m from the synagogue, complements the visit with a private museum dedicated to Sephardic culture—domestic life, festivals, music, famous women of Al-Andalus, Inquisition, and Maimonides. Not large but well done. Good preparation for understanding what unfolded in the neighboring quarter between the 10th and 15th centuries. 6 euros.

⏱ 60 min
4

Calleja de las Flores, a 50 m dead-end alley, has become Córdoba's postcard photo: pots of geraniums hanging on white walls, view of the Mezquita's tower at the end. Touristy but well done, especially in May during the Fiesta de los Patios (best-decorated patio contest, UNESCO-listed since 2012).

⏱ 20 min
5

The Alcázar de los Reyes Cristianos, 200 m from the Mezquita, served as headquarters for the Catholic Monarchs during the Granada campaign (1482-1492). Isabella received Christopher Columbus here in 1486 to discuss the expedition project. It also became the seat of Córdoba's Inquisition tribunal, from the 15th century until the definitive abolition of the Spanish Inquisition in 1834. Andalusian-style terraced gardens, view over the Guadalquivir. 5 euros.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Dinner at Casa Pepe de la Judería (calle Romero 1), institution of traditional Cordoban cuisine. Specialties: salmorejo (cold tomato-bread-ham soup, denser than gazpacho), flamenquín (ham-cheese roll, breaded), rabo de toro (braised oxtail). Count on 30-40 euros per person. Beautiful flowered patio upstairs, reserve to eat there.

⏱ 90 min

Nighttime crossing of the Roman Bridge (1st century BC, 16 arches) to the Torre de la Calahorra and back. Illuminated Mezquita viewed from afar, reflections on the Guadalquivir, not a Cordoban in sight after 10 PM. 800 m round trip, 30 minutes.

⏱ 45 min

Medina Azahara

★★★★☆
1

Madinat al-Zahra was Córdoba's ghost city: a palace-city built in 936 by Abd al-Rahman III for his favorite Zahra ('the flower'), capital of Al-Andalus for 70 years. Three terraced levels, 10,000 workers, pink marble from Tarragona, ivory, jasper imported from Byzantium. Sacked and pillaged in 1010 by the Berbers, abandoned, forgotten for 900 years. Rediscovered in the 19th century, only 10% has been excavated—the rest sleeps beneath olive groves. UNESCO since 2018. Abd al-Rahman III's Rich Hall is the obligatory stop: the only fully restored room, carved stucco in arabesques.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Interpretation Center, 2 km from the site, recounts the caliphal history in 45 minutes of multimedia projection and models. Well done, essential for understanding what you'll then see on the site itself—which has almost no walls left standing. Do this before the shuttle to the ruins.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Lunch on the way to Granada, at Bodegas Campos leaving Córdoba. Century-old cellar-restaurant (1908), produces its own Montilla-Moriles wines. Barrels signed by Charlton Heston, Federico García Lorca, King Juan Carlos. Count on 35-50 euros for the menu.

⏱ 90 min

Lucena

★★★☆☆
1

Lucena is one of Spain's great Sephardic unknowns: a majority-Jewish city between the 9th and 12th centuries under Al-Andalus, nicknamed Eliossana—'God has saved us.' Capital of Andalusian Judaism for 300 years, renowned Talmudic school. Its population fled before the Almohad invasions of 1146-1148, which forced conversion or death. The Castillo del Moral, where Boabdil was reportedly imprisoned after his defeat at Lucena in 1483, is now the municipal museum—the episode is minor in the Reconquista timeline but narrated locally as a great feat.

⏱ 60 min
2

Lucena's Jewish necropolis, discovered in 2006 following roadworks, is the largest known on the Iberian Peninsula: 346 tombs, dated from the 9th to 11th centuries. Inscriptions in Hebrew and Arabic—proof of a linguistic coexistence unimaginable today. Site partially visible behind a fence at the city's northern exit, free access. Modest but moving.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Coffee-tapas break at La Casa del Vino, on Plaza Nueva. Montilla-Moriles wines (neighboring DOC), regional specialty of draft fino. Tapas of salmorejo and Iberian salchichón. Good spot to catch your breath before hitting the road to Granada.

⏱ 60 min

Granada

★★★★★
1

The Alhambra is the last Nasrid palace in Spain—and Al-Andalus's final breath. Built from 1238 by Mohammed I, expanded under Yusuf I and Mohammed V in the 14th century, surrendered on January 2, 1492, by Boabdil to the Catholic Monarchs. The Court of the Lions (124 white marble columns, fountain carried by 12 stone lions), the Hall of the Two Sisters with its muqarnas (stucco stalactites) defying the laws of statics, the Court of the Myrtles and its reflecting pool—everything is designed to demonstrate that only God is eternal, and earthly beauty is but a metaphor. Mandatory reservation 2-3 months in advance, imposed time slot for the Nasrid Palaces. 19 euros.

⏱ 240 min
2

The Generalife, summer palace of the Nasrid sultans built around 1319, completes the Alhambra visit. Patio de la Acequia with its 50 crisscrossing water jets, terraced gardens, orange and cypress orchards. This is where the sultans came to escape Granada's heat in July-August—it's always 3-4°C cooler than downtown. Included in the general ticket.

⏱ 60 min
3

12 km south of Granada, on the Motril road, lies the Puerto del Suspiro del Moro—'the Pass of the Moor's Sigh.' This is where Boabdil reportedly cast a final look at Granada, which he had just surrendered on January 2, 1492, and burst into tears. His mother Aisha then reportedly launched the famous phrase: 'Weep like a woman for what you could not defend like a man.' The pass today is a parking lot on the N-323 with a modern statue and a restaurant—the staging is basic, but the panorama of the Alhambra from afar is striking.

⏱ 30 min
4

The Royal Chapel, adjoining the Cathedral, houses the tombs of Isabella the Catholic and Ferdinand of Aragon—the two monarchs who toppled Granada, expelled the Jews, and financed Christopher Columbus, all in 1492. Carrara marble funeral sculptures commissioned by Charles V from Domenico Fancelli. But the real shock is the crypt below: the lead coffins of the two monarchs and their daughter Joanna the Mad and her husband Philip the Fair are visible, placed on the ground, sealed. Austere to the point of severity. 6 euros.

⏱ 60 min
5

The Madrasa Yusufiyya, on the square adjoining the Cathedral, is the last Quranic school of Al-Andalus still standing—founded in 1349 by Yusuf I. The original mihrab with its stucco arabesques has survived, now embedded in a baroque building. After 1492, the Catholic Monarchs transformed it into a city hall, which explains its preservation. Small, 3 euros, but striking in contrast: Islamic architectural grammar encircled by Christian baroque.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Dinner at Bodegas Castañeda (calle Almireceros), taberna since 1929, steps from the cathedral. Barrels of house vermouth, old saint paintings, daily specials in chalk. Specialty: the tabla de variados—board of ham, lomo, cheese, salchichón. Free tapas with each drink, old-school style—Granada practice maintained here, rare elsewhere. 20-25 euros per person.

⏱ 90 min

Nighttime stroll on the Paseo de los Tristes, at the foot of the Alhambra on the Carrera del Darro side. This is where funeral processions passed toward the Sacromonte cemetery in the 16th century—hence the name ('walk of the sad ones'). Illuminated Alhambra viewed from below, 1 km along the Darro.

⏱ 45 min

Granada

★★★★★
1

The Albaicín, the former Moorish quarter preserved almost intact, climbs opposite the Alhambra on the facing hill. White sloping alleys, cármenes (houses with flowered patios hidden behind blind walls), fountains, old ladies still sweeping their doorsteps. UNESCO since 1994 as complement to the Alhambra. Morisco residents (forcibly converted Muslims) were expelled in 1568 after the Alpujarras revolt—the quarter was repopulated by Christians from other regions, but the Arab urbanism hasn't budged.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Mirador de San Nicolás offers Granada's most famous view: the Alhambra face-on, at the same altitude, with the snow-capped Sierra Nevada in the background. Bill Clinton stopped here in 1997 and declared it 'the most beautiful sunset in the world'—the phrase is engraved on a plaque. Always packed at sunset, with itinerant musicians, djembe players, beer vendors. Go in the morning instead for the photo, or to the Mirador de San Miguel Alto just above, quieter.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Huerta de San Vicente, 1.5 km from downtown in Parque García Lorca, is the summer house where Federico García Lorca spent every summer of his life between 1926 and 1936. This is where he wrote the majority of his late work—'Blood Wedding,' 'Yerma,' 'The House of Bernarda Alba.' The piano, the desk, annotated manuscripts, the room where he spent his last night on August 16, 1936, before his arrest. Frozen atmosphere, few tourists. 3 euros, guided tour every 30 minutes.

⏱ 75 min
4

The Barranco de Víznar, 8 km north of Granada in a pine forest of the Sierra de Alfacar, is one of the great memorial sites of the Civil War. Federico García Lorca was shot here at dawn on August 19, 1936, by the Nationalists, along with Republican schoolteacher Dióscoro Galindo and two anarchist bullfighter banderilleros—Joaquín Arcollas and Francisco Galadí. His body has never been found despite repeated excavations since 2009. Discreet memorial in the middle of the pine forest, ceramic plaques with the names of the executed. About 3,000 Republicans executed here between 1936 and 1939, mass grave still not exhumed. Powerful place, imposed silence.

⏱ 60 min
5

Sacromonte, the hill parallel to the Albaicín, is Granada's former gypsy quarter—troglodyte dwellings carved into rock since the 16th century. Still inhabited today (200 occupied caves), zambras (family flamenco shows) in the evening. The Sacromonte cave museum gives access to several restored cave-houses, 5 euros, panoramic view of the Alhambra from the top of the hill.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Dinner at Cunini (Pescadería 14), Granada institution for fish—Granada is 1 hour from the sea (Motril), daily catch. Specialty: pijota frita (small fried hake eaten whole with fingers), urta a la roteña (red mullet in Rota tomato sauce). 40-55 euros per person. Fancier than yesterday's tabernas.

⏱ 105 min

Zambra in Sacromonte. Family flamenco show in a cave, more authentic than downtown tablaos. Addresses: Cueva de la Rocío, Cueva los Tarantos, Cueva María la Canastera. Count on 25-30 euros, duration 1 hour, bring a layer (caves are cool).

⏱ 60 min

Alhama De Granada

★★☆☆☆
1

Alhama de Granada—'al-hammam,' the bath—is one of the strongholds that fell before Granada during the Reconquest war. On February 28, 1482, the Catholic Monarchs seized it by night assault. The fall was so striking that a Castilian romance, '¡Ay de mi Alhama!,' was composed immediately after and sung for centuries as the announcement of the Nasrid kingdom's end. The village of 6,000 residents today clings to the edge of a vertiginous 50 m canyon, the Tajo de Alhama. Intact old quarter, white sloping alleys, houses with flowered balconies. Half a day suffices.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Casa de la Inquisición, on the main square, is an early 16th-century Gothic-Renaissance palace that never served as a Holy Office tribunal—the local name is misleading. But the golden stone facade with its coats of arms and Gothic windows is one of western Andalusia's finest. Today private property, exterior visit only, free and open. The supposed presence of the Inquisition in Alhama more likely relates to the persecution of Moriscos after 1568 in the neighboring Alpujarras.

⏱ 20 min
3

The Iglesia del Carmen, 17th-century baroque, is a spectacular ruin on the edge of the Tajo. Burned during the Civil War in 1936, its roof collapsed in the 1960s after heavy snowfall. Restoration project underway since 2018. Belvedere on the neighboring square for the plunging view into the canyon—one of the village's signature photos.

⏱ 30 min
4

Alhama's Mazmorras, underground dungeons carved during the Almohad period in two jar-shaped silos (12th century), served as medieval prison, then grain reserve, then shelter during the Civil War, and today as cultural space. Exceptional acoustics for concerts, permanent exhibition on historical uses. 2 euros, closed Mondays.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Bath at the Alhama Thermal Baths, 2 km from the village. Natural springs at 47°C known since Roman times (1st-century BC bridge right next door), developed into free outdoor pools by the town hall. Open 365 days a year. Bring swimsuit, towel, non-slip sandals. Go early in the morning (before 9 AM) or on weekdays to avoid weekend crowds. Ideal in winter, the water steams in contact with the cold air.

⏱ 120 min

Ronda

★★★★★
1

Ronda sits astride a 100 m deep canyon—el Tajo—that cuts the city in two. The Puente Nuevo, completed in 1793 after 40 years of construction, is the mythical bridge linking the two banks. During August 1936, the Republicans of Ronda who controlled the city threw hundreds of Nationalists alive from the top of the Tajo—the episode is recounted by Ernest Hemingway in chapter 10 of 'For Whom the Bell Tolls' (1940), who had lived in Ronda. A few months later, the Nationalists recaptured the city and executed in turn. The bridge today serves as a postcard; the toll of both purges remains a sensitive local subject.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Plaza de Toros de Ronda, completed in 1785, is Spain's oldest bullfighting arena still in operation and the birthplace of modern bullfighting on foot. Before 1726, bulls were killed from horseback. Francisco Romero (from Ronda) invented bullfighting on foot with muleta here, codified by his grandson Pedro Romero (1754-1839), who reportedly dispatched more than 5,000 bulls without a single injury during his career. Capacity 5,000 seats, two levels of golden stone arcades. Bullfighting museum inside, open except on bullfight days. 9 euros.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Arab Baths of Ronda, built in the 13th century in the Moorish quarter of San Miguel downhill, are the best-preserved Arab baths in Spain. Classic three-room plan (cold, warm, hot), vaults pierced with star-shaped skylights for light, still-visible functional hypocaust. Public baths from Al-Andalus times, abandoned after 1485 (reconquest of Ronda). 4 euros, closed Mondays.

⏱ 45 min
4

The Palacio de Mondragón, former Nasrid palace remodeled in the 16th century after the reconquest, preserves three Mudéjar patios with original azulejos. Now houses the municipal archaeology museum, with Bronze Age objects found in the Sierra de Grazalema. Spectacular view from the terrace over the sierra and plain. 4 euros.

⏱ 60 min
5

The Casa del Rey Moro, on the edge of the Tajo, never housed a Moorish king despite its name—it's an 18th-century palace built on Nasrid foundations. Its centerpiece: the Mina, a staircase carved into rock in the 14th century that descends 231 steps to the bottom of the gorge to reach the Guadalevín River. During sieges, Christian prisoners were forced to haul water up in jugs. Very narrow, dim light, not for the claustrophobic. 8 euros.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Dinner at Tragatá (calle Nueva 4), inventive tapas by Benito Gómez—double Michelin-starred chef with Bardal 100 m away. Tragatá is the informal version, gently priced: liquid potato tortilla, baby squid croqueta, fried sea anemones. 25-35 euros per person. No reservations, line at 8 PM.

⏱ 90 min

Sunset at the Mirador de Ronda (paseo Blas Infante, just in front of the bullring). Plunging view over the sierra, golden light on the old town's white walls. Classic Tajo photo accessible from the edge by walking 200 m.

⏱ 45 min

Antequera

★★★☆☆
1

The Dolmens of Antequera open another dimension in the road trip: death on a grand scale, 5,700 years before us. The Dolmen de Menga, built around 3750-3650 BC, is one of Europe's largest megalithic tombs—27.5 m long, 32 stone blocks, the heaviest weighing 180 tonnes (slightly more than a Boeing 737). When it was opened in the 19th century, the skeletons of several hundred people were found inside. Atypical orientation: not toward the rising sun like most European dolmens, but toward the Peña de los Enamorados, a human face-shaped mountain 7 km away. Direct ritual link with the landscape. UNESCO 2016.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Dolmen de Viera, 70 m from Menga, is later (2500-2000 BC) and more modest—long narrow corridor leading to a rectangular burial chamber. It's the only one of the three oriented toward the rising sun: at the spring and summer equinoxes, the sun's rays traverse the corridor and illuminate the chamber. Five minutes to visit, but see it to understand the diversity of funerary conceptions on a single site.

⏱ 15 min
3

The Peña de los Enamorados, an 880 m limestone mountain toward which Menga points, owes its name to a local legend from Al-Andalus times: Tagzona, Moorish princess of Antequera, falls in love with Christian soldier Tello, taken prisoner. Pursued by the girl's father, they jump together from the top of the cliff. The rock's shape, seen from Antequera, indeed evokes a human face in profile looking toward the sky. Legend or not, the dolmen's prehistoric orientation predates this story by 5,000 years.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Lunch in Antequera before resuming the road to Málaga. Restaurante Arte de Cozina, calle Calzada 27, seasonal Andalusian cuisine at reasonable prices (25-35 euros menu). Local specialty: porra antequerana, Antequera's version of Cordoban salmorejo, thicker, garnished with hard-boiled egg and tuna.

★★★★☆
1

The San Rafael Cemetery, 4 km north of downtown Málaga, holds the most brutal Civil War memory on the coast. Between 1937 and 1957, more than 4,000 Republicans were executed and thrown into mass graves here—magistrates, teachers, trade unionists, militants, simple sympathizers. Franco's regime used Málaga, taken in February 1937, as a targeted extermination camp. Exhumations began in 2006, recovering 2,840 bodies. Memorial inaugurated in 2009, with identified names engraved on black granite steles arranged in a circle. Visit with audio guide in English, free entry. A place still little known to tourists, and that's why you must go.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Gibralfaro Castle, 14th-century Nasrid fortress perched 130 m above the port, offers the view from where the boats of the Desbandá departed in February 1937. 150,000 Republican civilians fleeing the advancing Nationalist troops toward Almeria on the N-340—the coastal road—were machine-gunned from the sea by the cruisers Canarias and Almirante Cervera, and bombed from the sky by Italian and German aviation. Between 3,000 and 5,000 dead in 5 days according to conservative estimates, among them a majority of women, children, and elderly. Doctor Norman Bethune documented the carnage. No plaque on site. Many Spaniards still don't know the episode. 3.50 euros for the castle.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Alcazaba of Málaga, downhill from Gibralfaro, is one of the best-preserved Muslim palatial fortresses in Spain. Built by the Hammudids in the 11th century on Phoenician and Roman foundations, expanded by the Almoravids then the Nasrids. Formidable defensive system: 110 towers, three concentric enclosures. Plunging view over the Roman theater attached below—Phoenician-Roman-Muslim stacked on 30 m of height. Combined ticket with Gibralfaro at 5.50 euros.

⏱ 90 min
4

The Roman Theatre of Málaga, uncovered in 1951 during construction work on the House of Culture (which was eventually demolished to save it), dates from the 1st century AD under Augustus. 31 m in diameter, estimated capacity 220 spectators. Built partly with stones recycled from an older edifice, and itself partially recycled by the Muslims to build the Alcazaba above. Free entry.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Final lunch at El Pimpi (calle Granada 62), Málaga's mythical taberna since 1971, near the Picasso museum. Barrels of sweet Malagueño wine signed by Antonio Banderas (co-owner), Pablo Neruda, Joaquín Sabina. Classic tapas (croquetas, boquerones, ensaladilla), gentle prices (20-25 euros). Touristy but sincere, well done.

⏱ 90 min

Journey to Málaga-Costa del Sol airport (AGP), 8 km west of downtown. Car return at the terminal—agencies grouped at P2. Cercanías C-1 train from Málaga-Centro Alameda (3 euros, 12 minutes) or taxi flat rate 25-30 euros. Allow 2 hours before a Schengen flight, 3 hours for long-haul.
