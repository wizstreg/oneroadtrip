📆 Best period

June, July, August, September

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 12 and 16 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Summer (June-August): Peak season with warmest weather (15-25°C), accessible remote areas, and traditional festivals including Naadam. All tourist infrastructure operational but highest prices and crowds.

Spring (April-May): Mild temperatures (5-20°C), fewer tourists, and reasonable prices. Some remote areas may still be inaccessible due to weather, but cities and main attractions fully operational.

Autumn (September-October): Comfortable temperatures (8-18°C), stunning fall colors, and fewer crowds. Excellent photography weather with clear skies, though nights become quite cold.

Winter (November-March): Extremely cold (-20 to -10°C) but unique experiences like ice festivals and winter nomadic life. Lowest prices but limited accessibility to remote areas and harsh conditions.

🌍 What to discover in MN?

Nomadic paradise with vast steppes, traditional culture, and incredible hospitality. Experience yurt living, horseback adventures, and authentic nomadic lifestyle. The country offers cultural immersion with natural beauty and adventure activities. Mongolia provides life-changing cultural experiences with natural wonders and cultural authenticity.

Khovd

★★☆☆☆

Khar-Us Nuur Lake - about forty kilometers east of Khovd, this 1,153 km² freshwater lake (the country's second largest) has been a Ramsar site since 1999 for its important wetlands. Hyargas Nuur

UNESCO-listed saltwater lake stretching 75 km, a vital stopover for Siberian migratory birds

★★☆☆☆
📍 Khyargas Nuur Lake Mongolia📍 Hyargas Lake Mongolia📍 Uvs Province Mongolia
1

Hyargas Nuur Lake - saltwater lake in Uvs province, designated a nature reserve since 2000 and integrated into the Great Lakes Basin UNESCO World Heritage site since 2003. At 75 km long and 31 km wide with an average depth of 50 meters, it's one of the country's four largest lakes. Its semi-desert shores, white marble cliffs to the south, and the emerging Khet-suu Khad rock - serving as a perch for migratory cormorants from April to October - make it a remarkable ornithological site. The lake's south side is particularly photogenic.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Migratory bird watching on the lake shores - Hyargas Nuur is a major stopover on the migration route between Siberia and South Asia. Herons, cranes, geese, spoonbills, and, if you're lucky, the white-headed duck (a rare species) are visible in wetland areas bordering the lake. Bring binoculars and visit the shores early morning for best observations.

⏱ 90 min

Horseback or walking excursion around the lake - local providers offer horseback rides along the saline shores. It's an opportunity to cross little-frequented semi-desert steppe zones, observe local wildlife (ibex on heights, marmots), and converse with nomadic families camping near the lake in summer.

Terkhiin Tsagaan Nuur

Volcanic lake at 2,060 m altitude, born from Khorgo's lava flow 8,000 years ago

★★★☆☆
📍 Khorgo Volcano Mongolia📍 Khorgo crater Arkhangai📍 Terkhiin Tsagaan Nuur birdwatching
1

Khorgo Volcano and its lava flows - east of the lake, the Khorgo crater (2,240 m altitude) is Mongolia's last active volcano, extinct for 8,000 years. Its crater measures 200 meters in diameter and 70 to 80 meters deep, surrounded by small groves. From the summit, the panorama over Terkhiin Tsagaan Lake and the surrounding volcanic chain is one of Arkhangai's most spectacular. Around the volcano, the 'basalt yurts' - solidified lava bubbles - form a surprising geological landscape.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Ascent of Khorgo volcano - allow about 3 to 4 hours round trip to reach the crater from camp. The hike is relatively easy and follows a trail up the basalt-covered flanks. Descending into the crater bottom reveals a small central lake and dense vegetation contrasting with the black lava flows. Good walking shoes suffice.

⏱ 180 min

Bird watching on Terkhiin Tsagaan Lake - the lake is designated a wetland of international importance. In summer, swans, cranes, geese, and wild ducks land on its shores. The lake surroundings also shelter marmots and golden eagles. Bring your binoculars.

★★★★★

Ulaan Tsutgalan Falls - it's the Ulaan Tsutgalan River, 'Red Confluence,' that forms this waterfall as it plunges into the Orkhon. Orkhon Valley - designated a UNESCO World Heritage site since 2004, the valley covers approximately 1,220 km² and includes the Karakorum site, former capital of the Mongol Empire. Kharkhorin

Site of Genghis Khan's ancient capital, home to Erdene Zuu's 108 stupas

★★★★★
📍 Erdene Zuu Monastery Kharkhorin📍 Karakorum Museum Mongolia📍 Turtle Rock Karakorum Mongolia
1

Erdene Zuu Monastery - founded in 1585 by Abtai Sain Khan on Karakorum's ruins, it's Mongolia's oldest Buddhist monastery of fixed structure. Its construction stones came directly from the ancient capital's ruins. The enclosure wall of 108 white stupas - 108 being the sacred number of beads in a Buddhist rosary - surrounds a vast grassy terrain punctuated by three Chinese-style temples. In 1939, leader Choibalsan's communist purge destroyed nearly everything; only three temples and the wall remained. The monastery has been active again since 1990. Inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage site in 2004.

⏱ 90 min
2

Karakorum Museum - steps from the monastery, this Japanese-co-financed museum assembles objects from archaeological excavations of Genghis Khan's imperial city. Founded in 1220, Karakorum was the Mongol Empire's capital until Kublai Khan transferred the capital to Beijing. Some vestiges of the city (including two granite tortoises that guarded the gates) are visible in the surroundings.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Walk around Karakorum vestiges - spot the two granite tortoises surviving from the ancient city, as well as the hill east of the monastery offering an overview of the site. If you're there in July, it's possible to attend Kharkhorin's local Naadam, which blends traditional sports (wrestling, horse racing) and Buddhist ceremonies.

Tsagaan Suvarga

Sixty-meter limestone cliffs in colored layers, an ancient seabed in the central Gobi

★★★★★
📍 Tsagaan Suvarga White Stupa Mongolia📍 White Stupa Dundgovi Mongolia📍 Tsagaan Suvarga cliffs Gobi
1

Tsagaan Suvarga, the White Stupa - limestone and clay cliffs 60 meters high and 400 meters long that surge from the desert in a stratified wall of ochre, pink, orange, and reddish colors. What was once the bed of a vanished sea is now an open-air geological book: sedimentary layers reveal marine fossils and seashells millions of years old. The cliff owes its name to its resemblance to a Buddhist stupa. Allow about 1 hour's walk to reach the summit and walk along the edge - watch out for wind, it can be violent. Sunset on the cliffs is particularly spectacular.

Bayanzag

Red sandstone cliffs where the first dinosaur eggs were discovered in 1922

★★★★★
📍 Bayanzag Flaming Cliffs Mongolia📍 Flaming Cliffs dinosaur fossils Mongolia📍 Bayanzag saxaul forest Mongolia
1

Bayanzag, the Flaming Cliffs - these red and ochre sandstone cliffs were christened 'Flaming Cliffs' by American paleontologist Roy Chapman Andrews who visited the site in 1922. It was here that fossilized dinosaur eggs were discovered for the first time, along with specimens of Velociraptor, Protoceratops, and Oviraptor. The sedimentary strata of the Djadokhta geological formation date back over 70 million years. North of the cliffs, a saxaul forest offers an unexpected vegetative contrast in this desert environment. Sunset on the cliffs is particularly intense.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Sunset walk on the Flaming Cliffs - late afternoon's raking light sets the ochre and red cliff tones ablaze spectacularly. This is the ideal moment to photograph the site and walk along the cliff edge. Don't approach the edge too closely, the rock is crumbly in places.

📍 Ongiin Khiid Monastery Mongolia📍 Ongi River Mongolia📍 Ongiin Khiid ruins Dundgovi
1

Ongiin Khiid Monastery ruins - founded in 1660 on both banks of the Ongi River, this monastic complex was one of the country's most important: at its peak, it comprised 30 temples, 4 Buddhist universities, and over 1,000 monks. Activities
