Salamanca

★★★★★
1

Salamanca's Plaza Mayor is often called Spain's most beautiful square—and once you see it, you'll understand why. Commissioned by Philip V in 1710, built from 1729 by Alberto de Churriguera and completed only in 1755, it's a masterpiece of Spanish Baroque framed by two-story arcades. Look for the sculpted medallions on the arches: you'll find kings, conquistadors, and even Franco—though he's since been removed following historical memory legislation. At the corner location, Café Novelty has been serving since 1905; it's the perfect spot for a break with a view. Arrive early morning or after 8pm for local atmosphere, as school groups flood the square during the day.

⏱ 60 min
2

Salamanca's twin cathedrals are joined together and visited as one. The Catedral Vieja, begun around 1150 in Romanesque style, houses one of Spain's most complete collections of medieval altarpieces—53 painted panels from the 15th century. The Catedral Nueva, built between the 16th and 18th centuries in late Gothic style, is notably more imposing. On the north facade, restored in 1992, look for an astronaut and a demon eating ice cream—added according to the tradition that each restoration should include a contemporary figure. You enter the old cathedral through the new one, with a single combined ticket.

⏱ 90 min
3

The facade of Salamanca's University, founded in 1218 by Alfonso IX of León—one of Europe's oldest—is covered with plateresque carvings so dense they're sometimes compared to stone lace. The classic game: find the hidden frog on the facade. Legend says whoever finds it will pass their exams. The interior, notably the old lecture hall called Aula de Fray Luis de León, is open for visits and worth the stop.

⏱ 45 min
4

The Casa de las Conchas is a 15th-century Gothic civil palace whose facade is covered with over 350 stone scallop shells—the emblem of the Order of Santiago to which its owner, Rodrigo Arias Maldonado, belonged. Now a public library, entry is free and the interior courtyard is worth five minutes of your time.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Late in the day, walk down to the Puente Romano and the riverbank viewpoint along the Tormes to watch the silhouette of both cathedrals at sunset. It's the best photo angle in Salamanca. From the Roman bridge—26 arches, built under Trajan, the city's only access point for two thousand years—the orange light on Villamayor stone is the spectacle that earned the city its nickname 'La Dorada' (the Golden One).

⏱ 45 min

Parque Natural De Arribes Del Duero

★★★☆☆
1

The Arribes del Duero Natural Park stretches for nearly 100 kilometers of canyons carved by the Duero River through granite, forming the natural border between Spain and Portugal. The cliffs plunge up to 500 meters—the deepest gorges in the entire Iberian Peninsula. The Mirador del Fraile, at Aldeadávila de la Ribera, offers the most vertiginous viewpoint: a walkway overhangs the canyon directly, not recommended if you have a fear of heights. From there, the Duero's waters seem motionless, they're so far below. Bring water as the trails are exposed.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Pozo de los Humos is one of the peninsula's most spectacular waterfalls: the Uces River plunges into the canyon from over 50 meters, creating a permanent cloud of spray that gave it its name ('well of smoke'). The trail from Masueco is short (2.3 km round trip) and accessible to all, but stones are slippery in wet season. In summer, flow can be low depending on the year's rainfall—choose spring or autumn to see the falls at full force.

⏱ 75 min
3

Make a detour through Fermoselle, a town declared a historic and artistic site, perched on the heights with views over the confluence of the Duero and Esla. The town is nicknamed 'the town of a thousand cellars'—caves carved into the rock beneath houses to store wine from the Arribes terroir, a developing denomination of origin with eleven vineyards planted on terraces. The viewpoints at the castle and Las Escaleras are worth the stop.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walk sections of the GR-14, the long-distance trail that follows the Duero from its source to Porto. In the Arribes sector, several sections above the gorges are accessible in 1 to 3 hours. The microclimate warmer than the rest of Castile—due to the deep gorges trapping heat—allows olive trees, almonds and vines to grow at this latitude. Look up: golden eagles, griffon vultures and black storks nest on the rock walls.

Zamora

★★★☆☆
1

Zamora's cathedral is nicknamed 'The Pearl of the Duero'—and once you see its scaled stone dome, you'll understand why. Built between 1151 and 1174, consecrated in 1175, it's the oldest cathedral in Castile and León. Its cimborrio (lantern tower) with sixteen double arches showing Byzantine, Islamic and French Romanesque influences is a unique creation in Spanish Romanesque art. Inside, don't miss the Cathedral Museum which houses one of the world's finest collections of 15th-century Flemish tapestries, from the workshops of Arras, Tournai and Brussels. Check opening times before coming, as the cathedral closes during certain exhibition periods.

⏱ 75 min
2

Zamora has over twenty Romanesque churches in the old town—making it the densest concentration in Europe according to several art historians. Follow the Romanesque circuit from Plaza de Viriato: the church of La Magdalena, with its carved doorway and oriental sepulcher, then Santiago de los Caballeros, where tradition places El Cid's knighting by Ferdinand I. The Mirador del Troncoso, at the end of the ramparts, gives panoramic views over the Duero and the plains of Castile.

⏱ 90 min
3

Zamora's ramparts extend over a kilometer along the rocky promontory overlooking the Duero. Built in several phases from the 9th century onward, they're among the best preserved in Spain. Look for the Portillo de la Traición—a small gate in the wall through which, according to legend, the assassin of King Sancho II was introduced during the siege of Zamora in 1072. This is the origin of the Spanish proverb 'Zamora wasn't taken in an hour.'

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Late afternoon, head down to Playa de Los Pelambres on the Duero's banks to view the silhouette of the cathedral and castle from the opposite shore. The medieval stone bridge, crossing point of the Via de la Plata—the Roman road that linked Mérida to Astorga—holds one of Spain's oldest sets of aceñas (water mills). This is the best angle for photographing the historic ensemble.

Las Medulas

★★★★☆
1

Las Médulas is Castile and León's strangest landscape: reddish sand pinnacles rise from a forest of oaks and chestnuts, like a fantasy film set. This isn't natural geology—it's the direct result of the Roman Empire's largest open-pit gold mine. The Romans extracted approximately 800,000 kilos of gold here between the 1st and 3rd centuries, using a process called 'ruina montium': they stored colossal amounts of water in mountain basins, then released it all at once to explode the rock. Over 400 km of canals and aqueducts were built to feed this system. Listed as UNESCO in 1997, the site has remained intact since the mines were abandoned. Start at the Interpretation Center in the village to understand the operation's scale before heading out on the trails.

⏱ 45 min
2

Climb up to the Mirador de Orellán for the overview of the site—from here the spectacle is most striking: the jagged red formations stand out against the green forests, with the Aquilanos mountains in the background. The sunset from this viewpoint is one of the most photographed in León province. Allow 30 minutes uphill from the Orellán parking lot.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Walk the Senda de las Valiñas, a circular route from Las Médulas village that penetrates the heart of the mining site. The trail crosses the open-air galleries, skirts Lake Carucedo (formed by stagnant water accumulation during Roman works) and passes through the caves of La Cuevona and La Encantada—galleries hand-dug for the hydraulic system. Easy, marked, about 2 hours. If you have more time, the Peripheral Trail (4h) makes the complete circuit and includes the ruins of the Orellán settlement.

Penalba De Santiago

★★★☆☆
1

To reach Peñalba de Santiago, you must cross the entire Valley of Silence by a single-lane road once called 'the monks' path.' Perched at 1,100 meters in the Aquilanos mountains, this village of about fifty inhabitants is one of Spain's most beautiful: stone and slate houses with wooden balconies, lanes so narrow they seem never to have changed since the 10th century. Park at the entrance lot—the lanes are impassable by car.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Mozarabic church of Santiago de Peñalba is the only remnant of the monastery founded by San Genadio in the 10th century, completed in 937 by Abbot Salomón. It's a jewel of Spanish Mozarabic architecture—a style born from the fusion of Visigothic traditions and Arab influences. Enter through the double door with horseshoe arches supported by three marble columns: inside, the horseshoe arches divide the nave, and remnants of medieval wall paintings survive on the walls. This is where the body of San Genadio rests.

⏱ 45 min
3

From the village, a half-hour walk on the old monks' path leads to the Cave of San Genadio, carved into a vertical rock face at the very entrance to the Valley of Silence. The Bishop of Astorga retreated here to meditate in the 9th century. A local legend says he ordered the Oza River to be silent so as not to disturb his prayer—and the river disappeared underground at that spot.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Valley of Silence is also a beautiful invitation to hike in a landscape of oak, chestnut and walnut forests crossed by the Oza River. The trail climbing to the Monastery of San Pedro de Montes (founded in the 7th century by San Fructuoso, rebuilt in the 12th-13th centuries) is one of the region's most beautiful. Allow 3 hours round trip from Peñalba.

⏱ 180 min

★★★☆☆
1

The Episcopal Palace of Astorga is one of Gaudí's few works outside Catalonia—and it's as surprising as any of his Barcelona buildings. After the old palace burned down in 1886, Bishop Joan Grau i Vallespinós, originally from Catalonia and Gaudí's childhood friend, commissioned him to build the new one. From 1887, Gaudí designed a neo-Gothic edifice in white granite combining the forms of a castle, temple and manor house. The moat, battlements, four circular towers and multicolored stained glass windows—inspired by León Cathedral—bear his signature. Bishop Grau's death in 1893 prompted Gaudí to abandon the site, with the top floor completed without him by architect Ricardo García Guereta. Today the palace houses the Museum of the Ways of Saint James. Book tickets online; audio guides are available in English.

⏱ 75 min
2

Astorga Cathedral adjoins Gaudí's palace, forming a striking ensemble. Begun in 1471 on the site of an earlier Romanesque cathedral, it blends Gothic, Renaissance and Baroque according to the centuries of construction. Its Baroque facade is particularly ornate. On an apse pinnacle, spot the statue of Pedro Mato, a legendary figure of the Maragatería whose identity remains mysterious. Inside, the main altarpiece and carved choir are worth stopping for.

⏱ 45 min
3

Astorga is a Roman town founded under Augustus as Asturica Augusta, and the remains are remarkably well preserved. The Roman Museum, accessed through the Ergastula (2nd-century Roman dungeon still standing), displays discoveries from local excavations. The baths, forum and Domus of the Bear and Birds—with its Roman mosaics in place—complete the visit. The city was the hub of several major Roman roads, including the one linking Bordeaux to Astorga. This crossroads also explains its role as an essential stop on the Way of St. James.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Five kilometers from Astorga, the village of Castrillo de los Polvazares is the best-preserved example of Maragato architecture—reddish stone houses aligned on a single paved street, carriage gates, absolute silence. The Maragatería is a unique region whose inhabitants, the Maragatos, had a reputation as muleteer-transporters of goods between Galicia and Castile. Their traditional dish, cocido maragato, is served backwards from other Spanish cocidos: meat first, broth last.

Burgos

★★★★☆
1

Burgos Cathedral is the only religious building in Spain inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage list independently. The first stone was laid by Ferdinand III and Bishop Mauricio on July 20, 1221; construction stretched until 1765, summarizing in itself the entire history of Spanish Gothic. Its 84-meter towers topped with openwork spires from the 15th century dominate the old town for miles. Inside, look for the tomb of El Cid and his wife Doña Jimena beneath the central lantern tower. Don't miss the Chapel of the Constables (Capilla del Condestable), a masterpiece of late Gothic, or the Papamoscas that chimes the hours from a balcony between two bells. Arrive late afternoon from Astorga: the illuminated cathedral at dusk is a spectacle in itself. Book tickets online.

2

It's one of the summits of Spanish Gothic sculpture. Entry is free. From the forecourt, the Arco de Santa María and the Paseo del Espolón along the Arlanzón River offer a beautiful transitional walk before the drive to Orbaneja.

Activities

If time is tight, prioritize the cathedral on arrival evening and the Cartuja on departure morning.

Orbaneja del Castillo is perhaps Spain's most improbable village: a 25-meter waterfall emerges directly from the Cueva del Agua, flows through the village dividing houses into two quarters, and plunges into the Ebro below. Frias

★★★☆☆
1

Frías is considered Spain's smallest 'town'—it received this title in 1435 and today has only about a hundred inhabitants, a member of Spain's Most Beautiful Villages since 2014. From the bottom of the Tobalina valley, the castle stands atop the limestone outcrop called La Muela: built between the 12th and 15th centuries on Alfonso VIII's orders, it contains 13th-century Romanesque capitals depicting warriors on horseback and fantastic birds. ⏱ 75 min
2

Along the slope below the castle, Frías's medieval lanes reveal the 'hanging houses'—half-timbered constructions clinging to the cliff edge, upper floors jutting out into space above the gorge. Descend to the medieval bridge over the Ebro (143 meters, 9 arches) with its 14th-century defense tower. The San Vicente church is also notable: its original Romanesque portal was detached and shipped to New York when the tower collapsed in 1904—it's now in an American museum.

⏱ 60 min
3

Forty-six kilometers from Frías, Puentedey takes its name from its geology: the 50-inhabitant village is built on a natural limestone bridge sculpted by the Nela River over 85 to 90 million years. It's the most photographed image in Burgos province after the cathedral. From the suspended viewpoint of San Andrés, the overall view of the natural bridge and the houses perched on it is striking. On the arch itself, the San Pelayo church (11th-century Romanesque elements) and the Brizuela Palace (15th century, two rectangular towers with coats of arms) merit a stop.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Walk the cobbled lanes climbing toward the castle. The medieval structure of the town can be read in the tangle of passages and stairs.

⏱ 60 min

Soria

★★☆☆☆
1

The cloister of San Juan de Duero Monastery is one of Spain's strangest—and that's precisely what makes it unforgettable. Built in the 13th century by the Order of the Hospitallers of Jerusalem on the banks of the Duero, its four galleries each present a different arch style: Romanesque, Gothic, Islamic and interlaced horseshoe arches with no equivalent in the West. Some are inspired by Andalusian mosques, others by the Crusades. This unique architectural syncretism reflects exactly medieval Spain at the crossroads of cultures. Entry is free for EU citizens. On leaving, walk along the Duero to the Hermitage of San Saturio, carved into the rock in the 18th century over older caves—the Baroque interior is a surprise in this rock-hewn setting.

⏱ 75 min
2

In Soria's historic center, the Co-Cathedral of San Pedro preserves a 12th-century Romanesque cloister with capitals carved with fantastic animals, vegetation and biblical scenes—three of its four original galleries still stand. Tradition has it that the Romanesque tower fell in 1531 because a city councilor asked an architect to remove a pillar that blocked the view of a statue of the Virgin. The Alameda de Cervantes Park, along the Duero, is the city's green lung with over 130 recorded plant species.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Eight kilometers from Soria, the ruins of Numancia are a place steeped in history: here the Celtiberians heroically resisted the siege by Scipio Aemilianus's Roman legions for 14 months, before choosing collective death over surrender in 133 BC. The site on Cerro de la Muela features house reconstructions and an archaeological route. The Numantine Museum in town gathers objects found at the site.

Calatanazor

★★★☆☆
1

To optimize the day before heading to Segovia in the afternoon, start at opening time with La Fuentona de Muriel: a karst spring that surges from a rocky amphitheater as if water appears from nowhere. An accessible trail follows the Abión River to the spring, then a fork leads to a waterfall (with water in wet season). Start from the Muriel de la Fuente parking lot, about 7 km round trip, 3 hours with breaks.

⏱ 75 min
2

Two kilometers from La Fuentona, the Sabinar de Calatañazor Nature Reserve is Europe's densest forest of Spanish junipers (Juniperus thurifera). Some specimens reach 15 meters in height and 5 meters in diameter, with ages potentially exceeding 400 years. The resinous, slightly incense-like scent emanating from these twisted trees is unforgettable. Allow about an hour's walk.

⏱ 60 min
3

Finish with the village of Calatañazor—'Qal'at al-Nusur' in Arabic, the 'castle of eagles.' Declared a historic and artistic ensemble in 1962, it preserves its arcaded medieval main street, adobe and juniper-beam houses, and castle ruins. History says that here in July 1002 Christian armies inflicted on Arab general Almanzor the wound from which he would die a month later, giving birth to the Spanish saying 'At Calatañazor, Almanzor lost his drum.' Head to Segovia around 1pm.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walk through the juniper forest where centenarian trees create a mystical atmosphere. Thrushes, magpies and crows feed on their berries in winter.

⏱ 60 min

Push on to the natural monument of La Fuentona, a karst rock spring giving birth to the Abión River. Underwater caves open in its depths.

Segovia

★★★★☆
1

Segovia's aqueduct is the first thing you see entering the city—and you never get used to it. Probably built around AD 50, under emperors Vespasian and Trajan, to bring water from the sierra to the city, it reaches its highest point at Plaza del Azoguejo: 28.10 meters, 167 arches, 20,400 granite blocks joined without any mortar. What fascinates engineers: after 2,000 years, the blocks maintain perfect balance through geometry alone. Walk under the arches, then climb the rampart path to see the aqueduct head-on—it's the best angle.

⏱ 45 min
2

Segovia's Alcázar sits on a rocky spur at the confluence of two rivers, like a fairy-tale castle. Walt Disney reportedly drew inspiration from it for Snow White's castle. Begun in the 11th century, expanded in the 13th-16th centuries, ravaged by fire in 1862 and rebuilt in an even more spectacular neo-Gothic style. This is where Isabella the Catholic was proclaimed queen in 1474. Climb the 152 steps of the Torre de Juan II for the best view over the old town and Guadarrama sierra.

⏱ 90 min
3

Segovia's cathedral, nicknamed 'the Lady of Cathedrals,' is the last Gothic cathedral built in Spain. Construction began in 1525 under Charles V to replace the Romanesque cathedral destroyed during the Comuneros revolt in 1520. Inside, the Flemish Gothic cloister comes from the destroyed old cathedral—it was dismantled stone by stone and rebuilt here. Allow about 1h30.

⏱ 75 min
4

The Judería quarter and its lanes around Calle de la Judería hold traces of the Sephardic community that lived in Segovia until the 1492 expulsion. Don't miss the Casa de los Picos, a 15th-century palace whose facade is entirely covered with granite points—a unique architectural curiosity. The Barrio de los Caballeros groups the Renaissance palaces of Segovian nobility.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Twelve kilometers from Segovia, the Royal Palace of La Granja de San Ildefonso is a miniature version of Versailles at the foot of the Guadarrama sierra: built in the 18th century by Philip V, it's surrounded by French-style gardens with 26 monumental fountains fed by gravity. The water displays run at certain times weekly in summer—check times before going, as this spectacle is what makes the trip worthwhile.

⏱ 120 min

Pedraza

★★★☆☆
1

Pedraza is the best-preserved medieval village in Castile and León—perhaps in all Spain. Declared a historic site in 1951, it has only one access gate (16th-century wood), cobbled lanes lined with houses bearing noble coats of arms, and an arcaded Plaza Mayor ranking among the region's most beautiful. Park at the outside lot and enter on foot. To avoid weekend crowds, prefer a weekday or arrive before 10am.

⏱ 60 min
2

Pedraza's castle occupies a rocky outcrop at the village's edge. Built in the 13th century, transformed into a Renaissance palace in the 16th-17th centuries, it was saved from ruin by painter Ignacio Zuloaga who made it his residence and studio. Its keep now houses a museum with some of his works. A little-known historical anecdote: the sons of King Francis I of France were imprisoned in this castle in 1526 as hostages to guarantee the Treaty of Madrid—they stayed four years. Book tickets online.

⏱ 60 min
3

Pedraza's medieval prison (Cárcel de la Villa), a 13th-century building rebuilt in the 16th, is one of Spain's best preserved. It's visited with guided tours that recreate medieval prison life. If you're there on the first or second Saturday evenings of July, don't miss under any circumstances the Noche de las Velas: electric lights are turned off and thousands of candles illuminate the lanes while concerts take place outdoors.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Stroll Calle Mayor between blazoned houses and noble 16th-17th century constructions, witnesses to the village's textile prosperity.

⏱ 60 min

Savor cordero asado, wood-oven roasted lamb that made Pedraza's gastronomic reputation. The British Times in 2024 ranked restaurant El Soportal among the 28 best Mediterranean tables.

⏱ 60 min

Attend the Candle Nights on the first two Saturdays of July. Thousands of candles illuminate lanes, balconies and squares while classical music concerts resound.

Avila

★★★★☆
1

Ávila's wall is quite simply the most complete and best preserved of medieval Spain. Built from 1090 on Alfonso VI's orders by Count Raymond of Burgundy, it draws a regular rectangle of 2,516 meters perimeter, pierced by 9 gates and flanked by 88 semi-circular towers. UNESCO-listed in 1985. Buy the rampart walk ticket (about €5): 1,700 meters of marked route atop the walls with plunging views over the city and sierra. The cathedral's apse, directly integrated into the wall, is one of the world's only examples of a cathedral playing a defensive role.

⏱ 90 min
2

The intra-muros old town of Ávila deserves to be walked without a fixed route. Stop at the Basilica of San Vicente, a 12th-century Romanesque masterpiece built on the saint's martyrdom site, with an exceptional Gothic cenotaph. Also visit the Royal Monastery of Santo Tomás, commissioned by the Catholic Monarchs in the late 15th century—Grand Inquisitor Tomás de Torquemada is buried there. Plaza del Mercado Chico is the city's daily heart.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Cuatro Postes (Four Columns) viewpoint, 500 meters outside the walls, is the best vantage point for Ávila's wall in its entirety. Four granite columns form the structure—they were reportedly erected at the spot where child Teresa of Ávila, en route to be martyred by the Moors, was intercepted by her uncle. From here photographers capture the wall's silhouette at sunset. Allow 5 minutes by car or 20 minutes on foot from Puerta del Puente.

⏱ 30 min

Sierra De Gredos

★★★☆☆
1

The Sierra de Gredos is Castile and León's wildest massif—and one of Spain's most beautiful. Pico Almanzor, at 2,592 meters, is the highest summit in the Spanish Central System. The starting point for all major hikes is the Plataforma de Gredos, at 1,780 meters altitude, accessible from Hoyos del Espino via the AV-931 (12 km, toll per vehicle at park entrance). In summer and on weekends, the barrier opens at 7:30am—arrive early as parking fills fast. For the return, Ávila is 80 km away (1h15) and Madrid 170 km (2h) via the A-5.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The hike to Laguna Grande de Gredos from the Plataforma is the massif's most beautiful and most frequented (4.6/5 rating on AllTrails, 162 reviews). The trail follows the old Calzada Romana, gradually climbing toward the Mirador de los Barrerones (2,149 m) where the glacial cirque suddenly appears—a breathtaking spectacle. The lake is nestled at 1,950 meters in the rocky amphitheater, guarded by Pico Almanzor. Along the way, Iberian ibex—protected in the park since 1905—are frequently visible on rocky slopes. Allow 14 km round trip, 677 m elevation gain, 5-5.5 hours walking. The Refugio Elola at the lake's edge (80 places) allows overnight stays for summit attempts the next morning—reservation mandatory in season.
