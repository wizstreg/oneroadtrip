Kanto Flower & Landscape Journey: 15 Days Through Japan's Hidden Nature

📅 15 days📍 15 stops🚗 950 km⏱ 20h20

Hitachinaka → Daigo → Kasama → Ashikaga → Nikko → Oze → Nakanojo → Maebashi → Chichibu → Tachikawa → Kisarazu → Futtsu → Yokohama → Hakone → Kamakura

From the nemophila blue seas of Hitachinaka to Ashikaga's century-old wisteria, from Nikko's thundering waterfalls to the turquoise waters of Shima Blue, this circuit traverses Kanto's most spectacular landscapes. Climb into the Oze marshlands, ride Nagatoro's rapids, face the vertigo of Nokogiri, and finish in Kamakura before the bronze Great Buddha. Off-the-beaten-path Japan, season after season.

✨ Highlights
  • 4.5 million nemophila at Hitachinaka from mid-April to early May across 3.5 hectares
  • 160-year-old wisteria classified as natural monument at Ashikaga, chosen by CNN among 10 dream destinations worldwide
  • Kegon Falls at Nikko (97m), one of Japan's three great waterfalls, and UNESCO Tōshō-gū Shrine
  • Great Buddha of Kamakura (121 tons, 1252), in open air since the 1498 tsunami
📆 Best period

April, May, October, November

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 13 and 17 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Spring (March-May): Cherry blossom season, perfect weather, mild temperatures, iconic sakura viewing, peak tourist season, advance booking essential, magical atmosphere.

Fall (September-November): Autumn colors, comfortable weather, clear skies, beautiful foliage, excellent touring, fewer crowds, ideal photography, pleasant conditions.

Summer (June-August): Hot humid weather, rainy season, festival season, vibrant atmosphere, mountain hiking, beach activities, air conditioning essential.

Winter (December-February): Cold weather, snow sports, illuminations, onsen season, fewer crowds, winter festivals, cozy atmosphere, skiing opportunities.

🌍 What to discover in JP?

Unique culture with ancient traditions, modern innovation, and incredible attention to detail. Experience temples, technology, and authentic Japanese lifestyle. The country offers cultural immersion with natural beauty and culinary excellence. Japan provides transformative cultural experiences with historical depth and modern sophistication.

📋 Important notes

This itinerary combines seasonal events from different times of year. In spring (mid-April to mid-May): nemophila at Hitachinaka, azaleas at Kasama, wisteria at Ashikaga, moss phlox at Chichibu, rapeseed at Isumi. In summer (June-August): daylilies at Oze, lush greenery at Nikko. In autumn (October-November): red kochia at Hitachi, maple foliage at Fukuroda and Nikko, pampas grass at Hakone and Sarushima, ginkgos at Showa Kinen. In winter (December-February): frozen waterfalls at Fukuroda, ice stalactites at Chichibu, ice bubbles at Akagi. Adjust your stops according to your travel season.

Day 1

Hitachinaka

Where 4.5 million baby-blue nemophila flowers meet the Pacific horizon

★★★☆☆
📍 Hitachi Seaside Park Hitachinaka📍 Miharashi Hill Hitachi Seaside Park📍 Nakaminato Fish Market Hitachinaka
1

Hitachi Seaside Park stretches across 190 hectares along the Pacific coast, 1.5 hours by train from Tokyo (JR Joban Line from Ueno to Katsuta, then bus). The park's star attraction is Miharashi Hill—from mid-April to early May, 4.5 million nemophila (translucent baby-blue flowers) blanket its 3.5-hectare slopes. The gradient between flower blue, sky blue, and ocean blue is breathtaking. Arrive before 8:30am if you're visiting during Golden Week (April 29–May 5)—after 10am, crowds get dense. Spring brings nemophila (April-May). Autumn transforms the hill with crimson kochia (October). Summer blooms with sunflowers.

⏱ 120 min
2

Beyond Miharashi Hill, the park hosts a million daffodils (late March to mid-April), 170 tulip varieties, and a 100-meter Ferris wheel offering aerial views of the grounds. Cycling paths crisscross the park—if time permits, rent a bike to reach the more distant zones. Before leaving, try the nemophila ice cream (turquoise blue, ramune flavor) sold at the food court stalls at the hill's base.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

After the park, detour to Nakaminato fishing port, 10 minutes by car. The fish market here is outstanding—you can lunch on ultra-fresh sashimi served directly by fishermen. It's one of the biggest Pacific fishing ports in the region.

Day 2

Daigo

Gateway to Fukuroda's four-tiered cascade and mountain onsen retreats

★★★☆☆
📍 Fukuroda Falls Daigo Ibaraki📍 Fukuroda Observation Platform Daigo📍 Ryujin Suspension Bridge Hitachiota
1

Fukuroda Falls (120m high, 73m wide) ranks among Japan's three most famous waterfalls, alongside Nachi Falls (Wakayama) and Kegon Falls (Nikko). Water cascades down four rocky tiers—hence the alternative name "Yodo-no-Taki" (Four-Stage Falls). A 276-meter tunnel carved through rock leads to a multi-level observation platform: at the bottom, you stand meters from the base; at the top (elevator access), you command panoramic views of all four tiers. The monk Saigyo Hōshi supposedly said one must visit once each season to grasp its full beauty—and he was right: verdant spring, cool summer refuge, flaming autumn foliage (early November), frozen ice curtains in winter (late December–February).

⏱ 75 min
2

If you're in the area, push on to Ryūjin Suspension Bridge in Hitachiota—it spans 375 meters across the Ryūjin Dam at 100 meters height. It's one of Japan's longest pedestrian bridges. Local operators offer bungee jumping here if you're feeling brave.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In winter (November to January), nighttime illuminations transform the falls and access path into a sound-and-light spectacle. If you come in autumn (early November), combine the falls with an overnight at a ryokan in the small hot spring town of Daigo—the atmosphere is peaceful and the onsen excellent.

⏱ 60 min

A short hiking trail (20-30 minutes, steep) leads from the tunnel exit to Namase Falls, less crowded. If you have more energy, continue 45 additional minutes to a small temple dedicated to Kannon, the Goddess of Mercy, hidden in the mountains.

⏱ 75 min

Day 3

Kasama

Where 8,500 azaleas paint hilltops crimson and pottery kilns have fired for centuries

★★☆☆☆
📍 Kasama Tsutsuji Park Ibaraki📍 Kasama Inari Shrine Ibaraki📍 Kasama Pottery District
1

Kasama Azalea Park (Kasama Tsutsuji Kōen), perched at 143 meters altitude, explodes with color from mid-April to mid-May during the azalea festival. About 8,500 azaleas of 25 varieties blanket the hillside—from vivid crimson to pale pink to pure white. From the summit, views sweep over the rolling Ibaraki countryside. Arrive late afternoon for golden light on the flowers—and stay for sunset, which is superb from the hilltop viewpoint.

⏱ 75 min
2

Kasama is also a pottery town—Kasama-yaki ceramics have been produced since the 18th century. Tour the artisan quarter where you can watch potters at work and purchase pieces directly. Kasama Inari Shrine, one of Japan's three greatest Inari shrines, makes a good complement—its vermilion torii gates in the forest echo Fushimi Inari in miniature.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

If pottery appeals to you, workshops offer beginner sessions on the potter's wheel (allow 1 to 1.5 hours). You shape your piece and it's mailed to you after firing—a unique souvenir to bring home from Japan.

⏱ 75 min

Day 4

Ashikaga

Home to Japan's largest wisteria garden, where 160-year-old vines cascade like violet waterfalls

★★★☆☆
📍 Ashikaga Flower Park Tochigi📍 Ashikaga Gakko School📍 Banna-ji Temple Ashikaga
1

Ashikaga Flower Park (94,000m²) is Japan's largest wisteria garden—over 350 trees, including a wisteria more than 160 years old classified as a natural monument of Tochigi Prefecture. CNN ranked it among the "10 dream destinations in the world" in 2014. The great wisteria's trellis covers 1,000m² (equivalent to 600 tatami mats) and produces about 80,000 flower clusters in violet. The white wisteria tunnel stretches for 80 meters—when you walk through in May, the sweet floral scent is intoxicating. Blooming from late April to mid-May: first the pinks, then violets, whites, and finally yellows.

⏱ 120 min
2

While you're in Ashikaga, visit Ashikaga Gakkō, considered Japan's oldest school (founded in the 9th century according to some sources). The wooden building, with its reconstructed classrooms and library of Confucian texts, offers a rare glimpse into education in feudal Japan.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Stay past sunset: the park's nighttime illuminations (Night Wisteria) transform the wisteria into luminous cascades. The show has been certified as "Japanese Night View Heritage." If you come in winter (late October to late January), the Flower Fantasy illuminations with over 5 million LEDs replace the flowers—it's one of Kanto's three biggest illumination events.

⏱ 75 min

Try the wisteria ice cream (fuji soft cream), a house specialty of the park—violet, lightly perfumed, sold at counters inside the park. It's become Ashikaga's signature culinary souvenir.

⏱ 20 min

Day 5

Nikko

Sacred mountains where 97-meter waterfalls thunder beside the Tokugawa shoguns' gilded mausoleum

★★★★★
📍 Kegon Falls Nikko📍 Lake Chuzenji Nikko📍 Toshogu Shrine Nikko📍 Shinkyo Bridge Nikko
1

Kegon Falls (97 meters) is one of Japan's three great waterfalls. Water from Lake Chūzenji plunges into a chasm surrounded by rock walls—the roar reaches you well before you arrive. A paid elevator carved through rock descends you to the observation platform at the falls' base—from there, you feel the spray and the water's raw power. In autumn (October), maples frame the cascade in crimson and gold. In winter, the falls freeze partially—the spectacle of blue ice is rare and striking.

⏱ 60 min
2

Lake Chūzenji, at 1,269 meters altitude, formed about 20,000 years ago following an eruption of Mount Nantai. It's Japan's largest high-altitude lake. Take a boat tour to admire the forested shores—in autumn, colors reflect on the water like a mirror.

⏱ 45 min
3

Descend toward central Nikko to visit Tōshō-gū, the mausoleum of Tokugawa Ieyasu (first shogun of the Edo period), completed in 1636 and UNESCO-listed. It's Japan's most ornate shrine—15,000 workers labored on it. Don't miss the Yōmeimon gate, covered with over 500 painted and gilded carvings, and the famous three monkeys ("see no evil, hear no evil, speak no evil") carved on the sacred stable.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Upon arriving in Nikko, stop at Shinkyō bridge (the sacred bridge), lacquered vermilion, spanning the Daiya River at the site entrance. According to legend, the monk Shōdō Shōnin crossed the river in 766 on the backs of two serpents—the bridge marks this spot. The classic photo is taken from the roadside below, with the cedar forest as backdrop.

⏱ 20 min

Day 6

Oze

High-altitude marshland where wooden boardwalks traverse seas of yellow daylilies

★★★☆☆
📍 Oze Marshland Gunma📍 Ozegahara Wetland📍 Mount Shibutsu Oze
1

The Oze Marshlands, listed under the Ramsar Convention, form a high-altitude wetland zone 2km by 6km tucked between Mount Shibutsu and Mount Hiuchigatake at about 1,400 meters elevation. It's one of Japan's last great pristine marshes. Wooden boardwalks (mokudō) traverse the marsh—you literally walk above the water and peat. In summer (June-July), Nikko daylilies (Nikko-kisuge) carpet the meadows in yellow, with snow-capped Mount Shibutsu as backdrop. In spring, white cotton grass (mizubasho) transforms the marsh into a cotton field.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

Accommodation is in mountain huts (sansō)—book ahead in high season. The huts are simple but clean, with meals included. Bring good hiking boots and water—there are no vending machines on the trails. The return can be made via a different trail to vary the scenery.

⏱ 60 min

Day 7

Nakanojo

Where 500-year-old hot springs meet the electric turquoise of Shima Blue waters

★★☆☆☆
📍 Lake Okushima Shima Blue Nakanojo📍 Shima Onsen Nakanojo Gunma
1

Lake Okushima (Shima Dam), 4km in circumference, is famous for its intense turquoise color nicknamed "Shima Blue." This tint comes from mineral particles dissolved in water from surrounding volcanic springs. On sunny days, the water is so clear you can see the bottom—and the blue shifts toward emerald depending on the light. Best season to see it: spring (April-May), when the still-tender vegetation contrasts with the turquoise. In autumn, red maples around the lake add spectacular contrast.

⏱ 60 min
2

Shima Onsen, the neighboring hot spring resort, has a history spanning over 500 years. Its waters are reputed to cure "40,000 ailments" (shi-man = 40,000 in Japanese, hence the name). The old thermal quarter, with its stone alleys and wooden ryokan, has a retro Shōwa charm—some say Miyazaki's Spirited Away drew partial inspiration from it.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Treat yourself to a night in a Shima Onsen ryokan. The outdoor baths (rotenburo) with river views are blissful in every season—in winter, steam rises into the frozen air and it's almost surreal. Several ryokan offer baths open to day visitors if you're not staying overnight.

⏱ 90 min

Day 8

Maebashi

Gateway to Mount Akagi's frozen lake where methane bubbles crystallize in winter ice

★★☆☆☆
📍 Lake Onuma Mount Akagi Maebashi📍 Mount Akagi Caldera Gunma📍 Maebashi Rose Garden
1

Lake Ōnuma, in the caldera of Mount Akagi (1,828m), is the mountain's largest caldera lake. In winter (January-February), when the lake freezes and there's little snow, a rare phenomenon occurs: methane bubbles rising from the bottom remain trapped in transparent ice, creating a spectacle of "ice bubbles" (kōri no awa)—it looks like bubbles frozen in glass. It's one of the rare places in Kanto where this phenomenon is observable. Check weather conditions before going—it requires intense cold and little snow.

⏱ 60 min
2

In any season, Mount Akagi offers beautiful hikes around the lake and toward surrounding peaks. In spring, wild azaleas color the slopes; in autumn, maples reflect in the lake. The trail circling Lake Ōnuma (about 1 hour) is accessible to everyone.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Descend to Maebashi for lunch. The city is known for its tonkatsu (breaded pork cutlets) and for Maebashi Municipal Rose Garden, which brings together over 600 rose varieties—free and lovely in spring and early summer.

⏱ 60 min

Day 9

Chichibu

Where 400,000 moss phlox carpet hillsides and ancient rivers carved stone tatami

★★★☆☆
📍 Hitsujiyama Park Shibazakura Chichibu📍 Nagatoro Iwadatami Rocks Chichibu📍 Misotsuchi Icicles Chichibu
1

Chichibu's Hitsujiyama Park transforms from mid-April to early May into a carpet of moss phlox (shibazakura)—over 400,000 plants of 9 varieties blanket the gentle slopes in pink, white, purple, and red. On clear days, Mount Bukō rises in the background. It's the spring equivalent of Provence's lavender fields, Japanese-style. The Shibazakura festival draws large crowds—come on a weekday if possible.

⏱ 75 min
2

At Nagatoro, the Arakawa River has sculpted the Iwadatami ("stone tatami") rock formations over 500 meters, classified as a National Natural Monument. These crystalline schist slabs, stacked like giant tatami mats, date back several hundred million years. From the bridge above the river, the view is spectacular—especially in autumn with maples ablaze.

⏱ 45 min
3

In winter only (January-February): the ice stalactites of Misotsuchi form a wall 30 meters wide and 10 meters high along a cliff. In the evening, they're illuminated in colors (red, blue, yellow, orange)—an ephemeral spectacle lasting only a few weeks.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

At Nagatoro, operators offer boat descents (nagabune) on the Arakawa River through the rock formations—the ride lasts about 20 minutes and passes through light rapids. It's playful and refreshing in summer. If you prefer more adrenaline, rafting is also offered on certain sections.

⏱ 45 min

Before leaving Nagatoro, try kakko dango, grilled rice dumplings brushed with sweet soy sauce—a local specialty sold in shops along the river.

⏱ 20 min

Day 10

Tachikawa

Where golden ginkgo tunnels frame autumn and limestone caves hide beneath Tokyo

★★★★☆
📍 Showa Kinen Park Tachikawa📍 Nippara Limestone Cave Okutama📍 Okutama Lake Tokyo
1

Showa Kinen Park (Shōwa Kinen Kōen) is a vast 180-hectare space created to commemorate Emperor Shōwa's reign. Its autumn centerpiece: a 200-meter ginkgo avenue whose golden leaves form a golden tunnel in November. In spring, cherry blossoms and tulips take over; in summer, sunflowers; in autumn, cosmos. If you have children, the immense playground and picnic areas make it an excellent family park.

⏱ 90 min
2

For underground enthusiasts, Nippara Limestone Cave (Nippara Shōnyūdō) in Okutama is one of Japan's seven great caves—800 meters of galleries with formations illuminated by colored LEDs. The "Shidenorama" formation (calcite curtain) is particularly impressive. Interior temperature is constant around 11°C—bring a layer.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

If you have time after Nippara, detour to Lake Okutama, a dam surrounded by forest where you can rent a pedal boat or simply stroll on the floating bridge. The Okutama region is also known for its outdoor onsen—perfect for relaxing after a day of walking.

⏱ 60 min

Day 11

Kisarazu

Where tide-mirrored fishing poles create the Uyuni Salt Flat of Kanto

★★★★☆
📍 Isumi Railway Rapeseed Fields Chiba📍 Ishigami Rapeseed Field Chiba📍 Egawa Coast Kisarazu
1

The Isumi Railway, in the Bōsō Peninsula, crosses bright yellow rapeseed fields (nanohana) from March to April. Retro diesel trains with one or two cars roll through the midst of flowers—it's one of the region's most prized photo spots. The Ishigami rapeseed field, along the tracks, is the most photogenic. Position yourself trackside 10 minutes before the train passes for the perfect shot: yellow flowers in foreground, vintage red train, blue sky.

⏱ 60 min
2

Egawa Coast in Kisarazu is nicknamed "the Uyuni Salt Flat of Kanto." At high tide, when water covers the sandbanks, fishing poles (lined up in the sea) reflect on the surface like a giant mirror. Best moment: 1 to 2 hours before low tide, in calm weather. At sunset, the effect is striking—the orange sky doubles in the water.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

If you're at Egawa Coast in late afternoon, wait for sunset—the light conditions transform the beach into an almost surreal landscape. Bring rubber boots if you want to walk on the still-wet sandbanks.

⏱ 45 min

Day 12

Futtsu

Where saw-tooth cliffs plunge 330 meters into Tokyo Bay beneath a 31-meter stone Buddha

★★★☆☆
📍 Mount Nokogiri Futtsu Chiba📍 Nihon-ji Temple Nokogiri📍 Jigoku Nozoki Hell Peek Nokogiri
1

Mount Nokogiri (329m) owes its name ("saw-tooth mountain") to the stone quarries that notched its flanks for centuries. You can ascend by ropeway or on foot (trails from 30 to 90 minutes depending on difficulty). At the summit, the "Jigoku Nozoki" (Hell Peek) viewpoint is a rock projection overhanging the void—you lean out over 330 meters of cliff with Tokyo Bay below. Vertigo-inducing.

⏱ 45 min
2

Nihon-ji Temple, on Mount Nokogiri's flanks, houses a great seated Buddha 31 meters tall carved into rock in 1783. It's Japan's largest stone Buddha. The path leading to it passes 1,553 small arhat statues (rakan) carved into the mountain in the 18th century, each with a different expression.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hike between Mount Nokogiri's different viewpoints—the main trail connects Hell Peek, the great Buddha, and the rakan in about 2 hours. Stairs are steep in places—bring water and good shoes. The view from the summit embraces all of Tokyo Bay, and on clear days you glimpse Mount Fuji.

⏱ 120 min

Day 13

Yokohama

Port city where red-brick warehouses face the bay and Japan's largest Chinatown thrives

★★★★★
📍 Yokohama Red Brick Warehouse📍 Sarushima Island Yokosuka📍 Yokohama Chinatown
1

Yokohama's Red Brick Warehouses (Akarenga Sōko), built in 1911 and 1913, are among the last vestiges of the port opened to international trade in 1859. Today converted into galleries, shops, and restaurants, they form a very pleasant waterfront for strolling. In the evening, lighting highlights the brick facades with the illuminated Bay Bridge as backdrop. In winter, a European-inspired Christmas market sets up here.

⏱ 60 min
2

Take the ferry from Mikasa Park (Yokosuka, 30 minutes south of Yokohama by train) to Sarushima Island, the only uninhabited natural island in Tokyo Bay. The crossing takes 10 minutes. On the island, moss-covered brick tunnels and abandoned bunkers dating from the Meiji era give a post-apocalyptic atmosphere. From late September to early November, golden pampas grass (susuki) covers the island's heights—a startling spectacle just offshore from Tokyo.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Returning to Yokohama, tour Chinatown (Chūkagai)—Japan's largest Chinese quarter, with over 500 restaurants and shops spread across 10 streets. Nikuman (steamed meat buns) are sold on every corner. If you have time, ascend Yokohama Landmark Tower (296m), whose observatory offers views of the bay and, on clear days, Mount Fuji.

⏱ 75 min

Day 14

Hakone

Volcanic resort where silvery pampas fields glow golden beneath fumarole valleys

★★★★☆
📍 Sengokuhara Pampas Grass Hakone📍 Owakudani Hakone📍 Lake Ashi Hakone
1

The pampas grass fields (susuki) of Sengokuhara, between Hakone and Gotemba, stretch across a vast plain. From late September to early November, silvery plumes sway in the breeze and gradually change color—from silvery green to deep gold as autumn advances. At sunset, raking light illuminates the plumes by transparency—that's when you need to be there. A short trail crosses the field.

⏱ 60 min
2

Take advantage of your time in Hakone to revisit Owakudani Valley and its volcanic fumaroles (see Day 6 of the classic Japan itinerary), or for a cruise on Lake Ashi with views of Fuji on clear days. The torii gate of Hakone-jinja Shrine planted in the lake is one of the region's most famous photo spots.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

End the day in a Hakone onsen—the hot spring resort is one of the most renowned in Kanto. Many establishments offer outdoor baths (rotenburo) with views of mountains or valley. Some are open to drop-in visitors for a few hours.

⏱ 90 min

Day 15

Kamakura

Former shogunate capital where a 121-ton bronze Buddha has sat in open air for 500 years

★★★★☆
📍 Kotoku-in Great Buddha Kamakura📍 Hasedera Temple Kamakura📍 Tsurugaoka Hachimangu Shrine Kamakura📍 Hokokuji Bamboo Temple Kamakura
1

Kamakura's Great Buddha (Daibutsu), at Kōtoku-in Temple, measures 11.3 meters tall (13.35m with base) and weighs about 121 tons of bronze. Built starting in 1252, it was originally gilded and sheltered in a wooden temple—but a tsunami in 1498 swept away the building, leaving the Buddha in the open air ever since. You can enter inside the hollow statue. It's Japan's second-largest seated bronze Buddha, after the one in Nara. Allow 30-45 minutes for the visit.

⏱ 45 min
2

A few minutes' walk away, Hase-dera Temple, founded in the 8th century, perches on a hillside with views over Sagami Bay. Its eleven-headed Kannon statue (9.18m) is one of Japan's largest wooden statues. The terraced gardens, lotus ponds, and cave with thousands of small Benten statues are enchanting. Climb to the viewpoint at the temple's top—the view over sea and city is Kamakura's finest.

⏱ 60 min
3

Tsurugaoka Hachiman-gū Shrine, founded in 1063 by Minamoto no Yoriyoshi, is Kamakura's spiritual heart. A long avenue bordered by cherry trees (Dankazura) leads from the sea to the shrine—in April, it's a flower tunnel. From the top of the stairs, views plunge down the entire avenue to the ocean.

⏱ 45 min
4

If you have time, push on to Hōkoku-ji Temple (bamboo temple), founded in 1334. Its bamboo forest, more intimate than Arashiyama's in Kyoto, can be toured in a few minutes. Sit in the tea house to enjoy matcha facing the bamboo—it's one of the most zen moments of the entire itinerary.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Kamakura is ideally explored on foot or by renting a bicycle. The Daibutsu hiking trail (about 3km, 1h30) connects Kita-Kamakura Station to the Great Buddha, passing through Jōchi-ji Temple and Zeniarai Benzaiten Shrine (a shrine in a cave where visitors wash their coins to make them multiply). It's a beautiful route to conclude your Kanto circuit.

⏱ 90 min
