Northern Vietnam Circuit: Rice Terraces, Mountains, and Ha Long Bay

📅 11 days📍 11 stops🚗 1130 km⏱ 27h

Hanoi → Ninh Binh → Pu Luong → Mai Chau → Moc Chau → Sapa → Ha Long

Hanoi → Ninh Binh → Pu Luong → Mai Chau → Moc Chau → Sapa → Ha Long

This 11-day circuit through northern Vietnam connects Hanoi to the Sapa mountains via the karst landscapes of Ninh Binh, the terraced rice fields of Pu Luong, and the Thai villages of Mai Chau. It concludes with a cruise in Ha Long Bay, a UNESCO World Heritage site since 1994, featuring 1,969 limestone islets rising from the waters of the Gulf of Tonkin. An itinerary designed for those who want to see multiple faces of the North - the colonial city, ethnic mountains, and the South China Sea - without sacrificing authenticity.

Stop 1

Hanoi

Where thousand-year temples meet French colonial bridges that survived American bombs

★★★★★
📍 Hoan Kiem Lake Hanoi📍 Ngoc Son Temple Hanoi📍 Old Quarter Hanoi📍 Long Bien Bridge Hanoi
1

Drop your bags and head out on foot into the labyrinth of the Old Quarter, where the first trading alleys date back to the 15th century. Each street still bears the name of the craft once practiced there: Hang Bac for silver, Hang Gai for silk, Hang Ma for votive paper objects. The quarter developed during the Lê dynasty era, when artisans from across the Red River Delta came to settle in guilds. Arrive early in the morning - traffic is already dense by 8am - or in the evening when the pedestrian streets come alive around the lake. Be wary of street vendors near Hoan Kiem Lake: the pressure can be intense. Better to move one or two streets away for some peace.

⏱ 90 min
2

Hoan Kiem Lake - 'Lake of the Returned Sword' - takes its name from a 15th-century legend: Emperor Lê Lợi is said to have returned his magic sword to a divine tortoise here after driving out the Ming invaders. The lake's last giant tortoise died in 2016; its stuffed body is displayed in Ngoc Son Temple, established on Jade Island since the 1865 restoration supervised by scholar Nguyễn Văn Siêu. It's accessed via the The Huc Bridge - 45 meters of red-lacquered wood, built that same year. The temple honors General Trần Hưng Đạo, who repelled three Mongol invasions in the 13th century. Plan to visit in late afternoon: when the lights come on around 6pm, the reflection of the red bridge on the water is truly worth the detour.

⏱ 60 min
3

About fifteen minutes' walk northeast of Hoan Kiem Lake, Long Bien Bridge spans the Red River for 1,682 meters. Built between 1898 and 1902 by the French company Daydé & Pillé for a cost of 6.2 million francs of the time, it was then one of the four longest bridges in the world and the first steel bridge to cross the Red River. American aviation bombed it repeatedly between 1967 and 1972; today only nine of the original nineteen spans remain, the others having been rebuilt in a different form. You can cross it on foot or by bike - only two-wheelers and pedestrians are now admitted. The sunset from the middle of the bridge, over the river's channels and the banana fields of Phu Xua Island, is photographed every evening by dozens of Hanoians.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In Hanoi, missing the egg coffee would be an unforgivable mistake. The recipe was born in the 1940s, when a Sofitel Metropole bartender named Nguyễn Văn Giảng replaced milk - a scarce commodity in wartime - with egg yolks beaten with sugar and condensed milk. The result: a thick, creamy foam crowning a strong robusta coffee, served in a bowl of hot water to maintain the temperature. Vendors in the Old Quarter, particularly in Nguyễn Hữu Huân alley, serve this drink in ancestral houses where you sometimes have to walk through the ground-floor shop and climb a wooden staircase to reach the room. Don't look for big signs - it's the small, discreet addresses that serve the best versions.

⏱ 30 min

In the evening, the Old Quarter transforms: the pedestrian streets around Hoan Kiem Lake come alive with street vendors, phở sellers, and bánh mì stands. Walk along Hang Buom or Hang Bac after 7pm - that's when Hanoians come out to sit on their small plastic stools to eat. Don't miss the bún chả, grilled pork skewers served in a fish sauce broth with rice noodles - it's the quintessential evening dish in this part of the city.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 2

Ninh Binh

Southeast Asia's first mixed UNESCO site, where ancient dynasties ruled from limestone fortresses

★★★★★
📍 Tam Coc Ninh Binh📍 Hang Mua Ninh Binh📍 Trang An Ninh Binh📍 Hoa Lu ancient capital Vietnam
1

An hour's drive from Hanoi, Ninh Binh conceals one of the most forgotten pages of Vietnamese history: Hoa Lu was the country's first unified capital, founded in the 10th century by King Đinh Bộ Lĩnh after he ended the rival lordships. The capital remained here from 968 to 1010, before the court moved to Thang Long - present-day Hanoi. The two main temples, built in the 17th century on the site of the old palaces, pay tribute to the Đinh and Lê dynasties. The site is nestled between 200-meter limestone peaks that served as natural ramparts - a deliberate strategic choice. Arrive at opening if you want to avoid the tourist buses that arrive around 10am.

⏱ 60 min
2

Inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage list in June 2014 - the first mixed (cultural and natural) site in Southeast Asia - the Trang An complex covers 6,226 hectares of limestone karsts, tropical forests, and partially flooded valleys connected by a network of underground rivers. Excavations in the caves have revealed traces of human occupation over more than 30,000 years, from Paleolithic hunter-gatherers to Neolithic farmers. The visit is done exclusively by rowboat, on circuits of two to three hours that pass through up to nine caves. Choose your boat as early as possible in the morning - around 7:30am if you can - and avoid weekends when the traffic jam of boats in the caves is anything but poetic.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Tam Coc - 'three caves' in Vietnamese - is the most photogenic and most touristic version of Ninh Binh: flat-bottomed boats go up the Ngô Đồng River between vegetation-covered limestone peaks, before crossing three caves carved into the rock. Unlike Trang An, the boatmen here row with their feet, hands free, a local habit that never ceases to fascinate visitors. The landscape is splendid - especially in May-June when the rice fields are jade green, and in September-October when they turn golden. Expect souvenir sales attempts midway: politely decline and enjoy the view.

⏱ 90 min

From below, Hang Mua hill doesn't look like much. But at the top of its 500 steps carved into the limestone, you'll have before your eyes the panorama that illustrates almost all the brochures on Ninh Binh: the Ngô Đồng River winding between the peaks, rice fields as far as the eye can see, and sometimes morning mist lingering in the valleys. Wear good shoes - the steps can be slippery in wet weather - and climb preferably early in the morning before 8am or late afternoon to avoid the heat and crowds.

Stop 3

Pu Luong

A 17,600-hectare sanctuary where bamboo water wheels still irrigate terraces carved by Thai and Muong hands

★★☆☆☆
📍 Pu Luong Nature Reserve Thanh Hoa📍 Ban Don Village Pu Luong📍 Terraced rice fields Pu Luong📍 Kho Muong Village Pu Luong
1

The Pu Luong Nature Reserve - 'the highest mountain' in Thai language - extends over more than 17,600 hectares straddling the districts of Ba Thuoc and Quan Hoa, in Thanh Hoa province. It's inhabited primarily by Thai and Muong people who cultivate rice on terraces using methods handed down through generations. What distinguishes Pu Luong from the better-known destinations of northwest Vietnam is its authenticity: few tourists, villages still focused on agriculture, and bamboo water wheels - norias - that still irrigate the rice fields as they have for centuries. The valley is particularly beautiful during the flooded rice paddy season (February-March) and at harvest time (September-October).

⏱ 60 min
2

Ban Don is considered the most accessible and dynamic village in the reserve: 76 households, wooden stilt houses topped with thatched or palm roofs, and women who still weave brocade by hand according to patterns specific to each family. Ban Hieu, perched higher up, offers a view of the terraced rice fields and a waterfall tumbling into the forest. These villages aren't frozen for tourists: residents live and work there, and that's precisely what makes them interesting. Take the rice field paths rather than the road - the spectacle changes dramatically.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Local operators offer bamboo raft descents on the Chàm River, which crosses the reserve's central valley. It's not sporty rafting - the current is calm and navigation is done by pole - but it's a radically different way to see the rice fields and limestone cliffs that border the river. The experience lasts between one and two hours depending on the chosen departure point. Book through your accommodation the day before, as there may be a wait if several groups are present on the same day.

⏱ 90 min

Sleeping in a Thai stilt house in Pu Luong isn't just about comfort - it's access to a world you wouldn't see otherwise. Dinner is shared with the host family: sticky rice cooked in bamboo (com lam), vegetables from the garden, river fish, and often rice alcohol drawn from a communal jar with a bamboo straw. Mosquito nets delineate sleeping spaces in a large common room. There's little or no wifi, electricity can be intermittent, and the rooster crows well before dawn. Those are the rules of the game, and that's why you're here.

⏱ 480 min

Stop 4

Mai Chau

Where White Thai families have woven brocade and danced in stilted houses for seven centuries

★★★★☆
📍 Lake Village Mai Chau📍 Pom Coong Village Mai Chau📍 Mai Chau Valley Hoa Binh📍 Thung Khe Pass Mai Chau
1

On the road between Pu Luong and Mai Chau, Thung Khe Pass rises to about 1,000 meters altitude and offers one of the most striking viewpoints in northwest Vietnam. At this spot, the road carved into the white rock overlooks the entire length of Mai Chau valley - a carpet of rice fields framed by karst mountains. Stop for about twenty minutes: Muong vendors offer grilled corn and sticky rice cooked in bamboo (com lam), ideal for breaking up the journey before descending into the valley.

⏱ 30 min
2

Mai Chau Valley presents itself as a green basin 150 km southwest of Hanoi, surrounded by karst mountains. It's populated mainly by White Thai people. Lac village has housed generations of five large Thai families for over 700 years. Pom Coong village, whose name means 'drum-hill' in Thai, has about 70 households of two-meter-high wooden stilt houses with large windows opening onto the rice fields. Women there still weave intricate patterned brocades that you can buy directly from them - much more pleasant than the souvenir shops in big cities.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Before hitting the road to Mai Chau, leave early in the morning for the loop around Ban Bang - a village circuit that crosses terraced rice fields and forest trails. In the morning, mist often clings to the hillsides for an hour or two, and residents begin their days in the fields: a sober, authentic spectacle you won't encounter on tourist routes. A local guide is recommended to avoid getting lost at the junctions - your accommodation can arrange one the evening before.

⏱ 180 min

Mai Chau valley is flat - and that's rare in northwest Vietnam. The villages of Lac, Pom Coong, and Van are connected by dirt paths that run alongside rice fields and cross a few small streams at fords. Bicycles are available at most accommodations for a handful of dollars per day. Set off without GPS: the valley isn't large, and getting lost has no consequences here. The children who follow you shouting 'hello!' are part of the journey.

⏱ 120 min

In the evenings in the valley villages, Thai families organize traditional dance performances in the large common rooms of the stilt houses. This isn't folklore manufactured for tourists: residents wear costumes they display at real festivals, and the dances - notably the Khen dance, performed with a bamboo wind instrument - tell stories of rural life and agricultural cycles. You'll be invited to dance, and the rice alcohol in the communal jar (Ruou Can, drunk through a straw) always ends up loosening inhibitions.

Stop 5

Moc Chau

A highland plateau where tea hills form heart-shaped curves and plum blossoms paint winter white

★☆☆☆☆
📍 Moc Chau Plateau Son La📍 Tea hills Moc Chau📍 Plum valley Moc Chau Na Ka📍 Hmong village Moc Chau
1

Perched at 1,050 meters altitude, the Moc Chau plateau extends 80 kilometers long and 25 kilometers wide, and enjoys a much cooler climate than the rest of the country - average temperatures hover around 18-20°C all year. The tea plant hills draw green S-curves or hearts depending on the plantation, much prized by photographers. Late in the day, when mist descends on the valleys and tea pickers return with their baskets, the atmosphere is particularly beautiful. Arrive on weekdays if you want to avoid crowds of Vietnamese tourists who come up on weekends from Hanoi.

⏱ 60 min
2

What makes Moc Chau visually spectacular is the succession of floral seasons: in January-February, the plum trees of Na Ka valley - 16 kilometers from town - cover the hills with a white blanket that Hmong residents compare to a first snow. In autumn, fields of yellow mustard and wild sunflowers take over. Some producers have developed techniques to advance flowering by a month, extending the season. If you pass outside these windows, the tea hills remain a beautiful alternative year-round. Check with your accommodation about the current blooms for your travel dates.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Moc Chau is one of the few regions in Vietnam to practice large-scale dairy farming, an activity developed in the 1970s. Operators offer guided farm visits where you can see the herds, watch milking, and taste local dairy products - yogurts and cheeses made on-site. It's not the most authentic visit of the trip, but it's a real curiosity in a country where fresh milk is traditionally absent from the diet.

⏱ 60 min

If you pass through on a Sunday, the Pa Co ethnic market, about twenty kilometers from Moc Chau town, brings together Hmong, Thai, and Muong people in a very colorful weekly gathering. They sell loose tea, spices, medicinal herbs, and hand-embroidered textiles. Otherwise, the central market in Moc Chau town is lively in the morning on ordinary days - river fish, wild vegetables, corn alcohol in jugs. Arrive between 7am and 9am for the atmosphere.

Stop 6

Sapa

Mountain town gateway to Hmong valleys and the cable car to Indochina's rooftop at 3,143 meters

★★★★☆
📍 Sapa town Lao Cai Vietnam📍 Cat Cat Village Sapa Hmong📍 Muong Hoa Valley Sapa📍 Mount Fansipan Sapa
1

Perched at 1,600 meters altitude in the Hoang Lien Son range, Sapa was a French hill station founded in 1903. The remaining colonial buildings - stone church from 1930, former governor's residence - coexist today with modern hotels and the stalls of Hmong women who come down each morning to sell brocades and silver jewelry. Walk around the central market when you arrive: that's where you'll see city residents and ethnic villagers side by side, before the tourist crowd takes over around 10am.

⏱ 60 min
2

Cat Cat village is located just 3 kilometers from central Sapa - a half-hour descent on foot via a trail that crosses rice fields. Formed in the 19th century by Black Hmong families, the village preserves traditional craft practices: women weave and embroider indigo-dyed clothing, and demonstrators still show the dyeing work on-site. A hydroelectric plant built by the French is still operating beside the waterfall. It's the most touristic village in the region - for something more authentic, save your energy for the villages of Muong Hoa valley the next day. But for a quick first immersion when arriving in Sapa, Cat Cat does the job very well.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Sapa sits at 1,600 meters altitude on the slope of the Hoang Lien Son range. When the sky is clear - which isn't guaranteed - the view of Muong Hoa valley from viewpoints above town is striking: 15 kilometers of terraced rice fields stacked on the slopes, dotted with Hmong and Dao hamlets. In late afternoon, the oblique light on the flooded terraces (May-June) or golden stalks (September-October) yields photographs you'll remember for a long time. If fog arrives - and it often does - don't worry: Sapa's misty atmosphere has its own admirers.

⏱ 60 min

In the evening in Sapa, Black Hmong and Red Dao women come down to town and set up on the sidewalks of the central square to sell their embroidery by lamplight. It's not an organized market - it's simply their daily way of earning a bit of money after the day. Take time to sit beside them, even without buying. The atmosphere of this square in the evening, with mists descending from the mountains and the sounds of the night market, is one of the most distinctive images of the entire journey.

⏱ 60 min

Sapa

Mountain town gateway to Hmong valleys and the cable car to Indochina's rooftop at 3,143 meters

★★★★☆
📍 Ta Van Lao Chai villages Muong Hoa valley📍 Fansipan cable car Sun World Sapa📍 Bac Ha Sunday market Lao Cai📍 Lao Cai train station night train
1

Muong Hoa Valley extends for about fifteen kilometers southeast of Sapa, between 900 and 1,600 meters altitude. The terraced rice fields covering its slopes are ranked among the most spectacular in Southeast Asia: each level has been carved by hand by successive generations of Hmong farmers, and the flooded terraces of May-June or golden ones in September-October change color and texture with each hour of the day. Interpretive signs in several languages explain the traditional irrigation system. Take time to sit by the edge of a canal before setting off on your trek - the spectacle deserves five minutes of stillness.

⏱ 60 min
2

Muong Hoa Valley is the backbone of trekking in Sapa. It extends about fifteen kilometers and houses two of the most interesting villages in the region. Lao Chai is a Black Hmong village - the indigo-dyed clothing of the women stains hands if you touch it, and that's not a bad way to start a conversation. Ta Van, two kilometers away, is a Giay village whose wooden stilt houses are built differently from those of neighboring Hmong. Between the two, rice terraces descend in wide steps toward the valley floor. The trail connecting the two villages crosses wooden bridges, cardamom fields, and a few small streams at fords. Count on 4 to 5 hours for the complete loop from Sapa.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

Fansipan peaks at 3,143 meters, making it the highest point in Vietnam, Laos, and Cambodia combined - hence the nickname 'Roof of Indochina.' The cable car inaugurated in 2016, one of the longest in the world, connects the Sapa station to the summit in 15 minutes. At the top: a giant Buddha statue, several pagodas, and in clear weather, a panorama of the Hoang Lien Son range. Dress warmly - it's generally 5 to 10°C cooler than in town, and fog can arrive without warning. If you prefer exertion, the ascent on foot takes two to three days with a mandatory guide.

⏱ 180 min

Bac Ha, 110 kilometers from Sapa, hosts every Sunday one of the most colorful and least artificial ethnic markets in northern Vietnam. The town is the capital of the Flower Hmong - whose women wear embroidered costumes predominantly orange that artisans take 4 to 6 months to make. More than 14 ethnic groups gather there each Sunday, with stalls of meat, medicinal plants, spices, corn wine, silver jewelry, and brocade. Arrive before 10am - that's when tour buses arrive. If your day 7 isn't a Sunday, skip it; the village without the market has little interest.

Hanoi

Where thousand-year temples meet French colonial bridges that survived American bombs

★★★★★
📍 Temple of Literature Hanoi📍 Vietnam Museum of Ethnology Hanoi📍 Water puppets Hanoi Thang Long📍 Hanoi Railway Station
1

The Temple of Literature is one of the few preserved architectures from the pre-colonial era in Hanoi. Founded in 1070 under King Lý Thánh Tông, it was dedicated to Confucius and housed Vietnam's first institution of higher learning, the Quoc Tu Giam, founded in 1076. For nearly 700 years, scholars from across the country came to take the mandarin examinations. The tortoise steles in the central courtyard - on which are engraved the names of doctorate recipients between 1442 and 1779 - have been classified as UNESCO Memory of the World since 2011. Come on weekdays and avoid weekends. The morning light in the main courtyard, filtered through the ginkgos, is worth the effort of an early departure after a night train.

⏱ 60 min
2

If you only visit one museum during this trip, make it this one. The Hanoi Museum of Ethnology, opened in 1997 with support from France, documents the 54 officially recognized ethnic groups in Vietnam. The collections cover costumes, funeral practices, architecture, musical instruments, and rituals - everything you observed in the villages of Pu Luong, Mai Chau, and Sapa takes on another dimension when you see it explained and placed in context. In the outdoor garden, traditional houses have been reconstructed at full scale: a Hmong house, a Tay stilt house, a Gia Rai tomb. Allow at least 1h30 to avoid rushing through.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Lao Cai-Hanoi night train is an experience in itself, not just a means of transport. To take the train, you first need to reach Lao Cai from Sapa by bus or taxi (about 45 minutes). Departures are between 8pm and 10:30pm and arrival in Hanoi is between 4:30am and 7am depending on the chosen train. Private companies offer more comfortable carriages than state trains - 4-berth cabins with air conditioning, bedding, electrical outlets. The journey takes about 8 hours over 253 kilometers of winding meter-gauge track. This train is affectionately called the 'TGV - Train of Great Vibrations.' Book in advance in high season; lower berths go first.

⏱ 480 min

Water puppet theater is an art form born in Red River Delta villages in the 11th century. The Thang Long Theater, on the shore of Hoan Kiem Lake, is the most renowned in the capital. Puppeteers control the characters from behind a curtain, standing in water up to their waists. Shows last 50 minutes and tell scenes of Vietnamese life through music and humor: fishing, plowing, the legend of the lake tortoise. It's a good way to end your stay in Hanoi - accessible, festive, and authentically Vietnamese.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 9

Ha Long

Where 1,969 limestone karsts sculpted over millions of years rise like dragons from emerald waters

★★☆☆☆
📍 Tuan Chau Port Ha Long cruises📍 Ha Long Bay Vietnam UNESCO📍 Sung Sot Cave Ha Long Bay📍 Bai Tu Long Bay Ha Long
1

Ha Long Bay covers 1,553 km² and has 1,969 limestone karst islands, of which 989 bear names often inspired by their silhouette: Elephant Islet, Fighting Cock Islet, Teapot Islet. Inscribed on the UNESCO heritage list since 1994, the bay formed over several hundred million years from a Paleozoic seabed, eroded by rains and underground rivers after the sea receded. Its name - 'Ha Long' - means 'descending dragon' in Vietnamese. Sung Sot Cave ('Cave of Surprises'), discovered by the French in 1901, is one of the largest: its two vast chambers reach 30 meters high in places. To avoid crowds, choose a junk that sails toward Bai Tu Long Bay, to the north - the landscapes are just as spectacular and the boats much fewer.

⏱ 360 min
2

Discovered by the French in 1901, Sung Sot Cave is the largest and most visited in Ha Long Bay. It consists of two vast chambers, the first reaching 30 meters in height. The lighting installed by authorities accentuates the shapes of the stalactites - one rock resembles a general in armor, another a giant tortoise; guides will point them out. It's not the most secluded visit in the bay, but the dimensions of the chambers remain impressive. Arrive among the first boats of the morning to have a few minutes of calm before the groups.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Bai Tu Long Bay, north of the main Ha Long Bay, is the best alternative for those who want to avoid the tourist highway while enjoying identical landscapes. Operators offer 2-day, 1-night cruises aboard wooden junks with 10 to 24 cabins. The first day's afternoon includes a kayaking session in caves and lagoons, a cave visit, and a sunset from the upper deck. The night is spent at anchor in a sheltered bay, in a calm that the motorized boats of central Ha Long no longer offer. Book in advance in high season and specify that you want a junk in Bai Tu Long Bay.

⏱ 480 min

The first evening in Ha Long Bay is often the one you remember most. Go up to the junk's upper deck an hour before sunset and settle in with a beer or fruit juice. The oblique light on the karsts shifts from gold to orange then red in the space of twenty minutes, and the silhouettes of islets stand out against an often clear sky. There's nothing particular to do - just watch. Bring a light jacket; the sea cools quickly after 6pm even in summer.

Ha Long

Where 1,969 limestone karsts sculpted over millions of years rise like dragons from emerald waters

★★☆☆☆
📍 Floating village Ha Long Bay📍 Cat Ba Island national park📍 Ha Long Port junk disembarkation📍 Lan Ha Bay Cat Ba kayak
1

The floating villages of Ha Long Bay - including Cua Van, the most visited - bring together fishing families who have lived on platforms anchored between the islets for generations. These families raise fish in cages under the floors of their floating houses and move exclusively by boat. The visit is generally done by rowboat, early in the morning. It's a quiet moment, far from group activities - arrive before the other junks to have the bay to yourself.

⏱ 60 min
2

Thien Canh Son Cave is one of the star attractions of Bai Tu Long Bay, less known than Sung Sot but just as beautiful. It cuts into several galleries, some of which open onto a closed lagoon accessible only by kayak. Unlike the caves of the main Ha Long Bay, you'll generally encounter only three or four other boats during your visit - making it one of the most peaceful underground experiences in the region. Wear non-slip shoes; the wet floor can be treacherous.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Most junks offer a sunrise tai chi session on the upper deck, led by a crew member. It's not an activity to take too seriously - half the participants join in pajamas with legs still sleepy - but getting up at 5:30am to see the karsts emerge from the morning fog in the silence of the bay is truly worth the effort.

⏱ 30 min

Cat Ba Island, the largest in the archipelago at 260 km², houses a national park classified as a UNESCO biosphere reserve since 2004. More than half the island is covered in dense tropical forest, crossed by 18 kilometers of trails. The park notably shelters the Cat Ba golden-headed langur, a critically endangered monkey of which only about sixty individuals remain in the wild. If your junk stops at Cat Ba before disembarkation, a half-day hike in the park or a bike tour to the fishing village of Viet Hai is a beautiful way to close the cruise.

Stop 11

Hanoi

Where thousand-year temples meet French colonial bridges that survived American bombs

★★★★★
📍 Dong Xuan Market Hanoi📍 Old Quarter Hanoi📍 Noi Bai Airport Hanoi📍 Hang Gai Street silk Hanoi
1

Dong Xuan Market is Hanoi's largest covered market. It was built by the French in 1889, rebuilt after a fire in 1994, and today houses several hundred shops spread over five floors. You'll find everything: fabrics, clothing, kitchen utensils, spices, toys, dried flowers, and a particularly lively food section on the ground floor in the morning. It's not a tourist market - it's a wholesale and retail market for Hanoians. It's a good place to buy practical souvenirs: Vietnamese spices, tea, fabrics, or ceramic dishes.

⏱ 60 min
2

Hang Gai Street - originally 'hemp street' - is today the main address for silk and linen in the Old Quarter. You'll find fabric shops by the meter, tailors, and clothing houses that produce ao dai (traditional tunics) in two or three days. It's also where lacquer and silk painting shops are concentrated. Prices are negotiable but reasonable. If you're looking for gifts to bring back - silk scarves, embroidered tablecloths, linen clothing - it's the most convenient place in the quarter to find everything in an hour.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Before heading to the airport, take one last stroll through the Old Quarter alleys. Hang Gai Street is the best address for silk and linen clothing. Hang Bac Street for silver jewelry. Hang Ma Street for paper objects and lanterns. If you haven't yet tasted bánh cuốn - steamed rice crepes stuffed and drizzled with fish sauce - stop at one of the morning shops around Dong Xuan Market. It's a typical Hanoian breakfast.

⏱ 90 min

Noi Bai International Airport is located 45 kilometers north of central Hanoi. The road is quick outside rush hours (7am-9am and 5pm-7pm), but can take 90 minutes on busy days. Plan to arrive at the airport at least 2h30 before departure for international flights - security check lines can be long during high season.
