✨ Highlights
  • Panama Canal: Miraflores locks raise ships 26 meters between Pacific and Atlantic—one of the world's most visited hydraulic engineering marvels
  • Volcán Barú (3,474 m): the only place on Earth where you can simultaneously see both oceans on clear days, from Panama's highest point
  • Coiba (UNESCO): former penal colony (1919-2004) whose isolation preserved 80% of its primary forest and one of the eastern Pacific's richest reefs
  • San Blas / Guna Yala: 365 coral islets autonomously managed by the Guna people since their 1925 revolution, without chain hotels or mass tourism
📆 Best period

January, February, March, April

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 6 and 10 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Dry Season (December-April): Peak tourist season with minimal rainfall, comfortable temperatures (24-32°C), and excellent conditions for outdoor activities and beach visits. Highest prices and crowds.

Shoulder Season (May, November): Good weather with brief afternoon showers, moderate prices, and fewer tourists. Excellent value while maintaining pleasant conditions for most activities.

Green Season (May-November): Higher rainfall with lush landscapes, significantly lower prices, and fewer crowds. Morning activities work well before afternoon rains arrive.

Regional Variations: Caribbean coast wetter year-round, Pacific coast has distinct dry/wet seasons. Mountain areas cooler with different precipitation patterns than lowlands.

🌍 What to discover in PA?

Central American bridge with canal engineering, biodiversity, and cultural diversity. Experience traditional cultures, incredible nature, and historical significance. The country offers cultural immersion with natural beauty and engineering marvels. Panama provides diverse experiences with natural wonders and cultural authenticity.

📋 Important notes

The Bocas del Toro → San Blas transfer (D15) requires a domestic flight Bocas → Panama City (1h) + 4x4 drive to Cartí (2-3h) + boat. This is a tight one-day journey: plan to leave very early. San Blas (Guna Yala) is an autonomous Guna territory; an entry fee is collected upon arrival.

⚠️ SAFETY NOTE: Panama is generally safe in tourist areas, though standard precautions apply regarding petty crime in cities. Avoid displaying valuables, use official transportation, and stay in well-established tourist zones. Some remote border areas with Colombia should be avoided due to security concerns.

Panama

★★★★★
📍 Cinta Costera Panama City📍 Letras PANAMA frente al mar📍 Casco Viejo Panama City📍 Mercado de Mariscos Panama City
1

Start at the Cinta Costera to see the giant letters spelling "PANAMA" on the waterfront—the skyline behind you, the canal entrance ahead. This coastal boulevard connects the banking district to Casco Viejo along the Pacific bay: a 3 km promenade perfect for immediately grasping the capital's dual identity between skyscrapers and heritage.

⏱ 60 min
2

Casco Viejo was born from fire. In 1671, Welsh pirate Henry Morgan sacked and burned the old city, forcing the Spanish to rebuild everything on a more defensible peninsula. The new quarter officially opened on January 21, 1673, laid out on a military grid centered on Plaza Mayor—today's Plaza de la Independencia, measuring 57 by 57 meters. A UNESCO World Heritage site since 1997, Casco Viejo blends baroque colonial facades, wrought-iron republican-era balconies, and interior courtyards. Don't miss Plaza Bolívar, the San José church with its entirely gilded wooden altar rescued from the 1671 fire, and the Paseo Las Bóvedas along the water for the late-afternoon light. Allow at least 90 minutes to wander without a fixed plan.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Mercado de Mariscos, on the waterfront a stone's throw from Casco Viejo, is the capital's fish market. Arrive early in the morning when boats unload and stalls overflow with fresh corvina, lobster, and shrimp. On the ground floor, ceviche stands serve generous portions from 7 AM onward. It's noisy, colorful, and the best place in the city to taste Panamanian ceviche for under a dollar per bowl.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Sunset stroll on the Cinta Costera: settle by the water as the sky turns orange over the skyline. Panamanians gather here in the evening to jog, walk children, and watch container ships enter the bay. The light on the glass towers is particularly beautiful between 6 and 6:30 PM.

Panama

★★★★★
📍 Cerro Ancon Panama City📍 Panama Viejo ruinas📍 Miraflores Locks Canal de Panama📍 Parque Nacional Soberania Pipeline Road Gamboa
1

Cerro Ancón rises 199 meters above Panama City—a tropical forest hill enclosed within the city, ten minutes by taxi from Casco Viejo. It's the capital's highest point, and the views from the summit are striking: on one side Casco Viejo and the glass towers, on the other the Miraflores locks and the Bridge of the Americas spanning the canal entrance. In 1671, Morgan's scouts climbed here to survey the city's defenses before attacking. Leave early: the ascent takes 30 to 40 minutes and it gets hot quickly. You'll encounter sloths, toucans, and coatis along the paved trail.

⏱ 75 min
2

Panamá Viejo is where it all began. Conquistador Pedrarias Dávila founded the first permanent European settlement on the Pacific coast of the Americas here on August 15, 1519. The city quickly became a hub for gold and silver destined for Spain, reaching 10,000 inhabitants by the mid-17th century—before being sacked by Morgan in 1671. What you see today: 28 hectares of ruins across 19 different sites, including the cathedral tower, the only structure still standing after the fire. Climb the tower to see Panama City's skyline from the colonial rubble—the contrast is striking. The on-site museum is well designed and worth an hour's visit.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Miraflores locks are the closest to the capital—20 minutes by car—and the only ones offering a four-story museum in addition to the observation platform. Ships rise or descend 16 meters depending on their direction. From the terrace, you watch ocean giants—some carrying 140,000-ton cargoes—glide past you with millimeter precision. The museum explains how Ferdinand de Lesseps began construction in 1881 and abandoned it after thousands of deaths, leaving the Americans to complete the canal in 1914. An IMAX 3D film narrated by Morgan Freeman rounds out the visit. Check the Canal Authority's website for large vessel passage times before going: mornings are best.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Soberanía National Park / Pipeline Road: 45 minutes from Panama City, this 17 km trail through primary forest is a global reference for birdwatching. From 1975 to 1995, Pipeline Road held the Audubon Society's Christmas Bird Count world record for 20 consecutive years with 357 species identified in 24 hours—a continental record. In a 3-4 hour morning, you can easily spot 100 to 150 species: toucans, motmots, trogons, Guianan cock-of-the-rock, plus 4 monkey species and sloths in the trees. The Rainforest Discovery Center, in the same area, offers a 32-meter observation tower into the canopy. Leave at sunrise—birds are active from 6 AM and the light is much better.

El Valle De Anton

Village nestled at 600m in a dormant stratovolcano's crater, surrounded by rare orchids and tropical waterfalls

★★★☆☆
📍 Altos de Campana parc national Panama📍 El Valle de Anton artisanal market📍 El Valle de Anton caldeira volcanique
1

On the Panama City → El Valle route, stop at Altos de Campana park—Panama's oldest national park, created in 1966, with panoramic views of the Pacific coast and the volcanic chain. The road then plunges into the caldera: you literally descend into the crater of an ancient stratovolcano dormant for 13,000 years, one of the only continuously inhabited volcanic calderas in the world.

⏱ 60 min
2

El Valle's municipal market runs Sunday mornings on the main avenue, from around 7 AM until 5 PM. Farmers from the surrounding hills come down with their stalls: tropical fruits, orchids including the national flower (Espíritu Santo), hand-woven hammocks, soapstone carvings shaped like animals—a local specialty—painted wooden plates and trays, molas from indigenous communities, and life-size tropical birds carved from wood. It's a genuine village market, not a tourist market: you'll encounter as many locals doing their shopping as visitors. Arrive before 9 AM to see it at its busiest.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Warm-up walk in the volcanic caldera: El Valle sits at 600 meters elevation on the floor of a 6 km diameter crater, surrounded by walls rising to 1,000 meters. A stroll along the main streets helps you grasp this unique geography. The volcanic hot springs on the village edge, where water reaches 40°C and displays reddish tones from minerals, are worth a stop after the drive.

El Valle De Anton

Village nestled at 600m in a dormant stratovolcano's crater, surrounded by rare orchids and tropical waterfalls

★★★☆☆
📍 La India Dormida El Valle de Anton📍 APROVACA orchid garden El Valle📍 Mariposario El Valle de Anton📍 Chorro El Macho waterfall El Valle📍 Chorro Las Mozas El Valle
1

The La India Dormida hike is the valley's most famous. Its name comes from the mountain profile which, viewed from the crater floor, draws the silhouette of a reclining woman. The panoramic loop offers 360° views of the entire caldera and the Pacific coast on clear days. Allow 2 to 4 hours depending on your pace. Along the way, you'll pass the Piedra Pintada, a huge boulder covered with pre-Columbian petroglyphs whose meaning remains debated—regional map, agricultural calendar, or religious symbols, nobody knows for sure.

⏱ 180 min
2

The APROVACA orchid garden, founded in 2001 with financial support from the Japanese government, conserves more than 140 threatened indigenous orchid species. Its mission: greenhouse propagation for forest reintroduction. You'll see the Espíritu Santo orchid (Peristeria elata), Panama's national flower, cultivated under surveillance in a dedicated greenhouse—each plant has a sponsor.

⏱ 45 min
3

The butterfly house (Mariposario) is a covered aviary housing dozens of tropical species. An educational station explains the lepidopteran life cycle and pesticide-free conservation methods. It's brief—allow 30 minutes—but a lovely pause before the waterfalls.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

El Macho waterfall is the valley's highest at 35 meters. It plunges into a natural pool accessible after a short walk through vegetation—swimming possible depending on water levels. The path follows the Quebrada Amarilla, and you'll often encounter colorful frogs along the banks.

⏱ 90 min

Las Mozas waterfall and its natural pools: less tall than El Macho but more accessible, Las Mozas is particularly appreciated for its natural basins carved into volcanic rock. If you only swim at one waterfall, choose this one for the pool experience.

Fishing village turned Panama's surf capital, gateway to UNESCO-listed prison island Coiba since 2005

★☆☆☆☆
📍 Santa Catalina village Panama📍 Playa El Estero Santa Catalina Panama
1

The El Valle → Santa Catalina drive is long (about 3h30) but traverses spectacular Pacific panoramas in the final kilometers, with views of the Gulf of Chiriquí and offshore islands. Santa Catalina itself is a tiny fishing village—one main street, no reliable ATM, frequent power outages—that surfers worldwide have put on the map. Playa El Estero, 2 km east of the village, is a wide dark sand beach with a sandy bottom and long, regular waves—Panama's best beginner beach according to most local instructors. No reefs, no treacherous currents at this spot, 1 to 1.5 meter waves that let you stay up for a long time. This is where you settle in to scout spots, take a lesson if you're starting out, or simply watch the late-afternoon sessions.

Coastal walk to the Punta Roca viewpoint: 30 minutes on foot from the village along exposed reefs offers a view of the La Punta entrance, the legendary advanced surfers' break. Stop by in late afternoon to watch the light on the ocean and observe experienced surfers' sessions.

Santa Catalina

Fishing village turned Panama's surf capital, gateway to UNESCO-listed prison island Coiba since 2005

★☆☆☆☆
📍 Coiba Island National Park Panama📍 Granito de Oro Coiba snorkeling📍 Isla Coiba penitenciar colonie
1

Coiba is Central America's largest island (503 km²) and its history deserves attention. From 1919 to 2004, the island housed Panama's most feared prison. Under the Torrijos and Noriega dictatorships, political opponents were sent here, tortured, and executed. Sharks reputed to patrol its waters discouraged any escape attempts. This forced isolation is precisely what protected its forests: 80% of the island remained intact. Declared a national park in 1992 and UNESCO World Heritage site in 2005, Coiba today shelters 760 fish species, 33 shark species, 20 cetacean species, and a 1,700-hectare coral reef—the largest on the American Pacific coast. Plan a boat trip from Santa Catalina (about 1h crossing) with an authorized operator.

⏱ 480 min

Activities

Snorkeling around Coiba's reefs and Granito de Oro islet: the water is translucent for dozens of meters deep, with stingrays, nurse sharks, sea turtles, and dense tropical fish schools. Between mid-August and mid-December, humpback whales migrate to the Gulf of Chiriquí to give birth—if you're lucky, you'll see them pass from the boat. Bring your own equipment if possible; operators rent gear but quality varies.

Playa Santa Catalina, the village's main beach, is protected by Isla Catalina offshore—the waves here are very gentle, ideal for young children or absolute beginners. From the beach, you have a view of La Punta, the legendary advanced surfers' break that international competitions have made famous. La Punta's waves are long, hollow rights, sometimes tubular in swell season (April to October), with a reputation extending far beyond Panama.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Surf lessons at El Estero for beginners, or session at La Punta for advanced surfers. El Estero offers ideal learning conditions: sandy bottom, regular 1 to 1.5 meter waves, no dangerous current. La Punta is a powerful reef right, consistent year-round, reserved for intermediate and advanced surfers—access by cliff stairs or swimming from the village beach. Avoid El Estero in dry season (December-February): strong offshore winds make waves difficult for beginners to read.

⏱ 180 min

Coastal viewpoint exploration on foot: a 30-minute walk from the village along the rocky coastline leads to Punta Brava, a less crowded surf spot with rights and lefts, and open views of the Gulf of Chiriquí. The sunset here over the islets is one of the coast's most beautiful.

Boquete

Misty Chiriquí highlands, birthplace of the world's most expensive Geisha coffee and launching point for Volcán Barú

★★☆☆☆
📍 Boquete Chiriqui Panama📍 Rio Caldera Boquete promenade
1

The Santa Catalina → Boquete drive via David is long (about 4h) but the landscape changes dramatically: you leave the Pacific coastal heat to gradually climb toward Chiriquí's highlands, among Panama's most fertile. David, the provincial capital, is a practical stop for refueling and lunch. Boquete then appears in its misty valley at 1,000 meters elevation, framed by the slopes of Volcán Barú—you quickly understand why the town is nicknamed "the city of eternal spring."

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Late-afternoon walk along the río Caldera: the river flows a few minutes' walk from Boquete's center. Follow the bank northward from the central park—no official maintained trail, but the path naturally follows between riverbanks and coffee hedges. Allow 1h round trip for 2-3 km of easy walking. It's a good spot for hummingbirds (several species frequent coffee flowers), tanagers, and kingfishers hunting at water level. Also useful for gauging your fitness before the Barú ascent.

Boquete

Misty Chiriquí highlands, birthplace of the world's most expensive Geisha coffee and launching point for Volcán Barú

★★☆☆☆
📍 finca café Geisha Boquete Panama📍 Boquete coffee tour plantation📍 Boquete quetzal cloud forest bird watching
1

Visit a finca growing Geisha coffee: introduced to Panama in 1963 from Ethiopia via Costa Rica, this coffee was long ignored—its plants are low-yield and difficult. The turning point came in 2004 when the Peterson family of Hacienda La Esmeralda presented it for the first time at the Best of Panama competition and shattered all auction price records, selling their lot for $20 per pound. Since then, Boquete Geisha has dominated global specialty coffee auctions. It grows between 1,500 and 2,000 meters elevation on Barú's slopes in rich volcanic soil, where cool nights slow maturation and concentrate aromas. In 2019, an Elida Estate lot sold for $1,029 per pound—a world record. A finca visit explains the complete chain: manual picking of red cherries, fermentation, drying, roasting.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Guided coffee tasting and roasting workshop: after the plantation visit, the workshop offers comparison of several Geisha processing methods (washed, natural, anaerobic) from the same terroir—the aromatic differences are striking. Notes of jasmine, bergamot, peach, and tropical fruits for the washed version; more full-bodied and fruity for the natural. This is where the term "specialty coffee" takes on full meaning.

⏱ 90 min

Resplendent quetzal watching in cloud forest: this passerine with iridescent emerald green plumage and a tail exceeding 60 cm is one of Central America's most sought-after birds. Volcán Barú National Park and its cloud forests shelter a significant population. The best observation period is February to May, when males display with their long tails. Join a local guide at dawn—quetzals are active early morning.

Boquete

Misty Chiriquí highlands, birthplace of the world's most expensive Geisha coffee and launching point for Volcán Barú

★★☆☆☆
📍 Lost Waterfalls Trail Boquete Panama📍 Volcan Baru 3474m summit trail📍 Parque Nacional Volcan Baru
1

The Lost Waterfalls Trail (3 waterfalls hike) is one of Boquete's most frequented trails: a roughly 5 km circuit through humid forest that successively reveals three increasingly large waterfalls, with the third plunging over 30 meters. The path is well marked and moderately difficult. Bring good shoes—the ground is often slippery—and water.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

Volcán Barú ascent (3,474 m), Panama's rooftop: this is one of the rare places on Earth where you can simultaneously see the Pacific Ocean and Caribbean Sea on clear days. The volcano is considered potentially active—its last eruption was about 500 years ago—but it's primarily a sleeping giant. The trail from Boquete covers 13.5 km with 2,500 meters elevation gain: allow 5 to 7 hours ascending depending on fitness. The classic option is leaving around midnight to reach the summit at sunrise. It's cold at the summit (near 0°C at night): bring warm layers and a headlamp. Local operators also offer 4x4 drives to near the summit if you prefer avoiding the night hike.

Caribbean archipelago with stilted houses, red-frog beaches, and coral reefs among Panama's most intact

📍 Almirante Panama ferry terminal📍 Bocas Town Isla Colon📍 Bocas del Toro Isla Colon stilt houses

The Boquete → Almirante drive (3-4h) descends toward the Caribbean coast through jungle landscapes and banana plantations. At Almirante, board a collective water-taxi to Isla Colón (30 minutes on the sea): it's your first plunge into Caribbean atmosphere—colorful wooden houses on stilts, reggae from waterfront bars, gentle air. Bocas Town, the archipelago's main town, was founded as a banana port in the 1820s; the banana industry's decline in the 1920s left it frozen in eclectic Antillean architecture blending African, Scottish, and Chinese influences.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Discovering Bocas Town on foot or by bike: the main streets bordered by brightly colored wooden houses can be covered in an hour. Rent a bike to explore the island's north coast in the evening. Christopher Columbus had anchored in this archipelago during his fourth voyage in 1502, and pirates used its sheltered bays to repair their ships throughout the 17th century.

Bocas Del Toro

Caribbean archipelago with stilted houses, red-frog beaches, and coral reefs among Panama's most intact

★★★☆☆
📍 Playa Estrella Starfish Beach Bocas del Toro📍 Boca del Drago Isla Colon Panama📍 Bocas del Drago beach
1

Playa Estrella (Starfish Beach) is accessible from Bocas Town by collective bus to Bocas del Drago (northwest corner of Isla Colón), followed by a 20-minute walk along the coast. This shallow, sheltered cove owes its nickname to the orange starfish populating its sandy bottom—you'll see them clearly from the surface. The water is calm and perfect for swimming. Don't touch the starfish and stay at the surface to observe: removing them from water, even briefly, can kill them.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

Light snorkeling and coastal walk between Bocas del Drago and Playa Estrella: the coastal path connecting both sites runs along rocks, small coves, and mangroves. Bring your mask and snorkel—the shallow reefs between the two beaches shelter colorful tropical fish. The complete round-trip walk with snorkeling takes about 3 hours.

★★★☆☆
📍 Red Frog Beach Isla Bastimentos Panama📍 Old Bank Isla Bastimentos Bocas del Toro📍 Bastimentos National Marine Park Panama

Red Frog Beach, on Isla Bastimentos, takes its name from the bright red poison dart frogs (dendrobates) that inhabit the coastal vegetation. The island itself was named "Bastimentos" (provisions) by Christopher Columbus during his fourth voyage in 1502, for the fresh water and abundant fruit he found there. The beach is a large strip of white sand bordered by dense jungle, with more powerful waves than elsewhere in the archipelago—swimming possible but watch for currents. To see the red frogs, walk through the vegetation at the beach edge immediately after arriving from the dock.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

Water-taxi Isla Colón ↔ Bastimentos and coastal vegetation walk: the water-taxi from Bocas Town takes 15 minutes and drops you at the Red Frog dock. Alternatively, take the boat to Old Bank (the island's Afro-Caribbean village) and hike through the jungle to Wizard Beach and Red Frog Beach (about 45 minutes). This forest path is notably more immersive than the direct boat arrival.

📍 Cayos Zapatillas Bocas del Toro Panama📍 Bahia de los Delfines Bocas del Toro📍 Isla Bastimentos National Marine Park Zapatillas

Excursion to Cayos Zapatillas via Bahía de los Delfines: the two Zapatilla islets (their name comes from "zapatilla," little shoe, their shape resembling a pair of shoes) are part of Bastimentos National Marine Park, created in 1988, Panama's first marine park. Surrounded by immaculate beaches and turquoise waters, they are completely uninhabited. The boat journey from Bocas Town crosses the Bahía de los Delfines where dolphins are frequently observed, but nothing is guaranteed—the probability depends on conditions and season.

⏱ 360 min

Activities

Snorkeling on the Zapatillas reefs: the coral reefs around both islets are among Bocas del Toro archipelago's most intact. You'll see sea turtles, stingrays, moray eels, and a great diversity of tropical fish in very clear waters. Enjoy lunch on Zapatilla 1's beach and the deserted beaches—it's the wildest and most preserved spot in the entire archipelago.

San Blas

365 coral islets autonomously administered by the Guna people, no chain hotels or mass tourism, translucent waters and Isla Perro's wreck

★★☆☆☆
📍 Isla Perro San Blas Panama epave📍 Carti San Blas port embarcadere📍 San Blas Guna Yala islands
1

Bocas → San Blas transfer: the direct overland route is physically unrealistic in one day (10h+ rough track). The most honest and reliable option is taking a domestic flight Bocas del Toro → Panama City (about 1h), then the 4x4 road to Cartí port (2h30 to 3h depending on track conditions) followed by boat to the islands (30 to 45 minutes). Leave Bocas very early to have margin. San Blas (officially Guna Yala, "Guna Land") forms an archipelago of 365 coral islets managed in total autonomy by the Guna people since their 1925 revolution against the Panamanian government. No large hotels, no paved roads: accommodations are bungalows built by Guna families themselves, on full board. An entry fee is collected upon arrival in the territory.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Swimming on the sandbars and snorkeling at Isla Perro's wreck: Isla Perro is San Blas's most famous island. Its main attraction is a gunboat wreck sunk in its shallow waters, now a vibrant artificial reef visible a few meters below the surface from the beach. Scuba diving is prohibited throughout the Guna Yala archipelago—only snorkeling is allowed. The waters are crystalline, the depths shallow, and the fish diversity is remarkable.
