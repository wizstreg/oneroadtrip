Frankfurt

Where Holy Roman emperors were crowned and Europe's financial towers now reach for the sky

★★★★★
1

The Römerberg has been Frankfurt's historic heart since the High Middle Ages. At the center of the square stands the Fountain of Justice (Gerechtigkeitsbrunnen) with its goddess Justitia, and on the west side, the Römer—a three-gabled town hall that's served as the seat of city administration since 1405. Inside, the Kaisersaal (Emperor's Hall) preserves portraits of all 52 emperors of the Holy Roman Empire, from Charlemagne to Francis II. This is where coronation banquets were held after ceremonies in the nearby cathedral. From the Golden Bull of 1356 until 1792, the Römer was the site of imperial elections. The hall and inner courtyard are freely accessible.

⏱ 60 min
2

Continue to the Kaiserdom (St. Bartholomew's Cathedral), whose red Gothic tower rises 95 meters. Consecrated in 1239, it was officially designated the coronation site for Holy Roman Emperors in 1356—ten ceremonies took place here between 1562 and 1792. The current building is a postwar reconstruction, but the cathedral treasury preserves authentic pieces, including a Gothic priestly vestment dated to 1350. Climb the 324 steps of the tower for panoramic views over Frankfurt's rooftops.

⏱ 60 min
3

Just steps away, the Paulskirche (St. Paul's Church) deserves a stop. In 1848, it served as the seat of Germany's first freely elected National Assembly—the founding event of modern German parliamentarism. It was one of the first buildings reconstructed after the war, a testament to its symbolic importance. The austere interior now hosts exhibitions and official ceremonies.

⏱ 30 min
4

Cross the Eiserner Steg, an iron footbridge built in 1869 over the Main River, to reach Sachsenhausen on the south bank. From the bridge, the view aligns half-timbered houses of the Römerberg, Gothic towers, and the financial district skyscrapers in the background—capturing Frankfurt's paradox, nicknamed 'Mainhattan' for this coexistence of medieval and modern. Water level markers engraved in the pillars recall historic floods.

⏱ 25 min
5

The Alt-Sachsenhausen quarter preserves its cobbled lanes and half-timbered houses. This is where the city's cider taverns concentrate: Apfelwein (or Ebbelwoi in local dialect) is served in blue stoneware pitchers in diamond-shaped glasses. Wander the alleys around Frankensteiner Platz before heading to the Museumsufer along the waterfront.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walking tour through the Altstadt: circuit Römerberg → Kaiserdom → Paulskirche → Goethe House (Großer Hirschgraben 23). The writer's birthplace, destroyed during the war, has been completely reconstructed identically with original furniture and objects. You can see the study where Goethe wrote The Sorrows of Young Werther at age 23.

⏱ 120 min

One-hour panoramic cruise on the Main departing from the Museumsufer embankments. The circuit passes the quay's 39 museums, under the Eiserner Steg, and offers a complete reading of the city from the river.

⏱ 60 min

Frankfurt

Where Holy Roman emperors were crowned and Europe's financial towers now reach for the sky

★★★★★
1

The Städel Museum, founded in 1815 by banker Johann Friedrich Städel as Germany's first civic museum foundation, is the crown jewel of the Museumsufer. Its collection spans 700 years of European art: 3,100 paintings, 660 sculptures, and over 100,000 drawings and prints. In 1937, the Nazis confiscated 77 paintings and 700 prints deemed 'degenerate'—the remaining works were evacuated to Bavaria to protect them from bombing. The museum preserves masterpieces by Dürer, Botticelli, Cranach, Rembrandt, Vermeer, Monet, and Picasso. The underground extension inaugurated in 2012, lit by circular skylights at surface level, houses contemporary art.

⏱ 120 min
2

Head back toward the center to reach the Main Tower, one of the few Frankfurt skyscrapers with a public viewing platform on the 55th floor, at 200 meters height. On clear days, views extend to the Taunus mountains to the north and the Odenwald to the south. From here you best understand the 'Mainhattan' skyline: 39 glass and steel towers forming continental Europe's densest financial district.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Palmengarten (botanical garden), open since 1871, is one of Germany's largest with 13 hectares of greenhouses and themed gardens. The tropical greenhouses shelter rare species from the Amazon, Africa, and Asia. It's a breathing space cherished by families and strollers.

⏱ 75 min
4

Finish with a stroll along the Museumsufer, the museum boulevard that lines the south bank of the Main for about 2 km. Over thirty museums cluster here—cinema museum, architecture museum, Jewish museum, communications museum. Every last weekend of August, the Museumsufer Festival transforms this boulevard into one of Europe's largest outdoor cultural events.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Apfelwein tasting in a traditional Sachsenhausen tavern. The local apple cider is served in stoneware pitchers in a glass called Geripptes (ribbed). The tradition of cider taverns in this quarter dates to the 18th century.

⏱ 60 min

Bacharach

A wine village on the Rhine with 14th-century ramparts, frozen in UNESCO amber

★★★★☆
1

Arrive via the right bank of the Rhine—the road follows the river between Rüdesheim and Bacharach, offering a succession of terraced vineyards, perched castle ruins, and half-timbered villages characteristic of the UNESCO World Heritage Upper Middle Rhine Valley. Before reaching Bacharach, stop at Rüdesheim for the Drosselgasse, a 144-meter cobbled alley entirely lined with wine taverns and beer gardens. The wine served is mainly Rheingau Riesling.

⏱ 45 min
2

Ride the cable car (Seilbahn) up to the Niederwald Monument, a bronze statue of Germania built between 1877 and 1883 from sculptor Johann Schilling's design, commemorating the 1870-71 victory and German Empire unification. The ensemble measures 38 meters in height and offers panoramic views over the Rhine and Rheingau vineyards.

⏱ 60 min
3

Enter Bacharach through the ancient fortified gates. The 14th-century ramparts are among the best preserved in the entire Rhine valley. Walk along the rampart path to see the slate roofs, vineyards, and Rhine below from above. The village is first mentioned in a document dated 923; its name evokes Bacchus, god of wine, whose cult already existed in Celtic and Roman times.

⏱ 50 min
4

The Gothic ruins of Wernerkapelle, halfway up the slope between the village and Stahleck Castle. The chapel was begun in 1289 in memory of Werner of Oberwesel, a 16-year-old boy murdered in 1287. Its construction was never completed, interrupted by pogroms against the local Jewish community, falsely accused of the murder. A plaque today commemorates this past. The partially preserved Gothic structure remains striking despite the centuries.

⏱ 25 min
5

Stahleck Castle dominates Bacharach from a ridge 160 meters above the Rhine. Its first mention dates to 1135. Destroyed by French troops during the War of the Palatine Succession in 1689, it was reconstructed in 1925 and now houses a youth hostel. Access to the inner courtyard is free—from the terrace, the view over the Rhine and valley meanders is one of the finest in the area.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Riesling tasting at one of the village wine estates. Bacharach has produced wine since Roman times. Legend has it that Pope Pius II had it shipped by cart all the way to Rome.

⏱ 60 min

Short Rhine cruise from Bacharach pier to Kaub, to see from the water the fortress of Pfalzgrafenstein perched on an island in the middle of the river—a former medieval customs post still intact. Round trip about 1h15.

⏱ 75 min

Cochem

Where the Moselle bends and Reichsburg castle watches over terraced vineyards

★★★★☆
1

Arrive in Cochem via the road that follows the Moselle: steep vineyards frame the river's meanders, and the pointed silhouette of Reichsburg appears from afar, perched 100 meters above the village. Stroll along the Moselle quays—promenades lined with terraces, pleasure boats, and slate-roofed half-timbered houses.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Marktplatz of Cochem and St. Martin's Church, built on the ruins of an 11th-century chapel, form the old town's heart. The square is framed by restored half-timbered houses and a former baroque town hall. From the square, stepped streets climb toward the castle.

⏱ 30 min
3

Walk up via Schlossstrasse to Reichsburg (20 minutes from Marktplatz), the largest hillside castle on the Moselle. Built around the year 1000, first mentioned in 1051, it was destroyed in 1689 by troops of Louis XIV—then purchased in 1868 by Berlin merchant Louis Ravené, who had it completely rebuilt in the neo-Gothic romantic style then in vogue. The castle has 50 rooms, 6 of which are visitable by guided tour: the Gothic-style knights' hall, the Romanesque hall, the Renaissance dining room with its woodwork, and a room housing a 1 km secret passage to the Capuchin monastery in town. In the courtyard, a 50-meter-deep well once supplied water to all inhabitants.

⏱ 90 min
4

Then ascend to Pinnerkreuz by chairlift (Sesselbahn), a viewpoint at 290 meters altitude dominating the Moselle loop. The panorama over terraced vineyards, river meanders, and wooded Eifel hills is the most complete in the entire Cochem region.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

One-hour panoramic cruise on the Moselle from Cochem marina. The circuit heads upriver toward the wine villages of Beilstein or Ediger-Eller, skirting steep terraced vineyards and medieval castle ruins.

⏱ 60 min

Photo walk on the Moselle bridge in Cochem late afternoon: from the bridge, the view axis aligns quays, steeples, and castle silhouette—Cochem's classic composition, particularly flattering in golden evening light.

⏱ 30 min

Cochem

Where the Moselle bends and Reichsburg castle watches over terraced vineyards

★★★★☆
1

Burg Eltz is one of the rare European castles never destroyed and still owned by the same family for 33 generations—the first written trace dates to 1157, when Rudolph 'of Eltz Castle' was cited as witness in an act of Emperor Frederick Barbarossa. The castle rises from the depths of a forested valley, perched on a 70-meter rocky spur surrounded on three sides by the Elzbach stream. In 1268, three brothers divided the property according to a Ganerbschaft system (medieval co-ownership): three family branches cohabited in their own quarters around a narrow inner courtyard, hence the mixed styles—Romanesque, Gothic, Renaissance—visible in the facades. The guided tour (~35 min) covers the knights' hall, the armory (with the world's oldest cannon arrows), the Rübenach Hall furnished in 1311—housing a Virgin and Child by Lucas Cranach the Elder—and the reception hall.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Schatzkammer (Treasury), accessible with the same ticket, is the only place in the castle where photography is allowed. It houses over 500 pieces accumulated by the family over eight centuries: goldsmithery, jewelry, weapons, chalices, armor. It's one of Germany's most important private collections.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Approach hike from the village of Moselkern (on the Moselle bank) to Burg Eltz: a 2.5 km forest trail (35 minutes) along the Elzbach, progressively descending into the wooded valley. The castle appears suddenly at the turn of the last meander. This path is the best way to arrive—and avoid parking lot lines.

⏱ 75 min

Wurzburg

★★★★☆
1

The Würzburg Residence is the city's most ambitious edifice, built between 1720 and 1744 for the prince-bishops of the Schönborn dynasty. Architect Balthasar Neumann designed the plans; inside, Venetian painter Giovanni Battista Tiepolo created between 1750 and 1753 the world's largest ceiling fresco in the Treppenhaus (staircase hall), depicting the four then-known continents. The gilded and stuccoed Imperial Hall is the ensemble's high point. The Residence and its baroque garden were designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1981. A single night of bombing in March 1945 destroyed 90% of Würzburg; miraculously, the Residence survived almost intact.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Festung Marienberg dominates the city from its ridge to the west, accessible from the Alte Mainbrücke via a 25-minute vineyard path (Tellsteige trail). The site has been occupied since Celtic antiquity; a round chapel was built there as early as 704, making it one of Bavaria's oldest Christian structures. The prince-bishops resided here from 1253 to 1720, when they moved to the newly completed Residence. The fortress was stormed only once—by Swedish troops in 1631 during the Thirty Years' War. It now houses the Mainfränkisches Museum, which preserves wood sculptures by Tilman Riemenschneider, the region's Gothic master. From the ramparts and Fürstengarten (princes' garden), the panorama over the city, Main valley, and vineyards is exceptional.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Alte Mainbrücke has connected the old town to the fortress since the 15th century. Its stone construction began in 1476 and was completed in 1543; the twelve baroque statues of saints and historical figures adorning its balustrades were added between 1720 and 1730. In the evening, locals gather here standing, glass of Silvaner in hand, facing the illuminated fortress—a well-established local custom. A wine shop sits at the bridge entrance.

⏱ 45 min
4

Rothenburg Ob Der Tauber

The medieval town that survived centuries and a mayor's legendary drinking feat

★★★☆☆
1

Arrive in Rothenburg ob der Tauber early afternoon—enter through one of the fortified gates and walk the ramparts. The Stadtmauer (city wall) dates from the 13th and 14th centuries and runs 2.5 km with 42 towers and gates. It's one of the rare medieval ramparts in Germany accessible by wall walk for almost their entire length, free of charge. The complete circuit takes about 1h30.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Marktplatz has been Rothenburg's heart since the Middle Ages. The Rathaus (town hall) is a composite building: a 13th-century Gothic wing with a 60-meter belfry, and a Renaissance facade added in 1572. The Ratstrinkstube (former notables' tavern) houses the tourist office—its 1683 clock animates every hour from 11am to 3pm and 8pm to 10pm a scene illustrating the legend of the Meistertrunk: in 1631, during the Thirty Years' War, Catholic general Tilly threatened to raze the Protestant town. In a moment of humor, he promised to spare it if a notable could drain in one gulp a 3.25-liter tankard of wine. Former mayor Nusch accepted the challenge and saved Rothenburg.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Plönlein is Germany's most photographed medieval perspective: a small triangular Y-shaped square where the street divides, framed by the Siebersturm (14th century) on the left and the Kobolzeller Tor on the right, with a yellow and red half-timbered house in the foreground. It's said this composition inspired Pinocchio's village in the Disney cartoon. Arrive before 8am—after 9am tourist groups flood in.

⏱ 30 min
4

The Burggarten (castle garden) occupies the space of a former fortress destroyed by an earthquake in 1356. It juts out as a promontory above the Tauber valley, offering late-day views over the river's meanders, red-tiled roofs, and old town towers—one of Rothenburg's best viewpoints.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Photo walk through the alleys at daybreak (before 8am): Herrngasse, Schmiedgasse, Plönlein. The low morning light is ideal and the alleys are empty. After 9am, tour buses accumulate.

⏱ 90 min

Rothenburg Ob Der Tauber

The medieval town that survived centuries and a mayor's legendary drinking feat

★★★☆☆
1

St. Jakob's Church (St. Jakobskirche), 14th-century Gothic, houses the Altarpiece of the Holy Blood (Heilig-Blut-Altar), a masterpiece by Tilman Riemenschneider carved between 1499 and 1505. The altarpiece depicts the Last Supper in carved linden wood worked to let light filter through—the transparency effect of deeply carved wood is striking. Added to this are the Twelve Apostles altarpiece by Friedrich Herlin (1466) and a set of Gothic stained glass windows in the choir.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Mittelalterliches Kriminalmuseum (Medieval Crime Museum) is housed in the former commandery of the Knights of St. John. Four floors of judicial documents, torture and execution instruments, shame masks, and witchcraft trials covering European criminal law from the 9th to 19th centuries. Over 50,000 pieces—one of the most important collections of its kind in Europe.

⏱ 60 min
3

Climb the Rathaus tower (Rathausturm): at 60 meters height, the panorama over the old town's red roofs, ramparts, and Tauber valley is the most complete accessible in Rothenburg.

⏱ 25 min
4

The Reichsstadtmuseum, housed in the former Dominican convent (founded in 1258), presents in its cellars a perfectly preserved medieval kitchen and Mayor Nusch's silver tankard—material evidence of the Meistertrunk legend.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walk down into the Tauber valley via one of the paths beyond Kobolzeller Tor gate. Below, the river flows between meadows and mill remnants. The upward view of Rothenburg's ramparts and towers from the valley floor differs completely from that of the wall walk.

Dinkelsbuhl

Rothenburg's quieter sister—equally beautiful, mercifully less discovered

★★★★☆
1

Dinkelsbühl is often called Rothenburg's 'little sister'—but rightly less known, therefore less crowded. The town is surrounded by ramparts dating partly from 1370, with 16 towers and 4 gates still standing, accessible along their entire perimeter. Unlike Rothenburg, Dinkelsbühl suffered no damage during World War II—three-quarters of its houses are over 350 years old. Walk the outer rampart path (2.7 km, about 45 min) to see the towers, moats, and gardens bordering the walls.

⏱ 60 min
2

St. George's Minster (Münster St. Georg) is Dinkelsbühl's central monument. Built between 1448 and 1499 in just 51 years, it's one of the most beautiful Gothic hall churches in southern Germany. The three naves, covered by a network vault, are of equal height, creating a rare sense of unified space in Gothic architecture. The Romanesque tower predates the nave: it dates from the 12th century.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Marktplatz and Weinmarkt (wine market), two adjacent squares separated by the Minster, form the historic heart. The Deutsches Haus on Weinmarkt is a patrician half-timbered house in German Renaissance style, with a facade adorned with sculptures, one of the region's finest.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Night watchman's round (Nachtwächter): every evening, a guide in medieval costume, lantern in hand, leads visitors through the illuminated old town to recount Dinkelsbühl's history.

⏱ 75 min

Augsburg

Where Roman emperors left their mark and the Fuggers invented social housing in 1516

★★★★☆
1

Augsburg is Germany's second-oldest city after Trier, founded in 15 BC by Roman legions—it takes its name from Emperor Augustus. Its financial grandeur is visible on Maximilianstrasse, an Italian-inspired Renaissance boulevard flanked by three bronze fountains representing Augustus, Mercury, and Hercules, designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2019. These fountains, erected around 1600, are the first monumental bronze works to have been cast in Augsburg.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Fuggerei is Augsburg's most singular site: founded in 1516 by banker Jakob Fugger the Rich (the world's richest man in his time, creditor to Hapsburg emperors and the Vatican), it's the world's oldest social housing complex in continuous use. The quarter comprises 67 houses and 147 apartments across 8 streets. Tenants still pay an annual rent of 1 euro in exchange for three daily prayers for the Fugger souls. Mozart's great-great-grandfather, Franz Mozart, was one of its most famous residents.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Town Hall (Rathaus), built between 1615 and 1624 by architect Elias Holl, is one of Germany's finest Renaissance buildings. Its interior is dominated by the Goldene Saal (Golden Hall), a 33-meter-long reception hall with gilded coffered ceiling. The adjacent Perlachturm tower (70 meters) dates from the 11th century and offers views over the entire city.

⏱ 45 min
4

Augsburg Cathedral (Dom) dates to the 9th century and contains Germany's oldest figural stained glass windows, dating from the mid-12th century—prophets depicted in colored glass. The cathedral also houses four paintings by Hans Holbein the Elder on the aisle columns.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Visit the Schaezlerpalais, a baroque palace built in 1770, which houses a rococo ballroom with Tiepolo-style frescoes and the Staatsgalerie—a collection of Renaissance and medieval paintings including Dürer's portrait of Jakob Fugger the Rich.

⏱ 75 min

Sonnenbuhl

Where neo-Gothic castles crown the Swabian Jura's green ridges

★★★☆☆
1

Lichtenstein Castle stands on a rocky spur at 817 meters altitude, above the Echaz valley in the Swabian Jura. In 1826, writer Wilhelm Hauff published a historical novel featuring the medieval castle of Lichtenstein as its setting. The novel was a resounding success, and Count Wilhelm von Urach, cousin of the King of Württemberg, was so enthused he purchased the site and had the current neo-Gothic castle built between 1840 and 1842—a few years before Ludwig II of Bavaria did the same at Neuschwanstein. The interior houses a collection of medieval weapons and wall frescoes. From the ramparts, the view over the valley and Swabian Jura plateaus is remarkable.

⏱ 60 min
2

Hohenzollern Castle perches at 855 meters atop Mount Zoller, an isolated outlying hill above the Swabian plain. It's the ancestral seat of the Hohenzollern family, from which Prussia's kings and Germany's emperors descended. The first fortress was erected in the 11th century; destroyed in 1423 after a ten-month siege, rebuilt from 1454 to 1461. The current castle, third version, was commissioned in 1846 by King Frederick William IV of Prussia and designed by architect Friedrich August Stüler in neo-Gothic style. Among the collection's pieces: the Prussian royal crown, a letter from President George Washington thanking Baron von Steuben for his role in the American War of Independence, and personal effects of Frederick the Great. On clear days, views from the ramparts extend to the Black Forest.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Sunset viewpoint from the overlooks below Hohenzollern Castle parking lot. The fortress silhouette cutting against the sunset over the Swabian hills is one of Baden-Württemberg's most photographed panoramas.

Hohenschwangau

The Alpine village where Ludwig II dreamed before building his swan castle above

★★★★☆
1

Hohenschwangau Castle is Ludwig II of Bavaria's childhood castle. His father, King Maximilian II, had the dilapidated building restored from 1832 in neo-Gothic style, having medieval legend scenes depicted on the walls—including episodes of the swan knight Lohengrin, with whom Ludwig strongly identified. One room still displays the piano on which Richard Wagner played before the young king. It was here, facing the site of two former ruined castles on the opposite hill, that Ludwig II made the decision in 1868 to build Neuschwanstein there.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Museum der Bayerischen Könige (Museum of Bavarian Kings), on the shore of the Alpsee, traces the Wittelsbach family's history from the Middle Ages to the 20th century. Collections include portraits, jewelry, and personal objects of Bavarian kings.

⏱ 60 min
3

Walk along the Alpsee, a glacial lake with transparent waters at the foot of both castles. The complete circuit trail is about 3.5 km. In clear weather, the reflections of the Alps and castles in the water are striking.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Late afternoon reconnaissance of the paths leading to Neuschwanstein and the Marienbrücke bridge. The ascent from the Ticket Center takes 30 to 40 minutes on foot. Identifying the route the day before allows you to depart at opening without fumbling.

★★★★★

Neuschwanstein sits on a rocky spur at 970 meters altitude, dominating the valley by 200 meters. Ludwig II had it built from 1869 as a 'temple worthy of the divine friend'—meaning Richard Wagner, whose operas inspired all the interior frescoes: Tannhäuser in the singers' hall, Lohengrin in the bedrooms, Parsifal in the throne room. The castle was never completed. Ludwig II spent only 172 nights of his life there. In June 1886, doctors appointed by the government declared him insane and interned him; three days later, he was found dead in Lake Starnberg. The unfinished rooms were opened to visitors just seven weeks after his death—to repay his debts. Today, 1.4 million people visit the castle annually. Advance booking is absolutely essential.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Marienbrücke bridge towers 92 meters above the bottom of the Pöllat gorge and spans a 45-meter waterfall. Originally built in wood then remade in iron in 1866, it bears the name of Marie of Prussia, Ludwig II's mother. From this bridge comes the world's most iconic view of the castle. In high season, lines form here—prefer early morning.

⏱ 30 min
3

Arrive in Munich mid-afternoon. First discovery walk: Isartor → Viktualienmarkt → Marienplatz. The Viktualienmarkt is Munich's permanent open-air market, founded in 1807, selling Bavarian specialties, charcuterie, cheese, and beer at the central Biergarten. Marienplatz has been the city's heart since 1158: the Marian column (Mariensäule), erected in 1638 to celebrate the end of Swedish occupation, stands at the square's center.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Walk up from the Ticket Center to the castle (30-40 min) for the morning opening slot, before tour buses arrive. Visitor numbers exceed 6,000 people per day in summer—the earliest slots are significantly less crowded.

Munchen

★★★★★
1

The Munich Residence (Residenz) is Germany's largest urban palace—130 rooms, 10 interior courtyards, 40,000 m² of surface area. Seat of the Wittelsbach dynasty from 1508 to 1918, it offers a complete panorama of 16th to 19th century architecture and decorative arts. Its jewel is the Antiquarium, a Renaissance hall 69 meters long built between 1568 and 1571 by Duke Albrecht V to house his collection of ancient sculptures—it's the largest secular Renaissance hall north of the Alps. The barrel vault is pierced by 17 pairs of windows and covered with 102 lunette frescoes depicting Bavarian towns.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Schatzkammer (Treasury), accessible with the same ticket, houses over 1,200 precious objects from Bavarian ruling dynasties: crowns, scepters, reliquaries, jewels, goldsmith's tableware—spanning two millennia of history. The Cuvilliés Theatre, designed between 1751 and 1755 by architect François de Cuvilliés, is one of Europe's finest rococo theaters. Mozart's opera Idomeneo premiered here in 1781.

⏱ 60 min
3

Marienplatz and the Neues Rathaus: the Glockenspiel animates its 32 life-size figures at 11am, noon, and 5pm from March to October. The two scenes depicted—the tournament at William V's wedding in 1568 and the coopers' dance (Schäfflertanz) commemorating the end of a plague epidemic—have been performed since 1908.

⏱ 45 min
4

The Frauenkirche (Cathedral of Our Lady), with its two 99-meter onion-domed towers, is Munich's symbol. Its Gothic construction dates from 1468 to 1488. Inside, look for the mysterious 'devil's footprint' (Teufelstritt) in the entrance floor—a footprint allegedly left by Satan himself, who came to inspect the building.

⏱ 30 min
5

The Englischer Garten (English Garden), created in 1789, is one of the world's largest urban parks—larger than New York's Central Park with its 370 hectares. In the southern section, the Eisbachwelle is a permanent artificial wave where surfers practice in the heart of the city, an emblematic Munich image.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Walking tour through the Innenstadt: Odeonsplatz → Feldherrnhalle → Theatinerkirche (yellow baroque facade, built from 1663 as an ex-voto for the birth of an heir) → Hofgarten → return toward Marienplatz.
