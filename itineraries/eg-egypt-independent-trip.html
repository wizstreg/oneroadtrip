Cairo

Where minarets pierce the skyline and pharaohs sleep in underground galleries

★★★★★
1

Start with Saladin's Citadel, perched on the Mokattam heights 75 meters above the city. Built from 1176 by Saladin to defend Cairo against the Crusaders, it served as Egypt's seat of power for nearly 700 years. Inside, step into the Mosque of Muhammad Ali—nicknamed 'the Alabaster Mosque' for its interior alabaster cladding rising 11 meters high. Its central dome soars to 52 meters and its Ottoman silhouette dominates Cairo's skyline. From the terrace, views sweep across Old Cairo's minarets and, on clear days, to the Giza pyramids on the horizon. Allow 1.5 hours.

⏱ 90 min
2

Head down to the Sultan Hassan Mosque, below the Citadel—a masterpiece of Mamluk architecture. Built between 1356 and 1363 under Sultan An-Nasir Hassan, it measures 150 meters long with its southern minaret reaching 68 meters, medieval Cairo's tallest. The interior impresses with its proportions: the central courtyard opens onto four iwans (vaulted halls), each dedicated to one of the four schools of Sunni jurisprudence. Opposite, the Al-Rifai Mosque (1912) offers striking contrast—same Mamluk style but built 550 years later. It houses the tombs of Iran's last shah and several Egyptian royal family members.

⏱ 60 min
3

Al-Azhar Mosque, founded in 970 by the Fatimids, is Cairo's oldest mosque and home to the world's second-oldest continuously operating university. Its 12,000 square meters blend Fatimid, Mamluk, and Ottoman architectural layers—note the three minarets, built in 1340, 1469, and 1510 respectively, each in a different style. The white marble courtyard dates from the original construction. Visit late morning and you might see students studying Quranic interpretation under the arcades, as they have for over a thousand years.

⏱ 45 min
4

If time permits, walk 15 minutes south of the Citadel to the Ibn Tulun Mosque—the world's third-largest mosque (26,000 m²), built between 876 and 879. It's Cairo's oldest mosque to preserve its original form. Its exterior minaret with spiral staircase, unique in Egypt, recalls Samarra's minaret in Iraq. Climb it for a plunging view of the inner courtyard and Cairo's rooftops. Right next door, the Gayer-Anderson Museum (two 17th-century Ottoman houses connected by a passage) deserves a 30-minute detour.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Plunge into Khan el-Khalili souk, founded in 1382 by Emir Djaharks al-Khalili on the former burial ground of Fatimid caliphs. Over 900 shops crowd into a labyrinth of covered alleys—goldsmiths, spice vendors, hammered copper, perfumes. Walk up Al-Muizz Street, one of Cairo's oldest (10th century), lined with 29 UNESCO-listed Islamic monuments. Don't miss mint tea at El-Fishawy café, open continuously since 1773. The souk hits its stride late afternoon when the heat breaks and the alleys come alive.

⏱ 150 min

Dinner in Old Cairo—Egyptian street food delivers: koshari (lentils, rice, pasta with spicy tomato sauce), ful medames (stewed beans), ta'ameya (Egyptian falafel made from fava beans rather than chickpeas). The hole-in-the-wall joints around Khan el-Khalili serve generous portions. For a more relaxed experience, several restaurants with terraces overlook Al-Hussein Square.

Cairo

Where minarets pierce the skyline and pharaohs sleep in underground galleries

★★★★☆
1

Coptic Cairo is a neighborhood most tours neglect—wrongly. The Fortress of Babylon, built by the Romans in the 1st century, shelters within its walls the Hanging Church (Al-Moallaqa)—one of Egypt's oldest churches, its nave literally suspended above a fortress passageway. Its carved wooden ceilings and Coptic icons date from the 13th century. The Church of Saints Sergius and Bacchus (Abu Serga), below, marks the traditional spot where the Holy Family took refuge during their flight into Egypt. Just next door, the Ben Ezra Synagogue (9th century) is Egypt's oldest synagogue—its genizah (sacred document repository) yielded thousands of priceless medieval manuscripts discovered in the 19th century.

⏱ 120 min
2

The National Museum of Egyptian Civilization (NMEC), opened in 2021 in the Fustat district 10 minutes from Coptic Cairo, perfectly complements the GEM. Where the GEM focuses on Pharaonic Egypt, the NMEC covers the entire story—from early humans to the 20th century, including Coptic, Islamic, and modern periods. The Royal Mummies Hall, in the basement, presents 22 pharaonic mummies (including Ramses II, Seti I, Hatshepsut) under optimal conservation conditions—soft lighting, controlled temperature, an atmosphere of reverence. The experience feels more intimate than at the GEM. Allow 2 hours.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Walk through the Fustat district, Cairo's oldest neighborhood (founded in 641 by Arab conquerors, even before the Fatimid founding of Cairo in 969). Al-Azhar Park, created in 2005 on a former 30-hectare garbage dump, offers panoramic views of the old city and its minarets—one of Cairo's rare green spaces. A good place for a breather in a packed day.

Giza

Four and a half millennia of stone defying gravity at the edge of the desert

★★★★★
1

The Grand Egyptian Museum (GEM), inaugurated November 1, 2025, is the world's largest archaeological museum dedicated to a single civilization—500,000 m² total area, 100,000 objects displayed across 12 permanent galleries covering seven millennia of history. It sits 2 km from the pyramids on the Giza Plateau. From the entrance, the colossal statue of Ramses II (11 meters, 83 tons, 3,200 years old) greets you in the atrium. The Grand Staircase, spanning six levels and 6,000 m², is lined with sixty monumental statues with direct views of the pyramids through the glass façade. Tutankhamun's complete collection—5,398 pieces, many displayed for the first time—occupies 7,500 m² alone. Budget at minimum 4 hours, ideally 6 hours for a complete visit. Tickets are no longer sold on-site—book online.

⏱ 300 min
2

After the museum, head to the Giza Plateau 2 km away—a pedestrian walkway now connects the two sites. The Pyramid of Khufu, sole survivor of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World, has stood for over 4,500 years. The numbers stagger: 230 meters per side, 146 meters tall originally (138 today), 2.3 million limestone blocks averaging 2.5 tons each, total weight 5 million tons. It was the world's tallest building for 3,800 years. If you enter (extra fee), expect a narrow, steep corridor leading to the King's Chamber—10.5 m by 5.2 m in solid granite. It's physical and claustrophobic, but the experience is worth it. Arrive at opening (8am) to dodge crowds and heat.

⏱ 120 min
3

The Sphinx, carved directly from a rock outcrop on the plateau, measures 73.5 meters long and 20 meters high—antiquity's largest sculpted monolith. Its face, probably that of Pharaoh Khafre (4th Dynasty, around 2500 BC), gazes due east toward sunrise. Don't miss the Panoramic Point (Nine Pyramids viewpoint) on the road circling the plateau to the south—from here you'll have all three great pyramids and the three smaller queens' pyramids aligned in the same field of vision.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Sunset over the pyramids from the Panoramic Point or from one of the terrace restaurants on the Fayoum Road that skirts the plateau. The golden light on the pyramids late in the day offers the best photography moment—tour buses have left and the heat is bearable. Some arrange camel or horseback rides around the plateau in late afternoon.

⏱ 90 min

Cairo

Where minarets pierce the skyline and pharaohs sleep in underground galleries

★★★★☆
1

Early departure to Saqqara, 30 km south of Cairo—the necropolis of ancient capital Memphis. The Step Pyramid of Djoser, built around 2660 BC by vizier Imhotep, is Egypt's oldest pyramid and humanity's first monumental stone building. Six stacked mastabas of decreasing size form its 62-meter height. The entrance through the colonnade—40 pillars sculpted like papyrus—strikes with its modernity. Beneath the pyramid, a 5 km network of underground corridors descends 28 meters deep to the burial chamber. You can visit part of the underground passages for an extra fee. The site is far less crowded than Giza—take advantage.

⏱ 120 min
2

Continue to Dahshur, 15 km south of Saqqara—an even quieter site. Here you'll see the evolution of construction techniques: the Bent Pyramid (105 meters, around 2600 BC), recognizable by its mid-height slope change—Sneferu's architects had to reduce the angle during construction to prevent collapse. Right beside it, the Red Pyramid (104 meters), history's first successful smooth-sided pyramid. It served as the direct model for Khufu's Great Pyramid. You can enter the Red Pyramid via a 63-meter corridor—the ammonia smell is strong, but the three interior chambers in red stone are worth the effort.

⏱ 90 min
3

Detour to the Imhotep Museum at Saqqara before leaving—small but remarkable. The blue faience tiles recovered from inside Djoser's pyramid, the mummy of Merenre I, and Imhotep's wooden coffin are displayed here. It's a good complement to understanding the necropolis.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Stop at Memphis, the ancient capital of the Old Kingdom, 3 km north of Saqqara. The Mit Rahinah open-air museum displays the reclining colossus of Ramses II (10 meters long, in limestone) and an 80-ton alabaster sphinx from the New Kingdom. Memphis was founded around 3100 BC by Menes (Narmer), the pharaoh who unified Upper and Lower Egypt—it remained the capital for over a thousand years. Today, almost nothing remains of the city itself, swallowed by groundwater and modern construction. The museum takes 30 to 45 minutes to visit.

⏱ 45 min

Faiyum

Where the desert surrenders whale bones from when Egypt was an ocean floor

★★★★☆
1

Drive to Fayoum (100 km southwest of Cairo, 2 hours), then continue to Wadi al-Hitan—Whale Valley, UNESCO-listed since 2005. This open-air museum in the middle of the desert harbors fossil skeletons of primitive whales dating back 40 million years—when this area was covered by the Tethys Sea. The site is the world's most important for documenting whale evolution from terrestrial to marine mammals. You'll see near-complete skeletons of Basilosaurus (up to 21 meters long) and Dorudon, some with hind legs still visible—proof these animals once walked on land. A small modern museum traces the history of discoveries. The site can only be explored on foot or by camel along marked trails.

⏱ 150 min
2

On the return journey, stop at the Wadi el-Rayan waterfalls—two artificial lakes connected by Egypt's only waterfall. Lake Qarun, Egypt's largest saltwater lake, is prime migratory bird-watching territory (flamingos, herons, egrets) in winter. The contrast between the surrounding desert and these bodies of water is striking.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Wake at 4:30am for a hot air balloon flight over the West Bank—one of Egypt's most spectacular experiences. The balloon launches at sunrise and soars over the Valley of the Kings, Hatshepsut's temple, and sugarcane fields along the Nile for 45 minutes to an hour. Book the day before—flights are canceled in strong winds.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Valley of the Kings holds 63 tombs carved into rock between 1539 and 1075 BC—New Kingdom pharaohs had abandoned pyramids in favor of underground burials to escape looters. Your ticket includes 3 tombs of your choice. Most impressive for their paintings: Ramses IV (KV2), Ramses IX (KV6), Merenptah (KV8). Seti I's tomb (KV17), considered the valley's most beautiful, requires a substantial supplement. Tutankhamun's tomb (KV62), discovered in 1922 by Howard Carter, is small with less spectacular paintings than others—the 5,398 treasure objects are now at the GEM in Giza. Arrive before 7am—there's no shade in the valley and heat quickly becomes unbearable.

⏱ 150 min
3

The Colossi of Memnon, two seated statues of Amenhotep III standing 18 meters each, guard the entrance to what was Luxor's largest mortuary temple—now vanished. Carved from 720-ton blocks of quartzite each, transported from Gebel el-Ahmar quarries near Cairo 675 km away. In antiquity, the northern colossus emitted a sound at sunrise (probably due to stone dilation from a crack caused by an earthquake in 27 BC)—Greeks and Romans came from afar to hear it. The stop is quick—15 to 20 minutes suffices.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Free afternoon to recover from the 4:30am wake-up. If you still have energy, detour to Deir el-Medina, the village of artisans who built and decorated Valley of the Kings tombs for 500 years. The workers' small tombs—notably those of Sennedjem and Inherkhau—are paradoxically better painted than some royal tombs, with colors of astonishing freshness. The site is little-visited and offers a glimpse into the daily lives of royal tomb builders.

Luxor

The world's greatest open-air museum spread across two banks of the Nile

★★★★☆
1

Hatshepsut's mortuary temple at Deir el-Bahari backs against a 300-meter cliff that serves as its natural backdrop—the architectural spectacle is unique in Egypt. Three monumental terraces connected by ramps rise against the rock face. The first measures 120 meters deep by 75 wide. The temple, built in 15 years by architect Senenmut, tells on its walls the story of the trading expedition to the Land of Punt (present-day Somalia/Eritrea)—you'll see incense trees transported by boat—and the queen's divine birth. Hatshepsut, the only woman to reign as pharaoh for 22 years, saw most of her representations hammered out by her successor Thutmose III after her death in 1458 BC. Arrive early—the temple stands in full sun from 9am.

⏱ 90 min
2

Continue to Medinet Habu—Ramses III's mortuary temple, less frequented than Karnak but whose painted reliefs are the best preserved on the entire West Bank. Battle scenes against the Sea Peoples (around 1178 BC) still retain their original colors—blue, red, yellow. The second portico's ceiling preserves traces of vivid pigments after 3,000 years. The outer enclosure, inspired by a Syrian fortress (the Migdol), is unique in Egyptian temple architecture. If you see only one 'off the beaten path' temple in Luxor, make it this one.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Ramesseum, Ramses II's mortuary temple, deserves a stop for its monumental ruins—notably a shattered colossus of Ramses II that weighed over 1,000 tons and measured about 18 meters standing. This colossus inspired Shelley's 1818 poem Ozymandias. The hypostyle hall still preserves part of its ceiling with astronomical paintings. The site is less restored than Karnak, giving it the charm of romantic ruins.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Late morning, visit the Valley of the Queens and Nefertari's tomb (QV66)—considered all of Egypt's most beautiful tomb for the vibrancy of its wall paintings. Every inch of wall is painted with exceptional finesse—Nefertari guided by the gods, scenes from the Book of the Dead, star-studded ceiling in deep blue. The supplement is steep and visitor numbers limited, but it's a moment apart.

Luxor

The world's greatest open-air museum spread across two banks of the Nile

★★★★☆
1

Excursion to Dendera (75 km north of Luxor, 1.5 hours' drive). The Temple of Hathor, founded in 54 BC and completed under the Romans, is one of Egypt's best-preserved temples—and the only one where you'll see original colors on the ceilings. The hypostyle hall of 24 columns with Hathoric capitals (the goddess's face on a sistrum) supports an astronomical ceiling restored in the 2010s—over 1,000 m² of bas-reliefs have recovered their superb blue tones obtained from a copper derivative. On the ceiling of a rooftop chapel was the famous Dendera Zodiac, a circular star map dating from 50 BC—the original has been in the Louvre since 1822, a copy stands in place. Climb to the temple roof via the staircase—the view of the surrounding countryside and the distant Nile is magnificent. The underground crypts, partially accessible, contain reliefs of exceptional fineness.

⏱ 120 min
2

Continue to Abydos (100 km north of Dendera, 1.5 hours). The Temple of Seti I is a cenotaph sanctuary dedicated to Osiris, god of regeneration, built by Seti I (19th Dynasty) and completed by his son Ramses II. Its bas-reliefs in slight relief are unanimously considered Egypt's finest—precision, line elegance, colors still visible after 3,300 years. The temple has a unique L-shaped plan with seven chapels dedicated to seven deities. The Gallery of Kings, in a side corridor, displays cartouches of 76 pharaohs from Narmer to Seti I—a major historical source. Three names are deliberately absent: Akhenaten (the heretic pharaoh), Tutankhamun, and Hatshepsut. Behind the temple, the Osireion—a mysterious underground cenotaph—remains partially accessible.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Return to Luxor late afternoon (3 hours total drive). It's a long road day but Dendera and Abydos are two of Egypt's most exceptional temples—and the least visited. Most standard tours skip them, making them exclusive to this grand tour.

Edfu

A falcon god's sanctuary where every column and ceiling survived intact

★★★★☆
1

Wake on the Nile—the boat sailed overnight from Luxor and passes through the Esna Lock early morning (sometimes with a 1-2 hour wait depending on river traffic). Arrive in Edfu mid-morning. The Temple of Edfu, dedicated to falcon god Horus, is the best-preserved Ptolemaic temple in Egypt—and the second-largest after Karnak. Built between 237 and 57 BC, it took 180 years to complete. Its entrance pylon rises 36 meters high with reliefs showing Ptolemy XII smiting his enemies before Horus. Inside, the hypostyle hall with 18 columns stands intact, with its ceiling still in place—extremely rare in Egypt. The gray granite naos in the sanctuary, which housed Horus's statue, remains in position. Allow 1 to 1.5 hours.

Activities

1

Morning stop at Kom Ombo—the temple perches spectacularly on a promontory overlooking the Nile, meters from the boat landing. The small adjacent crocodile museum displays Nile crocodile mummies recovered from the surroundings. Allow 45 minutes to 1 hour.

Aswan

Where granite islands float like mirages and Nubian voices carry over water

★★★★☆
1

The Temple of Philae, dedicated to goddess Isis, stands on Agilkia Island, accessible only by water taxi (10-15 minutes from Shellal). Built between 380 BC and the 3rd century AD, it was completely dismantled then reassembled on this island between 1972 and 1980 to escape drowning by Lake Nasser—a UNESCO international campaign. Isis worship persisted here until 550 AD, long after the fall of the Roman Empire. Trajan's Kiosk, with its 14 columns each with different capitals, is Aswan's most photographed structure. Allow 2 hours with the crossing.

⏱ 150 min
2

The Unfinished Obelisk, in the pink granite quarries, would have measured 42 meters and weighed 1,200 tons—the largest ever attempted. A crack doomed it around 1450 BC. The Aswan High Dam (3,600 m long, 111 m high), built with Soviet assistance between 1960 and 1970, created Lake Nasser (500 km long). Both visit quickly (30 min each).

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Afternoon by felucca: cross to Elephantine Island (Nubian village with painted houses, ancient nilometer) then Kitchener Island (19th-century botanical garden). Return via the Corniche at sunset—Aswan's most beautiful moment of the day.

Aswan

Where granite islands float like mirages and Nubian voices carry over water

★★★★★
1

Depart 3am for Abu Simbel—280 km of desert road in convoy (3 to 3.5 hours). Ramses II's Great Temple: façade 33 meters high and 38 wide, four seated colossi of 20 meters each. Inside, a hypostyle hall of eight Osiride pillars leads to the sanctuary 63 meters into the rock. Twice a year (February 22 and October 22), sunrays penetrate all the way back to illuminate three of the four statues—a 3,200-year-old astronomical alignment. The Small Temple of Nefertari, just beside, is dedicated to the queen and goddess Hathor—an unprecedented honor. In 1964-1968, both temples were cut into 1,042 blocks and reassembled 65 meters higher by UNESCO.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

Return to Aswan around 2pm. Well-deserved rest afternoon after the 3am wake-up. Last evening in Aswan—dinner at a Nubian restaurant on Elephantine Island or karkadé tea on the Corniche.
