Kerala Backwaters, Tea Hills & Coast Circuit

📅 12 days📍 10 stops🚗 589 km⏱ 15h

From cosmopolitan Kochi to spiritual Varkala, this 10-day circuit traverses the best of Kerala: the colonial lanes of Fort Kochi, the tea plantations of Munnar at 1,600 meters, the backwater canals of Alleppey aboard a houseboat, the millennial port of Kollam, and the Arabian Sea cliffs of Varkala. A journey between water, mountain, and sand that follows the slow, soothing rhythm of South India.

✨ Highlights
  • Kolukkumalai: the world's highest tea plantation at 2,400 m, 1930s factory still in operation
  • Alleppey backwaters: kettuvallam cruise on Vembanad Lake, India's longest lake (96 km)
  • Paradesi Synagogue from 1568: hand-painted Chinese porcelain floor, Belgian chandeliers, and 1,600-year-old copper plates
  • Kollam: one of the world's four great trading ports in the 13th century alongside Alexandria, Quanzhou, and Malacca
📆 Best period

October, November, December, January, February, March

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 7 and 11 days.

Summer (April-June): Hot weather, challenging conditions, hill stations popular, lowest prices, fewer tourists, air conditioning essential.

Monsoon (June-September): Heavy rainfall, lush landscapes, romantic atmosphere, lowest prices, challenging travel, cultural experiences, spiritual season.

Regional Variations: Diverse climates, altitude differences, seasonal patterns, weather monitoring, regional planning, flexible scheduling essential.

🌍 What to discover in IN?

Incredible diversity with ancient spirituality, vibrant colors, and rich cultural heritage. Experience Taj Mahal, spiritual traditions, and authentic Indian culture. The country offers cultural immersion with historical significance and culinary excellence. India provides life-changing cultural experiences with incredible diversity and spiritual depth.

📋 Important notes

The southwest monsoon (June to September) renders the backwaters impassable and mountain roads to Munnar treacherous. Houseboats must dock before sunset. Book houseboats well in advance during peak season (December-January). Paradesi Synagogue is closed on Fridays, Saturdays, and Jewish holidays.

Day 1

Kochi

Where centuries of spice trade left layers of Portuguese, Dutch, and British heritage

★★★★★
1

You land at Cochin International Airport in Nedumbassery, roughly 40 km from Fort Kochi. Plan on a solid hour's drive to reach the historic quarter—the route cuts through paddy fields and coconut-lined canals that set the tone for the entire journey. Check into your accommodation and take time to acclimatize to Kerala's humid warmth.

⏱ 90 min
2

Late afternoon, head to Fort Kochi Beach promenade to witness the Chinese fishing nets—massive cantilevered structures of teak and bamboo mounted on granite counterweights, in use since the 14th century and introduced, according to tradition, by merchants from Kublai Khan's court. The spectacle peaks at sunset, when fishermen work the nets by sheer muscle power. If hunger strikes, vendors right beside the nets will grill the fresh fish you pick from their catch.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Wander through Fort Kochi's lanes as dusk falls. Portuguese, Dutch, and British colonial facades succeed one another within a few hundred meters—an architectural palimpsest found nowhere else in India. You'll stumble upon contemporary art galleries installed in former spice warehouses, cafés tucked into Dutch houses, and street art murals. The atmosphere is serene, a world apart from the chaos of India's megacities.

⏱ 75 min
Day 2

Kochi

Where centuries of spice trade left layers of Portuguese, Dutch, and British heritage

★★★★★
1

Start at St. Francis Church, built of timber in 1503 by Portuguese Franciscan friars—it's the oldest European church in India. The current stone building dates to 1516. Vasco da Gama was buried here in 1524 during his third voyage to India; his remains weren't repatriated to Lisbon until 14 years later, but you'll still see his original tombstone inside. The church passed from Portuguese hands to the Dutch in 1663, then to the British in 1795—each occupant left its mark. Free entry, open 9am–5pm except during Sunday services.

⏱ 45 min
2

A few minutes' walk brings you to Santa Cruz Cathedral Basilica, originally erected by the Portuguese in 1505 and rebuilt in its present neo-Gothic form in 1902. The interior will surprise you—pastel ceiling frescoes and a richly carved altar offer a burst of color that contrasts sharply with the whitewashed exterior. Stained-glass windows filter soft light into the nave, lending it a contemplative atmosphere even at midday.

⏱ 40 min
3

Catch an auto-rickshaw or ferry to Mattancherry to visit Paradesi Synagogue, built in 1568 by Sephardic Jews who fled Spain and Portugal. The floor is paved with hundreds of hand-painted porcelain tiles imported from Canton in the 18th century—each one unique. 19th-century Belgian crystal chandeliers hang above a brass pulpit. The synagogue also preserves copper plates engraved in ancient Malayalam 1,600 years ago, detailing privileges granted to the Jewish community by the Cochin raja. Note: closed on Fridays, Saturdays, and Jewish holidays. Enter barefoot.

⏱ 60 min
4

Exiting the synagogue, lose yourself in the alleys of Jew Town and Mattancherry spice market. The air is thick with cardamom, pepper, turmeric, and cloves—the same spices that drew Phoenicians, Romans, Arabs, and Europeans to this coast for millennia. Antique shops overflow with colonial furniture, vintage porcelain, and old coins. Bargain, but keep in mind that prices here are already more reasonable than Delhi or Mumbai.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

In the evening, attend a Kathakali dance performance. This four-century-old theatrical form from Kerala blends dance, mime, and music. Artists spend over an hour applying makeup—green faces for heroes, red for villains—using natural pigments layered on thickly. Arrive 30 minutes before the show to watch the makeup application, often open to the public. Count on around 1.5 hours for the full experience.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 130 km · 4h
Day 3

Munnar

Where mist clings to emerald tea slopes and the world's highest plantation touches the clouds

★★★★☆
1

The drive from Kochi to Munnar (130 km, 4 hours) is a spectacle in itself—don't treat it as mere transit. You climb gradually from the coastal plain into the Western Ghats, passing cardamom, pepper, and rubber plantations. Stop at Cheeyappara Falls, a seven-tiered cascade visible from the road. The higher you climb, the cooler the temperature and the thicker the mist rolling between the hills.

⏱ 240 min
2

Arriving in Munnar, perched around 1,600 meters above sea level, you'll be struck by the sweep of tea estates—rows of emerald-green bushes conforming to every hillside curve as far as the eye can see. Take an initial stroll through the tea gardens near the town center. The air is crisp and fragrant—a bracing contrast to the humid heat of Kochi just hours earlier.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Settle into your lodging and treat yourself to a tea tasting. Munnar produces some of India's finest teas—Broken Orange Pekoe and Flowery Orange Pekoe varieties are particularly prized. If your accommodation sits amid the plantations, the sunset view from the terrace, with mist rising between the tea rows, is a moment of pure tranquility.

⏱ 60 min
Day 4

Munnar

Where mist clings to emerald tea slopes and the world's highest plantation touches the clouds

★★★★☆
1

Rise before dawn to reach Kolukkumalai Tea Estate, the world's highest tea plantation at 2,400 meters. Access is by 4x4 jeep only—17 km of bone-rattling dirt track from Suryanelli. The ride is rough but the views are breathtaking. The factory, dating from the 1930s, still produces tea by the orthodox colonial method, no modern machinery. You'll watch pickers harvest 60 kg of leaves per day by hand. Sunrise over the Tamil Nadu plains from the summit is one of South India's finest.

⏱ 180 min
2

Back in Munnar, visit the Tea Museum in the Nallathanni estate. It traces the region's tea-growing history, launched by the British in the late 19th century, with period machinery, colonial photographs, and craft artifacts. Open 9am–5pm, closed Mondays. Allow 45 minutes.

⏱ 45 min
3

If time permits, push on to Top Station, Munnar's highest point (around 1,880 m), 32 km from town. The view plunges into the Theni valley on the Tamil Nadu side and across the Western Ghats. On clear days you can glimpse the Nilgiris in the distance. Morning is best—by afternoon, mist swallows everything.

⏱ 75 min
4

For wildlife enthusiasts, Eravikulam National Park shelters the Nilgiri tahr, an endangered endemic mountain goat with only around 2,500 individuals remaining. The park is accessible on foot from the Rajamalai entrance. Tahrs are habituated to visitors and allow close approach—a rare sight. The park closes during the calving season (January–March). Check opening times before heading out.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Late in the day, savor a quiet moment facing the mist-shrouded hills. If you've got energy left, a short hike to Pothamedu viewpoint offers a panorama over tea, coffee, and cardamom plantations—especially beautiful in the golden evening light.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 165 km · 4h30
Day 5

Alleppey

Floating through canals where rice grows below sea level and kingfishers guard the banks

★★★★☆
1

The descent from Munnar to Alleppey (165 km, 4.5 hours) is as spectacular as the ascent: you leave the tea estates, crossing spice forests, waterfalls, and Tamil villages before returning to the coastal plain. The lower you drop, the more heat and humidity return—brace yourself for the shift. Stop en route for lunch at a "meals" restaurant (local canteen): rice, sambar, rasam, and vegetables served on a banana leaf.

⏱ 270 min
2

Arriving in Alleppey, board a kettuvallam—these traditional houseboats, 20 to 25 meters long, built from jackwood planks assembled without a single nail, bound with coir rope (coconut fiber), and waterproofed with fish oil. Once used to transport rice from the flooded paddies of Kuttanad, they've been converted into floating accommodations with bedroom, bathroom, and galley. You glide through a labyrinth of coconut-fringed canals, passing fishermen casting nets and children waving from the banks.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Dine aboard the boat—the cook prepares Keralan dishes with ingredients bought that very morning from riverside villages. Karimeen pollichathu (pearl spot grilled in a banana leaf) is the local specialty not to miss. Watch the sun set from the upper deck, with Vembanad Lake—India's longest lake at 96 km—turning shades of orange and rose. Boats must moor before nightfall so as not to interfere with fishing.

⏱ 120 min
Day 6

Alleppey

Floating through canals where rice grows below sea level and kingfishers guard the banks

★★★★☆
1

Wake on the water—breakfast served on the boat deck, facing the silent morning canals, is one of those moments that alone justify the journey. The morning cruise passes through Kuttanad, nicknamed the "rice bowl of Kerala": it's one of the few places on earth where rice is grown 1.5 to 2 meters below sea level. The FAO has designated this farming system a Globally Important Agricultural Heritage System.

⏱ 120 min
2

After disembarking, explore the village on foot to observe daily life along the canals: coir rope making, fish drying, women in saris washing clothes on the ghats. Locals are welcoming and curious—a smile and a "namaskaram" open every door.

⏱ 60 min
3

For deeper immersion, board a wooden canoe—these flat-bottomed craft slip into narrow channels where houseboats can't go. You'll glide beneath tunnels of tropical vegetation, threading between water lilies and kingfishers. Count on 1 to 1.5 hours for this silent excursion.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Spend the afternoon birdwatching along the Vembanad banks. Herons, cormorants, egrets, kingfishers—the diversity is remarkable. If you're into photography, the late-afternoon light on the canals offers perfect conditions. Finish the day with a stroll on Alleppey Beach, a long ribbon of golden sand facing the Arabian Sea, often deserted by tourists who stick to the backwaters.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 70 km · 2h
Day 7

Kollam

An ancient harbor that welcomed Marco Polo's ships and still whispers tales of silk and spice

★★★★☆
1

Leave Alleppey southward for Kollam (70 km, 2 hours), one of the world's oldest trading ports. Marco Polo described it in the 13th century under the name "Coilum," and Ibn Battuta in the 14th century counted it among the five greatest ports he'd seen in 24 years of travel. Phoenicians, Romans, Chinese, and Arabs traded spices, silk, and porcelain here for millennia. Today, the town is the global cashew capital—you'll catch the roasting aroma in certain neighborhoods.

⏱ 120 min
2

Visit Ashtamudi Lake, Kerala's second-largest body of water, a Ramsar-designated wetland. Its name means "eight arms" in Malayalam, referring to the eight channels branching through the mangroves. It's the southern gateway to the backwaters, far quieter than Alleppey. If you haven't had your fill of canals, a short cruise on Ashtamudi will offer wilder scenery, with coconut palms leaning over the water and sleepy fishing villages.

⏱ 90 min
3

Detour to Munroe Island (Munroethuruthu), an archipelago of eight small islands at the confluence of Ashtamudi and the Kallada River. You reach it by boat. Life here has remained virtually unchanged—copra drying, toddy (palm wine) production, shrimp fishing in the canals. Allow 2 to 3 hours for a boat tour with a local guide.

⏱ 150 min

Activities

Late in the day, climb Thangassery Lighthouse, a relic of Portuguese and then Dutch presence on this coast. From the top, the view sweeps across the Arabian Sea and the red roofs of the old colonial quarter. Thangassery was the first European trading post established in Kollam in 1502. Catch the sunset from the adjacent beach.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 40 km · 1h
Day 8

Varkala

Where red cliffs cradle a sacred beach and sunset over the Arabian Sea feels like benediction

★★★☆☆
1

The drive from Kollam to Varkala is short (40 km, 1 hour)—you'll arrive mid-morning. Varkala is the antithesis of Kerala's flat beaches: here, reddish laterite cliffs 15 to 20 meters high plunge straight into the Arabian Sea. The path along the clifftop, lined with cafés, boutiques, and bougainvillea, is one of South India's loveliest coastal walks.

⏱ 60 min
2

Descend the steps to Papanasam Beach, the main beach at the cliff's foot. Its name means "destruction of sins" in Sanskrit—Hindus believe bathing here washes away sins. The beach is monitored by lifeguards, but watch for currents—they can be strong, especially during the monsoon. Early morning, you'll see fishermen hauling nets onto the sand.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Settle on the clifftop for sunset—it's Varkala's daily ritual. Terrace cafés fill up as the sun descends toward the horizon. The vibe is laid-back, almost hippie—far from the mass tourism of Goa. If you fancy an Ayurvedic massage, reputable centers along the cliff offer traditional treatments. Stick with those affiliated to the Kerala government for quality.

⏱ 90 min
Day 9

Varkala

Where red cliffs cradle a sacred beach and sunset over the Arabian Sea feels like benediction

★★★☆☆
1

Dedicate the morning to a surf lesson on Papanasam or Black Beach. Local operators offer beginner sessions of 1.5 to 2 hours, equipment included. Waves are moderate and suited to beginners—it's one of Kerala's best learning spots. Book early morning when waves and heat are gentler.

⏱ 120 min
2

Visit Janardhana Swami Temple, a shrine dedicated to Vishnu over 2,000 years old, perched on the cliff above Papanasam Beach. The temple is an important pilgrimage site—Keralans perform funerary rites by the sea here. The interior is reserved for Hindus, but the exterior architecture, sculptures, and atmosphere are worth the visit. A great sacred banyan shades the entrance.

⏱ 45 min
3

In the afternoon, push north to Kappil Beach, 6 km from Varkala. This beach is bordered by a lagoon—the mouth of the Edava River meets the sea in a landscape of sand, freshwater, and coconut palms. Much quieter than Papanasam, it's ideal for a calm swim or a picnic.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Late in the day, join a Keralan cooking class. Operators in Varkala offer 2- to 3-hour workshops where you'll learn to prepare a tamarind fish curry, appam (fermented rice pancake), and payasam (coconut milk dessert). You cook and eat your own meal—a delicious way to close your penultimate evening in Kerala.

⏱ 150 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 184 km · 3h30
Day 10

Kochi

Where centuries of spice trade left layers of Portuguese, Dutch, and British heritage

★★★★★
1

The drive from Varkala to Kochi (184 km, 3.5 hours) takes you back north. If you leave early, you'll have time to visit Mattancherry Palace (also called the Dutch Palace), built by the Portuguese in 1555 as a gift to the Cochin raja, then renovated by the Dutch in 1663—hence its nickname. Don't miss the 17th-century murals illustrating scenes from the Ramayana and Mahabharata—they rank among Kerala's finest wall paintings. The palace also houses royal portraits, palanquins, and ceremonial garments.

⏱ 60 min
2

If your flight is in the evening, use the remaining time to explore Jew Town's antique shops—vintage ceramics, textiles, and brass objects. Or enjoy one last Keralan meal at a Fort Kochi restaurant: a full thali with rice boat (rice shaped like a boat), fried fish, mango pickle, and payasam for dessert. Cochin International Airport is about 1 hour 15 minutes from Fort Kochi—allow a comfortable margin.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Before leaving Kerala, if time allows, swing by Broadway Market in Ernakulam (the mainland part of Kochi). This covered market, far less touristy than Mattancherry, is where locals buy their spices, fabrics, and kitchen utensils. You'll find black pepper, green cardamom, and turmeric powder at unbeatable prices—perfect for useful souvenirs.

⏱ 60 min
