Hamburg

★★★★★
1

The smell of mud and roasted coffee rises from the canals of the Speicherstadt in the early morning. This district of red-brick warehouses was built between 1883 and 1927 on wooden pilings and designated UNESCO in 2015 alongside the Kontorhausviertel. Its 26 hectares and its fleetes (internal canals) make it one of the largest historical warehouse complexes in Northern Europe. Arrive before 9 AM—tour groups start pouring in from 10 AM.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Speicherstadt vanishes underfoot—and there rises the Elbphilharmonie, perched atop the former Kaispeicher A warehouse from 1963 like a glass iceberg on brick. Inaugurated in 2017 following designs by Herzog & de Meuron, it soars to 110 m. The Grand Hall is considered one of the world's best-designed concert halls acoustically, with its stucco walls cut like lace. The Plaza on the 8th floor is free and open daily.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Elbphilharmonie still divides Hamburgers—a massive budget overrun, ten years late, and yet no one regrets it anymore. The Reeperbahn a few streets away is the flip side of the city: this is where the Beatles played their first real gigs in 1960-1962 in the clubs on Große Freiheit. At night, the street becomes one of Germany's liveliest—worth seeing even if music isn't your priority.

⏱ 60 min
4

The Kunsthalle Hamburg, founded in 1869, is one of Northern Germany's largest art museums. Seven centuries of painting coexist here, from Gothic art to contemporary works—with a dense collection of Caspar David Friedrich that echoes Greifswald later on the route. The Neo-Renaissance building adjoins a separate modern gallery. Closed Monday; allow at least 1.5 hours.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

If you climb to the Elbphilharmonie Plaza on a windless day, the panorama over the Elbe, the Speicherstadt, and the harbor cranes captures in a single image what makes Hamburg distinctive. Access is free, but concert tickets sell out months in advance—book now if you want to hear something.

⏱ 45 min

Walk or bike along the Elbe from HafenCity to the Fischmarkt: on Sunday mornings from 5 AM, this century-old fish market spills herring, fruit, and vendors' shouts onto the quay for three hours. It's kitschy and sincere—a way to see Hamburg outside the polished circuits.

⏱ 90 min

An Alster ferry crossing (Alsterdampfer) from Jungfernstieg takes 1 hour and loops the two inner lakes that carve through the city. Seen from the water, Hamburg changes completely: church steeples, ambassadorial villas, and gardens replace the industrial docks. Boats run year-round, more frequently April to October.

⏱ 60 min

Lubeck

★★★★☆
1

Built between 1464 and 1478 under the direction of Hinrich Helmstede, the Holstentor leans slightly on its marshy foundation—and it's precisely this flaw that makes it human. Its two round red-brick towers appear on the German two-euro coin. It's one of only two of Lübeck's four medieval gates still standing. The interior museum traces Hanseatic commercial power: allow one hour, arrive early.

⏱ 75 min
2

Lübeck's seven spires hover permanently over the city, but it's the Marienkirche that induces vertigo. Its two towers reach 125 m—it was for a long time the city's tallest structure. Its melted bells, which fell during the 1942 bombing, remain in place on the floor as testimony. A lesser-known detail: Dietrich Buxtehude served as organist here from 1668 to 1707, and the young Bach traveled from Arnstadt to hear him play.

⏱ 60 min
3

Lübeckers saved their Altstadt from a wave of demolitions in the 1970s thanks to citizen mobilization—the city was designated UNESCO in 1987, the first in the world in Northern Europe. The ensemble comprises 81 hectares with medieval streets, gabled houses, and the six Salzspeicher (salt barns, built 1579-1745) facing the Holstentor. The marzipan manufactured here since the 15th century is sold by the piece in Niederegger boutiques.

⏱ 60 min
4

The European Hansemuseum, opened in 2015 in the former convent buildings of the Burgkloster, traces a thousand years of Hanseatic trade across three floors and 5,000 m². It's the largest museum dedicated to the Hansa in Europe. The scenography avoids archive mode—reconstructions of merchant offices in Bruges, Novgorod, and Bergen give flesh to what this network was.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Stroll through Fischergrube, the old sailors' street descending toward the Trave. Halfway down, the Buddenbrookhaus recreates the universe of Thomas Mann's novel published in 1901—his family's original house truly stood here. Entry isn't free, but the museum is brief and well done.

⏱ 60 min

The scent of marzipan invades Breite Straße from morning on. The Niederegger confectionery, installed opposite the Rathaus since 1806, sells its almond paste in two hundred forms—in slices, figurines, cakes. The upstairs café offers a view of the pedestrian street without having to order anything expensive.

⏱ 30 min

Crossing the Trave by rowboat from the Holstenbrücke quay takes 20 min and provides a view of the Salzspeicher and Holstentor from the river—an angle street photographers don't capture. Boatmen also offer longer circuits to the outer harbor. Season April-October, departures as demand arises.

⏱ 30 min

Travemunde

★★★★☆
1

Not touristy in the facade-seaside sense, but authentically Baltic: Travemünde still lives with its ferries to Helsinki and Stockholm, its fishermen, and its 19th-century resort villas. The waterfront combines an active port, a pedestrian promenade, and the museum sailing ship Passat (1911) moored at the quay, accessible for visits.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Leuchtturm Travemünde, commissioned in 1827 and deactivated in 1972 when a hotel obscured it, is today a maritime museum 31 m tall. Its 142 steps lead to an observation gallery with plunging views over the Baltic and the ferries maneuvering in the channel. It's the second-oldest lighthouse on the German Baltic coast after the one at Kap Arkona.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Rent a Strandkorb—the woven wicker beach chair, symbol of the Baltic—and watch the ferries on the Helsinki-Travemünde line pass a few hundred meters from shore. These orange-hulled Finnlines vessels are impossible to miss. The white sand beach is pleasant even off-season; parking near Strandbahnhof remains affordable.

⏱ 60 min

Wismar

★★★★☆
1

The Wasserkunst stands in the center of Wismar's Marktplatz like a Dutch pavilion dropped there by mistake. This Renaissance bronze pavilion was built between 1579 and 1602 by Philipp Brandin and supplied the city with drinking water until 1897. Wismarians make it their emblem. The Marktplatz is one of northern Germany's largest, bordered by the Alter Schwede dating from around 1380.

⏱ 45 min
2

St.-Nikolai-Kirche, consecrated in 1459, impresses with its central nave 37 m high. Built starting in 1381 to honor sailors and fishermen, it survived World War II almost intact, making it one of the region's best-preserved medieval buildings. Free entry.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Alter Schwede leans slightly—it's intended, or rather time intended it. This Gothic brick facade dates from around 1380, and the name (the 'Old Swede') comes from a 19th-century tavern installed there in memory of Swedish occupation. Wismar was under Swedish rule from 1648 to 1803, a period of which the Zeughaus (Karl XII's baroque arsenal) bears witness.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Walk along the Grube, a medieval canal dug in the 13th century to supply the city with fresh water, for its two kilometers through the old town. This canal is the last inner medieval moat preserved in a northern German old town. The walk passes former mills and brick facades.

⏱ 45 min

If you have a stomach for smoked fish, try the Bückling—whole hot-smoked herring—in one of the Old Port stalls. Wismar's port has been active since the Middle Ages and still hosts reconstructed Kogge vessels. The Poeler Kogge Wissemara offers sailing excursions in summer.

⏱ 60 min

Schwerin

★★★★☆
1

Schwerin Castle floats on its lake—and we're hardly exaggerating; it sits on an island connected to the mainland by a narrow bridge. The current castle was rebuilt in neo-Gothic historicist style between 1847 and 1857 by Friedrich Franz II. It houses today both a museum and the Landtag (regional parliament), making it one of the few historic residences still in political use in Germany.

⏱ 90 min
2

In Schwerin Cathedral (Dom zu Schwerin), silence is absorbed by the Gothic brick walls. The main construction dates from the 14th century, but older parts date from the 13th. The nave contrasts with the lightness of its columns—a classic of Mecklenburg Backsteingotik. The tower is accessible and provides views over the lake and rooftops.

⏱ 45 min
3

Schwerinians call it the 'Neuschwanstein of the north'—this isn't flattering hyperbole but sincere architectural comparison, both castles being contemporaneous and sharing the aesthetic of 19th-century romantic historicism. The Schlossgarten designed by Peter Joseph Lenné in the 1840s surrounds the castle with French-style gardens and an orangery.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Rent an electric boat on Schwerin Lake: the lake surrounds the castle on three sides and offers a perspective that tourists who stay on the lawns never see. The south shore is uncrowded on weekdays. Rental at the marina 10 min walk from the castle.

⏱ 90 min

If you pass on a Saturday, the market on Alter Garten, the main square facing the castle, features local producers, flowers, and grilled Mecklenburg sausages. The square is framed by the Staatliches Museum Schwerin, which houses a Flemish and Dutch collection assembled by the dukes in the 17th century.

⏱ 60 min

Bad Doberan

★★☆☆☆
1

Doberan Minster, from a Cistercian monastery founded in 1171, isn't a city cathedral but a country abbey church—which makes its interior even more astonishing. The Gothic red-brick construction began around 1280 and concluded with the consecration of 1386. The 76-m-long basilica houses a gilded canopy choir from the 14th century and the tombs of Mecklenburg princes. Visit in 45 min.

⏱ 45 min
2

A small spa town since 1793, gigantic medieval heritage: Bad Doberan is this permanent contradiction. Cistercian builders shaped Baltic Backsteingotik techniques here for over a century, and their methods spread throughout the North Sea region. The Visitor Center in the Marstall explains this influence with a model of the 1550 monastery.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Climb aboard the Molli, a narrow-gauge steam train connecting Bad Doberan to Kühlungsborn since 1886, passing through the town's shopping streets—yes, the train literally runs between the houses. The full journey takes about 45 min and hugs the Baltic coast. Ticket at Bad Doberan station.

⏱ 90 min

Rostock

★★★★☆
1

Kröpeliner Straße, Rostock's wide thoroughfare converted into a pedestrian zone in 1968—one of East Germany's first—is well-restored and honest, without the commercial staging of other Hanseatic cities. It stretches from Neuer Markt to Kröpeliner Tor for about 600 m, alternating brick Gothic, Renaissance, and some GDR-era concrete buildings. The Universitätsplatz midway is the historic heart.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Kröpeliner Tor tower rises 54 m above the end of the pedestrian zone, built around 1270 as part of the city fortifications. Of the twenty-two gates Rostock once possessed, four have survived. A small local history museum lodges in the tower. The view from the rampart walk justifies the stairs.

⏱ 40 min
3

The Marienkirche houses a medieval astronomical clock still in operation. This clock dates from the 14th century and displays the liturgical calendar, moon phases, and solar time with a precision its builders took a century to perfect. Each day at noon, the upper dial's figurines parade. The church itself dates from the 13th century and its 117-m spire is visible from Warnemünde on clear days.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

A 15-min S-Bahn ride separates Rostock from Warnemünde, the historic seaside district at the Warnow estuary. The white sand beach, aligned Strandkörbe, and old red-brick lighthouse form the most repeated image of the Mecklenburg coast. Less touristy than Binz, the waterfront remains local and calm outside August.

⏱ 120 min

If you're there in August (typically first week), the Hanse Sail brings together each year on the Warnow one to two hundred traditional sailing ships and museum vessels. It's one of northern Germany's major maritime gatherings, open to the public and free from the quays. Boarding excursions are also offered.

⏱ 120 min

Stralsund

★★★★☆
1

Stralsund's Alter Markt resembles a theater set planted by mistake in reality: the Gothic red-brick Rathaus, its gable pierced with blind arcades, the Nikolaikirche crushing everything behind it, the Wulflamhaus from 1380 on the corner. Stralsund has been a founding Hansa member since 1259, and its center has barely changed in layout since the 14th century. Joint UNESCO with Wismar since 2002.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Ozeaneum on the harbor island opens onto 8,700 m² of exhibitions on the North and Baltic Seas. Its aquariums include what's presented as Germany's largest aquarium with 2,600 m³ of water, devoted to the North Sea. The architecture by Behnisch, Behnisch & Partner (2008)—four volumes symbolizing sunken rocks—received the title of European Museum of the Year 2010.

⏱ 120 min
3

Stralsund's Marienkirche soared to 151 m before the Gothic spire collapsed in a 1495 storm—rebuilt even higher, it was destroyed by lightning in 1647 and replaced by the current baroque dome of 104 m. Its 366 steps lead to a platform with views over the sea, Rügen island, and the Rügenbrücke (2.83 km, Mecklenburg-Vorpommern's longest). Before climbing: the top steps are narrow.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Climb the 366 steps of Stralsund's Marienkirche: the ascent takes 20 min but the view over the Strelasund, Rügen island, and the red-brick roofs of the Altstadt justifies the effort. Fewer tourists here than in Hamburg or Lübeck—groups prefer to stay below.

⏱ 45 min

A Hanseatic city but entirely surrounded by water—Stralsund sits on a peninsula between the Strelasund and several interior ponds. This insular geography saved it during the Thirty Years' War. Rent a bike and circle the island at low tide: the views of the old town from the Strelasund, with the spires of three major churches in the background, are the finest ways to see the city.

⏱ 60 min

Binz

★★★☆☆
1

Binz Pier, inaugurated in 1902 and rebuilt in 1994 at 370 m (versus 560 m originally), has a heavy history: a section collapsed in 1912, killing several people, which led to the creation of the DLRG (German lifesaving society). The current pier ends with a pontoon above the waves—in rough seas, you really feel the movement.

⏱ 45 min
2

The white villas of Binz's Strandpromenade were built between 1880 and 1930 in Bäderarchitektur style: turrets, carved wooden verandas, bay windows. Binz is recognized as the largest coherent ensemble of this style on the island. Binz was officially declared a seaside resort in 1885. The promenade runs along 4 km of fine sand.

⏱ 60 min
3

Jagdschloss Granitz is worth the detour from Binz: this mid-19th-century hunting lodge sits on a wooded hill 4 km away. Its central 38-m tower offers views on clear days to neighboring islands. Accessible by car or on foot from Binz in 1 hour.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Board the Rasender Roland, the narrow-gauge steam locomotive connecting Binz to Putbus since 1895. The journey crosses forests and marshes, with stops at Sellin and Baabe. The wooden period carriages are original or very close—the steam breath and panting rhythm are part of the voyage.

⏱ 90 min

Islanders call it the Riviera of the East—and the comparison holds better than it seems: blue sea, generous summer sun, white architecture, and waterfront restaurants. Outside July-August, Binz recovers its provincial calm. The best plan remains waking at 7 AM, being on the beach before cruise groups, and swimming in 18-20°C water.

⏱ 120 min

Konigsstuhl Jasmund

★★★★☆
1

Pure white against deep blue: the Königsstuhl is Rügen's tallest and best-known chalk cliff, at 118 m above the Baltic. The chalk dates from about 70 million years ago. The Skywalk, a suspended walkway above the void, opened in 2023 and offers a direct view of the wall face. Jasmund National Park (3,000 ha) is Germany's smallest national park.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Königsstuhl cliff plunges 118 m into the Baltic and erosion makes it one of the coast's most unstable formations. Entire sections regularly fall into the sea—a natural process since the last glaciation. The Stubnitz beech forests bordering the cliffs have been designated UNESCO World Natural Heritage since 2011.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Descend to the pebble beach from Sassnitz (8 km from the National Park Center) via the coastal trail—allow 2 hours round-trip. From below, the view of the chalk cliffs is more impressive than from the summit. Early morning, the site is calm and the raking light brings out the chalk's whiteness.

⏱ 120 min

Kap Arkona

★★★★☆
1

Three towers on a Nordic promontory—it's too many, and yet each deserves a stop. The Schinkelturm (1827, the oldest) was designed following plans by the Prussian Architecture Commission and remains the second-oldest lighthouse on the German Baltic coast. The Tall Lighthouse (1902, 35 m) is active. The Peilturm (1927) served as a radio beacon for the Sassnitz-Trelleborg ferry line.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Schinkelturm, built in 1827 in square red brick, is the oldest lighthouse still standing on the German Baltic coast after Travemünde lighthouse. At its feet: the Burgwall of Jaromarsburg, a Slavic rampart from the 6th to 12th centuries protecting the temple of the god Svantovit. In 1168, Danish King Valdemar I conquered and razed it. Only the circular embankment remains.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Leave the car at Putgarten (paid parking) and walk the 1.3 km to the cape's tip—no traffic, sea wind, views over the 45-m cliff. In good weather, the Danish coast of Møn is visible on the horizon, about 30 km away. The village of Vitt, 200 m below the cape, is an untouched fishing hamlet with an octagonal 19th-century chapel.

⏱ 90 min

Sellin

★★★☆☆
1

Sellin Pier measures 394 m and is Rügen island's longest pier. Its first version, inaugurated in 1906, measured 508 m before being reduced by storms and ice—the last destruction dates from winter 1941/42. The current pier, rebuilt in 1998 following models from 1906 and 1927, descends from the top of the cliff to the sea in a spectacular staircase, with a diving gondola at the end.

⏱ 60 min
2

Destroyed multiple times by ice and storms, then rebuilt in 1998 exactly identical to the 1927 model: this is the Seebrücke's story, but also a metaphor for Sellin itself. The villas of Wilhelmstraße, in carved white wood and open terraces, passed through the GDR era in a state of disrepair then were restored after 1990 one by one. The result is strangely intact.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walk down Wilhelmstraße lined with Bäderarchitektur villas from the Rasender Roland station to the sea in 10 min on foot. The street is steeply sloped—which is why the pier also descends via a monumental staircase to beach level. Below, the restaurants over the water serve fresh fish and pretzels at sunset.

Greifswald

★★★☆☆
1

Friedrich called it 'my beloved ruin'—Eldena Abbey Ruins, founded in 1199 by Cistercians, provided Caspar David Friedrich with his principal motifs for three decades. He was born in Greifswald in 1774, spent his childhood there, and sketched these walls from his adolescent visits. The paintings derived from them are preserved in Berlin and in Greifswald itself at the Pommersches Landesmuseum.

⏱ 45 min
2

Greifswald's Altstadt stayed standing thanks to a surrender without combat in 1945—the garrison commander chose to surrender rather than let fighting destroy the city. Result: the Gothic brick ensemble is intact. St. Nikolai Cathedral, the Marktplatz, and the university museum (the university dates from 1456) form a compact ensemble visitable in two hours.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Caspar-David-Friedrich-Bildweg follows on foot or by bike from the painter's birthplace in the Altstadt to Eldena Abbey Ruins, passing through the historic port and the fishing village of Wieck with its wooden drawbridge. The route is about 6 km and takes 2 hours on foot. The Caspar-David-Friedrich-Zentrum in the birthplace house opens onto his work and local motifs.

⏱ 120 min

Hamburg

★★★★★
1

HafenCity unfolds along the Elbe on former port lands as Europe's largest intra-city urban renewal project according to developers' data—157 hectares under development until around 2030. On the Elbpromenade side, the Marco-Polo-Terrassen offer a viewpoint over the Elbe free from any construction, rare in a port city.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Reeperbahn deserves an hour in the evening, even if clubs don't interest you: this is where the Beatles myth began in the Große Freiheit clubs between 1960 and 1962, before Liverpool. The Beatlemania Museum and commemorative panels on Star-Club Street trace this precise chapter. The street is lively, safe, and lined with theaters.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Dine in the Schanzenviertel, the alternative district north of the Reeperbahn, before heading to the airport. The restaurants on Schulterblatt are open evenings, prices reasonable, the atmosphere less formatted than HafenCity. Hamburg Airport is 15 min by U-Bahn from Hauptbahnhof.

⏱ 90 min
