Windsor

★★★★☆
1

Yes, it's packed with tourists. The State Apartments are worth the hour-long queue anyway: the painted ceilings commissioned by Charles II after the Restoration display a scale of grandeur England rarely produces. Windsor Castle has been in continuous use for over 900 years—residence of 40 successive monarchs. Book online (around £30, exact price at rct.uk); the castle closes Tuesdays and Wednesdays. Allow at least 2½ hours.

⏱ 150 min
2

Four point eight kilometres of lawn mown flat as a billiard table, and at the far end a castle silhouette that always looks too small for the distance. The Long Walk is one of the longest vistas in Europe—laid out under Charles II, free, accessible on foot from the castle gate. The Copper Horse statue sits atop Snow Hill: walk up there to grasp the scale.

⏱ 90 min
3

Founded in 1440 by Henry VI, Eton College has educated nineteen British prime ministers. Walk across Thames Bridge from Windsor to see the Elizabethan facades from the street—it's free. Guided tours of the interior run outside term time (around £10). Check the calendar on their website before coming.

Virginia Water

★★★★☆
1

Genuine Roman columns in an English park: that's the paradox of Virginia Water. The fragments were a gift to George IV in 1816 from the ruler of Tripoli—they came from the Roman city of Leptis Magna, in present-day Libya. After eight years at the British Museum for lack of a project, royal architect Jeffry Wyatville arranged them into picturesque ruins in 1826. Eight-and-a-half kilometre lake circuit (2 hours), nine-metre waterfall, Canadian totem pole. Parking £5, park free.

Farnham

★★☆☆☆
1

Farnham residents simply call it 'the Keep'—as if only one castle existed. Founded in 1138 by Henry of Blois, brother of King Stephen and Bishop of Winchester, the keep dominates the Georgian town from its Norman motte. Free, open daily except in winter. Climb up on foot from the High Street for views over the red-tiled rooftops.

⏱ 60 min
2

Cistercian monks from France arrived here in 1128 to found Waverley Abbey, England's first Cistercian house. The ruins survive beside the Wey in a silent meander—13th-century vaulted cellarium still standing, close-cropped grass, deer sometimes. English Heritage, free access. Three kilometres east of Farnham.

⏱ 50 min
3

Farnham's High Street is perhaps the best-preserved Georgian townscape in Surrey: red-brick facades, pilastered doorways, small-paned windows. The Museum of Farnham (free) traces the local hop industry that supplied London's breweries. Thirty minutes of strolling will suffice.

Winchester

★★★★☆
1

One hundred and seventy metres of Gothic nave—the longest in Europe—and in the crypt, a bronze diver staring at the water. William Walker worked alone in total darkness beneath the foundations from 1906 to 1911, placing by hand 25,800 bags of cement and 900,000 bricks to stop the cathedral collapsing into the peat. His statue memorialises him there. Adult around £10.

⏱ 75 min
2

It's not the real Round Table—but nobody suspects upon entering the Great Hall. This disc of English oak, 5.5 metres in diameter, dates from the 13th century, made for a tournament of Edward I; Henry VIII had it painted, placing his own portrait as King Arthur. Free. The Great Hall is all that remains of Winchester's medieval castle.

⏱ 45 min
3

Walk past the college founded in 1382 by William of Wykeham, one of England's oldest public schools. Guided tours (£10) allow access to the medieval courts outside term time. Check dates on their website—the calendar varies year to year.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Follow the River Itchen from Winchester's centre. This crystalline stream, one of England's most celebrated trout rivers, flows between medieval mills. Winchester City Mill, rebuilt in 1744 on a mill site recorded in the Domesday Book (1086), is operational—you can buy flour milled on site.

Avebury

★★★★★
1

Stonehenge has the photos, Avebury has the stones. The world's largest megalithic circle—1.3 kilometres in circumference, UNESCO-listed since 1986—surrounds an entire village, complete with pub and sheep. You walk among the sarsen blocks, not behind a barrier. Arrive before 10am or after 4pm to avoid the coaches. Parking £7, circle free.

⏱ 75 min
2

Thirty metres of chalk piled by hand, circa 2400 BC: Silbury Hill is Europe's largest prehistoric mound, and nobody knows why it was built. No burial chamber, no objects—just this conical mass dominating the landscape. English Heritage, exterior free, open access. Visible from the road; observe from the edge.

⏱ 20 min
3

If you have a torch, West Kennet Long Barrow is one of the circuit's most striking experiences. You enter a 5,000-year-old Neolithic collective tomb—side chambers of sarsen stones, chalk floor, total silence. The bodies of more than 46 individuals were deposited here. Free access, English Heritage, 500 metres from Silbury on foot.

In 1835, from a window in this abbey, Fox Talbot exposed the first surviving photographic negative—a small lattice window, still visible today in the south gallery. 2

Bath

★★★★☆
1

Arrive at the Roman Baths before the school groups—the geothermal water rising from the spring at 46°C has fed this pool for two thousand years. The surrounding museum is exemplary: reconstruction of the goddess Sulis Minerva, votive ceremonies. Adult around £22. Book online and aim for opening time.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Royal Crescent is thirty identical houses in a sweeping arc, Palladian facade on lawn, view over the city below. John Wood the Younger built it between 1767 and 1775—symbol of Georgian urbanism at its zenith. The central lawn is public; a visit to No. 1 Royal Crescent (interior museum) adds £8.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Crossing Exmoor, take the coastal route via Porlock Hill. In autumn, this is rutting season: red deer stags bellow on the moors at dawn—a sonic and visual spectacle not to be missed if you have binoculars.

⏱ 60 min

Thermae Bath Spa, opened in 2006, is the only place in the UK where you can bathe outdoors in natural thermal waters. Its rooftop pool, at 40°C, offers a direct view of the Georgian skyline. The experience is especially pleasant in winter. Book ahead.

Bath Abbey, completed in 1616, has flying buttresses that resemble ladders—deliberately: a vision of the founder who saw angels ascending and descending toward heaven.
