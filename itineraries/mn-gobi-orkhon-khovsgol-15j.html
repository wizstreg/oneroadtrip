Ulaanbaatar

★★★★☆
1

Gandantegchinlen Monastery - built in 1809 as Shar Sum ('the yellow monastery') by the 5th Javzandamba, it took its current name in 1838 when relocated to Dalkha Hill. Inside, the statue of Megjid-Janraiseg towers over 26 meters high and weighs more than 20 tons of copper - remade in 1996 with gold donations from Nepal and Japan after the Soviets melted down the original in 1937. Hundreds of monks still live here today. Arrive early in the morning to witness the daily Buddhist ceremonies - worshippers come in droves, the monastery is fully alive.

⏱ 90 min
2

Sükhbaatar Square - in the heart of the capital, this grand plaza bears the name of revolutionary hero Damdin Sükhbaatar, who proclaimed Mongolia's independence here in 1921. The Government Palace closes off the far end of the square, with its large equestrian statue of Genghis Khan. This is where the political and symbolic heart of the nation beats.

⏱ 45 min
3

National Museum of Mongolia - the museum traces the country's history from Bronze Age nomads through the Soviet era. Traditional costumes, Mongol Empire weaponry, shamanic instruments and a reconstructed ger are on display. Allow about 1.5 hours for a thorough visit.

⏱ 90 min
4

Zaisan Memorial - perched on a hill south of the city, this memorial built for the 50th anniversary of the Revolution honors Soviet-Mongolian soldiers who died during World War II. The circular reinforced concrete panel illustrates Russian-Mongolian friendship through four scenes: the 1921 independence, the 1939 Battle of Khalkhin Gol, victory over Nazi Germany, and space exploration. Climb the 300 steps from the parking area - the panoramic view over Ulaanbaatar and the Tuul River is the best in the capital.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Stroll through downtown streets between Sükhbaatar Square and the shopping districts - a chance to see Soviet-era apartment blocks, urban gers on the outskirts, and glass towers in the center all coexisting.

⏱ 60 min

Sunset from Zaisan Hill - the best time to enjoy the view, with raking light across the plain and the Bogd Khan mountains in the background.

⏱ 45 min

Baga Gazriin Chuluu

★★★☆☆
1

Baga Gazriin Chuluu Canyon - 'stone of the small place' in Mongolian. This granite massif rises from the flat steppe at 1,751 meters altitude in Dundgovi Province, about 270 km south of Ulaanbaatar. Erosion has carved narrow passages between blocks of granite 210 million years old. Some corridors are barely wide enough to pass through face-forward. Inside the site, you'll find ruins of a 17th-century Buddhist temple destroyed by the Communists in the 1930s, along with inscriptions left in the rock by two hermit monks in the 19th century.

⏱ 90 min
2

Sacred spring of the rock - a narrow, deep fissure in the granite collects rainwater. Local legend credits it with the power to cure eye diseases; a spoon placed on the edge allows visitors to collect a few drops. Whether you believe it or not, the place is strange and the surrounding scenery is worth the detour.

⏱ 30 min
3

Hill ovoos - the hills surrounding the canyon are crowned with ovoos, those pyramids of stones and wood to which Mongolians tie khadaks (blue scarves) and circle clockwise to honor the spirits of the place. Clear views across the steppe from the heights.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Hike through the granite maze - one to two hours walking through gorges and between granite blocks. The path is unmarked, which gives the route an exploratory feel. Bring sturdy shoes; some passages require squeezing through or light scrambling.

⏱ 120 min

Night sky observation from camp - at this distance from Ulaanbaatar, with no light pollution, the steppe sky is exceptionally clear. Night in ger camp or tent.

⏱ 60 min

Tsagaan Suvarga

★★★★☆
1

Tsagaan Suvarga (the White Stupa) - this limestone formation 60 meters high and 400 meters long rises from the Gobi plain like the ruins of a ghost city. Millions of years ago, Dundgovi Province lay at the bottom of an inland sea; the sedimentary layers exposed by erosion tell, in their striped colors - cream white, orange, violet, ochre red - the different geological epochs. Stand on the east side to see the entire cliff face: from there it resembles a Buddhist stupa, which earned it its name. At sunset, the colors shift to deep red - the best time for photography.

⏱ 90 min
2

Red Stupa (Ulaan Suvarga) - about 20 km southwest of the White Stupa, a similar formation with more pronounced red tones. Less visited, it's worth the detour if you have time late in the day.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Hike along the White Stupa ridge - from the parking area on top of the plateau, descend along the cliff edge to view the colors of the wall from below. Allow an hour round trip. Beware the edge: the rock is friable and erosion has carved overhangs.

⏱ 60 min

Camelback ride at the foot of the cliffs - local operators offer rides on Bactrian camels (two-humped) in the semi-desert around the site. A way to see the landscape at human height and meet the nomadic camel-herding families of the region.

⏱ 90 min

Dalanzadgad

★★★☆☆
1

Yolyn Am Canyon - 'valley of the bearded vulture', in Gobi Gurvansaikhan National Park, 46 km from Dalanzadgad. At 2,500 meters altitude, this narrow gorge cuts through the Gurvan Saikhan mountains for several kilometers. Its walls are so high and so close together that sunlight barely penetrates - result: a field of ice several meters thick forms each winter and melts only in midsummer, if at all some years. In June, you can walk on ice in the middle of the Gobi Desert. The canyon is also home to argali (large wild sheep), ibex and - more rarely - the snow leopard.

⏱ 120 min
2

Gobi Wildlife Museum at the park entrance - just before the canyon trail, a small museum presents Gobi fauna with taxidermied specimens, eggs and dinosaur bones. Quick but useful for understanding what you might observe in the park.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Hike through Yolyn Am gorge - the trail follows a stream and winds between rock walls that gradually narrow until only two people can pass side by side. About 7 km round trip from the parking area, flat with no technical difficulty. Early in the season, much of the canyon floor remains frozen.

⏱ 180 min

Arrival in Dalanzadgad late afternoon - this small town is the administrative capital of Ömnögovi Province (South Gobi) and the main gateway to the Gobi. Take advantage of the local market to stock up before the following stages.

⏱ 45 min

Bayanzag

★★★★☆
1

Bayanzag, the Flaming Cliffs - Roy Chapman Andrews named this site 'the flaming cliffs' during his 1920s expeditions: at sunset, the red sandstone cliffs turn blood red, then burnt orange. Bayanzag means 'rich in saxaul', that twisted, dense shrub that camels browse in the Gobi. In 1923, a member of Andrews' expedition discovered here the first dinosaur eggs ever scientifically identified - Protoceratops eggs. Since then, the site has yielded Velociraptors, Oviraptors and numerous primitive mammal fossils. Fossil collecting is strictly forbidden, but you can see them on site from the ridge trails.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Walk along the cliff ridge - a trail runs along the plateau edge about ten meters above the cliffs. Looking down, you sometimes spot bone fragments or suspicious shapes in the rock. The rule: observe, don't take anything. The late afternoon light literally transforms the cliffs into embers.

⏱ 90 min

Saxaul forest at the foot of the cliffs - descend to explore this dwarf forest of shrubs whose wood is so dense it sinks in water. These saxaul have existed since the age of dinosaurs and play a key role in stabilizing Gobi soil. The site is bordered by numerous semi-wild camels.

⏱ 45 min

Khongoryn Els

★★★★☆
1

Khongoryn Els Dunes - 'Duut Mankhan', the singing dunes, is what Mongolians call them: when wind shifts the sand or you run down from a ridge, the grains emit a deep, hollow sound, a sort of low vibration that can be heard several hundred meters away. These dunes stretch about 180 km long and up to 27 km wide, with peaks reaching 300 meters - three times the height of the Dune du Pilat. They constitute the largest sand area in Mongolia, within Gobi Gurvansaikhan National Park. The pale yellow sand changes hue throughout the day: orange at dawn, blinding white at noon, powder pink at sunset.

⏱ 90 min
2

Khongoryn Gol Oasis - at the foot of the dunes, on the north side, the small Khongoryn Gol River runs alongside the massif for several kilometers, fed by underground springs. This green strip in an entirely sand landscape is surreal. Nomadic camel-herding families set up their gers here each summer; horses and Bactrian camels graze in the grass just steps from the first dunes.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Climbing a dune ridge at sunset - the hike up the ridge of a large dune takes 45 to 90 minutes depending on the height chosen. The sand you sink into with each step makes progress exhausting, but the view from the ridge - over the sea of sand on one side, the steppe and mountains on the other - is worth the effort. Bring shoes you can remove; the climb is often done barefoot. Set out at least 2 hours before sunset.

⏱ 120 min

Camelback ride at the foot of the dunes - local operators offer Bactrian camel rides along the oasis edge and into the first dunes. One to two hours to see the landscape from camel height and observe the life of nomadic camel-herding families.

⏱ 90 min

Ongiin Khiid

★★☆☆☆
1

Ongiin Khiid Monastery - founded in 1660 on both banks of the Ongi River, this complex at its peak housed over 1,000 monks across some thirty temples and four Buddhist universities. It was one of the largest religious centers in southern Mongolia. In 1939, under Khorloogiin Choibalsan's direction, the Communist army razed everything: 200 monks were executed, survivors imprisoned or deported. The river itself was diverted to supply mines. In 1990, three monks who had begun their education here sixty years earlier returned to the ruins and began rebuilding, stone by stone. Today one temple has been restored and two small museums display rescued religious objects, carved stones and original architectural elements.

⏱ 75 min
2

Ruins of the north site (Barlim Temple) - on the other side of the river, the foundations and partially standing walls of Barlim Temple, the largest of all, give an idea of the scale of what existed. One of its temples was the largest in all Mongolia. Walk through the ruins along the water's edge.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Hike in the surrounding hills - the site is surrounded by arid steppe hills that can be climbed for a view over the entire monastery and the Ongi River valley. An hour's walk is enough to reach a good viewpoint.

⏱ 60 min

Swimming in the Ongi River - after a week of desert and off-road driving, the river winding at the monastery's foot offers a chance for a refreshing stop. The water is clear and shallow.

⏱ 45 min

Kharkhorin

★★★☆☆
1

Karakorum / Kharkhorin - Ögödei Khan, son of Genghis Khan, officially founded this capital in 1235. For about forty years, the city was the political, commercial and diplomatic heart of the Mongol Empire, a crossroads on the Silk Road where Venetian merchants, French emissaries and Buddhist monks circulated. In 1260, Kublai Khan transferred the capital to Khanbalik (present-day Beijing); Karakorum declined and was then razed in 1388 by Ming Dynasty troops. Today only four stone tortoises that delimited the city gates remain, along with ongoing archaeological excavations.

⏱ 75 min
2

Erdene Zuu Monastery - built in 1585 by Abtai Khan directly on the ruins of Karakorum, whose stones it reused. It is the oldest permanent-structure Buddhist monastery in Mongolia. Its white enclosure of 108 stupas (108 is the sacred number in Buddhism, the number of beads on a rosary) measures about 400 meters per side. In 1872, it had 62 temples; in 1939, Communist purges reduced it to three temples and the enclosure wall. These three temples are now active museums, richly decorated with 17th-century thangkas. Listed as a UNESCO World Heritage site in 2004 under 'Orkhon Valley Cultural Landscape'.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Visit to the Karakorum Museum - this modern museum displays objects from archaeological excavations of the ancient capital: Chinese pottery, Islamic coins, architectural fragments. Helps understand the cosmopolitan dimension of the Mongol Empire.

⏱ 60 min

Walk around the stone tortoises - the four granite tortoises that supported the steles marking the gates of the ancient capital are scattered at the four corners of the site. Find them in the steppe around Erdene Zuu - two of them are visible from the monastery.

⏱ 30 min

Orkhon Valley

★★★★☆
1

Ulaan Tsutgalan Falls - the Ulaan Tsutgalan River ('the red confluence') plunges into the Orkhon over cliffs of black basalt in hexagonal columns, creating a waterfall about 20 meters high and 10 meters wide. These volcanic columns formed over 20,000 years ago during eruptions that covered the valley with lava. The contrast between white foam, black basalt and surrounding green steppe is striking. Flow varies greatly by season: very strong from June to August, the falls can be nearly dry in spring.

⏱ 90 min
2

Basalt canyon downstream from the falls - descend the trail along the gorge downstream. The walls in volcanic organ pipes - those regular hexagonal columns reminiscent of the Giant's Causeway in Ireland - plunge 20 to 30 meters. Wild peonies grow at the cliff base in July.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Descent to the foot of the waterfall - a trail of about 1 km descends from the plateau to the basin at the foot of the falls. The descent is moderate but slippery after rain. From below, the view of the waterfall and basalt columns is far more impressive than from above.

⏱ 90 min

Settling into the Orkhon Valley - arrival at ger camp by the river. The valley has been a UNESCO heritage site since 2004. Evening by the Orkhon, Mongolia's longest river (1,124 km).

⏱ 60 min

Orkhon Valley landscape - from the plateau overlooking the river, the view embraces the valley's green grasslands, herds of yaks and horses, and white gers scattered across the folds of the terrain. Immersion with a nomadic family - families settled in the valley often welcome travelers for salted milk tea, dried cheese (aaruul) and fermented milk vodka (arkhi). A chance to observe how a ger is assembled, how mares are milked to make koumiss, how a steppe herder's day unfolds.

Terkhiin Tsagaan Lake

★★★★☆
1

Tsetserleg and Zayan Gegeen Monastery - capital of Arkhangai Province, Tsetserleg is often considered the most pleasant and greenest of Mongolia's provincial towns. Zayan Gegeen Monastery, founded in the mid-17th century, was converted into an ethnographic and religious museum in 1997. It displays tangkas, religious objects, traditional musical instruments and elements of nomadic life. A few hours' stop en route westward.

⏱ 60 min
2

Lake Terkhiin Tsagaan Nuur (the White Lake) - at 2,060 meters altitude in the Khangai Mountains, this lake was born about 8,000 years ago from the eruption of Khorgo volcano, whose lava flow dammed the Suman River. It stretches 16 km long and up to 10 km wide (61 km²), with an average depth of 20 meters. Its waters are so pure you can drink them directly. Listed as a Ramsar site in 1998. The shores are surrounded by grasslands, basalt formations and the craters of other extinct volcanoes.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

⏱ 45 min

Settling in by White Lake - arrival late afternoon. Stroll along the lake's south shore, in peace. The evening light on the lake and surrounding wooded hills is particularly beautiful.

Terkhiin Tsagaan Lake

★★★★☆
1

Khorgo Volcano - this extinct volcano peaks at 2,240 meters east of the lake. Its last eruption was about 8,000 years ago, making it Mongolia's most recently active volcano. The climb to the crater rim, about 200 meters in diameter and 70 to 80 meters deep, offers a 360° view over White Lake and the Arkhangai steppe. Protected since 1965.

⏱ 30 min
2

Lava fields and 'basalt gers' - around Khorgo, vast solidified lava flows form a lunar landscape locals call 'the basalt gers': solidified lava bubbles resembling domes. Lava tubes run beneath the surface of some areas.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Climbing Khorgo volcano - from the camp by the lake, hike on foot to the crater rim. The trail crosses basalt fields and larch groves. The climb takes 1 to 2 hours depending on pace. View of the lake from the crater edge.

⏱ 150 min

Kayak or canoe outing on White Lake - local operators offer watercraft to explore the lake surface. The waters are clear to the bottom. In summer, families of mallards and whooper swans frequent the shores.

★★☆☆☆

Lake Khövsgöl - 'the blue pearl of Mongolia'. 136 km long, 36 km wide, 267 meters deep: Khövsgöl alone contains 2% of the world's freshwater reserves. Activities

Erdenet

★★☆☆☆
1

Erdenet - Mongolia's second city (about 90,000 inhabitants) rose from nowhere in 1973: a joint Soviet-Mongolian expedition had finalized plans in 1972 to exploit a massive copper deposit discovered in the 1950s. The mine is now the world's fourth-largest copper mine: 22 million tons of ore are extracted annually, yielding 126,700 tons of copper. In the mid-1980s, over half the inhabitants were Russians; after 1990, most returned home. Their influence is still felt in the architecture: Soviet concrete apartment blocks, wide boulevards. The city is surrounded by green hills and has a functional but pleasant atmosphere, very different from the rest of the circuit.

View of the open-pit mine - at the mining site entrance, accessible by showing your passport, you can observe the Erdenet mine's open pit from the perimeter. A pit several hundred meters deep carved into a hill - an industrial spectacle on a rarely seen scale. (closed Fridays)

⏱ 45 min

Central market and restocking - Erdenet is the last major commercial center before returning to Ulaanbaatar. Well-stocked supermarkets, markets, local restaurants.

Amarbayasgalant

★★★★☆
1

Amarbayasgalant Khiid Monastery - 'monastery of tranquil felicity'. One of Mongolia's three largest monasteries, built between 1727 and 1736 on order of Manchu Emperor Yongzheng in memory of Zanabazar, Mongolia's first Buddhist spiritual leader (Bogd Gegen). Construction cost 3,730 kg of silver from imperial state coffers. The architecture is remarkably stylistically coherent: Chinese style of the Manchu period with Mongolian and Tibetan influences in colors and sculptures. The plan is strictly symmetrical on a north-south axis - 28 buildings survive today of the original 40. The Soviets partially destroyed the site in 1937; restoration began in 1988. It's one of the only monasteries to have partially survived the purges, making it the best-preserved in the country.

⏱ 120 min
2

Iven Valley and foot of Mount Buren-Khaan - the monastery sits in an isolated valley at the foot of a massif of wooded hills. You arrive after 40 km of track through an increasingly narrow and silent valley - the surprise effect on arrival is total. Ovoos crown the surrounding hills.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Morning Buddhist ceremony - arrive early (around 8-9am) to attend the monks' morning prayer in the main temple. The drums, brass trumpets and lamas' voices create a particularly striking atmosphere in this remote setting.

⏱ 60 min

Hike to the stupas on the heights - behind the monastery, a trail climbs toward the hills where several white stupas are placed. In 20 to 30 minutes of climbing, the plunging view over the monastery complex and valley justifies the effort.

⏱ 60 min
