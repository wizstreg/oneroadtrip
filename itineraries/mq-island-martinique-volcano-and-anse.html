Fort De France

★★★☆☆
1

Start at the Schoelcher Library, corner of Rue de la Liberté and Rue Victor-Sévère. Architect Pierre-Henri Picq built it in 1887 in Paris's Tuileries Garden, then displayed it at the 1889 World's Fair on the Champ-de-Mars before it was dismantled, shipped by boat, and reassembled piece by piece in Fort-de-France, where it opened in 1893. It's named after Victor Schoelcher, who donated 10,000 books and 250 musical scores to Martinique on condition the library be open to all—especially for educating former slaves. Most of the original works were lost in Fort-de-France's 1890 fire. Entry is free.

⏱ 45 min
2

Just steps away, step inside Saint-Louis Cathedral on Rue Schoelcher. Picq built it starting in 1891 after the previous one was destroyed by that year's cyclone. The entire framework is metal, manufactured in mainland France and erected on the original foundations. The nave runs 66 meters long by 24 meters wide. Inaugurated July 2, 1895, it features 19 stained-glass windows by glass painter Georges Néret and an octagonal dome. The current spire—12 tons of steel clad in aluminum-bronze scales—dates from 1979, the original having been weakened by the 1953 earthquake.

⏱ 40 min
3

Cross through Parc de la Savane, an esplanade of royal palms facing Flamands Bay. The paths border Creole refreshment stands and lead to the empty pedestal of the former statue of Joséphine de Beauharnais, removed in July 2020 after repeated vandalism linked to debates over her alleged role in Napoleon's reinstatement of slavery in 1802.

⏱ 30 min
4

Walk out to Fort Saint-Louis at the tip of the peninsula. Founded around 1640 by Governor Du Parquet, reconfigured along Vauban's principles between 1676 and 1680 using volcanic rock from Pelée, the fort measures 600 meters long by 100 meters wide. Still an active French Navy base, it's open for guided visits of about 75 minutes (Tuesday–Saturday). Iguanas—descendants of the former 19th-century zoo residents—sometimes reaching 1.5 meters, bask on the ramparts.

⏱ 75 min
5

Head early to the Grand Marché covered market, Boulevard Allègre. Stalls overflow with spices—cacao sticks, colombo, bois d'Inde—and tropical fruits. Creole crafts (madras fabric, dolls, basketry) fill the back aisles. Arrive before 8 a.m. to beat the tour-group crowds.

⏱ 50 min

Activities

Take the ferry shuttle linking Fort-de-France to Trois-Îlets in 20 minutes. The crossing of Flamands Bay offers the finest view of the city and the Pitons du Carbet.

⏱ 60 min

Stroll the pedestrian zone downtown to admire colonial façades with wrought-iron balconies and the murals along Rue Schoelcher and Rue Victor-Hugo.

⏱ 60 min

Finish with accras and fresh juice at one of the snack stands in Parc de la Savane, watching the sun set over the Caribbean Sea.

⏱ 45 min

Saint Pierre

★★★☆☆
1

Saint-Pierre imposes its story the moment you arrive. On May 8, 1902, at 7:52 a.m., Mount Pelée's pyroclastic surge destroyed the city in under two minutes. Over 30,000 dead, blast speeds estimated at 500 km/h, and temperatures exceeding 500 degrees. What had been Martinique's economic and cultural capital—the "Little Paris of the West Indies"—ceased to exist. Start at the ruins of Saint-Pierre's Theatre, modeled on Bordeaux's theater. What remains is a monumental staircase and vestiges of the loges. Climb the steps and watch tropical vegetation reclaim what was once the hall.

⏱ 45 min
2

Just behind the theater, visit Cyparis's cell. Louis-Auguste Cyparis, jailed for a brawl, occupied a semi-underground cell with thick walls, facing away from the volcano. He survived, burned but alive, and was found on May 11—three days after the eruption. Pardoned, he was hired by the Barnum circus in the United States, where he exhibited his scars, before ending his life in anonymity in Panama. The cell is intact.

⏱ 20 min
3

The Memorial to the 1902 Catastrophe—Franck A. Perret Museum—was founded in 1933 by the American volcanologist of the same name, witness to the 1929 eruptions. It's Martinique's oldest museum. The bronze bell from Saint-Étienne Church, warped by heat, melted objects recovered from the rubble, and photos of the city before and after May 8, 1902, form the heart of the collection. Allow about an hour.

⏱ 60 min
4

Climb Rue Monte au Ciel, a cobbled stairway whose name evokes fleeing residents. Overlooking it, the remains of the Colonial Health House, built in 1839 as the first psychiatric hospital in the Antilles, where patients were treated by hydrotherapy—baths of cool water flowing down from Pelée.

⏱ 30 min
5

Head to the waterfront for a view of the roadstead, with Mount Pelée's silhouette behind. Colorful fishermen's boats line the quay. In the roadstead, more than 40 ships sank in 1902—their wrecks now rank among the Caribbean's finest dive sites.

⏱ 25 min

Activities

Walk the ruins circuit marked by the tourist office—a 2 km loop with interpretive panels. Alternatively, opt for the Cyparis Express, a small train offering a roughly 90-minute tour with onboard narration.

⏱ 90 min

Snorkeling or scuba trips let you observe the submerged wrecks in the roadstead. Local operators offer these excursions from the quay.

⏱ 120 min

In the evening, waterfront restaurants serve Creole dishes—fish court-bouillon, chicken colombo—facing the bay and black volcanic sand.

⏱ 60 min

Saint Pierre

★★★☆☆
1

The day starts before sunrise if you want to enjoy the summit. The Aileron trail, departing from a parking lot at 824 meters altitude in the commune of Morne-Rouge, climbs 7.6 km round-trip with 650 meters of elevation gain through rainforest, high-altitude moorland, then bare rock. The highest point—Chinois, born of the 1929 eruption—peaks at 1,397 meters. In clear weather, you can spot Dominica to the north. Bring a waterproof windbreaker even if it's 30°C on the coast—the summit can feel like 15°C. The parking lot often fills by 8:30 a.m.: set out between 5 and 6 a.m.

⏱ 30 min
2

On the way down, stop at Depaz Distillery on Pelée's flanks. The Montagne estate dates from 1651. The 1902 eruption destroyed everything—the entire Depaz family perished except Victor, then a student in Bordeaux. He rebuilt starting in 1917. The 80 hectares of sugarcane on volcanic soil produce AOC Martinique rum. The original steam engine still runs during distillation season (February–June). The neo-colonial Château Depaz overlooks the plantation.

⏱ 60 min
3

Anse Céron, a black-sand beach facing Îlet de la Perle, is lined with coconut palms and sea grapes. Nearby, Habitation Céron is a 17th-century sugar estate nestled beneath a giant zamana—one of the island's largest trees. A trail from the Habitation reaches Anse Couleuvre in 30 minutes: a wilder black-sand beach, accessible only on foot.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Climb to the summit of Mount Pelée via the Aileron trail. The route skirts the caldera, descends into the Étang Sec—the crater floor overtaken by vegetation, which had filled with water just before the 1902 eruption—before the final ascent to Chinois. Recommended departure between 5 and 6 a.m.

⏱ 240 min

Swim at Anse Céron after the hike. The water is usually calm near the rocks. Arrive before noon to enjoy the peace.

⏱ 60 min

Late afternoon, sample the various rum cuvées at Depaz's boutique—white cane, barrel-aged, and vintage vintages.

⏱ 45 min

Les Trois Ilets

★★★☆☆
1

The Musée de la Pagerie, a few kilometers from town, occupies the ruins of a 500-hectare estate where sugarcane, cotton, and cacao were cultivated with between 150 and 300 enslaved people. It was here, on June 23, 1763, that Marie-Josèphe-Rose Tascher de la Pagerie was born—the future Joséphine de Beauharnais, Empress of France from 1804 to 1814. Her birthplace was destroyed by a hurricane in 1766 and never rebuilt. The museum, created in 1929 in the old kitchens, displays her childhood bed of courbaril wood, her letters to Napoleon, and portraits. Since 2021, new scenography also addresses the history of the estate's enslaved people. Plan for a guided visit.

⏱ 60 min
2

Detour to the Church of Notre-Dame de la Délivrance, built in 1724 in the heart of town. Joséphine was baptized here. The "red town center," named for its bricks from the local pottery, displays brightly colored Creole houses.

⏱ 30 min
3

Village de la Poterie sits on the site of a brick and tile factory founded in the 18th century by the Jesuits—the island's oldest enterprise. The old kilns and workshops now house potter artisans, designer boutiques, and galleries. A small museum traces the factory's history.

⏱ 60 min
4

Anse Mitan beach unfolds its white sand facing Fort-de-France Bay. Beachfront restaurants, kayak rentals, dive clubs. The ferry to Fort-de-France departs roughly every 30 minutes from the neighboring dock.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Embark on a kayak tour through Trois-Îlets' mangrove. Mangroves form vegetal tunnels where herons, crabs, and fish coexist in shallow waters. Operators offer these outings from Anse Mitan.

⏱ 150 min

Drive out to Pointe du Bout, a peninsula whose marina hosts sailboats and catamarans. The late-afternoon view across the bay, with the Pitons du Carbet in the background, is worth the stop.

⏱ 45 min

Take the late-afternoon ferry to Fort-de-France and watch the sunset over the Pitons du Carbet from the deck. The crossing takes 20 minutes.

⏱ 60 min

Les Trois Ilets

★★★☆☆
1

La Savane des Esclaves, in the La Ferme neighborhood of Trois-Îlets, is one of the island's most moving visits. Gilbert Larose, a passionate Martinican, cleared and rebuilt this 3-hectare site single-handedly, without subsidies, starting in 2000—opened in December 2004. The route begins with an introductory film, then takes you through: a reconstructed Kalinago village, Rue Case-Nègres, Place de l'Esclave Romain (named after the enslaved person whose revolt contributed to the 1848 abolition), and Village Antan Lontan, which depicts rural Creole life after abolition. Allow 90 minutes to 2 hours.

⏱ 90 min
2

La Maison de la Canne, housed in a former distillery, explains the complete sugar production cycle: from cutting to rum aging. Models, tools, and photographs document three centuries of cane industry.

⏱ 45 min
3

Anse à l'Âne is a quieter white-sand beach than Anse Mitan. Sea grapes line the shore. Offshore, Îlet à Ramiers, a former defensive post of the bay, preserves the remains of a military battery—reachable in a few minutes by shuttle.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Join a cassava-making workshop at La Savane des Esclaves: grating the cassava, pressing, cooking on a hot griddle. These workshops happen on certain dates—call ahead.

⏱ 60 min

Rent a mask and snorkel at Anse à l'Âne to spot sea turtles frequenting the seagrass meadows of Fort-de-France Bay—green and hawksbill turtles are regularly sighted.

⏱ 60 min

Take the Forêt de Vatable trail for a shaded one-hour walk among giant ferns and red gommier trees.

⏱ 60 min

Le Marin

★★★☆☆
1

Welcome to the largest marina in the Antilles. Created in 1985 and developed in stages until 2010, Le Marin harbor now offers 830 berths and 100 moorings, welcoming 55,000 boaters annually. The docks come alive at 7 a.m. with departures for excursions to the southern islets and the Grenadines. Stroll the quays to watch the ballet of sailboats.

⏱ 45 min
2

Climb up to Saint-Étienne Church, a classified historic monument, overlooking the town from a promontory. Built in the 18th century, its Jesuit-style stone façade is distinguished by a bell tower separate from the building's body. Inside, the framework evokes an upturned ship's hull.

⏱ 25 min
3

Head to Pointe Marin beach, a long ribbon of white sand at the southern entrance to the bay, facing Diamond Rock on the horizon. The shallow waters suit family swimming. It's also one of the island's most renowned kitesurf spots.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Book a catamaran outing from the marina to reach the shallows and southern islets—sandbars submerged just centimeters below the surface, surrounded by turquoise water.

⏱ 240 min

Dive clubs at the marina offer introductory dives and trips to the bay's coral reefs. Diamond Rock (175 m) was turned into a warship by the Royal Navy between 1804 and 1805—HMS Diamond Rock—and the waters around it shelter rich coral fauna.

⏱ 150 min

Evenings, the quays buzz with life: seafood restaurants, rum bars, and Creole concerts on weekends.

⏱ 90 min

Sainte Anne

★★★☆☆
1

Les Salines beach is Martinique's best-known: nearly two million visitors annually according to road counts. Grande Anse stretches about 1.2 km of white sand, bordered by coconut palms planted in the 1950s. The water is turquoise and shallow. The lolos serve grilled fish, accras, and coconut sorbet. Arrive before 9 a.m. to avoid crowds—after 10 a.m., the parking lot is often full. Note that the beach is receding by roughly 1 meter per year over the past twenty years due to erosion.

⏱ 90 min
2

Walk along Étang des Salines, a wetland protected by the Coastal Conservancy, via observation boardwalks on stilts. Herons, egrets, and migratory shorebirds frequent the site. Between March and June, sea turtles venture onto the neighboring beach to nest.

⏱ 40 min
3

The town of Sainte-Anne lines its colorful houses around the church square. The island's southernmost and sunniest commune, its dry climate contrasts with the humid north. Wander the pedestrian lanes down to Anse Caritan, a small beach at the town entrance, quieter than Les Salines.

⏱ 35 min

Activities

Morning at Les Salines, then embark on a catamaran or sailboat trip to the southern islets and Sainte-Anne's shallows—submerged sandbars ringed by translucent water, ideal for swimming and snorkeling. Local operators offer these excursions from the marina or from the beach.

⏱ 180 min

Late-morning stroll along Étang des Salines on the boardwalks. Binoculars recommended for migratory birds.

⏱ 60 min

Late afternoon, the town's craft market offers madras fabric, Job's tears jewelry, and local spices.

⏱ 60 min

Sainte Anne

★★★☆☆
1

Cap Chevalier juts into the Atlantic at the island's southeastern tip. The white-sand beach faces Îlet Chevalier, reachable in a few minutes by shuttle. The coral barrier breaks the Atlantic swell and creates a calm lagoon with underwater seagrass meadows where starfish, white urchins, and multicolored fish live.

⏱ 60 min
2

Savane des Pétrifications forms a lunar landscape in the midst of this tropical island: ochre and red soil, cacti, salt winds, no shade. This dried marsh on an ancient volcano owes its name to the fossilized tree trunks once found there—all since plundered. Morne des Pétrifications peaks at 119 meters. Offshore, Table du Diable is a flat rock jutting from the waves that fuels local legends.

⏱ 30 min
3

Don't miss Pointe d'Enfer: the cliff drops straight into a raging sea, waves crashing violently. Vertiginous panorama over the wild Atlantic coast.

⏱ 20 min
4

Anse Trabaud, at the edge of the Savane, is a nearly deserted white-sand beach ringed by low vegetation. Car access is paid and difficult (degraded track), which preserves its tranquility.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Hike section 4 of the Trace des Caps from Anse Trabaud to Les Salines, crossing Savane des Pétrifications and Pointe d'Enfer. Start early—no shade on this stretch.

⏱ 150 min

From Cap Chevalier, a local shuttle reaches Îlet Chevalier for a half-day of swimming and snorkeling on the reefs.

⏱ 180 min

Swim at Anse Trabaud to wrap up the hike—the turquoise water rewards the effort under the Savane sun.

⏱ 60 min

Le Marin

★★★☆☆
1

Habitation Clément, a 160-hectare estate in Le François, has been a classified historic monument since 1996. Homère Clément—Martinique's first métis physician—bought the Acajou estate at auction in 1887 and built his distillery there in 1917. The 18th-century main house was restored in 2003: Creole furniture, ventilated galleries, and the "Bush-Mitterrand" cottage—where Presidents Mitterrand and Bush held their summit in September 1991.

⏱ 45 min
2

The 16-hectare park, labeled "Jardin remarquable" in 2015, unfolds an avenue of royal palms, a tropical orchard, a machinery garden (industrial equipment staged outdoors), and Caribbean contemporary artworks among ponds and bamboo groves.

⏱ 50 min
3

The Fondation Clément Pinacothèque, inaugurated in March 2023 in a 550-m² building with giant glass panels, exhibits more than 800 works by 250 Martinican and Caribbean artists assembled over 30 years. Entry is free.

⏱ 35 min
4

On the way back, stop along the banks of Cul-de-Sac du Marin to observe the mangrove at low tide. Mangroves plunge their aerial roots into calm water where crabs and small fish move about.

⏱ 25 min

Activities

Spend the morning at Habitation Clément: garden, Creole house, former distillery turned rum interpretation center, and art museum take 2.5 to 3 hours. Finish at the Atelier des sens for a tasting of Clément AOC rums.

⏱ 180 min

Kayak along Le Marin's mangrove. The channels between mangroves let you approach herons, egrets, and stingrays in shallow waters.

⏱ 120 min

Late afternoon, settle in on the marina quays for a final evening in the south.

⏱ 60 min

Fort De France

★★★☆☆
1

Walk up Rue Victor-Hugo and Rue de la Liberté to observe downtown's Creole façades: wrought-iron balconies, louvered shutters, red-tiled roofs from Trois-Îlets potteries. The 1906 Courthouse with neoclassical columns contrasts with the colorful architecture around it.

⏱ 40 min
2

The Museum of Pre-Columbian Archaeology and Prehistory, housed in the former 1898 Military Intendancy on Rue de la Liberté, exhibits artifacts from the Amerindian civilizations present before European arrival: pottery, stone tools, Arawak and Carib ceremonial objects. Allow about an hour.

⏱ 50 min
3

The waterfront promenade runs along Flamands Bay from the ferry terminal to La Française beach at the foot of Fort Saint-Louis—the capital's only beach, a cove of blond sand with calm waters 15 minutes on foot.

⏱ 40 min
4

The fish market, at the end of Boulevard du Général-de-Gaulle, comes alive early in the morning when fishermen land tuna, marlin, dorado, and conch.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

A final stop at the Grand Marché covered market to bring back spices (colombo, pepper, Martinique vanilla), arranged rum, and Creole crafts.

⏱ 60 min

Head to La Française beach for a last swim in the heart of Fort-de-France, before the drive to Aimé-Césaire Airport in Lamentin, 15 km away.

⏱ 60 min

If time permits, a final walk through Parc de la Savane—refreshment stands, Creole snacks, and bay views—wraps up the circuit.

⏱ 45 min
