Labuan Bajo

★★★★☆
1

Arrival in Labuan Bajo, the coastal port at the western tip of Flores and gateway to Komodo National Park. The town has boomed over the past decade — dirt roads have given way to paved avenues, the wharves have been renovated and adventure outfitters line every main street. Head to the waterfront in the late afternoon: the port comes alive, wooden phinisi boats (traditional sailing vessels of the archipelago) tie up between excursions and locals gather as if on a seaside boulevard. Settle in and book your Komodo trips for the next two days this evening — spots go fast in peak season.

⏱ 60 min
2

Batu Cermin cave ('Mirror Rock') lies 4 km east of the port. It's a natural tunnel carved through hills of dark volcanic rock whose walls reflect sunlight filtering through an opening at the top — a natural mirror effect that gives it its name. The walls are embedded with coral and marine-animal fossils, proof that this rock was underwater thousands of years ago. The best time to visit is mid-afternoon (1:30–3 pm), when the sun hits at the optimal angle. Hire a proper guide on site — without one, the passages and the cave's history lose most of their interest.

⏱ 75 min
3

Labuan Bajo's fish market, along the harbour, is active from 5 am. If you arrive early, it's one of the most vivid spectacles on Flores: fishermen unload catches of tuna, barracuda and hammerhead sharks to the shouts of sarong-clad market women. Even in the evening, the waterfront buzzes around warungs serving grilled fish ordered straight from the stalls.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Sunset from the Bukit Sylvia or Bukit Cinta viewpoint — two hills a 15-minute walk from the centre that overlook the entire bay. The sun drops behind the volcanic islets scattered across the Komodo Strait, silhouetting the phinisi boats anchored below. The perfect prelude to the days ahead.

⏱ 75 min

Dinner on the Labuan Bajo waterfront — the restaurants clustered along the main road serve grilled seafood, freshly caught fish and dishes from Flores cuisine (coconut-milk rice, garlic-sautéed vegetables, buffalo-stew soup). The town is surprisingly cosmopolitan for its size: Italian, Indonesian and local kitchens coexist within a 500-metre radius.

⏱ 75 min

Labuan Bajo

★★★★☆
1

Depart Labuan Bajo port at 6 am by speedboat for Padar Island. The climb to the summit (30 to 45 minutes on a loose-gravel trail) reveals one of the most singular views in Indonesia: three bays with sand of different colours (white, pink, black) separated by jagged volcanic ridges. The landscape of arid savanna and serrated passes has no equivalent anywhere in the archipelago. Leave early — the morning light on the bays is far better than at noon.

⏱ 90 min
2

On Komodo or Rinca island, the Komodo dragon (Varanus komodoensis) is the largest living lizard on earth: 2.60 m on average, 80 to 90 kg. Endemic to just 5 Indonesian islands, its wild population is estimated at fewer than 3,500 individuals — it has been classified as 'endangered' by the IUCN since 2021. A ranger escort is mandatory for the trek. The dragons can run at 20 km/h over short distances and their bite is mildly venomous — follow the ranger's instructions to the letter.

⏱ 120 min
3

Pink Beach, on Komodo Island, owes its colour to fragments of red coral mixed into the white sand. The reefs immediately accessible from the shore teem with marine life — parrotfish, moray eels, turtles and, with luck, reef sharks at the bottom of the drop-off. You can hire a mask and snorkel from the tour operator.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Snorkelling at Manta Point — one of the best sites in Southeast Asia for swimming with manta rays. The mantas can reach 4 to 5 metres in wingspan and gather in plankton-rich currents. The best chances of an encounter are between December and March. Fins are not recommended so as not to disturb the animals.

⏱ 90 min

Return to Labuan Bajo in the late afternoon — the speedboat cruises past islets covered in volcanic savanna in the orange light. Dinner in town and preparation for tomorrow's drive east.

⏱ 75 min

Labuan Bajo

★★★★☆
1

Second day cruising the national park: heading for Rinca Island, where the dragon population is even denser than on Komodo. The guided trek (1 to 3 hours depending on the circuit) crosses a Lontar-palm savanna with vegetation typical of the semi-arid Nusa Tenggara landscapes. Beyond dragons, you'll spot Timor deer, water buffalo, macaques and white-bellied sea eagles. Arrive early — the dragons are most active in the morning before the heat sets in.

⏱ 120 min
2

Taka Makassar is a pristine white sandbar that emerges at low tide in the middle of the strait, ringed by turquoise water on all sides — no vegetation, just sand, sea and the silhouettes of volcanic islands on the horizon. The snorkelling around the sandbar reveals corals in excellent condition. Arrive mid-morning to find the sandbar still well exposed.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Snorkelling or diving from the boat in the national park waters — sites around Batu Bolong or Kanawa Island offer reefs in superb condition with reef sharks, turtles and schools of trevally. Komodo Park's marine diversity is among the richest in Southeast Asia — more than 1,000 fish species have been recorded within the park.

⏱ 120 min

Return to Labuan Bajo, fish dinner at the port. Free evening to prepare for the big push east: charge your devices, dig out warm clothes for Ruteng (altitude 1,200 m, cool nights) and arrange your transport for tomorrow morning.

⏱ 60 min

Ruteng

★★☆☆☆
1

Drive from Labuan Bajo to Ruteng (130 km, about 4 hours) on the Trans-Flores highway: the road climbs gradually from the port through forested hills, isolated villages and terraced rice paddies that signal your entry into Manggarai territory. Ruteng sits at 1,200 metres — you're finally out of the coastal heat. Settle in and breathe: the weather here is cooler, sometimes misty in the morning, and the evenings are fresh. The people of Ruteng are the Manggarai, proud of a thousand-year-old farming and warrior culture.

⏱ 60 min
2

The spider-web rice fields of Cancar, 16–17 km west of Ruteng, are a farming phenomenon unique in the world. The Manggarai people divide each cultivable valley from a sacred central point called a lodok — as you would slice a pie — allocating each family a portion according to its place within the clan. The result, seen from the surrounding heights, is a spectacular geometric pattern: perfect circles radiating outward like giant green spider webs. The division was once sanctified by the sacrifice of a buffalo. Each web (lingko) was created according to precise rituals. Go in the late afternoon for the low-angled light across the paddies.

⏱ 90 min
3

Ruteng Pu'u, a traditional village 4 km from Ruteng, preserves several ancestral Manggarai houses. These are small round structures arranged around a central courtyard with a sacred tree — the village ancestors are buried there. The place is low-key and rarely visited; if a resident invites you to explain the meaning of the houses and the rituals, it's a rare opportunity.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Ruteng market at the end of the day — tropical fruits, highland vegetables, Manggarai ikat textiles and medicinal herbs share the space. This is where you feel the contrast with Labuan Bajo: tourists are scarce, prices are local and the vendors are simply surprised to see you. It's also the place to witness the Caci dance — a ritual whip fight between adult men, accompanied by drums and gongs. It doesn't take place daily but during village festivals or on request.

⏱ 75 min

Hike up to the Golo Curu cross — a hill easily reached on foot from central Ruteng in 30 to 40 minutes. The panorama over the terraced rice paddies, scattered villages and surrounding mountains is excellent at sunset. The spot is popular with locals for pilgrimage and evening walks — a good way to connect with the people of Ruteng.

⏱ 90 min

Ruteng

★★☆☆☆
1

Full-day excursion to Wae Rebo — the 'village above the clouds'. This Manggarai village perched at 1,200 metres is accessible only on foot from the village of Denge (2.5 to 4 hours of walking depending on your pace), about a 3-hour drive from Ruteng. The trail passes through coffee, vanilla and cinnamon plantations, then enters dense tropical forest before opening onto a clearing: seven conical Mbaru Niang houses stand in a circle, their palm-leaf roofs woven from lontar fronds sweeping down almost to the ground. It is the only village in the entire Manggarai region to have kept all its traditional houses intact. Awarded a UNESCO prize in 2012, it is home today to the 18th generation of descendants of Empu Maro, the village's founder.

⏱ 240 min
2

At Wae Rebo, the Mbaru Niang are five-level conical houses — each level has a specific function: family living space (lutur), food store (lobo), seed storage (lentar), drought reserves (lempa rae) and offerings to the ancestors at the very top (hekang kode, the most sacred level). Building a single house mobilises the entire community for several weeks and requires the palm leaves to be replaced every 10 to 15 years. On arrival, a welcoming ritual (Waelu'u) is conducted by the village chief: you offer coffee or betel nuts, and he asks the ancestors to receive you. Do not photograph anything before this ritual.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Tasting Wae Rebo's robusta coffee — the villagers grow and roast their own beans in the plantations surrounding the village, by hand and in the traditional way. Watch the women weave their textiles and the children play in the central courtyard. The return along the same trail at sunset casts long light across the humid forest — clouds often cling to the foothills at this hour.

⏱ 90 min

On the way back to Ruteng, you can stop at Liang Bua cave, an archaeological site 30 km from Ruteng. This is where the bones of Homo floresiensis were discovered in 2003 — a dwarf human species nicknamed 'the Hobbit' by the press, which lived on the island roughly 50,000 to 100,000 years ago. A small on-site museum displays a replica of the skeleton and explains the discovery that upended theories of human evolution.

⏱ 60 min

Bajawa

★★☆☆☆
1

Drive from Ruteng to Bajawa (130 km, about 4.5 to 5 hours) on the Trans-Flores — the road is winding, spectacular and sometimes tough. It drops down to the sea at Aimere, passes through bamboo groves and colourful-housed villages before climbing back to the Ngada highlands. Bajawa stands at over 1,000 metres above sea level at the foot of Inerie volcano (2,245 m), an almost-perfect cone that dominates the town from the south. Arriving in clear weather, it's one of the most beautiful town entrances on Flores.

⏱ 60 min
2

Bajawa's central market is the town's beating heart — highland fruits, vegetables, dried fish and Ngada ikat textiles coexist in an organised chaos. The Ngada people (roughly 60,000 strong) live around Inerie volcano. They are Catholics — the Portuguese introduced the faith as early as the 16th century, which is why Flores means 'flower' in Portuguese — who still practise animist rites: buffalo sacrifices during ceremonies, divination by examining chicken entrails, and megalithic altars in village courtyards.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Malanage (or Mengeruda) hot springs, a 20-minute drive from Bajawa. Volcanic waters heated by Inerie gush into pools carved out of the tropical forest. The spot is popular with locals on Sundays and virtually deserted on weekdays. After 4.5 hours of winding road, a soak in these thermal waters is exactly what's needed.

⏱ 90 min

Sunset with a view of Inerie from the heights of Bajawa — the volcano turns red-orange while the savanna at its base darkens. If you're feeling adventurous, ask your accommodation about climbing Inerie (2,245 m, depart at 4 am, 5 to 7 hours round trip) — the summit view over all three coasts of Flores and the surrounding islands is breathtaking.

⏱ 75 min

Bajawa

★★☆☆☆
1

Bena, 13–15 km south of Bajawa (45 minutes by road), is the best-preserved and most visited Ngada village. According to legend, it was founded over 1,000 years ago by Javanese sailors shipwrecked on Flores' south coast, and it has never moved from its original site. The village consists of two rows of traditional houses with tall thatched roofs, separated by a central courtyard (kisanatha) lined with clan altars: the Ngadhu (a carved pole topped with a warrior figure representing the male ancestor) and the Bhaga (a miniature thatched house representing the female ancestor). Megalithic sacrifice tables dot the courtyard — skulls of sacrificed buffalo adorn the façades. Arrive early: the women weave their indigo ikats on the house perches from first light.

⏱ 120 min
2

Gurusina, 25 km beyond Bena (1.5 hours from Bajawa), is one of the Ngada villages least visited by tourists. Founded in 1934, it gathers 33 families from 3 clans in a setting of rice paddies and forests at the foot of Inerie. Each clan has its sa'o saka pu'u (original mother-house, recognisable by the miniature house on its roof) and its clan totems. If you make it here, there's a good chance you'll be the only foreign visitors.

⏱ 90 min
3

Luba, on the road between Bajawa and Bena, is worth a 30-to-40-minute stop. This traditional village shares Bena's Ngada architecture in a more modest and less touristy setting. You'll see the same megalithic structures, rough-stone altars and, if you're lucky enough to come on a Sunday, residents in traditional dress returning from mass — the mix of Catholicism and animism that defines the entire region.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Ikat-weaving workshop in Bajawa or Bena. Ngada women use natural indigo as their base colour — a regional speciality that distinguishes them from other Flores villages where the base is white. The motifs embroider stories: symbols of fertility (bhaga), protection (the lance, ghi-u) and social standing (gajah, reserved for the highest ranks). Watching an artisan work is a lesson in patience — a single cloth can take several weeks.

⏱ 60 min

Hike from Bena to Gurusina on foot (7 to 8 km) through the foothills of Inerie, bamboo forests, vanilla orchards and isolated villages. Arrange a pickup vehicle from Gurusina for the ride back to Bajawa. It's the best way to see the landscape between the two villages without being stuck in a car.

⏱ 180 min

Ende

★★☆☆☆
1

Drive from Bajawa to Ende (140 km, about 4 hours): the road descends to the south coast, passes through villages of the Lio region and skirts the Sawa Sea before climbing back to Ende. Stop at Penggajawa Beach along the way — a globally unique beach covered in blue-turquoise and green pebbles. These stones owe their rare colour to volcanic minerals (copper and iron oxides) deposited by successive eruptions. Locals collect them and sell them as building material — they're exported as far as Japan.

⏱ 45 min
2

Soekarno's exile house in Ende is the most tangible testimony to the resistance of Indonesia's future first president against Dutch colonisation. Soekarno was banished to Ende from 1934 to 1938 by the Netherlands, who considered him a dangerous nationalist agitator. It was in this modest dwelling that he developed the philosophy of Pancasila — the five founding principles of the Indonesian state. After independence, he personally asked the owner to turn the house into a museum.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Taman Renungan Bung Karno (Bung Karno's Meditation Garden) is the park where Soekarno would sit beneath a large breadfruit tree (pohon sukun) facing the sea to reflect on the ideology of an independent Indonesia. It was in this garden that the five principles of Pancasila took their final form. The site is calm and rarely visited — a meditative stop in the footsteps of the founder of a nation of 270 million.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Ende ikat market — the town is one of the most important centres for Lio ikat weaving. Lio motifs differ from Ngada or Manggarai ones: more geometric, often in red and black on a brown background, loaded with symbolism tied to ancestors and life cycles. The best pieces are in the shops around the central market; look for artisans selling their own work.

⏱ 75 min

Dinner in Ende overlooking the bay. The town, squeezed between Iya volcano and the Sawa Sea, has the feel of a modest but not charmless port. The waterfront warungs serve fish grilled with Flores chilli sauce — spicier than in the west of the island.

⏱ 60 min

Ende

★★☆☆☆
1

The traditional village of Wologai, 50 km west of Ende on the road to Bajawa, is inhabited by the Lio people — distinct from the Ngada in architecture and customs. The houses are arranged in a circle around a sacred central space (tubu kanga) where offerings to the ancestors are placed. Before building a house, residents perform the Naka Wisu ritual: they fell trees in the forest at midnight and cut a chicken. Few tourists make it here — you'll often be alone.

⏱ 90 min
2

The terraced rice fields of Detusoko, halfway between Ende and Moni, offer a panorama of the forested hills of central Flores. The Lio region's farming landscapes are less spectacular than those of Manggarai but more intimate — each valley holds one or two villages linked by footpaths through the fields.

⏱ 45 min
3

The village of Ndona, a few kilometres from Ende, is known for its ikat-weaving cooperative. The artisans produce quality pieces using traditional natural dyes — indigo, tree bark, cocoa. The cooperative provides direct income to the village women and has kept alive a craft that was on the verge of disappearing in the 1990s.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Climb to the Nuabosi viewpoint, a hill overlooking Ende and its bay. On a clear day, you can see Iya volcano to the south, the Sawa Sea and the hills rolling towards Moni. The spot is popular with students and families at the end of the day — a nice way to meet ordinary Endenese.

⏱ 60 min

Evening in Ende before the early-morning departure for Moni and Kelimutu. Local drivers usually set the wake-up call for 3:30–4 am to reach Moni in time to climb the volcano at sunrise. Get to bed early — tomorrow starts before dawn.

Moni

★★★☆☆
1

Rise at 4 am to be at the Kelimutu car park by 5:30 — the drive from Moni takes 30 to 40 minutes on the 'Snake Road' (a hairpin-bend route). From the car park, a 15-to-20-minute walk on a gently sloping marked trail leads to the crater rims. Kelimutu tops out at 1,639 metres and last erupted in June 1968. Its three crater lakes — whose name means 'boiling mountain' in the Lio language — change colour independently according to the dissolved volcanic gases: at your visit they might be vivid turquoise, blue-black, reddish-brown, acid green or even black — no two visits are the same.

⏱ 120 min
2

The three lakes carry names in the Lio language reflecting local beliefs: Tiwu Ata Mbupu (Lake of the Souls of the Ancestors), Tiwu Nuwa Muri Koo Fai (Lake of the Souls of the Young) and Tiwu Ata Polo (Lake of the Bewitched Souls). The two closest are separated by a mere serrated stone rampart; they face each other like twins of opposite character. August 14 is a sacred day for the Lio people — thousands of pilgrims climb to bring offerings to the craters.

⏱ 60 min
3

The village of Moni, 12 km from Kelimutu, is the base for the excursion. It's a tiny hamlet on the Trans-Flores with a handful of guesthouses and warungs. Its only other attraction is a waterfall reachable on foot from the main road — ice-cold water guaranteed, perfect for waking up after a short night.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Traditional Lio dance performance at a restaurant or homestay in Moni — some organise cultural evenings. The dances of the Lio region are less well known than Bali's Kecak or Legong, but they're every bit as transporting: slow, precise hand movements, costumes of indigo ikat cotton.

⏱ 90 min

Hike through the rice paddies around Moni in the late morning — unmarked trails lead to neighbouring villages in 1 to 2 hours. The landscapes are gentle, rolling, surrounded by forested mountains. Ask your accommodation to point out a route that crosses at least one inhabited village.

⏱ 120 min

Moni

★★★☆☆
1

Second ascent of Kelimutu, this time in the late afternoon for sunset — a radically different experience from sunrise: you'll be almost alone (most visitors come in the morning), the lake colours under low-angled light are even more intense, and the interplay of shadow between the two side-by-side craters offers photographic contrasts that the morning doesn't allow. ⏱ 90 min
2

Exploration of Lio villages around Moni — Saga Village, Jopu or other hamlets nearby, reachable on foot or by motorbike. These are Catholic-animist communities perpetuating traditional architectural forms less spectacular than Bena but authentic: stilt houses, family altars, vegetable gardens on the hillsides.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Extended hike through the highlands around Moni — the terrain around Kelimutu offers trails through humid tropical forest, bamboo groves and small streams. On a clear day, the view of the two neighbouring volcanic peaks (Kelido and Kelibara) from the ridges is remarkable. Ask the national-park staff for access to less-frequented trails.

⏱ 180 min

Evening in Moni before tomorrow's departure for Maumere. The village is human-sized, with no tourist frenzy — one of the rare spots on Flores where you eat at the table of the family hosting you. Take the chance to ask your host about their Kelimutu stories and local beliefs about the souls of the ancestors.

Maumere

★★☆☆☆
1

Drive from Moni to Maumere (95 km, 2.5 to 3 hours) with a mandatory stop at Koka Beach — two coves separated by a rocky headland, with white sand and turquoise water framed by lush green hills. The beach is on the south coast, 2 km off the main road. By most accounts of travellers who have crossed the island, it's the finest beach in the Maumere area. Minimal infrastructure — bring water and food.

⏱ 120 min
2

Maumere, capital of the Sikka district and Flores' second city, was 90% destroyed by a magnitude-7.7 earthquake and the tsunami of 12 December 1992, which killed around 1,500 people and devastated coastal infrastructure. The town has quietly rebuilt itself, without tourist fanfare. Its central market remains lively and authentic — Sikka ikat textiles in warmer tones than those of the Lio region, fresh fish from the gulf and highland produce.

⏱ 60 min
3

The beaches of Maumere Bay — Waiara, Nangahale, Kajuwulu — offer quality shore-based snorkelling, with coral reefs recovering from the damage of blast fishing in the 1990s and 2000s. The underwater life is abundant: clownfish, turtles, stingrays and starfish.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Snorkelling in Maumere Bay from Waiara beach or by boat excursion to Babi and Pangabatang islands — these rocky islets offshore have seabeds largely untouched by industrial fishing. Seagrass and corals are still intact. Between November and December, whales regularly visit the gulf.

⏱ 180 min

Fresh-fish dinner on Maumere Bay — the waterfront warungs put on a simple and endearing show in the evening: boats coming in with the day's catch, lights flickering on across the fishermen's houses on the far side of the bay, and a sky that reminds you Flores has been far from mass tourism for a very long time.

⏱ 75 min

Maumere

★★☆☆☆

Mount Egon (1,703 m), 35 km east of Maumere, is an active volcano less visited than Kelimutu but more physically demanding. The 350-metre-wide crater, which holds a small volcanic lake in the rainy season, offers spectacular views of Maumere Bay and the surrounding islands from its summit. The hike takes 3 to 4 hours round trip from the departure village. The lower slopes cross dense forest before giving way to a lunar landscape of strangely coloured rock.

⏱ 240 min
2

Jopu village or other Lio communities reachable in a half-day from Maumere — the inhabitants carry on the weaving of Sikka ikat, whose patterns differ from those of other Flores regions (dominant brick-red and orange on a black background). These textiles are worn during ceremonies and weddings.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Boat excursion in Maumere Bay to Babi and Pangabatang islands — the beaches on these little-visited islets offer outstanding snorkelling in clear water. A full day by boat lets you combine 2 to 3 snorkelling sites with a beach lunch on an island.

⏱ 300 min

A wander through central Maumere in the evening — the town isn't pretty, but it's lively and sincere. Families gather around the night markets, students from the local university fill the cafés and fishermen ready their nets for the night. This is everyday Flores, a long way from Komodo-for-tourists-in-a-hurry.

Maumere

★★☆☆☆
1

Last morning in Maumere before departure. Depending on your flight time from Frans Seda Airport (MOF), use a final hour at the market to buy Sikka ikat pieces directly from the artisans — they often sell at the central market in the morning. The airport is 3 km from the town centre.

⏱ 75 min
2

The village of Nita, 15 km west of Maumere, is one of the most active Sikka ikat weaving centres in eastern Flores. The Sikka people produce pieces dominated by brick-red and vivid orange on a black background, with motifs inspired by sea spirits and ancestors — an aesthetic distinct from the indigo ikat of Bena or the Lio patterns of Ende. The women work on traditional looms under the house porches, visible from the road. Buying directly at source is cheaper than in the Maumere shops, and the quality of unmediated pieces is generally higher.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Final local meal before your flight — the warungs around Maumere market serve breakfasts of rice, stir-fried noodles and fish soup. Locally hand-roasted Flores coffee is especially good in this region. A last cup and a handshake with the market regulars will bring a fitting close to 14 days crossing Flores.

⏱ 60 min

Transfer to Frans Seda Airport (MOF), 3 km from the centre — allow 2 hours before your flight. The airport serves Kupang (West Timor), Ende and Labuan Bajo; for Bali or Java, a connecting flight is usually required. Check schedules well in advance: rotations on Flores are limited and may change with the season. The journey ends here after 14 days of winding roads, mountain trails and ferries — you'll have crossed Flores end to end, as very few travellers do.
