Alto Paraiso

★★☆☆☆
1

Leave Brasília early in the morning for the 230-kilometer drive north to Alto Paraíso—about 3.5 hours via BR-020 and then GO-118. The town sits precisely on the 14th parallel south, the same latitude as Machu Picchu, and has lived largely on spiritual tourism since the 1980s. Drop your bags at your pousada in early afternoon: the town center spans just two streets, but this will be your base for the days ahead. Fill up on fuel and withdraw cash as soon as you arrive—gas stations become scarce as you head toward Cavalcante.

⏱ 45 min
2

Mirante da Janela offers a stunning view down onto the twin waterfalls of Saltos do Rio Preto—80 and 120 meters high, which you'll see up close the following day. The viewpoint sits on private property 8 kilometers from Alto Paraíso, accessible via a dirt track. Count on 2 kilometers of easy walking from the parking area. Go in late afternoon: the golden light hits the waterfall and canyon downstream perfectly. Bring a headlamp for the return if you linger until sunset.

⏱ 90 min
3

Built around spiritual tourism in the 1980s, Alto Paraíso has kept its village heart. The Saturday morning organic market (if you time it right) offers cerrado fruits: pequi, baru, cagaita. Otherwise, wander the main street and choose one of the restaurants to try galinhada—the chicken-and-rice dish typical of Goiás—or peixe na telha, fish grilled on a clay tile. Several spots also serve vegetarian dishes, a legacy of the alternative community that settled here.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

To get into the rhythm on your first day, head to Cachoeira dos Cristais. It's 8 kilometers from town center on private property, with an easy 700-meter trail. Quartz crystals scattered across the riverbed give the water a silvery shimmer. The pool is shallow, perfect for a quick dip after the drive. Entry runs around 30 R$. Arrive late afternoon and you'll have the place almost to yourself.

⏱ 90 min

Before or after the mirante, settle onto the main street to sample local flavors. Pequi—a yellow, spiny fruit with a distinctive aroma—divides travelers: some love it, others hate it. Try it in arroz com pequi for a gentle introduction. Craft beers from São Bento microbrewery, brewed 8 kilometers from town, are served in several village bars.

⏱ 60 min

Alto Paraiso

★★☆☆☆
1

Head to Fazenda São Bento, 8 kilometers from Alto Paraíso on GO-239. This private property concentrates three major waterfalls and infrastructure rare in these parts: buffet restaurant, shaded parking, craft microbrewery. Combined entry costs 70 R$. Arrive at opening time (8:30 AM) to enjoy Almécegas I before the heat and tour bus crowds. Keep your ticket—it's valid for all three waterfalls today.

⏱ 30 min
2

Cachoeira Almécegas I makes you earn it. From the parking lot, count on 1.5 kilometers of steep, rocky descent to a wooden viewpoint, then another 600 meters to reach the pool at the waterfall's base. The cascade drops roughly 45 meters down a quartzite wall, with water that stays ice-cold even in high summer. The return is a real climb—take your time, benches punctuate the ascent. Skip the flip-flops; several passages are slippery.

⏱ 150 min
3

Jardim de Maytrea stretches along the roadside on the way to São Jorge. It's a wetland dotted with buriti palms—those trees with bright orange fruit you'll see everywhere in the cerrado—serving as a natural water reservoir for the entire region. Fifteen to twenty minutes is enough for the walk. Sunset over the buritis is worth catching. Local legend holds that the name comes from a spiritual vision an inhabitant had in the 1990s.

⏱ 30 min
4

Vale da Lua isn't like other waterfalls. The Rio São Miguel has spent millions of years carving through carbonate-matrix conglomerate, creating craters, tunnels, and smooth bathtubs that resemble a lunar surface. The rocks range from 500 million to 2.1 billion years old depending on the layer. Count on 1.2 kilometers of easy trail from the parking area, then watch your step on wet stones. Entry is private, around 50 R$. Avoid the rainy season—flash floods are deadly.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

On the way back toward Fazenda São Bento, take advantage of Poço São Bento. The trail runs 500 meters, flat, accessible to children. The pool is wide and shallow, with an 8-meter waterfall perfect for cooling off after Almécegas I. This is also where the fazenda's annual water polo tournament takes place. Buffet at the pousada right next door for 90 R$—wood-fired comida mineira.

⏱ 90 min

If you still have energy late afternoon, Fazenda São Bento offers the park's longest zipline, the Voo do Gavião: 850 meters of cable, 100 meters high, at 55 km/h. Two positions available: seated (around 200 R$) or lying down Superman-style (around 250 R$). The activity runs weekends from 9 AM to 4 PM, or weekdays by reservation for groups of six minimum. Otherwise, head back to Alto Paraíso at a leisurely pace and treat yourself to a massage at your pousada—the local specialty.

⏱ 60 min

Sao Jorge

★★★☆☆
1

Leave Alto Paraíso for São Jorge, the small gateway village to the national park, 36 kilometers away on GO-239. The streets are red dirt, the pousadas rustic, the vibe hippie-eco. Drop your things and walk five minutes to the visitor center: this is where you buy your park ticket (47 R$ standard rate, book online at Parquetur's site), watch the mandatory orientation video, and sign the waiver. The park opens 8 AM to 6 PM, last entry around noon.

⏱ 60 min
2

Trilha dos Saltos is the park's grand classic. Roughly 10 to 11 kilometers round trip for a full day out. The viewpoint for Salto 120 comes after 4 kilometers of walking: the waterfall drops in a vertical ribbon 120 meters into a canyon you'll only see from above, the pool being inaccessible. Continue 800 meters to Salto 80, whose wide, deep pool allows swimming. The water is ice-cold, invigorating after the hike. Follow the yellow arrows all the way.

⏱ 240 min
3

Before heading back, push on to the Corredeiras do Rio Preto. A flat 1.5-kilometer trail from Salto 80 leads to a series of small cascading pools—natural hydromassage where the water runs less cold than elsewhere. A boardwalk accessible to people with reduced mobility was built in 2019. Park guides advise skipping the 12–2 PM portion on the return: with no shade, the sun beats down hard.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Evening in São Jorge slows to a complete halt. Go for dinner at Risoteria Santo Cerrado, which serves risottos made with cerrado ingredients (pequi, baru). The village has only about ten restaurants, all within walking distance of each other. No nightclub, no shopping center—just campfires at certain pousadas and an exceptional starry sky thanks to zero light pollution.

⏱ 90 min

The park offers visitation noturna Wednesday through Sunday evenings: groups of six maximum, PNCV-accredited guide, headlamps mandatory (phones are not allowed). The sky here, far from cities, reveals the Milky Way to the naked eye. Book in advance at the visitor center—the ticket must be purchased before the ticket office closes.

⏱ 120 min

Sao Jorge

★★★☆☆
1

Second entry into the park, this time via the trilha vermelha—red arrows. Head for Cânions I and II, two gorges carved by the Rio Preto where water flows between quartzite walls. Cânion II offers a large swimming pool, deep and ice-cold, perfect for a plunge after the approach walk (4 kilometers from the entrance). Cânion I is a bit higher up the trail, with choppier water. Both are on the same loop.

⏱ 180 min
2

Virtually on the same loop, Cachoeira das Cariocas strings together several small cascades and stepped pools. The trail leading there extends the canyon route; count on 30 minutes of additional walking. This is where you'll encounter the fewest people in the park: groups usually stay at Cânion II. Pack a snack—the spot invites lingering.

⏱ 90 min
3

On the return, make a detour via Trilha do Cerrado (Seriema), a short, flat 800-meter path through plants typical of the biome: ipê, lobeira, paepalanthus. A local guide will explain, if you've hired one, why the cerrado is one of the world's oldest and most diverse tropical ecosystems, with 350 to 400 vascular plant species per hectare. It's also a good spot for birdwatching late afternoon.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

To break the rhythm, settle in at Mansa e Brava, an accessible stretch of the Rio São Miguel 10 minutes by car from São Jorge. Pebble beaches, shallow water on the "Mansa" side, playful currents on the "Brava" side. Brazilian families spend the afternoon here with coolers and ice-cold Coke. No entry fee. Ideal for resting your legs after two days of trilhas.

⏱ 120 min

In the evening, ask your pousada to arrange an appointment with a local therapist for a bamboo massage or herbal bath with cerrado plants. Healing practices have been an integral part of Alto Paraíso and São Jorge's identity since the 1990s, inherited from the alternative communities that settled on the fourteenth parallel.

⏱ 90 min

Cavalcante

★★☆☆☆
1

The drive to Cavalcante takes 2.5 to 3 hours from São Jorge: loop back through Alto Paraíso, then take GO-118 south to town. The dirt track from Cavalcante toward the Kalunga community of Engenho II adds another 27 kilometers, in fair condition—count on an hour in a standard car, more in rainy season. Fill up on gas in Cavalcante and withdraw cash: Engenho II has neither ATM nor fuel pump, and some merchants don't accept cards.

⏱ 60 min
2

Engenho II is one of 39 hamlets in Kalunga territory, Brazil's largest quilombola community. The Kalunga descend from enslaved people who escaped Goiás gold mines in the 18th century, took refuge in these isolated valleys, and lived there for three centuries in near obscurity—anthropologist Maria Baiocchi from UFG "rediscovered" them in 1982. The territory covers 262,000 hectares, designated Sítio Histórico e Patrimônio Cultural Kalunga by Goiás State in 1991, and the process for national cultural heritage designation was launched by IPHAN in March 2026. At the CAT (tourist center), you pay entry and hire a mandatory local guide.

⏱ 60 min
3

The turquoise waters of Cachoeira Santa Bárbara alone explain why you made this entire drive. The waterfall stands 30 meters high, the pool 4 meters deep—learn to swim beforehand or rent a life jacket. The trail runs an easy 1.5 kilometers, after a 4-kilometer transfer in a local pau-de-arara cattle truck (10 R$ round trip). Arrive before 11 AM to catch the sun head-on in the water and capture maximum blue. After 2 PM, shade falls and the color dims.

⏱ 120 min
4

An 800-meter walk downstream, Cachoeira da Capivara is less crowded but splendid: a cascade over 40 meters into a large, deep pool formed at the confluence of several watercourses. The trail descends steeply for 800 meters with ropes to help. The guide will explain that "Kalunga" comes from Bantu and means "sacred place, place of protection"—an etymology claimed by the community.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Have lunch with a local family at Engenho II. Several families open their homes to visitors: everything is cooked over wood fire, most ingredients come from the garden or neighboring fields. Count on 25 to 40 R$ per dish, reserve through your guide early in the morning so they have time to prepare. Ask for the galinhada or grilled fish from the Rio Tocantins. Cerrado fruit juices (cajá, cagaita, pequi) are absolutely worth trying.

⏱ 90 min

If you have time late afternoon, make a quick stop at Mirante de Nova Aurora, 6 kilometers from Cavalcante toward Engenho II. The panoramic view embraces the entire chapada to the south, and sunsets are spectacular in dry season, when the air is charged with golden dust. No entry fee, no facilities—just the viewpoint and silence.

⏱ 60 min

Brasilia

★★★★☆
1

The return drive Cavalcante–Brasília runs about 320 kilometers; count on 4.5 to 5 hours leaving early. You'll loop back through Alto Paraíso then take GO-118 south to Formosa before joining BR-020. If your flight is late afternoon or evening, you'll have time for a half-day in Brasília. Otherwise, spend the night and catch your plane the next day. The federal capital, designed by Lúcio Costa and Oscar Niemeyer, was inaugurated in 1960 and inscribed on the UNESCO list in 1987.

⏱ 30 min
2

If you have 2–3 hours on-site, head to Praça dos Três Poderes to see the alignment of Palácio do Planalto, Supremo Tribunal Federal, and Congresso Nacional with its two inverted domes. Next, the Catedral Metropolitana, completed by Niemeyer in 1970: its 16 hyperbolic white concrete columns support a roof of stained glass designed by Marianne Peretti. Inside, Alfredo Ceschiatti's suspended angels float in colored light.

⏱ 90 min
3

Before the airport, make a final stop at Memorial JK to understand how Juscelino Kubitschek brought a capital up from red earth in less than four years, between 1956 and 1960. The president's mausoleum is displayed under a large glass cube, his tomb oriented toward Brasília. Thirty minutes is enough for the express visit. The airport is 15 minutes by car.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

For a final Brazilian meal before your flight, sit down at a churrascaria in the Asa Sul neighborhood. The rodízio—rotating meat service where you pay a fixed price and servers come to your table with skewers—is an institution. Count on 120 to 180 R$ per person. If you prefer something lighter, the food trucks at Pontão do Lago Sul serve good grilled fish with views over Lake Paranoá.

⏱ 90 min

If your flight is very late and you want one last breath of modern architecture, cross the Ponte JK over Lake Paranoá. Inaugurated in 2002, its three asymmetric arches of 240 meters' span each reflect in the water. It's the postcard image you bring home from Brasília. Small parking at each end for photos.

⏱ 30 min
