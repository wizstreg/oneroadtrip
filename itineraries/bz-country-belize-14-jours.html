San Pedro Town

★★★☆☆
1

Front Street (officially Barrier Reef Drive) is San Pedro's main artery, running parallel to the reef. This fishing village was founded between 1848 and 1849 by families fleeing the Caste War from Bacalar, Mexico, and has grown into the second-largest town in Belize District. Only three streets—Front, Middle, and Back—make up the center, where golf carts and bicycles rule the sandy roads. Wander down to the small Central Park and the old cemetery at the street's end.

⏱ 60 min
2

San Pedro's central beach faces directly onto the Mesoamerican Barrier Reef, visible from shore. This reef is the world's second-longest after Australia's Great Barrier Reef, stretching roughly 300 km along the Belizean coast. It lies less than a kilometer offshore—so close you can see the white line of waves breaking across it.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Rent a golf cart to explore Ambergris Caye. The island stretches about 40 km long and less than 2 km wide—it's Belize's largest caye. The north road extends over 15 km, passing a succession of beachfront resorts. Golf carts are the primary mode of transport here, with cars virtually nonexistent.

⏱ 60 min

Catch sunset from San Pedro's municipal pier or one of the waterfront docks on the west side. After dark, Front Street comes alive with live music spilling from beach bars. Ambergris Caye inspired the song 'La Isla Bonita'—the opening words mention San Pedro.

⏱ 45 min

San Pedro Town

★★★☆☆
1

Secret Beach sits on the leeward (western) coast of Ambergris Caye, about 12 km northwest of San Pedro. Until 2014, this shore was accessible only by boat. A road built through the mangrove lagoons opened up this side of the island. The water here is calm, shallow, and sargassum-free—unlike the reef side. Count on 30 to 40 minutes by golf cart from San Pedro.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Snorkel Hol Chan Marine Reserve and Shark Ray Alley. Hol Chan—'little channel' in Maya—was established in July 1987 as Belize's first marine protected area. It now covers over 41,000 hectares. Zone A centers on a natural break in the reef, 23 meters wide and 9 meters deep, where more than 160 fish species congregate. Shark Ray Alley (Zone D), added in 1999, is a shallow sandy bottom where fishermen once cleaned their nets—the scraps attracted nurse sharks and rays, which still return daily.

⏱ 180 min

Caye Caulker

★★★☆☆
1

The Split is the channel that cuts Caye Caulker in two. It was carved—or dramatically widened—by Hurricane Hattie on October 31, 1961, a Category 5 cyclone with 260 km/h winds that devastated British Honduras. After the storm passed, locals hand-dug the passage wider to let small boats through. Today the channel runs about 6 meters deep. It's the island's most popular swimming spot.

⏱ 60 min
2

Stroll through Caye Caulker village, whose unofficial motto is 'Go Slow'. The limestone island measures about 8 km long, and the village occupies barely 1.5 km. With roughly 1,800 residents, this former fishing community has become a beloved budget traveler destination. Sandy streets carry foot and bike traffic—no paved roads in the village. The island has no natural running water: residents depend on rainwater catchment cisterns.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Kayak or stand-up paddle in The Split channel. The water is clear and the current moderate. It's also a good spot to watch rays and small sharks from the surface, especially along the west-side docks.

⏱ 60 min

★★★☆☆
1

Docks and shore on Caye Caulker's western (leeward) side. These calm, shallow waters are prime for spotting stingrays from the edge. The island hosts over 120 bird species; the northern mangroves shelter numerous waders and migratory birds.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Snorkel the Caye Caulker Marine Reserve, a section of the Mesoamerican Barrier Reef reachable in 10 to 15 minutes by boat. The reef has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1996. Expect green turtles, groupers, barracuda, and brain and elkhorn corals. Shallow waters (1 to 5 meters in places) make it beginner-friendly.

Free morning on the island: swim from the docks, wander the sandy lanes, browse the small craft market. The island's south end offers quieter swimming spots than The Split.

Activities

Formed as a limestone cave during the ice ages, it collapsed and flooded when sea levels rose about 10,000 years ago. San Ignacio

★★★☆☆
1

The Belize Zoo, on the Western Highway roughly midway between Belize City and San Ignacio, is worth a stop. Founded in 1983, it houses only animals native to Belize—jaguars, Baird's tapirs (the national animal), keel-billed toucans, crocodiles—all rescued or born in captivity. The enclosures replicate their natural rainforest habitat.

⏱ 75 min
2

Cahal Pech—'place of the ticks' in Yucatec Maya (named for the cattle pastures that covered the site in the 1950s)—is one of the oldest known Maya sites in Belize. The earliest occupation dates to 1200–1000 BCE. The site has 34 structures arranged around seven plazas, including two ball courts and pyramid temples. Structure A1, the tallest, rises 23 meters. Perched on a hill less than 2 km from downtown San Ignacio, Cahal Pech offers panoramic views over the Belize Valley and the Maya Mountains to the south. The small on-site museum displays pre-Columbian figurines, obsidian tools, and a remarkable four-cavity shell inkwell still stained blue, red, and black.

⏱ 75 min
3

San Ignacio is the largest town in Cayo District and the country's second after Belize City. Situated about 110 km inland from the coast, on the Macal River, it's linked to its twin town Santa Elena by the Hawkesworth Bridge—Belize's only suspension bridge. Burns Avenue, the main pedestrian street, is lined with colorful shops, food stalls, and small galleries. The Saturday market draws farmers, Mennonite artisans, and vendors from across the region.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Travel Caye Caulker → Belize City by ferry (~45 min), then drive west on the Western Highway (George Price Highway) to San Ignacio (~2h30). This two-lane road crosses the coastal plains, passes the Belize Zoo at Mile 29, then continues through Belmopan before reaching Cayo District. Optional stop at the Belize Zoo en route.

⏱ 210 min

San Ignacio

★★★☆☆
1

Actun Tunichil Muknal—'cave of the stone sepulcher' in Maya—is a roughly 5 km underground network in the Tapir Mountain Nature Reserve, about 1.5 hours' drive from San Ignacio. Discovered by geomorphologist Thomas Miller in 1986 and scientifically explored from 1993 by Belizean archaeologist Jaime Awe, the cave opened to the public in 1998. The main network includes a 3.2 km river passage. The main chamber measures about 200 m long by 50 m wide. Fourteen individuals' skeletons have been recovered—seven children and seven adults—likely sacrificed between the 8th and 10th centuries to appease the rain god Chaac.

⏱ 60 min
2

The cave's most famous skeleton, nicknamed the 'Crystal Maiden' (initially thought female, likely a young man around 17), has rested for over 1,100 years in an upper chamber. Coated in calcite over the centuries, his bones have acquired a crystalline sheen. Two crushed vertebrae suggest a violent death. More than 1,400 artifacts have been catalogued in the cave—pottery sherds, water vessels, tools, stingray spines used in bloodletting rituals. Since 2012, cameras have been banned inside the cave after a tourist dropped his on a thousand-year-old skull.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Guided ATM Cave expedition (full day). Access begins with 45 minutes of forest hiking, three crossings of Roaring Creek, then swimming into the cave entrance. Inside, you spend three hours wading through water, scrambling over rocks, and squeezing through tight passages before reaching the dry chamber where the skeletons and artifacts lie. Tours are only possible with a licensed guide (groups limited to 8). You must remove shoes and wear socks in the upper chamber to protect the site.

⏱ 480 min

San Ignacio

★★★☆☆
1

Xunantunich—'stone woman' in Yucatec Maya—is a Classic Period archaeological site (600–890 CE) perched on a ridge above the Mopan River, 12 km from San Ignacio and 1 km from the Guatemalan border. To reach it, you cross the Mopan on a hand-cranked ferry from the village of San José Succotz. The site covers about 2.6 km² and includes six ceremonial plazas surrounded by some twenty temples and palaces. The site was discovered by Westerners in the late 19th century, when Irish surgeon Thomas Gann became district commissioner of Cayo in 1892. The first major excavations took place in 1959–60 by a Cambridge team led by Euan MacKie.

⏱ 75 min
2

El Castillo, Xunantunich's main pyramid, rises 40 meters—it's the second-tallest man-made structure in Belize, after Caana Pyramid at Caracol. Its facades are adorned with stucco friezes depicting astronomical symbols—sun, moon, Venus. The original east-facade frieze, dated around 800 CE, has been covered with an exact replica to protect it from the elements. From the summit, views extend over the Mopan Valley and into neighboring Guatemala. In 2016, Jaime Awe's team discovered beneath the temple the largest royal Maya tomb ever unearthed in Belize, containing the remains of a 20- to 30-year-old man accompanied by rich burial goods.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Barton Creek Cave by canoe in the afternoon. This river cave extends over 7 km beneath the limestone hills of Cayo District. Visitors paddle about 1.6 km into the underground river, lit by headlamps. The passages feel like cathedrals, with ceilings draped in stalactites. The Maya used this cave for ceremonial rituals between 250 and 900 CE: on ten rock ledges identified by archaeologists, human remains, pottery, corn-grinding stones, and a necklace of carved animal phalanges have been found. Belize's longest underground river accessible by canoe.

Hopkins Village

★★★★☆
1

Hopkins is considered the cultural heart of the Garifuna community in Belize. This coastal village of about 1,000 residents stretches nearly 2.5 km along a golden beach lined with coconut palms. The Garifuna descend from West African peoples and indigenous Kalinago (Carib) from the island of Saint Vincent. Exiled by the British in 1797, they settled along the coasts of Honduras, Guatemala, and Belize. The first Garifuna arrived in Belize in 1802—a date commemorated every November 19 on Garifuna Settlement Day. The present village of Hopkins was founded around 1942, when a hurricane destroyed the Newtown camp to the north and families relocated by dugout canoe.

⏱ 75 min
2

Walk the Hopkins waterfront. Unlike resort towns, the village has kept its authentic character: wooden fishing boats on the beach, nets drying under the palms, colorful wooden houses. The north and south ends of the village are separated by Hopkins Road (4.5 km of paved road from the Southern Highway). Garifuna language, music, and dance are inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Drive San Ignacio → Hopkins (~2h30). The Western Highway leads first to Belmopan (Belize's capital since 1970, built after Belize City's destruction by Hurricane Hattie in 1961). Then take the scenic Hummingbird Highway, crossing the rainforest and foothills of the Maya Mountains, with an optional stop at Blue Hole National Park (a freshwater sinkhole, different from the marine Blue Hole).

Hopkins Village

★★★★☆
1

Mayflower Bocawina National Park, created in 2001, protects 2,870 hectares of rainforest on the eastern slopes of the Maya Mountains, about twenty minutes' drive from Hopkins. The park shelters several waterfalls: Bocawina Falls (accessible via an easy, flat trail), Antelope Falls (steep climb with about 300 meters' elevation gain, rewarded by views over the canopy to the Caribbean Sea), and Big Drop Falls (for experienced hikers). It also holds three un-excavated Maya sites—Mayflower Plaza, Maintzunun, and T'au Witz—whose mounds remain jungle-cloaked. Over 200 bird species have been recorded in the park, including the keel-billed toucan and great curassow.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hike Mayflower Bocawina Park in the morning—trail to Bocawina Falls (easy, ~30 min) or Antelope Falls (challenging, ~1h30 one-way). Swim in the natural pools at the base of the falls.

⏱ 180 min

Garifuna drumming workshop in late afternoon at Hopkins. Garifuna drums are carved from hollowed hardwood (mahogany or mayflower) and covered with peccary hide. The primero (tenor drum, melodic) and the segunda (bass, rhythmic) are the two main types. Their polyrhythms accompany traditional dances: punta (fast, festive), paranda (acoustic guitar and melancholy songs), and jankunu (masked dance, Christmas season). A drum center was founded in Hopkins in 2002 to pass this heritage to village youth.

Placencia

★★★☆☆
1

The Placencia Sidewalk, 1,240 meters (4,071 feet) long and barely 1.2 meters wide, is known as the world's narrowest main street—a distinction long attributed by the Guinness Book (though the official record now belongs to a Reutlingen, Germany alley). Originally it was a simple fishermen's path for wheeling fish without bogging down in the sand. The concrete version was commissioned by an Anglican bishop who wanted a walkable path between his house and the church. Vehicles are banned; the walkway is lined with art galleries, craft shops, and small restaurants.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Placencia Peninsula stretches 26 km along Belize's southern coast, between the Caribbean Sea to the east and a mangrove lagoon to the west. It's the only spot on the Belizean mainland offering true white-sand beaches. The village of Placencia, at the southern tip, was an isolated fishing hamlet accessible only by sea until 1984, when the road linking it to the Southern Highway was built. Hurricane Iris (Category 3) destroyed 95% of the village's buildings in October 2001, but the community rebuilt with determination.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Transfer Hopkins → Placencia by coastal road (~50 min). The route follows the Stann Creek District coast, passing through the Garifuna village of Seine Bight before reaching Placencia.
