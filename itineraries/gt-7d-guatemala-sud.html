Guatemala

An overlooked capital where colonial palaces and world-class Maya museums await

★★★★☆
📍 Palacio Nacional de la Cultura Guatemala City📍 Plaza de la Constitución Guatemala City📍 Catedral Metropolitana Guatemala City📍 Museo Nacional de Arqueología y Etnología Guatemala City📍 Museo Popol Vuh Guatemala City📍 Torre del Reformador Guatemala City
1

Arrive at La Aurora international airport and get your first taste of the capital. Guatemala City is the largest metropolis in Central America — don't let its reputation put you off. Zona 1, the historic centre, is well worth half a day. Everything revolves around the Plaza de la Constitución, where the Palacio Nacional de la Cultura (open for visits) showcases the country's early 20th-century official architecture. Allow 45 minutes to walk through its rooms.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Catedral Metropolitana flanks the north side of the square: its twin bell towers and neoclassical-baroque facade have survived several earthquakes since the 18th century. Step through the main entrance to see gilded altarpieces and colonial paintings — the interior is far more ornate than the exterior suggests.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Museo Popol Vuh, housed on the campus of Universidad Francisco Marroquín, holds one of the largest collections of Maya art on the continent. Expect pre-Hispanic ceramics, funerary masks, and a recreation of the Book of the Popol Vuh — the Maya cosmogonic text discovered in Chichicastenango in the 18th century. Allow at least 1h30 to see it without rushing.

⏱ 90 min
4

The Torre del Reformador in Zona 9 is an unmistakable nod to the Eiffel Tower — same lattice steel structure, commissioned in 1935 to honour Justo Rufino Barrios, the 'reformer president'. It's viewed from the outside; mostly a good excuse to stroll through this quieter, more residential neighbourhood.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Dive into the pedestrianised 6th Avenue in Zona 1 — this is where Guatemala City shows its true colours: street vendors, open-air markets, locals heading home from work. The Mercado Central, just behind the cathedral, is the perfect spot for a first local meal (chicken pepián, tamales) and a glimpse of everyday life with no tourist filter.

⏱ 75 min

In the evening, if you're staying in Zona 10, head to the Paseo de la Reforma for a drink on a terrace. This upscale district is a world apart from the historic centre — high-rises, contemporary art galleries, restaurants. A good place to unwind and plan the days ahead.

Stop 2

Antigua

A baroque royal capital frozen in time, three volcanoes as its backdrop, UNESCO-listed since 1979

★★★★★
📍 Arco de Santa Catalina Antigua Guatemala📍 Parque Central Antigua Guatemala📍 Catedral de Santiago Antigua Guatemala📍 Iglesia La Merced Antigua Guatemala📍 Convento Las Capuchinas Antigua Guatemala📍 Convento Santa Clara Antigua Guatemala📍 Cerro de la Cruz Antigua Guatemala
1

Antigua was founded in 1543 as Santiago de los Caballeros de Guatemala — Bernal Díaz del Castillo, Cortés's former companion, served as its governor for years. A UNESCO World Heritage Site, the city spreads across the Panchoy Valley, framed by three volcanoes: Agua (3,760 m, dormant), Fuego (3,763 m, active), and Acatenango (3,976 m). Start at the Parque Central: surrounding it you'll find the Catedral de Santiago (only its grand facade remains after the 1773 earthquakes), the Palace of the Captains General, and the City Hall. Take a moment to sit on a bench and soak it in — this is when the locals come together.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Arco de Santa Catalina is the city's most iconic landmark: this saffron-yellow arch was built in 1694 to let cloistered nuns from the convent cross the street unseen. The clock crowning it is a Lamy & Lacroix made in France, added in the 1830s — it's still wound by hand every three days. Get there before 8 AM to avoid the tour groups and catch Volcán Agua framed in the background. From Calle del Arco, you also get a clear line of sight to La Merced church.

⏱ 30 min
3

Iglesia La Merced, instantly recognisable by its bright yellow and white facade in the Spanish baroque style, is one of the best-preserved churches in Antigua. Its cloister houses a fountain 27 metres in diameter — the largest in Central America. Climb to the roof for a view over the fountain and the city's tiled rooftops.

⏱ 40 min
4

The Convento de Las Capuchinas, founded in 1736, is one of the best-preserved ruined convents in the city. Its circular galleries of cells arranged around a central tower are unique in colonial American architecture. Light filters through the collapsed vaults — the atmosphere is striking. Allow 45 minutes.

⏱ 45 min
5

Head up to Cerro de la Cruz in the late afternoon: this viewpoint north of the city offers the finest panorama of Antigua and all three volcanoes. The climb from the centre takes about 20 minutes on foot. Time it for golden hour — the volcanoes take on an orange glow that contrasts beautifully with the city's red-tiled roofs.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Wander the cobblestoned alleys between two ruined convents: Santa Clara (founded in 1700 — its collapsed arches frame the sky like perfect oculi) and Santo Domingo (destroyed in the 1773 earthquake, its interior gardens partly repurposed). Both sites can be covered in about an hour and a half total.

⏱ 90 min

Local operators offer Guatemalan cooking classes or food tours through the streets of Antigua — a great way to learn the basics of the local cuisine (pepián, kak'ik, hand-pressed tortillas) alongside locals. Failing that, the Mercado Municipal, steps from the centre, is ideal for a no-frills lunch.

⏱ 120 min

★★★★★

1

Hobbitenango is an ecological park perched in the hills above Antigua, designed as a nod to the world of Tolkien — round houses, gentle sloping paths through the pines, and unobstructed views over all three volcanoes and the valley below. It's mainly a good excuse to take a breath and enjoy the scenery without any effort. Allow 1 hour on site.

⏱ 60 min
2

Back from Pacaya in the late afternoon, make a final detour to Convento Santo Domingo — the largest convent in Antigua, whose construction dates back to the Dominicans who arrived in Guatemala in 1538. Destroyed by the 1773 earthquake, its ruins now house a museum and interior gardens. The collapsed nave is one of the most photographed spots in the city: the open ceiling lets light pour in as if it were an open-air cathedral. Paid entry; allow 40 minutes.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

The hike up Pacaya is the most accessible volcanic excursion in Guatemala. The volcano stands at 2,552 metres and remains one of the country's three active volcanoes alongside Fuego and Santiaguito. From the entrance of the Parque Nacional Volcán Pacaya (at San Francisco de Sales, 1 hour from Antigua), the trail loops 5.6 km with 453 metres of elevation gain — allow 2h30 to 3h round trip. The pine forest gradually gives way to a black, lunar landscape of solidified lava. At the summit, fissures release sulphurous heat — guides will offer to roast marshmallows on sticks. The views from the top take in Agua, Fuego, and Acatenango. Leave early in the morning (before 11 AM the cone is often shrouded in cloud). A guide is mandatory on site.

⏱ 240 min

Back in Antigua in the evening, a night-time stroll through the lamp-lit cobblestone streets reveals a different city entirely: the coloured facades take on coppery tones under the lantern light. A terrace at one of the cafés around the Parque Central is perfect for a last cold beer before heading to the lake the next day.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 4

Panajachel

Gateway to Lake Atitlán, the perfect base for exploring the lakeside Maya villages

★★☆☆☆
📍 Indian Nose Rostro Maya San Juan La Laguna📍 Lago Atitlán Panajachel📍 Calle Santander Panajachel📍 Embarcadero Panajachel Lago Atitlán
1

Lake Atitlán formed roughly 84,000 years ago when a volcanic explosion carved out a caldera several kilometres wide. Today, at 335 metres deep, it is the deepest lake in Central America. Three volcanoes tower over its shores: San Pedro (3,020 m), Tolimán (3,158 m), and Atitlán (3,537 m). Panajachel is the main gateway — Calle Santander, a pedestrianised street lined with handicraft shops, leads straight to the dock. Don't worry if Panajachel feels overly touristy: it's a launchpad, not a destination in itself.

⏱ 45 min
2

Santa Cruz La Laguna is reachable by lancha from Panajachel (15 minutes) and offers one of the lake's most peaceful panoramas — a small village clinging to a cliff, accessible only by water or via a steep trail from above. No paved road, no cars: just stone houses, lake-facing terraces, and dense vegetation. It's the favourite village of those who want to see the lake without the crowds. Allow an hour and a half on site.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Pre-dawn wake-up for the Indian Nose (Rostro Maya / Nariz del Indio) — this is the defining experience of Lake Atitlán. The 2,093-metre summit above San Juan La Laguna offers a 360° view of the lake and its volcanoes at sunrise. Two options: hike up from San Juan (about 2h30, 492 m of elevation gain) or take a shuttle from Panajachel or San Pedro to Santa Clara (only 30 minutes of climbing, but steeper). Leave at 4 AM to reach the top for golden hour. Going with a guide or local agency is strongly recommended — the trail crosses private land and solo hikers have reported safety issues on certain stretches at night.

⏱ 240 min

Return to the lake in the morning by lancha shuttle from the Panajachel dock to San Pedro La Laguna or San Juan La Laguna. These small collective boats run regularly, connecting villages in 20 to 30 minutes depending on the destination. A lazy afternoon by the water is a welcome reward after the early rise — kayaks and stand-up paddleboards are available for rent in most villages.

Panajachel

Gateway to Lake Atitlán, the perfect base for exploring the lakeside Maya villages

★★☆☆☆
📍 Santiago Atitlan Lago Atitlán Guatemala📍 Iglesia Parroquial Santiago Apostol Santiago Atitlan📍 San Juan La Laguna Guatemala📍 Cooperativa tejedoras San Juan La Laguna📍 Chocolate artesanal San Juan La Laguna
1

Santiago Atitlán is the capital of the Tz'utujil Maya — the lake's largest indigenous community, whose history stretches back to 900 BC. Take the lancha from Panajachel (25 minutes). As soon as you step off the dock, children will offer to guide you to Maximón: accept — it's the best way to find him. Maximón (also known as San Simón) is a syncretic Maya-Catholic deity whose coral-wood effigy, about one metre tall, is dressed in costumes and 36 neckties. The figure is moved each year to a new family's home — a tremendous honour for the household that receives it. Devotees offer alcohol and cigarettes in exchange for protection. The scene is strange, vivid, and entirely authentic.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Iglesia Parroquial Santiago Apóstol, built by the Franciscans in 1541, is one of Guatemala's oldest churches. On the entrance walls, plaques commemorate the 'Martyrs of Santiago', including Father Stanley Rother, an American missionary assassinated by paramilitaries in 1981 — his heart was buried here at the request of the Tz'utujil community. Inside, statues of saints are dressed each year by village women in handwoven traditional garments.

⏱ 40 min
3

San Juan La Laguna, a 15-minute lancha ride from Santiago, is the lake's artisan village: its narrow streets are covered in murals depicting Tz'utujil cosmology, and several women's cooperatives keep alive the tradition of backstrap-loom weaving with natural dyes (the red colour comes from crushed cochineal insects). Step into one of the workshops for a live demonstration — it's free or nearly so.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

In San Juan La Laguna, an artisanal chocolate workshop lets you follow the entire process: from cacao pod to finished bar. Cacao cultivation in the region is an ancestral practice among the Tz'utujil Maya. Allow 30 to 45 minutes for the tour and tasting.

⏱ 45 min

Melipona bees — the stingless bees sacred to the Maya — are kept in a handful of gardens in San Juan. Local operators offer visits to the apiary and tastings of various organic honeys. An unexpected curiosity, and a way to directly support community families.

⏱ 45 min

Head back to Panajachel by lancha in the late afternoon. Catch the sunset from the dock or from one of the rooftop terraces on Calle Santander — the volcanoes silhouetted against the last orange glow in the sky.

Antigua

A baroque royal capital frozen in time, three volcanoes as its backdrop, UNESCO-listed since 1979

★★★★★
📍 Mercado de Chichicastenango Guatemala📍 Iglesia Santo Tomás Chichicastenango📍 Pascual Abaj Chichicastenango📍 Cementerio Chichicastenango
1

Chichicastenango (or 'Chichi') sits at 1,965 metres in the Quiché highlands, a 1h30 drive from Panajachel. Its market, one of the largest in Central America, has been held on Thursdays and Sundays for centuries — traders from across the region converge at dawn to sell textiles, pottery, dance masks, medicinal plants, copal, and corn. Arrive at opening (around 6 AM) to watch the stalls go up before the tour-bus wave hits around 9:30.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Iglesia de Santo Tomás, built around 1545 on the platform of a Maya temple, is the most striking example of religious syncretism in Guatemala. Its 18 steps represent the 18 months of the Maya calendar — K'iche' shamans burn copal incense and swing their censers on them throughout the day. Inside, the floor is strewn with flower petals and candles arranged in precise patterns, while worshippers murmur prayers in the Quiché language. Photography inside is strictly forbidden. Enter through the side door and stay silent.

⏱ 45 min
3

The cemetery of Chichicastenango is one of the country's most striking surprises: its mausoleums and headstones are painted turquoise (for mothers), white (for fathers), and yellow (for grandparents), creating an explosion of colour on the hillside. Maya and Catholic rituals coexist here on a daily basis. Not to be missed if you have any appreciation for popular funerary art.

⏱ 40 min
4

Pascual Abaj (Sacrifice Stone) is a Maya shrine on a hilltop 20 minutes' walk south of town. The path leads through the cemetery. At the top, a black stone idol represents Huyup Tak'ah, the Earth God — K'iche' shamans still perform daily ceremonies here with offerings, candles, and incense. Access is open but respectful behaviour is expected.

⏱ 50 min

Activities

Plunge into the market aisles in search of the best finds: huipiles (embroidered blouses) and ceremonial dance masks are Chichicastenango's specialities. Vendors on the north side of the square sell more authentic handcrafted goods; the tourist-oriented stalls cluster near the south entrance. Take your time: bargaining is expected, and artisans appreciate visitors who stop to look properly.

⏱ 90 min

Shuttle back to Antigua (about 3 hours by road via Chimaltenango). Overnight in Antigua to ease the airport transfer the following morning.
