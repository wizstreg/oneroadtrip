Cyclades Island Hopping: Mykonos, Delos, Naxos, Paros & Antiparos

📅 5 days📍 5 stops🚗 93 km⏱ 2h41

Mykonos → Naxos → Paros → Antiparos

Start from Mykonos with its 16th-century Venetian windmills, then cross to Delos, a sacred uninhabited island and UNESCO site that's Apollo's mythical birthplace. Breathe the open sea at Naxos among giant kouroi and marble villages, then glide to Paros and its Byzantine hundred-door basilica. Finish at Antiparos, where an 85-meter-deep cave and deserted beaches close out this tour of the central Cyclades.

✨ Highlights
  • Delos archaeological site, UNESCO-listed, boat excursion from Mykonos
  • Antiparos Cave: 85 m deep, 45-million-year-old stalagmite
  • Naxos's Portara: sunset through the axis of the marble gateway
  • Panagia Ekatontapiliani of Paros: the Cyclades' largest early Christian sanctuary
📆 Best period

May, June, September, October

To make the most of it, plan between 6 and 10 days.

Day 1

Mykonos

Where Venetian windmills spin above the cosmopolitan Aegean shore

★★★★★
📍 Chora Mykonos Town📍 Moulins Kato Mili Mykonos📍 Petite Venise Mykonos📍 Panagia Paraportiani Mykonos📍 Archaeological Museum Mykonos📍 Folklore Museum Mykonos
1

Chora, Mykonos's capital, is a whitewashed labyrinth said to have been designed deliberately to confuse marauding pirates. Wander its narrow lanes early in the morning before the cruise ship crowds descend on the port—after 10 a.m., the town belongs to the masses. The whitewashed houses with blue doors and balconies overflowing with bougainvillea set the quintessential Cycladic scene.

⏱ 90 min
2

The seven windmills of Kato Mili overlook the port of Alefkandra—built in the 16th century by the Venetians to grind wheat shipped from neighboring islands, they turned thanks to the Meltemi, the north wind that blows nearly 300 days a year over Mykonos. Go in late afternoon when the orange light hits their whitewashed walls—it's the island's postcard shot. Watch out for gusts if you climb up to them; the wind can catch you off guard.

⏱ 45 min
3

Little Venice gets its name from the 18th-century sea captains' houses built right at the water's edge, their colorful balconies practically dipping into the Aegean. It's one of the few places in the Cyclades where you can have sunset drinks with your feet almost in the waves. If you're here in the evening, book a table ahead in summer—the terraces fill up by 7 p.m.

⏱ 60 min
4

Panagia Paraportiani is an oddity across the entire archipelago: not one church but five, built between 1425 and the 17th century, stacked and melded together to form a uniquely asymmetrical white mass. Listed as a Greek historical monument since 1936, it's usually closed inside—but it's the exterior you come to see. Visit at different times of day; the rounded forms change dramatically with the light.

⏱ 30 min
5

The Archaeological Museum of Mykonos holds a selection of the ceramics and sculptures recovered from the island of Delos during excavations—notably amphorae, red-figure vases, and some funerary stelae. If you're planning to visit Delos the next day, this museum provides a good preview to help you understand what you'll see on-site.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Stroll through Chora's lanes in the morning to catch the fish market near the old port—fishermen sell their dawn catch there at prices far more reasonable than in the tavernas. It's one of the last authentic moments of the town before mass tourism takes over.

⏱ 45 min

The beaches of Paradise and Super Paradise, about ten kilometers south of Chora, are the island's party playgrounds: loud music, beach clubs, international vibe. Buses run there from Chora in summer. If you're after more peace, head to Agrari just next door—fine sand, fewer people, water just as clear.

⏱ 180 min

In the evening, settle into Little Venice for aperitivo, then explore Chora on foot: luxury boutiques rub shoulders with jewelers and art galleries in a maze designed for getting lost—and that's intentional. Mykonos's nightlife is legendary across the Mediterranean; if that's your thing, the night here rarely starts before midnight.

⏱ 120 min

Try kopanisti, Mykonos's sharp PDO cheese—a fermented soft paste with an intense flavor, spread on toasted bread with a drizzle of olive oil. You'll find it in local groceries and some tavernas.

⏱ 30 min

Louza, Mykonos's spiced dried pork charcuterie, is eaten as mezze with cheese and olives. It's a Cycladic tradition you won't easily find elsewhere in Greece—ask for it in the old port tavernas.

⏱ 30 min

Day 2

Mykonos

Where Venetian windmills spin above the cosmopolitan Aegean shore

★★★★★
📍 Delos Archaeological Site Greece📍 Terrace of the Lions Delos📍 Temple of Apollo Delos📍 Ancient Theatre Delos📍 Mount Kynthos Delos📍 Archaeological Museum Delos
1

Delos, reachable in 30 minutes by boat from Mykonos's old port, is one of the Mediterranean's most important archaeological sites: the entire 3.5 km² island has been a UNESCO site since 1990, and no modern construction is permitted. According to Greek mythology, this is where Apollo and Artemis were born. The French School of Athens has been excavating here since 1873, and only a third of the site has been uncovered. Plan at least 3 hours on-site.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Terrace of the Lions is Delos's most famous image: a row of marble lions from Naxos, offered by the Naxians in the 7th century BC to guard Apollo's sanctuary. Originally numbering 16, only 5 replicas remain on-site—the originals, worn by erosion, are preserved in the island's museum. One lion that ended up in Venice still sits outside the Arsenal, where it's been since the 17th century.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Hellenistic houses in the theater quarter preserve some of the finest floor mosaics in the Greek world, notably the famous mosaic of Dionysus on a panther. These mansions belonging to wealthy 2nd-century BC merchants give a concrete sense of Delos at the height of its glory—one of the most active cosmopolitan ports in the Mediterranean.

⏱ 45 min
4

The ancient theater of Delos, built between 314 and 250 BC, could seat between 5,000 and 6,500 spectators. Sit in the marble tiers and imagine the festivals honoring Apollo that took place here—the Delia, a panhellenic festival comparable to the Olympic Games, occurred every four years.

⏱ 45 min
5

Mount Kynthos rises to 113 meters—not a major climb, but the view from the summit sweeps across the entire island and the surrounding Cyclades on clear days. This, according to tradition, is where Apollo was born. A sanctuary dedicated to Zeus and Athena once stood at the top. Bring good shoes—the path is rocky.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Take the boat early in the morning from Mykonos's old port—first shuttles depart around 9 a.m. The site closes at 3 p.m. in low season, so check the hours before departing. There's no restaurant or water for sale on the island once the site opens: bring water and a snack, as the sun is merciless in summer.

⏱ 60 min

The Archaeological Museum of Delos, built in 1904 by the Archaeological Society of Athens and expanded twice since, houses the originals of the terrace lions, a 6th-century BC kouros torso, and a collection of mosaics, jewelry, and inscriptions. Allow 45 minutes for the nine rooms.

⏱ 45 min

Amygdalota, small almond cookies coated in powdered sugar, are Mykonos's signature sweet, made in the monastic tradition. You'll find them in artisan bakeries in Chora when you return from Delos—they keep for several days.

⏱ 20 min

Day 3

Naxos

The marble giant of the Cyclades, crowned by ancient kouroi

★★★★☆
📍 Portara Temple of Apollo Naxos📍 Kastro Venetian Naxos Chora📍 Archaeological Museum Naxos📍 Temple of Demeter Sangri Naxos📍 Kouros Apollonas Naxos📍 Village Apiranthos Naxos
1

Naxos's Portara is the first thing you'll spot arriving by ferry: a massive marble doorway standing 6 meters tall on the islet of Palatia at the harbor entrance. Each of the four blocks composing it weighs about 20 tons—which explains why the Venetians, who pillaged all the other temple materials to build their Kastro, left it standing. The Temple of Apollo it belonged to dates to 530 BC and was never completed. Go at sunset when the sun descends exactly through the doorway's axis toward Delos, Apollo's birthplace.

⏱ 45 min
2

Chora Naxos's Kastro was founded in 1207 by Marco Sanudo, the first Venetian duke of Naxos, on the town's highest point. Its outer walls are formed by the rear facades of Venetian nobles' palaces, many of which still bear their stone coats of arms. You enter through only two gates: Paraporti to the south and Trani to the north, topped by the Crispi tower—the only one still standing. Inside you'll find the 13th-century Catholic cathedral, the archaeological museum (housed in the former Jesuit school founded in 1627, where Nikos Kazantzakis, author of Zorba, was a student), and the della Rocca-Barozzi Venetian museum.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Temple of Demeter at Sangri, about fifteen kilometers south of Chora, is a 6th-century BC Doric temple partially reconstructed with its own original materials. The small site museum preserves the sculpted fragments found during excavations.

⏱ 75 min
4

The Kouros of Apollonas, at the island's northern tip, is a giant statue measuring 11 meters long and weighing 80 tons, abandoned in its quarry in the 7th century BC—probably due to a crack in the marble. It lies on its back, still attached to the rock. You'll feel tiny discovering it. Access is free, right by the road.

⏱ 45 min
5

Apiranthos is one of Naxos's best-preserved villages: its lanes are entirely paved in marble, its houses built in dry-stone Cycladic construction, and its inhabitants largely descended from Cretan refugees who arrived in the 17th century. The village has four small museums. Take time for a taverna on the main square.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

The road between Chora and Apollonas crosses Naxos's mountainous interior: olive groves, vineyards, marble villages, and Aegean views unfold in succession. It's the best way to see the island in its daily reality, far from the beaches. Allow half a day for the round trip with stops.

⏱ 180 min

Arseniko (or graviera of Naxos) is one of Greece's finest cheeses: hard, aged, with a powerful and slightly sharp flavor, produced under PDO on the island. You'll find it in all of Chora's cheese shops. Pair it with local honey—the combination is a Naxos classic.

⏱ 20 min

Kitron is a liqueur unique in the world, distilled since 1896 in Halki from the leaves of a particular lemon tree that grows only on Naxos. The Vallindras distillery on Halki's main square offers tastings. Three versions exist—white, golden, and green—depending on the sugar level.

⏱ 30 min

The beaches along Naxos's southwestern coast—Agios Prokopios, Agia Anna, Plaka—are among the Cyclades' finest: fine sand, shallow water, turquoise. Agios Prokopios is the most developed; Plaka is wilder and stretches for 5 km. All are accessible by bus from Chora.

⏱ 180 min

Day 4

Paros

Where the oldest Byzantine basilica guards shores of legendary marble

★★★★☆
📍 Panagia Ekatontapiliani Parikia Paros📍 Kastro Parikia Paros📍 Naoussa port Paros📍 Kolymbithres Beach Paros📍 Village Lefkes Paros📍 Butterfly Valley Petaloudes Paros
1

Panagia Ekatontapiliani—"the church of a hundred doors"—is the Cyclades' largest early Christian sanctuary and Greece's third largest. According to tradition, Saint Helen, mother of Emperor Constantine, vowed to build one during her stop at Paros in the 4th century. The current structure dates to the 6th century under Justinian. Its name comes from a legend: the church has 99 visible doors, and the hundredth will open the day Constantinople becomes Greek again. Don't miss the 4th-century baptistery attached to the church—it's the oldest and best-preserved in the Christian East.

⏱ 75 min
2

Parikia's Kastro, built in the 13th century by Marco Sanudo, perches on the hill overlooking the port. Its walls are made from blocks of columns and lintels salvaged from ancient temples on the site—look carefully climbing the steps and you'll still see the column drums. The view from the top sweeps across the entire bay of Parikia.

⏱ 45 min
3

Naoussa, in the island's north, is Paros's most charming fishing port: its white houses cascade down to the water, its tavernas spill onto the quay, and fishermen's caiques bob in the bay. A small 14th-century Venetian kastro guards the harbor entrance—it's half in the water, giving it a singular appearance.

⏱ 60 min
4

Lefkes, the largest inland village, was Paros's capital during the Ottoman era. Its white marble lanes, blue-shuttered houses, and two grand churches make it one of the Cyclades' most beautiful villages. An 8th-century Byzantine marble path, partially restored, links Lefkes to neighboring villages.

⏱ 60 min
5

The Valley of the Butterflies (Petaloudes), in the island's center, is a nature reserve where thousands of moths (Callimorpha quadripunctaria) gather each summer on the valley's damp trees. It's an unusual spectacle for the typically arid Cyclades.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Kolymbithres beach, north of Naoussa, owes its charm to granite rock formations sculpted by erosion into coves and natural pools. The water is calm, deep blue, and the rocks create ideal underwater topography for snorkeling. Accessible by water taxi from Naoussa or by car.

⏱ 120 min

Revythada is a signature Paros dish: chickpeas cooked very slowly in a potter's oven for hours, with no seasoning beyond olive oil and salt. The result is creamy, tender. Ask for it in Parikia or Naoussa tavernas—some only serve it on Sundays.

⏱ 60 min

Gouna is a fishermen's specialty: mackerel split in half and sun-dried for several days, then grilled over charcoal. It's a typical Paros mezze, eaten with a glass of ouzo while gazing at the sea. Look for it in the port tavernas of Naoussa or Parikia.

⏱ 45 min

Paros is one of the world's top kitesurfing destinations, with Golden Beach (Chryssi Akti) leading the way—the wind is consistent and predictable thanks to the Meltemi. Rental centers offer equipment and lessons for all levels.

⏱ 120 min

Day 5

Antiparos

A quiet escape harboring one of Greece's deepest caves

★★★☆☆
📍 Antiparos Cave stalactites📍 Kastro Antiparos village📍 Chora Antiparos port📍 Soros Beach Antiparos📍 Glyfa Beach Antiparos📍 Despotiko islet Antiparos
1

Antiparos Cave is one of Greece's most significant: 85 meters deep, accessible via 411 steps carved into the rock. It harbors an 8-meter-tall stalagmite that's 45 million years old, considered Europe's oldest. The cave was known since antiquity—geometric and classical pottery has been found inside—and it served as a cult site for Artemis. On December 24, 1673, the Marquis de Nointel, Louis XIV's ambassador to Constantinople, celebrated Christmas Mass here before 500 people, using the great stalagmite as an altar. It closes in late afternoon—plan to go in the morning.

⏱ 75 min
2

Antiparos's Kastro was built in 1440 by Giovanni Lorendano, Venetian lord of the island, to protect inhabitants from pirate attacks. The fortress is unique in the Cyclades: the outer walls of the inhabitants' houses form the castle's ramparts. The single entrance is through a door next to Saint Nicholas Cathedral.

⏱ 45 min
3

Antiparos's Chora is the antithesis of Mykonos: a quiet, authentic village where local life resumes its rhythm come September. Its lanes flowering with bougainvillea, family-run tavernas, and port facing Paros offer a welcome decompression after the more tourist-heavy islands.

⏱ 60 min
4

The islet of Despotiko, accessible in 10 minutes by boat from Agios Georgios in southern Antiparos, harbors a 7th-century BC archaic sanctuary of Apollo that's been under excavation since 1997. The islet's deserted beaches are among the Aegean's finest—crystalline waters, white sand bottom, no tourist development whatsoever.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Paros-Antiparos crossing from Punta takes 10 minutes by ferry, with connections every 30 minutes from 6 a.m. to midnight in summer. On the Antiparos side, rent a scooter or bike at the port to explore the island at your own pace—roads are flat and distances short.

⏱ 30 min

The beaches of Soros and Glyfa, on Antiparos's southern coast, are among the island's finest: fine sand, remarkably clear turquoise waters, very few people even in high season. Bring water and provisions—there are no tavernas on-site.

⏱ 180 min

In the evening at Chora, traditional tavernas serve fish caught that very day, grilled octopus, and homemade mezzes in a relaxed, family atmosphere. Prices are well below those of Paros or Mykonos for equivalent quality.

⏱ 90 min

Paros's assyrtiko, a dry, mineral white wine produced on the island's granite soils, pairs perfectly with seafood and grilled fish. Paros wines carry a PDO designation and can be found in the island's groceries and tavernas.

⏱ 30 min
