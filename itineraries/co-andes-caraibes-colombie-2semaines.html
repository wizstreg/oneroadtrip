☀️ What weather should you expect?

Dry Season (December-March, July-August): Best weather overall, peak tourist season, higher prices, ideal for most activities.

Wet Season (April-June, September-November): More rainfall, fewer crowds, lower prices, lush landscapes, some areas challenging.

Regional Variations: Caribbean coast hot year-round, Andes cooler, Amazon humid, Pacific coast rainier, plan by region.

Year-Round Destination: Equatorial location means consistent temperatures, seasonal rainfall variations, eternal spring in many areas.

🌍 What to discover in CO?

Emerald landscapes, coffee culture, and vibrant cities create South America's most exciting destination. Experience Cartagena's colonial beauty, Amazon rainforest, and incredible biodiversity. The country offers salsa dancing, incredible hospitality, and diverse ecosystems from coast to mountains. Colombia combines natural beauty with passionate culture and improving safety.

Colombia Road Trip: Andes to Caribbean in 14 Days

📅 14 days📍 14 stops🚗 1330 km⏱ 18h15

Bogota → Salento → Medellin → Guatape → Rincon Del Mar → Carthagene

From Bogotá high in the Andes, through Salento's coffee plantations, the colorful hills of Medellín and Guatapé, to the beaches of Rincón del Mar and the ramparts of Cartagena de Indias. A 14-day circuit crossing Colombia from south to north.

Day 1

Bogota

Where Andean peaks meet pre-Columbian gold and vibrant street art

★★★★★
📍 Plaza de Bolívar Bogotá📍 Catedral Primada Bogotá📍 Museo del Oro Bogotá📍 Museo Botero Bogotá📍 Chorro de Quevedo Bogotá
1

La Candelaria, Bogotá's historic heart, stands exactly where conquistador Gonzalo Jiménez de Quesada founded the city on August 6, 1538. Back then, the settlement consisted of just twelve thatched huts and a church. Walk up Carrera 2 from Plaza del Chorro de Quevedo—the city's founding spot—past wooden-balconied colonial houses and 19th-century Republican façades. Street art exploded across these walls starting in 2013, when Bogotá's youth won the right to paint freely throughout the neighborhood.

⏱ 90 min
2

On Plaza de Bolívar, the neoclassical Catedral Primada de Bogotá likely sits where the first mass was celebrated after the city's founding. Surrounding the square stand the Capitolio Nacional (Congress), the Palacio de Justicia, the Palacio Liévano (city hall), and the Casa de Nariño (presidential residence). Simón Bolívar's statue has commanded the center since independence in 1810.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Museo del Oro, established by the Bank of the Republic in 1939, houses roughly 55,000 pieces including 34,000 pre-Hispanic gold objects—the world's largest collection of pre-Columbian goldwork. Across three floors, galleries showcase the Muisca, Quimbaya, Calima, Zenú, and Tairona cultures. Pause before the Balsa Muisca (Muisca Raft), a gold figurine discovered near Pasca: it depicts the ritual that sparked the El Dorado legend, when the Muisca chief, covered in gold dust, would sail to the center of Lake Guatavita.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

In the morning, walk the La Candelaria street art circuit along Carrera 2 and Calle 10. The murals tell stories of Colombia's conflict, the memory of the disappeared, and the neighborhood's cultural rebirth. Allow 1.5 hours for the walk.

⏱ 90 min

Day 2

Bogota

Where Andean peaks meet pre-Columbian gold and vibrant street art

★★★★★
📍 Cerro de Monserrate Bogotá📍 Basílica Señor de Monserrate📍 Mercado de Usaquén Bogotá📍 Biblioteca Luis Ángel Arango Bogotá
1

Take the funicular (inaugurated in 1929, built by a Swiss company) or the cable car (1955) up to Cerro de Monserrate, perched at 3,152 meters—550 meters above the city. The Muisca people called this mountain "Quijicha Caca" (grandmother's foot) and watched the sunrise here during the June solstice. A first sanctuary was built around 1650. The current neo-colonial basilica dates from 1925, replacing a chapel destroyed in the 1917 earthquake. Inside, a 1656 sculpture of the Señor Caído de Monserrate survived that quake.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Museo Botero, housed in the Bank of the Republic's cultural complex, displays works by Colombian painter Fernando Botero alongside canvases by Picasso, Renoir, Dalí, and Monet. Entry is free. Next door, the Casa de Moneda presents a collection of historic coins, and the Biblioteca Luis Ángel Arango—44,000 square meters—is the country's largest cultural complex.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Late in the day, head to the summit of Monserrate for sunset. The view sweeps across the vastness of Bogotá and its eight million inhabitants spread across the savanna valley, ringed by the Eastern Andes. Bring a sweater: at this altitude, temperatures plummet quickly after 5 PM.

⏱ 60 min

Day 3

Salento

Colonial village gateway to the world's tallest palms

★★★★☆
📍 Plaza de Bolívar Salento📍 Calle Real Salento📍 Mirador Alto de la Cruz Salento📍 Iglesia Nuestra Señora del Carmen Salento
1

Morning flight to Pereira, then drive to Salento (45 min). This village at 1,895 meters elevation is the oldest in Quindío department: founded in 1842, its current location results from a flood of the Río Quindío that destroyed the original settlement of Boquía around 1854. Carrera 6, known as Calle Real, lines up bahareque houses (bamboo guadua frames filled with earth) painted in bright colors—an architectural style typical of Antioquian colonization, inscribed on UNESCO's World Heritage list as part of the Colombian Coffee Cultural Landscape.

⏱ 75 min
2

Climb the 253 steps of Mirador Alto de la Cruz at the end of Calle Real. From the top, views extend over the village rooftops, the Río Quindío valley, and the first foothills of the Central Cordillera. On clear days, the snow-capped Nevado del Tolima (5,215 m) appears on the horizon.

⏱ 45 min
3

On Plaza de Bolívar, the Iglesia Nuestra Señora del Carmen overlooks the square where Willys jeeps park—old American military jalopies converted to collective transport in the 1950s, now an icon of the coffee region.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Late afternoon, play a round of tejo on the village square: this Colombian game involves hurling metal discs at a gunpowder wick buried in clay. When the disc hits, the wick explodes with a sharp crack—a popular national sport played among friends over beer.

⏱ 60 min
Terrazas de Salento9.7
Day 4

Salento

Colonial village gateway to the world's tallest palms

★★★★☆
📍 Valle de Cocora Salento📍 Bosque de Palmas Cocora📍 Reserva Natural Acaime Salento📍 Casa de los Colibríes Cocora
1

The Cocora Valley shelters the Quindío wax palm (Ceroxylon quindiuense), Colombia's national tree since 1985 and the world's tallest palm: some specimens exceed 60 meters in height. This species grows only in the Andean cordillera, between 2,000 and 3,000 meters elevation. Naturalist Alexander von Humboldt first described it in 1801. These extremely slow-growing palms can live over 200 years. The name "Cocora" comes from the Quimbaya language, spoken by the region's ancient inhabitants, and means "star of water."

⏱ 60 min
2

Deep in the cloud forest, the Acaime nature reserve serves as a hummingbird sanctuary. Several species feed at feeders installed outside the ranger's cabin. The trail also crosses suspension bridges over the Río Quindío and penetrates a dense Andean forest populated by tree ferns and orchids.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In the morning, catch a Willys jeep from Plaza de Bolívar (11 km, 25 min) to the trailhead. The full loop covers 12 km through grasslands, humid tropical forest, and fields of giant palms. The trail climbs to viewpoints overlooking the valley, then descends through the forest. Start early: Andean mist often envelops the ridges by early afternoon.

⏱ 300 min
Terrazas de Salento9.7

★★★★☆
📍 Finca cafetera Salento📍 Plantación de café Salento
1

Coffee farms around Salento open their doors to visitors. A plantation tour follows every stage of Colombian coffee production: hand-picking ripe cherries, depulping, fermentation, sun-drying on "paseras," then roasting. Quindío, together with Caldas and Risaralda, forms the "Coffee Triangle," a region producing much of Colombia's arabica coffee grown between 1,200 and 1,800 meters elevation.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Join a coffee tasting at a finca. Local producers explain the differences between varieties (Castillo, Caturra, Colombia) and preparation methods. You'll leave able to distinguish the aromas of a washed coffee from a honey-processed one.

Day 6

Medellin

City of eternal spring, reborn through art and innovation

★★★★★
📍 Comuna 13 San Javier Medellín📍 Escaleras eléctricas Comuna 13 Medellín📍 Graffiti tour Comuna 13 Medellín
1

Comuna 13 (San Javier), clinging to Medellín's western hills, is home to roughly 160,000 residents. In the 1980s-90s, this neighborhood served as a corridor for drug trafficking and a stronghold for armed groups. In 2002, Operation Orión deployed 3,000 soldiers here. Since then, six outdoor escalator sections—the world's first of their kind—were inaugurated in late 2011 to open up the neighborhood and spare residents the 350 daily steps. Walls once riddled with bullet holes are now covered in murals painted by local artists who tell the story of the conflict and the neighborhood's rebirth.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Ride the outdoor escalators of Comuna 13 up to the viewpoint overlooking Medellín. Along the route, breakdancing and hip-hop performances break out spontaneously on the landings. Local associations offer guided tours of the neighborhood, led by residents who share their personal stories.

⏱ 90 min

Day 7

Medellin

City of eternal spring, reborn through art and innovation

★★★★★
📍 Plaza Botero Medellín📍 Museo de Antioquia Medellín📍 Jardín Botánico Medellín📍 Parque Arví Medellín📍 Metro de Medellín
1

Plaza Botero, in central Medellín, displays 23 monumental bronze sculptures by Fernando Botero, donated by the artist to his native city. These voluptuous figures—women, men, animals—each weigh several tons. The Museo de Antioquia, in the Art Deco former city hall bordering the square, houses more Botero works along with pre-Columbian and contemporary art collections.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Jardín Botánico de Medellín, founded in 1972, spreads across 14 hectares north of downtown. Its Orquideorama—a giant flower-shaped wooden structure—shelters over 4,500 plant species. The garden also serves as a refuge for iguanas, turtles, and more than 100 bird species in the heart of the city.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Take the Medellín metro (Colombia's first metro system, inaugurated in 1995) then the metrocable to Parque Arví, a 1,761-hectare nature reserve in the city's eastern heights. The cable car ascent offers a bird's-eye view of working-class neighborhoods clinging to the slopes.

⏱ 180 min

Day 8

Guatape

Where hand-painted houses mirror in the shadow of a 220-meter monolith

★★☆☆☆
📍 Guatapé pueblo Colombia📍 Calle del Recuerdo Guatapé📍 Plazoleta de los Zócalos Guatapé📍 Malecón Guatapé
1

Guatapé, founded in 1811 in Antioquia department, is instantly recognizable by its zócalos: brightly painted cement bas-reliefs adorning the base of each house. Every family decorates its façade with motifs telling its trade or story—animals, tools, everyday life scenes. Calle del Recuerdo displays the finest examples of this folk art. Plazoleta de los Zócalos gathers the most elaborate façades.

⏱ 75 min
2

In 1978, construction of a hydroelectric dam flooded over 2,000 hectares of farmland around Guatapé, submerging part of the old village. A cross still emerges from the water marking the former church location. What was trauma became the region's tourist draw: the artificial lake, dotted with green islets, creates a singular landscape visible from Piedra del Peñol.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Late afternoon, stroll along the malecón (waterfront promenade) and watch the hills emerging from the water. Fishing boats and tourist lanchas tie up at colorful docks.

⏱ 45 min

Day 9

Guatape

Where hand-painted houses mirror in the shadow of a 220-meter monolith

★★☆☆☆
📍 Piedra del Peñol Guatapé📍 Embalse de Guatapé📍 Lago de Guatapé
1

Piedra del Peñol is a granite and quartz monolith 220 meters high (2,135 m elevation at the summit), weighing roughly 10 million tons. The Tahamí, indigenous people of the region, venerated it under the name "mojarrá." In July 1954, three friends—Luis Villegas, Pedro Nel Ramírez, and Ramón Díaz—made the first ascent in five days, using sticks wedged into the rock's cracks. Classified as a national monument since 1940, the rock bears the letters "GI" on its western face: in the 1980s, Guatapé residents tried to paint their town's full name, but neighbors from the village of El Peñol stopped the work after the first two letters.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Climb the 740 steps installed in a natural crack in the rock. Landings every 25 steps let you catch your breath. At the summit, a 360° view embraces the artificial lake, green islets, and Antioquia hills. Count on 30 to 45 minutes up.

⏱ 90 min

In the afternoon, board a lancha for a lake tour. The boat skirts the islets, passes an abandoned hacienda ruin, and offers a unique perspective on the monolith seen from the water.

⏱ 60 min

Day 10

Rincon Del Mar

Fishing village where the sea glows blue at night

★☆☆☆☆
📍 Rincón del Mar San Onofre📍 Playa La Punta Rincón del Mar📍 Manglares Rincón del Mar
1

Morning flight Medellín → Caribbean coast (Montería or Tolú), then road transfer to Rincón del Mar—count on 3 to 4 hours from Montería. This fishing village in Sucre department, part of San Onofre municipality, has just one unpaved main street running between colorful wooden houses and the sea. The white-sand beach stretches for seven kilometers, lined with coconut palms. Facing the village, less than an hour by lancha, the San Bernardo archipelago arrays its ten coral islands.

⏱ 60 min
2

Cross the bridge spanning the arm of sea linking the coast to the mangrove. On the other side, Playa La Punta is the village's quietest beach—no boats, no music, just sand and turquoise water. Fishermen return with their catch between 1 and 2 PM: the moment to choose your meal's fish directly on the beach.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

At nightfall, board a boat with a fisherman to observe bioluminescent plankton. Stirring the water by hand, thousands of microorganisms glow blue-green in the darkness. The phenomenon is most intense on moonless nights.

⏱ 90 min
Suite privada frente al mar, Tolú9.3
Day 11

Rincon Del Mar

Fishing village where the sea glows blue at night

★☆☆☆☆
📍 Isla Mucura San Bernardo📍 Isla Tintipán San Bernardo📍 Santa Cruz del Islote
1

Santa Cruz del Islote, a tiny artificial islet of 0.012 km² in the San Bernardo archipelago, is considered one of the world's most densely populated islands: roughly 500 residents live permanently on barely one hectare. Concrete and wooden houses squeeze together without leaving a square meter free. There are no cars, no roads—just narrow alleys between dwellings.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Catch a lancha early in the morning (40-minute crossing) for a day on the San Bernardo islands. The archipelago is part of the Corales del Rosario y de San Bernardo National Natural Park, protected since 1977. Snorkeling stop on the coral reef between Isla Mucura and Isla Tintipán—the latter, the archipelago's largest, is ringed by mangroves and remains largely wild. Grilled fish lunch on Mucura beach.

⏱ 360 min

Day 12

Carthagene

Fortress city that held the Caribbean for Spain and never fell

★★★★★
📍 Ciudad Amurallada Cartagena📍 Murallas de Cartagena📍 Torre del Reloj Cartagena📍 Plaza de Bolívar Cartagena📍 Catedral Santa Catalina Cartagena📍 Palacio de la Inquisición Cartagena
1

Morning drive to Cartagena de Indias (2h30). The Ciudad Amurallada, inscribed on UNESCO's World Heritage list since 1984, is girded by 11 km of walls built between the late 16th century and 1796—nearly two centuries of construction in local coral rock, rising 6 to 8 meters high. Military engineer Bautista Antonelli drew up the plan in 1586, on Philip II's order, after corsair Francis Drake sacked the city that same year.

⏱ 60 min
2

Pass under the Torre del Reloj, the old main gate to the fortified city, to enter the historic center. Plaza de Bolívar, flanked by the Palacio de la Inquisición (Holy Office tribunal active from 1610 to 1821) and the Catedral Santa Catalina de Alejandría (construction begun in 1575, damaged by Drake's cannons in 1586, completed in the 17th century), concentrates the main monuments of the colonial quarter.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Convento de San Pedro Claver, founded by the Jesuits in the 17th century, bears the name of the Spanish monk who devoted his life to caring for and baptizing enslaved people disembarked at Cartagena's port. The city was, along with Veracruz in Mexico, the main slave port of the Spanish Empire in America starting in 1550.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Late afternoon, walk along the ramparts on the sea side to watch the sun descend over the Caribbean. The bastions of Santo Domingo and Santa Clara offer the best perspectives on the horizon and the tiled rooftops of the historic center.

Day 13

Carthagene

Fortress city that held the Caribbean for Spain and never fell

★★★★★
📍 Castillo San Felipe de Barajas Cartagena📍 Getsemaní Cartagena📍 Plaza de la Trinidad Cartagena📍 Las Bóvedas Cartagena
1

Castillo San Felipe de Barajas, perched on San Lázaro hill 40 meters above the city, is the largest fortress built by the Spanish in all their colonies. A first fortified outpost existed on this hill as early as the 1530s; the current castle was commissioned in 1630 and completed in 1657, then greatly expanded in 1762 until it covered the entire hill. Its zigzag walls, thick at the base and narrow at the top, protect a labyrinthine network of underground tunnels designed to trap the enemy: narrow corridors, blind angles, echoes amplifying the slightest footstep. In 1741, English Admiral Edward Vernon attacked with 186 ships and 27,000 men. Spanish commander Blas de Lezo, who had already lost an eye, a leg, and an arm in combat, repelled him with only 3,600 soldiers. The fortress was never taken.

⏱ 120 min
2

Head down to Getsemaní, the old suburb of slaves and poor workers outside the walls. Long neglected, this neighborhood is experiencing an artistic renaissance: façades are covered in brightly colored murals. Plaza de la Trinidad, the neighborhood's heart, comes alive evenings with cumbia and champeta musicians. Here, in this working-class suburb, Cartagena's first declaration of independence was proclaimed on November 11, 1811.

⏱ 75 min
3

Las Bóvedas, 23 stone vaults backing onto the ramparts on the northeast side, served as military storerooms and dungeons in colonial times. Today they house craft shops. From the parapet walk above the vaults, the view extends over the sea and the modern Bocagrande district.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Evening, explore Getsemaní on foot: painted walls change color under night lighting, bars open their doors to the street, and rhythms of salsa and champeta echo from Plaza de la Trinidad.

⏱ 90 min

Day 14

Carthagene

★★★★★
📍 Islas del Rosario Cartagena📍 Playa Blanca Barú Cartagena📍 Isla Barú Cartagena
1

The Islas del Rosario archipelago, one hour by lancha from Cartagena, counts 27 coral islands protected since 1977 in the Corales del Rosario y de San Bernardo National Natural Park. The shallow waters shelter coral reefs and dense marine life: parrotfish, barracudas, starfish, brain corals, and gorgonians.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Embark for a day of snorkeling on the Islas del Rosario reefs. Lanchas depart early morning from the La Bodeguita dock in the Manga neighborhood. Option to combine with a stop at Playa Blanca on Barú island—a strip of white sand facing translucent waters. Last day on the Caribbean coast before the return flight from Cartagena's Rafael Núñez airport.
