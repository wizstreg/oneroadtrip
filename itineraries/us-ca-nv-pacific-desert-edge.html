Day 1

Las Vegas

Where desert springs flowed for 15,000 years before running dry in 1962

★★★★☆
📍 Las Vegas Springs Preserve Nevada📍 Downtown Las Vegas Fremont Street
1

Start at the Springs Preserve, three miles west of the Strip—this is where Las Vegas literally sprang to life. For over 15,000 years, natural springs gushed here and fed verdant meadows: Mexican scout Rafael Rivera discovered them in 1829 while following the Las Vegas Wash, giving the valley its name—'las vegas' means 'the meadows' in Spanish. The springs ran dry in 1962 due to groundwater overexploitation. Today, the 180-acre site, listed on the National Register of Historic Places since 1978, houses the Origen Museum (natural and human history of the desert), a recreation of 1905 Las Vegas called Boomtown, and more than three miles of trails. It's the perfect introduction to Mojave ecology before you plunge into the deserts ahead.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Use the evening to stock up on provisions and check your vehicle: water in quantity (minimum one gallon per person per day in the desert), energy snacks, first-aid kit. Turn in early—pre-dawn departures in the desert have real value: temperatures are tolerable and the light is sublime.

Day 2

Beatty

Gateway to Rhyolite's 50,000-bottle ghost town and Death Valley's northern threshold

★★☆☆☆
📍 Red Rock Canyon National Conservation Area Nevada📍 Calico Hills Red Rock Canyon📍 Rhyolite Ghost Town Nevada📍 Goldwell Open Air Museum Beatty Nevada
1

Before leaving Las Vegas, detour to Red Rock Canyon, 15 miles west. The 13-mile scenic loop (one-way) runs along cliffs of Aztec Sandstone—a Jurassic-age sandstone 180 million years old, formed by the lithification of massive sand dunes. The blood-red and ochre hues come from iron oxides. The Calico Hills in particular are an explosion of striated color that geologists attribute to the same formation. More than 1,200 climbing routes have been established on these walls, which can rise to 3,000 feet—this is one of the premier climbing destinations in the United States. Look for petroglyphs near Petroglyph Wall (1,000-foot trail from Willow Spring): they were carved at least 800 years ago by ancestral Southern Paiute peoples. Reserve your entry in advance (pass required in season, timed entry).

⏱ 120 min
2

Two and a half miles west of Beatty on SR-374, take the marked BLM road that leads to the ruins of Rhyolite. This mining town erupted from nowhere in 1905 after gold was discovered in the Bullfrog Hills: 5,000 residents in six months, three banks, a stock exchange, an opera house, a hospital, concrete sidewalks, and electricity. The John S. Cook & Co. bank, completed in 1908, cost more than $90,000 (approximately $3.2 million today) with its Italian marble stairs and stained glass imported from Europe. By 1916, the lights went out for good. What remains is the bank facade, the train depot, and the famous Bottle House—built in 1906 by Tom Kelly with 50,000 bottles salvaged from saloons, for lack of wood in the desert. Just below, the Goldwell Open Air Museum presents a dozen outdoor sculptures installed starting in 1984, including the haunting Last Supper by Belgian artist Albert Szukalski. Free admission, open dawn to dusk.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

On US-95 between Las Vegas and Beatty, keep an eye on the roadside: wild burros regularly roam the Amargosa Valley, descendants of pack animals abandoned by miners. Don't approach them and don't feed them—they remain wild animals. Allow about two and a half hours of driving from Red Rock Canyon to Beatty.

Day 3

Furnace Creek

Where Earth's hottest temperature was recorded: 134°F in the shade of July 1913

★★☆☆☆
📍 Zabriskie Point Death Valley📍 Mesquite Flat Sand Dunes Death Valley📍 Badwater Basin Death Valley📍 Artist's Drive Death Valley📍 Artist's Palette Death Valley
1

Rise before dawn to reach Zabriskie Point at sunrise—this is one of the most photographed geological spectacles in the United States. Named in honor of Christian Zabriskie, director of the Pacific Coast Borax Company, this overlook commands borax-tinged clay badlands that turn gold and amber with the first rays. Manly Beacon, the central pinnacle rising to 823 feet, bears the name of one of the first gold-rush pioneers to cross Death Valley. These folded hills are sediments from an ancient valley-floor lake, eroded over millennia by the rare but violent desert rains. Arrive 30 minutes before dawn: after 8 a.m., the parking lot fills quickly.

⏱ 75 min
2

Mesquite Flat Sand Dunes, 1.2 miles east of Stovepipe Wells, are the park's most accessible dunes. For a dune field to form, you need a source of sand, a prevailing wind, and a barrier to trap the sand—here, the Panamint Mountains to the south play that role. The tallest dune rises about 100 feet. In the morning, nocturnal animal tracks (kangaroo rats, sidewinder snakes) trace arabesques across the smooth sand before daytime visitors erase everything. Go at dawn or sunset for the play of shadows and light on the crests. In summer, don't set foot there after 10 a.m.

⏱ 75 min
3

Badwater Basin, nine miles south of Furnace Creek, is the lowest point in North America: 282 feet below sea level. You're literally walking beneath the surface of the oceans. On July 10, 1913, the temperature reached 134°F at Furnace Creek—the world record for atmospheric temperature ever recorded. The salt crust cracked into polygons is the residue of a prehistoric lake, Lake Manly, which covered the valley about 10,000 years ago. In summer, the ground can exceed 200°F—the heat is suffocating even in shade. Go early morning or evening, never midday.

⏱ 60 min
4

The one-way Artist's Drive loop (nine miles starting from Badwater Road) traverses one of the park's most colorful geological formations. Artist's Palette, at the midpoint, presents a range of tones that few natural landscapes equal: reds and pinks come from hematite (iron oxide), greens from chlorite (mica family), purples from manganese. These colors are the legacy of volcanic eruptions over five million years ago, whose ashes were chemically altered by water and heat. Visit in late afternoon for particularly intense light.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

In the evening, park rangers host ranger talks at Furnace Creek seasonally—check the program posted at the Furnace Creek Visitor Center upon arrival. If the sky is clear, Death Valley is a certified Dark Sky park: step outside after 10 p.m. for a starry vault unmatched in the lower 48 states.

Day 4

Baker

Home to the world's tallest thermometer, standing 134 feet in tribute to Death Valley's inferno

★★☆☆☆
📍 World's Tallest Thermometer Baker California📍 Mojave National Preserve Kelso Dunes📍 Kelso Depot Mojave National Preserve
1

Arriving in Baker, you can't miss the world's tallest thermometer: 134 feet of steel structure, visible from Interstate 15. Its height—exactly 134 feet—is a tribute to the temperature record set at Furnace Creek on July 10, 1913: 134°F. It was Willis Herron, a local businessman, who financed it in 1991 to draw travelers to his town. Desert winds toppled it shortly after its unveiling; it was rebuilt with 4,400 cubic feet of concrete in its structure. The Temp 134 shop at its base tells the whole story, including a frying pan left outside permanently to test whether the heat is enough to cook eggs (it takes 158°F to do so).

⏱ 30 min
2

Kelso Dunes, in Mojave National Preserve, rank among the world's only thirty 'singing' dunes. These dunes sprawl over 45 square miles and rise nearly 650 feet above the desert floor—the round-trip hike is about four miles with 650 feet of elevation gain. The sand, composed of quartz and feldspar, comes from deposits of the ancient Mojave River and accumulated here about 25,000 years ago. When grains slide down slopes in dry wind, they produce a deep hum audible from a distance—an acoustic phenomenon linked to the specific grain size and extreme aridity of the site. Bring water; the heat climbs fast. No trail markers on this terrain: choose your bearing and head straight for the crest.

⏱ 150 min
3

Kelso Depot, a historic 1924 station built in Mission Revival style by the Union Pacific Railroad, served to supply steam locomotives with water and coal in the heart of the desert. Restored, it now houses the park visitor center with exhibits on railroad history and the geology of the preserve. Quick stop but useful before or after the dunes.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Descend into the lava tube of the Cima volcanic field, a few miles from the dunes. A metal ladder provides access to this basaltic tunnel formed between 15,000 and 20,000 years ago when exterior lava cooled faster than the interior, creating a hollow conduit. Bring a headlamp—the cave is dark at the entrance, but natural skylights illuminate the central sections. Pack a light jacket: the temperature remains cool even in summer, around 60°F.

Day 5

Las Vegas

Where desert springs flowed for 15,000 years before running dry in 1962

★★★★☆
📍 Cima Dome Joshua Tree forest Mojave📍 Neon Museum Las Vegas📍 Downtown Arts District Las Vegas Fremont Street
1

On the return route to Las Vegas, cross Cima Dome and its surroundings: this flattened granite dome 15 miles in diameter once sheltered the world's densest Joshua tree forest before the 2020 fires. The traces of charred vegetation now create a ghostly landscape of striking beauty—the Joshua trees are slowly regrowing, and the regeneration zone testifies to the desert's resilience after fires. Stay on marked roads: the fragile soil of regrowth zones cannot withstand off-road passage.

⏱ 60 min
2

Back in Las Vegas, the Neon Museum (Neon Boneyard) exhibits more than 200 illuminated signs from Strip casinos and hotels, from the 1930s to today. These sculptures of steel and blown glass tell the visual history of the city: the original Stardust sign, the Moulin Rouge, the Vegas Vic cowboy... The evening guided tour is particularly spectacular when certain signs are lit up.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Finish with Fremont Street and the Downtown Arts District—a neighborhood in full transformation where contemporary art galleries rub shoulders with giant murals and vinyl bars. Very different from the Strip, more authentic, less tourist-saturated. This is the Las Vegas of locals, and it's a fine way to close this desert circuit.
