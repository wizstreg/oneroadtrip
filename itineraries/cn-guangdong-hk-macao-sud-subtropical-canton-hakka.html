Trip info

✨ Highlights▾
  • Guangzhou's port has operated continuously for 2,200 years, making it China's oldest uninterrupted maritime port
  • Kaiping's diaolou: 1,833 fortified towers still standing, UNESCO-listed in 2007, blending Greek, Roman, Gothic, and Islamic styles
  • The Hong Kong-Zhuhai-Macau Bridge measures 55 km, opened October 24, 2018—the world's longest sea-crossing bridge-tunnel
  • Lantau's Tian Tan Buddha weighs 250 tons and stands 34 meters tall including base, completed in 1993 after 12 years of construction
⏳ How many days▾

12 days suggested. 11 days would technically be enough, but we prefer to let each stop breathe.

📋 Notes▾

Macau and Hong Kong are special administrative regions: border crossings and immigration stamps apply even for mainland Chinese citizens. For French nationals: Hong Kong visa-free (90 days), Macau visa-free, but visa required for mainland China. Avoid summer (June-August), which is unbearably humid and hot. October through December or March through April offer the best weather windows.

Subtropical Southern China in 12 Days: Canton, Hakka Diaspora & Hong Kong Peninsula

📅 12 days📍 12 stops🚗 910 km⏱ 12h20

Guangzhou → Foshan → Kaiping → Macao → Shenzhen → Hong Kong → Meizhou → Chaozhou → Shantou

Guangzhou → Foshan → Kaiping → Macao → Shenzhen → Hong Kong → Meizhou → Chaozhou → Shantou

This 12-day itinerary explores four major culinary traditions of southern China (Cantonese, Macanese, Hakka, Teochew), crosses the country's two special administrative regions (Macau and Hong Kong), and plunges into the extraordinary UNESCO-listed diaolou architecture of Kaiping. You move from ancient Canton to Cotai's casinos, from Zili's fortified towers to Hakka walled villages, from Tanka fishing beaches to Teochew diaspora ports bound for Thailand. A complete immersion in the subtropics—avoid summer.

Day 1

Guangzhou

1

Arrive early on Shamian Island, before couples in Chinese wedding gowns flood the tree-lined avenues. A British and French concession from 1859 onward, Shamian today unfolds beneath century-old banyan trees, among pastel-colored colonial buildings, the former Catholic Church of Our Lady of Lourdes, and the old HSBC building. It's Guangzhou's most striking contrast: 0.3 km² of hushed calm pressed against a metropolis of 18 million souls.

⏱ 90 min
2

Descend into the burial chamber of Zhao Mo, second king of Nanyue, preserved intact beneath the museum built atop it. The tomb was discovered by chance in 1983 during construction work. Dating from 122 BC, it contained a jade burial suit sewn with silk thread, over 1,000 artifacts, and even the skeletons of sacrificed concubines. Allow two hours—it's one of the most eloquent archaeological sites in southern China.

⏱ 120 min
3

The Chen Clan Ancestral Hall dates to 1894. Seventy-two Chen lineages across Guangdong pooled funds to build this immense ancestral temple, now a museum of Cantonese folk arts. Look up at the ceramic roof friezes: hundreds of polychrome figurines depict entire Chinese operas. The wood carvings and brick sculptures rank among the finest in China. An hour is enough, but you'll revisit it ten times in your mind.

⏱ 75 min
4

Sit for ten minutes in the courtyard of the Temple of the Six Banyan Trees, founded in 537 and now wedged between two skyscrapers—it still draws monks and worshippers. Its Flower Pagoda, 57 meters tall and rebuilt in 1097, is Guangzhou's silent emblem. The poet Su Dongpo gave it its name in 1100 after seeing six banyan trees in the courtyard. Modest entry fee, contemplative atmosphere—perfect after the bustle of the Chen Hall.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Dim sum was born in Guangzhou: the yum cha (tea drinking) tradition has made Cantonese brunch a 2,000-year-old ritual. Step into Panxi or Guangzhou Restaurant to taste har gao (shrimp dumplings), siu mai, char siu bao, and chicken feet in black bean sauce. Choose your dishes from circulating carts. Expect 80 to 150 yuan per person. Arrive before 11 a.m. to secure a table.

⏱ 90 min

Ascend Canton Tower at sunset. At 604 meters, it ranks among the world's tallest towers, and its twisted-hourglass silhouette has become the city's icon. The observation deck at 488 meters offers 360° views of the Pearl River and the skyscraper forests of Zhujiang New Town. For the brave: the Sky Drop, the world's highest thrill ride certified by Guinness. Book online to skip the line.

⏱ 90 min
Day 2

Guangzhou

1

Climb the five-story Zhenhai Pagoda in Yuexiu Park, built in 1380—it offers the finest historic view over the city. The park also shelters the Statue of the Five Rams, Guangzhou's founding myth: five immortals descended from heaven on rams bearing grain to end a famine. Locals gather here to dance, sing, and fly kites. Free entry, allow two hours.

⏱ 120 min
2

Wander Yongqingfang without a plan, get lost—it's a chance to see how Guangzhou reinvents its past without turning it into a museum. This traditional Liwan district was renovated between 2016 and 2020: gray-brick qilou arcade houses, cobbled lanes, artisan workshops, design boutiques. Bruce Lee lived here as a child; his family home is open to visitors on a neighboring street. Allow 1.5 hours.

⏱ 90 min
3

Pause beneath the glass panels on Beijing Road: at your feet, authentic Tang dynasty paving stones layered atop those from Song, Ming, and Qing—eleven strata of pavement spanning 1,300 years. The street is one of China's oldest commercial arteries, now pedestrianized and crowded with contemporary brands, but these archaeological windows are unmissable.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The Pearl River night cruise runs about an hour. You board at Tianzi Pier or Dashatou and glide beneath illuminated bridges, facing Canton Tower as it shifts colors every few minutes. It's touristy, yes, but the skyline spectacle from the water is impossible to replicate on foot. Expect 100 to 150 yuan, departures between 7 p.m. and 9 p.m.

⏱ 75 min

In the evening, head to Bingsheng or Pearl River Restaurant to taste Guangdong's most emblematic dishes: crispy-skin chicken (spit-roasted then flash-fried in boiling oil), roast Shenjing goose, seafood congee, choy sum greens in oyster sauce. Cantonese cuisine prizes extreme freshness and minimal cooking. Reserve ahead, especially on weekends.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 25 km · 30min
Day 3

Foshan

1

Step into the Ip Man Museum, opened in 2002 within the ancestral temple complex. Foshan is the birthplace of Wing Chun, the martial art of Bruce Lee and his master Ip Man, and this small museum traces the grandmaster's life through his garments, wooden training weapons, and period photographs. If you've seen the Ip Man films, it's a pilgrimage; if not, the Wing Chun introduction is well worth the visit. Allow one hour.

⏱ 60 min
2

Look up inside Foshan Ancestral Temple, founded in 1078 under the Northern Song. The polychrome Shiwan ceramic figurines adorning the roofs are among the province's finest. Dedicated to Beidi, Emperor of the North, the temple also houses the Wanfu Theater, Guangdong's oldest still-active stage. Lion dance demonstrations take place on weekends. An hour and a half suffices—don't miss the stele courtyard.

⏱ 90 min
3

Stroll along Lingnan Tiandi, Foshan's renovated old quarter where traditional Cantonese arcade houses, interior courtyards, and the former Jian family mansion (circa 1860)—late-Qing tobacco magnates—still stand. Now blending cafés, design shops, and artisan workshops, the district strikes a coherent balance between heritage and contemporary life. Allow an hour for a late-afternoon wander.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Immerse yourself in the Shiwan ceramic kilns, firing continuously for over 1,000 years. The Nanfeng Ancient Kiln, in continuous operation since the Ming dynasty (circa 1506), remains one of the world's oldest dragon kilns still in service. You'll see artisans throwing, modeling, and painting the polychrome figurines sold across southern China. Buy a figurine directly from the workshop—quality incomparable to airport souvenirs.

⏱ 90 min

Reserve dinner at a traditional Cantonese restaurant in Foshan: roast char siu pork, fish soup with rice noodles, and the region's oyster fritters stand out for rusticity closer to the countryside than to Guangzhou's refinement. The area around the ancestral temple concentrates the best addresses—expect 70-100 yuan per person. Family atmosphere, few tourists, gentle prices.

⏱ 75 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 110 km · 1h40
Day 4

Kaiping

1

Stand in the rice paddies of Zili Village: before you, nine UNESCO-listed diaolou, the largest grouping of the four protected clusters. Built between 1917 and 1949 by wealthy Cantonese emigrants returning from North America and Southeast Asia, these fortified tower-houses blend Byzantine domes, Roman columns, Gothic gargoyles, and Chinese roofs. Peasants served as architectural models from postcards received by mail. An hour and a half on-site is enough for the magic.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Ruishi Diaolou, behind Jinjiangli Village, is the tallest of the diaolou: nine stories built in 1921 by the Huang family, with a Roman dome and Byzantine roofline. Inside, original furniture remains in place, as if frozen in time. You climb a narrow staircase to the panoramic terrace from which the family surveyed the countryside—and fired, when necessary. Some windows still retain their machinegun crenellations.

⏱ 60 min
3

Wander through Liyuan Garden, built between 1926 and 1936 by émigré Xie Weili upon returning from the United States. This isn't a diaolou strictly speaking but a residential complex blending Chinese-style pavilions, artificial lakes, and Italian Baroque ornamentation. European-imported furniture, Art Deco stained glass, and local wood carvings make it a striking witness to the era's cultural cross-pollination. Included in the 180-yuan combined ticket.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

In total, over 3,000 diaolou were built across the region between 1900 and 1940; roughly 1,833 still stand today in Kaiping's countryside. Rent an electric bike to link Zili, Majianglong, and Jinjiangli along the dikes between rice paddies. It's flat, shaded, and you'll discover dozens of abandoned towers—some still inhabited by the great-grandchildren of their builders. Three hours of riding to truly grasp the phenomenon's scale.

⏱ 180 min

In the evening, rural Cantonese dinner in Kaiping: classic sweet-and-sour pork (not the tourist version), steamed fish with ginger and spring onions, Hakka stuffed tofu. Head to Chikan, the old market town 10 minutes by taxi, for dinner in a restored qilou arcade house. The atmosphere rivals the cuisine, and prices run far lower than in Guangzhou.

⏱ 75 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 130 km · 2h
Day 5

Macao

1

The Ruins of St. Paul's are the surviving façade of Macau's most iconic church, completed in 1602 by Jesuits with the help of exiled Japanese Christians. Fire ravaged it in 1835, leaving only this Baroque frontispiece carved with scenes blending Christian iconography and Chinese symbols. Climb the 68 steps at sunset, descend to see the crypt and Museum of Sacred Art behind. Free entry.

⏱ 75 min
2

Light an incense stick at A-Ma Temple, Macau's oldest sanctuary, built in 1488—it gave the city its name (Portuguese arrivals in 1557 understood "A-Ma-Gao," the Bay of A-Ma). Dedicated to Mazu, goddess of seafarers, it climbs the hillside in intertwined Taoist, Buddhist, and Confucian pavilions. Incense coils burn for weeks suspended from ceilings. Half an hour suffices.

⏱ 45 min
3

Settle on Senado Square and its Portuguese wave-patterned pavement in white and black: it's the heart of the historic center, UNESCO-listed in 2005. All around, pastel façades, St. Dominic's Church from 1587, the Leal Senado municipal building from 1784, and century-old shops compose the legacy of 442 years of Portuguese presence (1557-1999). You're in Europe, 15 minutes from mainland China.

⏱ 60 min
4

Climb up to Monte Fort, completed in 1626 by Jesuits to defend the colony against the Dutch—its bronze cannons still aim toward the sea. The Macau Museum, installed within its walls, recounts 600 years of exchanges between China and Europe—one of Asia's finest small museums. Free panoramic view from the ramparts, ideal at sunset.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Taste Macanese cuisine, a fusion born from the encounter of Portuguese cooks, African slaves, Malays, and Cantonese Chinese. Must-tries: galinha à africana (African chicken with piri-piri and coconut milk), bacalhau à brás (shredded cod), minchi (ground beef with potatoes, the unofficial national dish), and pastel de nata. Riquexó or A Lorcha for the classics. Expect 250 patacas per person.

⏱ 90 min

In the evening, cross to Cotai via the Sai Van Bridge for a casino tour: Venetian (the world's largest casino by floor area, opened 2007), Wynn Palace and its dancing fountain lake, Grand Lisboa shaped like a golden lotus. You're not obliged to gamble—the sheer delirious architecture merits the visit. Free cable car at Wynn, Venetian gondolas at Venetian.

⏱ 120 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 5 km · 30min
Day 6

Shenzhen

1

Take a turn along Lover's Road, Zhuhai's 28-km coastal promenade facing the South China Sea, where the Fisher Girl statue in granite stands—sculpted in 1982 and now the city's modern symbol. You arrive via the Gongbei border crossing from Macau, one of the world's busiest—up to 350,000 crossings per day. Unobstructed views of Macau in the distance and the HZMB bridge stretching toward Hong Kong.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Take the Hong Kong-Zhuhai-Macau Bridge shuttle bus, or step back to view it: 55 km of viaducts and underwater tunnels opened on October 24, 2018—the world's longest sea-crossing bridge-tunnel. The 6.7-km immersed tunnel at 45 meters depth passes beneath navigation corridors for the world's largest container ships. The viewpoint on the Zhuhai side offers the best perspective. Pure engineering prowess. Late afternoon, HSR train from Zhuhai to Shenzhen in 1.5 hours.

⏱ 75 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 130 km · 1h30
Day 7

Shenzhen

1

Shenzhen grew from 30,000 inhabitants in 1980, when Deng Xiaoping designated it China's first Special Economic Zone, to over 17 million today—the world's youngest megacity. Lianhuashan Park offers the best overview: climb to the summit to see Ping An Finance Centre (599 m, the world's 4th-tallest tower) rising from a skyscraper forest. At the summit, a bronze statue of Deng faces the skyline he engendered.

⏱ 90 min
2

Reserve 3-4 hours for Splendid China and the Folk Culture Village, opened in 1989: at 1:15 scale, you traverse the Great Wall, the Forbidden City, the rice terraces of Yunnan, and 100 iconic monuments. The Folk Culture Village adds ethnic performances (Miao, Dai, Tibetans, Uyghurs). It's unabashedly kitsch but a solid synthesis for those who won't cover the whole country. Combined ticket around 200 yuan.

⏱ 180 min
3

Drop by Sea World in Shekou, the old port district born in the 1980s around the Ming Hua, a French ocean liner purchased by Deng Xiaoping in 1983 and converted into a hotel-restaurant—it presides over the square's center. The district today gathers international restaurants, terraces, English-language bookshops, and expats. It's Shenzhen's other face, more cosmopolitan and relaxed. An hour's stroll at dusk.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

OCT-LOFT is a former 1980s industrial complex reconverted into a contemporary art district in 2004. Galleries, independent bookshops, architecture studios, microbreweries, and design cafés occupy the red-brick warehouses. The Shenzhen Hong Kong Bi-City Biennale of Urbanism\Architecture is held here every other year. Ideal for half a day, magnificent light in late afternoon.

⏱ 120 min

Huaqiangbei is the world's largest electronics market, nicknamed China's Silicon Valley. Across several pedestrian streets and entire shopping centers (Huaqiang Electronic World, SEG Plaza), you'll find everything: bare components, drones, smartwatches, unreleased prototypes. Even without buying anything, the experience is dizzying. Avoid smartphone scams, negotiate everything, pay via WeChat or Alipay.

⏱ 120 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 35 km · 50min
Day 8

Hong Kong

1

The Tian Tan Buddha presides over Ngong Ping plateau at 482 meters elevation on Lantau Island. Completed on December 29, 1993 after 12 years of construction, it weighs 250 tons and stands 34 meters tall including base, erected from 202 bronze pieces. You climb 268 steps to reach the seated Buddha facing north—a unique orientation, symbolizing blessings upon China. Visible from Macau on clear days.

⏱ 90 min
2

Sample the vegetarian cuisine at Po Lin Monastery, adjacent to the Big Buddha: the monks' canteen serves a set meal for 110 HKD with fermented tofu, stir-fried wheat gluten, and seasonal vegetables. The monastery was founded in 1906 by three pilgrim monks. Its great hall, lotus gardens, and three bronze Buddhas (past, present, future) make it Hong Kong's major Buddhist center. Opens at 11:30 a.m.

⏱ 75 min
3

Tai O is a Tanka fishing village—a people traditionally living on water, now settled in wooden stilt houses above the tidal channel. Artisan shrimp paste and small dried fish have been produced here for centuries. Stroll the colorful pedestrian bridge, buy dried fish from stalls, and board a 25-minute boat to try spotting the Pearl River's pink dolphins—an endangered species but visible.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Take the Ngong Ping 360 cable car from Tung Chung: 25 minutes gliding above the bay, Lantau's mountains, and the South China Sea, up to Ngong Ping village. Opt for the Crystal cabin with transparent floor (130 HKD extra). Morning offers better light and shorter lines. In case of frequent fog at the summit, skip it—you'd see nothing.

⏱ 90 min

Taste Tai O's seafood in late afternoon: dried shrimp, ginger crab, and grilled shrimp paste cakes eaten standing at stalls on the colorful bridge. The village has lived from the sea for 300 years and remains one of the last refuges of the Tanka people. Expect 100-150 HKD per person, an atmosphere completely different from Hong Kong Island. Many stalls close at 6 p.m.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 50 km · 1h15
Day 9

Hong Kong

1

Walk along the eastern dam of High Island Reservoir: before you, 100-meter cliffs striped with hexagonal prisms 1.2 meters in diameter. The Hong Kong UNESCO Global Geopark, designated in 2011, protects these rhyolitic rock columns sculpted by a supervolcano 140 million years ago—a formation ranked among the world's 100 major geological sites by the IUGS. The trail is flat, 1.5 hours round-trip from the parking lot.

⏱ 120 min
2

Pass through the narrow gate of the Hakka walled village of Kat Hing Wai, built in 1670 and surrounded by a 6-meter stone wall and moat. The gate is studded with cannons (once stolen by the British in 1899, returned in 1925). Still inhabited by 400 descendants of the Tang clan, the village testifies to Hakka distinctiveness: Han people migrating from the north, clan organization, distinct dialect, defensive autonomy.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Ten Thousand Buddhas Monastery in Sha Tin is not managed by monks but by laypeople since 1957. You climb 431 steps flanked by 500 golden luohan statues, all different, to the main temple housing over 12,800 small Buddha statues along the walls. The founder, Yuet Kai, is displayed mummified in a glass case—a rare practice in China. Free entry, allow an hour and a half.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

In the evening at Sai Kung Town, sit down on Hoi Pong Street: point at your fish, crab, or live shrimp in the tanks, and the restaurant cooks them to your specification (steamed with ginger, salt and pepper, or typhoon shelter style). It's Hong Kong's most authentic seafood experience, far from Causeway Bay's neon. Expect 400-600 HKD for two. Reservations are difficult—arrive at 6 p.m.

⏱ 120 min

Hike the Dragon's Back trail if you have the morning: 8.5 km of ridgeline between Shek O and Big Wave Bay, ranked one of Asia's most beautiful urban hikes by TIME. The trail snakes along the mountain crest facing the South China Sea, with views of Tung Lung Chau and the outer islands. Bus 9 from Shau Kei Wan to the trailhead. Allow 3 hours, easy level but start early before the heat.

⏱ 180 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 380 km · 3h15
Day 10

Meizhou

1

Begin your day at the Hakka Museum in Meizhou, housed in a replica tulou (traditional round house) inaugurated in 2008. The museum traces 1,700 years of migrations from the Yellow River plains and gives you the keys to everything that follows: over 80 million Hakka live worldwide, many having passed through this city. Statues of famous Hakka: Sun Yat-sen, Lee Kuan Yew, Deng Xiaoping (Hakka mother).

⏱ 90 min
2

Drive 30 km south of Meizhou to Yanyang Lou, a traditional Hakka horseshoe-shaped house (weilong) built around 1730 by the Luo family. You immediately see Hakka social organization rendered as architecture: communal rooms at center, bedrooms at the periphery, ancestral altar in the dominant position. The building housed up to 200 people from a single clan. An hour and a half on-site, 50 yuan ticket.

⏱ 90 min
3

Push on to Songkou, an old Hakka market town on the banks of the Mei River built during the Ming. Its Confucian academy (Yuanchang Lou), where young Hakka studied the classics before imperial examinations, has been restored and is open to visitors. Cobbled lanes and gray-brick houses recall a prosperous 19th-century rural Hakka. Quiet, lightly touristed, perfect for understanding the diaspora's other face.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Taste Hakka cuisine: yong tau foo (tofu stuffed with ground meat), salt-baked chicken cooked in a thick salt crust for 90 minutes, braised pork with pickled mustard greens (mei cai kou rou), bouncy fish balls. Hakka cuisine, shaped by centuries of migration, relies on preservation: salting, drying, fermentation. Mei Xian Wei restaurant among locals, roughly 60-100 yuan per person.

⏱ 90 min

Participate in a traditional Hakka tea ceremony, lei cha (pounded tea): green tea blended with herbs, peanuts, and seeds pounded in a mortar, served as a savory porridge—one of China's rarest tea preparations. Several teahouses in Meizhou offer the experience for 50-80 yuan. It's disconcerting but authentic, and practiced since the Han dynasty by Hakka ancestors.

⏱ 60 min
Day 11

Chaozhou

1

Be on-site at 5 p.m. to watch the dismantling of the 18 boats composing Guangji Bridge's central section—it's the spectacle. Built from 1171 onward under the Southern Song, this bridge (also called Xiangzi) has spanned the Han River for 850 years. It's one of China's Four Great Ancient Bridges, considered one of the world's first bascule bridges. Its 24 stone piers once supported as many pavilion-markets. Nighttime illumination at 8 p.m.

⏱ 75 min
2

Sit for ten minutes in the courtyard of Kaiyuan Temple, founded in 738 under Tang Emperor Xuanzong—it's one of southern China's largest Buddhist monasteries and its atmosphere remains genuinely religious: monks in saffron robes, worshippers in meditation. Its wooden halls and ancient statues distinguish it from typical museum-temples. Free entry, an hour suffices, but the silence is worth the detour.

⏱ 60 min
3

Walk up Paifang Street, the commemorative archway street aligning 23 Ming and Qing portals over 1.9 km. Each archway honors a local dignitary, an upright official, or a virtuous widow. Destroyed during the Cultural Revolution, they were rebuilt between 2006 and 2009 according to original plans. The street is lined with calligraphy shops, Chaozhou embroidery, and snack restaurants—it's the old town's backbone.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Teochew (Chaozhou) cuisine is one of China's eight great regional cuisines and the south's most refined. Must-tries: braised goose in soy sauce, hand-beaten beef balls pounded for hours (texture bounces like rubber), rice porridge with seafood, and ceremonial Kung Fu tea served in tiny cups. Pat Gua Street is the snack epicenter. Expect 60-100 yuan per person.

⏱ 90 min

Teochew opera, 580 years old, is one of China's oldest opera styles still alive. Elaborate makeup, hand-embroidered costumes, nasal singing over zither and percussion—it's demanding for European ears, but unforgettable. Check for performances at Paifang Street or the municipal theater, often Friday and Saturday evenings. Modest entry, 30-80 yuan.

⏱ 120 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 45 km · 50min
Day 12

Shantou

1

Walk through the old Xiaogongyuan quarter, nicknamed the "Little Paris of the Orient": across 2 km², hundreds of Dutch-Chinese qilou arcade houses built between 1860 and 1930 by wealthy Teochew merchants returning from overseas. Shantou was the embarkation port for the Teochew diaspora to Thailand, Cambodia, and Singapore in the late 19th century. Many buildings are crumbling, but renovation has been underway since 2014. A striking sight.

⏱ 120 min
2

Drive 35 km north of Shantou to Qianmei Village to discover the Chen Cihong Residence, a 506-room Hakka-Teochew palace built between 1870 and 1922 by a magnate from the diaspora returning from Thailand. Interior courtyards, gilded wood carvings, European stained glass, frescoes painted in gold leaf—it's the south-China version of the diaolou, even more Baroque. Allow 2 hours, 50 yuan ticket.

⏱ 120 min
3

Step into Shantou's Tianhou Temple, built in 1879 by Teochew sailors: the main hall, its permanent incense, and votive statues offered by overseas Chinese recall that the Teochew diaspora financed the region's religious life for a century. Mazu, goddess of seafarers, occupies a central place in eastern Guangdong's coastal culture.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

For your final meal, Lao Ma Gong restaurant in Xiaogongyuan or Xinghu night market: Teochew seafood congee, marinated goose, steamed dumplings stuffed with bamboo shoots, bingfen (chilled fruit jelly). The Teochew are obsessed with product freshness—an obsession shared with the Cantonese but carried to superior refinement. Expect 80 yuan per person.

⏱ 90 min

If you have time before your flight, take the 11-km bridge to Nan'ao Island, accessible since 2015: deserted beaches, Song-era Song Dading Temple, Ming anti-pirate fortifications, and tea plantations. The Teochew hid their treasures here during wars. Allow half a day. Shantou Jieyang Airport serves Beijing, Shanghai, Bangkok.
