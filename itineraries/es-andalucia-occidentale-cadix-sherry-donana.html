Winter (November-March): Mild temperatures south, cooler north, fewer crowds, lowest prices, cultural attractions, ski season Pyrenees.

Stop 1

Cadiz

Where Phoenician founders built the oldest city in the Western world on an Atlantic peninsula

★★★★☆
📍 Catedral Nueva Cádiz📍 Teatro Romano Cádiz📍 Torre Tavira Cádiz📍 Barrio del Pópulo Cádiz📍 Playa de la Caleta Cádiz📍 Castillo de San Sebastián Cádiz📍 Parque Genovés Cádiz
1

Cádiz Cathedral blends Baroque grandeur with neoclassical restraint. Its construction spanned 116 years—from 1722 to 1838—under five successive architects. The dome, sheathed in gleaming golden tiles, once served as a landmark for sailors navigating the Atlantic. In the crypt lies composer Manuel de Falla, a native son of the city.

⏱ 50 min
2

Climb the 170 steps of Torre del Poniente for a sweeping 360-degree panorama of Cádiz Bay, whitewashed rooftops, and the endless Atlantic. This vantage point makes it clear why the Phoenicians chose this peninsula in 1100 BC, making Cádiz the oldest continuously inhabited city in Western Europe.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Roman Theatre of Gades was discovered by chance in 1980 beneath the houses of El Pópulo quarter. Built in the 1st century BC by Balbus, it seated 20,000 spectators. With a diameter of 120 meters, it ranks among the largest Roman theatres in the empire, a testament to Cádiz's ancient prominence.

⏱ 30 min
4

Enter the Barrio del Pópulo through one of its three 13th-century medieval arches: Arco del Pópulo, Arco de la Rosa, or Arco de los Blancos. This oldest neighborhood in Cádiz preserves its labyrinth of narrow lanes and shaded squares, straddling Roman ruins and medieval foundations.

⏱ 30 min
5

Torre Tavira stands tallest among the 160 watchtowers that Cádiz merchants built to spot their ships arriving from the Americas. At 45 meters high, its camera obscura projects live images of the city onto a concave screen—a mesmerizing 18th-century technology still captivating visitors today.

⏱ 30 min
6

Walk along the Paseo Campo del Sur facing the ocean to reach Parque Genovés. This clifftop botanical garden showcases century-old palms, artificial waterfalls, and exotic species brought back from the Americas by Cádiz navigators during the golden age of transatlantic trade.

⏱ 25 min

Activities

La Caleta beach, the only beach in the historic center, nestles between the castles of Santa Catalina and San Sebastián. It featured in the James Bond film "Die Another Day." At sunset, colorful fishing boats and the castle silhouette create a picture-perfect scene against the amber sky.

⏱ 60 min

Step into a freiduría and order a paper cone of pescaíto frito: crispy-fried shrimp, baby squid, hake strips, and bienmesabe in golden batter. This is Cádiz's signature street food, best enjoyed standing in the lively La Viña neighborhood with a cold beer in hand.

⏱ 60 min

The Gran Teatro Falla, built in neo-Mudéjar style, hosts Cádiz Carnival every February—one of Spain's most celebrated festivals. Outside carnival season, catch a performance in this 19th-century architectural gem that seamlessly blends Moorish and Spanish influences.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 2

Jerez De La Frontera

Capital of sherry and Andalusian equestrian art at the heart of the sherry triangle

★★★☆☆
📍 Alcázar de Jerez de la Frontera📍 Catedral San Salvador Jerez📍 Real Escuela Andaluza Arte Ecuestre Jerez📍 Bodegas Tío Pepe Jerez📍 Barrio de Santiago Jerez📍 Plaza del Arenal Jerez
1

The Alcázar of Jerez, a 12th-century Almohad fortress, contains nearly every element of a Moorish palace: vaulted halls, Arab baths with starlight filtering through ceiling perforations, a tiny mosque converted into a chapel, and an ancient olive oil mill. From the tower summit, views sweep across the cathedral and Jerez's rooftops.

⏱ 50 min
2

San Salvador Cathedral rises on a platform where a mosque once stood. It mingles Gothic, Renaissance, and neoclassical styles across centuries of construction. The bell tower, likely a former minaret, forms a beautifully illuminated ensemble with the Alcázar at night. Inside, a Zurbarán painting depicts the Virgin Mary as a contemplative child.

⏱ 35 min
3

Step inside a sherry bodega to understand the solera and criaderas system: American oak barrels stacked in tiers, with wine slowly flowing from youngest to oldest, creating a perpetual blend that maintains consistency across decades. The Atlantic air from 12 kilometers away gives fino its characteristic saline tang.

⏱ 60 min
4

The Royal Andalusian School of Equestrian Art, founded in 1973, occupies a 19th-century palace. Its 1,600-seat arena hosts "Cómo bailan los caballos andaluces"—a ballet of Andalusian horses in 18th-century costumes, accompanied by traditional Spanish music in a breathtaking display of precision and grace.

⏱ 60 min
5

Wander through the Santiago quarter, birthplace of Jerez flamenco, where colorful buildings and flower-filled patios line picturesque alleys. The Gothic church of San Miguel rises at the end of a plaza where flamenco peñas keep the tradition of bulería alive with passionate performances.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Join a guided tasting of different sherry types: bone-dry, saline fino; amber amontillado with nutty aromas; dark, powerful oloroso; and rare, complex palo cortado. Jerez, El Puerto de Santa María, and Sanlúcar form the "Sherry Triangle," the only region where authentic sherry can be produced.

⏱ 75 min

Attend the equestrian show on performance days. Riders execute classical dressage and doma vaquera—the traditional horsemanship of Andalusian cattle herders—on pure Spanish horses selected for their grace, power, and connection with their riders.

⏱ 75 min

In the evening, settle into a tabanco in the old town. These traditional sherry bars serve wine straight from the cask, paired with tapas: crispy chicharrones, shrimp fritters, and cheese from the Sierra de Cádiz mountains.

⏱ 60 min
Hotel Casa Palacio María Luisa9.6

Stop 3

Sanlucar De Barrameda

Historic port of Magellan renowned for its manzanilla wine and extraordinary seafood

★★★☆☆
📍 Castillo de Santiago Sanlúcar de Barrameda📍 Bajo de Guía Sanlúcar de Barrameda📍 Plaza del Cabildo Sanlúcar de Barrameda📍 Iglesia Nuestra Señora de la O Sanlúcar📍 Palacio Medina Sidonia Sanlúcar📍 Fábrica de Hielo Sanlúcar de Barrameda
1

Santiago Castle has dominated Sanlúcar since the 15th century. Built by the Dukes of Medina Sidonia to control the Guadalquivir estuary, it preserves the Puerta de la Sirena, a sculpted Gothic arch. From the Torre del Homenaje, panoramic views embrace whitewashed houses, the river, and Doñana's marshlands across the water.

⏱ 40 min
2

Magellan set sail from Sanlúcar in 1519 on the first circumnavigation of the globe. The "Legua Cero" monument at Bajo de Guía marks this historic departure point. In the former ice factory, now Doñana's visitor center, a replica of the Nao Victoria retraces the expedition that changed the world.

⏱ 30 min
3

Climb from the Barrio Bajo to Barrio Alto through zigzagging lanes inherited from Moorish times. The 14th-century Gothic church of Nuestra Señora de la O features a richly carved Mudéjar portal. The Palace of the Dukes of Medina Sidonia, built in the 16th century atop the old Andalusian alcázar, houses exceptional historical archives.

⏱ 40 min
4

Plaza del Cabildo, Sanlúcar's heart, buzzes around its central fountain and tapas bar terraces. Whitewashed facades and wrought-iron balconies compose an authentic Andalusian scene, refreshingly off the tourist trail.

⏱ 20 min
5

Visit a manzanilla bodega, home to this unique dry white wine whose saline taste comes from humidity captured by vines exposed to Atlantic breezes. These "cathedrals of manzanilla"—vast cellars with soaring ceilings—shelter centuries of winemaking tradition passed down through generations.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Board a traditional barge from Bajo de Guía to cross the Guadalquivir estuary into Doñana National Park. The 3-hour cruise follows the river along marshlands populated by pink flamingos and gray herons, offering intimate access to one of Europe's wildest landscapes.

⏱ 180 min

Settle into a restaurant at Bajo de Guía facing the Guadalquivir and Doñana beyond. This former fishing quarter has become one of Andalusia's seafood capitals: Sanlúcar langoustines, white shrimp, coquina clams, and shrimp fritters, all washed down with ice-cold manzanilla.

⏱ 75 min

In August, Sanlúcar hosts Spain's oldest beach horse races, documented for over 200 years and formalized in 1845. At sunset, riders launch their mounts across the sand of Las Piletas beach, with Doñana as a dramatic backdrop.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 4

Vejer De La Frontera

Clifftop white village overlooking the coast, famous for its cobijada tradition

★★★☆☆
📍 Castillo de Vejer de la Frontera📍 Plaza de España Vejer de la Frontera📍 Murallas de Vejer de la Frontera📍 Iglesia Divino Salvador Vejer📍 Mirador de la Cobijada Vejer📍 Molinos de viento Vejer de la Frontera
1

Vejer de la Frontera, declared a Historic-Artistic Ensemble in 1976, reveals itself as you climb cobbled lanes to the 10th-11th century castle. Pass through its single horseshoe arch: two patios and ramparts offer plunging views over the Barbate valley and the distant sea.

⏱ 35 min
2

Medieval ramparts snake 2 kilometers around the old town. Four gates survive: Arco de la Segur, Arco de Sancho IV, Arco de la Villa, and Puerta Cerrada. The latter, facing the Jewish quarter, was walled up for centuries to protect the town from North African pirates.

⏱ 30 min
3

Plaza de España, the village heart, centers on a fountain adorned with multicolored Sevillian tiles. The 16th-century church of Divino Salvador, built atop the former mosque, retained the original minaret converted into its bell tower.

⏱ 25 min
4

Head to Mirador de la Cobijada to discover the statue of this enigmatic figure: a woman wrapped in a black cloak revealing only one eye. This vestimentary tradition of Castilian origin, dating to the 16th century, has become Vejer's symbol. The viewpoint extends to Barbate's beaches and, on clear days, to Africa.

⏱ 25 min
5

Vejer's seven windmills, built in the 1960s, stand on the windy ridge above the village. They've become the town's iconic image, offering panoramic views of the coast, El Palmar beach, and the rolling hills of La Janda.

⏱ 20 min
6

Lose yourself in the old Jewish quarter, the judería, where vaulted passages and whitewashed houses press so close they nearly touch. The 18th-century Casa del Mayorazgo displays a patio overflowing with 450 flowerpots in a riot of color.

⏱ 25 min

Activities

The Barbate and Zahara de los Atunes region perpetuates bluefin tuna fishing by almadraba, a millennia-old technique inherited from the Phoenicians. From May to June, Vejer restaurants showcase tuna in every form: encebollado, tartare, tataki, grilled belly—celebrating this ancient harvest of the sea.

⏱ 75 min

Descend to El Palmar beach, part of Vejer municipality, 8 kilometers from the village. This wild ribbon of sand pounded by Atlantic waves draws surfers and sunset lovers to one of the coast's most spectacular and unspoiled stretches.

⏱ 90 min

Sample local gastronomy in a downtown restaurant: Vejer-style chicken pie, couscous with tuna and pumpkin, or retinta—meat from indigenous cattle raised free-range in the surrounding countryside, prized for its deep flavor and tenderness.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 5

Donana

Europe's largest wetland and UNESCO site, last refuge of the Iberian lynx

★★★★☆
📍 Parque Nacional de Doñana📍 El Rocío Huelva📍 Ermita del Rocío📍 Centro de Visitantes El Acebuche Doñana📍 Marismas del Guadalquivir Doñana📍 Dunas móviles Doñana
1

Doñana National Park, created in 1969, spans 54,000 hectares of marshlands, mobile dunes, pine forests, and Mediterranean scrub at the Guadalquivir estuary. Designated a Biosphere Reserve and UNESCO World Heritage site, it protects Western Europe's largest wetland and over 360 bird species.

⏱ 30 min
2

Four-wheel-drive circuits cover 70 kilometers through the park's four ecosystems in a 4-hour journey: beach and mobile dunes reaching 30 meters high, flooded marshlands, wooded reserves, and Guadalquivir riverbanks. Guides spot flamingos, imperial eagles, red deer, fallow deer, and marsh horses in their natural habitat.

⏱ 240 min
3

The village of El Rocío, with its unpaved streets and low whitewashed houses, seems suspended in another era. The Ermita del Rocío, a Marian sanctuary, draws over a million pilgrims each spring during the Romería—one of Spain's largest religious pilgrimages.

⏱ 40 min
4

El Acebuche Visitor Center, the park's main gateway, offers marked hiking trails and bird observatories. The more remote José Antonio Valverde observatory, accessible by 4x4, features a glass wall overlooking the marshlands where flamingo colonies can be observed at water level.

⏱ 45 min
5

Palacio del Acebrón, near the Arroyo de la Rocina, houses an exhibition on the park's ecosystems. A circular trail through gallery forest allows you to interpret animal tracks and discover plant species endemic to Doñana's unique environment.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Rise before dawn for a birdwatching session. Doñana sits at a migratory crossroads between Europe, Africa, and Asia: over 500,000 waterbirds winter here annually. Flamingos, spoonbills, storks, glossy ibises, and gray herons create a perpetual spectacle of wings, color, and sound.

⏱ 120 min

With patience and considerable luck, you might glimpse the Iberian lynx, the world's most endangered feline. Around twenty individuals live in or near the park. Wooded areas crossed late in the day during the 4x4 circuit offer the best chances.

⏱ 60 min

On June 26, for over five centuries, Almonte ranchers have organized the Saca de las Yeguas: hundreds of mares and foals cross the park in a spectacular transhumance. Outside this date, horseback rides in the park's peripheral zones offer immersion in the dune and forest landscapes.

⏱ 90 min
