Santo Domingo

★★★★★
1

Santo Domingo's Zona Colonial deserves a full morning of exploration. Founded by Bartolomé Colón in 1496 on the east bank of the Ozama River, then relocated to the west bank in 1502 under Governor Nicolás de Ovando's orders, it was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1990. Start at Parque Colón, where Christopher Columbus's statue faces the Catedral Primada—the first cathedral built in the New World, begun in 1514. The entire zone spans just 11 blocks, perfect for unhurried walking.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Alcázar de Colón commands Plaza de España from its coral stone terrace. Commissioned by Diego Colón—Christopher's son—after his appointment as Governor of the Indies in 1509, the palace was built between 1510 and 1514 by 1,500 indigenous workers. Originally 55 rooms, 22 remain today across two levels. Conquistadors like Hernán Cortés gathered here to plan their expeditions. In 1586, privateer Francis Drake looted it. Allow an hour for the museum, which displays period furniture, 16th-century Flemish tapestries, and weapons of the era.

⏱ 60 min
3

Fortaleza Ozama is the oldest European military fortification still standing in the Americas. Built from 1502 at the Ozama River's mouth, it protected the port against English and Dutch pirates. Climb the Torre del Homenaje for sweeping views over the river and colonial rooftops.

⏱ 45 min
4

Calle Las Damas, considered the first paved street in the Americas, connects the fortress to the Alcázar. Named after Spanish noblewomen who strolled here during colonial times, its 16th and 17th-century facades remain remarkably preserved. This is the best stretch to absorb the city's characteristic Plateresque architecture.

⏱ 30 min
5

The National Pantheon, housed in an 18th-century former Jesuit church, holds the remains of Dominican independence heroes. The austere, solemn central nave contrasts sharply with the rest of the Zona—admission is free and the visit brief, but the building merits attention.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Head to the Malecón in late afternoon, the oceanfront boulevard stretching for kilometers along the Caribbean Sea. This is where Santo Domingo breathes: families stroll, vendors sell coco fríos, and the sea breeze finally cools the day. Continue to the Fray Antonio de Montesinos monument—honoring the Dominican friar who defended Taíno rights in 1511—a dramatic sculpture facing the ocean.

⏱ 60 min

To experience Santo Domingo like a Dominican, find a bar or terrace in the Zona at sunset to hear bachata or merengue. Calle El Conde, the pedestrian commercial street cutting east-west through the Zona, is also the best spot for dinner and observing the colonial quarter's nighttime energy.

⏱ 90 min

Punta Cana

★★★★☆
1

Playa Bávaro is Punta Cana's iconic beach—ten kilometers of white sand protected by a coral reef that ensures calm, crystalline waters. The Costa del Coco, the name for Punta Cana's entire coastline, stretches nearly 48 km. Arrive early before group excursions descend. The eastern section toward Cap Cana is noticeably less crowded.

⏱ 120 min
2

Playa Macao is Punta Cana's exception: no all-inclusive hotels, no rows of loungers, just raw beach. It's the east coast's surf spot, with consistent waves against a backdrop of coconut palms and a fishing village vibe. Count on 20 minutes' drive from Bávaro.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Book a catamaran or speedboat excursion to Isla Saona, the area's must-do. The island lies within Cotubanamá National Park—Christopher Columbus named it "Saona" in 1494. The crossing from Bayahibe takes about 30 minutes. Once there: white sand beaches, giant starfish in the natural pool, mangroves, and Mano Juan village with its sea turtle recovery center. Avoid weekends when the island can host up to 2,500 people daily.

⏱ 360 min

In the evening, wander through El Cortecito neighborhood, the only real shopping and entertainment street in the Bávaro area. Handicrafts, fish restaurants, bars—it's where local life still exists among the resort complexes.

⏱ 60 min

★★★★☆
1

Isla Catalina, about 30 minutes by boat from La Romana, is the region's premier snorkeling and diving spot. The island covers only 9.6 km² and has no permanent residents, which has preserved its underwater ecosystems. Two main dive sites: "Wall" and "Aquarius." You'll also find the wreck of the Quedagh Merchant, a 17th-century pirate ship discovered in 2007, resting at about 21 meters. Bring mask and snorkel if your excursion doesn't provide them.

⏱ 240 min
2

At Altos de Chavón, a reconstructed 16th-century Mediterranean village perched on the Río Chavón cliffs. Yes, it's a reconstruction—inaugurated in 1982—but the stone lanes, Saint Stanislaus Church, and the 5,000-seat amphitheater overlooking the valley form a genuinely spectacular ensemble. Frank Sinatra sang at the inauguration.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Return to Playa Bávaro late in the day to catch sunset from the beach. It's also the best time to try grilled fish at one of the local beach restaurants, before a final night in the Punta Cana area.

Miches

★★☆☆☆
1

Playa Esmeralda is Miches' main beach—several kilometers of coastline still largely untouched, not yet colonized by mass tourism. Some tourism professionals call it "tomorrow's Punta Cana," offering what the east coast had twenty years ago: turquoise waters, coconut palms, and silence. Arrive early to enjoy it before the day's few other visitors appear.

⏱ 120 min
2

Montaña Redonda is a rounded summit at 329 meters elevation with 360-degree views over Samaná Bay, Limón and Redonda lagoons, the town of Miches, and El Seibo. Hammocks and swings are installed at the summit for photos. It's one of the island's most photogenic viewpoints. Wear good shoes—the trail gets muddy in wet season.

⏱ 90 min
3

Laguna Limón is a 7 km² freshwater lagoon at the heart of Las Lagunas National Park, accessible by kayak or horseback. Its waters are surrounded by tropical forests rich in endemic bird species. Neighboring Laguna Redonda has the distinction of connecting directly to the ocean.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Punta Cana to Miches road along the northeast coast passes wild beaches and limestone cliffs. Make stops at Playa Limón, a semi-wild beach backed by a lagoon, and Uvero Alto for coastal views from the heights. Count on about 2 hours travel time with stops.

⏱ 60 min

Miches remains a genuine fishing village. In the evening, stroll streets animated by colorful murals depicting local history, sample fresh seafood at waterfront restaurants—menus change based on the day's catch.

Las Terrenas

★★★★☆
1

The Boulevard del Atlántico, the winding road descending toward Las Terrenas from the heights, offers a series of ascending curves with plunging views over coconut groves and sea. Several overlooks line this stretch—stop at Mirador Cosón for views of Playa Cosón, a 6-km golden sand beach reached in minutes on foot from the roadside.

⏱ 60 min
2

Playa Bonita lives up to its name. Located a few kilometers west of Las Terrenas village, it's a beach with palms, calm sea, and a handful of small guesthouses. Less crowded than the main beach, it's ideal for a peaceful late afternoon. The sunset here is particularly photogenic.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Las Terrenas is a Franco-Dominican village—dozens of French and European expats settled here in the 1980s and transformed this small fishing port into a bohemian destination. Wander the center's lanes mixing beach restaurants, artisan galleries, and street stalls. It's unlike anywhere else in the Dominican Republic—an atmosphere reminiscent of the Seychelles or Nosy Be.

⏱ 90 min

In the evening, head to Las Terrenas' main beach for a sunset walk. Beach bars come alive as daylight fades. It's also a good time to book tomorrow's excursion to El Limón waterfall—leave early in the morning to avoid crowds.

⏱ 60 min

Las Terrenas

★★★★☆
1

El Limón waterfall is the essential excursion on the Samaná Peninsula. Perched at about 300 meters altitude above El Limón village—15-20 km from Las Terrenas—this 40 to 52-meter waterfall (sources vary) plunges into a natural swimming basin. The trail crosses cacao, coffee, and coconut plantations. Go on foot or horseback, count on 30 to 50 minutes depending on pace. Arrive before 9am to have the waterfall to yourself—groups arrive later. Bring closed-toe shoes; the trail gets muddy in wet season.

⏱ 180 min
2

Los Haitises National Park, accessible by boat from Sánchez or Samaná, is one of the Caribbean's most spectacular natural reserves. Its name means "land of mountains" in Taíno. Dozens of limestone mogotes emerge from the mangroves, sheltering caves with Taíno petroglyphs and seabird colonies (frigatebirds, pelicans, herons). The karst formations recall Vietnam's Halong Bay. The boat excursion lasts about 4 hours.

⏱ 240 min

Activities

Depending on your return time from El Limón or Los Haitises, head to Playa El Valle, a wild beach accessed by dirt track, known to locals but virtually ignored by tourists. SUV access recommended. The underwater environment here is among the peninsula's best for snorkeling.

Puerto Plata

★★★☆☆
1

Fortaleza San Felipe has dominated Puerto Plata's port since 1577. Built on orders of Philip II of Spain in 1564 to protect the port from pirate attacks, it's the only 16th-century remnant still standing in the city—the rest was destroyed during the restoration wars. At the courtyard's center, a pit filled with water and spikes recalls that the fortress didn't joke with invaders. Admission is nominal; the view over the bay and lighthouse is worth the visit.

⏱ 45 min
2

Puerto Plata's cable car is the only one of its kind in all the Caribbean. It climbs to Loma Isabel de Torres—793 meters elevation—in 10 to 15 minutes suspended above tropical forest. At the summit, a replica of Rio's Christ the Redeemer (inaugurated in 1975, a gift from Brazil), a botanical garden with 600 plant species, and panoramic views over Puerto Plata, the Atlantic, and the northern mountain range. Go in the morning—the site is often shrouded in clouds in the afternoon. Cabins only depart on demand and hold just 17 people.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Amber Museum, housed in a 19th-century Victorian villa, presents a collection of Dominican amber pieces among the country's finest. Dominican amber comes from a now-extinct pine species—some pieces contain insects or plant fragments tens of millions of years old. The shop offers excellent jewelry, as well as larimar, the blue semi-precious stone unique in the world, extracted in the southwest.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The 27 Waterfalls of Damajagua, about thirty kilometers from Puerto Plata, is the north coast's most memorable adventure excursion. Count on 45 minutes climbing through tropical forest, then a descent via natural slides over successive pools carved in rock. Most circuits stop at the 7th or 12th waterfall—count on about 4 to 5 hours for all 27, reserved for people in good physical condition.

⏱ 240 min

Late in the day, stroll through Puerto Plata's Parque Central Independencia, built in 1879 under President Gregorio Luperón. The white San Felipe Cathedral dominates the plaza, surrounded by the pastel-colored Victorian architecture characterizing the city—one of the Caribbean's finest concentrations of Victorian houses.

Santiago De Los Caballeros

★★★☆☆
1

You'll see the Monumento from afar as soon as you enter Santiago—this 67-meter tower dominates the city from a hill at 175 meters elevation. Commissioned by dictator Trujillo in 1944 under the name "Monument to the Peace of Trujillo," it was renamed after his assassination in 1961 as the "Monument to the Heroes of the Restoration." Inside, a staircase of 365 steps leads to the summit—Trujillo wanted to signify that he watched over his people every day of the year. The interior frescoes were created by Spanish painter Vela Zanetti. The view over Santiago and the Cibao Valley justifies the ascent.

⏱ 60 min
2

Centro León is the most important Dominican art and culture museum in the country. Founded by the León family, whose tobacco empire built much of Santiago's fortune, the Center brings together permanent collections of Dominican contemporary art, pre-Columbian ethnography, and tobacco history. The building's architecture itself is an attraction.

⏱ 90 min
3

Fábrica La Aurora is the oldest cigar factory still operating in the Dominican Republic, founded in 1903. Santiago is the world capital of premium cigars—the country is the world's leading exporter of high-end cigars. Guided tours show the manufacturing process: leaf selection, hand assembly, aging in cellars. If you don't smoke, the visit remains fascinating.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Explore Santiago's Mercado Modelo—one of the country's largest covered markets—for handicrafts, spices, local products, and a popular atmosphere far removed from Punta Cana's shopping centers. Count on an hour to wander among stalls of larimar, amber, carved wood, and cigars.

⏱ 60 min

In the evening, head to the neighborhood around the Monumento for Santiago's nightlife. The country's second-largest city, with 700,000 inhabitants, has a very active music scene—bachata, merengue típico, and Dominican rock coexist in bars and clubs with a very local vibe.

⏱ 90 min

Constanza

★★☆☆☆
1

Constanza will surprise you on arrival: at 1,200 meters elevation, Hispaniola's highest town resembles an Andean village more than a Caribbean settlement. Nicknamed "the Switzerland of the Caribbean," it's surrounded by fields of strawberries, raspberries, ornamental flowers, and temperate vegetables. In 2018, a 7-cm snowfall was recorded here—in the Caribbean. Tour the local market to buy artisanal strawberry jams and fresh flowers at unbeatable prices.

⏱ 60 min
2

Salto de Aguas Blancas is one of the Caribbean's highest waterfalls. The water takes on a white foamy color as it tumbles over rock faces, hence the name. It's located about fifteen kilometers from Constanza in Valle Nuevo National Park—count on about an hour round trip from town.

⏱ 90 min
3

Valle Nuevo National Park (also called Juan Bautista Pérez Rancier) is a high plateau at 2,200 meters, covered with pine forests and cloud forests. Over 500 plant species live here, including 138 endemics impossible to see elsewhere. In winter, morning frosts coat the landscape. At the park's heart, the Pirámides Ciclópeas—pyramidal structures carved by Spanish artisans in 1958 to commemorate road construction—constitute a unique curiosity.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

The Jarabacoa-Constanza road (DR-12) is one of the country's most spectacular: tight hairpins, cliffs, bridges over gorges, and vegetation transitioning from tropical palms to mountain pines in a matter of kilometers. Take time to stop at viewpoints along this mountain stretch.

Barahona

★★☆☆☆
1

The Barahona-Enriquillo coastal road is one of the island's most beautiful. The Sierra de Bahoruco mountains plunge into the Caribbean Sea forming spectacular cliffs, and the road winds between the two offering breathtaking views. Founded in the early 19th century, the city of Barahona is nicknamed "the fiancée of the Caribbean" or "the Pearl of the South."

⏱ 90 min
2

Playa San Rafael, about twenty kilometers south of Barahona, is a white pebble beach where a river flows directly into the Caribbean Sea. The natural freshwater pool formed at the mouth is refreshing even in summer. Dominicans gather here in numbers on weekends—on weekdays, the place is quiet.

⏱ 90 min
3

Los Patos is unique in the Caribbean: the river of the same name is one of the world's shortest, and its freshwater mingles with sea waves just meters from its source. The beach is simple but the icy freshwater swim after the road's heat is unforgettable.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Barahona is the only place in the world where larimar is extracted—this blue-green semi-precious stone that recalls the Caribbean Sea. The mines are in the mountains about twenty kilometers from town. Workshops in town allow you to see the cutting and polishing process. If you're seeking an authentic, unique souvenir, this is the place.

⏱ 60 min

Barahona's waterfront promenade in late afternoon offers direct views of the Sierra de Bahoruco's steep cliffs and the Caribbean Sea's horizon. Fishermen return with their pirogues loaded with lobsters and fish that local restaurants serve that same evening.

Pedernales

★★★☆☆
1

Bahía de las Águilas is the Dominican Republic's most remote and pristine beach—and probably one of the Caribbean's most beautiful. Eight kilometers of white sand inside Jaragua National Park, with no hotels, no bars, no umbrellas. It's 25 km from Pedernales, accessible by 4x4 from Cabo Rojo or by speedboat. Manatees and hawksbill turtles frequent these waters. Bring food and water—nothing is available on-site, except a local vendor who can bring a meal by reservation.

⏱ 300 min
2

Laguna de Oviedo is a saltwater lagoon in Jaragua National Park with a salt concentration three times higher than the sea. It's home to thousands of flamingos—spectacular during breeding season—as well as pelicans, herons, rhinoceros iguanas, and turtles. A boat excursion through the mangroves lasts about 2 hours.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Pedernales province borders Haiti, and this proximity shows in the local cuisine—Haitian Creole influences blend with Dominican dishes. Simple downtown restaurants serve lobsters, shrimp, and red snappers caught that morning, served with plantains and rice. Some of the country's best seafood, at very accessible prices.

⏱ 90 min

Pedernales

★★★☆☆
1

Lago Enriquillo is the Caribbean's largest lake and one of the world's few hypersaline lakes—its salinity is two to three times higher than the sea. It's located about 40 meters below sea level, making the area the Caribbean's lowest point. The boat excursion to Isla Cabritos at the lake's center allows you to observe adult American crocodiles, rhinoceros iguanas (a species endemic to Hispaniola), and flamingos. The contrast between cactus vegetation and the lake's shimmering surface is striking.

⏱ 180 min
2

Hoyo de Pelempito is nicknamed the "Dominican Grand Canyon"—a vertiginous chasm in the Sierra de Bahoruco mountains, accessible by mountain road from Pedernales. The viewpoint at the precipice's edge embraces the entire Sierra cloud forest down to the Caribbean Sea. One of the country's least-visited panoramas.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Sierra de Bahoruco National Park, inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List as part of the Jaragua-Bahoruco-Enriquillo Transboundary Biosphere Reserve, shelters over 50% of the Dominican Republic's orchids and exceptional biodiversity: dry forests, cloud forests, Caribbean pines. Guided hikes of 3 to 6 hours traverse the Sierra from various access points.

⏱ 240 min

Santo Domingo

★★★★★
1

Parque Los Tres Ojos (The Three Eyes) is an astonishing karst site a few kilometers from the airport: three freshwater lagoons in limestone caves connected to each other. The waters are bluish, the walls covered with stalagmites, and the atmosphere almost mystical. Worth doing if your flight is in the afternoon—the site can be visited in under an hour.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Faro a Colón is the gigantic mausoleum shaped like a Latin cross built in 1992 for the 500th anniversary of the discovery of the Americas. Its luminous cross projected into the sky is visible for dozens of kilometers at night. This is supposedly where Christopher Columbus's remains are preserved—though Seville and Havana make the same claim. The mystery remains unsolved.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Final stroll through the Zona Colonial for those with late flights—purchases of cigars, larimar, and amber in the historic quarter's artisan shops, last Dominican coffees at Parque Colón, and taxi departure to Las Américas Airport (SDQ) about 25 km from downtown.

⏱ 90 min
