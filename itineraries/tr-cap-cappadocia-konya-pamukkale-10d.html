☀️ Weather▾

Best time: April-May and September-October, temperatures 15-25°C, clear skies for hot air balloons in Cappadocia. Avoid July-August (scorching heat in Konya and Pamukkale, 35-40°C) and December-February (harsh frost in Cappadocia, possible snow). Large day-night temperature swings at altitude.

📋 Notes▾

A diagonal traverse of Central Anatolia, from the white travertine terraces of Pamukkale to the fairy chimneys of Cappadocia, via the capital of Roman sculpture (Aphrodisias, UNESCO), the Sufi city of Konya and the Silk Road embodied in the Sultanhanı caravanserai. A rare itinerary that strings together three distinct UNESCO sites, an underground city plunging 85 metres deep and a sunrise hot-air balloon flight - without ever touching the coastal resort circuits.

Pamukkale

★★★★★
1

Terraced limestone formations created by 17 thermal springs, some reaching 45°C. As the water cools, it deposits calcium carbonate, forming white stepped pools cascading down a 200-metre hillside. Listed as a UNESCO World Heritage site since 1988. Walk barefoot on the authorised sections - shoes must be carried in a bag.

⏱ 90 min
2

Built during the reign of Septimius Severus (2nd-3rd century), this theatre could seat 15,000 spectators. The stage is adorned with bas-reliefs depicting a ritual procession and a sacrifice to Artemis of Ephesus. From the upper tiers, the view over the white terraces of Pamukkale is breathtaking.

⏱ 45 min
3

One of the largest ancient necropolises in Anatolia, with over 1,200 tombs spreading north of the city. Marble sarcophagi, tumuli, monumental family tombs: each type reflects the social status of its occupants. The Greek inscriptions on the sarcophagi are a precious source of information about daily life in the ancient city.

⏱ 45 min
4

This thermal pool filled with toppled ancient columns is open for swimming. The 36°C mineral water floats between submerged marble drums and capitals, remnants of the Roman portico that collapsed during an earthquake. Access to swim requires a separate ticket beyond the general entrance fee.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Climb the basins from the south entrance (South Gate) all the way to the top of the plateau. The water is cold in the morning, warmer in the afternoon. Bring a waterproof bag for your shoes. The surface can be slippery: walk carefully, especially on areas that are still wet.

⏱ 60 min

Stay until evening: when the sun drops over the Denizli plain, the travertines turn gold and orange. The Byzantine ramparts on the eastern side offer an unobstructed viewpoint, away from the tour groups who leave by bus from 5pm onwards.

⏱ 45 min

Pamukkale

★★★★★
1

262 metres long and 50 metres wide, this stadium with two curved ends could accommodate 30,000 spectators - while the ancient city's population barely reached 10,000, testimony to its regional importance. It's the best-preserved ancient stadium in the world. It hosted Greek games, festivals and public votes.

⏱ 30 min
2

A monumental gateway with four rows of Corinthian columns, built in the mid-2nd century. It marked the entrance to the sanctuary of Aphrodite for pilgrims' processions. Restored to 85% from original pieces between 1982 and 1990, it's now the site's most photographed image.

⏱ 20 min
3

Built in the 3rd century BC, this white marble Ionic temple was transformed into a Christian basilica around AD 500. A unique architectural operation: the temple was literally turned inside out, with its columns reoriented. The 14 columns still standing allow you to read both historical phases simultaneously.

⏱ 30 min
4

Housed within the site itself, this museum presents an exceptional collection of marble sculptures: imperial portraits, reliefs from the Sebasteion (a complex dedicated to the cult of Julio-Claudian emperors), statues from the local school. It's here you understand why sculptors from Aphrodisias were sought after throughout the Empire.

⏱ 60 min
5

A cult complex dedicated to Augustus and the Julio-Claudian emperors, built in the 1st century. Its two colonnaded galleries on three superimposed levels were decorated with sculpted reliefs depicting mythological scenes and imperial conquests. It's one of the best-documented sets of narrative reliefs from the Roman world.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Arrive at opening time to enjoy the site before the rare tour groups that turn up in early afternoon. Morning light reveals the sculpted details of the Tetrapylon, particularly on the western facade. The site covers several hectares: allow 3 to 4 hours to see everything without rushing.

⏱ 240 min

A few kilometres northeast of the site lie the quarries that fed all of Aphrodisias's sculptural production. White rocks exposed in the open air, on the mountainside. A short detour that gives a concrete sense of the complete chain, from raw stone to masterpiece.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 240 km · 4h

Konya

★★★☆☆
1

Identified and excavated in 2010, this three-aisled 4th-century basilica is the seventh of the seven churches of Asia mentioned in the Book of Revelation. Its north and south aisles are paved with mosaics. The baptistry is in opus sectile. It's now one of the main Christian pilgrimage sites in Turkey.

⏱ 45 min
2

Built in the Hellenistic period and recently restored (2022), this theatre held 8,000 spectators. It was used until the 7th century AD. From the cavea, you can glimpse the white terraces of Pamukkale in the distance, a view that was already worth the journey for ancient inhabitants.

⏱ 30 min
3

The western agora covers 35,000 m². Its back wall, 100 metres long and 11 metres high, was covered with frescoes - a rare case for a commercial agora. The colonnaded street bordering it was the city's main north-south artery, 1,500 metres long.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Laodicea is a living archaeological site: some areas are being restored, others have just reopened. Allow 1.5 to 2 hours to see the essentials. The audio guide available on site helps distinguish what's ancient from what's been reconstructed - the difference isn't always obvious at first glance.

⏱ 120 min

The route via Isparta and Lake Eğirdir lengthens the journey slightly but crosses one of Turkey's most beautiful mountain lakes, ringed by pines and orchards. Lake Eğirdir sits at about 1,000 metres altitude - a chance to take a lakeside lunch break before resuming the drive to Konya.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 1 km · 15min

Konya

★★★☆☆
1

The former tekke (monastery) of the Mevlevi dervishes houses the tomb of Jalaluddin Rumi, covered with an enormous turban symbolising his spiritual authority. The turquoise dome dates from 1274. The museum preserves 13th-century manuscripts, ancient carpets, musical instruments used during sema ceremonies, and Rumi's personal belongings. The Semahane - the hall where the ritual dance was performed - is also visible. Remove your shoes and cover your head (women) before entering the mausoleum.

⏱ 90 min
2

Built in 1221 under Sultan Alaeddin Keykubad, this mosque is one of Turkey's oldest Seljuk mosques. It crowns Alaeddin Hill, a Neolithic tell inhabited for 7,000 years, which later became the citadel of the Seljuk kings. In the neighbouring enclosure lie the tombs of eight Seljuk sultans as well as the remains of the 12th-century royal palace.

⏱ 45 min
3

This Seljuk theological school built in 1251 by vizier Celaleddin Karatay now houses the Museum of Ceramic Tiles. The highlight of the visit is the ceiling of the central hall: a dome entirely covered with blue and white star-patterned tiles from the 13th century. The entrance portal is a classic example of Seljuk carved stone ornamentation.

⏱ 45 min
4

This other 13th-century Seljuk medrese owes its name to its slender minaret decorated with glazed bricks. Now a lapidary museum, it displays stone and wood sculptures from the Seljuk period, including animal reliefs recovered from the city walls.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Sema ceremonies - the ritual meditation dance of the Mevlevi dervishes, listed as UNESCO intangible heritage - take place on Saturday evenings at the Mevlana Cultural Centre (Mevlana Kültür Merkezi), near the museum. In summer (June-September), free ceremonies are held on Thursday evenings in the Mevlana Museum garden. Arrive 30 minutes early to find a seat.

⏱ 90 min

Konya is famous for two specialties: etli ekmek, a thin flatbread topped with minced meat and baked in a wood-fired oven (eat with your fingers, that's the local rule), and fırın kebabı, lamb slowly braised in the oven. Restaurants around the central square also serve tantuni (cumin-spiced sautéed meat in thin bread) and various honey pastries.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 110 km · 1h30

Guzelyurt

★★★★☆
1

Built between 1229 and 1236 by Sultan Alaeddin Keykubad I, following plans by a Damascus architect. After a fire, it was restored and expanded in 1278 to reach 4,800 m² - Turkey's largest caravanserai. The structure comprises two parts: an open-air courtyard used in summer and a heated covered hall reserved for winter. The monumental entrance, 13 metres high in sculpted marble, is the finest surviving example of Seljuk portal art. A mosque raised on arches sits at the centre of the courtyard.

⏱ 60 min
2

A 5.5-kilometre valley carved into volcanic tuff, sheltering 28 rock-cut churches dating from the 4th to 11th centuries. Saint Gregory of Nazianzus, one of the Cappadocian Fathers, chose his episcopal seat here in the 4th century to spread Christianity across Anatolia. The Büyük Kilise Cami, former St Gregory's Church built in 385, converted to a mosque in 1924, is the first monument past the entrance.

⏱ 90 min
3

Accessible from the very entrance to Monastery Valley, this underground city is distinguished from the region's larger cities by its vertical connection system between levels (vertical shafts rather than horizontal galleries). It contains storage spaces, a rock-cut church, and rooms high enough to shelter camels, suggesting use as an underground caravanserai.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The caravanserai is well enough preserved for you to roam freely through the courtyard, stalls, covered space and climb to observe the central mosque from its arches. Allow 45 to 60 minutes. The acoustics under the covered vault are particularly gripping: a rare chance to grasp what Silk Road hospitality meant.

⏱ 60 min

The town of Güzelyurt preserves its houses carved into the rock and its Ottoman Greek facades, abandoned since the 1924 population exchange. Wander without a map: the alleyways climb towards a viewpoint over the valley with a view of Mount Hasan (3,268 m). In the village centre, the former Orthodox church converted to a mosque still serves Friday worshippers.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 25 km · 30min

Goreme

★★★★★
1

A volcanic canyon 14 kilometres long and 100 metres deep, ranked among Cappadocia's greatest natural and archaeological sites. Over a hundred dwellings and rock-cut churches are carved into the tuff cliffs. The best-preserved frescoes are found in the Ağaçaltı Kilisesi (Church under the Trees), Yılanlı Kilisesi (Serpent Church) and Kokar Kilisesi. The Melendiz River flows at the bottom, lined with poplars and vegetable gardens.

⏱ 210 min
2

Carved into a volcanic formation at the northern end of Ihlara Valley, Selime is Cappadocia's largest rock-cut monastery. It comprises a cathedral with two naves, kitchens with chimneys cut into the rock, stables and monastic cells on several levels. The complex is integrated into a cliff that takes on the appearance of a natural castle when viewed from afar.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Descend into the canyon via the main entrance at Ihlara village (360 steps). Walk back up at Belisırma after 3 to 4 kilometres walking along the river - that's where the best frescoes are concentrated, in the shade of poplars. Riverside restaurants in Belisırma serve local trout: perfect for lunch.

⏱ 180 min

The site isn't comprehensively signposted - explore at your own pace, climb the stairs carved into the rock, enter the halls. The rock-cut cathedral is naturally lit through openings carved in the cliff. From the top of the rock, the view over the Cappadocian plain, the lava villages and the silhouette of Mount Hasan completes the day.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 90 km · 1h15

Goreme

★★★★★
1

A rock-cut monastic complex inhabited from the 4th to 13th centuries, recognised as a UNESCO World Heritage site in 1985. The marked circuit passes through some thirty spaces: churches, chapels, refectories, cells, kitchens. Frescoes cover the vaults with gospel scenes - Nativity, Betrayal of Judas, Crucifixion - whose colours remain vivid despite the mutilation of eyes, performed superstitiously by villagers who feared the evil eye. Photography is forbidden inside all the churches.

⏱ 120 min
2

The museum's most famous church. Its name comes from the fact that it originally had only one small window. This lack of light has preserved its 11th-century frescoes in a remarkable way: Christ Pantocrator, Nativity, Transfiguration, Betrayal by Judas and Crucifixion are still astonishingly vivid in colour. A 14-year restoration was needed to remove layers of pigeon droppings. Separate ticket in addition to the main ticket.

⏱ 30 min
3

50 metres below the museum entrance (same ticket), this is Göreme's largest and most remarkable church for its frescoes. It results from the fusion of two successive churches: the old one (AD 910) with its complete narrative cycle of Christ's life, and the new one (960-1010 AD) decorated by a master from Constantinople, in deep red and blue tones.

⏱ 30 min
4

One of the best-preserved on the main circuit, with frescoes on the theme of Christ's life and the apostles arranged around a central dome. The colours - ochres, blues, greens - have weathered the centuries well.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

The site has explanatory panels in English but a local guide helps you understand Byzantine iconography, distinguish pre-iconoclastic from post-iconoclastic frescoes and identify scenes without exhausting yourself searching. Independent guides offer their services at the museum entrance. Allow 1.5 to 2 hours with a guide for the complete circuit.

⏱ 120 min

In the evening, head down into the village itself. Göreme is organised around a bazaar and alleyways where troglodyte hotels pierce directly into the cliff. Find a rooftop terrace to watch the sunset over the surrounding fairy chimneys - the colours shift from yellow to fiery orange in a matter of minutes. The village also has several restaurants serving testi kebabı (stew cooked and served in sealed pottery).

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 5 km · 10min

Goreme

★★★★★
1

Cappadocia's highest point at 1,300 metres altitude. This volcanic pinnacle has been hollowed out with galleries, apartments, chapels and storerooms connected by a labyrinth of 20 levels, from the Hittite era (15th century BC) to the last Greek inhabitants in the early 20th century. From the summit, the view embraces Göreme and its fairy chimneys on one side, the Central Anatolian plain on the other.

⏱ 60 min
2

The valley connecting Uçhisar to Göreme owes its name to the pigeon houses carved into the tuff cliffs, by the hundreds. These cavities were used to collect pigeon droppings, a precious fertiliser for Cappadocian vineyards and orchards. Today, some pigeon houses are still active. The 3.5-kilometre walk between Uçhisar and Göreme follows the tuff river, between vineyards and orchards.

⏱ 90 min
3

On the south bank of the Kızılırmak (the Red River, Turkey's longest), Avanos has been Cappadocia's pottery centre since antiquity. The red clay extracted from the river banks is thrown on foot-powered wheels, using a technique handed down since Hittite times. Each workshop is family-run: you'll be shown the different stages (throwing, drying, firing, glazing) and can try for yourself.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

This 3.5-kilometre walk (about 1 hour) starts from the bottom of Uçhisar and arrives in Göreme. The path descends gently between cliffs decorated with pigeon houses, passes abandoned vineyards and partially collapsed troglodyte houses. No signposting required - just follow the valley floor. Prefer early morning for light and coolness.

⏱ 60 min

Most Avanos workshops offer free demonstrations then, for a modest sum, the chance to shape your own piece on the wheel. The red clay from the Kızılırmak is dense and responsive - harder to work than white clay. Pieces can be shipped home if you don't wish to carry them.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 30 km · 30min

Goreme

★★★★★
1

Turkey's deepest accessible underground city: 8 open levels out of a total of 18, descending to 85 metres. It could shelter up to 20,000 people with their animals and food reserves. The Phrygians laid its foundations in the 8th-7th century BC; the Byzantines then considerably expanded it. Circular stone doors could be closed from the inside, blocking each level independently. A cruciform church occupies the 3rd and 4th levels. The city was connected by an 8 to 9-kilometre tunnel to the city of Kaymaklı.

⏱ 75 min
2

Devrent Valley can be explored freely (no ticket required) and explored on foot in 30 to 45 minutes. The pink tuff formations here take unexpected shapes depending on viewing angle: a camel, a skull, a seal, a face. The rock is lighter and softer than elsewhere - the place is renowned for photography in slanting light.

⏱ 45 min
3

Paşabağ is famous for its triple-headed fairy chimneys (a single chimney with three superimposed caps), Cappadocia's most photographed. Hermit monks from the 9th-10th centuries carved cells into the rock - some are still visible halfway up the columns. The valley's vegetation (vines, apricot trees) contrasts with the white formations.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The visitor circuit follows red arrows (descent) and blue arrows (ascent). The corridors are narrow and sometimes low-ceilinged - bring clothes you don't mind getting dirty and closed shoes. The interior temperature is stable at around 13°C whatever the season. The lower levels are the most impressive: the circular stone doors, the descending ventilation shaft and the large barrel-vaulted hall on the 2nd level.

⏱ 75 min

Rose Valley (Güllüdere Vadısı) catches fire at sunset: the ferruginous rock turns vivid orange-red for 20 to 30 minutes. From the viewpoint above Çavuşin, the view encompasses the fairy chimneys and glowing tuff terraces. The nearby Red Valley (Kızılçukur) offers another, wilder angle, with an easy 2-kilometre walk.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 1 km · 5min

Goreme

★★★★★
1

Cappadocia is one of the world's best destinations for ballooning thanks to the reliability of morning thermal winds, the density of geological formations and atmospheric clarity. Hundreds of balloons rise simultaneously at dawn from different points across the region, creating a spectacle both from the air and from the ground. Flight duration is about 1 hour; the complete journey (transfer + inflation + flight + champagne) lasts 2.5 to 3 hours. Flights can be cancelled due to wind. Book at least a week in advance in high season (April-October).

⏱ 90 min
2

An easy 2 to 4-kilometre walk through a valley carved with pink fairy chimneys and ancient troglodyte houses. The trail starts from the Çavuşin viewpoint and descends towards Göreme, revealing rock formations melting in the early morning hours. Several frescoed rock chapels are accessible along the way.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Kayseri Airport (ASR) is about 75 km from Göreme (1 hour drive). Nevşehir Airport (NAV) is closer (40 km, 40 min) but serves fewer domestic flights. Direct shuttles from Göreme serve both airports. If your flight is in late afternoon, you have time for a final market visit in Göreme village or a last pottery workshop in Avanos.

⏱ 90 min

After ballooning and the morning walk, enjoy the village calm before the day's coaches arrive. A coffee on a troglodyte terrace, a market browse for local spices (saffron from Safranbolu, pistachios, tea), a final plate of mantı (Turkish ravioli): Göreme deserves an unhurried farewell.

⏱ 60 min
