Medan

★★★☆☆
1

Start with the Tjong A Fie Mansion (Jl. Ahmad Yani No.105), a 35-room residence spanning 8,000 m² built in 1895 by a Hakka merchant who arrived in Sumatra with nothing and became the island's first millionaire. Tjong A Fie (1860–1921) landed in Medan in 1875 with a few silver coins sewn into his belt, built an empire in tobacco and plantations, employed over 10,000 workers, and personally financed the construction of both the Masjid Raya mosque and the Maimun Palace. Inside, dark imported woodwork, mother-of-pearl marquetry, Art Nouveau chandeliers and Taoist shrines collide — a cultural collision that says everything about colonial Medan. Allow an hour and take the included guided tour.

⏱ 75 min
2

Steps away, the Maimun Palace (Istana Maimun) is the residence of the Deli Sultanate, built between 1887 and 1891 by Sultan Ma'mun Al Rashid Perkasa Alamyah to plans drawn by the Dutch architect Theodoor Van Erp. Its 2,772 m² blend Malay heritage, Mughal architecture, Spanish furniture and Italian ornamentation — a colonial syncretism you won't find anywhere else in Sumatra. Only the throne room is open to the public; it's here you'll see the royal kris and a collection of court ceremony photographs frozen in time.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Grand Mosque Masjid Raya Al Mashun, just across from the palace, was jointly funded by the sultanate and Tjong A Fie himself — a telling detail about the alliances of the era. Built between 1906 and 1909, it combines Middle Eastern architecture, Indian influences and Spanish elements in an octagonal floor plan with four cardinal wings. Remove your shoes at the entrance and step inside: the mosaics and arabesque work around the mihrab are well worth the stop.

⏱ 45 min
4

Finish with the Sri Mariamman Hindu Temple (Jl. H. Zainul Arifin), founded in 1884 — Medan's oldest. Its multi-tiered Dravidian gopuram covered in colorful deities looks wildly out of place in this commercial neighborhood. It's a reminder of the large Tamil community brought here by the Dutch to work the 19th-century plantations. Entry is free; if a ceremony is underway, stay discreetly to one side.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Stroll along Jalan Ahmad Yani in the historic Kesawan quarter, the colonial-era artery where Dutch facades, Chinese signboards and modern cafes sit side by side. This is where Medan reveals its multicultural DNA: Batak, Malay, Chinese, Tamil and Dutch have all left their mark within the same 500 meters. In the evening, head to the Merdeka Walk or the street stalls around Merdeka Square for local food — the nasi goreng batak here is nothing like Bali's version.

⏱ 90 min

Medan

★★★☆☆
1

The North Sumatra Museum (Museum Negeri Sumatera Utara) deserves a full morning. Housed in a building shaped like a traditional rumah adat, it spans everything from the island's earliest Neolithic civilizations to the Hindu-Buddhist and later Islamic kingdoms. The sections on the Batak Toba, Karo and Mandailing peoples provide essential context before heading into the Highlands. Allow 90 minutes.

⏱ 90 min
2

Take a walk through the Indian quarter (Little India) around Jalan Semarang: a dense cluster of textiles, spices and temples that recalls the decades when Medan was a city of Tamil coolies employed in rubber and tobacco plantations. The Shri Mariamman Hindoo temple (not to be confused with yesterday's) and the storefronts of sari shops and saffron vendors give this corner of town a character all its own.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Dive into Petisah Market, Medan's busiest. Multiple levels of stalls overflow with exotic fruit, Batak spices, grilled meats and clothing — arrive early in the morning to catch the deliveries and the haggling. This is where you'll find fresh durian at reasonable prices: Medan is reputed to have the best durian in Sumatra, a local specialty that residents eat in the open street with unabashed pride.

⏱ 90 min

Prepare for tomorrow's departure to Bukit Lawang: check with your accommodation about transport (roughly 3 hours by road) and book your jungle guide in advance. Local operators offer one-day or multi-night treks into Gunung Leuser National Park — an overnight trek is highly recommended to boost your chances of spotting orangutans away from the busy areas.

⏱ 60 min

Bukit Lawang

★★★★☆
1

Bukit Lawang is the gateway village to Gunung Leuser National Park, one of only two wild areas on Earth where the Sumatran orangutan (Pongo abelii) still roams free. The park covers 7,927 km² across the provinces of Aceh and North Sumatra and was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2004 — with an "in danger" tag added in 2011 due to deforestation. A rehabilitation center was established in 1973 to reintroduce orangutans rescued from captivity back into the wild. Settle into your riverside guesthouse on the Bohorok River and familiarize yourself with the park rules: never touch the animals, keep a minimum distance, and never feed them.

⏱ 60 min
2

In the late afternoon, do a reconnaissance at the park entrance and information center to understand the accessible zones and the species present. The park is home to 130 mammal species including the Sumatran orangutan, Sumatran tiger, Sumatran elephant, Sumatran rhinoceros and sun bear — plus 285 bird species including the rhinoceros hornbill. Statistically, your chances of encountering a tiger or rhinoceros are vanishingly small; orangutans and Thomas leaf monkeys, on the other hand, are very much around.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

First jungle sortie at dusk from the village outskirts: the semi-wild orangutans living on the forest edge often come down in the late afternoon. It's a gentle introduction before tomorrow's full-day trek. Soak in the falling darkness on the Bohorok River — the sound of the nocturnal jungle, with gibbons calling and hornbills settling in, is an experience in itself.

⏱ 90 min

Bukit Lawang

★★★★☆
1

Full day trekking in Gunung Leuser National Park with a certified guide — it's mandatory and essential to the experience. The park has been described as the largest wilderness area in Southeast Asia, and unmarked trails vanish rapidly in the dense vegetation. A 6- to 7-hour outing will take you far enough past the busy zones to dramatically improve your chances of observing wild orangutans in their natural behavior — not begging for food, but foraging for fruit, building their nests or swinging through the canopy.

⏱ 420 min

Activities

River tubing down the Bohorok in the late afternoon: local guides provide inner tubes for riding the rapids from the upper reaches of the park. It's the most enjoyable return you could wish for after a long day of hiking — the water is cool, the forested banks slide past, and monkeys watch you from the branches. Count on 90 minutes to 2 hours depending on the starting point.

⏱ 120 min

Parapat

★★☆☆☆
1

Parapat is the town on the eastern shore of Lake Toba and the embarkation point for Samosir Island. Lake Toba itself is a staggering sight: 100 km long, 35 km wide, 505 meters at its deepest — the largest volcanic lake in the world, formed roughly 74,000 years ago by a supervolcanic eruption so powerful that some scientists believe it triggered a global volcanic winter and halved the human population. The fact that this cataclysmic site is now covered with terraced rice paddies, Batak villages and cloud reflections on still water feels utterly surreal.

⏱ 60 min
2

From the Parapat lookout, spot Samosir Island in the center of the lake — a 650 km² landmass (roughly the size of Singapore) connected to the mainland by an isthmus cut through with an artificial canal. Its peak rises to 1,630 meters above the lake surface, or 2,600 meters above sea level — making it technically an island in a lake, on an island, on a volcano. Hard to beat in terms of improbable geography.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Arrival in the late afternoon after the long drive from Bukit Lawang — count on 4 to 5 hours on the road. Settle in at Parapat and locate the morning ferry departure for Samosir (frequent crossings to Tuk Tuk, about 1 hour). The evening by the lake, with the lights of the far-shore villages reflecting on the water, is the best possible introduction to why the Batak call this lake the origin of the world.

⏱ 60 min

Samosir

★★★☆☆
1

Tuk Tuk, the peninsula on Samosir's east shore, is the island's tourist hub — but a few kilometers inland is all it takes to reach traditional villages where rumah adat (Batak longhouses with saddle-shaped roofs and curved gables) shelter families whose genealogies stretch back 18 generations. Start with the village of Ambarita and its tribunal stones: an open-air stone courtyard where King Siallagan dispensed justice centuries ago, complete with stone seats for the accused and a chopping block for the condemned. Stark and chilling.

⏱ 60 min
2

The tomb of King Sidabutar is one of the best-preserved royal Batak tombs on Samosir — a sculpted sarcophagus shaped like an overturned boat, adorned with ancestral heads and geometric friezes. The Batak Toba built their tombs so that ancestral spirits could watch over the living: every architectural detail carries meaning. Mount Pusuk Buhit, visible from Samosir, is considered the mythical birthplace of the Batak — the home of Si Raja Batak, ancestor of the entire people.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Huta Bolon Museum in Simanindo, in the north of the island, features a royal rumah adat over 140 years old and a collection of Batak crafts — ulos textiles with complex geometric iconography, two-stringed hasapi lutes, ritual carvings. Tortor dance performances are held on most mornings.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

If you have the energy, hike up to Lake Sidihoni, perched on the Samosir plateau at 900 meters — a lake within an island within a lake, surrounded by rice paddies and total silence. The round trip from Tuk Tuk takes half a day. In the late afternoon, settle into one of the lakeside cafes at Tuk Tuk and order a glass of tuak, the Batak palm wine served at every significant occasion — weddings, funerals, Sundays after church.

⏱ 120 min

Bukittinggi

★★★☆☆
1

Bukittinggi means literally "high hill" in Malay — the town sits at 930 meters in the Minangkabau Highlands, 90 km northeast of Padang, at the foot of volcanoes Singgalang (dormant) and Marapi (active). Its landmark is the Jam Gadang clock tower, built by the Dutch in 1926, whose roof was restyled under Japanese occupation and then converted again to a curved Minangkabau-style roof at independence — three chapters of history told by a single building. Grab a table at the terrace across the square and watch the plaza come alive.

⏱ 30 min
2

Sianok Canyon (Ngarai Sianok), a 10-minute walk from the center, is a 15-km gorge sliced through volcanic rock — locals compare it to a miniature Grand Canyon. From the Taman Panorama on the cliff edge, the view drops to a jungle floor threaded by a silver river, with the houses of Kota Gadang (famous for its goldsmiths) clinging to the opposite wall. Beneath your feet, tunnels dug by prisoners of war under Japanese occupation run for kilometers — part of the network is open to visitors.

⏱ 75 min
3

Fort de Kock (Benteng Fort de Kock) was built in 1825 by Captain Bouer on orders from Governor Hendrik Merkus de Kock, at the height of the Padri War (1821–1837) — an internal Minangkabau conflict between Islamic reformers and guardians of adat tradition that the Dutch exploited to gain a foothold. Little of the original fortification survives, but the surrounding park commands a panorama over the town and the Limpapeh suspension bridge linking the hilltop to the nearby zoo and museum.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Wander through Pasar Atas (the upper market), Bukittinggi's liveliest, for a first taste of Minangkabau cuisine: rendang (beef slow-cooked in coconut milk and spices, named "the world's most delicious food" by CNN in 2011), gulai otak (brain curry), lado mudo (fresh green chili). Sit down for a full meal at a traditional warung — the dishes are laid out on the table and you pay only for what you eat.

⏱ 90 min

1

Descend into Sianok Canyon to the village of Kota Gadang, renowned since the 17th century for its goldsmiths and embroiderers. 2

Activities

Siberut

★★★☆☆
1

From Padang (2 hours by road from Bukittinggi), take the ferry to Siberut Island — the largest of the Mentawai Islands, 4,480 km², designated a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve and located 150 km off the west coast of Sumatra. The crossing takes 6 to 12 hours depending on the boat; slow ferries depart in the evening and arrive in the morning. Siberut is home to the Mentawai, nicknamed the "flower people" — one of the last Neolithic populations in Indonesia, carrying on a tradition stretching back 3,000 years. They know neither rice cultivation, nor ironwork, nor weaving: their life revolves around hunting, fishing and forest gathering.

⏱ 60 min
2

The basic social unit of the Mentawai is not the village but the uma, a large communal wooden longhouse on stilts shared by 5 to 10 families from the same clan. It serves simultaneously as home, courthouse, ceremonial space and temple — decisions are reached by consensus after meetings that can run through the entire night. Since Suharto's fall in 1998, families have begun rebuilding umas in the forest, after decades of forced sedentarization during which the Indonesian army burned these houses down to "modernize" the population.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

First encounter with the sikerei — the Mentawai shamans — accompanied by a local guide. The sikerei hold a central role: they diagnose illness by communicating with forest spirits, prepare hunting poisons (a blend of toxic leaves and bark that can kill a wild boar in under 5 minutes) and lead the great puliajat collective purification ceremonies. Their bodies are covered in geometric tattoos applied using a brass-tipped stick and an ink of soot and sugarcane juice — a lifelong practice, from the feet up to the face. Choose a guide who speaks Mentawai and is himself from the island.

Full day deep in the Siberut forest with the host clan. A ritual requesting the forest spirits' blessing precedes every hunting expedition.

Padang

★★☆☆☆
1

Back in Padang after the ferry from Siberut. The capital of West Sumatra is the birthplace of Minangkabau cuisine — known worldwide simply as "nasi Padang." This way of serving dozens of small dishes simultaneously on the table (you pay only for what you touch) is practiced in thousands of restaurants across Indonesia and beyond. 2

Pantai Air Manis beach, 3 km from the center, is home to the Malin Kundang rock — a human-shaped rock formation that, according to legend, is an ungrateful son turned to stone by his own mother after he disowned her in front of his sailors. It's one of the best-known legends in Indonesia. The beach itself is pleasant in the late afternoon, with views over the forested hills.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Prepare for tomorrow's flight to Bengkulu (domestic flight from Padang's Minangkabau Airport, about 1 hour). Spend the evening in old Padang (Kota Tua) and its 18th-century Dutch facades along the Batang Arau river — a quarter of wharves and warehouses that recalls two centuries when Padang was the main pepper port on Sumatra's west coast.

Bengkulu

★★★☆☆
1

Fort Marlborough is Bengkulu's centerpiece and one of the largest forts ever built by the British in Southeast Asia — 44,000 m² on an artificial hilltop overlooking the Indian Ocean. The British East India Company took five years to build it (1713–1719) under Governor Joseph Collett, naming it after the Duke of Marlborough, victor at the Battle of Blenheim. The British held Bengkulu for 140 years (1685–1825) before ceding it to the Dutch in exchange for Malacca and Singapore under the Treaty of London of 1824. It was here that Sir Stamford Raffles — yes, the man behind Singapore — served as governor from 1818 to 1824.

⏱ 90 min
2

The exile house of Sukarno, Indonesia's first president, is an essential stop. The Dutch banished him to Bengkulu in the 1930s for his nationalist activities. It was here that he met his wife Fatmawati — mother of Megawati Sukarnoputri, herself a future president — and spent the years leading up to independence in 1945. The house preserves its original furniture, photographs and archives from the period.

⏱ 45 min
3

Pantai Panjang stretches 7 km of white sand lined with coconut palms — one of the longest beaches in Sumatra. In the late afternoon, it's the locals' favorite spot for sunset walks along the Indian Ocean. Bengkulu sees very few visitors and benefits from the quiet: you'll barely cross paths with another foreign traveler.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Ask your accommodation or a local guide about a forest outing to search for the Rafflesia arnoldii — the world's largest individual flower, endemic to the forests of Bengkulu, with a diameter that can exceed 1 meter. It blooms only a few days a year, and its smell of rotting flesh (hence its nickname "corpse flower") attracts pollinating flies. It was discovered in 1818 by Sir Stamford Raffles and Dr. Joseph Arnold, who lent it their names. Local operators organize outings when a flower is about to open.

⏱ 120 min

Pagaralam

★★☆☆☆
1

Pagaralam is a small mountain town in the heart of the Pasemah Highlands, at 1,000 meters above sea level, overlooked by Gunung Dempo (3,159 m) — the highest peak in South Sumatra. Scattered across the rice paddies and tea plantations around the town lie sculpted megaliths dating back roughly 2,000 years — full-relief warriors wearing necklaces and carrying weapons, figures mounted on elephants or buffaloes, dolmens and stone tombs. The Dutch archaeologist Van der Hoop catalogued them in 1932, drawing comparisons with European Celtic Bronze Age monuments. Some theories even link them to the Sailendra Dynasty, the same that built Borobudur in Java.

⏱ 120 min
2

The so-called Batu Gajah ("Elephant Stone") at Belumai is the most spectacular: a 3.35-meter-long rock carved with a man clinging to a buffalo, clutching a knife and ropes. The stone tilts slightly backward, creating a striking illusion of movement. The rice paddies surrounding it and the silhouette of Dempo in the background make this one of the most underrated archaeological sites in Indonesia.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

For hikers, Gunung Dempo can be climbed in a single long day from Pagaralam with a 2 AM departure to reach the summit at sunrise. The trail passes through the tea plantations of Perkebunan Nusantara, then primary forest up to the active crater. The ascent takes 5 to 6 hours for 1,200 meters of elevation gain — demanding but non-technical. A local guide is recommended as Sumatran tigers have been reported on the massif.

Jambi

★★★☆☆
1

Jambi is a modern city on the Batang Hari River, but its treasure lies 25 km downstream: the Muaro Jambi complex, the largest Buddhist temple ensemble in Sumatra — 82 red-brick structures spread across 2,062 hectares along the river, connected by a network of artificial canals. The site was a Buddhist learning center from the 7th to the 14th century, on the Maritime Buddhism Route traveled as early as 414 AD by Chinese and Indian scholars. Chinese pottery dating from the 7th to 9th centuries and a statue evoking the Prajnaparamita of Singosari testify to the commercial connections of the era. The site is a candidate for UNESCO World Heritage status.

⏱ 150 min
2

Jambi's central market (Pasar Raya) is the commercial heart of the city, reachable on foot or by dokar (horse-drawn carriage). Then walk along the Batang Hari wharves to see the fishermen's stilt houses and the wooden boats that still handle river transport to upstream villages — a river life that has barely changed in centuries.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Take a motorboat up the Batang Hari to reach Muaro Jambi by river (about 30 minutes from Jambi) rather than by road — it's the way 8th-century Buddhist pilgrims arrived. The site is laced with canals still visible and partially restored. Village guides at Muaro Jambi, trained by archaeologists, offer walking tours through the ruins and the forest between the temples.

Jambi

Final morning in Jambi: head back to the Batang Hari riverbanks at sunrise — the hour when fishermen cast their nets and mist rises from the water in the orange light. Tanggo Rajo Park along the river is where locals stroll on weekends. It's a gentle way to close an intense journey before heading to Sultan Thaha Airport for flights to Jakarta or onward international connections.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Sample the local Jambi coffee: the province is one of Sumatra's major robusta producers — less famous than Mandheling or Aceh Gayo, but with an earthy, intense profile that appeals to connoisseurs. Traditional coffee shops (kopi warung) serve it in a thick glass with palm sugar. Pick up a few bags to take home — Sumatran coffee travels very well.
