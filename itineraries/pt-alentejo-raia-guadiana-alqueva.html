Faro

★★★☆☆
1

Built on the ruins of a Moorish medina, Faro's Cidade Velha can be explored in under an hour—just enough to soak up the Iberian atmosphere. The Largo da Sé with its orange trees and the cathedral with its 18th-century azulejos form a human-scale historic center, crowd-free before 10 a.m. Free access to the square.

⏱ 45 min
2

The scent of warm bread and orange blossoms fills Rua de Santo António from early morning—it's the main shopping street, pleasant for a coffee before hitting the road. Understated, not spectacular, but honest.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Pick up your rental car at the airport and head straight north on the N122. The Serra do Caldeirão gives way to the arid hills of the Baixo Alentejo. Budget 1 hour 45 minutes to Mértola.

⏱ 105 min

Mertola

★★★☆☆
1

In Portugal, only two churches still contain an original mihrab—and Mértola is the only one where it remains fully visible. The Igreja Matriz, a former 12th-century Almohad mosque, was converted in 1238 without demolishing its niche oriented toward Mecca or its five Muslim naves. The stucco decoration is still legible. Free visit.

⏱ 50 min
2

This castle defends a confluence, but the real interest lies beneath the cobblestones. The Castelo de Mértola, perched on a rocky spur above the Guadiana and Oeiras rivers, contains Roman, Visigothic, and Umayyad stratigraphic layers unearthed by archaeologists from the Campo Arqueológico de Mértola since the 1980s. The views over both watercourses more than justify the admission price.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Museu de Mértola is spread across a dozen sites throughout town—early Christian basilica, Roman house, Islamic workshop. Each room is a slice of superimposed centuries, making Mértola a rare archaeological case in Europe. Combined ticket available at the Tourist Office, best purchased first.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Head down to the Guadiana riverbank in late afternoon, at the foot of the ramparts. The river forms a calm loop here, ideal for a 30-minute walk along the towpath. The late-afternoon light in the Baixo Alentejo is particularly intense—photographers know this well.

⏱ 40 min

If the sun is still high, drive 18 km east to the Mina de São Domingos for a preview before tomorrow's visit. The panorama over the rust-colored hills in evening light is worth the trip even from the car.

⏱ 45 min

Mina De Sao Domingos

★★★★☆
1

Reopened in 1855 under British capital after Roman exploitation, the Mina de São Domingos closed in 1966. The mining village built for Portuguese workers and British managers—who had their own cemetery, as non-Catholics—has remained intact, half-inhabited, half-ghost town. Free visit, signs in Portuguese and English.

⏱ 45 min
2

Pools of blood-red water surround the ruins of the old Corta da Mina—the main pit, now filled with acidic waters laden with iron and sulfates. The landscape evokes the surface of another planet. The Praia Fluvial da Tapada Grande, at the other end of the village, offers a freshwater swim in a reservoir built by the British in the late 19th century.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Follow the marked trail (about 5 km) around the Corta da Mina, past the old office buildings and the English Garden. The hike runs alongside rusted railway tracks, once used to transport ore to the Guadiana. Free, no reservation needed.

⏱ 90 min

Beja

★★★☆☆
1

Nobody comes to Beja on purpose. That's precisely what makes it interesting. A discreet district capital, Roman (Pax Julia), Visigothic, Moorish, it has survived everything without ever becoming famous. The Praça da República at the center, lined with whitewashed houses with colorful trim, is one of the finest squares in the Alentejo without appearing on any guidebook cover.

⏱ 30 min
2

The keep of the Castelo de Beja rises 40 meters—the Torre de Menagem, built entirely of marble, features an eight-pointed star vault at the summit, characteristic of the region's medieval military architecture. From the battlements, views stretch endlessly across the Alentejo plains. Small admission fee.

⏱ 50 min
3

A 15th-century convent houses Portugal's most famous love correspondence. The Museu Rainha Dona Leonor, installed in the former Convento da Conceição founded in 1459, is where the nun Mariana Alcoforado allegedly wrote her five letters to a French officer in 1666—published in Paris in 1669 as Lettres portugaises. Real or fake, the debate has never been settled. Check hours before visiting: the main convent is sometimes closed for renovations.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Settle in at the Praça da República and order a plate of migas alentejanas or carne de porco à alentejana at one of the restaurants around the square. Beja's cuisine comes from a town that feeds on its own fields: no frills, direct flavors.

⏱ 75 min

The town of Beja breathes Alentejo gastronomy right into its bakeries: local sweets made with nata (cream) and almonds can be found in the downtown pastelarias. A late-afternoon stop for coffee and bolo de mel beats any restaurant dessert.

⏱ 30 min

Reguengos De Monsaraz

★★☆☆☆
1

Reguengos de Monsaraz is the wine capital of the Alentejo, anchored to the most productive region of Portugal's vineyards. The town itself is quiet, but the Largo do Município and the 18th-century parish church make a pleasant stop between cellars.

⏱ 20 min
2

The cellars smell of oak and ripe fruit: Herdade do Esporão, a few kilometers from town, produces on an estate of over 700 hectares under organic farming. Its medieval Torre Esporão, a classified national monument, stands amid the vines—a curious blend of 14th-century stone and stainless steel tanks.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Book a tasting at Herdade do Esporão in advance—guided tours in foreign languages are scheduled at fixed times. Budget 1 to 1.5 hours. The on-site restaurant offers lunch featuring estate products, recommended if timing allows.

⏱ 90 min

Monsaraz

★★★☆☆
1

If you arrive in Monsaraz without a reservation in high season, turn back: the few rooms within the walls are booked months in advance. The village perched 195 meters above Lake Alqueva is entirely pedestrian—park below. All white, all schist, all silence. The interior of the medieval ramparts consists of a single main street.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Torre das Feiticeiras—the Witches' Tower—is worth the detour: from the top of the Castelo de Monsaraz, the view reaches Spain on clear days. In the inner courtyard of the castle, a bullfighting arena carved into the rock is still in use during local festivals. Free entry.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Igreja Matriz Nossa Senhora da Lagoa, a Renaissance-style building on the main street, houses the tomb of a Templar knight. Monsaraz belonged to the Order of the Temple until its dissolution in the 14th century, then to the Order of Christ. The interior is austere, the funerary slab visible.

⏱ 25 min

Activities

Head down to the shores of Lake Alqueva at sunset, to the Monsaraz river beach. Alqueva is the largest artificial lake in Western Europe, filled in 2002. The late-afternoon light on 250 square kilometers of flat water is uncommonly soft.

⏱ 60 min

Zero light pollution, maximum stars: the Alqueva region received the world's first Starlight Tourism Destination certification in 2011, awarded by the Starlight Foundation. At night in Monsaraz, municipalities cut their public lighting. The Observatório do Lago Alqueva (Cumeada, 10 km away) offers telescope sessions by reservation Tuesday through Saturday.

⏱ 90 min

Evora

★★★★☆
1

Évora's Roman temple survived because it served as a butcher shop in the Middle Ages—the cold stone suited meat preservation. Ironic history for this 1st-century monument, dedicated to the imperial cult and not to Diana as still commonly believed. Its 12 granite and marble columns can be visited freely at any time, free exterior access.

⏱ 30 min
2

Évora's Capela dos Ossos is uncomfortable to look at. That's intentional. The walls and pillars of this 16th-century Franciscan chapel are lined with the bones of some 5,000 Évora citizens. The inscription at the entrance says it all: Nós ossos que aqui estamos pelos vossos esperamos—'We bones who are here await yours.' Open 9 a.m.–6:30 p.m. in summer, 9 a.m.–5 p.m. in winter. Admission: around €6.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Sé of Évora will impose itself on you around every corner of the center: impossible to miss, impossible to avoid. The Romanesque-Gothic cathedral, whose construction began in the late 12th century, is the largest of medieval Portugal. Its cloister and rooftop terrace justify the separate ticket.

⏱ 50 min

Activities

Wander the Praça do Giraldo at sunset: this central square of Évora, adorned with a Renaissance fountain, has been the town's beating heart since the 15th century. Café terraces fill up before 7 p.m. No need for a plan—just stay.

⏱ 60 min

The University of Évora was founded in 1559 by the Jesuits, suppressed in 1759 when Pombal expelled the Society of Jesus, then reopened only in 1979—two centuries of silence. Its cloister and halls lined with 18th-century azulejos depicting natural history are worth an hour's visit.

⏱ 60 min

Evora

★★★★☆
1

Climb to the roof of the Sé cathedral in Évora at opening time to avoid tour groups. The terrace offers a 360° view over Roman tile roofs and the Alentejo plains—clearly the best view of the historic center. Separate ticket from the cloister.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Cromeleque dos Almendres is 15 km from the center, but this megalithic circle of 95 granite monoliths—dating back about 6,000 years—is one of the best-preserved megalithic ensembles on the Iberian Peninsula. Dirt road for the last 2 kilometers. Free visit, always deserted on weekdays before 10 a.m.

⏱ 60 min
3

Évora's ramparts encircle the historic center for several kilometers, blending 2nd-century Roman sections with medieval reconstructions. The stretch in the northeast of town, near Rua do Raimundo, is the best preserved. Free walk.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

The Évora municipal market wafts olive oil and sheep cheese from 8 a.m. onward. The Alentejo is one of Portugal's great cheese-producing regions: aged queijo de Évora, charcuterie, and mel de eucalipto. Ideal for stocking up on road provisions.

⏱ 45 min

Alentejo gastronomy is meant to be eaten seated, unhurried, preferably in an adega (cellar-restaurant). Açorda alentejana, migas de batata, ensopado de borrego—the stewed dishes that form the backbone of local cuisine deserve a proper table. Évora has several good addresses around Giraldo Square.

⏱ 90 min

Evoramonte

★★☆☆☆
1

The Convention signed here on May 26, 1834, ended the only civil war in Portuguese history. The Castelo de Evoramonte, rebuilt after the 1531 earthquake in a Renaissance style inspired by Italian palatial architecture—four circular towers with Manueline rope moldings—still houses the hall where Dom Miguel surrendered. Classified as a national monument since 1910. Small admission fee.

⏱ 50 min
2

The Alentejo spreads beneath Evoramonte's ramparts to the horizon without interruption. The village perched at 481 meters on the Serra de Ossa is tiny—one main street, white houses with blue shutters, pottery at the doorsteps. What Monsaraz has become touristy, Evoramonte remains preserved.

⏱ 25 min

Activities

Walk the castle's wall-walk circuit in under an hour. The views over the olive groves and cork oaks of the Serra de Ossa are particularly beautiful in late morning, before the heat raises a haze.

⏱ 45 min

Estremoz

★★★☆☆
1

Estremoz is made of a single material: marble. Not as decoration—as urban fabric. The sidewalks, facades, fountains, and doorsteps are all carved from white stone extracted from surrounding quarries for centuries. You end up not noticing it anymore, which is even stranger.

⏱ 40 min
2

The Castelo de Estremoz and its Torre das Três Coroas, 27 meters tall and entirely in marble, dominate the 13th-century Cidade Velha. The castle is now a pousada—public visit of the tower and wall-walk possible. The view over the Alentejo plains from the summit is one of the most unobstructed in the region.

⏱ 45 min
3

Estremoz's clay figurines are classified as UNESCO intangible heritage. These hand-painted figurines, made in the region since the 17th century, are sold in downtown workshops and displayed at the Museu Municipal Professor Joaquim Vermelho, right across from the castle. About a dozen active artisans remain.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

If you're there on a Saturday, the Rossio Marquês de Pombal transforms into a vast flea market and farmers' market from 8 a.m. onward. Ceramics, charcuterie, cheeses, work clothes—the largest weekly market in the interior Alentejo. Nothing folksy, everything for the locals.

⏱ 90 min

Visit a marble quarry around Estremoz—several offer tours, some about ten kilometers from town. The open-pit extraction, the cubes of white marble stacked across hectares, and the fine dust in the air give a sense of the industrial scale underpinning this museum-town.

⏱ 60 min

Vila Vicosa

★★☆☆☆
1

The Paço Ducal de Vila Viçosa, 110 meters of façade entirely clad in regional marble, is the most imposing civil monument in the Alentejo. Its construction began in 1501 at the initiative of the 4th Duke of Braganza. Over 50 rooms spread across 3 floors: baroque and rococo furniture, Flemish tapestries, ducal picture gallery. Guided tour required—reservation recommended.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Praça da República is paved with white marble and lined with orange trees in blossom in spring—one of the most elegant squares in Portugal. The bronze equestrian statue of King João IV stands in the center, a reminder that the Braganza family, starting from this village, ruled Portugal from 1640 to 1910.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Take the guided tour of the Paço Ducal before 10 a.m. to avoid school groups. The tour is guided only (language: mainly Portuguese). The adjacent Arms and Carriage museums are included in the ticket. Closed Mondays and Tuesday mornings.

⏱ 90 min
