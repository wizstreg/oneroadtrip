Stop 1

Xian

Where the Silk Road began and emperors buried armies in clay

★★★★★
📍 Terracotta Warriors Museum Xi'an📍 Xi'an City Wall South Gate📍 Bell Tower Xi'an📍 Drum Tower Xi'an📍 Great Mosque Xi'an📍 Muslim Quarter Xi'an
1

The Terracotta Army Museum (Bīngmǎ Yǒng), 40 km east of Xi'an, houses around 8,000 clay soldiers, 130 chariots with 520 horses, and 150 cavalry horses discovered in 1974 by farmers digging a well. This underground army was buried around 210 BC to accompany China's first emperor, Qin Shi Huang, into the afterlife. It occupies three pits, the largest (Pit No. 1) measuring 230 meters long by 62 meters wide. Each soldier stands between 1.75 and 2 meters tall and bears a unique face—no two are identical. UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1987.

⏱ 150 min
2

Xi'an's city walls, built between 1370 and 1378 during the Ming dynasty, form a 13.7-km rectangle, 12 meters high and 15 to 18 meters thick at the base. They're China's best-preserved ancient city fortifications. You can walk the entire circuit or rent a bike along the ramparts—about 1.5 hours by bicycle. The Bell Tower (Zhōnglóu), erected in 1384 at the city's heart, and the Drum Tower (Gǔlóu), built in 1380, mark the entrance to the Muslim Quarter (Huímín Jiē), inhabited since the Tang dynasty by Central Asian merchants who converted to Islam.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Wander through Xi'an's Muslim Quarter, one of China's oldest Hui neighborhoods. Street vendors serve yangrou paomo (crumbled bread in lamb broth), cumin-grilled lamb skewers (kǎo ròu chuàn), and sesame flatbreads (ròu jiāmó). The Great Mosque, founded in 742 during the Tang dynasty and rebuilt under the Ming, follows entirely Chinese architectural style—no domes or minarets, just wooden pavilions, interior courtyards, and calligraphic stone tablets.

Lanzhou

Gateway to the Hexi Corridor on the Yellow River's banks

★★★☆☆
📍 Gansu Provincial Museum Lanzhou📍 Zhongshan Bridge Lanzhou📍 White Pagoda Park Lanzhou📍 Yellow River Lanzhou📍 Waterwheel Park Lanzhou
1

The Gansu Provincial Museum, on the Yellow River's south bank, holds over 350,000 artifacts including the "Galloping Horse" (Mǎ Tà Fēiyàn), a bronze statuette from the Eastern Han dynasty (25-220 AD) depicting a horse at full gallop balanced on a flying swallow—now the emblem of Chinese tourism. The Silk Road galleries display silks, Roman coins, and Buddhist objects found along the Hexi Corridor.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Zhongshan Bridge (Zhōngshān Qiáo), the first permanent bridge built across the Yellow River, was erected in 1907 with materials imported from Germany and assembled by American engineers. Before its construction, people crossed the Yellow River on rafts made from inflated sheepskins (yángpí fázi), a method used for centuries. From the bridge, climb to White Pagoda Park (Báitǎ Shān Gōngyuán), built during the Yuan dynasty (13th century) to honor a Tibetan lama who died in Lanzhou en route to Genghis Khan's court.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Sample Lanzhou beef noodle soup (Lánzhōu niúròu miàn), considered China's most widespread street food—an estimated 100,000+ restaurants nationwide serve it. The clear spiced broth, hand-pulled noodles made before your eyes, sliced beef, cilantro, and chili oil form the traditional recipe's "five colors."

Stop 3

Zhangye

Where 24 million years of geology painted mountains in stripes

★☆☆☆☆
📍 Zhangye Danxia Geopark📍 Rainbow Mountains Zhangye📍 Giant Buddha Temple Zhangye📍 Mati Temple Zhangye📍 Zhangye Wetland Park
1

Zhangye Danxia National Geological Park, designated a UNESCO Global Geopark in 2020, covers 322 km² in the northern foothills of the Qilian Mountains. Its striped sandstone formations—red, orange, yellow, green—resulted from 24 million years of sediment deposits, folded by the collision of the Indian and Eurasian plates (the same tectonic movement that formed the Himalayas), then sculpted by wind and rain. The colors come from oxidation of different minerals: hematite (red), limonite (brown), goethite (yellow), chlorite (green). Five viewing platforms connected by shuttle buses over 20 km let you explore the site in 3-4 hours. Sunset from Platform 1 brings out the most intense colors.

⏱ 240 min
2

The Giant Buddha Temple of Zhangye (Dàfó Sì), built in 1098 during the Western Xia dynasty, houses a 34.5-meter-long reclining clay Buddha—one of China's largest. According to local tradition, Marco Polo stayed in this temple for a year during his passage along the Silk Road. The wooden and clay body is hollow: Buddhist objects were concealed inside for centuries.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Stop 4

Jiayuguan

The last fortress where China ends and the desert begins

★★☆☆☆
📍 Jiayuguan Fort📍 Overhanging Great Wall Jiayuguan📍 Great Wall Museum Jiayuguan📍 First Beacon Tower Jiayuguan📍 Jiayuguan Pass
1

Jiayuguan Fortress (Jiāyùguān), built in 1372 during the Ming dynasty, marks the western terminus of the Great Wall of China. The fort rises between two mountain chains—the snow-capped Qilian Mountains to the south and the Mazong Mountains to the north—in a natural choke point called "the most impregnable pass under heaven." Its outer walls measure 733 meters in perimeter and 11 meters high, pierced by three gates. According to local legend, the fort's architect calculated the exact number of bricks needed; when construction finished, exactly one brick remained—you can still see it resting on a ledge above the west gate, proof of the calculation's precision.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Overhanging Great Wall (Xuánbì Chángchéng), 8 km north of the fort, is a section that climbs the cliff face of the Mazong Mountains at a 45° angle. Built in 1539, it's nicknamed "Gansu's little Badaling." From the summit, views stretch across the Gobi Desert to the north and snow-capped mountains to the south. The Great Wall Museum at the fort's base traces the history of the entire 21,196-km defensive network built between the 7th century BC and the 16th century.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Walk Jiayuguan Fort's ramparts at sunset. The battlements overlook the Gobi Desert to the west—beyond the fort, Silk Road travelers entered unknown territory. This is where exiles and merchants left Chinese civilization behind, a moment Tang dynasty poets compared to "departing the world."

Dunhuang

Where Buddhist pilgrims carved paradise into cliff faces

★★☆☆☆
📍 Mogao Caves Dunhuang📍 Crescent Lake Dunhuang📍 Mingsha Sand Dunes Dunhuang📍 Yumen Pass Dunhuang📍 Dunhuang Museum
1

The Mogao Caves (Mògāo Kū), 25 km southeast of Dunhuang, constitute the world's largest, richest, and oldest Buddhist cave art complex. Carved starting in 366 AD—when the monk Le Zun had a vision of a thousand Buddhas bathed in golden light on the cliff face—the caves were expanded over ten centuries until the 14th century. 492 painted caves survive, containing 45,000 m² of murals and over 2,000 polychrome sculptures. Cave 96, the most spectacular, holds a 35.5-meter-tall Buddha. Cave 17, called the "Library Cave," discovered in 1900 by a Taoist monk, contained over 50,000 manuscripts, silk paintings, and textiles sealed for nearly 1,000 years—the greatest medieval East Asian document discovery. UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1987. Advance booking required.

⏱ 240 min

Activities

Ride a camel across the Mingsha sand dunes as Silk Road merchants did for millennia. The singing sands produce an eerie humming sound when grains shift in the wind.

Dunhuang

Where Buddhist pilgrims carved paradise into cliff faces

★★☆☆☆
📍 Mingsha Sand Dunes Dunhuang📍 Crescent Moon Lake Dunhuang📍 Yumen Pass Dunhuang📍 Western Thousand Buddha Caves📍 White Horse Pagoda Dunhuang
1

The Singing Sand Dunes of Mingsha (Míngshā Shān), along the southern edge of the Dunhuang oasis, stretch 40 km long and rise over 250 meters high. Their name comes from the deep rumble the sand produces when it slides down slopes in dry weather—an acoustic phenomenon linked to the particular grain size of the sand particles. At the dunes' foot, Crescent Moon Lake (Yuèyá Quán) is a natural crescent-shaped spring 218 meters long and about 50 meters wide, fed by an underground water table that prevents sand from covering it. Historical texts mention this spring since the Han dynasty (2nd century BC).

⏱ 150 min
2

Yumenguan Pass (Yùmén Guān), 90 km northwest of Dunhuang, was one of two gateways out of the Chinese empire toward the western routes. Its name, "Jade Gate Pass," comes from jade from Khotan that passed through this post. Only a square rammed-earth fort remains, 24 meters per side and 10 meters high, isolated in the Gobi Desert. Tang dynasty poets made it a symbol of frontier garrison solitude.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Camel ride through the Mingsha dunes at sunset. Camel caravans follow a marked circuit from the dunes' base up toward the ridges. The descent can be done on sand sleds. In the evening, the dunes take on orange then purple hues.

⏱ 90 min

Turpan

China's furnace floor, kept green by channels older than history

★★☆☆☆
📍 Jiaohe Ancient City Turpan📍 Karez System Turpan📍 Emin Minaret Turpan📍 Astana Tombs Turpan📍 Gaochang Ancient City Turpan
1

The ancient city of Jiaohe (Jiāohé Gùchéng), 10 km west of Turpan, is the world's largest and best-preserved rammed-earth city. Perched on a 30-meter-high leaf-shaped loess plateau between two ravines of dried rivers, it served as a natural defense—no ramparts needed. Founded in the 2nd century BC as the capital of the Cheshi kingdom, it prospered as a Silk Road relay during the Tang dynasty (7th-10th century), when it housed the Protectorate of the Western Regions, the highest Chinese authority in Central Asia. The complex covers 370,000 m², with a central avenue, residential quarters, a palace, and a Buddhist temple complex. Destroyed by the Mongols in the 13th century, the site was preserved by Turpan's extremely dry climate. UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2014.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Karez irrigation system (Kǎnérjǐng), over 2,000 years old, is an underground canal network that channels meltwater from the Tian Shan Mountains to the Turpan oasis across the desert, without evaporation. The Turpan area has about 1,100 karez over a total network of roughly 5,000 km of tunnels—considered one of ancient China's three great engineering works along with the Great Wall and the Grand Canal. At a dedicated museum, you descend 10-50 meters underground to see the canals, access wells, and understand this gravity irrigation principle.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Emin Minaret (Sūgōng Tǎ), 3 km east of Turpan, is China's tallest Islamic tower at 44 meters. Built in 1777 from raw clay bricks, its conical shaft is decorated with 15 different geometric patterns forming diamonds, waves, and flowers—all in earth, without a single nail or piece of wood.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Day 1: Drive through the legendary Flaming Mountains, spectacular red sandstone formations stretching over 100 km—China's hottest place and setting of a famous Chinese legend featuring a monkey king and a wise monk.

⏱ 60 min

Day 2: Hike through the Flaming Mountains gorges from Tuyok, a challenging 5-hour trek crossing the red sandstone massif to emerge into landscapes of extraordinary geological drama.

Turpan

China's furnace floor, kept green by channels older than history

★★☆☆☆
📍 Flaming Mountains Turpan📍 Tuyok Village Turpan📍 Bezeklik Thousand Buddha Caves📍 Grape Valley Turpan📍 Turpan Museum
1

The Flaming Mountains (Huǒyàn Shān), 30 km east of Turpan, are a 100-km-long chain of eroded red sandstone whose gullies and striations give the illusion of flames under the sun. Famous throughout China thanks to the classic 16th-century novel Journey to the West, where the Monkey King Sun Wukong crosses them with a magic fan. Ground temperature can exceed 80°C in summer—Turpan, in a depression 154 meters below sea level, is China's hottest city.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Tuyok Valley (Tǔyùgōu), nestled in a Flaming Mountains gorge, shelters a traditional Uyghur pisé village where time seems suspended. Raw clay brick houses, grape trellises drying on rooftops, and winding alleys compose an intact landscape. Above the village, Buddhist caves carved between the 5th and 9th centuries preserve fresco fragments. The site is also a Muslim pilgrimage destination—some consider it a "little Mecca."

⏱ 90 min
3

The Bezeklik Thousand Buddha Caves (Bǎizīkèlǐkè), in a canyon at the foot of the Flaming Mountains, number 77 caves carved between the 5th and 14th centuries. Their frescoes blend Indian Buddhist, Chinese, and Uyghur influences. Many were damaged by Muslim iconoclasts in the 15th century, then cut out and carried off by European explorers in the early 20th century—fragments now reside in Berlin's museum and St. Petersburg's Hermitage.

⏱ 75 min

Urumqi

Where the Tian Shan mountains meet the crossroads of Central Asia

★★★☆☆
📍 Xinjiang Regional Museum Urumqi📍 Heavenly Lake Urumqi📍 Red Hill Park Urumqi📍 Grand Bazaar Urumqi📍 Erdaoqiao Market Urumqi
1

The Xinjiang Regional Museum in Urumqi displays the Tarim Basin mummies, 3,800 years old, including the famous "Beauty of Loulan" with her Caucasian features—proof of exchanges between eastern and western populations long before the Silk Road's official opening. Collections cover the cultures of Xinjiang's 13 ethnic groups (Uyghurs, Kazakhs, Kyrgyz, Mongols, etc.) through textiles, musical instruments, and everyday objects.

⏱ 120 min
2

Heavenly Lake (Tiānchí), 115 km east of Urumqi in the Tian Shan Mountains, sits at 1,910 meters altitude. This 3.4-km-long, 1.5-km-wide glacial lake is framed by spruce forests and dominated by Bogda Peak, which rises to 5,445 meters. Kazakh herders pitch their yurts here in summer. The site, important to Taoism and Buddhism, is listed as UNESCO World Heritage as part of the Tian Shan network.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

Stroll through the Erdaoqiao International Grand Bazaar, Central Asian China's largest covered market. You'll find carpets, Uyghur knives, dried fruits (Turpan raisins, walnuts, apricots), spices, and musical instruments. Food stalls serve laghman (Uyghur hand-pulled noodles), samsa (meat pastries), and nan (tandoor-baked bread).

Kashgar

The bazaar at the edge of empire where the world still trades

★★★☆☆
📍 Id Kah Mosque Kashgar📍 Kashgar Old Town📍 Apak Hoja Tomb Kashgar📍 Sunday Bazaar Kashgar📍 Kashgar Ancient City
1

Id Kah Mosque (Àitígǎěr Qīngzhēn Sì), in Kashgar's heart, is China's largest mosque. Founded in 1442, it can accommodate up to 20,000 worshippers in its courtyard and prayer hall. Its yellow facade flanked by two minarets faces the city's central square, the convergence point of social and commercial life. Friday prayer gathers thousands from across the region.

⏱ 60 min
2

Kashgar's Old Town (Kāshí Gǔchéng), one of Central Asia's most ancient quarters, is a labyrinth of pisé alleyways, houses with carved wooden balconies, and interior courtyards. Artisans work copper, wood, and pottery in workshops opening onto the street. The potters' quarter, backed against a clay cliff to the northeast, has produced glazed jars and plates in Kashgar's distinctive style for centuries. The old town was partially restored but retains the topography and atmosphere of a Silk Road oasis city.

⏱ 120 min
3

The Apak Hoja Mausoleum (Xiāngfēi Mù), 5 km northeast of downtown, is Xinjiang's most important Islamic funerary monument. Built in 1640, it houses the tombs of five generations of the Hoja family in a hall topped by a dome tiled in blue and white faience. Chinese legend associates it with the "Fragrant Concubine," a Uyghur princess who supposedly naturally exhaled a musky scent.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Watch local artisans at work in old town workshops—coppersmiths, knifemakers, and musical instrument makers maintaining skills passed through generations of Uyghur craftspeople.

⏱ 60 min

Meet Uyghur families in surrounding villages.

⏱ 60 min

Wander through commercial districts and observe the Han and Uyghur cultural blend.

Kashgar

The bazaar at the edge of empire where the world still trades

★★★☆☆
📍 Sunday Bazaar Kashgar📍 Livestock Market Kashgar📍 Old Town Artisan Quarter Kashgar📍 Id Kah Square Kashgar📍 Kashgar Ancient City
1

Kashgar's Sunday Grand Bazaar (Yèkèshànbā Bāzhā), direct heir to Silk Road markets, is one of Central Asia's largest open-air markets. Thousands of merchants from surrounding countryside and neighboring countries (Pakistan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan) sell carpets, silks, spices, knives, leather, and dried fruits. The livestock market on the outskirts sees sheep, goats, donkeys, horses, and yaks change hands—deals sealed with a handshake and tea.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

Walk through old town artisan workshops: forging Uyghur knives (pìchàk) with bone or horn handles, pottery wheels in the potters' quarter, stringed instrument making (dutar, rawap). Artisans work at street level, allowing you to watch their hands at work. Late afternoon, sample polo (Uyghur lamb pilaf with carrots) and tea in a traditional teahouse.

Urumqi

Where the Tian Shan mountains meet the crossroads of Central Asia

★★★☆☆
📍 Red Hill Park Urumqi📍 People's Park Urumqi📍 Grand Bazaar Urumqi📍 Erdaoqiao Market Urumqi📍 Urumqi City Center
1

Red Hill (Hóngshān), a small park in Urumqi's center, offers views over the city and the Tian Shan Mountains in the background. At the summit, a 10.6-meter red brick pagoda built in 1788 during the Qing dynasty was meant to prevent the mythical dragon of the Urumqi River from causing floods.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Final stop at Urumqi's dried fruit and spice market. Xinjiang produces China's finest raisins, dried apricots, walnuts, and figs—the region's dry, sunny climate gives them high sugar concentration. Free time before departure flight.
