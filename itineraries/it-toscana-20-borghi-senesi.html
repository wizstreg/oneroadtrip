The Most Beautiful Villages of Siena Province

Circle the medieval ramparts of Monteriggioni, fictional birthplace of Ezio Auditore, then explore the fortified cellars of Castellina and the Grand Duke's ice house in Radda. Cross Colle Val d'Elsa, world capital of crystal and birthplace of Arnolfo di Cambio, before diving into medieval Siena with its seventeen contrade and its Palio. The Crete Senesi lead to Asciano and the Abbey of Monte Oliveto, then the UNESCO-listed Val d'Orcia unfolds its landscapes from Montalcino to Pienza. To the south, Monte Amiata and its Aldobrandeschi fortresses guard Tuscany's borders.

✨ Highlights
  • Val d'Orcia landscapes, a UNESCO World Heritage Site
  • Palio di Siena (July 2 and August 16)—the world's most famous horse race
  • Brunello di Montalcino and Vino Nobile di Montepulciano, two of Italy's most prestigious red wines
  • Etruscan bronzes of San Casciano dei Bagni, major archaeological discovery of 2022
  • Pienza, the Renaissance's first ideal city according to Alberti's canons

Day 1

Radda In Chianti

Former capital of the Chianti League, where heraldic arms adorn the podestà's palace

★★★☆☆
1

Palazzo del Podestà and Piazza Ferrucci: Built in the 15th century, nearly destroyed during the Florentine invasion of 1478, then swiftly rebuilt, this palace served as the seat of the Lega del Chianti, which Radda had commanded since the 11th century. The façade preserves the heraldic coats of arms of the podestas who governed when Radda controlled the three terzieri of the League. On the same square stands the 13th-century church of San Niccolò, whose monumental façade was redesigned in 1926 by Carlo Coppedè.

⏱ 45 min
2

Grand Duke's Ice House: Just outside the walls, this truncated cone structure buried in the earth dates from the Grand Duchy of Tuscany. Built to transform winter snow into ice blocks that could be preserved year-round, it's one of the few surviving examples of this type of structure in Chianti.

⏱ 20 min
3

Castello di Volpaia: Six kilometers north of Radda, this fortified hamlet from the 11th century has remained unchanged since the Middle Ages. All wine and olive oil production from the village still passes through the cellars and deconsecrated churches connected by a remarkable underground 'wine pipeline.' The estate is open for visits from April to October.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Walk along the ramparts: The remaining sections of walls can be admired from the Porta Fiorentina and along the village's northern perimeter. The lanes inside the walls reveal small dry-stone gardens characteristic of Chianti.

⏱ 30 min

Radda nel Bicchiere: This wine festival organized by the Proloco of Radda in Chianti takes place every summer. Over 80 wines from the village are offered for tasting at stands set up throughout the streets, no reservation required.

⏱ 120 min

Day 2

Castellina In Chianti

Chianti's fortified heart, crowned by a Florentine fortress above vaulted streets

★★☆☆☆
1

Via delle Volte: A 200-meter underground gallery carved along the ancient ramparts, designed by Filippo Brunelleschi during the village's 15th-century fortification to withstand gunpowder explosions. Today, its openings onto the Chianti landscape and its traditional taverns make it the village's most striking architectural feature.

⏱ 30 min
2

Rocca di Castellina: A 15th-century fortress built by Florence to strengthen its southern border against Siena. Its imposing keep dominates the town from the hilltop. Inside, the Archaeological Museum of the Sienese Chianti, opened in 2006, displays discoveries from the Bronze Age through the Middle Ages, including an Etruscan chariot.

⏱ 60 min
3

Etruscan Tumulus of Montecalvario: A few minutes' walk from the center, this 6th-century BC necropolis contains five tombs with four chambers and one cist, freely accessible. Nearby, the Poggino necropolis at Fonterutoli also holds 6th-century BC tombs.

⏱ 45 min
4

Church of San Salvatore: The original building dates from the 15th century, but it was almost entirely destroyed during World War II and rebuilt in neo-Romanesque style by Sienese architect Menotti Lucatelli. Inside are preserved a Madonna and Child by Maestro di Signa and a wooden statue of Saint Barnabas.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Chianti Classico tasting: Castellina sits at the heart of the Chianti Classico DOCG, whose symbol—the black rooster—takes its name from a medieval legend pitting Siena against Florence. The vineyards surrounding the village offer tastings of Chianti Classico and Chianti Classico PDO olive oil.

⏱ 90 min

Day 3

Monteriggioni

Where fourteen thirteenth-century towers crown a perfect circle of stone

★★★★☆
1

Medieval walls of Monteriggioni: Built between 1214 and 1219 by the Republic of Siena to defend against Florence, the circular wall measures 570 meters in perimeter and rises 2 meters thick. Its 14 square towers, regularly spaced, have remained almost intact since the 13th century. Dante Alighieri immortalized them in the Inferno of the Divine Comedy, comparing these giant towers to the guardians of the abyss—'Monteriggioni di torri si corona.' From the ramparts open to the public, the view sweeps across Chianti and Val d'Elsa.

⏱ 60 min
2

Monteriggioni in Arme Museum: Housed in the village's former buildings, this museum presents life-size reproductions of medieval and Renaissance armor and weaponry, siege machines, and knight's equipment. According to local legend, a Ubisoft developer stayed here and drew inspiration from the place to make it Ezio Auditore's birthplace in Assassin's Creed II—a story not officially confirmed by Ubisoft, but one that has introduced Monteriggioni to new generations.

⏱ 45 min
3

Church of Santa Maria Assunta and Piazza Roma: At the heart of the walls, the 13th-century church blends Romanesque and Gothic on a simple stone façade. Piazza Roma, with its central well and a handful of palaces, sums up the scale of the village—three parallel streets connect the two gates, Porta Franca on the Siena side and Porta di Ponente on the Florence side.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Walk on the ramparts: The accessible section runs along the north, east, and west faces for a partial loop of about 500 meters with views of Chianti Colli Senesi vineyards. In July, the medieval festival Monteriggioni di Torri si Corona transforms the village into a life-size recreation of 13th-century crafts.

⏱ 45 min

Local products tasting: The village produces Chianti Colli Senesi Monteriggioni DOC and extra virgin olive oil from the olive trees surrounding the ramparts—shops on Via Matteotti offer these specialties.

⏱ 30 min

Day 4

Colle Val Elsa

Where Italy's crystal empire was born in the shadow of Arnolfo's tower

★★☆☆☆
1

Colle Alta and Arnolfo di Cambio's tower-house: The upper town, accessible through the Porta Volterrana (a medieval gate with drawbridge), is one of the best-preserved historic centers in Val d'Elsa. Here stands the 13th-century tower-house where Arnolfo di Cambio, the sculptor and architect behind Florence's Cathedral of Santa Maria del Fiore and Palazzo Vecchio, was supposedly born in 1245. Inside, frescoes depict medieval battles linked to the town.

⏱ 60 min
2

Crystal Museum: Housed in the former kilns of the Schmidt glassworks, the first to fire up a furnace in 1820, this museum traces the history of local crystal production up to the introduction of lead crystal manufacturing in 1963. Colle Val d'Elsa today produces nearly all Italian crystal and, according to figures from local players, a significant share of worldwide production—earning it the nickname 'Bohemia of Italy' since the 19th century.

⏱ 60 min
3

Duomo (Co-Cathedral of Saints Alberto and Marziale) and Palazzo Campana: The neoclassical cathedral in brick and sandstone rises on the remains of an old medieval church. The early 16th-century Palazzo Campana, an example of Tuscan Mannerism, marks the transition between the Borgo di Santa Caterina and the Castello.

⏱ 30 min
4

Ranuccio Bianchi Bandinelli Archaeological Museum: Housed in the Palazzo Pretorio, this museum displays objects found in the Val d'Elsa territory, from Etruscan bronze mirrors to large Hellenistic vases. The Via delle Volte, in the upper town, is a hundred-meter covered passage with medieval atmospheres, lined with former artisan workshops.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Visit to a crystal workshop: The factories in the lower town host groups to observe the different phases of hot work (8:30-11:00 AM) and cold work—engraving, sculpture, grinding. Direct purchases at the workshop avoid intermediaries.

⏱ 90 min

Funicular between Colle Bassa and Colle Alta: Installed in 2006, this free ski lift overcomes a 40-meter elevation gain in a few minutes and operates 24/7. San Gimignano, with its 14 UNESCO-listed medieval towers, is 16 km to the north—a natural excursion from this base.

⏱ 10 min

Day 5

Casole Elsa

An elliptical fortress village guarded by Martini's Renaissance towers

★☆☆☆☆
1

Medieval walls and circular towers: The ramparts of Casole d'Elsa have a characteristic elliptical shape, about 420 meters long on the main axis and 130 meters wide. Two circular towers built in 1481 on the eastern perimeter are the work of Francesco di Giorgio Martini, the greatest military architect of the Renaissance. Contested between Volterra, Siena, and Florence throughout the 13th century, the town definitively passed under Sienese rule in 1260.

⏱ 45 min
2

Rocca Senese and Arte Viva Art Gallery: A 14th-century fortress now housing the town hall, the Rocca hosts a collection of drawings and paintings created by children from across the province, gathered by artist Giuseppe Ciani. The façade shows traces of defensive apparatus on corbels shaped like inverted pyramids.

⏱ 30 min
3

Collegiate Church of Santa Maria Assunta: Consecrated in 1161, the Romanesque-Gothic church houses the cenotaph of Beltramo Aringhieri, a remarkable 14th-century funerary sculpture. In the adjacent presbytery, the Archaeological Museum of the Collegiate displays Etruscan pieces, paintings, and goldwork.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Hiking in Selva di Sogno Park: Between Casole and Chiusdino, this forest park features sculptures and murals integrated into an oak wood. Marked trails, free access.

⏱ 90 min

Day 6

Sovicille

Where medieval bishops ruled beneath the Montagnola's slopes

★★☆☆☆
1

Historic center of Sovicille: Perched at the base of the Montagnola Sienese and encircled by 15th-century walls, the town was contested in the Middle Ages between the bishoprics of Siena and Volterra, then between the Republic of Siena and the noble Ardengheschi family. The 12th-century Palazzo dei Vescovi, property of the bishops of Siena, is its most visible legacy.

⏱ 30 min
2

Abbey of Torri: A few kilometers from Sovicille, this monastic complex is distinguished by a spectacular triple-colonnade cloister—a 13th-century stone row, a 14th-century terracotta one, and a 15th-century wooden one. The 14th-century Baroque church has a superb inlaid choir.

⏱ 45 min
3

Pieve di Ponte allo Spino: Among the most remarkable rural Romanesque churches in Tuscany, this pieve preserves in its adjacent courtyard the remains of a 13th-century cloister and double-arched windows from a building where bishops stayed.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Visit to Villa Cetinale: Built in the late 17th century for the Chigi family by architect Carlo Fontana, this villa is accompanied by a citrus garden and the Thebaid Park—a wood crossed by a meditation path leading to chapels, grottoes, ponds, and statues. The Hermitage of the Scala, accessible by 300 penitential steps carved in stone, dominates the whole.

⏱ 90 min
Relais Le Macine Di Stigliano9.6

Day 7

Siena

Gothic city of seventeen contrade, where the Palio thunders twice each summer

★★★★☆
1

Piazza del Campo: Laid out between 1293 and 1349, this shell-shaped square is divided into nine sectors symbolizing the Council of Nine that governed Siena at the height of its power. The red brick paving is bordered by eleven lanes converging toward the center—an urban composition unmatched in Italy. The Palazzo Pubblico (13th century) closes the concave arc and the Torre del Mangia rises to 102 meters, built from 1338 to 1348, the third tallest medieval tower in Italy.

⏱ 60 min
2

Civic Museum (Palazzo Pubblico): On the palace's first and second floors, the Hall of the Mappamondo and the Hall of Peace preserve Ambrogio Lorenzetti's frescoes on the Effects of Good and Bad Government (1338-1340)—one of the first secular painting cycles in Western history, representing the city of Siena itself.

⏱ 75 min
3

Santa Maria della Scala Complex: A former pilgrims' hospital founded in the 11th century, one of Europe's oldest, this 19,000 m² complex is now a vast museum. The Sala del Pellegrinaio, painted between 1440 and 1444 by Domenico di Bartolo and Vecchietta, documents medieval hospital life with striking documentary precision.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Walk through the contrade: Siena is divided into 17 contrade (districts), each with its own church, museum, and animal emblem. Getting lost in the steep lanes between Campo and Duomo—passing by the 12th-century Fonte Branda and Via di Città—reveals a remarkably preserved Gothic city, a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

⏱ 120 min
La Terrazza Sul Campo-Rooms Only9.7
Day 8

Siena

Gothic city of seventeen contrade, where the Palio thunders twice each summer

★★★★☆
1

Cathedral (Duomo di Siena): Built in white Carrara marble and black Montagnola marble, the polychrome 13th-14th century façade is one of the peaks of Italian Gothic. The interior, with three naves of 50 alternating white and black marble columns, houses a floor in marquetry of 56 inlaid marble panels created between 1369 and 1547, long covered to protect it. The Piccolomini Library (1495), painted by Pinturicchio, documents the life of Pope Pius II in ten large scenes of exceptional Renaissance realism.

⏱ 90 min
2

Baptistery of San Giovanni: Attached to the cathedral's apse, this 14th-century Gothic baptistery houses the monumental baptismal font (1427-1430), a collective work by Jacopo della Quercia, Donatello, Lorenzo Ghiberti, and other Florentine and Sienese masters—a unique synthesis between the two schools.

⏱ 45 min
3

National Picture Gallery: Housed in the 14th-century Palazzo Buonsignori, this collection traces 400 years of Sienese painting, from the gold backgrounds of Duccio di Buoninsegna to Simone Martini, Ambrogio Lorenzetti, and the 15th-century masters. Nowhere else can you measure so clearly the specificity of the Sienese school versus Florence.

⏱ 90 min
4

Sanctuary of Saint Catherine: On St. Anthony's Hill, the old dyers' quarter houses the birthplace of Catherine Benincasa (1347-1380), Doctor of the Church. The ensemble of chapels, oratories, and loggias built around the family home in the 15th century constitutes one of Siena's most intimate places of devotion.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Palio di Siena: Italy's most famous horse race pits ten of the seventeen contrade against each other each year on July 2 and August 16 on the Piazza del Campo covered with sand. Documented origins date back to the 14th century. The silk banner bearing the image of the Madonna—the 'Cencio'—rewards the victorious district. Book grandstand seats several months in advance.

⏱ 180 min

Day 9

Asciano

Gateway to the Crete Senesi and the frescoed cloisters of Monte Oliveto

★★★☆☆
1

Palazzo Corboli Museum: Housed in a 12th-century palace that belonged to the noble Bandinelli family, this museum unfolds over three floors a collection of sacred art and archaeology. The archaeological section gathers Etruscan and Roman remains from the territory, including Ambrogio Lorenzetti's Badia a Rofeno triptych and the remarkable 14th-century Wheel of Barlaam.

⏱ 60 min
2

Torre della Mencia and Piazza del Grano: Standing 25 meters high, this medieval tower dominates Asciano's oldest square, once Siena's granary. The Fontana della Mengia at the tower's base, with its stone and bronze Gorgon head, is accompanied by a legend: whoever drinks from this fountain always returns to Asciano.

⏱ 30 min
3

Abbey of Monte Oliveto Maggiore: Founded in 1313 by Bernardo Tolomei, a Sienese nobleman renouncing his fortune for a hermit's life, this red brick Benedictine abbey emerges from the woods of the Crete Senesi. Its Great Cloister is entirely decorated with frescoes by Luca Signorelli (late 15th century) and Il Sodoma (early 16th century) on the life of Saint Benedict—one of the masterpieces of Italian Renaissance painting. The monks still chant Gregorian vespers.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Hiking in the Crete Senesi Park: The Crete Senesi Life Park covers the Asciano territory with marked, not-difficult trails through the clay hills. Trail No. 2, starting from Asciano, offers the widest views of the biancane—these characteristic whitish clay formations—and rejoins the historic center to finish at the Corboli Museum.

⏱ 120 min

Palio dei Ciuchi: Every Sunday in September, Asciano's seven districts compete through the village streets—on donkeyback rather than horseback, a popular wink at Siena's Palio.

⏱ 120 min

Day 10

Buonconvento

Where intact medieval walls guard a Madonna by Duccio

★★★★★
1

Porta Senese and ramparts: Buonconvento is one of the Italian villages that best preserves its medieval ramparts. Built from the late 13th century by Siena to protect this strategic node of Val d'Arbia on the Via Cassia, the walls still completely encircle the center. The Porta Senese, to the north, preserves its massive original wooden doors—the Porta Romana on the south side was destroyed in 1944 during the German retreat to delay the Allied advance.

⏱ 30 min
2

Museum of Sacred Art of Val d'Arbia: Housed in the Palazzo Ricci Socini, a 19th-century building restored in Art Nouveau style in 1907 by architect Gino Chierici, this museum is a double curiosity—Sienese paintings from the 13th to 19th centuries displayed in a still-intact Liberty interior. The collection founded in 1926 by Father Don Crescenzio Massari includes the Madonna of Buonconvento by Duccio di Buoninsegna (1290-1295), Pietro Lorenzetti, Luca di Tommé, and canvases from the 17th-century Sienese school.

⏱ 75 min
3

Church of Saints Peter and Paul and Palazzo Podestarile: The 14th-century church, rebuilt in the 18th century, was according to tradition the death place of Emperor Henry VII of Luxembourg in 1313—an engraved marble stone on its left façade bears the date 1103, possibly that of its foundation. The neighboring Palazzo Podestarile, with its Clock Tower and 25 podestà coats of arms, recalls that Buonconvento was Val d'Arbia's most important administrative center.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Nova Eroica: Every spring, Buonconvento hosts this amateur cycling ride on dirt roads (strade bianche) with routes for all levels, including families and children—an accessible version of Gaiole's Eroica.

⏱ 180 min

Day 11

Montalcino

Last fortress of Sienese freedom, where Brunello ages in pentagonal walls

★★★★☆
1

Fortress of Montalcino: Built in 1361 by reusing earlier 13th-century structures, this pentagonal fortress was the last bastion to resist during the fall of the Republic of Siena to the Medici in 1555—Sienese refugees held out here until 1559. Its interior enoteca allows you to taste Brunello in the very symbol of republican resistance. From the ramparts, the view stretches across Val d'Orcia, Monte Amiata, and the Maremma.

⏱ 60 min
2

Piazza del Popolo and Palazzo dei Priori: The 13th-century communal palace, adorned with heraldic coats of arms from Montalcino's history, has a slender bell tower whose carillons still punctuate the life of the town. It's the central square of a network of narrow, steep lanes typical of Sienese medieval urbanism.

⏱ 30 min
3

Civic and Diocesan Museum of Sacred Art: Housed in the former 14th-century convent of Sant'Agostino (with its two 16th-century cloisters), this museum preserves over 200 works on three floors, including the 12th-century Crucifix of Sant'Antimo, a Madonna by Simone Martini, and the Coronation of the Virgin by Bartolo di Fredi.

⏱ 60 min
4

Abbey of Sant'Antimo: Ten kilometers south via the SP55, in a valley hollow planted with cypresses and olive trees, this Benedictine Romanesque abbey is mentioned as early as 813 in an act of Louis the Pious. The main church, built in the 12th century in the Cistercian Burgundian style, is one of the finest examples of French Romanesque architecture in Italy. Since 1992, a community of Premonstratensian canons has sung the Gregorian offices—vespers at 6:30 PM can be heard from afar.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Brunello di Montalcino tasting: Brunello is produced exclusively from Sangiovese Grosso on the commune's territory. Clemente Santi was the first to get it recognized in 1869. The fortress cellars or surrounding estates (Poggio di Sotto, Ciacci Piccolomini) offer tastings of the current vintage and riserva.

⏱ 90 min

Sagra del Tordo: Every last Sunday in October, Montalcino's four districts compete in archery in this medieval festival commemorating the region's hunting traditions.

⏱ 120 min

Day 12

San Quirico Orcia

Where Romanesque portals and Renaissance gardens frame the Via Francigena

★★★☆☆
1

Collegiate Church of Saints Quirico and Giulitta: One of the masterpieces of Romanesque architecture in Val d'Orcia, first mentioned in 712 during a dispute between the bishoprics of Siena and Arezzo. Its three portals are of different styles and periods: the main 12th-century Romanesque portal, supported by two columns on two lions with crocodiles in the architrave; the 13th-century Lombard portal attributed to Giovanni Pisano (Gothic inscription 'Iohes' in the spire); and a third Baroque one. Inside, a polyptych by Lucas Cranach the Elder offered by Frederick III of Germany in 1504.

⏱ 45 min
2

Horti Leonini: An Italian garden created around 1581 by Diomede Leoni on land offered by Francesco I de' Medici. The diamond-shaped plan of the lower part, with its boxwood hedges and triangular parterres, is one of the rare Renaissance public garden achievements open to travelers—Leoni had wanted it accessible to Via Francigena pilgrims. At the center, a statue of Cosimo III de' Medici by Giuseppe Mazzuoli (1688). Above, a Roman-era double-headed Janus marks the boundary between the geometric garden and the wild ragnaia.

⏱ 45 min
3

Palazzo Chigi Zondadari: Built in the second half of the 17th century by Cardinal Flavio Chigi, nephew of Pope Alexander VII, this palazzo faces the Collegiate Church and dominates Via Dante Alighieri. Seriously damaged during World War II, it now belongs to the commune.

⏱ 20 min
4

Chapel of the Madonna di Vitaleta: Three kilometers southeast of San Quirico, at the end of a marked 800-meter dirt path, this small 16th-century chapel surrounded by cypresses is one of the most photographed images of Val d'Orcia. The cypress avenue is most evocative at sunrise.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Festa del Barbarossa: Every last weekend in June, San Quirico commemorates the 1155 meeting between Emperor Frederick Barbarossa and Pope Adrian IV's emissaries. Medieval costume parades, flag throwing, and archery competitions.

⏱ 120 min

Val d'Orcia hiking trails: San Quirico lies at the intersection of stage 35 and stage 36 of the Tuscan Via Francigena. The section toward Bagno Vignoni (4 km) runs along cypress fields and vineyards of the Orcia DOC appellation.

⏱ 120 min

Day 13

Pienza

The Renaissance's dream made stone, Pius II's vision of the perfect city

★★★★★
1

Piazza Pio II and Renaissance ensemble: In 1459, Pope Pius II—born Enea Silvio Piccolomini in the village of Corsignano—commissioned Bernardo Rossellino, a student of Leon Battista Alberti, to transform his birthplace into a model city. In just three years (1459-1462), Rossellino erected the cathedral, Palazzo Piccolomini, Palazzo Borgia, and the trapezoidal Piazza—the first concrete realization of Renaissance humanist urban ideals. Listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1996.

⏱ 60 min
2

Cathedral of the Assumption: Built between 1459 and 1462 by Rossellino, its exterior façade is purely Renaissance in travertine, while the three-nave interior draws on the late Gothic of South German churches—a compromise wanted by Pius II, influenced by German cardinal-philosopher Nicholas of Cusa. It houses the Assumption by Il Vecchietta, the Madonna in Majesty by Matteo di Giovanni, and Flemish tapestries in the basement.

⏱ 45 min
3

Palazzo Piccolomini: Pope Pius II's summer residence built in 1459 by Rossellino on the model of Florence's Palazzo Rucellai. The triple-level loggia on the south side opens onto a hanging garden with panoramic views of Val d'Orcia, Monte Amiata, and Enea Silvio's birthplace, Corsignano. The interior preserves period furniture, a library, and a weapons collection.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Fiera del Cacio: On the first Sunday in September, Pienza's six districts compete in the game of cacio al fuso—rolling a wheel of Pecorino di Pienza as far as possible—along with tastings and markets.

⏱ 120 min

Pecorino di Pienza tasting: Pienza's sheep cheese comes in fresh, semi-stagionato, and stagionato varieties. The shops on Corso Rossellino offer tastings with Val d'Orcia DOC wines.

⏱ 45 min
Agriturismo Bonello9.4

Day 14

Montepulciano

Renaissance citadel above tuff-carved cellars aging Italy's noble wine

★★★★☆
1

Piazza Grande: At the hilltop (605 m), this Renaissance square is surrounded by the 15th-century Palazzo Comunale (a miniature Sienese version of Florence's Palazzo Vecchio, with panoramic tower), the Cathedral of Santa Maria Assunta begun in 1592 by Ippolito Scalza, Palazzo Contucci by Antonio da Sangallo, and Palazzo Tarugi. At the center, the Pozzo dei Grifi e dei Leoni, a Renaissance well designed by Sangallo.

⏱ 45 min
2

Historic cellars of Vino Nobile: Beneath the Renaissance palaces of the center, a network of tunnels carved into tuff and brick vaults houses the oak barrels in which Vino Nobile di Montepulciano DOCG rests. The Contucci family has produced wine here since the 11th century; the Cantina De' Ricci extends over several medieval levels. Vino Nobile, based on Prugnolo (local Sangiovese), was already being exported to the Medici court in the 15th century.

⏱ 90 min
3

Tempio di San Biagio: Five hundred meters below the ramparts, this Renaissance church begun in 1518 by Antonio da Sangallo the Elder is one of the finest examples of central-plan Renaissance architecture in Italy—inspired by Bramante's Tempietto. The travertine façade dominates the Valdichiana vineyards.

⏱ 45 min
4

Strade bianche and Val d'Orcia: Montepulciano is the natural starting point for exploring the white roads from Val di Chiana to Val d'Orcia. The road toward Monticchiello (4 km) runs along vineyards and cypresses with views of classic Tuscan panoramas.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Vino Nobile DOCG tasting: The cellars in the center offer guided tours of the tunnels carved into tuff with comparative tastings of Rosso di Montepulciano DOC, Vino Nobile DOCG, and Vino Nobile Riserva.

⏱ 90 min

Bravio delle Botti: Every last Sunday in August, Montepulciano's eight districts compete by pushing 80 kg barrels along Via Gracciano nel Corso to Piazza Grande—a tradition documented since the 14th century.

Day 15

Sarteano

Where the Infernal Quadriga races across fourth-century Etruscan walls

★★★☆☆
1

Castello Manenti: Built in the 12th-13th centuries and restructured in the 15th century, the castle is surrounded by a double wall enclosing a square keep and round towers. It was a Sienese bastion throughout the Middle Ages. Francesco Todeschini Piccolomini was born in Sarteano in 1439—he became pope for less than a month in 1503 as Pius III, immediate successor to Alexander VI.

⏱ 30 min
2

Civic Archaeological Museum: Housed in the 16th-century Palazzo Gabrielli (whose structure dates to the Middle Ages), this museum inaugurated in 1997 traces Sarteano's history from the 9th to the 1st century BC from the surrounding Etruscan necropolises. The lower floor reconstructs in life-size—a technique unique in Italy—the 4th-century BC Tomb of the Infernal Quadriga, whose frescoes depict the god Charun driving a chariot pulled by two lions and two griffins, followed by a three-headed serpent and a funeral banquet scene.

⏱ 75 min
3

Pianacce Necropolis: One kilometer from the center, this necropolis discovered in 1954—with major revelations in 2003—contains 21 tombs carved into travertine. Visits to the original Tomb of the Infernal Quadriga are done only with the archaeological museum, which provides guides.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Sarteano thermal baths: Freshwater thermal pools (23-28°C) are found in the Sarteano area, in a natural setting among vineyards and olive groves of Val di Chiana.

Day 16

Cetona

From Paleolithic caves to Etruscan tombs beneath a volcanic peak

★★☆☆☆
1

Rocca di Cetona: The first mentions of the castle date to the 10th century. In the Middle Ages, Cetona first belonged to the Republic of Orvieto then to that of Siena, which fortified it in the 15th century. The fortress, surrounded by centuries-old holm oaks and now private property, overlooks Piazza Garibaldi—a large oval square laid out in the 16th century, surrounded by the Terrosi, Vitelli palaces and two churches, Santissima Annunziata and San Michele Arcangelo.

⏱ 30 min
2

Civic Museum of Prehistory of Monte Cetona: Housed in the Palazzo Comunale, this museum documents human occupation of the territory from the Paleolithic to the Bronze Age, from discoveries made in caves and archaeological sites of Monte Cetona during the 20th century. The adjacent natural archaeological park, called Belvédère, includes travertine cavities that served as places of worship and burial.

⏱ 60 min
3

Collegiate Church of the Santissima Trinità: A 12th-13th century Gothic church that houses a fresco by Pinturicchio, one of the painters of the Sistine Chapel. The San Francesco convent (2 km from the village), founded according to tradition by Saint Francis of Assisi in 1212, marks the border between Tuscany and Umbria.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Hike on Monte Cetona (1,148 m): The volcanic mountain dominating the village offers views west toward Val d'Orcia and Monte Amiata, and east toward Valdichiana and Umbria. The first section is drivable, then an hour's walk to the summit.

Day 17

San Casciano Bagni

Where thermal springs preserved twenty-four bronze gods for two millennia

★☆☆☆☆
1

Etruscan-Roman Sanctuary of Bagno Grande: Excavations conducted from 2019 uncovered in November 2022 more than twenty-four bronze statues dated from 200 BC to 100 AD, perfectly preserved in the mud of thermal springs—including five nearly a meter tall representing Apollo, Hygieia, and matrons. With over 5,000 pieces in gold, silver, and bronze, Jacopo Tabolli's team (University for Foreigners of Siena) called this discovery 'the most important since the Riace bronzes in 1972.' The sanctuary, founded in the 3rd century BC by the Etruscans, was developed by the Romans under the name Balnea Clusinae. The thermal water (42°C) preserved the Etruscan and Latin inscriptions of donor families.

⏱ 90 min
2

Archaeological site of Bagno Grande: The bronzes from the 2022-2024 excavations are circulating in a traveling exhibition (Quirinale, Naples, Reggio Calabria, Berlin in 2025) while the permanent museum—planned in a 16th-century palace in the historic center—is under construction, with a first inauguration expected in late 2026. Meanwhile, the site itself is visitable via guided tours organized every Saturday in summer by the municipal archaeological group, departing from the Stanze Cassianensi at the Palazzo Comunale.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Thermal baths: The springs of San Casciano dei Bagni have been known since the 9th century BC and are still used today for their therapeutic properties. Modern thermal establishments welcome visitors year-round.

Day 18

Radicofani

Where a gentleman bandit's basalt fortress towers above the Val d'Orcia

★★★☆☆
1

Fortress of Radicofani: Perched at 896 meters on a basalt cliff, this is one of the most spectacular positions in all of Tuscany. First mentioned in 973, transferred to the Carolingians in the 9th century, it passed to the Papal State in 1153 then to Siena in the late 15th century. In 1297, Messer Ghino di Tacco—a gentleman bandit from Torrita di Siena, mentioned by both Dante in the Divine Comedy and Boccaccio in the Decameron—seized it and made it his base for years of benevolent brigandage. The Cassero Museum inside traces the site's history from the Bronze Age to the 16th century.

⏱ 90 min
2

Medieval village and Medici Post: The town at the Rocca's foot preserves stone lanes and palaces including the Palazzo Pretorio and the 16th-century Villa Medici. The Casale della Posta (Osteria Grossa), built by the Medici in the 1500s and framed by a double row of cypresses, served as a postal relay for Via Francigena travelers until the 19th century—Casanova, Stendhal, Dickens, and Goethe stayed here.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

View from the Rocca tower: The view from the summit extends to Monte Cetona, Val d'Orcia, Monte Amiata, and on clear days, Lakes Trasimeno and Bolsena. Radicofani is one of the five Val d'Orcia communes listed as UNESCO World Heritage Sites since 2004.

⏱ 30 min

Bagni San Filippo thermal baths: Twenty kilometers northwest of Radicofani, these natural pools of 48°C sulfurous water are freely accessible in a forest setting. The white travertine waterfalls have shaped natural basins known since antiquity.

⏱ 120 min

Day 19

Piancastagnaio

Ancient stronghold beneath Monte Amiata's chestnut forests

★★☆☆☆
1

Rocca Aldobrandesca: A quadrilateral fortress in lava stone first mentioned in 1194 in a diploma of Emperor Henry VI, it successively belonged to the Abbey of San Salvatore, the Aldobrandeschi family, the Orsini of Pitigliano, then the Republic of Siena which seized it between 1415 and 1430. Its two towers—the large one serving as keep, and the small one defending the gate—are characteristic of Aldobrandeschi fortresses on Amiata. Now a museum, it offers panoramic views of the Paglia valley.

⏱ 60 min
2

Church of San Francesco and Palazzo Bourbon del Monte: The church, from a 15th-century convent, has a façade preceded by a simple loggia and preserves fragments of frescoes from the 14th-century Sienese school. The Palazzo Bourbon del Monte, at the village's edge, was the residence of feudal lords from 1601 to the late 18th century, when Grand Duke Leopold I made Piancastagnaio the capital of an autonomous community.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Hiking in the chestnut woods of Monte Amiata: The forests surrounding Piancastagnaio are dominated by the Amiata chestnut, a protected species. Trails depart from the village for loops of 5 to 10 km up to the first slopes of the extinct volcano (1,738 m).
