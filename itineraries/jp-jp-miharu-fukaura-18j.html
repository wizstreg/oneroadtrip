Miharu

Thousand-year weeping cherry designated national monument in 1922
Miharu Takizakura MiharuMiharu Takizakura illumination nocturneCerisiers pleureurs Miharu Fukushima
✈️ Book a flight🚗 Rent a car
1

The Miharu Takizakura is simply one of Japan's three greatest cherry trees - designated a national monument as early as 1922, making it the first tree in the archipelago to receive this honor. Standing 12 meters tall with branches extending 20 meters in every direction, it creates the impression of a waterfall of pink petals cascading toward the ground. The tree has lived for over 1,000 years and its trunk measures 9.5 meters in circumference. Arrive early in the morning during peak bloom (mid to late April): the road leading to the site gets congested quickly, and a special shuttle runs from Miharu Station to avoid the traffic jams.

⏱ 90 min
2

From the top of the small hill that serves as the tree's pedestal, the sweeping view over the blossoming tree and the rice paddies of the plain is magnificent. Then descend to ground level for the upward perspective: the branches tower above you and petals fall like pink snow. Note that the tree has been surrounded by a protective fence since the heavy snowfalls of 2005-2006 broke several of its branches - locals hand-cleared the snow to save it. The 2011 earthquake fortunately caused no damage, and the tree has since become a symbol of resilience for the entire Fukushima region.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The nighttime illumination of the Takizakura is something special: the tree projects its reflections into the darkness and the petals glow under the spotlights. If you're here during bloom season, don't miss the evening version - the site stays open after dark during peak season and the perspective differs radically from the daytime visit.

⏱ 60 min

The town of Miharu - whose name means 'three springs' - also boasts 10,000 cherry trees scattered throughout the surrounding countryside, including 70 century-old weeping specimens. If you've rented a car, explore the country lanes connecting these trees to the Takizakura: it's one of the best hanami circuits away from the crowds.

⏱ 90 min

At the stand near the site entrance, sample local sakura-flavored specialties: ice creams, teas, and cherry blossom cakes. The site entrance fee (approximately 300 yen during bloom season) directly funds the tree's maintenance and protection.

Nunobiki Kogen

Nunobiki Plateau Wind Farm KoriyamaKoriyama Nunobiki Kogen champ tournesolsLake Inawashiro Fukushima

The Nunobiki Plateau, perched at 1,080 meters altitude south of Lake Inawashiro, hosts one of Japan's largest wind farms: 33 Enercon E-70 turbines standing 100 meters tall generate 66 megawatts - enough to power approximately 35,000 households. The facility entered service in February 2007. The panoramic view from the plateau embraces Lake Inawashiro, Mount Bandai, and the Azuma peaks. Bring warm clothes even in summer: the wind is constant and the temperature cool.

⏱ 60 min
2

From early August to early September, the plateau becomes covered with approximately 200,000 sunflowers planted in three successive waves - visit between August 10th and 25th for peak bloom. The yellow flowers facing the 100-meter windmills create one of Fukushima Prefecture's most photographed tableaux. Don't leave without tasting the Nunobiki Plateau daikon radish sold at local stands - a specialty renowned throughout the region.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Marked trails allow you to walk among the windmills and flower fields. In autumn, the sunflowers give way to purple and pink cosmos. The observatory at the far end of the plateau offers a 360° view that hikers of all levels can reach without particular effort.

⏱ 60 min

Lake Inawashiro - Japan's fourth largest lake - stands out for its intensely blue waters and reflection of Mount Bandai. If you descend from the plateau toward the lake, stop at the Hideyo Noguchi Museum, dedicated to the Japanese doctor born in Inawashiro in 1876 who became a national figure in bacteriology: his portrait appears on the thousand-yen bill.

Urabandai

Five-colored ponds born from Mount Bandai's 1888 eruption
Goshikinuma Urabandai FukushimaBishamonnuma UrabandaiLake Hibara UrabandaiMount Bandai Eruption Memorial Museum
✈️ Book a flight🚗 Rent a car
1

The Goshikinuma ponds - 'five colored ponds' - are the inadvertent gift of a volcanic catastrophe. In July 1888, Mount Bandai (1,816 m) exploded in a devastating eruption, burying several villages and killing hundreds. The debris dammed the rivers and created more than 300 lakes and ponds on the highlands behind the mountain. These lakes then absorbed volcanic minerals which, depending on each pond's composition, refract light differently and produce colors ranging from emerald green to turquoise to cobalt blue. A marked 4 km trail links the main ponds - count on an hour on foot, more if you stop to photograph.

⏱ 90 min
2

Bishamonnuma is the largest pond (4 km perimeter) and the only one where you can rent a rowboat to paddle across emerald-colored water with Mount Bandai as backdrop. From the observation bridge built 5 minutes' walk from the visitor center, the view of the pond and mountain is the most photographed in the entire region. The Akanuma, Rurinuma, and Aonuma ponds offer different hues depending on the hour and light - the best light comes in the morning before the tour buses arrive.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

The Mount Bandai Eruption Memorial Museum, located in Urabandai itself, offers a 'sonic body' simulation that recreates the 1888 eruption - an astonishing experience for understanding how this improbable landscape was born in a single day. An adjacent 3D theater presents a film about the mountain.

⏱ 60 min

The hike from the Goshikinuma trail to the Bandai summit takes 3 to 4 hours one way - the trailhead starts from the western end of the Goshikinuma path. You climb through a lunar landscape of volcanic rocks, with the colored ponds below and the Tohoku Alps on the horizon. Bring good shoes and a waterproof layer.

⏱ 240 min

Urabandai's colors are as spectacular in autumn as in spring: maples and beeches cover the shores in a gradient of red, orange, and gold from mid-October to early November. The region is also popular for skiing in winter and fishing in the lakes in summer.

⏱ 60 min

The ropeway climbs to 1,660 meters from Zao Onsen up to Jizô Sancho Station - from there, the ice monsters extend for several kilometers on foot.

2

The Zao massif is also one of Tohoku's best ski resorts, with 28 runs and several lifts. ⏱ 60 min

Activities

The nighttime illumination of the juhyo runs every evening in winter from the ropeway station (check seasonal schedules). Colored spotlights transform the ice monsters into a fairy-tale setting - one of Japan's most surprising winter spectacles. Dress warmly: temperatures at the summit regularly drop below -15°C.

⏱ 60 min

Outside winter season, Zao Onsen offers hikes to ridges and alpine wetlands. The Dai-Rotenburo - large open-air bath with white sulfurous waters - is one of Tohoku's most spectacular onsen. Several inns offer day-use access. Plan to linger in the evening: the steam in the cold mountain air creates a particular atmosphere.

Zao Okama

Volcanic crater lake with shifting colors at 1,672 meters
Lake Okama Zao MiyagiMont Zao cratère KattadakeZao Echo Line route panoramiqueSommet Katta Zao observatoire

1

Lake Okama is one of Tohoku's most spectacular natural sites: a volcanic crater lake perched at 1,672 meters altitude, in the heart of the Mount Zao massif (highest peak at 1,841 m, on the Yamagata-Miyagi border). Its name means 'the cauldron' in Japanese, referring to the round, deep shape of the crater. The lake measures 325 meters in diameter and 27.6 meters deep - its water is so acidic that neither fish nor plants can survive in it, which partly explains its surreal colors. The Japanese also call it Goshiki-numa (five colors) because its waters shift from emerald green to turquoise depending on light, time, and weather. Arrive in clear weather to see the true colors: in bad weather or fog, the effect is greatly diminished.

⏱ 90 min
2

To reach Okama, take the Zao Echo Line road to the Kattadake parking area (1,758 m) - 5 minutes' walk from the crater rim. The Zao Highline, the final toll section (~600 yen), is open from late April to early November. At the Katta summit, don't miss the small Shinto shrine dedicated to Zao Gongen: mountaineers make a brief stop here before walking along the crater rim. The lake closes in winter - if you're here off-season, Zao Onsen and its juhyo (Day 4) are the main attraction.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Umanose trail ('horse's back,' 1h round trip from the main viewpoint) runs along the crater rim to Kumanodake summit. It's the only way to see the lake from multiple angles and observe how the water's colors change with the light angle. Caution: the trail crosses unstable volcanic terrain with steep elevation changes - wear closed shoes.

⏱ 75 min

Late in the day, descend toward Zao Onsen (10 km) for your overnight stay. The Dai-Rotenburo, a large public open-air bath with milky sulfurous waters, stays open until evening - an invigorating onsen after a day at altitude, with views of the wooded ridges of the massif.

⏱ 60 min

Matsushima

260 pine-covered islets, one of Japan's three most beautiful views
Temple Zuiganji MatsushimaTemple Godaido MatsushimaBaie de Matsushima croisièreFukuura Island Matsushima
✈️ Book a flight🚗 Rent a car
1

Matsushima - 'pine islands' - is one of Japan's three most beautiful views (Nihon Sankei), designated by Confucian scholar Hayashi Gahō (1618-1680) alongside Amanohashidate and Itsukushima. The bay contains approximately 260 islets covered with pines twisted by sea winds, each one the emerging summit of an ancient valley flooded by tectonic movements. Poet Matsuo Bashô (1644-1694) was so overwhelmed he reportedly couldn't find words to compose a haiku - an admission of defeat before beauty. The 2011 tsunami reached the bay, but the islets acted as natural breakwaters, significantly reducing the damage.

⏱ 90 min
2

Zuiganji Temple is one of Tohoku's most important Zen temples, founded in the 9th century and rebuilt in 1609 by Lord Date Masamune to serve as his clan's family temple. Its richly painted gold interior decoration is one of the finest from the Edo period. Godaido Hall - founded in 828, rebuilt in 1604 - sits on a rock facing the pier: it's one of Matsushima's most photographed visual symbols. The Kanrantei tea house, a gift from Toyotomi Hideyoshi to Date Masamune, offers a majestic view of the bay from the shore.

⏱ 90 min
3

Since the late Edo period, four panoramic viewpoints of the bay have been selected and named the 'four great views of Matsushima' (Shidaikan): Ôtakamori (105 m), Tomiyama, Tamonzan, and Ogitani. Reaching Ôtakamori by car or on foot from Higashi-Matsushima lets you see the entire bay at once - no buildings obstruct the view from the summit. Allow an extra half hour for this detour.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The bay cruise is the best way to see the 260 islets up close. Several options depart from the pier: a 50-minute loop around the bay, or a longer cruise to Shiogama (neighboring town with an exceptional seafood market). Bilingual audio commentary identifies the most remarkable islands. If you have time, combine the cruise with the ferry return from Shiogama - you avoid backtracking by car.

⏱ 60 min

Fukuura-jima Island is connected to the mainland by a 252-meter red-lacquered bridge - one of the bay's visual landmarks. The island itself is a wild botanical garden with 300 plant varieties. An hour's walk is enough to circle it, with splendid views of the other islets' rock formations.

⏱ 75 min

Matsushima is also famous for its oysters: the bay is a major producer. Don't leave without tasting some fresh oysters grilled on the barbecue or in kaki nabe (oyster soup) at one of the waterfront restaurants. The best time for oysters is the Kaki Matsuri (Oyster Festival) every first Saturday in February, but they're available year-round.

⏱ 60 min

Ginzan Onsen ('silver mine hot spring') takes its name from the Nobesawa silver mine that prospered here during the Edo period. Miners discovered a hot spring within the mine itself, and the spa village grew alongside the mining activity. A major flood of the Ginzan River in the 1910s-1920s destroyed most of the inns, which were rebuilt in the Taisho architectural style (1912-1926): bare wood, white plaster, ornate balconies across three or four floors. These buildings have survived a century without being transformed - there are no modern buildings within the village perimeter, giving it a rare architectural homogeneity in Japan. The village is a pedestrian zone; cars stop at the parking lot at the entrance.

⏱ 90 min
2

The television series Oshin (1983), the most-watched in NHK history, was partly filmed here and made the village famous throughout Japan. Studio Ghibli fans also recognize the atmosphere that reportedly inspired Hayao Miyazaki to create Chihiro's bathhouse in Spirited Away - the Notoya ryokan in particular resembles the film's facade. The Fujiya ryokan was renovated by architect Kengo Kuma in a bold fusion of old and modern that has received much international attention.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

After sunset, gas lamps light up along the streets and river - this is the village's most photographed hour. In winter, snow accumulates on roofs and bridges, and the lamps' reflections shimmer on the river's black water. The Naruko Gorge extends for 4 km along the Otani River west of the spa village. Naruko Onsen is one of Tohoku's oldest hot spring resorts, appreciated since the 9th century according to chronicles. The Yokote Kamakura Festival is one of Japan's most touching winter celebrations, with nearly 450 years of history. Lake Tazawa is Japan's deepest lake, with 423.4 meters below the surface - ranking it 17th globally. Its extreme depth prevents it from freezing in winter, giving it a unique thermal advantage in this snowy region. Its waters vary from lapis lazuli blue to jade green depending on sunlight, and their transparency is striking. A drive around the lake takes half an hour without stopping; by bicycle, count on 2 to 3 hours.

2

The golden statue of Tatsuko, on the lake's western shore, is Tazawa's unmissable visual symbol. Legend tells that Tatsuko was a girl of exceptional beauty who prayed for 100 consecutive nights to preserve her beauty eternally. A deity directed her to a sacred spring in the mountains. She drank so much from it that she was transformed into a dragon and sank to the bottom of the lake to become its guardian. Her lover Hachiro, also transformed into a dragon, comes to visit her every autumn from Lake Hachiro-gata - which explains why the latter freezes in winter when its guardian is absent, while Tazawa always remains ice-free.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

⏱ 60 min

Gozanoishi Shrine, on the lake's north shore, is marked by a large vermilion torii at the water's edge. This is where feudal lords once came to draw inspiration from the lake's beauty. Near the shrine is the Katagashira spring - the very spot where Tatsuko reportedly drank too much water to preserve her beauty.

⏱ 45 min

Nyuto Onsen, a hot spring village hidden in forests 20 minutes by bus from the lake, groups seven rustic ryokan around seven distinct springs. The atmosphere is that of a wild mountain bath - some establishments offer day-use access to their open-air baths (rotenburo) directly on the river.

The Hachimantai Dragon Eye is an ephemeral natural phenomenon that exists only two weeks per year, typically from late May to mid-June. Ryusendo Cave is one of Japan's three great limestone caverns - along with Akiyoshido (Yamaguchi) and Ryugado (Kochi) - designated a national natural monument since 1938. The total explored length exceeds 4,088 meters (an estimate of 5,000 m for the whole), of which 700 meters are open to the public. Ryusendo's distinctive feature is the presence of four underground lakes fed by artesian springs: the third reaches 98 meters deep and holds the most transparent waters ever measured in a cave anywhere in the world - in winter, when rains no longer disturb the sediments, visibility is total to the bottom.

⏱ 90 min
2

The waters of Ryusendo's underground lakes have a tint locally called 'dragon blue' - a shade of intense deep blue caused by light reflection on minerals suspended in exceptionally pure water. LED lighting installed in 2011 highlights the reliefs and stalactites. The temperature inside the cave is a constant 10°C year-round - bring a jacket, even in midsummer.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The adjacent Ryusen Shindo cave (discovered in 1967), separated by the Shizu River, houses the 'world's first natural cave science museum' according to its managers. Pottery discovered during the 1967 excavations and explanations of karst geology are exhibited there. Ryusendo's bottled water is sold as premium spring water throughout Japan - and it's the same water that comes from Iwaizumi residents' taps.

⏱ 60 min

Thirty minutes north of Iwaizumi lies the Kitayamazaki Coast: 200-meter-high basalt cliffs plunge directly into the Pacific Ocean for several kilometers. Boats offer cruises at the foot of the cliffs from Omoto Port in summer. The Sanriku Coast, of which Iwaizumi is part, is designated a national park.

The Oirase Gorge is the only watercourse that escapes from Lake Towada, running 14 km from Nenokuchi to Yakeyama. Classified as a Special Scenic Site and National Natural Monument, the gorge is nicknamed 'the road of waterfalls': a dozen falls pour directly into the stream from the gorge walls, some from dizzying heights. Choshi Otaki waterfall - 7 meters high and 20 meters wide - is the most emblematic: fish cannot swim beyond it, which means Lake Towada has never had perennial bottom-dwelling fish. The marked trail that follows the water on foot is one of Japan's most pleasant.

⏱ 120 min
2

Lake Towada is one of Japan's largest caldera lakes, formed by volcanic activity that began 200,000 years ago. Its deep blue waters are surrounded by wooded cliffs that plunge directly into the lake. Towada Shrine, founded according to tradition in 807 by Sakagami Tamuramaro, is built on the peninsula that juts into the lake - one of Tohoku's most mystical places.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The best strategy for the Oirase Gorge is to walk from Ishigedo (mid-route) toward Nenokuchi, then take the bus back - or vice versa. You can also rent a bike at the gorge entrance. The fresh greenery from mid-May to early June is the most beautiful season, but the autumn colors (mid-October to early November) rival in intensity. In winter, the waterfalls freeze into spectacular blue ice columns (guided tours required).

⏱ 150 min

The Lake Towada cruise (50 min between Yasumiya and Nenokuchi) is an excellent complement to the gorge hike. From the boat, you see the beech and birch forests descending to the water. In autumn, the reflection of maples in the lake creates a tableau that alone justifies the detour. The statue of two women facing each other (work by Kotaro Takamura, 1953) stands at the lake edge at Yasumiya.

⏱ 60 min

Tsuta Onsen

Thousand-year hot spring in the virgin beech forest of Hakkoda

1

Tsutanuma Pond is one of seven marshes of Tsuta Nananuma, nestled in a virgin forest of Japanese beech at the foot of the Hakkoda Mountains. The Tsuta Onsen, a ryokan adjoining the marshes, has been documented since the year 1147 (Heian period) - over 850 years of hot spring history. The spring surges directly beneath the baths, never touching air between the source and the tub - an absolute rarity in Japan. The Numa Meguri no Komichi trail (2.8 km) links six of the seven marshes in two hours of leisurely walking under the beeches.

⏱ 90 min
2

Tsutanuma is nationally famous for its sunrise spectacle in autumn (typically mid to late October): the morning's slanting light illuminates the beeches and red maples on the opposite shore, which reflect in the perfectly still water of the marsh. This spectacle exists only in windless weather, making it unpredictable and all the more precious. Reservations are now required to access the site during peak season (tickets in advance).

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Arrive at Tsutanuma marsh at dawn if you're in full autumn season - the observation platform on the shore gets crowded after 7am. The scene lasts only a few minutes after sunrise, until the light rises and the wind picks up. Outside autumn, the marsh is calm and beautiful in all seasons, with birds nesting in June.

⏱ 90 min

The Hakkoda Mountains, 20 minutes away, are a volcanic massif of sixteen peaks (highest point Odake at 1,585 m) with alpine wetlands and trails for all levels. Sukayu Onsen, founded in 1684, is one of Tohoku's most renowned onsen with its large mixed-gender cypress wood bath for 1,000 people (senninburo). It's also one of Japan's snowiest regions in winter.

⏱ 90 min

The Hakkoda-Towada Gold Line road, which connects Sukayu Onsen to Lake Towada, is one of northern Japan's most beautiful roads in autumn. Tunnels of yellow and red trees overhang the road for kilometers.

⏱ 60 min

Hirosaki

Park with 2,600 cherry trees of 50 varieties, northern Japan's best hanami

1

Hirosaki Castle Park is universally recognized as one of Japan's three most beautiful cherry blossom viewing sites. Its 2,600 cherry trees of more than 50 varieties - some over 300 years old - bloom from mid-April to early May, 2 to 3 weeks after Tokyo. The distinctive feature of these trees: gardeners apply apple tree pruning techniques (Aomori is Japan's top apple-producing region), giving the cherry trees more spreading branches and denser blooms than elsewhere in the archipelago. Over 2 million visitors come each year during the festival.

⏱ 120 min
2

Hirosaki Castle dates from 1611 (built by Nobuhira Tsugaru); lightning destroyed it in 1627, and reconstruction didn't begin until 1810 - the current tower is one of twelve original castle towers still standing in all of Japan, and one of the smallest. It's classified as an Important Cultural Property. During the cherry blossom festival, you can see the rafts of petals floating in the outer moats at the end of bloom - a fragile pink spectacle that the Japanese call hanaikada.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

The cherry tree tunnel along the west moats is Hirosaki's most photographed image: the branches meet above the path and form a complete pink canopy for a few days. Arrive before 8am to avoid crowds or after 6pm to see the nighttime illumination (until 10pm during the festival) - the vision of illuminated cherry trees in darkness with the moats as mirror is sumptuous.

⏱ 90 min

The Neputamura Museum, in the park's northern section, is dedicated to Tsugaru arts - the Hirosaki region. You can see demonstrations of Tsugaru shamisen (northern electric fiddle) and large lanterns from the Neputa Festival (Hirosaki's counterpart to Aomori's Nebuta Festival, in August). Pottery and lacquer workshops are also offered.

⏱ 75 min

Hirosaki has a historic downtown with Meiji and Taisho period buildings, notably the Catholic cathedral (1910), the old public library, and Higashi-Kogyo school. The Nakamachi district concentrates merchant and minor samurai residences. Outside the festival, Hirosaki is also famous for its apples - the local apple pie is a must-try.

⏱ 60 min

Fukaura

Cobalt blue Aoike Pond in the heart of UNESCO's Shirakami-Sanchi forest

1

Aoike Pond ('blue pond') is the jewel of Juniko - the twelve lakes of the Shirakami-Sanchi virgin forest, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1993 as one of the world's most extensive massifs of primitive beech forests. In reality, the area contains 33 lakes and marshes, but only twelve are visible from the summit of Mount Okuzure - hence the name. Aoike is known for water of such intense cobalt blue you'd think it was ink-dyed; the exact reason for this color remains scientifically unexplained. With 9 meters of visibility, you can spot beech trunks lying on the bottom from the wooden walkway. In the morning, the water is light blue; in the afternoon, it shifts to deep blue.

⏱ 90 min
2

A 10-minute walk from Aoike, Wakitsubo Pond is fed by a natural spring whose water is so pure you can drink it directly or sample green tea made from it at the neighboring Juniko-an tea house. It's one of the rare free-access natural water springs in Japan. The 'Nihon Canyon' (Japanese canyon) hiking trail, with its brown and red rock walls contrasting with the greenery, starts from the Juniko entrance - count on 30 minutes one way.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Juniko's therapy forests are certified as 'Forest Therapy Road' by the Japanese forest therapy organization - the 2.8 km marsh walk through virgin beech forest immerses you in near-total silence, punctuated by the sound of springs and birdsong. Local guides offer guided hikes into the less accessible parts of Shirakami-Sanchi.

⏱ 120 min

The coast along Fukaura at the edge of the Sea of Japan offers landscapes of rocky capes and basalt cliffs, notably Cape Yukiai (brown-red rocks in blue sea). The Gono Line train route along this coast is one of Tohoku's most beautiful - the Resort Shirakami slows at the best viewpoints. If you're heading to Aomori or Akita by train, this coastal journey is worth the detour.

⏱ 60 min

Note: access to Juniko and Aoike closes from December to March due to snow. Plan your visit from April to November. The site is 15 minutes by bus from JR Juniko Station, served by the Gono Line from Hirosaki (2h30) or Akita (2h). By car from Hirosaki, count approximately 1h30.
