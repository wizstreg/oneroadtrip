From Naples to the Lucanian Dolomites: Secret Campania and the Amalfi Coast

This 11-day itinerary crosses Campania and Basilicata, blending two Italys that seem worlds apart: the grand art cities and famous coasts, and the forgotten villages lost in the mountain hinterland. From Naples, you'll reach the colorful island of Procida, the giant palace of Caserta, the overlooked lanes of Calitri in Irpinia, the vertiginous cliffs of Castelmezzano in the Lucanian Dolomites, then the postcard-perfect Amalfi Coast before looping back to Pompeii.

Napoli

★★★★★
📍 Cappella Sansevero Napoli📍 Spaccanapoli Napoli📍 Via San Gregorio Armeno Napoli📍 Basilica Santa Chiara Napoli📍 Chiesa del Gesù Nuovo Napoli📍 Quartieri Spagnoli Napoli📍 Via Toledo Napoli
1

Begin your Neapolitan stay at the Cappella Sansevero, a small Baroque jewel tucked away in an alley in the historic center. The centerpiece is Giuseppe Sanmartino's Veiled Christ (1753), a strikingly realistic marble sculpture—the veil seems genuinely draped over the body. Prince Raimondo di Sangro, who commissioned the work, was also an alchemist: downstairs, you'll see two anatomical machines with eerily preserved circulatory systems. Book your tickets online: the chapel is tiny and queues can be long. Arrive right at opening time at 9 a.m.

⏱ 45 min
2

Next, dive into Spaccanapoli, the arrow-straight street that literally splits Naples in two, dating back to Greco-Roman times. You're crossing the heart of the UNESCO-listed historic center, amid Baroque palaces, churches, and laundry hanging from windows. Detour down Via San Gregorio Armeno, famous for its nativity scene workshops—a tradition stretching back to the 18th century. Every shop overflows with terracotta figurines, including contemporary personalities reimagined as nativity characters.

⏱ 60 min
3

Step inside the Basilica of Santa Chiara and its Clarisses' Cloister, a haven of peace amid Naples' tumult. The cloister, remodeled in the 17th century, is lined with octagonal columns covered in hand-painted majolica tiles depicting pastoral and mythological scenes. Orange trees offer welcome shade. Just across the street, the Chiesa del Gesù Nuovo surprises with its diamond-point facade—this is the former Sanseverino palace, converted into a church by the Jesuits in 1584.

⏱ 50 min
4

Walk up Via Toledo, Naples' main shopping artery. Over a kilometer long, it connects Piazza del Plebiscito to the archaeological museum, cutting through the city from south to north. Halfway up, veer into the Quartieri Spagnoli: this grid of narrow alleys laid out in the 16th century to house Spanish garrisons is Naples at its most authentic. Laundry strung between buildings, street shrines to Maradona, street art, and the scent of ragù wafting from kitchens.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

You can't leave Naples without tasting true Neapolitan pizza. Via dei Tribunali is packed with historic pizzerias. The dough is soft, the edges puffed (the cornicione), wood-fired baking lasts 60 to 90 seconds at 485°C. Order a Margherita—San Marzano tomato, buffalo mozzarella, basil—and you'll understand why Neapolitan pizza has been UNESCO Intangible Heritage since 2017. Expect queues at the most famous spots.

⏱ 60 min

Late in the day, embark on a walking street food tour of Naples. Sample sfogliatella riccia (crispy layered pastry filled with ricotta and semolina, scented with orange blossom), cuoppo fritto (cone of fried seafood), and pizza a portafoglio (pizza folded in four, eaten standing up). Via Toledo and its surroundings have the best stalls.

Stop 2

Napoli

★★★★★
📍 Museo Archeologico Nazionale Napoli📍 Certosa di San Martino Napoli📍 Castel Sant'Elmo Napoli📍 Castel dell'Ovo Napoli📍 Piazza del Plebiscito Napoli📍 Teatro San Carlo Napoli📍 Lungomare Napoli
1

This morning, head to the Museo Archeologico Nazionale, one of Europe's greatest archaeological museums. You'll find the Farnese Collection with its colossal sculptures—the Farnese Hercules stands 3.17 meters tall—and above all the mosaics and frescoes recovered from Pompeii and Herculaneum. Don't miss the Secret Cabinet, long closed to the public, which displays Roman erotic art. Allow at least 2 hours, and arrive at opening to avoid the crowds.

⏱ 120 min
2

After lunch, take the central funicular from Via Toledo up to Vomero. In a few minutes, you shift from street chaos to hilltop calm. The Certosa di San Martino, a Carthusian monastery founded in 1325 and remodeled in Baroque style in the 17th century, offers the finest view in Naples: the gulf, Vesuvius, Capri, the historic center at your feet. The museum inside displays a collection of 18th-century Neapolitan nativity scenes of stunning refinement.

⏱ 90 min
3

Right next door, Castel Sant'Elmo, a star-shaped fortress built by the Angevins in the 14th century then restructured by the Spanish in 1537, dominates the entire city from 250 meters above sea level. Climb onto the ramparts for a 360° panorama—this is the most comprehensive viewpoint over Naples and its bay. On clear days, you can make out the islands of Procida and Ischia.

⏱ 40 min
4

Descend to Piazza del Plebiscito, Naples' largest square at 25,000 m². On one side, the Palazzo Reale, residence of the Spanish viceroys then the Bourbons; on the other, the colonnade of the Basilica of San Francesco di Paola, directly inspired by Rome's Pantheon. Just beside it, the Teatro San Carlo, inaugurated in 1737—41 years before Milan's La Scala—is the world's oldest opera house still in operation.

⏱ 40 min
5

End the day with a stroll along the Lungomare to Castel dell'Ovo, Naples' oldest fortress, perched on the islet of Megaride. Legend has it that Virgil hid a magic egg in the foundations—as long as the egg holds, Naples holds. Climb onto the ramparts for a panoramic view over the gulf, Vesuvius, and Capri. Entry is free and it's one of the best spots for sunset.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Descend beneath the city with a visit to underground Naples (Napoli Sotterranea). Under Piazza San Gaetano, 40 meters deep, you'll explore a network of galleries carved into tuff in Greek times (4th century BC), later used as a Roman cistern, then as an air-raid shelter during World War II. Some passages are very narrow—wear comfortable clothing.

⏱ 75 min

Stop 3

Procida

★★★★☆
📍 Museo di Capodimonte Napoli📍 Catacombe di San Gennaro Napoli📍 Palazzo dello Spagnolo Napoli📍 Marina Grande Procida
1

Dedicate the morning to the Museo e Real Bosco di Capodimonte, perched on the hill of the same name. The palace, built in 1738 for Charles of Bourbon, houses the Farnese Collection of paintings: works by Caravaggio, Titian, Raphael, and Botticelli. The surrounding 134-hectare park is Naples' green lung—perfect for catching your breath between galleries. Allow 2 to 3 hours for the museum alone. Entry is free the first Sunday of each month.

⏱ 150 min
2

Descend toward the Sanità district to visit the Catacombe di San Gennaro, southern Italy's largest catacombs. Carved into tuff from the 2nd century onward, they extend over two levels and preserve remarkable early Christian frescoes. This is where San Gennaro, Naples' patron saint, was buried in the 5th century. The guided tours are run by a local cooperative that has revitalized the entire neighborhood.

⏱ 60 min
3

In the same neighborhood, stop outside the Palazzo dello Spagnolo, a masterpiece by architect Ferdinando Sanfelice (1738). Its monumental "falcon's wing" (ali di falco) staircase is one of Naples' most photographed. Fifty meters further, the Palazzo Sanfelice offers a similar staircase. These two palaces illustrate Neapolitan Baroque at its most theatrical.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Mid-afternoon, head to Molo Beverello (Naples port) and board the ferry to Procida—about 40 minutes' crossing. The island measures barely 4 km² and has 10,000 inhabitants. Arriving at Marina Grande, the spectacle begins immediately: pastel facades—yellow, pink, blue—stacked one atop another. Drop your bags at your accommodation and use the evening light for a first stroll around the harbor.

⏱ 90 min
Hotel La Corricella9.5
Stop 4

Procida

★★★★☆
📍 Marina Corricella Procida📍 Terra Murata Procida📍 Abbazia San Michele Arcangelo Procida📍 Palazzo d'Avalos Procida📍 Casale Vascello Procida📍 Spiaggia Chiaiolella Procida
1

Head to Marina Corricella, the island's most photogenic fishing village. No cars circulate here—you descend via staircases lined with arches and loggias. This is where Il Postino (The Postman, 1994) starring Philippe Noiret was filmed. Settle on a terrace facing the colorful boats for a coffee. The best viewpoint is from the terrace near the church of Santa Maria delle Grazie Incoronata, with its majolica dome.

⏱ 60 min
2

Climb up to Terra Murata, the island's highest point at 90 meters. This fortified village built in the 15th century protected inhabitants from pirates. You'll find the Abbazia di San Michele Arcangelo, dedicated to the island's patron saint, with its cloister and religious artworks. From the ramparts, the view embraces the entire Gulf of Naples, Capri, Ischia, and Vesuvius.

⏱ 60 min
3

Right next to the abbey, the Palazzo d'Avalos, a 16th-century palace built by the noble family of the same name, served as a prison from 1830 to 1988. Inmates there learned linen working, a craft tradition of Procida. Guided tours including the cells are organized, along with contemporary art exhibitions. From the palace, descend toward Casale Vascello, the island's oldest quarter, with its pastel-toned houses featuring external "giraffe neck" (a collo di giraffa) staircases.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In the afternoon, take bus L1 or L2 from Marina Grande to reach Spiaggia di Chiaiolella, in the south of the island. This is Procida's largest sandy beach, bordered by a tranquil marina. The water is calm and shallow—perfect for swimming. From there, you can see the islet of Vivara, a nature reserve shaped like a crescent, remnant of an ancient volcanic crater.

⏱ 120 min

Taste the lingua di bue (ox tongue), Procida's signature pastry: crispy puff pastry filled with lemon custard made from the island's lemons. Procida's limone pane is unique—its thick, fragrant peel also flavors granitas, ice creams, and liqueurs. Settle by the harbor to enjoy your treat facing the sea.

⏱ 30 min
Hotel La Corricella9.5

Stop 5

Caserta

★★★★☆
📍 Reggia di Caserta📍 Parco Reale Caserta📍 Giardino Inglese Caserta📍 Casertavecchia📍 San Leucio Caserta
1

Take the morning ferry back to Naples, then drive 30 km north to Caserta. The Reggia di Caserta awaits: with its 1,200 rooms spread over 5 floors, 245-meter facade, and more than 2 million cubic meters of volume, it's the world's largest royal residence. Charles of Bourbon had it built from 1752 by architect Luigi Vanvitelli to rival Versailles. The king never slept there—called to the Spanish throne in 1759, he left Naples before construction was completed.

⏱ 120 min
2

Inside, don't miss the Grand Staircase of Honor, which inspired the one in Saint Petersburg's Alexander Palace. The Palatine Chapel, inspired by Versailles' chapel, features a polychrome marble floor beneath majestic colonnades. The Royal Apartments alternate between 18th-century Baroque decor and Empire style—Joachim Murat, King of Naples from 1808 to 1815, remodeled several rooms. The Royal Theatre, inaugurated in 1769, has a unique feature: its backstage could open onto the park, creating a natural backdrop.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Royal Park extends 120 hectares behind the palace. A 3-km axis aligns fountains, basins, and monumental cascades in perspective. The Grande Cascade, 78 meters high, is fed by the 38-km-long Carolino Aqueduct. At the foot of the cascade, the Fountain of Diana and Actaeon stages the goddess and her nymphs in a spectacular sculptural group. You can traverse the park on foot, by bicycle, or by carriage. Allow at least 1.5 hours to reach the cascade and return.

⏱ 90 min
4

Before leaving the park, explore the English Garden, laid out in the late 18th century by botanist John Andrew Graefer on orders from Queen Maria Carolina of Austria. The atmosphere radically differs from the French garden: bodies of water hidden in vegetation, fake ruins, a cryptoporticus imitating a Roman temple, and the Bath of Venus with its statue of the goddess emerging from a basin. More than 200 botanical species coexist here.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Late afternoon, drive up to Casertavecchia, the medieval village perched on the hill overlooking the modern town. At 400 meters altitude, this town founded by the Lombards in the 8th century has preserved its medieval charm intact: cobbled lanes, Norman tower, and the splendid 12th-century Romanesque cathedral with its tuff dome and Arab-Norman-inspired interlace. The panorama over the plain of Caserta and distant Vesuvius is worth the climb.

⏱ 75 min

If you still have time, detour to the Belvedere di San Leucio, 3 km north of Caserta. This hamlet was transformed in the late 18th century by Ferdinand IV into a model silk manufactory, with a socially avant-garde set of rules: compulsory education, equal pay for men and women, ban on dowries. The palace and workshops are UNESCO-listed along with the Reggia.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 6

Calitri

★☆☆☆☆
📍 Borgo Castello Calitri📍 Chiesa di San Canio Calitri📍 Museo della Ceramica Calitri📍 Centro storico Calitri
1

Leave Caserta heading east and plunge into the countryside hinterland. After 2 hours driving through the mountains of Irpinia, you reach Calitri—nicknamed the "Positano of Irpinia" by the Italian Touring Club. The village is perched at 530 meters altitude on a tuff ridge, at the border of three regions: Campania, Basilicata, and Puglia. Its triangular silhouette of colorful houses, stacked in parallel tiers, is visible from afar in the landscape.

⏱ 30 min
2

Climb to Borgo Castello, the upper part of the historic center built around the ruins of the 13th-century medieval castle, erected under Frederick II. The 1980 earthquake devastated this area—then inhabited—and froze it in time. Today, the ruins have been partially restored and the Ceramics Museum is installed there. Calitri is a member of the Italian Ceramic Towns: the pottery tradition dates back to Antiquity, with kilns and workshops documented since the 17th century.

⏱ 60 min
3

Descend into the labyrinthine centro storico of narrow lanes. You'll encounter portals in carved local stone, noble palaces that survived the earthquakes, and the famous "cupe"—underground passages carved into the rock that once served as cellars. Step through the door of the church of San Canio, the village's patron saint celebrated on May 25, whose Baroque interior contrasts with the exterior sobriety.

⏱ 45 min
4

Walking the streets, you'll notice caves carved into the tuff beneath the houses. Some still serve to age Caciocavallo di Grotta, a stretched-curd cheese hung in these natural cellars where humidity and constant temperature ensure slow aging. It's a typical product of eastern Irpinia, and local artisans will gladly let you taste it.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Treat yourself to a wine tasting from Irpinia at one of the surrounding estates. The region produces three great vintages: Taurasi DOCG (Aglianico, often compared to Barolo), Fiano di Avellino DOCG (mineral and elegant white), and Greco di Tufo DOCG (white with almond notes). Irpinia is one of Italian wine's best-kept secrets—the vineyards grow between 400 and 700 meters altitude, yielding wines of remarkable freshness.

⏱ 90 min

Late in the day, taste local specialties: acqua sala (slices of bread soaked in salted boiling water with hard-boiled eggs and spicy oil), cavatelli with beans, and Calitri's bread, baked in a wood oven according to ancestral tradition, whose aroma fills the lanes on baking days. It's peasant cooking, no frills, that warms the heart.

⏱ 60 min

Castelmezzano

★★☆☆☆
📍 Castelmezzano centro storico📍 Gradinata Normanna Castelmezzano📍 Chiesa Santa Maria dell'Olmo Castelmezzano📍 Volo dell'Angelo Castelmezzano📍 Dolomiti Lucane📍 Pietrapertosa
1

Mountain road from Calitri (about 1h40) through the wild landscapes of Basilicata. Arriving in Castelmezzano, the visual impact is immediate: this village of 800 souls is literally embedded in the sandstone spires of the Lucanian Dolomites, as if thrust out of the rock. Its name, from the Latin Castrum Medianum (the castle in the middle), recalls its position between Pietrapertosa and Albano di Lucania. Classified among Italy's "Most Beautiful Villages," it was founded by shepherds fleeing Saracen incursions.

⏱ 30 min
2

Climb the Gradinata Normanna, a staircase of 54 steps carved directly into the rock, which once led to the watchtower of the Norman-Swabian castle. The ascent is short but impressive—no handrail, vertiginous overhang. From the top, the view plunges over the village, the Basento valley, and the wind-eroded sandstone spires. The visit is only possible with a licensed local guide—book in advance.

⏱ 45 min
3

Descend to the Chiesa Madre Santa Maria dell'Olmo, built in local stone in the 13th century. Inside, you'll find a polychrome wooden statue of the Virgin and Child dating from the 14th century and a Baroque altar. On the exterior walls, a Templar cross is still visible—the Knights Templar left traces in the village, also visible in the municipal coat of arms which reproduces the Templar seal of two knights on a single horse.

⏱ 30 min
4

Traverse the village using the lanes that run along the bases of the sandstone monoliths. The juxtaposition of stone houses and massive cliffs is striking—in places, the rock serves as the load-bearing wall of dwellings. Reach the panoramic viewpoint east of the village for an unobstructed view over the entire chain of the Lucanian Dolomites and the village of Pietrapertosa, clinging in an amphitheater on the other side of the valley.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

If you love adrenaline, the Volo dell'Angelo is the region's star attraction. Hooked to a steel cable stretched between Castelmezzano and Pietrapertosa, you fly over the valley at an altitude of 400 meters, over a distance of 1,452 meters, at speeds reaching 120 km/h. There are two lines: the Peschiere line (Castelmezzano → Pietrapertosa) and the San Martino line (return). The ticket includes both flights and a shuttle between the two villages.

⏱ 90 min

For hikers, the Path of the Seven Stones links Castelmezzano to Pietrapertosa through the valley—allow 1.5 hours one way. Seven stations mark the route, each marked by a sculpture and a poem evoking local legends. The path descends into the wooded valley before climbing back up to the other village. It's a perfect complement to the Volo dell'Angelo—do one in one direction, the other in the other.

Stop 8

Amalfi

★★★★★
📍 Cattedrale di Sant'Andrea Amalfi📍 Chiostro del Paradiso Amalfi📍 Museo della Carta Amalfi📍 Centro storico Amalfi
1

Long driving stage from Castelmezzano (about 3 hours via the A3 motorway then the SS163). You'll arrive around noon on the Amalfi Coast, a UNESCO site since 1997. Get settled, have a leisurely lunch, then set out to explore Amalfi in early afternoon. The former maritime republic rivaled Venice, Genoa, and Pisa in the Middle Ages. The Cathedral of Sant'Andrea (9th century, remodeled in the 13th) dominates the central square with its Campanian facade covered in golden mosaics and its 57-step climb.

⏱ 45 min
2

Descend into the Chiostro del Paradiso (1268), the former cemetery of Amalfi's nobility, an Arab-Norman cloister of 120 interlaced columns forming pointed arches. The blend of Eastern and Western influences recalls that Amalfi traded actively with the Arab world. From the cloister, you access the crypt housing the relics of Saint Andrew the Apostle, brought from Constantinople in 1208.

⏱ 30 min
3

Walk up the Valle dei Mulini, the small valley that penetrates behind the town. Amalfi was one of the first papermaking centers in Europe, a technique brought from the Arab world. The Museo della Carta is installed in a former 13th-century paper mill: the period wooden machines are still in working order and a demonstration shows you the artisanal manufacturing process. You can leave with handmade sheets.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Use the late afternoon to wander Amalfi's lanes. Via Lorenzo d'Amalfi, the main artery, is lined with shops selling ceramics, limoncello, and artisanal stationery. Walk to the small harbor to observe fishing boats and have a drink facing the sea. If you have time, climb the few steps to the upper town quarter—tourists are rare there and the view over the rooftops and cathedral is superb.

⏱ 60 min

Positano

★★★★★
📍 Ravello Villa Rufolo📍 Ravello Villa Cimbrone📍 Grotta dello Smeraldo Conca dei Marini📍 Praiano📍 Positano centro
1

Drive up to Ravello first thing in the morning, perched 350 meters above the sea, 7 km from Amalfi. Villa Rufolo, built in the 13th century, inspired Richard Wagner for Klingsor's garden in Parsifal—he stayed there in 1880. Every summer, a classical music festival is held there on a terrace suspended above the sea. The gardens, with their medieval towers and palm trees, offer a vertiginous panorama over the Gulf of Salerno.

⏱ 60 min
2

A few minutes' walk away, Villa Cimbrone boasts the famous Terrazza dell'Infinito—the Terrace of Infinity. Bordered by 18th-century marble busts, it overlooks the sea from 350 meters high. Gore Vidal, who stayed there regularly, described it as the most beautiful view in the world. The gardens mix statues, neoclassical temples, and grottoes in a romantic setting. Arrive early—the terrace fills up quickly.

⏱ 60 min
3

Head back down toward the coast and stop at the Grotta dello Smeraldo (Emerald Grotto) at Conca dei Marini, 4 km from Amalfi. This sea cave, discovered in 1932, owes its name to the emerald color the water takes under the effect of light filtering through an underwater opening. You reach it by elevator carved into the cliff or by boat from the sea. Inside, stalactites and stalagmites frame a turquoise pool.

⏱ 40 min
4

Continue west on the SS163. Stop at Praiano, between Amalfi and Positano. This village clinging to the mountain is far less touristy than its neighbors. The church of San Gennaro, with its colorful majolica dome, dominates a small square from which the view over the coast is superb. Below, the cove of Marina di Praia—a tiny beach squeezed between two cliffs—is one of the coast's most photogenic spots.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Arrive in Positano late afternoon. Descend the stairs to the Chiesa di Santa Maria Assunta, recognizable by its yellow, green, and blue majolica dome gleaming in the sun. Inside, a 13th-century Byzantine icon of the Black Madonna. Continue to Spiaggia Grande to enjoy the golden evening light on the village—this is when the colors are most beautiful and the crowds begin to disperse.

⏱ 75 min

Positano

★★★★★
📍 Sentiero degli Dei Agerola Nocelle📍 Fornillo beach Positano📍 Positano Spiaggia Grande📍 Chiesa Santa Maria Assunta Positano
1

Devote the morning to Positano itself. The former fishing village became a chic resort in the 1950s when writer John Steinbeck introduced it to the world in an article for Harper's Bazaar. Stroll the stepped lanes among artisan shops, art galleries, and bougainvillea. For more tranquility, follow the cliff west to Fornillo beach, smaller and less crowded than Spiaggia Grande.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Set out on the Sentiero degli Dei (Path of the Gods), one of Italy's most beautiful hikes. The 7-km trail links Bomerano (hamlet of Agerola) to Nocelle (above Positano), at 630 meters altitude. Allow 2 to 3 hours walking on a moderate-difficulty path, among rosemary bushes, olive trees, and vertiginous coastal panoramas. Legend has it that this path was traced by gods descending from heaven to save Ulysses from the sirens' song. From Nocelle, 1,700 steps descend to Positano.

⏱ 210 min

Late in the day, treat yourself to a tasting of artisanal limoncello. The Amalfi Coast produces sfusato amalfitano lemons, larger and more fragrant than ordinary lemons. Limoncello is prepared by macerating the zest in pure alcohol for at least a month. Several local producers offer tastings accompanied by lemon cakes—a gentle end to the day after the morning's exertion.

Stop 11

Napoli

★★★★★
📍 Scavi di Pompei📍 Foro di Pompei📍 Anfiteatro di Pompei📍 Villa dei Misteri Pompei📍 Casa dei Vettii Pompei📍 Terme Stabiane Pompei📍 Orto dei Fuggiaschi Pompei
1

Leave Positano early morning and drive toward Pompeii (about 1h15). The archaeological site extends over 66 hectares, of which 45 have been excavated—it's the only ancient city in the world offering such a complete vision of a Roman town. The eruption of Vesuvius in AD 79 buried Pompeii under 6 meters of ash and pumice. About 20,000 inhabitants lived there; more than 1,100 bodies have been found since excavations began in 1748. Arrive right at opening at 9 a.m. and allow at minimum 3 to 4 hours.

⏱ 30 min
2

Start with the Forum, the heart of public life: the basilica (courthouse), the temples of Jupiter and Apollo, the covered market (macellum). You're walking on the same basalt paving stones as the Romans, with chariot wheel ruts still visible. The raised stones in the streets served as pedestrian crossings—Pompeii had a wastewater drainage system running through the streets.

⏱ 45 min
3

Don't miss the House of the Vettii, recently restored, one of Pompeii's most richly decorated domus. The Vettii brothers, former slaves who became wealthy merchants, had their walls painted with frescoes in the Pompeian "fourth style"—mythology, genre scenes, still lifes. The interior garden with its water jets and columns has been reconstructed based on archaeological traces.

⏱ 30 min
4

Head to the Amphitheatre, the oldest existing stone Roman amphitheater in the world, built in 80 BC—nearly 150 years before the Colosseum. It could accommodate up to 20,000 spectators. Right beside it, the Grande Palestre (gymnasium) with its plane trees planted by the Romans, some of whose fossilized trunks are still visible.

⏱ 30 min
5

Pass through the Garden of the Fugitives (Orto dei Fuggiaschi), where 13 plaster casts reproduce the bodies of victims caught by the pyroclastic flow as they attempted to flee toward the Nocera gate. These casts, obtained by pouring plaster into cavities left by decomposed bodies—a technique invented by Giuseppe Fiorelli in 1863—are profoundly moving.

⏱ 20 min
6

Finish with the Villa of the Mysteries, on the northwest periphery of the site. This large suburban villa houses Pompeii's most famous fresco: a cycle of 29 life-size figures painted on a Pompeian red background, likely representing a Dionysian initiation rite. The quality of preservation and intensity of colors, after nearly 2,000 years, are astonishing.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

If your flight or train isn't until the next day, you can climb Vesuvius late afternoon. From the national park entrance, a 1-km trail (about 30 minutes' walk) leads to the crater rim, at 1,281 meters altitude. The crater measures 700 meters in diameter and 200 meters deep. In clear weather, the view embraces the entire Gulf of Naples, Capri, the Phlegraean Fields, and the mountains of Basilicata. Vesuvius is continental Europe's only active volcano—its last eruption was in 1944.
