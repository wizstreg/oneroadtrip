Castleton

★★★☆☆
1

Built in 1086 by William Peveril, loyal follower of William the Conqueror, Peveril Castle has held its limestone promontory for nearly 1,000 years. Henry II raised the keep in 1176; today it belongs to the Duchy of Lancaster. The climb is steep but the view over Hope Valley from the summit more than compensates. Allow 1 hour, book online to skip the queue.

⏱ 75 min
2

Locals have called it The Devil's Arse for centuries, the official name Peak Cavern being more presentable. It's the only one of Castleton's four caves that's entirely natural, with the largest cave entrance in Britain - 18 m high, 30 m wide. Rope-makers still worked here into the 20th century, sheltered by the limestone vault. Bring a fleece: it's 8°C inside.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The limestone walls of Winnats Pass close in on the road like giant hands: this 28% gradient gorge is banned to HGVs and coaches. It's been the only western access to Castleton since the Mam Tor flank collapsed in 1977. A 45-minute walk from the bottom offers a perspective the car can't give.

⏱ 60 min
$$$9.4Chandler House
🚗Drive to next stop: 5 km · 10min

Mam Tor

★★★★☆
1

Shivering Mountain: it's not the wind but a 4,000-year-old landslide that justifies the name. Mam Tor's southeast flank - 517 m, ancient Bronze Age fort - still moves several centimetres each year according to the BGS (British Geological Survey). It put an end to the A625 in 1979: the accumulated tarmac layers reached 2 metres thick. 360° view over Hope Valley and Edale.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

If you set off early from the National Trust car park (S33 8WA), the Great Ridge is often deserted before 9am. The 6.5 km circuit from Castleton takes 3 to 4 hours; the final ascent up Mam Tor is short but steep. Walking poles useful in wet conditions: the limestone gets slippery.

⏱ 180 min
$$$9.3Bulls Head, Castleton
🚗Drive to next stop: 30 km · 35min

Chatsworth

★★★★☆
1

Too grand, too rich, too beautiful - Chatsworth deserves its reputation as the 'Palace of the Peak'. The seat of the Dukes of Devonshire holds 30 rooms open to the public, including the apartments where Mary Queen of Scots was held prisoner between 1569 and 1584. Bess of Hardwick began construction in 1549; the current Duke, 12th of that name, still lives here.

⏱ 90 min
2

Chatsworth's garden spans 105 acres and reflects five centuries of uninterrupted horticulture. The maze, fountains, rock garden and 17th-century cascade are the highlights. It takes a team of ten gardeners to maintain it.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Fallow deer approach without wariness in the 1,000-acre park surrounding Chatsworth. A free 5 km walk follows the Derwent and climbs towards the woods. The park is free and always open - it's the best value for time if you're pressed.

⏱ 60 min
$$$9.5Rock Mill Retreat
🚗Drive to next stop: 75 km · 1h20

Malham

★★★★☆
1

A giant waterfall vanished here in the 19th century. The curved cliff of Malham Cove - 70 m high - has barely seen water flow abundantly since exceptional floods; a rare episode occurred in December 2015 during Storm Desmond. Today the stream plunges underground a kilometre upstream. On the plateau, the limestone pavement (clints and grykes) served as backdrop for a Harry Potter film. Ascent via 400 steps cut into the left wall.

⏱ 75 min
2

Unknown to most visitors who stop at Malham Cove, Gordale Scar is actually a gorge with two active waterfalls framed by overhanging limestone cliffs around a hundred metres high. Turner made sketches here. Access from Malham centre takes 25 minutes on foot.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Walk on the limestone pavement above the Cove to feel the strangeness of the landscape - the fissures (grykes) plunge more than a metre deep. The classic Malham circuit takes in the Cove, the tarn and Gordale Scar in roughly 8 km. Waterproof boots essential in wet weather.

⏱ 150 min
$$$9.8Former Methodist Church - Uk49641
🚗Drive to next stop: 30 km · 45min

Ribblehead

★★★★☆
1

24 arches, 400 metres, over 100 deaths according to the parish registers of Chapel-le-Dale: the human cost of Ribblehead Viaduct. Built between 1870 and 1875 for the Midland Railway, it stands 32 metres high and cost £343,000 at the time. The 2,300 navvies lived in camps christened Sebastopol and Belgravia. The Settle-Carlisle train still crosses.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

The wind whistles between the arches like inside an open-air cathedral. A 20-minute walk from the B6255 car park leads beneath the viaduct to appreciate the scale of the pillars. The Station Inn, right next door, offers pints with direct views - one of the finest outposts of its kind.

⏱ 40 min
$$$10Barn Owl
🚗Drive to next stop: 22 km · 30min

Aysgarth

★★★★☆
1

Turner sketched here. Wordsworth and his sister Dorothy stopped while waiting for their stagecoach. More recently, the Robin Hood and Little John quarterstaff duel was filmed at the Upper Falls for the 1991 movie. The Ure's three waterfalls cascade over nearly 2 km of limestone slabs. The spectacle is most dramatic after rain.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Less spectacular in dry summer, absolutely impressive after autumn or winter rains. The path linking the three falls (Upper, Middle, Lower) takes 45 minutes at a leisurely pace. The National Park Centre by the car park is free and posts current water levels.

⏱ 60 min
$$$10Romantic Luxury Hideaway, Aysgarth Falls
🚗Drive to next stop: 60 km · 1h10

York

★★★★☆
1

2.75 miles of ramparts: the best preserved in England. Founded by the Romans in 71 AD, buried by the Vikings under earth banks in the 9th century, rebuilt in stone in the 13th and 14th centuries - York's wall carries four layers of history beneath its 4 metres of height. The complete walk takes about 2 hours and is free. Micklegate Bar also served as deterrent: traitors' heads were displayed on spikes here.

⏱ 90 min
2

The facades lean so much they nearly touch above the street at The Shambles. It's one of the best-preserved medieval streets in Europe; the timber-framed houses date from the 14th to 16th centuries. Now souvenir shops and cafés, but the architecture remains genuine. 15-minute stroll; avoid midday in high season - very crowded.

⏱ 30 min
3

Touristy, but the recreated smell of 900s Viking York genuinely takes you by surprise at the JORVIK Viking Centre. The site is based on real excavations conducted in the 1970s that unearthed complete streets from the Danish capital of England. The ride-through lasts 20 minutes; allow 1 hour with the exhibition. Online booking mandatory in season.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

York Minster holds the largest collection of medieval stained glass in Britain. Construction began in 1220; the west front with its two towers reaches 60 m. Entry is ticketed but includes the climb up 275 steps of the central tower for a view over York's rooftops. See it early morning for the light through the glass.

⏱ 90 min

Finish with a pint at the Golden Fleece on Pavement - York's most haunted pub according to its regulars. The Snickelways, those medieval spiral alleyways connecting inner courtyards, merit a 30-minute exploration after dark.

⏱ 60 min
$$$9.4Minster Walk Guesthouse
🚗Drive to next stop: 50 km · 55min

Pickering

★★☆☆☆
1

The heather turns purple in August across the North York Moors - 554 km² of open moorland, one of the largest heather moorlands in the UK. The road from Pickering to Whitby via Saltersgate crosses breathtaking ridges. The views are most intense between Fylingdales and Goathland.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The North Yorkshire Moors Railway crosses 18 miles of moorland by steam locomotive between Pickering and Whitby. It's one of England's finest heritage lines, also used as backdrop in several TV productions. A single journey takes 1h30 - possibility to get off at Goathland (Aidensfield in Heartbeat) and catch a later train.

⏱ 90 min
$$$1017 Costa Way
🚗Drive to next stop: 25 km · 35min

Whitby

★★★★☆
1

Bram Stoker stayed across the road, in a West Cliff boarding house, and invented Dracula while gazing at these ruins in 1890. Whitby Abbey was founded in 657 by Abbess Hild; the Synod of Whitby was held here in 664 to fix the date of Easter. Henry VIII dissolved it in 1539; German shells in the Great War finished crumbling the nave. Entry (English Heritage) includes a museum.

⏱ 75 min
2

199 steps: too crowded in July, but the climb from the harbour to St Mary's churchyard and the ruins is worth the effort at dawn. The graveyard directly inspired scenes in Dracula - Stoker observed sailors' tombstones worn away by time. View over the entire harbour and North Sea.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Get the finest fish and chips on the Yorkshire coast at the Magpie Café on Pier Road - queue guaranteed but constant since 1939. Whitby harbour, with its fishing boats and lobster pots, remains authentically a working fishing port. Whitby's Goth Weekend (twice yearly, April and October) makes it the world capital of English Gothic.

⏱ 60 min
$$$9.5Sneaton Castle
🚗Drive to next stop: 8 km · 15min

Robin Hoods Bay

★★★☆☆
1

In the 18th century, a bale of silk could cross the entire village from bottom to top without ever leaving the houses - the underground tunnels connecting cottages to each other formed a smuggling network. Robin Hood's Bay was the most active smuggling hub on the Yorkshire coast. Tea, gin, brandy, silk - everything arrived from the Netherlands and France to dodge taxes. Smugglers' wives poured boiling water from windows onto customs men.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The lanes plunge so steeply towards the harbour that cars are banned in the lower part. The walk from the upper car park to the Bay Hotel at the water's edge - official endpoint of Wainwright's Coast to Coast - takes 15 minutes. The rock pools at low tide are among the best fossil zones in Yorkshire.

⏱ 60 min
$$$9.5Maypole Cottage
🚗Drive to next stop: 85 km · 1h30

Northallerton

★★★☆☆
1

Northallerton is a North Yorkshire market town you might pass through without stopping - but its lively High Street offers a welcome contrast with the wild landscapes before and after. The town is mentioned in the Domesday Book of 1086; the Battle of the Standard, in 1138, was fought two miles north.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Strategic lunch stop on the High Street before the remaining 150 km to the Lake District. The Golden Lion, a former coaching inn on the main street, serves Yorkshire dishes at reasonable prices. Northallerton is also known for its independent shops - a coffee and short break do you good.

⏱ 45 min
$$$10Ewe and Me Glamping
🚗Drive to next stop: 145 km · 2h30

Windermere

★★★★☆
1

Too crowded in August to be honest - 10.5 miles long, England's largest natural lake, and everyone remembers. But the water stays beautiful and the 18 islands merit the cruise. The first steamboats plied Windermere from 1845; some classic-hulled vessels date from the 1930s. Windermere Lake Cruises runs departures every 30 minutes from Bowness.

⏱ 90 min
2

Bowness-on-Windermere, Victorian lakeside resort turned epicentre of Lake District tourism, merits a walk through its streets to understand how the railway's arrival in 1847 transformed a hamlet into a tourist station. The World of Beatrix Potter is here - for families with young children.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

If weather cooperates, the evening cruise (5pm departure from Bowness, Red Cruise to Ambleside) offers the softest light on the fells. Tickets allow you to get off at any pier and catch the next boat. Full duration to Waterhead: 35 minutes.

⏱ 75 min
$$$9.9Abbys Cottage
🚗Drive to next stop: 10 km · 20min

Grasmere

★★★☆☆
1

Wordsworth lived here from 1799 to 1808: it was in this cottage that Keats, De Quincey and Coleridge came to visit him, that Dorothy kept her intimate journals, and some of English Romantic poetry's most important texts were written. The house is preserved in its period state. Wordsworth is buried in St Oswald's churchyard a stone's throw away.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The smell escapes from the 1854 shop and captures passers-by before they even see the sign - Grasmere Gingerbread, a secret recipe still contract-protected. Half-biscuit half-cake, sold by the piece from the old village school. Join the queue, it's short. Add a rowing-boat tour of Grasmere lake - hire on site.

⏱ 60 min
$$$9.6Quarters
🚗Drive to next stop: 40 km · 55min

Wasdale Head

★★★★☆
1

79 metres deep: the bottom of Wast Water lies below sea level. It's England's deepest lake, ringed by the Screes - 550 m of scree cliffs plunging straight into black water. Scafell Pike, England's highest peak (978 m), towers at the eastern end. In 2007, Wast Water was voted 'Britain's favourite view' by ITV viewers.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

No marked path, no café, no Wi-Fi - that's precisely what makes Wast Water valuable. The narrow road runs along the lake for 5 km to Wasdale Head. The Screes path (south shore) is walkable in 2h30 return from the end of the lake - unstable terrain, hiking boots essential.

⏱ 90 min
$$$9.2Glaramara Hotel
🚗Drive to next stop: 30 km · 45min

Buttermere

★★★★☆
1

The fells reflect in Buttermere's calm water with almost unreal sharpness in still weather. This 2 km lake is framed by Fleetwith Pike, Haystacks (favourite fell of the great walker Alfred Wainwright, whose ashes rest here) and Red Pike. Less visited than the southern lakes, it retains an untouched wild character.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

7 km around the lake: doable in 2h30 for an average walker, without significant elevation. The circuit includes forest passages, a tunnel cut through rock and several viewpoints over Crummock Water. The Bridge Hotel in the village sells packed lunches - handy for a picnic en route.

⏱ 150 min
$$$9.2Bridge Hotel
🚗Drive to next stop: 18 km · 30min

Keswick

★★★★☆
1

The English call it the most atmospherically-sited stone circle in Britain - and for once, the superlative is defensible. Castlerigg dates from about 3,000 BC, 38 standing stones in a natural amphitheatre of fells, free and accessible 24/7. Dawn, here, is an experience.

⏱ 45 min
2

Queen of the Lakes, they say - and for once the label sticks. Derwentwater, ringed by fells including Skiddaw and Cat Bells, offers navigable waters by kayak, motor boat or rowing boat, 10 minutes' walk from Keswick centre. The National Trust has managed the shore since 1902 - the first Lake District property protected by the charity.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Pencil Museum sounds naff on paper but hides a real story: graphite from Seathwaite, discovered in the Borrowdale valley in the 16th century, gave birth to 14 pencil factories in Keswick. The museum houses the world's longest colouring pencil (8 m) and a WWII pencil with maps hidden inside. Entry £6.60.

⏱ 60 min
$$$9.7Fellpack House
🚗Drive to next stop: 50 km · 1h

Durham

★★★★☆
1

Begun in 1093, Durham Cathedral is the greatest monument of Norman architecture in England according to UNESCO, which granted it World Heritage status in 1986. The round arches and ribbed vaults of the nave predated Gothic art by several decades. The bodies of Saint Cuthbert (evangelist of Northumbria) and the Venerable Bede rest here. Bill Bryson called it 'the best cathedral on the planet'.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Wear forms an almost perfect loop around Durham's peninsula, creating a natural promontory where cathedral and castle have faced each other for 900 years. The 3.5 km walk around the loop is flat, crossed by woods and meadows, with alternating views towards both monuments. Free, accessible to all.

⏱ 75 min
$$$9.9Durham Holiday Home
🚗Drive to next stop: 45 km · 50min

Housesteads

★★★★☆
1

A Roman fort with latrines still perfectly visible after 1,700 years - it's the detail that captures everyone at Housesteads. This 5-acre auxiliary fort, occupied for nearly 300 years, housed up to 800 soldiers. Named Vercovicium ('the place of effective fighters'), it sits on the Whin Sill escarpment with a view towards Scotland. It's the best-preserved Roman fort in Britain according to English Heritage.

⏱ 75 min
2

Walking on Hadrian's Wall gives concrete scale to Roman ambition: 117 km from Wallsend to Bowness-on-Solway, built from 122 AD on Emperor Hadrian's orders. UNESCO-listed in 1987 - and acknowledged inspiration for George R.R. Martin's Wall in Game of Thrones. The section from Housesteads to Steel Rigg (4 km one way) is the most spectacular.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Parking at Housesteads managed by Northumberland National Park. AD122 bus from Hexham or Haltwhistle - year-round service, day ticket recommended to combine fort and Sycamore Gap in one day.

⏱ 30 min
$$$9.9The Old School House
🚗Drive to next stop: 5 km · 10min

Sycamore Gap

★★★★☆
1

The tree was felled on the night of 28 September 2023 by two men sentenced to 4 years and 3 months in prison in July 2025. The Sycamore Gap sycamore, planted about 200 years ago in a photogenic dip in the wall, was the UK's most photographed tree and had appeared in Robin Hood: Prince of Thieves (1991). The stump is visible; a wood sculpture made from the trunk by artist Charlie Whinney is exhibited at the park visitor centre. Shoots are growing from the stump.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The dip of Sycamore Gap remains one of Hadrian's Wall's most recognisable shots - with or without the tree. The walk from Steel Rigg car park (NY751677) takes 20 minutes one way. Combine with the climb onto the Whin Sill ridge for a panorama over Crag Lough and Greenlee Lough. 5 km circuit from Steel Rigg.

⏱ 60 min
$$$9.2The Bowes Hotel
🚗Drive to next stop: 60 km · 1h10

Alnwick

★★★☆☆
1

Owned by the Percys for over 700 years, Alnwick is England's second-largest inhabited castle after Windsor. It served as backdrop for the first two Harry Potter films (Hogwarts, 2001 and 2002), as well as Downton Abbey and Transformers. The outer courtyard is where Harry learns to fly in Philosopher's Stone. The State Rooms are accessible on guided tour.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Try the broomstick lesson on Alnwick's lawn - costumed instructors included. For non-Potter fans, the neighbouring Alnwick Garden (separate, distinct ticket) has one of the world's largest treehouses and a Poison Garden - deadly plants explained by specialist guides.

⏱ 60 min
$$$10Blakelaw Court
🚗Drive to next stop: 20 km · 25min

Bamburgh

★★★★☆
1

Nine acres on basalt, 45 metres above the North Sea - Bamburgh Castle has imposed its silhouette for 1,400 years. First mentioned in 547 AD, seat of the kings of Northumbria, first English castle to fall to cannon (Wars of the Roses, 1464). William Armstrong bought it in 1894 and restored it as his residence; his descendants still live here.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

The beach below the castle is worth as much as the castle itself. From the Battery Terrace, the view embraces the Farne Islands and Lindisfarne. From the beach, the castle stands out against the sky with almost cinematic force - it's one of the most striking photographic sites on England's east coast.

⏱ 45 min
$$$9.7Betony
🚗Drive to next stop: 20 km · 25min

Lindisfarne

★★★★☆
1

The first Viking raid in documented history took place here on 8 June 793 - a shock for all Western Christendom. Lindisfarne monastery had been founded in 635 by Saint Aidan. The Lindisfarne Gospels were created here, around 710-725, a masterpiece of Insular art preserved in the British Library. The visible ruins are those of the 12th-century Benedictine priory.

⏱ 60 min
2

Lindisfarne's Tudor castle was transformed in 1902 by architect Edwin Lutyens for publisher Edward Hudson, in an Arts & Crafts interior style contrasting with the exterior fortress. The garden was designed by Gertrude Jekyll. The view from the ramparts over the bay and Bamburgh Castle is one of Northumberland's finest.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Check the tides: the 1.3 km causeway disappears twice daily for several hours. The crossing is passable roughly 3 hours after high tide until 2 hours before the next. Cars get caught there regularly every year - shame and towing included. Tide tables are posted at the causeway entrance and on Northumberland's official website.

⏱ 60 min
$$$9.5White Swan Inn
🚗Drive to next stop: 65 km · 1h10
