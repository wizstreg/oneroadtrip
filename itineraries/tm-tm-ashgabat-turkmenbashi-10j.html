☀️ Weather

Spring (March-May): Pleasant temperatures (15-25°C) before extreme summer heat with blooming desert landscapes. Best weather for outdoor activities and comfortable sightseeing conditions.

Autumn (October-November): Cooling temperatures after summer heat with clear skies and stable weather. Excellent for desert excursions and cultural exploration.

Winter (December-February): Cool temperatures (0-15°C) but manageable for tourism with clear days. Less challenging than summer heat but some mountain areas inaccessible.

Summer (June-September): Extreme heat (35-50°C) making outdoor activities dangerous during daytime. Indoor attractions only, early morning or late evening activities possible.

Ashgabat

★★★★☆
1

The National Museum of Turkmenistan occupies an eight-pointed star building at the far end of Independence Park. Its collections span 6,000 years of history, from the Jeitun civilization to the ivory rhytons of Nisa. Allow 90 minutes and expect an English-speaking guide—they're mandatory and included in the ticket price.

⏱ 90 min
2

Eighteen kilometers west of the city center, the Parthian ruins of Nisa plunge you into the history of the 3rd century BC. As the first capital of the Parthian Empire, the site has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2007. Two distinct fortresses stand here: Old Nisa (residential) and New Nisa (dynastic).

⏱ 75 min
3

The Independence Monument soars to 118 meters, crowned with a golden crescent. The 140-hectare park surrounding it is lined with fountains, statues of Turkmen heroes, and immaculate lawns. Stroll here at sunset: the 4.5 million square meters of white marble reflect an otherworldly light.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

When evening falls, Ashgabat's avenues light up and empty out. Only white cars cruise the streets—an unofficial rule but one that's strictly observed. Walk along Garashsyzlyk Avenue to grasp the strangeness of this city-as-stage-set: $14 billion spent on a film set with no actors.

⏱ 60 min

Ashgabat

★★★★☆
1

The Turkmenbashi Ruhy Mosque in Gypjak, 4 km from the center, is the country's largest: four 91-meter minarets, a 50-meter main dome, and capacity for 10,000 worshippers. It adjoins the mausoleum of Niyazov, Turkmenistan's first president, whose name is inscribed in golden letters.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Carpet Museum houses nearly 2,000 pieces, including a 301 m² specimen that entered the Guinness Book of Records in 2001—the world's largest hand-knotted carpet. The gül patterns specific to each Turkmen tribe function like a visual alphabet that staff members are delighted to decode for you.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Alem Center houses what was the world's largest enclosed Ferris wheel when it opened in 2012: 57 meters in diameter, built by Italy's Fabbri Group for $90 million. Yet another record in a city that collects them.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

From the cable car station at the foot of the Kopet Dag, the ascent to 1,290 meters offers a striking panorama of Ashgabat's white marble expanse. On clear days, Iranian ridges close the horizon to the south. Allow 90 minutes round-trip.

Kowata

★★☆☆☆
1

One hundred kilometers west of Ashgabat, Bakharden Cave conceals a 70-meter-long thermal lake 55 meters below ground. The water maintains a constant 36°C and contains 38 mineral elements including sulfur, iodine, and magnesium—hence its distinctive smell. The descent requires 250 to 350 lit steps; changing cabins await below. Home to the largest bat colony in the former USSR.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

After your swim, the road continues north through the first ridges of the Karakum. The landscape shifts rapidly: the foothills of the Kopet Dag give way to the vast, ochre flatness of the desert. This is where the true Turkmen adventure begins.

Darvaza

★★★☆☆
1

The Darvaza crater—officially the "Reflection of Karakum"—measures 60 to 70 meters in diameter and 30 meters deep. In 1971, Soviet drilling pierced a methane-filled cavern whose roof collapsed; engineers lit the gas to prevent poisoning nearby villagers. In 2025, authorities reduced the flames by two-thirds by drilling capture wells around the crater—but the nocturnal spectacle remains intact.

⏱ 120 min
2

A few kilometers from the fire crater, two other craters merit a detour: one filled with greenish water where gas bubbles rise to the surface, the other with bubbling gray mud. Together, the three form a planetary landscape with no equivalent anywhere.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The night in a yurt camp at the crater's edge is the circuit's highlight. Dinner by barbecue in the absolute silence of the Karakum, then return to the rim after 9 PM: even reduced, the fire cuts through the desert darkness with an orange glow unlike anything else.

⏱ 180 min

Koneurgench

★★★☆☆
1

The Kutlug Timur Minaret dominates the UNESCO site of Konye-Urgench: 60 meters high, with a base 12 meters in diameter tapering to 2 meters at the summit, featuring 18 decorative bands in fired brick, three bearing Kufic inscriptions. It's the tallest medieval monument in the region after Afghanistan's Minaret of Jam. Construction began in the 11th century, completed between 1320 and 1330 under Kutlug Timur. It leans slightly at the top—likely earthquake damage.

⏱ 40 min
2

The Turabek Khanum Mausoleum, built for Kutlug Timur's wife, displays a dome covered with star-and-flower mosaics—a 14th-century technical masterpiece whose influence reached as far as Mughal architecture in India. The Il Arslan Mausoleum (12th c.) is the oldest standing monument on the site.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Sultan Tekesh Mausoleum (12th c.) is recognizable by its 30-meter blue conical dome, visible like a beacon across the plain. Here, in 1333, Ibn Battuta described Konye-Urgench as "the greatest and most beautiful of Turkic cities."

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Walking through this UNESCO site (listed 2005) in late afternoon, when the raking light gilds the fired brick, completely transforms how you read the monuments. Konye-Urgench was destroyed by Genghis Khan in 1221 then razed by Tamerlane in 1388—what remains is all the more precious.

⏱ 60 min

Mary

★★★☆☆
1

Start with Mary's History Museum to contextualize the collections from Merv and Gonur Depe—background knowledge radically changes how you read the ruins. Then head 30 km east to ancient Merv: 353 hectares of archaeological park listed as UNESCO in 1999, with 4,000 years of continuous history.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Sultan Sanjar Mausoleum (1157) dominates ancient Merv at 27 meters. During his reign, the city had an estimated population of 500,000—the world's most populous city in the 12th century. The walls are 5 meters thick. Its original turquoise dome disappeared during the Mongol sack of 1221; what you see today is a 20th-century restoration. Nearby: Erk Kala and Gyaur Kala fortresses, Shahriyar Ark citadel, Askhab mausoleums (Islamic pilgrimage site).

⏱ 180 min
3

Gonur Depe, 90 km north of Mary, was the capital of the Margiana civilization (2300-1500 BC). Discovered by Soviet archaeologist Viktor Sarianidi in 1972, the site reveals a royal palace, necropolis, and Zoroastrian fire altars—one of Central Asia's oldest civilizations. Accessible only by 4x4 on a track, 2 hours each way.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Explore this Silk Road oasis and 3rd-century BC settlement

Awaza is the anti-Karakum: 5,000 hectares of seaside resort built from 2007 onward on the eastern shore of the Caspian Sea, 12 km from Turkmenbashi. Eighteen hotels, a 7-km artificial canal, a water park opened in 2018, and a fountain complex with 24-meter jets synchronized to music.
