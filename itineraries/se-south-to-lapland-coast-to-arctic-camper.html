  • Gammelstad: UNESCO church town with 424 wooden houses, the world's best preserved
  • Kiruna: underground tour of the world's largest iron ore mine at 540 m depth
  • Abisko: Aurora Sky Station, one of the best northern lights spots thanks to the blue hole microclimate
  • Allemansrätten: the right to camp freely anywhere in Sweden, a campervan paradise
📆 Best period

June, July, August, February, March

⏳ How many days

To make the most of it, plan between 9 and 13 days.

☀️ Weather

Summer (June-August): Perfect weather conditions (18-25°C) with midnight sun in north, white nights, and ideal conditions for outdoor activities. Peak tourist season with highest prices but most comfortable temperatures and endless daylight hours.

Late Spring (May): Excellent weather emergence with blooming landscapes, fewer crowds, and moderate prices. Traditional Midsummer celebrations and optimal conditions for hiking and cycling.

Early Autumn (September-October): Beautiful fall colors with comfortable temperatures, fewer crowds, and moderate prices. Excellent for cultural activities and northern lights begin appearing.

Winter (December-March): Cold temperatures (-5 to -15°C) with snow, northern lights, and winter activities. Lower prices, authentic Nordic winter experience, and excellent conditions for winter sports.

🌍 Discover

Nordic paradise with pristine nature, progressive society, and rich cultural heritage. Experience midnight sun, traditional culture, and incredible natural beauty. The country offers cultural authenticity with natural preservation and progressive values. Sweden provides Nordic experiences with cultural depth and environmental leadership.

📋 Notes

Route designed for campervan or car. The Right of Public Access (Allemansrätten) allows free camping almost anywhere. Plan for fuel reserves in the Far North: petrol stations become scarce beyond the Arctic Circle. In winter, studded or winter tyres mandatory in the north. Distances between stages are significant from Day 8 onward – expect long driving days.

Stop 2

Uppsala

Where a 118-metre cathedral towers over Linnaeus's botanical legacy and sixth-century royal mounds

★★★★☆
📍 Uppsala Cathedral📍 Gamla Uppsala burial mounds📍 Linnaeus Garden Uppsala📍 Gustavianum museum Uppsala📍 Sigtuna oldest town Sweden
1

En route to Uppsala, stop at Sigtuna, Sweden's oldest town (founded around 980). Its lanes by Lake Mälaren, medieval church ruins, and runestones make for a good hour's stop. St Mary's Church, in Gothic brick, is the best preserved.

⏱ 60 min
2

Uppsala Cathedral, completed in 1435, is the largest church in Scandinavia – 118-metre neo-Gothic spires. Inside lie the tombs of Gustav Vasa and Carl Linnaeus. Facing the cathedral, the Gustavianum houses a 17th-century anatomical theatre and Anders Celsius's original thermometer.

⏱ 90 min
3

Linnaeus Garden (Linnéträdgården) is the botanical garden Carl Linnaeus directed from 1743 to 1778 – here he developed his method for classifying living organisms. Linnaeus's adjoining house preserves his insect collections and notes. Allow an hour for both.

⏱ 60 min
4

Gamla Uppsala, 5 km north, is the cradle of the Swedish nation. Three great royal burial mounds from the 6th century rise on a plain – climb the tallest for the view. A small 12th-century church was built on the foundations of an ancient pagan temple described in medieval chronicles.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

End the day by the Fyrisån river in central Uppsala – waterside cafés with terraces. If you're there on 30 April (Valborg), tens of thousands of students run down a hill throwing their white caps – a tradition centuries old.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 3

Gavle

Home to the world's most arson-prone Christmas decoration, a 13-metre straw goat with its own Twitter account

★★☆☆☆
📍 Gävlebocken Slottstorget Gävle📍 Gamla Gefle wooden quarter Gävle📍 Järnvägsmuseet railway museum Gävle📍 Boulognerskogen park Gävle
1

Gävle is home to the Gävlebocken, the world's most famous Christmas straw goat. Every first Sunday of Advent since 1966, a 13-metre giant goat weighing 3.6 tonnes of straw is erected on Slottstorget. The problem: it's regularly torched by vandals before Christmas. In 2001, an American tourist burned it down believing it was a legal tradition – he spent 18 days in jail. The goat even has its own Twitter account. If you pass through in December, you'll see it (maybe).

⏱ 30 min
2

Gamla Gefle is the old fishing quarter north of the Gavleån river – colourful 18th-century wooden houses, narrow lanes, a village atmosphere within the city. It's the only district that survived the 1869 fire that destroyed much of Gävle.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Swedish Railway Museum (Sveriges Järnvägsmuseum) is the country's largest railway museum – 19th-century steam locomotives, royal carriages, and a reconstructed 1900 station. Even if you're not a train enthusiast, the scenography is worth the visit. Allow 90 minutes.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Boulognerskogen park, in the city centre, is one of northern Sweden's finest urban parks – tall trees, a river, and open-air concerts in summer. If you have the campervan, several parking areas are accessible on the city outskirts.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 4

Sundsvall

The Stone City risen from ashes, where 1888's flames forged Sweden's finest neo-Renaissance streetscape

★★☆☆☆
📍 Stenstaden Sundsvall stone city📍 Stora Torget Sundsvall📍 Norra Berget open-air museum Sundsvall📍 Kulturmagasinet Sundsvall
1

Sundsvall is nicknamed Stenstaden – 'the Stone City'. On 25 June 1888, a spark from a steamboat on the Selångersån river triggered the largest urban fire in Swedish history: the entire city burned in nine hours, leaving 9,000 people homeless. The decision was made to rebuild everything in stone rather than wood. Result: 51 brick and stone buildings in neo-Renaissance style now line wide streets around Stora Torget. In 2017, Sundsvall was voted Sweden's most beautiful city.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Kulturmagasinet, four former harbour warehouses converted for culture, houses the municipal library, Sundsvall Museum (city history and the fire) and a photography museum. It's the cultural heart of the city – free entry for most exhibitions.

⏱ 60 min
3

Climb Norra Berget, the northern hill (141 m), for the panorama over the city, sea inlets and bridges. An open-air museum at the summit presents the region's history and culture. The trail from the centre is steep but short – 20 minutes' climb.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

In the evening, stroll through Stenstaden at sunset – the brick facades take on a golden hue. The fountain in Vängåvan park, created in 1886 (two years before the fire and miraculously intact), depicts lions, dolphins and bears – animals most Sundsvall residents of the time had never seen.

⏱ 45 min

Stop 5

Ornskoldsvik

Gateway to the UNESCO High Coast, where the earth still rises eight millimetres a year from glacial memory

★★★☆☆
📍 Skuleskogen national park entrance south Docksta📍 Slåttdalsskrevan gorge Skuleskogen📍 Naturum Höga Kusten📍 High Coast bridge Höga Kusten
1

The High Coast (Höga Kusten), listed as UNESCO World Heritage in 2000, is the site of the world's highest post-glacial rebound: 20,000 years ago, 3 km of ice weighed on this land, pushing it down 800 metres. Since the ice melted, the land has been rising 8 mm per year – new islands regularly emerge. The High Coast suspension bridge (1,867 m) on the E4 is your gateway.

⏱ 30 min
2

Skuleskogen National Park, created in 1984, is where this geological phenomenon is most visible. Slåttdalsskrevan is its centrepiece: a 200-metre-long, 40-metre-deep fissure in red granite. The interior has something cathedral-like – walls rising on each side and light filtering from above. From the southern entrance (Docksta), allow 90 minutes' walk return to the gorge. If you climb to the summit of Slåttdalsberget (280 m), the view over the archipelago is one of Sweden's finest.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

Before or after the hike, stop at Naturum Höga Kusten at the foot of Skuleberget: the exhibition clearly explains isostatic rebound. Bring good shoes – the terrain is rocky and some passages slippery.

⏱ 45 min

Umea

The City of Birches where thousands of trees were planted as firebreaks after history's cruelest coincidence

★★★★☆
📍 Umeå city centre birches📍 Västerbottens museum Umeå📍 Guitars The Museum Umeå📍 Norrbyskär island Umeå
1

Umeå is nicknamed 'the City of Birches' – after a devastating fire in 1888 (the same day as Sundsvall's, 25 June), thousands of birches were planted as firebreaks. They still line the main avenues and give the city a unique appearance in autumn when the leaves turn yellow. With 90,000 inhabitants and a large university, it's northern Sweden's biggest city and served as European Capital of Culture in 2014.

⏱ 60 min
2

Västerbottens Museum traces the region's history with emphasis on Sami culture and the forest industry. Outside, an open-air museum with old farms and buildings. Guitars – The Museum, opened in 2014, displays over 500 vintage guitars including pieces that belonged to famous musicians. Allow an hour per museum.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Umeå has a surprisingly vibrant café culture for its latitude – students keep the city buzzing. Fika (Swedish coffee break with pastry) is almost a sport here. Walk along the Umeälven river in the evening: the quays are lively in summer. If you have an extra half-day, the island of Norrbyskär (30 minutes by boat) shows an old 19th-century sawmill village in an archipelago setting.

⏱ 90 min

Stop 7

Skelleftea

Where timber reaches for the Arctic sky in a twenty-storey cathedral to sustainable architecture

★★☆☆☆
📍 Sara Kulturhus Skellefteå📍 Bonnstan church town Skellefteå📍 Nordanå kulturcentrum Skellefteå📍 Skellefte river Skellefteå
1

Sara Kulturhus in Skellefteå is one of the world's tallest wooden buildings: 20 storeys, 75 metres, inaugurated in September 2021. Designed by White Arkitekter, the building is constructed almost entirely from regional timber (forests within 200 km, sawmill 60 km away). It houses a theatre, museum, art gallery, library and a 205-room hotel. The glass facade reveals the raw timber structure inside – go to the top floor for views over the forests and Baltic Sea.

⏱ 90 min
2

Bonnstan is Skellefteå's medieval church town: 116 small red wooden cabins grouped around the parish church. Like Gammelstad (which you'll see further north), these houses served farmers from distant parishes who came to sleep at weekends to attend mass. Bonnstan is smaller than Gammelstad but far less touristy – you'll likely be alone in the lanes.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Skellefte river, flowing through town, offers a pleasant evening walk. Nordanå cultural centre, in a former wooden estate by the water, hosts temporary exhibitions and a café overlooking the river. This is where Skellefteåbor gather in summer.

⏱ 60 min

Lulea

Keeper of Gammelstad's 424 wooden secrets, a fifteenth-century church town frozen in devotional amber

★★★★☆
📍 Gammelstad kyrkstad Luleå UNESCO📍 Nederluleå church Gammelstad📍 Luleå archipelago📍 Hägnan open-air museum Gammelstad
1

Gammelstad kyrkstad is the world's best-preserved church town, inscribed on UNESCO World Heritage in 1996. At the centre, Nederluleå stone church dates from the 15th century. All around, 424 small red wooden houses (Falun red paint) were built by farmers from surrounding parishes to sleep in at weekends – distances and climate made it impossible to return home after mass. In the 17th century, isostatic rebound made the harbour too shallow for ships – the trading town moved to the coast and took the name Luleå, leaving the religious complex intact.

⏱ 90 min
2

The visitor centre's guided tour lasts about an hour and opens doors closed to the public. Outside tours, freely wander the lanes between the red houses: the place is still inhabited during religious festivals like Christmas and Easter.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Luleå archipelago counts over 1,300 islands – in summer, boats serve the main ones. Gammelstadsviken nature reserve, right next to the church town, is one of northern Sweden's best spots for observing migratory birds.

⏱ 120 min

Stop 9

Jokkmokk

Where four centuries of unbroken winter markets have gathered the Sami beyond the Arctic Circle since 1605

★★★☆☆
📍 Arctic Circle marker E45 Jokkmokk📍 Ájtte Sami museum Jokkmokk📍 Jokkmokk church red📍 Jokkmokk winter market site
1

Just before Jokkmokk, you cross the Arctic Circle on the E45 – a marker for photos by the roadside. North of this line, the sun doesn't rise in winter and doesn't set in summer.

⏱ 20 min
2

Ájtte Museum is Sweden's national museum of Sami culture. Its name means 'storage building' in Luleå Sami. You'll see exhibitions on Sami nomadic life, reindeer herding, joik (Sami singing), ceremonial drums and costumes. It's Sweden's most comprehensive Sami museum. Allow at least 90 minutes.

⏱ 90 min
3

Jokkmokk's winter market has been held the first weekend of February since 1605 – over 400 years without interruption. King Karl IX decreed the creation of Lapland markets to collect taxes and spread Christianity. Today, 30,000 to 40,000 visitors descend on this town of 3,000 for three days of Sami handicrafts and reindeer races on the frozen lake. If you're not there in February, the small red Sami church with its separate bell tower recalls this history.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Jokkmokk is the gateway to Laponia, a 9,400 km² UNESCO site comprising Muddus, Padjelanta, Sarek and Stora Sjöfallet national parks. Local operators offer guided outings in the boreal forest around the village.

⏱ 60 min

Gallivare

Mining town carved from tundra, where Europe's deepest copper wound bleeds beside ancient boreal forest

★★★★☆
📍 Dundret mountain Gällivare midnight sun📍 Aitik copper mine Gällivare📍 Malmberget mining village Gällivare📍 Muddus national park Gällivare
1

Gällivare is a mining town of 10,000 inhabitants planted in the tundra. Aitik copper mine, 15 km south, is Europe's largest open-pit mine – a 3 km long, 450 m deep crater. Guided tours are possible in summer. At Malmberget, the neighbouring mining village, the same phenomenon as in Kiruna is occurring: the ground is subsiding and part of the village is being relocated.

⏱ 90 min
2

Dundret (820 m), the mountain overlooking Gällivare, offers a 360° panorama over forests, lakes and tundra. It's one of Lapland's best places to observe the midnight sun (late May to mid-July): a paved road climbs almost to the summit. In winter, Dundret is also an aurora viewing spot.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Muddus National Park, 50 km southwest, is one of Europe's last primeval forests – 700-year-old pines, peat bogs, Muddusfallet waterfall (42 m drop). If you have half a day, the walk to the waterfall (7 km one-way from the car park) is worthwhile.

⏱ 240 min

Stop 11

Kiruna

Where the world's greatest iron seam forces an entire city to walk eastward, leaving its wooden church behind

★★☆☆☆
📍 Kiruna Kyrka church wood 1912📍 LKAB mine visitor center Kiruna📍 Kiruna city relocation new town📍 Icehotel Jukkasjärvi
1

Kiruna's LKAB mine is the world's largest underground iron ore mine. Opened in 1899, it extracts ore at over 1,365 metres depth along a 4 km seam. Guided tours descend by bus to level 540 – 400 km of tunnels in total. Sweden is the European Union's top iron producer thanks to this deposit. Book ahead: places sell out quickly in high season.

⏱ 150 min
2

Kiruna's wooden church (Kiruna Kyrka), built between 1909 and 1912 by Gustaf Wickman, draws inspiration from Sami tents and Norwegian stave churches. Weighing 672 tonnes, it was voted Sweden's most beautiful building constructed before 1950. In 2025, it was moved 5 km to escape the mining subsidence zone – the entire town is being relocated eastward.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Icehotel in Jukkasjärvi (17 km east), rebuilt each winter since 1989 from Torne river ice, is the world's first ice hotel. The permanent section stays open year-round through refrigeration. An hour's visit suffices to appreciate the sculptures and ephemeral architecture.

⏱ 90 min

Abisko

Blessed by the blue hole microclimate, where the Arctic sky performs its greatest light shows above the King's Trail

★★★☆☆
📍 Abisko national park canyon📍 Lake Torneträsk Abisko📍 Naturum Abisko📍 Lapporten Abisko
1

The Kiruna→Abisko road (95 km) runs along Lake Torneträsk, Scandinavia's largest mountain lake – 330 km² of glacial water framed by peaks. In clear weather, you'll glimpse Lapporten (Čuonjávággi in Sami), the U-shaped glacial valley that's one of Lapland's symbols.

⏱ 30 min
2

Abisko National Park, founded in 1909, covers 77 km². Start at the Naturum, the park information centre. In the afternoon, follow the Abiskojåkka canyon trail – allow an hour return for the short version. The river has carved a deep gorge in the rock. Climb towards the mountains from Abisko Turiststation for the most impressive viewpoints.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

If you're here September to March, the Aurora Sky Station on Mount Nuolja is the evening rendezvous. Chairlift to 900 m, panoramic terrace and heated café. Abisko benefits from a microclimate called the 'blue hole': the mountains create a clear-sky zone even when it's raining in Kiruna. In summer, the same chairlift goes up to observe the midnight sun. Book ahead.

⏱ 180 min
