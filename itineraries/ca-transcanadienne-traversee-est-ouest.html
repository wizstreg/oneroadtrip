Cross-Canada Journey: From Québec City to the Rockies

Quebec

★★★★★
1

Place Royale, birthplace of French America: this is where Champlain built his fortified habitation in 1608 with 28 men—20 of whom would die of scurvy during that first brutal winter. In 1686, Intendant Champigny installed a bust of Louis XIV here, giving the square its name. The outline of Champlain's second habitation (demolished in 1624, rebuilt in stone between 1626 and 1627) is traced in the pavement near the church.

⏱ 60 min
2

Step inside the Notre-Dame-des-Victoires church, built in 1688 directly on the foundations of Champlain's original habitation. It's the oldest stone church in Canada with its original walls still standing. Severely damaged during the 1759 siege, it was hastily rebuilt, then in 1816 architect François Baillairgé undertook major renovations that gave the church its current appearance.

⏱ 30 min
3

Wander down Rue du Petit-Champlain, one of the oldest commercial streets in North America. The Breakneck Stairs (Escalier Casse-Cou), built as early as the 17th century, connect the Lower Town to the summit of Cap Diamant. The funicular, built beside the former residence of the Mississippi's European discoverer, offers sweeping views over the St. Lawrence River.

⏱ 45 min
4

The Château Frontenac has dominated Cap Diamant since 1893, when the Canadian Pacific Railway inaugurated this grand château-style hotel designed by architect Bruce Price. Inspired by 14th and 15th-century Loire Valley castles, it originally had 170 rooms—now over 600. In 1943, Churchill and Roosevelt met here for the Quebec Conference. The Dufferin Terrace, right in front, offers panoramic views of the river and Île d'Orléans.

⏱ 60 min
5

Walk the 4.6 km of fortifications that encircle Old Québec. Québec is the only walled city north of Mexico—a distinction recognized by UNESCO, which inscribed the historic district as a World Heritage Site in 1985. The Saint-Jean, Saint-Louis, and Kent gates served as strategic entry points. In the 1870s, Governor Lord Dufferin saved these walls from demolition.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Evening stroll through the Petit-Champlain quarter: artisan boutiques, galleries, and restaurants housed in 18th-century buildings.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 250 km · 2h50

Montreal

★★★★☆
1

Notre-Dame Basilica on Place d'Armes: constructed between 1824 and 1829 to designs by New York architect James O'Donnell, this was Canada's first major Gothic Revival church. It could hold up to 10,000 worshippers. The blue vaulted ceiling studded with golden stars and the massive carved altarpiece by Henri Bouriché date from the 1872-1880 renovations by Victor Bourgeau. The Jean-Baptiste bourdon bell in the west tower weighs 10,900 kg.

⏱ 60 min
2

Walk through Old Montréal to Pointe-à-Callière, the exact spot where Montréal was founded in 1642. The archaeology museum, built over the remains of the first Fort Ville-Marie, displays 17th-century foundations. Across the way, Place Royale de Montréal—named in 1892 for the city's 250th anniversary—served as a marketplace as early as 1676.

⏱ 75 min
3

Stroll along the Old Port to Bonsecours Market, built in 1847 in neoclassical style with its silver dome. For a time it served as the Parliament of the United Province of Canada. The adjacent Notre-Dame-de-Bon-Secours Chapel dates from 1771 and contains sailors' ex-votos hanging from the ceiling.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Dinner in Old Montréal: the cobblestone lanes between Rue Saint-Paul and Rue de la Commune are packed with restaurants set in former 19th-century grey stone warehouses.

⏱ 60 min

Montreal

★★★★☆
1

Climb to the summit of Mount Royal (233 m), a 200-hectare park designed in 1876 by Frederick Law Olmsted, the same landscape architect who created New York's Central Park. The Kondiaronk Belvedere—named after the Huron-Wendat chief who signed the Great Peace of Montréal in 1701—offers unobstructed views of downtown, the river, and the Montérégie hills.

⏱ 90 min
2

Descend toward the Plateau Mont-Royal, a neighborhood recognizable by its wrought-iron outdoor staircases and colorful facades. These stairs, typical of 19th-century Montréal architecture, saved precious interior space in the triplex buildings. Avenue Mont-Royal is lined with local shops and cafés.

⏱ 60 min
3

Continue to Mile End, a former working-class neighborhood turned creative heart of Montréal. This is where you'll find the two rival bagel bakeries, wood-firing according to traditional methods inherited from Eastern European Jewish immigrants who arrived in the early 20th century. Montréal bagels—smaller, denser, and boiled in honey water—bear no resemblance to their New York cousins.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Wood-fired bagel tasting in Mile End, followed by a walk through streets lined with street art and murals.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 195 km · 2h10

Ottawa

★★★☆☆
1

Parliament Hill overlooks the Ottawa River from a limestone outcrop. The Gothic Revival buildings, constructed between 1859 and 1866, were ravaged by fire in February 1916—only the Library, saved by an employee who closed its iron doors, survived. The Peace Tower, completed in 1927, rises 92 meters. Its carillon of 53 bells, commissioned to commemorate the 1918 armistice, still rings daily over the capital.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Rideau Canal stretches 202 km from Ottawa to Lake Ontario. Built between 1826 and 1832 under the direction of Colonel John By, it mobilized up to 5,000 workers annually—mainly Irish immigrants and French Canadians—hundreds of whom died from malaria or accidents. Designed as a military route after the War of 1812, it became a commercial waterway and later a natural skating rink in winter. A UNESCO site since 2007, it's the only World Heritage Site in Ontario.

⏱ 60 min
3

ByWard Market, operating since 1826, bears Colonel By's name. It's one of the oldest and largest public markets in Canada. Stalls of local products—cheeses, maple syrup, pastries—sit alongside art galleries and restaurants in 19th-century stone and brick buildings.

⏱ 60 min
4

Cross the bridge over the canal to the National Gallery of Canada, recognizable by its granite and glass towers. It houses the world's largest collection of Inuit art—stone, bone, and ivory sculptures from communities across the Far North. The building, designed by architect Moshe Safdie and opened in 1988, plays with natural light through glass canopies that frame views of Parliament Hill.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Bike ride or walk along the Rideau Canal in late afternoon: the path follows the Ottawa Locks, the eight-step flight of locks connecting the canal to the Ottawa River.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 365 km · 4h20

North Bay

★★★☆☆
1

North Bay, "Gateway to the North," developed with the arrival of the Canadian Pacific Railway in 1882. The restored historic station now houses a museum tracing the region's railway history. The city also owes its fame to the Dionne quintuplets, born here in 1934—an event that captured worldwide attention.

⏱ 45 min
2

Lake Nipissing, Ontario's third-largest lake entirely within the province (875 km², 65 km long), was a major trade route for the Anishinaabe peoples. Its Ojibwe name paradoxically means "little body of water"—compared to the nearby Great Lakes. In 1610, explorer Étienne Brûlé became the first European to navigate these waters, along the fur trade route linking the Ottawa River to Lake Huron via the French River.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Sunset walk along North Bay's waterfront: the white sand beaches of Shabogesic Beach rank among Ontario's finest, facing the lake's shallow waters (average depth only 4.5 m).

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 480 km · 5h40

Sault Ste Marie

★★☆☆☆
1

Sault Ste. Marie Canal National Historic Site: completed in 1895, this canal was the world's longest lock and the first to operate on electricity. It's the final link in the all-Canadian navigation route connecting the Atlantic to Lake Superior. The rapids of the St. Marys River—called Baawaating ("place of the rapids") in Anishinaabemowin—served as a fishing and trading center for Indigenous peoples for millennia. The historic red sandstone buildings were cut from stone excavated during canal construction.

⏱ 75 min
2

Stroll the boardwalk along the St. Marys River: from this promenade, you can watch freighters transit between Lake Huron and Lake Superior via the American locks (four US locks, still in commercial operation). The 6-meter elevation difference between the two lakes creates the rapids that give the city its name—"sault" meaning "rapid" in old French.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Scenic drive from North Bay via Sudbury and the Trans-Canada Highway (Route 17), a full day's drive through the boreal forest of the Canadian Shield.

⏱ 300 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 230 km · 2h50

Wawa

★★★☆☆
1

The Agawa Rock pictographs in Lake Superior Provincial Park (1,550 km², established 1944): on a white crystalline granite cliff rising 15 stories high, 35 red ochre paintings created by Ojibwe spiritual leaders between the 17th and 18th centuries. The most famous depicts Mishipeshu, the Great Lynx of the water, guardian of copper. The access trail (800 m) descends between rock crevices to a ledge on the lake's edge—accessible only in calm weather.

⏱ 90 min
2

Stop at Old Woman Bay, a cove framed by sheer cliffs plunging directly into Lake Superior. This region of Algoma is where the Group of Seven—those 1920s Canadian painters—created some of their most celebrated canvases. The landscape of twisted pines, pink rock, and deep blue water resembles a living painting.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Hike the Nokomis Trail or the coastal trail in Lake Superior Provincial Park, through boreal forest on the Canadian Shield.

⏱ 120 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 480 km · 5h40

Thunder Bay

★★★☆☆
1

From Hillcrest Park or the Terry Fox Memorial, observe the Sleeping Giant—a formation of diabase mesas on the Sibley Peninsula that traces the silhouette of a giant lying on his back. According to Ojibwe legend, it's Nanabijou, spirit of the deep waters, turned to stone for revealing the location of a silver mine to Europeans. The cliffs rank among Ontario's highest (250 m).

⏱ 60 min
2

The Terry Fox Memorial on the Trans-Canada Highway east of Thunder Bay marks where the young runner had to stop his Marathon of Hope on September 1, 1980, after 143 days and 5,373 km running on an artificial leg. The bronze statue, facing west—the direction he never reached—overlooks Lake Superior.

⏱ 30 min
3

Fort William Historical Park recreates the continent's largest inland fur trading post as it was in 1816. Forty-two buildings bring to life the North West Company depot, where voyageurs, traders, and First Nations gathered each summer to exchange furs, provisions, and news.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Hike to the top of the Sleeping Giant (Top of the Giant Trail): 22 km round trip, panoramic views of Lake Superior from the cliffs of the Sibley Peninsula.

⏱ 300 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 490 km · 5h40

Kenora

★★☆☆☆
1

Kenora, Ontario's last city before Manitoba, sits on the shores of Lake of the Woods—a sprawling lake covering 4,350 km² dotted with over 14,500 islands. A long day's drive through boreal forest, with stops at lookouts along the Trans-Canada Highway. The region was a crossroads of fur trade routes between the Great Lakes and the Prairies.

⏱ 45 min
2

Wander the lakefront in downtown Kenora and spot Husky the Muskie, a 12-meter-tall giant muskellunge statue—a nod to the sport fishing that made Lake of the Woods famous.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Sunset on Lake of the Woods: the calm waters mirror the pines and rocks of the Canadian Shield, a landscape typical of northwestern Ontario.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 210 km · 2h20

Winnipeg

★★★☆☆
1

The Forks, at the confluence of the Red and Assiniboine rivers: a gathering place, trading center, and site of peace treaties for Indigenous peoples for over 6,000 years. Archaeological excavations have uncovered nearly 400,000 artifacts, some dating back to 1100 CE. Today, the indoor market and riverside docks animate this national historic site.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Canadian Museum for Human Rights, adjacent to The Forks: opened in 2014, designed by architect Antoine Predock, it's the first national museum built outside the federal capital and the world's first dedicated to human rights. Its 11 galleries are connected by alabaster ramps. The cornerstone comes from Runnymede, England, where the Magna Carta was sealed in 1215.

⏱ 120 min
3

The Exchange District, north of Portage Avenue: some twenty blocks of brick and stone buildings constructed between 1880 and 1920, forming one of North America's best-preserved assemblages of early 20th-century commercial architecture. These streets witnessed the Winnipeg General Strike of 1919—six weeks of total paralysis, 30,000 strikers, and a Mounted Police charge on June 21 ("Bloody Saturday") that left two dead and marked the history of the Canadian labor movement.

⏱ 60 min
4

Cross the Provencher Bridge to Saint-Boniface, Western Canada's oldest francophone neighborhood. Saint-Boniface Cathedral, whose 1908 stone facade survived the 1968 fire, stands in ruins framing the new church. This is where Louis Riel, Métis leader and father of Manitoba, is buried in the parish cemetery.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Evening at The Forks Market: stalls of Indigenous, Métis, and multicultural cuisine in one of the Prairies' liveliest markets.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 275 km · 3h10

Wasagaming

★★★☆☆
1

Riding Mountain National Park (2,969 km², officially established 1933) stands atop the Manitoba Escarpment, 400 meters above the surrounding prairies. It's Canada's only national park protecting three ecosystems converging in one place: grasslands, boreal forest, and eastern deciduous forest. The East Gate, a national historic site, is a monumental log-and-stone entrance built in the 1930s by 1,200 workers as part of Great Depression relief programs.

⏱ 45 min
2

Walk along Clear Lake from the village of Wasagaming: this crystalline lake, 34 meters deep, is the recreational heart of the park. The village retains the charm of a 1930s resort town, with its log buildings and period theatre.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Lake Audy bison enclosure shelters a herd of about forty plains bison in 500 hectares of native prairie. These bison recall the immense herds that once covered the Canadian Prairies before their near-extinction in the 19th century.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hike the Grey Owl Trail (8.9 km one way) to Grey Owl's cabin, built in 1931 beside Beaver Lodge Lake. Archibald Belaney, a Briton who reinvented himself under the Indigenous identity of Grey Owl, was one of Canada's first conservationists. Hired by Parks Canada to reintroduce beavers, he lived six months in this L-shaped log cabin with his two beavers, Jelly Roll and Rawhide.

⏱ 240 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 490 km · 5h30

Regina

★★★☆☆
1

Stop in Moose Jaw, halfway between Winnipeg and Regina: beneath downtown streets, a network of underground tunnels served as refuge for Chinese immigrants persecuted by early 20th-century exclusion laws. These passages, discovered in the 1980s, are now recreated as museum tours.

⏱ 60 min
2

Wascana Centre in the heart of Regina spreads across 930 hectares around an artificial lake—one of Canada's largest urban parks. The Saskatchewan Legislative Building, constructed between 1908 and 1912 in Beaux-Arts style, reflects in the calm waters of Wascana Lake. On its lawns in 1935, the tragic end of the On-to-Ottawa Trek unfolded when thousands of unemployed men heading to the federal capital were stopped by police in a bloody riot.

⏱ 60 min
3

The RCMP Heritage Centre traces the history of the Royal Canadian Mounted Police since its creation in 1873. Regina is the RCMP's only training center in Canada: all cadets pass through here. The museum displays uniforms, equipment, and accounts of the first mounted patrols across the Western Prairies.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Long driving day across the Manitoba and Saskatchewan prairies (490 km): straight roads to the horizon, canola and wheat fields, grain elevators punctuating the skyline.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 680 km · 7h10

Drumheller

★★☆☆☆
1

The Royal Tyrrell Museum, opened in 1985 in Midland Provincial Park, is Canada's only museum dedicated entirely to paleontology. Named for Joseph Tyrrell, a geologist who discovered the first dinosaur skull in the Red Deer River valley in 1884 while searching for coal. The collection exceeds 160,000 fossil specimens, with over 800 on permanent display. The Borealopelta, an armored nodosaur discovered in the oil sands in 2011, is one of the world's best-preserved fossils—skin and stomach contents included.

⏱ 180 min
2

Horseshoe Canyon, 17 km west of Drumheller: a spectacular breach carved into the flat prairie, revealing layers of sandstone, mudstone, and coal 70 million years old. The horizontal strata, alternating grey, beige, and ochre, tell the passage from a subtropical climate to the ice age. A short but steep trail descends to the canyon floor.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Drumheller Hoodoos: these sandstone columns capped with harder blocks result from millions of years of erosion by wind and water. Each pillar stands balanced thanks to its "cap" of hard rock protecting the soft sandstone below. Further along, the Rosedale Suspension Bridge, a wire pedestrian walkway built for coal miners, sways above the valley.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Scenic drive on the Dinosaur Trail (48 km loop around Drumheller): cross the Red Deer River on the Bleriot Ferry, one of Alberta's last cable ferries, in service since 1913.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 135 km · 1h40

Banff

★★★★★
1

Banff's story begins in 1883, when three Canadian Pacific Railway workers accidentally discovered sulfurous hot springs on the flank of Sulphur Mountain. In 1885, the federal government created a 26 km² reserve around these springs—the seed of what would become Canada's first national park and the third in the world. The alpine village of Banff then stretched along Banff Avenue, backed by Cascade Mountain (2,998 m).

⏱ 60 min
2

Take the Banff Gondola to the summit of Sulphur Mountain at 2,281 m: the panorama encompasses the Bow Valley, Lake Minnewanka, and dozens of Rocky Mountain peaks. At the summit, a boardwalk leads to a viewpoint and the Sanson Peak weather station, manned by a solitary observer for 30 years in the early 20th century.

⏱ 90 min
3

Descend to Bow Falls, where the Bow River cascades over limestone benches. The Fairmont Banff Springs, visible from the falls, was built in 1888 in the same château style as Québec's Frontenac—both commissioned by the Canadian Pacific to attract wealthy tourists to its trains.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Soak in the Banff Upper Hot Springs: outdoor thermal spring at 47°C, at 1,585 m elevation, with views of Mount Rundle. Indigenous peoples frequented these springs long before European arrival.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 60 km · 50min

Banff

★★★★★
1

Lake Louise, at 1,731 m elevation, owes its turquoise color to glacial flour—rock particles finely ground by glaciers that refract light. The lake was named in 1884 in honor of Princess Louise Caroline Alberta, fourth daughter of Queen Victoria (the province also bears her name). The Plain of Six Glaciers trail (13.8 km round trip) leads to a teahouse built in 1924 by Swiss guides of the Canadian Pacific.

⏱ 120 min
2

Moraine Lake in the Valley of the Ten Peaks is accessible from June to October only. This landscape once graced the back of old Canadian twenty-dollar bills. The ten peaks surrounding it rise between 3,000 and 3,500 m.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hike Johnston Canyon (5.4 km round trip): a developed trail with catwalks and metal bridges along a limestone gorge leads to the lower falls (30 m) then the upper falls. The canyon walls, sculpted by erosion, reveal limestone layers 350 million years old.

⏱ 150 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 230 km · 3h20

Jasper

★★★★☆
1

The Icefields Parkway (230 km between Lake Louise and Jasper) cuts through the heart of the Canadian Rockies. Stop at Peyto Lake, whose shape evokes a wolf's head as seen from Bow Summit viewpoint at 2,069 m—the highway's highest point. The lake's brilliant blue color comes from glacial flour fed by Peyto Glacier.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Columbia Icefield covers 325 km² and feeds glaciers that drain toward three different oceans—Atlantic, Pacific, and Arctic. The Athabasca Glacier, the most accessible, extends 6 km from the icefield. Its thickness reaches 365 m in places, but it recedes about 5 meters per year.

⏱ 60 min
3

Athabasca Falls in Jasper National Park: the Athabasca River plunges 23 m into a narrow gorge carved in quartzite, a rock so hard it forces the water to compress into a passage less than 18 meters wide. The power of the flow is particularly impressive in early summer during snowmelt.

⏱ 45 min
4

Maligne Canyon, 11 km from Jasper townsite: the deepest gorge in the Canadian Rockies (50 m), carved by the Maligne River through limestone. Six bridges allow you to peer into the canyon depths. In winter, the gorge freezes into a landscape of ice cascades.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hike the Valley of the Five Lakes (4.6 km, easy) in Jasper National Park: five small lakes in shades ranging from turquoise to jade, fed by underground springs.

⏱ 90 min
