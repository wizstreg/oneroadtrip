Frankfurt

★★★★☆
1

The Römerberg has been Frankfurt's historic heart since the High Middle Ages. The square is dominated by the Römer, a three-gabled town hall that has served as the seat of municipal administration since 1405. Inside, the Kaisersaal (Emperor's Hall) preserves portraits of the 52 emperors of the Holy Roman Empire, from Charlemagne to Francis II—this is where coronation banquets were held after ceremonies in the neighboring cathedral. Following the Golden Bull of 1356, the Römer became the site of imperial elections until 1792. The square was destroyed in the 1944 bombings and faithfully reconstructed in the 1950s.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Kaiserdom (St. Bartholomew's Cathedral), whose red Gothic tower rises 95 meters, was consecrated in 1239 and designated the coronation site of Holy Roman Emperors in 1356—ten coronation ceremonies took place here between 1562 and 1792. Climb the 324 steps to the tower for panoramic views over Frankfurt's rooftops. The cathedral treasury houses a Gothic vestment dating from 1350.

⏱ 55 min
3

Cross the Eiserner Steg, an iron footbridge built in 1869 over the Main River, to reach Sachsenhausen. From the bridge, the view aligns the half-timbered houses of Römerberg, Gothic spires, and the skyscrapers of the financial district—nicknamed 'Mainhattan'—in the background. The Alt-Sachsenhausen quarter on the south bank preserves its cobbled lanes and cider taverns where local Apfelwein is served in stoneware pitchers and ribbed glasses.

⏱ 45 min
4

The Städel Museum, founded in 1815 by banker Johann Friedrich Städel as Germany's first civic museum foundation, spans 700 years of European art: 3,100 paintings, 660 sculptures, and over 100,000 drawings. In 1937, the Nazis confiscated 77 works deemed 'degenerate'; the remaining pieces were evacuated to Bavaria. The collection ranges from Dürer, Botticelli, and Cranach to Monet and Picasso. The underground extension inaugurated in 2012, lit by circular skylights at ground level, houses the contemporary art collection.

⏱ 100 min

Activities

Apple wine tasting at a traditional Sachsenhausen tavern. The cider tavern tradition dates back to the 18th century in this quarter; apple cider is served in blue stoneware pitchers in glasses called Geripptes.

⏱ 60 min

Wurzburg

★★★★☆
1

The Würzburg Residence, built between 1720 and 1744 for the Prince-Bishops of the House of Schönborn, ranks among Europe's most ambitious Baroque palaces. Architect Balthasar Neumann designed the plans; Venetian painter Tiepolo created the world's largest ceiling fresco in the staircase hall between 1750 and 1753, depicting the four continents then known. Listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1981, the Residence survived almost intact when 90% of Würzburg was destroyed in a single night of bombing in March 1945.

⏱ 90 min
2

Festung Marienberg dominates the city from the west, accessible via a path through the vineyards (25 minutes from the bridge). A round chapel was built here as early as 704, one of Bavaria's oldest Christian structures. The Prince-Bishops resided here from 1253 to 1720. The fortress was never taken except once—by Swedish troops in 1631 during the Thirty Years' War. It houses the Mainfränkisches Museum, whose collection of wood sculptures by Tilman Riemenschneider, a local Gothic master, is outstanding.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Alte Mainbrücke has connected the old town to the fortress since the 15th century—its stone construction began in 1476. The twelve Baroque statues of saints adorning its balustrades were added between 1720 and 1730. In late afternoon, locals gather here standing with glasses of Silvaner in hand, facing the illuminated fortress. A wine merchant is stationed at the bridge entrance.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Walk through the vineyards surrounding Marienberg Fortress, on the slopes of Würzburger Stein—Europe's largest steep-slope vineyard property. The wine produced here, mainly Silvaner and Riesling, is served in the distinctive bocksbeutel bottle, flat and round, recognized as the hallmark of Franconian wines.

⏱ 60 min

Blaubeuren

★★☆☆☆
1

The Blautopf ('blue pot') is Germany's second-largest karst spring, located at the edge of the Swabian Jura 16 km west of Ulm. The funnel-shaped basin reaches 21 meters deep; its intense turquoise-blue color results from light scattering by limestone particles suspended in extremely clear water. During heavy rainfall, the water 'boils' as it surges under pressure from the underground karst network of the Blauhöhlen caves, explored for over 18 km by cave divers since 1985—several divers have lost their lives here, prohibiting access to non-professionals. Swabian legend tells of a goblin pouring a bucket of ink each morning to maintain the color; another claims a water nymph steals the lead ropes that curious visitors attempt to lower to measure the depth—hence the nearby rock nicknamed Klötzle Blei ('little bit of lead').

⏱ 45 min
2

The Benedictine monastery of Blaubeuren, founded in 1085 on the heights above the Blautopf, is one of Baden-Württemberg's best-preserved Romanesque monastic complexes. The abbey church houses a 15th-century Gothic altarpiece and remarkable carved choir stalls. The Hammerschmiede (hammer forge), powered by waters from the Blautopf, is visible at the site entrance; a film there documents the exploration dives into the caves.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Museum of Prehistory in Blaubeuren (Urgeschichtliches Museum) preserves pieces discovered in the surrounding Swabian Jura caves—including replicas of the Venus of Hohle Fels, the ivory mammoth figurine discovered in 2008 by archaeologist Nicholas Conard in the Hohle Fels cave at Schelklingen (7 km away). Standing 6 cm tall and dating between 35,000 and 40,000 years ago, it's the oldest known figurative sculpture in the world. The same cave also yielded flutes made from vulture bone and ivory dating to approximately 35,000 years ago—among the oldest known musical instruments.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Guided tour of Hohle Fels cave at Schelklingen (7 km from Blaubeuren). The cave is open for guided tours Wednesday through Sunday from May to October. The site sits at 616 meters altitude in the Swabian Jura; excavations begun in 1870 have uncovered Upper Paleolithic artifacts across several meters of sediment.

Munchen

★★★★☆
1

The Ulmer Münster is the world's tallest church, with a spire reaching 161.53 meters completed in 1890—513 years after the first stone was laid on June 30, 1377. The cathedral was financed exclusively by the city's residents, without any support from clergy or authorities, which remains a unique case in the history of religious architecture. Its three-nave Gothic architecture reaches 41 meters in height under the vaults. Inside, the 15th-century choir stalls by Jörg Syrlin the Elder and original stained glass are remarkable. The tower climb (768 steps) offers views to the Alps on clear days.

⏱ 75 min
2

Descend toward the Fischer- und Gerberviertel (Fishermen's and Tanners' Quarter), nestled at the confluence of the Blau and Danube rivers. This maze of medieval half-timbered lanes, small bridges, and arcaded passages escaped the December 1944 bombings that destroyed 80% of Ulm's center. The Blau, which springs from the Blautopf at Blaubeuren, flows into the Danube here. The 'leaning house' (Schiefes Haus) on the Blau's edge—a hotel built around 1500—is one of Europe's most tilted buildings.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Altes Rathaus (Old Town Hall) dates from 1370, when it served as a merchant house. Its facade astronomical clock, added around 1520, is richly decorated. The adjacent 36-meter glass pyramid houses the municipal library—one of the city's most original buildings, offering a surprising view of the cathedral from inside.

⏱ 25 min

Activities

Scenic drive from Ulm to Munich (~90 minutes on the autobahn). Ulm marks the junction between Baden-Württemberg and Bavaria, on the banks of the Danube.

⏱ 90 min

Munchen

★★★★☆
1

Marienplatz has been Munich's heart since 1158. The Marian Column (Mariensäule), erected in 1638 to celebrate the end of Swedish occupation, stands at the center. On the facade of the Neues Rathaus (1908), the Glockenspiel animates 32 life-sized figures at 11am, noon, and 5pm from March to October: the upper scene reenacts the tournament from Duke Wilhelm V's wedding in 1568; the lower one, the coopers' dance (Schäfflertanz) which, according to tradition, took place after a plague epidemic to restore courage to the population.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Frauenkirche (Cathedral of Our Lady), with its twin onion-domed towers reaching 99 meters, is Munich's symbol. Its Gothic construction dates from 1468 to 1488. No building may legally exceed this height in the city center. Inside, look for the devil's footprint (Teufelstritt) in the entrance floor—a footprint allegedly left by Satan who came to inspect the building and was satisfied to see no windows from the entrance (a pillar concealed them).

⏱ 30 min
3

The Englischer Garten (English Garden), created in 1789, is one of the world's largest urban parks—370 hectares, larger than New York's Central Park. In the southern section, the Eisbachwelle is a permanent artificial wave created by a canal where surfers practice in the heart of the city, summer and winter alike. In summer, Müncheners spread their towels on the meadows or settle into the beer gardens.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Visit to Viktualienmarkt, a permanent open-air market founded in 1807, selling Bavarian specialties, cold cuts, cheeses, beers, and Brezeln at the central beer garden. It's Munich's oldest and liveliest food market.

⏱ 45 min

Evening at a traditional Munich beer hall: Bavarian beer is served by the liter (Mass) in stoneware mugs, accompanied by typical dishes like Obatzda (herbed cheese) or Schweinshaxe (roasted pork knuckle).

⏱ 90 min

Fussen

★★★★☆
1

Neuschwanstein perches on a rocky outcrop at 970 meters altitude, towering 200 meters above the valley. Ludwig II had it built starting in 1869 as a 'temple worthy of the divine friend'—meaning Richard Wagner, whose operas inspired all the interior frescoes: Tannhäuser in the singers' hall, Lohengrin in the chambers, Parsifal in the throne room. The castle was never completed. Ludwig II spent only 172 nights of his life here. In June 1886, doctors commissioned by the government declared him insane and had him interned; three days later, he was found dead in Lake Starnberg. The unfinished rooms were opened to visitors just seven weeks after his death—to pay off his debts. Advance booking is absolutely essential.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Marienbrücke spans 92 meters above the bottom of the Pöllat gorge and crosses a 45-meter waterfall. Built in 1866, it's named after Marie of Prussia, Ludwig II's mother. From this bridge, the frontal view of Neuschwanstein is the most famous—and the most anticipated. In high season, queues form here; arrive at opening time for the best experience.

⏱ 30 min
3

Hohenschwangau Castle was Ludwig II's childhood home. His father Maximilian II had it restored from 1832 in neo-Gothic style, decorating it with scenes from the legend of Lohengrin, the swan knight, with whom Ludwig identified from childhood. One of the rooms still displays the piano on which Richard Wagner played before the young king.

⏱ 60 min
4

The Benedictine monastery of St. Mang (Kloster St. Mang) in Füssen dates back to the 9th century and was entirely rebuilt in Venetian Baroque style between 1696 and 1726 by architect Johann Jakob Herkomer. The city museum housed in its wings preserves one of Europe's finest collections of antique lutes and violins—Füssen was one of the great European centers of lute-making in the 16th century. The Annakapelle contains Bavaria's oldest Dance of Death, painted in 1602 by Jakob Hiebeler.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walk from the Ticket Center up to Neuschwanstein (30-40 minutes) for the morning opening slot. Attendance exceeds 6,000 people per day in summer—the earliest time slots are much less crowded.

⏱ 45 min

Stroll along the Alpsee, a glacial lake with transparent waters at the foot of both castles. The complete loop trail is about 3.5 km. On clear days, the reflections of the Alps in the water are stunning.

⏱ 60 min

Lindau

★★★★☆
1

The island of Lindau (68 hectares) concentrates the entire old town in the middle of the Obersee, the eastern part of Lake Constance, at the junction of Germany, Austria, and Switzerland. The harbor entrance is guarded by two emblematic monuments built in 1856: a 6-meter Bavarian lion in sandstone, sculpted by Johann von Halbig, and a 37-meter lighthouse—Bavaria's only lighthouse and Germany's southernmost. The panorama from the quays across to the Austrian and Swiss Alps is one of Germany's most unexpected. The name Lindau ('island where linden trees grow') is first mentioned in 882 in a document by a monk from St. Gall.

⏱ 60 min
2

Maximilianstrasse, the island's main street, is lined with Gothic, Renaissance, and Baroque townhouses with colorful facades. The Altes Rathaus (Old Town Hall) stands out with its entirely painted south facade depicting scenes from Lindau's history. Peterskirche is the oldest church on Lake Constance; it houses the Lindau Passion, a series of remarkable frescoes.

⏱ 50 min

Activities

Boat excursion on Lake Constance from Lindau's pier, toward the Austrian shores or to Bregenz. The lake spans 536 km²; on clear days, the Alpine peaks reflect in the water.

Triberg Im Schwarzwald

★★★★☆
1

En route to Triberg, morning stop at Unteruhldingen for the Pfahlbaumuseum (Stilt House Museum), Germany's oldest open-air museum, founded in 1922. Twenty-three Neolithic and Bronze Age dwellings (from 4000 to 850 BC) have been reconstructed on stilts above Lake Constance, based on underwater excavations. The site is part of the 111 prehistoric pile dwelling sites around the Alps, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2011. Walkways cross the lake village; the reconstructed houses contain life scenes with mannequins, tools, and period domestic animals.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Triberg Waterfalls are Germany's highest waterfalls outside the Alpine zone, with a total height of 163 meters across seven distinct tiers. The Gutach River cascades over granite rocks amid a conifer forest. Three marked trails allow the ascent (between 45 minutes and 1.5 hours). The site is illuminated every evening until 10pm.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Schwarzwaldmuseum in Triberg, built in 1873, presents Black Forest traditional life across 1,600 m²: cuckoo clock manufacturing, regional costumes (including the Bollenhut with red pompoms for married women or black for unmarried), mining life, and one of Europe's largest collections of barrel organs. Triberg has claimed its position as the historic capital of Black Forest clockmaking since the 18th century—the first mechanical cuckoo clocks were manufactured here.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hike on one of the marked trails around the Triberg Waterfalls. The cascade path (Kaskadenweg, ~45 min) follows the successive tiers from bottom to top. Squirrels and uncommon forest bird species are frequently observed along the route.

⏱ 60 min

Freiburg

★★★★☆
1

The Freiburger Münster (Freiburg Cathedral) is a Gothic architectural masterpiece whose construction spanned from 1200 to 1513—one of the rare examples of a cathedral entirely completed in the Middle Ages. Its openwork tower of 116 meters was described in 1869 by Swiss art historian Jacob Burckhardt as 'the most beautiful tower on earth.' The interior houses original medieval stained glass commissioned by the city's guilds, an altarpiece by Hans Baldung Grien, and an Annunciation by Hans Holbein the Younger. The cathedral is the only religious building in Germany to house five organs. Each morning, the Münsterplatz market sets up directly at the foot of the facade.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Bächle are small open-air channels that wind through the old town streets since Freiburg's founding in 1120. Their total network reaches 15.5 km, of which 6.4 km are underground. Originally dug for water supply and firefighting, they run between the sidewalks and give the old town a unique atmosphere. According to local tradition, accidentally falling into a Bächle means you'll marry a Freiburger. The old town also preserves its two original medieval gates: the Martinstor (early 13th century) and the Schwabentor.

⏱ 45 min
3

Ascent to Schlossberg, a wooded hill east of the Altstadt, accessible on foot or by funicular. Once the site of a medieval fortress since demolished, it offers from its panoramic tower a 360-degree view over the old town, the Rhine plain, the Vosges, and the foothills of the Black Forest.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Wine tasting of Breisgau wines at the Münsterplatz market or in an old town cellar. The Freiburg region enjoys Germany's sunniest microclimate and produces renowned Spätburgunder (Pinot Noir) and Gutedel.

Frankfurt

★★★★☆
1

Stop in Heidelberg on the way to Frankfurt. Heidelberg Castle dominates the old town from Königstuhl hill, 70 meters above the Neckar River. Its construction began in the 13th century and spanned over three centuries under several Prince-Electors; the Wars of the Palatine Succession (1689-1693) left it two-thirds in ruins. These ivy-covered Gothic and Renaissance ruins deeply inspired German Romantics—Goethe, Schiller, and Hölderlin stayed here. In the castle cellars stands one of the world's largest wine casks, the Grosses Fass, holding 220,000 liters.

⏱ 75 min
2

Heidelberg's old town is organized around the Hauptstrasse, one of Europe's longest pedestrian streets (1.6 km). The Alte Brücke (Karl Theodore Bridge), built in red sandstone in 1788 on the site of 9 previous bridges, spans the Neckar across 180 meters. The view from the bridge of the castle dominating the wooded hill—this image painted by William Turner—is one of Germany's most famous. On the bridge, a bronze statue of a monkey has symbolized since the 15th century the idea that inhabitants of one bank are no better than those of the other.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walk along the Philosophenweg (Philosophers' Way), a 1.5 km path that runs along the north bank of the Neckar offering panoramic views of the old town, castle, and valley. Professors from the university—founded in 1386, Germany's oldest—once walked this path to reflect.

⏱ 60 min
