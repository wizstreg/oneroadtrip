Rothenburg Ob Der Tauber

★★★★☆
1

Start at the Plönlein in early morning—before 8am if you can, as tour buses don't arrive until after 9. This tiny Y-shaped intersection is framed by two 14th-century towers: the Siebersturm on the left (1385) and the Kobolzeller Tor on the right. The yellow-and-red half-timbered house wedged between them allegedly inspired the village setting in Disney's Pinocchio. The composition is perfect in the slanting morning light.

⏱ 30 min
2

Continue along the rampart walkway—4 kilometers punctuated by 42 towers, free to explore. Rothenburg survived the Thirty Years' War almost intact thanks to a delicious legend: in 1631, General Tilly, having seized the town, promised to spare it if a local notable could drain a 3.25-liter tankard of wine in one go. Former mayor Nusch accepted the challenge and succeeded. Every Whitsun, locals reenact the scene during the Meistertrunk festival.

⏱ 90 min
3

Head to the Marktplatz, the town's heart since medieval times. The town hall (Rathaus) blends two styles side by side: a Gothic 14th-century facade facing north, and a Renaissance facade facing the square. Climb the 60-meter belfry for a view over red-tiled roofs and the Tauber valley below. On the 1683 clock, figurines animate every hour between 11am and 3pm to reenact the Meistertrunk scene.

⏱ 60 min
4

Step inside St. James's Church (Sankt Jakobskirche), built between 1311 and 1484. It houses two masterpieces: the Altar of the Holy Blood carved by Tilman Riemenschneider around 1499-1505, considered one of the masterworks of German late Gothic art, and the Twelve Apostles Altarpiece by Friedrich Herlin (1466). Look up at the choir's stained glass windows, dating from the 14th century.

⏱ 45 min
5

In late afternoon, make your way to the Burggarten (castle gardens), occupying the site of the Hohenstaufen fortress built in 1142 and destroyed by an earthquake in 1356. Only St. Blaise Chapel survives. The garden offers a panorama over the Tauber valley and the vineyards below—best time to have it almost to yourself.

⏱ 30 min
6

The Medieval Crime Museum (Mittelalterliches Kriminalmuseum) installs its collections in the former commandery of the Knights of St. John. Over 50,000 objects related to medieval European justice: instruments of public humiliation, shame masks, cages, law books and seals. A disconcerting museum, often overlooked by rushed visitors.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Rothenburg is best explored on foot across its compact 1-square-kilometer surface. A nighttime guided tour starting from the Marktplatz with the Night Watchman in medieval costume is offered each evening—a different way to discover the town after the daytime crowds have left.

⏱ 75 min

Fussen

★★★★☆
1

Neuschwanstein Castle perches on a rocky spur 200 meters above the valley. Ludwig II of Bavaria had it built starting in 1869 as a "worthy temple" dedicated to his friend Richard Wagner—the interior frescoes illustrate the composer's operas. The king spent only 172 nights in his castle before being removed by the government in 1886, then found dead in Lake Starnberg three days later. Book your ticket well in advance: 6,000 visitors daily in summer.

⏱ 120 min
2

Climb to the Marienbrücke, built over the Pöllat Gorge, for the postcard view of Neuschwanstein—the one that inspired Sleeping Beauty's castle at Disneyland in 1955. Walt Disney explicitly referenced Neuschwanstein for the castle design. In winter, the bridge closes for safety reasons.

⏱ 30 min
3

Descend to Hohenschwangau Castle, Ludwig II's childhood residence, restored by his father Maximilian II starting in 1833 in neo-Gothic style. This is where young Ludwig grew up surrounded by murals illustrating medieval legends that would nourish his imagination for life. Combined ticket available with Neuschwanstein.

⏱ 60 min
4

In late afternoon, head to Füssen to visit the old town and the Hohes Schloss, the former summer residence of the Prince-Bishops of Augsburg. The town marks the southern terminus of the Romantic Road, just a few hundred meters from the Austrian border. The historic core with its painted facades and cobbled lanes deserves an hour of wandering.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Lake Alpsee, directly below the two castles, lends itself to a short stroll along its shores. Ludwig II himself walked these banks. In summer, rowboats are available for rent.

⏱ 45 min

Riedlhutte

★★★☆☆
1

The Bavarian Forest National Park (Bayerischer Wald) is Germany's oldest national park, created in 1970. It covers 24,000 hectares along the Czech border, continuous with Šumava National Park on the Bohemian side—together they form the largest protected forest area in Central Europe.

⏱ 60 min
2

Start at the Tierfreigelände in Neuschönau, a 250-hectare natural enclosure where 45 animal species live in conditions close to their wild habitat: wolves, brown bears, lynx, European bison, otters, beavers, deer and large raptors. A 7-kilometer trail winds through the site—allow 3 to 4 hours. Bring binoculars, as some animals keep their distance. Entry is free.

⏱ 180 min
3

Head to Rachelsee, a mountain lake at 1,071 meters altitude, surrounded by centuries-old beeches and spruces. Local legend attributes its name to the devil's grandmother, and tells that an evil woman was the last to navigate its waters, her coffin accompanied by a flight of black ravens. The trail from Spiegelau is well marked.

⏱ 120 min
4

Stop at the Hans-Eisenmann-Haus visitor center in Neuschönau, which presents the park's history and wildlife. It's also the starting point for the Baumwipfelpfad, a 1,300-meter aerial walkway that winds through the forest canopy.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hike the marked trails of the park from Riedlhütte or Neuschönau. Over 500 kilometers of paths crisscross the park, some passing through "primeval forest" zones where no human intervention has been permitted for decades. The areas around Lusen and Rachel peaks are the wildest.

⏱ 180 min

Garmisch Partenkirchen

★★★★☆
1

Start with the Partnachklamm, a 700-meter gorge carved by the Partnach River, with walls rising up to 80 meters in places. Designated a natural monument as early as 1912, it's accessible via a path carved into the rock alongside the glacial torrent. In winter, the waterfalls transform into spectacular frozen cascades. Allow 25 minutes on foot from the Olympic stadium.

⏱ 90 min
2

Head to Partenkirchen's Ludwigstrasse for a stroll through the historic heart. Garmisch and Partenkirchen were two separate municipalities for centuries before being forcibly merged by Hitler in 1935, ahead of the 1936 Winter Olympics. Their character remains distinct: Garmisch is more modern, while Partenkirchen retains its houses with painted trompe-l'oeil facades, a Bavarian decorative style called Lüftlmalerei.

⏱ 45 min
3

Dedicate the afternoon to the Zugspitze, Germany's highest point at 2,962 meters, on the Austrian border. In clear weather, the panorama extends to Italy. A 90-year-old cog railway connects Garmisch to the glacier plateau at 2,600 meters, from where a 1,000-meter cable car ascends to the summit. The cable car from Lake Eibsee (opened in 2017) holds three world records: tallest pylon tower, greatest vertical rise, and longest free span.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

Walk around Lake Eibsee, with its turquoise waters surrounded by limestone rocks and pine forests, at the foot of the Zugspitze. The complete loop is about 7 kilometers. In summer, rowboats can be rented on site.

⏱ 120 min

Triberg Im Schwarzwald

★★★★☆
1

Begin with the Triberg Waterfalls, Germany's highest: the Gutach River plunges 163 meters in seven successive cascades through the granite rocks of the Black Forest. The site is illuminated until 10pm. Come on weekdays or early morning—the main path can get crowded in high season. The entrance ticket includes access to the Black Forest Museum.

⏱ 75 min
2

Continue to the Schwarzwaldmuseum, established in 1873, which presents over 1,600 square meters of regional crafts and traditional life: clockmaking, folk costumes including the famous Bollenhut with red or black pompoms (indicating the wearer's marital status), collections of mechanical music boxes and orchestrions. Triberg has been at the center of the Clockmakers' Route since the 18th century.

⏱ 60 min
3

Make a detour to Triberg station, starting point for the Schwarzwaldbahn discovery trail. This historic line connecting Offenburg to Singen passes through Triberg with its 37 tunnels and a 700-meter elevation gain over 149 kilometers—an engineering feat when built between 1863 and 1873.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Hike one of three marked trails around the waterfalls: the Kaskadenweg (45 min), the Kulturweg (1h) or the Naturweg (1h30). All offer different perspectives on the falls and surrounding forest. After the ascent, continue on forest paths above the waterfalls to reach the small Bergsee lake.

⏱ 90 min

Baden Baden

★★★★☆
1

Take the Schwarzwaldhochstrasse (B500) to reach Baden-Baden via the heights—60 kilometers of forests and ridges, this is the panoramic route through the northern Black Forest. Take it from the south when arriving from Triberg.

⏱ 60 min
2

Descend to the old town to see the Roman bath ruins: beneath the market square, excavations discovered in 1847 unearthed baths built between 213 and 217 AD for Emperor Caracalla. Baden-Baden's springs gush from 2,000 meters deep at temperatures between 56 and 68 degrees—800,000 liters per day emerge from twelve artesian springs aged 12,000 to 17,000 years. The Romans called the town Aquae Aureliae.

⏱ 45 min
3

Book a session at the Friedrichsbad, built between 1869 and 1877 by architect Karl Dernfeld. Inspired by Roman baths and Irish baths, it offers 17 thermal stations under a 17.5-meter dome. Mark Twain visited and wrote: "After 10 minutes you forget time; after 20 minutes, the world." The bathing tradition here is mixed and clothing-free.

⏱ 180 min
4

End the day with a walk along the Lichtentaler Allee, which follows the Oos River for 2.3 kilometers between the town center and the Cistercian abbey of Lichtenthal founded in 1245. This tree-lined avenue of centuries-old trees was in the 19th century the open-air salon of Europe—Napoleon III, Queen Victoria, Dostoevsky and Brahms all stayed here.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The modern Caracalla Therme, opened in 1985 over 5,000 square meters, allows you to enjoy the thermal waters with swimwear. Several pools at temperatures between 18 and 38 degrees, outdoor pool open year-round, saunas and steam baths. More accessible than the Friedrichsbad, with or without reservation.

⏱ 120 min

Trier

★★★★☆
1

Begin with the Porta Nigra, built from 169-170 AD as the northern gate of the Roman city Augusta Treverorum. Its 7,200 sandstone blocks, weighing up to 6 tons each, are assembled without mortar, held by iron clamps sealed with lead. It owes its name to the dark color the stone has taken on over the centuries. If it survived, it's thanks to the hermit Simeon who settled there in the 11th century: Bishop Poppo transformed the gate into a church in his honor in 1040, thus sparing it from demolition. Napoleon had the medieval additions removed during his visit in 1804.

⏱ 60 min
2

Head to the Basilica of Constantine (Konstantinbasilika), the former imperial throne room—the largest intact building from antiquity: 67 meters long, 27 wide, 33 high. Trier was one of the largest cities in the Roman Empire, called the "Second Rome" in the late 3rd century. Constantine the Great resided here for six years in the early 4th century. The basilica's acoustics, with a 7-second echo, make it a concert venue today.

⏱ 45 min
3

Continue to St. Peter's Cathedral, Germany's oldest, built in the 4th century on the site of a palace from Constantine's era. The cathedral-Liebfrauenkirche ensemble has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1986. The Liebfrauenkirche, completed around 1260, is the first entirely Gothic church in Germany.

⏱ 45 min
4

Visit the Imperial Baths (Kaiserthermen), begun under Constantine in the early 4th century. They were meant to be the largest baths in the Roman Empire but were never completed—the building was converted into military barracks, then used as a quarry. Descend into the underground passages where slaves fed the hypocausts (underfloor heating systems).

5

Trier's amphitheater, built in the 2nd century, could accommodate 20,000 spectators—one of the best-preserved Roman amphitheaters north of the Alps. Descend into the underground cellars where gladiators and animals awaited their arena entrance.

Finish with a walk along the Moselle riverbanks and through the old town. Karl Marx was born in Trier in 1818—his birthplace is now a museum. To leave the city, Frankfurt is about 2 hours by train, Luxembourg 50 kilometers away.
