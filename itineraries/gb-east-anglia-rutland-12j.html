Cambridge

★★★★☆
1

Every university in the world has tried to look like Cambridge, yet none has come close. More than thirty colleges stretch along the Cam from the thirteenth century—King's College is the absolute emblem, with its Perpendicular Gothic chapel begun by Henry VI in 1446. The interior is one of the most breathtaking spaces in England. Avoid the crush between 11am and 2pm in summer.

⏱ 90 min
2

The vaults of Trinity College's Wren Library rise toward a carved oak ceiling, and in the display cases rest Newton's manuscripts alongside the original manuscripts of Winnie-the-Pooh and The House at Pooh Corner by A.A. Milne, bequeathed by the author himself. Completed in 1695 to Christopher Wren's designs, it remains in daily use. Open weekdays 12–2pm only—outside those hours, the facade from the cloister is already worth the detour.

⏱ 60 min
3

Founded in 1209, the University of Cambridge counts Newton, Darwin, and Hawking among its alumni—a density of Nobels per corridor that exists nowhere else. The Fitzwilliam Museum, the university museum opened in 1848, displays over 500,000 works and objects. Free admission, no booking required: a bargain for a collection of this calibre.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

You board a punt on the Cam, the flat-bottomed boat synonymous with Cambridge. In an hour, you glide beneath college bridges from Mathematical Bridge to the Backs. The experience is as much about the view as the conversation with your gondolier—often a student who knows their anecdotes. Book ahead in July–August.

⏱ 75 min

If you arrive for Evensong at King's College—5:30pm Monday to Saturday, 3:30pm Sunday—entry is free and the boy choristers leave you speechless. The chapel's acoustics transform every note. Arrive twenty minutes early for a seat; latecomers stand at the back.

⏱ 60 min

Duxford

★★★★☆
1

Planned in 1917 and operational as a training station by 1918, Duxford airfield played a decisive role during the Battle of Britain. Today, IWM Duxford houses nearly 200 aircraft, tanks, and military vehicles distributed across period hangars. The runway is still active: during flying season, you can watch Spitfires take off.

⏱ 180 min
2

A military museum, yes—but Norman Foster's American Air Museum is a piece of architecture in its own right. This curved concrete hangar, Grade II* listed, opened in 1997 to honour American airmen who fell in Europe during the Second World War—Duxford was an 8th Air Force base from 1943 to 1945. Allow at least half a day: the site is vast.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

You climb aboard a Concorde and explore the cockpit from a glass walkway. The aircraft belonged to British Airways—it's not a replica. Online tickets cost less and guarantee access to the Concorde Hall, sometimes closed during major air shows.

⏱ 45 min

Saffron Walden

★★★☆☆
1

The dark timbers of Saffron Walden's Tudor houses frame streets that nobody ever had to rebuild—the decline of the wool trade in the sixteenth century preserved the town better than any conservation scheme. Church Street and Bridge Street are the most striking examples. The Saturday morning market has animated the square for centuries.

⏱ 60 min
2

'Never rebuilt, never sacked': Audley End House is one of England's finest Jacobean examples. Henry VIII gave the estate to Sir Thomas Audley after the dissolution of the monasteries, and successive owners preserved its state rooms, Victorian kitchens, and gardens designed by Capability Brown. Walking distance from the station, 2km.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

You wander through the Bridge End Gardens, a Victorian plant maze hidden behind an unassuming gate. These garden rooms—formal lawns, rose garden, kitchen garden—were created by local banker Gibson in the nineteenth century. Free entry; the hedge maze is the only element requiring a key available from the tourist office.

⏱ 45 min

Lavenham

★★★☆☆
1

Lavenham is too beautiful to be true—yet it's authentic. With 340 listed buildings, this Suffolk village was in the early sixteenth century the fourteenth-wealthiest town in England thanks to the cloth trade. Its economic ruin in the sixteenth century is why nothing was demolished. Don't miss the Crooked House, a medieval dwelling whose beams have defied gravity for centuries.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Guildhall of Corpus Christi, built around 1530 by Lavenham's cloth merchants, is the market square's most spectacular building. This timber structure successively served as a guild hall, prison, poorhouse, granary, and wartime restaurant before joining the National Trust in 1951. The garden planted with woad—the plant that gave 'Lavenham Blue' its colour—is a curiosity in itself.

⏱ 75 min
3

St Peter & St Paul's church rises 45 metres above the village, largely funded by merchant Thomas Spring III in the late fifteenth century. Its carved south porch and hammerbeam roof make it one of Suffolk's great parish churches. The neighbouring Old Grammar School, where John Constable studied, is Grade I listed.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

You pace the twisted lanes of Water Street and Prentice Street, where every turn reveals a timber-framed house in a different shade—'Suffolk Pink' has actually been obtained for centuries with milk and ochre. It's by wandering at random that you find the most photogenic angles, far from marked routes.

⏱ 60 min

If you visit off-season, Lavenham empties out and the autumn light on the timbers is remarkable. The Guildhall National Trust tearoom (free access) stays open even off-season. In summer, the village can be busy at weekends—arrive in the morning.

⏱ 45 min

Long Melford

★★★☆☆
1

Kentwell Hall hides behind an avenue of centuries-old oaks—the surprise is total when the Tudor manor appears with its moat, 30 acres of gardens, and rare-breed farm. Built in the sixteenth century for the Clopton family, enriched by the wool trade, Kentwell is famous for its Tudor historical re-enactments organised since 1979. Open April to October, Wednesday to Sunday.

⏱ 90 min
2

Melford Hall looks austere from outside—red bricks, octagonal turrets, dry moat—but the interior reveals a collection of Dutch marines and a Chinese bedroom. This National Trust property dates from the sixteenth century and was regularly visited by Beatrix Potter. Its terraced garden on Long Melford's great lawn is worth the visit alone.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

You walk the impressive Long Melford green—two kilometres of meadow bordered by Georgian houses and red-brick pubs. It's one of the longest village greens in England. At the northern end stands Holy Trinity Church, whose Flemish stained glass and carved porch make it Suffolk's most-visited church after Lavenham's.

⏱ 45 min

Sudbury

★★☆☆☆
1

Gainsborough's House is one of East Anglia's pleasant surprises: the birthplace of Thomas Gainsborough (1727–1788) was transformed into a museum and gallery after a £10 million renovation completed in 2022. Three floors display his portraits, landscapes, and a Constable collection. The garden houses a mulberry tree planted over 400 years ago.

⏱ 75 min
2

On Market Hill, the statue of painter Gainsborough stands before the old Georgian town hall, palette in hand. Sudbury was a silk-weaving centre—this textile tradition explains why the gallery displays walls hung with silk produced locally in Sudbury. The Thursday morning market on the square is the one that has changed least over centuries.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

'His light was different': that's what locals repeat when asked why Gainsborough and Constable, born a few miles apart, painted so distinctively. The Stour valley around Sudbury is ideally explored on foot or by bike on the Stour Valley Path trails. Bike hire available in town centre.

Dedham

★★★☆☆
1

Dedham has changed little since Constable walked to school here from Flatford—the church tower he painted dozens of times is still the same. Dedham Vale National Landscape celebrates in 2026 the 250 years since the painter's birth in 1776. This landscape of meadows and oaks beside the Stour is designated an Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty.

St Albans

★★★☆☆
1

Verulamium was Roman Britain's third-largest city, founded after the AD 43 conquest. The Verulamium Museum displays mosaics and everyday objects from its former inhabitants, with reconstructions of period shops and rooms. St Albans' particularity: the modern city developed slightly away from ancient Verulamium, so most remains are still beneath the park's lawns, accessible for future excavation.

⏱ 90 min
2

St Albans Cathedral's nave blends Roman bricks salvaged from Verulamium with eleventh-century Norman pillars—a mixture of eras that exists nowhere else. Erected on the site of Alban's martyrdom, Britain's first Christian martyr executed around AD 209, the cathedral is one of the country's oldest. Free entry.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

You finish with the Roman Theatre of Verulamium, one of the few Roman theatres still visible in Britain. Its semi-circular stage, excavated in 1934, could accommodate thousands of spectators. It's adjacent to Verulamium Museum—a combined ticket is available. St Albans is 20 minutes by train from London St Pancras: easy access to finish the circuit.
