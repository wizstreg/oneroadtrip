Cambridge

★★★★☆
1

Every university in the world has tried to look like Cambridge, yet none has come close. More than thirty colleges stretch along the Cam from the thirteenth century—King's College is the absolute emblem, with its Perpendicular Gothic chapel begun by Henry VI in 1446. The interior is one of the most breathtaking spaces in England. Avoid the crush between 11am and 2pm in summer.

⏱ 90 min
2

The vaults of Trinity College's Wren Library rise toward a carved oak ceiling, and in the display cases rest Newton's manuscripts alongside the original manuscripts of Winnie-the-Pooh and The House at Pooh Corner by A.A. Milne, bequeathed by the author himself. Completed in 1695 to Christopher Wren's designs, it remains in daily use. Open weekdays 12–2pm only—outside those hours, the facade from the cloister is already worth the detour.

⏱ 60 min
3

Founded in 1209, the University of Cambridge counts Newton, Darwin, and Hawking among its alumni—a density of Nobels per corridor that exists nowhere else. The Fitzwilliam Museum, the university museum opened in 1848, displays over 500,000 works and objects. Free admission, no booking required: a bargain for a collection of this calibre.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

You board a punt on the Cam, the flat-bottomed boat synonymous with Cambridge. In an hour, you glide beneath college bridges from Mathematical Bridge to the Backs. The experience is as much about the view as the conversation with your gondolier—often a student who knows their anecdotes. Book ahead in July–August.

⏱ 75 min

If you arrive for Evensong at King's College—5:30pm Monday to Saturday, 3:30pm Sunday—entry is free and the boy choristers leave you speechless. The chapel's acoustics transform every note. Arrive twenty minutes early for a seat; latecomers stand at the back.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 14 km · 22min

Duxford

★★★★☆
1

Planned in 1917 and operational as a training station by 1918, Duxford airfield played a decisive role during the Battle of Britain. Today, IWM Duxford houses nearly 200 aircraft, tanks, and military vehicles distributed across period hangars. The runway is still active: during flying season, you can watch Spitfires take off.

⏱ 180 min
2

A military museum, yes—but Norman Foster's American Air Museum is a piece of architecture in its own right. This curved concrete hangar, Grade II* listed, opened in 1997 to honour American airmen who fell in Europe during the Second World War—Duxford was an 8th Air Force base from 1943 to 1945. Allow at least half a day: the site is vast.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

You climb aboard a Concorde and explore the cockpit from a glass walkway. The aircraft belonged to British Airways—it's not a replica. Online tickets cost less and guarantee access to the Concorde Hall, sometimes closed during major air shows.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 18 km · 28min

Saffron Walden

★★★☆☆
1

The dark timbers of Saffron Walden's Tudor houses frame streets that nobody ever had to rebuild—the decline of the wool trade in the sixteenth century preserved the town better than any conservation scheme. Church Street and Bridge Street are the most striking examples. The Saturday morning market has animated the square for centuries.

⏱ 60 min
2

'Never rebuilt, never sacked': Audley End House is one of England's finest Jacobean examples. Henry VIII gave the estate to Sir Thomas Audley after the dissolution of the monasteries, and successive owners preserved its state rooms, Victorian kitchens, and gardens designed by Capability Brown. Walking distance from the station, 2km.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

You wander through the Bridge End Gardens, a Victorian plant maze hidden behind an unassuming gate. These garden rooms—formal lawns, rose garden, kitchen garden—were created by local banker Gibson in the nineteenth century. Free entry; the hedge maze is the only element requiring a key available from the tourist office.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 35 km · 50min

Lavenham

★★★☆☆
1

Lavenham is too beautiful to be true—yet it's authentic. With 340 listed buildings, this Suffolk village was in the early sixteenth century the fourteenth-wealthiest town in England thanks to the cloth trade. Its economic ruin in the sixteenth century is why nothing was demolished. Don't miss the Crooked House, a medieval dwelling whose beams have defied gravity for centuries.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Guildhall of Corpus Christi, built around 1530 by Lavenham's cloth merchants, is the market square's most spectacular building. This timber structure successively served as a guild hall, prison, poorhouse, granary, and wartime restaurant before joining the National Trust in 1951. The garden planted with woad—the plant that gave 'Lavenham Blue' its colour—is a curiosity in itself.

⏱ 75 min
3

St Peter & St Paul's church rises 45 metres above the village, largely funded by merchant Thomas Spring III in the late fifteenth century. Its carved south porch and hammerbeam roof make it one of Suffolk's great parish churches. The neighbouring Old Grammar School, where John Constable studied, is Grade I listed.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

You pace the twisted lanes of Water Street and Prentice Street, where every turn reveals a timber-framed house in a different shade—'Suffolk Pink' has actually been obtained for centuries with milk and ochre. It's by wandering at random that you find the most photogenic angles, far from marked routes.

⏱ 60 min

If you visit off-season, Lavenham empties out and the autumn light on the timbers is remarkable. The Guildhall National Trust tearoom (free access) stays open even off-season. In summer, the village can be busy at weekends—arrive in the morning.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 6 km · 12min

Long Melford

★★★☆☆
1

Kentwell Hall hides behind an avenue of centuries-old oaks—the surprise is total when the Tudor manor appears with its moat, 30 acres of gardens, and rare-breed farm. Built in the sixteenth century for the Clopton family, enriched by the wool trade, Kentwell is famous for its Tudor historical re-enactments organised since 1979. Open April to October, Wednesday to Sunday.

⏱ 90 min
2

Melford Hall looks austere from outside—red bricks, octagonal turrets, dry moat—but the interior reveals a collection of Dutch marines and a Chinese bedroom. This National Trust property dates from the sixteenth century and was regularly visited by Beatrix Potter. Its terraced garden on Long Melford's great lawn is worth the visit alone.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

You walk the impressive Long Melford green—two kilometres of meadow bordered by Georgian houses and red-brick pubs. It's one of the longest village greens in England. At the northern end stands Holy Trinity Church, whose Flemish stained glass and carved porch make it Suffolk's most-visited church after Lavenham's.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 5 km · 10min

Sudbury

★★☆☆☆
1

Gainsborough's House is one of East Anglia's pleasant surprises: the birthplace of Thomas Gainsborough (1727–1788) was transformed into a museum and gallery after a £10 million renovation completed in 2022. Three floors display his portraits, landscapes, and a Constable collection. The garden houses a mulberry tree planted over 400 years ago.

⏱ 75 min
2

On Market Hill, the statue of painter Gainsborough stands before the old Georgian town hall, palette in hand. Sudbury was a silk-weaving centre—this textile tradition explains why the gallery displays walls hung with silk produced locally in Sudbury. The Thursday morning market on the square is the one that has changed least over centuries.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

'His light was different': that's what locals repeat when asked why Gainsborough and Constable, born a few miles apart, painted so distinctively. The Stour valley around Sudbury is ideally explored on foot or by bike on the Stour Valley Path trails. Bike hire available in town centre.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 15 km · 22min

Dedham

★★★☆☆
1

Dedham has changed little since Constable walked to school here from Flatford—the church tower he painted dozens of times is still the same. Dedham Vale National Landscape celebrates in 2026 the 250 years since the painter's birth in 1776. This landscape of meadows and oaks beside the Stour is designated an Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty.

⏱ 45 min
2

The smell of water and weeping willows lining the Stour at Dedham is exactly what Constable captured in his oils. The village possesses several Tudor and Georgian houses that have been subject to the same protections since the 1960s. The Munnings Art Museum—dedicated to equine painter Sir Alfred Munnings—is within walking distance from the centre.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

You walk down to Flatford Mill following the Stour's meanders from Dedham—about 35 minutes through water meadows. It's the exact path Constable took every morning. At Bridge Cottage, the National Trust runs a tearoom and small exhibition about the painter. Arrive early morning or late afternoon for light close to his canvases.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 3 km · 8min

East Bergholt

★★★☆☆
1

'He was never away from his native home for more than four days at a time': that's what they say of Willy Lott, Flatford farmer whose house appears in Constable's The Hay Wain. Flatford Mill—owned by the painter's father—is today run by the Field Studies Council, which organises painting courses there. The view from the mill is almost identical to the 1821 painting.

⏱ 60 min
2

St Mary the Virgin church in East Bergholt hides a rare curiosity in its churchyard: a wooden cage-belfry housing five bells weighing several tons, rung by hand for lack of a tower. It's the largest ground-rung peal of bells in England. Constable was baptised here in 1776 and his parents are buried here.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Willy Lott's House is one of England's most photographed homes—and one of the few not to have budged since the nineteenth century. Take time to hire a rowing boat from Flatford to go up the Stour; it's from the water that Constable's framings make complete sense. Hire on site, rates posted at Bridge Cottage.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 40 km · 50min

Sutton Hoo

★★★★★
1

In 1939, in a Sutton Hoo field, amateur archaeologist Basil Brown uncovered the imprint of a 27-metre ship and a burial chamber filled with seventh-century treasures—garnet-inlaid gold jewellery, helmet, Byzantine tableware. The site, a few kilometres from Woodbridge, rewrote the history of the English Early Middle Ages. The originals are in the British Museum; a replica helmet is displayed on site.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Ship's Company of Woodbridge is currently reconstructing the full-size burial ship in the Longshed on the Deben's banks—the 27-metre 'Ghost Ship' has been taking shape in oak timber since 2016. You can follow the construction on a free visit from Woodbridge quay, a few minutes' walk away.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

You explore Sutton Hoo's burial mounds with the audio guide provided at entry, then climb the viewing tower to grasp the site's scale from above. The River View Walk then follows the pine wood to the Deben's shores. National Trust: paid entry, members free, no booking required.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 5 km · 10min

Woodbridge

★★★☆☆
1

Woodbridge's Tide Mill still creaks beside the Deben—one of the few tide mills still in working order in England. Built in the eighteenth century on a much older mill site, it produced flour until 1957. Now restored by volunteers, it turns at high tide on weekends from May to September.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

You follow the cycle path along the Deben from Woodbridge towards Melton, with views of moored sailboats and reed beds. It's by bike that you best appreciate coastal Suffolk, flat and traffic-free. Bikes available for hire in Woodbridge; allow two hours for a leisurely round trip.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 22 km · 30min

Orford

★★★☆☆
1

Twelve sides, none round: Orford Castle is the only surviving polygonal keep of this type in England. Built by Henry II from 1165, it dominates the village and sea from its 30-metre keep. From the roof, the view over Orford Ness—a spit of shingle and sparse vegetation protected by the National Trust—is striking. The 'Wild Man of Orford', a merman captured here in the twelfth century, is part of local folklore.

⏱ 90 min
2

You take the ferry to Orford Ness, an isolated shingle spit that was a classified military testing ground throughout the twentieth century. Abandoned experimental buildings survive among the vegetation, creating a unique atmosphere. The National Trust manages the site and runs regular crossings from Orford quay.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Butley Creek oysters, smoked shrimps, and North Sea crab: Orford quay is where you eat these specialities direct from the fishermen. The Butley Orford Oysterage, founded in the 1950s, still smokes its herrings and eels on site. Avoid summer weekends—tables fill up fast.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 20 km · 30min

Aldeburgh

★★★★☆
1

Aldeburgh is a town that takes you by surprise: pebble facades, fishmongers directly on the flint beach, and Scallop—Maggi Hambling's sculpture dedicated to Benjamin Britten, plunged in the sea wind. This composer born in Lowestoft, who settled here in the 1940s, founded the Aldeburgh Festival in 1948, one of the UK's major classical music festivals.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Red House, former home of Benjamin Britten and tenor Peter Pears, is open to the public by appointment. The archives, manuscript collection, and garden have been preserved as they were at the composer's death in 1976. For music lovers, it's an intimate experience that complements listening to Peter Grimes or the War Requiem.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

You walk the pebble beach to the Scallop, Maggi Hambling's sculpture dedicated to Britten—two 4-metre steel shells facing the sea. The fishmongers on the seafront have been serving fish and chips for decades. Arrive outside June weekends to enjoy the atmosphere without festival crowds.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 25 km · 35min

Snape

★★★☆☆
1

Snape Maltings rises from the Alde's reed beds: these nineteenth-century red-brick malt buildings were transformed into a concert hall in 1967 on Benjamin Britten's initiative. The main hall—remarkable acoustics in a raw industrial setting—is recognised as one of Europe's finest chamber venues. Galleries, shops, and a riverside-terrace café complete the site.

⏱ 60 min
2

The salt marshes around Snape Maltings are part of the Suffolk Coast Path, the coastal trail linking Snape to Aldeburgh along the Alde's banks. The view over reed beds at dusk, with sheep in the meadows and yachts at anchor, explains why Britten composed here.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

You drive to Snape Maltings for a walk along the Alde's banks, facing reed beds and mudflats. This nineteenth-century malt building, converted into a concert hall in 1967, also houses galleries, shops, and a good riverside-terrace café. Concerts for the Snape Proms and Aldeburgh Festival depart from here every summer.

⏱ 75 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 17 km · 25min

Framlingham

★★★☆☆
1

Ed Sheeran sang about it—'Castle on the Hill'—and he grew up in Framlingham, which says something about the place's hold. The castle was built around 1190 by Roger Bigod, and it's here that Mary Tudor rallied her troops in 1553 before being proclaimed Queen of England. The wall-walk on the 10-metre-high ramparts offers a view over the medieval town's rooftops.

⏱ 90 min
2

Framlingham Castle's Tudor chimneys, each different—some purely decorative, others functional—form an unusual silhouette against the Suffolk sky. The neighbouring St Michael the Archangel church houses the tombs of the Howards, Dukes of Norfolk, including a richly carved sixteenth-century monument. Free access to the church.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

You walk around Framlingham Mere, the pond at the castle's feet, on a quiet twenty-minute path. It's the same body of water Constable drew from the ramparts—the herons and swans are still there. A free audioguide is available at the castle entrance.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 38 km · 50min

Bury St Edmunds

★★★★☆
1

After his martyrdom at Danish hands in 869, King Edmund was canonised and his monastery renamed St Edmund's Bury. The abbey became one of England's most powerful before being destroyed at the Reformation. Its ruins in the Abbey Gardens—free access—compose a Romantic backdrop around flowered lawns. The Norman Tower, a twelfth-century Romanesque entrance gate, is a remarkable example of Norman architecture still standing.

⏱ 60 min
2

Bury St Edmunds Abbey was destroyed at the Reformation, but its history continues in St Edmundsbury Cathedral, which occupies the former site of St James's church. The nave's ceiling, adorned with 30 painted and gilded angels, was completed in 2010. The Millennium Tower, finished in 2005, changed the town's skyline. Guided tours Monday to Saturday at 11:30am.

⏱ 60 min
3

St Edmundsbury Cathedral deserves to be seen slowly: the interior blends a 30-angel hammerbeam roof, Victorian stained glass, and a modern Elisabeth Frink sculpture—while remaining coherent. The Bury Psalter, a medieval illuminated psalter book from the abbey, is displayed in the Treasury. Free entry, donations appreciated.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

If you're there on a Wednesday or Saturday, Bury St Edmunds market sets up on the Cornhill—it's been there for over a millennium. Fruit, cheese, local crafts. The town is also known for Greene King, a brewery active since 1799. The brewery guided tour includes a tasting of three beers; booking required.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 40 km · 50min

Ickworth

★★★★☆
1

Ickworth House is one of England's most singular residences: a 30-metre-diameter central rotunda flanked by two curved wings, built by the 4th Earl of Bristol in the late eighteenth century. The Hervey family's painting collection—Velázquez, Gainsborough, Titian—is displayed in the state rooms. National Trust, 4km south of Bury St Edmunds.

⏱ 90 min
2

The 1,800-acre park designed by Capability Brown surrounds the house with centuries-old trees, meadows, and formal gardens. A path allows a 90-minute circuit of the estate on foot. The Ickworth Hotel restaurant, housed in the east wing, serves lunches open to non-guests.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

You finish the day at Ickworth House, 4km south, a National Trust property built by the 4th Earl of Bristol in the late eighteenth century. Its 30-metre-diameter central rotunda and 1,800-acre park designed by Capability Brown make it a remarkable detour. Open year-round; the park is accessible by car.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 38 km · 50min

Ely

★★★★☆
1

In 1322, Ely Cathedral's central tower collapsed. Instead of rebuilding it identically, sacrist Alan de Walsingham conceived the Octagon Tower—an octagonal stone structure topped by a 21-metre-diameter timber lantern, completed in 1342. No modern engineer would be confident they could replicate this engineering feat. The lantern lets skylight into the heart of the nave.

⏱ 90 min
2

Standing in the flat Fens, Ely Cathedral appears from nowhere—hence its nickname 'Ship of the Fens'. The abbey was founded in 673 by Saxon Queen Ætheldreda, and the current cathedral begins in 1083. The Lady Chapel, completed in 1349, is the UK's largest side chapel. The west facade with its lantern tower is particularly impressive at sunset.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

You climb the 170 steps of the Octagon Tower Tour with a specialist guide to enter the oak lantern and open the angel panels, revealing the nave 40 metres below. The view over the Fens from the roof is striking. Guided tour Monday to Saturday, 11am–3:15pm; £12 supplement for adults, online booking advised.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 33 km · 45min

St Ives Cambs

★★☆☆☆
1

A bistro, a medical surgery, a shady pub called 'Little Hell': these are the successive uses of the medieval chapel built into St Ives bridge, one of only four remaining bridge-chapels in England. Dating from the fifteenth century, this small limestone structure is Grade I listed. The key is available from Norris Museum or the town hall.

⏱ 30 min
2

Houghton Mill, two kilometres upstream, is the last working watermill on the Great Ouse. There has been a mill on this site for over a thousand years. National Trust, open weekends and school holidays—you can buy flour milled on site, which isn't common.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The walk from St Ives to Houghton Mill along the Ouse Valley Way is one of Cambridgeshire's best flat walks—water meadows, herons, locks. Allow 45 minutes each way. Oliver Cromwell signed the register at St Ives All Saints church, visible in the vestibule.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 4 km · 10min

Houghton

★★★☆☆
1

Houghton's watermill turns beside the Great Ouse in the most-awarded village in Victorian England—Houghton won the title of finest village several times. The site is a National Trust property: the mill still works at weekends, the millstone grinds grain, and they sell the flour at reception.

⏱ 60 min
2

Hemingford Grey Manor, less than two kilometres east, is one of England's oldest inhabited houses—its main nave dates from the twelfth century. Built in grey limestone by the river, it was the home of author Lucy Boston, who wrote her 'Green Knowe' series of novels there. Visits by appointment only.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

You paddle the Great Ouse by canoe between Houghton and St Ives, following water meadows and willows. This section is known as calm and safe for beginners. Canoe hire is available at Houghton; allow two hours for the one-way journey.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 35 km · 45min

Oundle

★★☆☆☆
1

Oundle is one of Northamptonshire's best-preserved market towns—its streets are lined with honey-coloured limestone houses that give the centre a provincial Cambridge air. The town has for centuries hosted Oundle School, founded in 1556, whose buildings blend into the urban fabric. The Thursday morning market is one of the region's oldest.

⏱ 45 min
2

St Peter's Church dominates the town with its spire—Northamptonshire's tallest at 64 metres. There has been a church on this site since the eighth century, but the current building dates mainly from the thirteenth to fifteenth centuries. The Venerable Bede already mentions a monastery at Oundle in 709. The interior is accessible outside services.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

You head down to the River Nene through the meadows of Barnwell Country Park, two kilometres south of Oundle. This nature park around former lakes offers flat paths and waterfowl observation. Lyveden New Bield—an unfinished sixteenth-century manor, 8km away—is worth a detour for Elizabethan history enthusiasts.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 6 km · 12min

Fotheringhay

★★★☆☆
1

A grassy mound by the Nene, a few fragments of masonry, sheep: that's all that remains of Fotheringhay Castle. But it's here that Mary, Queen of Scots was executed on 8 February 1587, after nineteen years of captivity in England. Her condemnation for plotting against Elizabeth I was the first legal execution of a crowned European monarch—though she had abdicated in 1567, her royal status remained central to the trial.

⏱ 30 min
2

Richard III was born here in 1452, and the tombs of the royal House of York adorn St Mary the Virgin and All Saints church at Fotheringhay, linked to as many episodes of English history as the neighbouring castle. Its lantern tower and limestone interior are among Northamptonshire's finest. Free access.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Sheep graze where History reigned—that's the Fotheringhay experience, and few places offer such silence a few metres from so dramatic an event. The walk from the medieval stone bridge to the castle mound takes ten minutes. Parking is limited: park on the main street and walk.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 18 km · 25min

Stamford

★★★★☆
1

Walter Scott said of Stamford that it was 'the finest stone town in England'—and while the attribution is quoted in various forms, it fits perfectly. Over 600 listed ochre-limestone buildings, spared by the Victorian railway that bypassed the town, form one of the country's most intact historic centres. Come on a Friday, market day, to see the square animated.

⏱ 60 min
2

Burghley House stretches over more than a hundred rooms and was built between 1555 and 1587 by William Cecil, Elizabeth I's chief advisor. The state rooms overflow with seventeenth-century paintings, tapestries, and porcelain. The park was designed by Capability Brown and hosts the Burghley Horse Trials every autumn. Open Wednesday to Sunday.

⏱ 120 min
3

You walk the Welland's meadows from Stamford centre, passing under the George Hotel's hanging sign—a coaching inn founded in the sixteenth century, whose sign has spanned the main street for centuries. It's Stamford's iconic image. The river's meanders offer a peaceful car-free walk.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

At St Martin's Antiques, drawers overflow with old photographs, mother-of-pearl buttons, and pocket watches. This maze of about sixty dealers occupies a former rectory in the pedestrian street—nothing is priced the same, everything is negotiable. Arrive in the morning, before coach groups from Peterborough descend.

⏱ 60 min

If you pass on a Friday, Stamford market occupies the marketplace—it's been there for centuries. Local produce, Lincolnshire cheeses, flowers, and crafts. Cornhill Indoor Market is accessible on weekdays. The town also has several independent art galleries in lanes off Broad Street.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 25 km · 30min

Oakham

★★★☆☆
1

A castle without a keep, a collection of royal horseshoes, and a centuries-old tradition: Oakham Castle is one of England's curiosities. The Norman Great Hall, built around 1180–1190, houses over 200 horseshoes given by visiting nobles and monarchs—tradition dictates that every peer of the realm passing through Oakham must give a horseshoe to the lord. Free entry.

⏱ 60 min
2

Rutland Water glistens east of town—a 1,255-hectare reservoir that's one of Western Europe's largest artificial lakes. Created in the 1970s, it now attracts yachts, birdwatchers, and cyclists who complete the circuit (38km). The bird observation centre at Egleton is particularly active in winter.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

You cycle around the reservoir—or part of it—on the marked cycle path along the shores. Bikes are available at Whitwell on the north shore. In autumn, geese and migrant ducks arrive by the thousands. The view from the north shore, with Normanton church in the water, is Rutland's postcard.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 11 km · 18min

Uppingham

★★★☆☆
1

Uppingham School, founded in 1584 by Robert Johnson—the same who founded Oakham School the same year—occupies much of the town centre. Its limestone buildings surround courtyards and gardens that can be freely visited outside class hours. In 2022, The Times named Uppingham the best place to live in the Midlands.

⏱ 30 min
2

Uppingham's Goldmark gallery exhibits internationally renowned contemporary artists in an intimate town-centre space. Recognised among the Midlands' most active private galleries, it regularly presents sculpture, ceramics, and painting exhibitions. Free entry.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Aged cheeses and artisan bread from Uppingham's Friday market taste best on the square's benches—unapologetic Anglo-Saxon simplicity. The town is dotted with antique dealers and second-hand bookshops that make wandering its streets highly engaging. Lunch at the Falcon Hotel, a sixteenth-century coaching inn.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 55 km · 1h05

Woburn

★★★☆☆
1

Woburn Abbey was founded by Cistercian monks in 1145, dissolved by Henry VIII in 1538, and given to the Russells, future Dukes of Bedford, who still live there. The 1,200-hectare estate houses nine deer species in its deer park—the UK's largest private enclosure. The abbey reopens in summer 2026 after major renovation: confirm opening before heading out.

⏱ 90 min
2

Woburn Safari Park isn't just a family attraction—it's one of England's largest drive-through wildlife parks, with lions, tigers, and rhinos observable from your car. The park, opened in 1970, also includes a walk-through area with lemurs and sea lions. If children aren't in the group, the deer park will suffice.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

You walk through Woburn village's Georgian centre, whose reconstruction after the 1724 fire created a remarkably homogeneous architectural ensemble. Independent shops and antique dealers line the cobbled streets. The Heritage Centre, housed in former St Mary's church, is free and gives a good overview of local history.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 16 km · 22min

Silsoe

★★☆☆☆
1

Wrest Park's Long Water reflects a French-château-style mansion—incongruous in the middle of Bedfordshire, and that's exactly the intended effect. This 300-metre-long water mirror is surrounded by three centuries of layered gardens, from seventeenth-century formal parterres to wooded landscapes designed by Lancelot Brown around 1758. The baroque Archer Pavilion at the Long Water's end is one of England's most elegant garden pavilions.

⏱ 90 min
2

Thomas de Grey designed Wrest Park house himself between 1834 and 1839, inspired by Loire châteaux—this first president of the Royal Institute of British Architects had precise ideas. The interior is partially furnished with exhibitions on the de Grey family's history. Closed Mondays and Tuesdays.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Wrest Park is one of Bedfordshire's most complete garden walks—a true puzzle of styles to explore at your pace. The Bath House hidden in the woods, William Chambers's Chinese Temple, and the restored rose garden each merit a detour. Allow at least two hours to explore the gardens, not counting the house.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 28 km · 35min

St Albans

★★★☆☆
1

Verulamium was Roman Britain's third-largest city, founded after the AD 43 conquest. The Verulamium Museum displays mosaics and everyday objects from its former inhabitants, with reconstructions of period shops and rooms. St Albans' particularity: the modern city developed slightly away from ancient Verulamium, so most remains are still beneath the park's lawns, accessible for future excavation.

⏱ 90 min
2

St Albans Cathedral's nave blends Roman bricks salvaged from Verulamium with eleventh-century Norman pillars—a mixture of eras that exists nowhere else. Erected on the site of Alban's martyrdom, Britain's first Christian martyr executed around AD 209, the cathedral is one of the country's oldest. Free entry.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

You finish with the Roman Theatre of Verulamium, one of the few Roman theatres still visible in Britain. Its semi-circular stage, excavated in 1934, could accommodate thousands of spectators. It's adjacent to Verulamium Museum—a combined ticket is available. St Albans is 20 minutes by train from London St Pancras: easy access to finish the circuit.

⏱ 45 min
