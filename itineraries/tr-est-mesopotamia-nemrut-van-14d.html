Gaziantep

★★★☆☆
1

The Zeugma Mosaic Museum houses the world's largest collection of Roman mosaics, spanning 30,000 m². These works were salvaged in an emergency operation in 2001 as a dam was about to flood the ancient city of Zeugma - roughly 30% of the site now lies underwater. The centerpiece is the Gypsy Girl (Gaia), a haunting portrait of a woman with piercing eyes dating from the 2nd century AD. The museum reconstructs entire Roman villa rooms with their original mosaic floors intact. Allow at least two hours and bring your reading glasses: the detail is stunningly precise.

⏱ 120 min
2

Gaziantep Castle dominates the historic center from a height that once allowed the Romans to survey the entire plain. Originally built as a Roman watchtower, it was substantially reinforced by Byzantine Emperor Justinian I in the 6th century. From the ramparts, you command a sweeping view of the entire city. Inside, the Defence Museum chronicles Gaziantep's resistance against French forces in 1920 - an episode the city hasn't forgotten, as evidenced by the title 'Gazi' (warrior) in its name.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Emine Göğüş Culinary Museum occupies a 19th-century Ottoman mansion. Gaziantep has been a UNESCO Creative City of Gastronomy since 2015, and the city claims over 500 distinct regional dishes. The museum documents antique kitchen tools, stone ovens, and handwritten recipe books. It's a solid introduction before heading out to eat.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Get lost in the Bakırcılar Çarşısı, the coppersmiths' bazaar, where artisans hammer copper by hand exactly as they did in the 16th century. Hand-engraved trays, coffeepots, and copper ladles make sturdy souvenirs - and the sound of hammers from first light reminds you you're far from Istanbul.

⏱ 60 min

End the day with a baklava tasting: Gaziantep is recognized as the world capital of this dessert. Antep baklava stands out for its local pistachios (fıstık antep), fattier and more aromatic than anywhere else. The best shops cluster around the citadel. Ask for 'taze' - fresh from today. Katmer, a flaky pastry with kaymak and pistachios, is eaten in the morning at places that open at 6am.

⏱ 30 min

Sanl Urfa

★★★★☆
1

Göbekli Tepe challenges everything we thought we knew about the origins of civilization. This UNESCO site dating to roughly 9600 BC - 6,000 years before Stonehenge and 7,500 years before Egypt's pyramids - was built by hunter-gatherers who erected T-shaped limestone pillars reaching 5.5 m in height, carved with foxes, vultures, snakes, and scorpions. More than 200 pillars have been detected by geophysics, but less than 10% of the site has been excavated. The visitor center does an excellent job explaining the context. Come in the morning, before the tour groups.

⏱ 150 min
2

In Şanlıurfa, the City of Prophets, the Pool of Abraham (Balıklıgöl) is one of the region's most story-laden sites. Legend holds that Abraham was thrown into a fire here by King Nimrod, and the flames turned to water, the wood to carp. These sacred fish now swim by the thousands in a marble-lined pool surrounded by Ottoman gardens and the Halil-ur Rahman Mosque. No one touches them. The mosque, built in the 12th century on the site of a Byzantine church, deserves a quiet visit.

⏱ 60 min
3

Şanlıurfa's covered bazaar, with its aisles of spices and fabric merchants, is one of eastern Turkey's most authentic. Less touristy than Istanbul's or Gaziantep's, it serves locals first. Regional spices - isot (smoked black pepper unique to the region), sumac, cumin - are sold by weight. The neighboring Gümrük Han, a 16th-century Ottoman caravanserai, houses craft shops and tea salons in its arcades.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Şanlıurfa Archaeology and Mosaic Museum is essential for contextualizing Göbekli Tepe. It displays casts of the pillars, anthropomorphic figurines discovered on-site, and a complete collection of Neolithic art from the Taş Tepeler region. The 'Urfa Man' statue, dated to 9000 BC, is one of the oldest known human representations.

⏱ 90 min

Dinner with a local specialty: Şanlıurfa's lahmacun differs from everywhere else - a crisp, thin flatbread topped with spiced minced meat, herbs, and isot. Eaten rolled with parsley and a squeeze of lemon. Lahmacun ovens open at 6am and close when the dough runs out.

⏱ 45 min
$$$9.3Kasr-ı Hayat
🚗Drive to next stop: 44 km · 40min

Sanl Urfa

★★★★☆
1

The village of Harran is known worldwide for its beehive houses - conical mud-brick domes, built without wood, designed to stay cool in summer and warm in winter. This type of architecture dates back at least 3,000 years in Mesopotamia; the current versions date from the 19th century and use bricks salvaged from ancient ruins. Harran is mentioned in Genesis as the city where Abraham stayed before departing for Canaan. Wander the lanes and imagine that the town has been continuously inhabited since the Bronze Age.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Harran mound (Tell Harran) rises 35.5 m above the ancient city center. Excavations have revealed a main street, baths, fountains, and a covered market dating from the Zengid and Ayyubid periods. On the north flank stand the ruins of Harran's Grand Mosque - one of the first built in modern-day Turkey, erected around 744 AD by Umayyad caliph Marwan II. The square minaret, 33 m tall, still stands.

⏱ 60 min
3

Harran University, founded in the 8th century, was one of the first centers of Islamic learning, specializing in astronomy, mathematics, medicine, and philosophy. It played a crucial role in transmitting classical Greek knowledge by translating it first into Syriac, then into Arabic. The ruins, now partially inaccessible due to works, include remnants of the prayer hall and courtyard.

⏱ 45 min
4

Harran's medieval walls form a 4 km perimeter, pierced by several gates of which only the Aleppo Gate (west side) remains. Built from the Assyrian period and remodeled by successive dynasties, they enclosed a city that at its peak was one of the most important in Upper Mesopotamia.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The Harran plain is part of the GAP (Southeastern Anatolia Project) that transformed these lands into irrigated agricultural zones. From the town, the view stretches over kilometers of cotton, corn, and wheat - a striking contrast with the ruins behind you. A few camels still graze in the surroundings if you're lucky.

⏱ 30 min
$$$9.3Kasr-ı Hayat

Adiyaman

★★★☆☆
1

Mount Nemrut peaks at 2,134 m in the eastern Taurus range. On its summit, King Antiochus I of Commagene (69-34 BC) built his tomb-sanctuary surrounded by colossal statues 8 to 10 meters tall representing syncretic Greco-Perso-Armenian deities: Zeus-Oromasdes, Apollo-Mithra-Helios-Hermes, Heracles-Artagnes-Ares, and himself. The heads, toppled by earthquakes, measure roughly 2 m and have gazed at the horizon for two thousand years. The tumulus of crushed stone covering the summit's center (50 m high) is believed to house Antiochus's tomb - no one has found it yet. UNESCO site since 1987.

⏱ 180 min
2

The east terrace is the meeting point for sunrise: five seated statues face the rising sun, with lions and eagles guarding the flanks. The west terrace, slightly different due to earthquakes, offers the most photographed views at sunset. On the west terrace you'll also find a relief showing Antiochus shaking hands with a god - the act of dexiosis, symbol of alliance - and an astronomical relief depicting a lion with 19 stars and three planets, dated by some scholars to July 7, 62 BC, possibly the day of Antiochus's coronation.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Sunrise at Nemrut is an experience unto itself. Tour buses arrive in the middle of the night from Kahta or Malatya to reach the summit before dawn. In June-July, it's already between 5 and 10°C at the top even in midsummer: bring a jacket. If you come in April or October, expect sweaters, hat, and gloves. The walk from the parking lot to the terraces is about 500 m (300 steps).

⏱ 120 min

On the return to Kahta, stop at the Cendere Bridge, a single-arch Roman bridge built in the 2nd century AD in honor of Emperor Septimius Severus. It spans the Kahta Çayı at 6 m height. Originally four columns marked its entrances - three still stand, the fourth having been toppled for political reasons in antiquity.

⏱ 30 min
$$$8.6Ramada by Wyndham Adiyaman

Diyarbak R

★★★☆☆
1

Diyarbakır's ramparts are the world's longest after the Great Wall of China - 5.8 km of black basalt walls, pierced by four main gates and flanked by 82 towers. Built in the Roman era (4th century AD) and UNESCO-listed in 2015, they still bear 63 inscriptions from different epochs. The exterior promenade along the base reveals the fortress's entire silhouette from the Tigris plain. Allow 2 hours for the complete circuit.

⏱ 120 min
2

Diyarbakır's Ulu Cami (Grand Mosque) is Anatolia's oldest mosque - its construction dates to the Arab conquest of 639, on the site of a former Byzantine church. The current building, remodeled in the Seljuk period (11th century), is a fine example of early Islamic architecture with its arcaded courtyard and numerous reused columns. The prayer hall differs from classic Ottoman mosques with its seven naves running north-south.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Hevsel Gardens stretch across 700 hectares between the ramparts and the Tigris banks. UNESCO-listed alongside the fortress, they've fed the city for 8,000 years and today constitute the region's main avifauna habitat, with over 180 bird species recorded. Otters, foxes, and hedgehogs also live here. Access isn't universal, but viewing platforms built into the ramparts offer a striking overview of this green carpet against the black walls.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Sur district, within the ramparts, is Diyarbakır's historic heart. Its cobbled lanes lead from mosque to Armenian church, from spice market to carved basalt houses. The city bears traces of every civilization that passed through: Roman, Byzantine, Arab, Turkish, Kurdish. Take time to explore on foot; some sections were damaged during 2015-2016 conflicts and are being restored.

⏱ 90 min
$$$9Turistik Palas Otel

Mardin

★★★★☆
1

Mardin's old town cascades down a golden limestone ridge commanding the Mesopotamian plain for dozens of kilometers. It's a view that stops you cold: hundreds of honey-colored stone houses descending in terraces to the plains of Syria. The ensemble is a palimpsest of communities - Arabic-speaking, Kurdish, Syriac, Turkish - whose mosques, madrasas, churches, and monasteries coexist in the same quarter. Start at the top, at the citadel (inaccessible as it's still military, but visible), and walk down.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Zinciriye Medrese (also called Sultan İsa Medresesi) was erected in 1385 by the Artukids. Its two fluted domes, finely carved portal, and tranquil courtyard make it one of the finest examples of Islamic architecture in the region. The madrasa also functioned as an astronomical observatory - a dual purpose that explains the attention paid to the building's orientation. From the terrace, the view over the Mesopotamian plain is one of Mardin's best.

⏱ 45 min
3

Deyrulzafaran Monastery ('Saffron Monastery', or Mor Hananyo) lies 5 km east of Mardin, on the site of an ancient Assyrian temple dedicated to the sun god Shamash. Founded in 493 AD, it served as the seat of the Syriac Orthodox Patriarch of Antioch from 1166 to 1932 and hosted 53 patriarchs. Its carved wooden doors, frescoes, and underground sanctuary dating from the pre-Christian temple make it a place of layered history. Guided group tours depart every 30 minutes.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Mardin's bazaars specialize in artisanal soap made from wild pistachio and virgin olive oil, Dibek coffee (traditional stone-ground coffee), plain spices, and silver filigree jewelry. The main street (Cumhuriyet Caddesi) is paved and long - descend it slowly, stopping in the courtyards of konaks transformed into cafés with views over Mesopotamia.

⏱ 60 min
$$$9.8Mardius Tarihi Konak
🚗Drive to next stop: 70 km · 1h15

Midyat

★★★☆☆
1

Old Midyat (Eski Midyat) is the craft capital of Tur Abdin, a plateau whose name means in Syriac 'the Mountain of the Servants of God'. Its cream limestone houses with arcades, carved windows, and interior courtyards resemble a flatter, quieter Mardin. The town is also the center of telkari, the art of silver filigree practiced by Syriac artisans for centuries. In the bazaar workshops, you can watch jewelers work silver into threads as fine as lace.

⏱ 90 min
2

Mor Gabriel Monastery, 20 km from Midyat on the İdil road, is the world's oldest continuously inhabited and active Christian monastery - founded in 397 AD by two monks, Mor Samuel and Mor Şimun. It housed up to 1,000 monks in the 6th century. Today a small community of monks, nuns, and Syriac language students lives here. The main church, completed in 512 with support from Byzantine Emperor Anastasius, preserves aniconic mosaics and two patriarchal thrones. Visits are guided and last about 45 minutes.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Tur Abdin still houses about a dozen active monasteries, most difficult to access. If you have time, Mor Şarbel (15 km from Midyat) and Mor Augen (on a rocky spur) are worth the detour for their total isolation and monks who still speak Aramaic. Ask for local directions as signage is sparse.

⏱ 120 min
$$$10Mehmet Bey Konağı Butik Otel
🚗Drive to next stop: 100 km · 1h30

Hasankeyf

★★★★☆
1

Hasankeyf is a difficult experience to classify. This town of 12,000 years of continuous occupation was partially drowned in 2020 by the Ilısu Dam on the Tigris. The old town - its cafés on stilts, medieval bridge, thousands of caves - now lies underwater. What remains visible: the citadel (Hasankeyf Kalesi), perched on its limestone spur 100 m above the new lake, and the New Hasankeyf Cultural Park, where eight major monuments were dismantled and rebuilt, including the Zeynel Bey Mausoleum (15th century, turquoise tiles of Iranian influence) and the El Rizk Mosque (1409). The citadel is reached by boat from the port, or by the mountain road circling the lake.

⏱ 120 min
2

The New Hasankeyf Cultural Park groups the monuments saved from the flood and a museum documenting the town's history and the dam controversy. The north cliffs of the site reveal caves carved millennia ago, some still open to view. The silence reigning here today contrasts with what the town was - heartbreaking for anyone who knows its history.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The boat crossing of the lake to reach the citadel is the only way to grasp the scale of the change. From the small ferry deck, you can see the tops of old houses emerging in places during low water, like urban ghosts. The local guide can explain what lies beneath the surface. A way of traveling in two eras at once.

⏱ 60 min
$$$8.9AQI Pegasos Resort
🚗Drive to next stop: 200 km · 3h

Van

★★☆☆☆
1

Lake Van is Turkey's largest (3,755 km²) and the world's largest soda lake - its water is alkaline and slightly soapy, which explains why you float more easily than in the sea. Akdamar Island lies 3 km offshore from the Gevaş landing. Crossing by boat (15 min) to reach the Armenian Church of the Holy Cross, built between 915 and 921 by King Gagik I of Ardzrouni, is one of the itinerary's highlights. The exterior bas-reliefs in pink volcanic tuff depict biblical scenes - Adam and Eve, Jonah and the whale, David and Goliath - an iconographic program unique in Armenian art. Inside, partially preserved frescoes.

⏱ 120 min
2

Van Castle (Van Kalesi) dominates the lake's east shore from a 100 m rocky promontory. Erected by the Kingdom of Urartu in the 9th century BC, it guarded Tuşpa, the ancient capital. Cuneiform inscriptions in Urartian are still readable on the rock faces. Below lie the ruins of the old city of Van (Eski Van), destroyed in 1915 and never rebuilt - only three minarets and some foundations emerge from a grassy field. It's a heavy place.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Van Archaeological Museum displays the most important objects from Urartian civilization: bronzes, ceramics, jewelry, and cuneiform inscriptions. The section dedicated to the lake presents Van's unique fauna, notably Van cats, a naturally bicolored breed often with eyes of two different colors.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Van breakfast (Van kahvaltısı) is a national institution: a dozen cheeses including otlu peynir (wild herb cheese), mountain honey, kaymak, tomatoes, olives, eggs prepared several ways. Cafés serving this breakfast open from 7am and are packed on weekends. It's the best in Turkey, Van locals will say - and they're probably not wrong.

⏱ 60 min
$$$9El Baboos Hotel
🚗Drive to next stop: 200 km · 3h30

Dogubayaz T

★★★☆☆
1

İshak Pasha Palace perches on a rocky ledge 5 km from Doğubayazıt, commanding the plain with Ararat as backdrop. This 366-room complex was built between 1685 and 1784 by three generations of the same pasha family. Its architecture is a deliberate blend of Ottoman, Persian, Georgian, and Armenian styles - a synthesis explained by the region's frontier position. The harem, ceremonial hall, and mosque are in good condition. The place is even more impressive from the viewpoint opposite, where you see the palace and Ararat together in the same frame.

⏱ 90 min
2

Mount Ararat (Ağrı Dağı), 5,137 m, is Turkey's highest peak - an extinct volcano whose snow-capped slopes dominate the plains for hundreds of kilometers. It's considered a sacred mountain by Armenians (symbol of the nation) and associated worldwide with Noah's Ark. You can only circle it with your eyes from Doğubayazıt, unless you've booked a guided expedition months in advance (permit required). The view from the palace viewpoint is one of Turkey's most photographed.

⏱ 60 min
3

The ruins of Old Bayazıt (Eski Bayazıt) occupy the foot of the castle hill, a few dozen meters behind İshak Pasha Palace. The town was destroyed by the Turkish army in 1930. House foundations, a restored mosque (Eski Bayezid Camii), and dressed stones from the Urartian fortress remain, some dating to the 9th century BC.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The Durupınar site, 30 km south, is presented by some as the remains of Noah's Ark - a boat-shaped rock formation at 2,000 m altitude. Geologists are skeptical and consider it a natural volcanic structure. For those interested in unsolved mysteries, it's a possible excursion; for others, the view of Ararat's foothills is the reward.

⏱ 90 min
$$$9.1Tehran Boutique Hotel

Kars

★★★☆☆
1

Kars Citadel dominates the town from a black basalt promontory. Its origins likely date to the Armenians of the Bagratid era (9th century), before being successively rebuilt by the Seljuks (1153), destroyed by Tamerlane (1386), and restored by the Ottomans (1579). Its military history is dense: besieged by Russians, contested during World War I, finally integrated into the Turkish Republic in 1920. Access to the upper courtyard is free and the views over the city and river are frank and beautiful.

⏱ 60 min
2

Kars's Church of the Holy Apostles (Kümbet Camii) was built in the 10th century by Bagratid King Abas I when Kars was still the Armenian capital. Its twelve bas-reliefs depicting the apostles are still visible on the dome's drum - a rarity for a building that has been successively converted into a mosque, a Russian Orthodox cathedral, a museum, and again a mosque in 1993. This building's history summarizes Kars's history in itself.

⏱ 45 min
3

Kars's Russian quarter is a uniqueness in Turkey. From 1878 to 1918, the city was a town of the Russian Empire, and its Baltic-style neoclassical buildings - wide avenues, dressed-stone facades, steep roofs - differ radically from the rest of eastern Turkey. Kars is also one of the few eastern cities where you'll easily find beer and wine in normal shops.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Kars is known for its kaşar cheese and mountain honey. The covered market sells aged cheese produced in the neighboring Caucasus pastures. If you're here December-March, Kars is the starting point of the Doğu Ekspresi (Eastern Express), the overnight train Istanbul-Kars that's become a tourist phenomenon in Turkey for its snowy landscapes.

⏱ 45 min
$$$8.1Kars-i Si̇ri̇n Otel
🚗Drive to next stop: 45 km · 50min

Kars

★★★☆☆
1

Ani is a ghost city UNESCO-listed since 2016, former capital of the Armenian Bagratid kingdom. At its peak in the 11th century, it counted between 100,000 and 200,000 inhabitants and rivaled Constantinople and Baghdad. The Mongols (1236), a catastrophic earthquake (1319), and the shift of trade routes emptied it of substance. Today a windswept plateau, a few square kilometers of ruins on a promontory that the Akhurian gorge (Turkish-Armenian border) cuts knife-sharp. You can see Armenia 500 meters away. The site is visited on foot (5-6 km for the complete circuit). Allow at least 3 hours.

⏱ 180 min
2

Ani Cathedral (1001) is the site's centerpiece - built by architect Trdat, the same who repaired the dome of Constantinople's Hagia Sophia. Its pointed arches and compound pillars appear in Europe only from the 12th century onward: some scholars think Gothic architecture drew inspiration from it. The dome collapsed during the 1319 earthquake and was never rebuilt. Entering, look for the star and cross markings in the stone.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Church of St. Gregory of Tigran Honents (1215) is the best-preserved and most ornate on the site. Commissioned by a wealthy merchant, its interior walls are covered with frescoes floor to ceiling. The entrance is marked by bas-reliefs above the portal. It's the monument at Ani that offers the fullest vision of medieval Armenian artistic wealth.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

From the cliffs overlooking the Akhurian, the view plunges onto the gorge and Armenia opposite. The remnants of the medieval bridge that once spanned the river are visible below - broken arches, symbol of a connection severed for centuries between two countries that still share no open border. It's one of the most emotionally charged viewpoints of the entire itinerary.

⏱ 30 min

Erzurum

★★★★☆
1

The Çifte Minareli Medrese (Twin Minaret Madrasa) is Erzurum's most recognizable monument. Built in the 13th century at the end of the Seljuk era, it's Anatolia's largest madrasa (35 x 48 m). Its two 26 m fluted brick minarets, adorned with blue faience details, rise above a portal covered in chisel carvings of a finesse few Anatolian buildings match. Tree of life, double-headed Seljuk eagle, geometric interlacing - every surface is inhabited. Today a museum with free courtyard access.

⏱ 60 min
2

Erzurum's Ulu Cami (Grand Mosque), built in 1179 by Saltukid Emir Nasreddin Aslan Mehmed, is one of the most austere and oldest examples of Anatolian Islamic architecture. Its seven north-south running naves and sixty columns create an unusual stone forest, very different from grand Ottoman mosques with central plans. The interior wooden framework in 'kırlangıç' (swallow) style is an architectural rarity.

⏱ 30 min
3

Erzurum's citadel occupies a hill dominating the city since the 5th century (restored by Byzantine Emperor Theodosius). Its Clock Tower (Tepsi Minaret), formerly a minaret repurposed, offers a panoramic view over the mountain-ringed city. The Üç Kümbetler (Three Tombs), 12th-century Seljuk mausoleums with conical roofs, stand 300 m south of the madrasa.

⏱ 60 min
4

The Yakutiye Medresesi (1310), built by the Mongol Ilkhanids, now houses the Museum of Turkish and Islamic Arts. Its double-headed eagle portal and Central Asian-style faience minaret make it a perfect complement to the twin minaret madrasa visit.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Erzurum is the regional capital at 1,750 m altitude, often wind-swept - jacket or warm clothing even in summer. The city offers highland cuisine: cağ kebabı (lamb roasted on a horizontal spit, local specialty served on lavash bread), ayran soup, and su böreği (boiled cheese pastry). It's also a dynamic university town, which shows in the center's liveliness.

Trabzon

★★★☆☆
1

The road from Erzurum to Trabzon (240 km, roughly 4h30) crosses the Zigana Pass (2,025 m), one of Turkey's most spectacular routes. The landscape changes radically in an hour: you pass from the arid steppes of eastern Anatolia to the beech and fir forests of Pontus, then plunge toward the Black Sea. The slopes are green twelve months a year, houses are wooden, villages cling to hillsides. The contrast with what you've seen for the past two weeks is striking.

⏱ 60 min
2

If you reach Trabzon before 4pm, there's time for a round trip to Sumela Monastery (47 km, 1h south). This 4th-century Greek Orthodox monastery clings 300 m up a vertical cliff in a forested gorge. The rock church's frescoes date from the 14th to 18th centuries. Sumela was closed for restoration from 2015 to 2019 and has since reopened. It's a fine introduction to the Black Sea coast.

⏱ 150 min

Activities

Trabzon, a port city on the Black Sea, was in the Middle Ages the capital of the Empire of Trebizond. The city has its own Hagia Sophia (13th century), now a mosque-museum, and some Byzantine ruins. From TZX airport, flights connect Istanbul and other Turkish cities. If you continue toward the Black Sea itinerary, Trabzon is the starting point toward Rize, Artvin, and the Georgian coast.
