Thessalonique

★★★☆☆
1

The White Tower is the first thing you'll see as you arrive along the waterfront — a cylindrical 15th-century fortification rebuilt under Suleiman the Magnificent, which initially served as an Ottoman prison and mass-execution site before being renamed the 'White Tower' in 1912 during the Greek reconquest. It now houses a museum on the history of Thessaloniki and offers a panoramic view from its summit over the Thermaic Gulf and, on a clear day, Mount Olympus. Allow 45 minutes for the visit.

⏱ 45 min
2

A few minutes' walk away, the Basilica of Agios Dimitrios is the largest church in Greece dedicated to the city's patron saint. Built between 629 and 634 on the ruins of a Roman bath where Demetrios was imprisoned and executed under Diocletian, it measures 53.6 metres long by 33 metres wide and has five naves. It was converted into a mosque from 1493 to 1912 — known then as the Kasimiye Camii — though Christian pilgrims were tolerated at certain times. Don't miss the 7th-century mosaics on the pillars and the underground crypt where the ancient Roman baths once stood. Free entry.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Galerius complex brings together two UNESCO-listed Roman monuments best seen together. The Arch of Galerius, inaugurated in 303 to celebrate victory over the Sassanid Persians at the Battle of Satala in 298, stands in the middle of Egnatia Street — the central arch is 9.7 metres wide and 12.5 metres high, with marble bas-reliefs still visible. 125 metres to the northeast, the Rotunda, begun in 306 as a mausoleum for Galerius, has a diameter of 24.5 metres and walls 6 metres thick — Galerius was ultimately buried elsewhere, and the building became in turn a temple, a church and then a mosque, whose last minaret in the city is still standing.

⏱ 60 min
4

Climb to the Ano Poli, the old upper town and one of the few areas to survive the devastating fire of 1917. Its narrow lanes of Ottoman houses with wooden balconies offer plunging views over the Thermaic Gulf. Don't miss the Heptapyrgion, the seven-towered Byzantine fortress that commands the city from the hilltop, nor the Church of Hosios David (Latomos Monastery), whose 5th-century mosaics are UNESCO-listed.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Head down in the late afternoon to Aristotelous Square, the city's nerve centre designed by a French architect after the 1917 fire in a style blending Art Deco with classical references. The café terraces fill up fast and the sea breeze blows in from the waterfront. Order a café frappé — Thessaloniki claims to have invented this iced drink — and watch local life unfold.

⏱ 60 min

For dinner, head to the Modiano Market, built in the 1920s and recently renovated: 75 shops, delicatessens, local cheeses and a handful of tavernas inside. The Ladadika neighbourhood just next door, in colourful former warehouses, is the heart of the nightlife with its rebetiko music and Macedonian cooking. Try the bougatsa, a cream or cheese pastry that's a Thessaloniki staple.

⏱ 90 min

Epanomi

★★★☆☆
1

Potamos beach at Epanomi, 35 km south of Thessaloniki, is a pointed sandy spit jutting into the Thermaic Gulf — its shape recalls Croatia's Zlatni Rat, but with far fewer people. On a clear day, Mount Olympus is visible across the gulf. What makes the site truly unusual is the wreck of a cargo ship that ran aground more than 50 years ago just off the shore: locals say Saint Antonios himself sank the vessel to protect the adjacent 550-hectare wetland, a refuge for migratory birds. Arrive in the morning for the calm before the Thessaloniki families descend.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Epanomi wetland stretches across 550 hectares between dunes and marshes — a sanctuary for migratory birds, with herons, passing flamingos and numerous aquatic species. A trail runs along the lagoon from the east car park of the beach. The site is especially active in spring and autumn during the migrations.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The wreck offshore is reachable by swimming or snorkelling from the beach — the water is clear and shallow here, making it an excellent snorkelling spot suitable for all levels. The west side of the bay, exposed to the wind, is popular with kitesurfers.

⏱ 120 min

The Epanomi area is known for its vineyards: the soil and microclimate produce characterful dry whites. A short drive through the surroundings will take you past several wineries open for visits in summer. End the day at sunset facing the gulf — Olympus turns orange.

⏱ 120 min

Poseidi

★★★☆☆
1

Entering the Kassandra peninsula, stop at Nea Fokea to see the Byzantine tower by the harbour. This 28-metre stone tower, built in 1407 to protect the metochion of the Saint Paul Monastery on Mount Athos, is the best preserved in all of Halkidiki — intact right up to the battlements. In 1821, it served as the headquarters of Emmanouil Papas, leader of the Greek uprising in Halkidiki. At its base lies the cave sanctuary of Saint Paul, a chapel hewn into the rock.

⏱ 45 min
2

Cape Possidi, at the south-western tip of Kassandra, is a shifting tongue of sand that juts into the sea — its shape changes with the tides and the seasons. The 19th-century lighthouse at its end is one of the most photographed sunset spots in Halkidiki. The village of Afitos, perched on the ancient acropolis a few kilometres away, is worth a detour for its stone houses, cobbled lanes and views over the Toroneos Gulf.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The beaches of Kassandra are among the most accessible in Halkidiki — several hold the Blue Flag, the sand is fine and the shallow waters are ideal for families. The coast around Possidi alternates between organised beaches with sunbeds and wild coves fringed with pines. Swim in the morning, before the buses from Thessaloniki arrive.

⏱ 180 min

Kalamitsi

★★★☆☆
1

Sithonia, Halkidiki's middle peninsula, is a different world from Kassandra. Here, pine forests plunge straight into emerald coves, winding roads reveal a new bay at every turn and the fishing villages haven't been swallowed by hotels. Drive the loop starting from the east coast — the views across to Mount Athos are breathtaking.

⏱ 60 min
2

Karydi Beach, near Vourvourou, regularly ranks among the finest beaches in northern Greece: powdery white sand, turquoise water barely knee-deep, set against pines and sculpted rocks. It's a wild beach — no sunbeds, no umbrellas — so bring everything you need. Arrive before 10am in summer; it fills quickly.

⏱ 90 min
3

Kavourotripes, also known as Orange Beach, is a chain of small coves separated by white cliffs tinged with orange. The water shifts from azure blue to emerald green. It's the most famous organised beach on Sithonia — sunbeds are available at the main cove, the others remain wild. If you're worried about the parking situation (it's chaos in August), arrive early.

⏱ 90 min
4

Neos Marmaras is Sithonia's liveliest port — dozens of waterfront tavernas, excursion boats and a marina. It's a practical base for exploring the peninsula, but also the place to enjoy fresh seafood in the evening under the harbour lights.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Rent a boat (no licence required) from Neos Marmaras or Vourvourou to explore the coves that can't be reached by road — particularly the beaches of Platanitsi and Armenistis, which look even wilder from the sea. Organised excursions from these ports also offer views of the Mount Athos coastline opposite.

⏱ 240 min

Porto Koufo, at Sithonia's southern tip, is an almost enclosed natural harbour: two sheer cliffs leave a 300-metre opening onto the sea. The effect is dramatic and the waters inside are perfectly still. Stop here for a lunch of grilled fish at a waterside taverna.

⏱ 90 min

Ouranoupoli

★★★☆☆
1

The Prosphorion Tower dominates the entrance to Ouranoupoli's harbour — a 14th-century Byzantine fortification built to protect the metochion of Vatopedi Monastery, and one of the best-preserved medieval monuments in the region. It houses a small museum on the history of the Athos peninsula. It's also the rallying point for every cruise to Mount Athos: you'll recognise it by the quay packed with pilgrims and tourists in the morning.

⏱ 45 min
2

Ouranoupoli — 'City of Heaven' in Greek — is the last town before the border of the Monastic Republic of Mount Athos, an autonomous theocratic state home to some 1,500 monks in 20 monasteries. Women and non-Orthodox laypeople are not permitted to enter (10 non-Orthodox per day, men only, by special permit). From the harbour, though, you can see the foothills of the peninsula rising from the sea.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Take the coastal cruise departing from the harbour — boats leave daily at 10:30am to sail along the western coast of the Mount Athos peninsula and observe the monasteries clinging to the cliffs from the sea. The cruise lasts about 3 hours 45 minutes and passes monasteries such as Xenophontos and Simonos Petras. It's open to everyone, men and women, and is the only way for the vast majority of visitors to get close to the site. Book ahead in summer.

⏱ 225 min

In the late afternoon, catch the small ferry to Ammouliani Island, 10 minutes from Ouranoupoli. This island, largely untouched by mass tourism, has crystal-clear waters and secluded beaches — notably Alikes beach, a spectacular crescent of golden sand. The return trip in the late afternoon leaves time for sunset over the harbour.

⏱ 180 min

Amfipoli

★★★☆☆
1

The Lion of Amphipolis has stood beside the Strymon since its reconstruction in 1936 — a colossal 8-metre-tall statue (including its base) in Thasian marble, dated to the 4th century BC. Recent excavations suggest it crowned the Kasta burial mound. It is thought to have been erected in honour of Laomedon of Mytilene, one of Alexander the Great's generals. From the road, the lion's silhouette is clearly visible above the river — stop for the photo.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Kasta tumulus, 4.5 km away, is the largest funerary monument ever discovered in Greece. Excavations since 2012 have revealed a 497-metre-long, 158-metre-diameter peribolos in Thasian marble, dated to the last quarter of the 4th century BC. In 2014, archaeologists uncovered inside a three-chambered Macedonian tomb with two sphinxes, two caryatids, a pebble mosaic depicting the abduction of Persephone by Hades and, in the third chamber, skeletal remains of at least five individuals. The identity of the deceased remains unknown. A protective building has been erected over the site — check opening times before visiting.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Archaeological Museum of Amphipolis, in the village, displays finds from local excavations: marble reliefs, gold jewellery, vases, sculptures and liturgical objects testifying to the intense commercial activity of Amphipolis, founded by the Athenians in 437 BC on the Thracian site of Ennea Hodoi. The city was in turn Athenian, Spartan and then Macedonian — Philip II seized it in 357 BC.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Stroll along the Strymon in the afternoon — the river was navigable in antiquity and Amphipolis' strategic position at its mouth explains why so many empires fought over it. The banks are quiet and shaded, with views over the surrounding Macedonian hills. The two Byzantine towers flanking the river, the better-preserved of which dates from 1367, are visible from the bridge.

⏱ 60 min

Dikella

★★★☆☆
1

On the way to Dikella, make a detour through Kavala — a port city built into a hillside descending to the Aegean, facing the island of Thasos. Its old Ottoman quarter of Panagia is best explored on foot from the 270-metre Kamares aqueduct, built in the 16th century under Suleiman the Magnificent. Climb to the citadel for the sea view. In the quarter's streets, look for the birthplace of Mehmet Ali, an Ottoman officer born here in the 18th century and considered the founder of modern Egypt.

⏱ 90 min
2

Twenty-five minutes north of Kavala, the site of Philippi is an essential stop. Founded in 360 BC by settlers from Thasos, fortified by Philip II of Macedon in 356, it witnessed the Battle of Philippi in 42 BC where Octavian (the future Augustus) and Mark Antony crushed Brutus and Cassius. In AD 50, the Apostle Paul founded the first Christian church in Europe here. A UNESCO World Heritage Site, the grounds include an ancient theatre still used in summer, early Christian basilicas, a Roman forum and a museum. Allow at least 2 hours.

⏱ 120 min
3

Dikella beach, on the northern Aegean coast, is a long stretch of golden sand and smooth pebbles with good facilities — sunbeds, showers, a gentle slope into the water — popular with families from the Alexandroupoli area. It's quiet, rarely visited by foreign tourists and makes for a peaceful end to the circuit before the return via Kavala (airport less than 30 km away).

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Spend the afternoon at Dikella beach — the water is clean and shallow, the Aegean sunset is serene and crowd-free. Mesi beach, a few kilometres along, is even wilder if you're looking for total solitude.

⏱ 180 min

Finish with a seafood dinner at one of the waterfront restaurants in the nearby village of Agia Paraskevi — octopus, Thracian mussels and grilled fish have been served here for decades by local fishing families. The cuisine of Eastern Macedonia blends Greek, Turkish and Balkan influences in a way quite different from the islands.
