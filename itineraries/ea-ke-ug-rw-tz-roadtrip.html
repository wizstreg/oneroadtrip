Nairobi is your base camp for the entire circuit. Stop 2

Naivasha

Geothermal gorges where zebras graze beside volcanic steam vents

★★★★★
📍 Hell's Gate National Park Kenya📍 Elsa Gate Hell's Gate📍 Ol Njorowa Gorge Hell's Gate📍 Fischer's Tower Hell's Gate📍 Lake Naivasha Kenya
1

Hell's Gate National Park is unlike any other park in Kenya: you can walk or cycle through it freely, without a vehicle, in the middle of zebras and buffalo. The 68 km² park takes its name from explorers Gustav Fischer and Joseph Thomson, who coined it in 1883 when they discovered the dramatic cliff breach — though the Maasai had a different, less cartographic explanation: for them, the steam rising from the ground was the breath of ancient spirits. Those geothermal plumes are no legend. The Olkaria power station — the first of its kind in Africa, commissioned in 1981 — now generates over 280 MW from right inside the park.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Ol Njorowa Gorge is the undisputed highlight: rock corridors carved by water over thousands of years, some so narrow you have to turn sideways to squeeze through. Watch your step near the thermal springs at the bottom — the rocks can burn. A local guide is mandatory for the lower gorge, which is no bad thing: they know every twist and turn, and their stories are as compelling as the scenery.

⏱ 120 min
3

Fischer's Tower is a 25-metre volcanic rock column rising from the valley floor. It's the park's iconic climbing site, but even without gear, circling the base on foot rewards you with photographic angles you won't find anywhere else.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walk or cycle through the park at your own pace among zebras, giraffes, elands and baboons. Hell's Gate is one of only two Kenyan national parks where unguided walking is permitted — a rare freedom in East Africa. The main loop runs 9 km from Elsa Gate to the gorge, mostly downhill on the way out.

⏱ 180 min

Evening walk along the shores of Lake Naivasha. Hippos surface at dawn and dusk — hearing them snort from the bank in the dark is oddly mesmerising. Crescent Island, reachable by boat from lodges on the south shore, lets you walk freely among giraffes and zebras with no vehicle in sight.

Stop 3

Kisumu

Kenya's only city on the shores of the vast inland sea of Lake Victoria

★★☆☆☆
📍 Kisumu Hippo Point Kenya📍 Kisumu Impala Sanctuary📍 Dunga Beach Kisumu📍 Lake Victoria Kisumu waterfront📍 Kisumu Museum
1

Kisumu is the only Kenyan city on the shores of Lake Victoria — and it changes the atmosphere entirely. The lake covers 59,947 km², making it the second-largest freshwater lake in the world, shared between Kenya, Uganda and Tanzania. John Hanning Speke named it after Queen Victoria in 1858, but the local Luo had long called it Nyanza. The Kisumu Museum offers a solid introduction to Luo culture and the lake's fishing heritage — allow 45 minutes.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Kisumu Impala Sanctuary hugs the lakeshore 3 km from the city centre. It's not a large park, but it's a great spot to see impalas, zebras, giraffes and hippos in a semi-wild setting with the lake in the background. Over 115 bird species have been recorded here — the African fish eagle's piercing cry is a near-constant soundtrack.

⏱ 90 min
3

Kenya–Uganda border crossing. The Busia post (or Malaba, 15 km further north) is the standard crossing for the Kisumu–Jinja route. Both posts are open 24/7. Budget an extra 1–2 hours depending on traffic. The East Africa Tourist Visa ($100) covers Kenya, Uganda and Rwanda on a single document — request it explicitly at the post or apply online before departure.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Sunset over Lake Victoria from Hippo Point or Dunga Beach. The name is slightly misleading — actual hippos are rare here — but the view across the lake at golden hour, with fishing canoes gliding back to shore, is one of the finest in western Kenya. Local boats offer hour-long trips to spot birds and the occasional hippo from the water. Swim only in hotel pools — the lake harbours bilharzia and crocodiles.

Stop 4

Jinja

Where the world's longest river draws its first breath

★★★☆☆
📍 Source of the Nile Jinja Uganda📍 Bujagali Falls Jinja📍 Jinja town centre Uganda📍 Nile River Explorers Jinja📍 Nalubale Rafting Jinja
1

Jinja is where the Nile begins its 6,696 km journey to the Mediterranean. Explorer John Hanning Speke identified this spot in 1862 as the source of the White Nile — the single outlet through which Lake Victoria's waters escape. The name Jinja comes from Luganda and Lusoga and simply means 'rock.' It's Uganda's second industrial hub, but the city has been transformed above all by adventure tourism since the 1990s. Stroll through the colonial centre, where the Indian-influenced shopfronts bear witness to the role of Gujarati traders in the town's growth.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Source of the Nile monument sits on a small island reached by boat from the north bank. From here, you can see where the lake hands the river its first breath — the water moves out slowly, almost reluctantly, before picking up speed toward the Bujagali rapids 8 km downstream.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Scout the Bujagali rapids in the late afternoon if you're planning to raft the following morning. The White Nile at Jinja is ranked among the world's best whitewater destinations for Grade 4–5 rapids. The construction of the Nalubaale Dam submerged the original falls in 2012, but the downstream rapids remain some of the most powerful in Africa. Nile River Explorers and Nalubale Rafting are the two long-standing operators — both with excellent safety records.

Entebbe

Victorian botanical gardens and the gateway to Uganda's papyrus swamps

★★★★☆
📍 Entebbe Botanical Gardens Uganda📍 Uganda Wildlife Education Centre Entebbe📍 Entebbe waterfront Lake Victoria📍 Nakiwogo Ferry Landing Entebbe
1

The Entebbe Botanical Gardens were established in 1898 by first curator Alexander Whyte on the shores of Lake Victoria. These 40 hectares of tropical forest and manicured gardens have an unexpected cinematic footnote: the forested section served as the backdrop for the Tarzan films starring Johnny Weissmuller in the 1940s. Allow 90 minutes to wander among the 300 tree species and 120 medicinal plants. The 100+ bird species include the African fish eagle, malachite kingfisher and various weavers.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Uganda Wildlife Education Centre (UWEC), founded in 1952 as a rehabilitation centre for animals confiscated from poachers, is Uganda's most serious wildlife facility. Think of it as a sanctuary rather than a zoo: Rothschild's giraffes, lions, chimpanzees, rhinos — most of the animals here cannot be returned to the wild. The behind-the-scenes option with the keepers is particularly well run.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Cross by boat from Nakiwogo landing to Mabamba — logistics prep for the early morning ahead. Or simply enjoy a quiet lakeside dinner: The Jungle restaurant at the Boma Hotel has a terrace overlooking the water, perfect for watching fishermen come in at sunset.

Stop 6

Entebbe

Victorian botanical gardens and the gateway to Uganda's papyrus swamps

★★★★☆
📍 Mabamba Swamp Shoebill Uganda📍 Nakiwogo Landing Site Entebbe📍 Mabamba Bay Lake Victoria
1

Mabamba is a 16,500-hectare wetland on the northern shore of Lake Victoria, 12 km west of Entebbe — designated a Ramsar site in 2006 for its international ecological significance. The swamp takes its name from the local lungfish known as emamba, which happens to be the shoebill's preferred prey. This prehistoric bird — sole representative of the family Balaenicipitidae — can stand motionless for hours, then strike with startling speed. Over 260 bird species have been recorded in the marsh.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Dugout canoe trip at sunrise to find the shoebill. Be up before 5:30am — the bird hunts and moves most actively in the first hours of daylight. The excursion lasts three to four hours, weaving through narrow channels cut through papyrus and water lilies. Your guide — often a former fisherman trained in conservation — knows exactly where the bird sleeps and feeds. The shoebill stands 1.2 m tall with a 2.4 m wingspan: when one stares you down from five metres away, the effect is prehistoric and unforgettable.

⏱ 210 min

Stop 7

Lake Mburo

Uganda's only park where impalas and Burchell's zebras share the same plains

★★★★☆
📍 Lake Mburo National Park Uganda📍 Rwonyo Camp Lake Mburo📍 Sanga Gate Lake Mburo📍 Lake Mburo boat cruise📍 Zebra track Lake Mburo
1

Lake Mburo National Park is the only park in Uganda where you'll find both impalas and Burchell's zebras — a geographical quirk rooted in the park's turbulent history. It was gazetted in 1983, displacing Banyankole Bahima pastoralists from their ancestral land. Their resistance led to the park's partial destruction in 1985, before it was re-gazetted in 1986. Today it covers 260 km² of acacia woodland, rolling hills and five interconnected lakes, with a leopard density that is remarkable for its size — around 25 individuals identified by camera trap.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Zebra Track is the park's most productive game drive loop: open plains fringed with acacias, with herds of zebras, buffalos, topis, impalas and the occasional leopard along the waterways. In the dry season, animals concentrate around the marshes, making sightings even more reliable.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Late afternoon game drive. There are no lions at Lake Mburo, which means walking safaris are also on the table. The lake cruise (2 hours) puts you face to face with hippos, crocodiles, African fish eagles and the occasional shoebill from the water. If time allows, the horseback safari offered by some lodges is one of the most original wildlife experiences in East Africa.

⏱ 180 min

Lake Bunyonyi

The lake of a thousand birds — where you can actually swim in Africa

★★★★☆
📍 Lake Bunyonyi Uganda📍 Punishment Island Lake Bunyonyi📍 Bushara Island Lake Bunyonyi📍 Kyahugye Island Lake Bunyonyi📍 Bwama Island Lake Bunyonyi
1

Lake Bunyonyi means 'place of many small birds' in Rukiga — and it delivers on the name, with over 200 species. Sitting at 1,962 metres above sea level in the terraced hills of southwest Uganda, it is said to be Africa's second deepest lake after Tanganyika, with soundings suggesting depths of up to 900 metres. It is also one of the very few African lakes with neither crocodiles nor hippos, making it completely safe to swim in — a rare luxury that should not be missed.

⏱ 45 min
2

Each of the lake's 29 islands carries its own story. Akampene, the smallest, is known as Punishment Island: Bakiga girls who fell pregnant outside of marriage were once abandoned there to die. Bwama Island housed a leprosy treatment centre founded in 1931 by Scottish missionary Leonard Sharp — its buildings now serve as schools. Kyahugye Island is home to impalas, zebras and waterbucks for a miniature game drive on the water.

⏱ 60 min
3

Uganda–Rwanda border crossing. The Cyanika post (between Kisoro and Musanze) is the most direct route from Lake Bunyonyi: 90 minutes of driving through mountain terrain, with tracks that can be slippery in rain. The easier alternative is Katuna (on the Kabale–Kigali road) — a larger crossing with a better road. Rwandan border formalities are generally quick — under 30 minutes if you have your visa or e-visa ready.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Traditional canoe trip among the islands. This is the real Lake Bunyonyi experience: a dugout carved from a single trunk, a quietly paddling local guide, the vertiginous terraced hills reflected in the still morning water. Allow two hours to visit Punishment Island, Bwama and a couple of neighbours. By evening, mist rolling down from the hills at sunset turns the lake into something out of a painting.

⏱ 120 min

Volcanoes Np Kinigi

The Virunga volcanoes, last sanctuary of the mountain gorilla

★★★★★
📍 Volcanoes National Park Rwanda Kinigi📍 Kinigi Park Headquarters Rwanda📍 Dian Fossey Karisoke Research Centre Rwanda📍 Golden monkey tracking Rwanda
1

Volcanoes National Park shelters five of the eight Virunga volcanoes: Karisimbi, Bisoke, Muhabura, Gahinga and Sabyinyo. Tucked into Rwanda's northwest, 2.5 hours from Kigali, these bamboo-forested, mist-shrouded summits form one of the last refuges of the mountain gorilla. The current global population stands at roughly 1,000 individuals — 604 of them in the Virunga massif — and is slowly growing thanks to sustained conservation efforts.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Karisoke research site, founded by Dian Fossey in 1967, is accessible by hiking trail from the Kinigi headquarters. Fossey spent 18 years shielding gorillas from poachers before being murdered in her cabin in 1985 — a crime that has never been officially solved. Her grave is on-site, surrounded by the gorillas she fought hardest to protect. The hike is physically demanding (roughly 3 hours return), but the historical weight of the place is extraordinary.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Golden monkey trekking. If your gorilla permit is for tomorrow, start today with the golden monkeys — less famous, but just as extraordinary. These primates, endemic to the Albertine Rift (Cercopithecus mitis kandti), crash through the bamboo in noisy troops of up to 80. Five habituated groups live in the park; the trek lasts two to four hours depending on where the troop has wandered that day.

★★★★★
📍 Gorilla Trekking Rwanda Volcanoes National Park📍 Mount Bisoke crater Rwanda📍 Kinigi briefing point Rwanda gorillas📍 Musanze Rwanda
1

Gorilla trekking in Volcanoes National Park: you end up a few metres from a silverback — a dominant male that can weigh over 200 kg — watching you with an unnerving calm. Mountain gorillas share 98% of our DNA. Each family is visited by a maximum of 8 people for one hour per day only. The trek itself can last anywhere from 30 minutes to 6 hours depending on where the family has roamed that morning — bring gaiters, a rain jacket and solid hiking boots.

⏱ 360 min

Activities

Option: climb Mount Bisoke (3,711 m). If you have a second free day in the park, Bisoke offers a 6-hour return hike to a summit crater lake. The vegetation shifts every hour — bamboo forest, then Hagenia woodland, then alpine moorland. On a clear day the view stretches across the Virunga range all the way into the DRC.

Gisenyi Lake Kivu

Rwanda's only public beach, on an African great lake with no crocodiles

★★★☆☆
📍 Rubavu Beach Lake Kivu Rwanda📍 Gisenyi Rubavu waterfront Rwanda📍 Congo Nile Trail Rwanda start📍 Pfunda Tea Plantation Rubavu📍 Napoleon Island Lake Kivu
1

Gisenyi — officially Rubavu today — is Rwanda's border town with the Congo, sitting on the northern shore of Lake Kivu. This 2,700 km² lake is one of Africa's most overlooked great lakes, which is a shame: no crocodiles or hippos, clear water, sandy beaches and volcanoes on the horizon. In 2002, lava from Nyiragongo flowed into the lake on the Congolese side — which accounts for the stretches of black volcanic sand you'll notice along the shore. The Bralirwa brewery — Rwanda's only brewery — has been operating in town since the 1950s.

⏱ 45 min
2

Rubavu public beach, Rwanda's only public beach, runs for a few hundred metres along the waterfront. The lake water is clear and safe — swimming is popular and risk-free here. The Avenue de la Coopération promenade follows the lakeshore toward the Congolese border under a canopy of palms and flamboyant trees.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Sunset dugout canoe trip on Lake Kivu to the nearby islands. Napoleon Island is home to a colony of straw-coloured fruit bats that take to the air en masse at dusk — an unexpectedly spectacular natural display. Local canoes depart from the main pier.

Stop 12

Nyungwe

Central Africa's largest mountain forest, with walkways suspended 70 metres above the canopy

★★★☆☆
📍 Nyungwe Forest National Park Rwanda📍 Uwinka Visitor Centre Nyungwe📍 Nyungwe Canopy Walk Rwanda📍 Gisakura Guest House Nyungwe📍 One Only Nyungwe House Rwanda
1

Nyungwe is one of the largest montane forests in central Africa: 1,000 km² of unbroken primary forest that has stood for millennia. It supplies 75% of Rwanda's water resources — roughly half the rivers drain toward the Nile, the other half toward the Congo. The park harbours 13 primate species, 300 bird species (26 of them endemic to the Albertine Rift) and over 1,000 tree species. The Uwinka visitor centre, halfway along the Butare–Cyangugu road, is the departure point for all activities.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The Canopy Walk is the highest suspended walkway in the region: 160 metres long, 70 metres above the forest floor, built in 2010 by USAID. You reach it via the 2 km Igishigishigi trail — not the moment to discover you have a fear of heights. From up there, the forest stretches unbroken to the Congo horizon. Black-and-white colobus monkeys sometimes leap between trees at eye level.

⏱ 180 min

Option: chimpanzee trekking in the Cyamudongo forest patch. This 19 km² fragment of forest tucked between tea plantations is home to a well-habituated group of 30 chimpanzees. The trek is 4 hours return from the Gisakura reception. Nyungwe's chimps are more mobile than those in Kibale, Uganda — sightings are less guaranteed, but tracking them through tea-covered hills is an experience in its own right.

Stop 13

Kigali

Africa's cleanest capital and a city that carries the full weight of 1994

★★★★☆
📍 Kigali Genocide Memorial Gisozi Rwanda📍 Kimironko Market Kigali📍 Niyo Art Gallery Kigali📍 Inema Arts Center Kigali📍 Kigali Convention Centre
1

The Kigali Genocide Memorial, inaugurated in April 2004 on the tenth anniversary, is the resting place of 250,000 victims murdered in Kigali in 1994. It was inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List in 2023. The visit opens with a chronological exhibition — Belgian colonisation, 1980s propaganda, the hundred days from April to July 1994 — before building in intensity to the children's room, with photographs and individual stories of each child killed. It is difficult. Take your time. The audio guide is strongly recommended.

⏱ 120 min
2

Kimironko Market is the largest covered market in Kigali — a maze of kitenge fabric stalls, imigongo geometric art (made from cow dung), vegetables and music sellers. The atmosphere is lively and largely untouched by tourism. Haggling is expected.

⏱ 60 min
3

Rwanda–Uganda border crossing. The Gatuna (Rwanda) / Katuna (Uganda) post is 90 minutes north of Kigali on the Kabale road. It's the main road crossing between the two countries — the best equipped and the fastest. From Kabale, the drive to Bwindi is a further 2–3 hours depending on which sector you're heading to — Buhoma or Nkuringo — largely on dirt tracks.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Kigali is Africa's cleanest capital — plastic bags have been banned since 2008, and the last Saturday of every month is a national cleaning day called Umuganda in which everyone participates. The Nyamirambo neighbourhood, a historically Muslim working-class quarter, is worth an hour on foot for its restaurants, coffee shops and genuine local energy. Dinner at one of East Africa's best restaurants before hitting the road again tomorrow.

Stop 14

Bwindi

The impenetrable forest that shelters half the world's mountain gorillas

★★☆☆☆
📍 Bwindi Impenetrable National Park Uganda📍 Buhoma Sector Bwindi Uganda📍 Nkuringo Sector Bwindi Uganda📍 Bwindi gorilla trekking Uganda
1

Bwindi means 'place full of darkness' in Runyakitara — an honest description of this 321 km² forest where some sections are so dense that no light reaches the ground. The forest served as a refuge for species during the Pleistocene glaciations, which explains its extraordinary biodiversity: over 1,000 flowering plant species, 350 bird species, 120 mammals. And above all, roughly 459 mountain gorillas — nearly half of the world's remaining population.

⏱ 45 min
2

Bwindi is divided into six sectors: Buhoma, Nkuringo, Ruhija, Rushaga, Nyundo and Rubuguri. Buhoma (north sector) is the most developed and has the longest tourism history — the first gorilla trek took place here in April 1993 with the Mubare family. Nkuringo (south sector) is the most physically demanding — vertiginous slopes in the mist — but delivers the most dramatic scenery.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Gorilla trekking at Bwindi — if your permit is for today. The permit costs $800 (versus $1,500 in Rwanda) for one hour with a habituated family, groups capped at 8. The trek can last anywhere from 1 to 7 hours depending on where the gorillas have moved that day. Porters available at the sector entrance are strongly recommended — $15 for the round trip — less for carrying your bag than for hauling you up the slopes.

Stop 15

Queen Elizabeth Np Mweya

The Kazinga Channel and the fig-tree lions of Ishasha

★★★★☆
📍 Queen Elizabeth National Park Uganda Mweya📍 Kazinga Channel boat cruise Uganda📍 Mweya Safari Lodge Uganda📍 Ishasha sector tree climbing lions📍 Queen Elizabeth NP game drive
1

Queen Elizabeth National Park is one of Uganda's most biodiverse parks: 95 mammal species, 620 bird species, elephants, buffalos, hippos and lions. The Mweya Peninsula juts into the Kazinga Channel like a tongue of land between two lakes — Edward and George. The lawns of Mweya Safari Lodge offer an unbeatable view over both lakes at sunset.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Ishasha sector, in the park's southwest corner, is one of only two places in the world where lions climb trees — specifically into giant fig trees. This behaviour — Ishasha lions routinely spend their days draped 4–5 metres up in the branches — has never been satisfactorily explained by researchers. The only other tree-climbing lion population is at Lake Manyara in Tanzania.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Kazinga Channel cruise (2 hours). This 40 km waterway linking Lake George to Lake Edward holds one of Africa's highest hippo concentrations — over 5,000. The boat hugs the bank within metres of hippos wallowing, elephants drinking, buffalos and a corridor of 600+ bird species. Watch for the African fish eagle colony at its regular perch.

⏱ 120 min

Game drive in the northern sector (Kasenyi tracks or Channel Drive). Lions, buffalos, elephants and leopards are most commonly seen around waterholes during the dry season.

Stop 16

Murchison Falls

The Nile forced through 7 metres of rock before a 43-metre plunge

★★★★★
📍 Murchison Falls National Park Uganda📍 Top of the Falls Murchison📍 Paraa Safari Lodge Murchison Falls📍 Baker's Point Murchison Falls📍 Nile boat cruise Murchison Falls
1

Murchison Falls is where the White Nile forces 300 cubic metres of water per second through a 7-metre-wide gorge before plunging 43 metres. It is technically one of the most powerful waterfalls in the world — visitors sometimes feel a faint tremor in the ground when standing at the edge. Explorer Samuel Baker named it in 1864 after the president of the Royal Geographical Society. Ernest Hemingway crashed his plane just downstream in 1954 — he survived, though international newspapers prematurely announced his death.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Top of the Falls viewpoint: from the bank you watch the broad, calm Nile suddenly compress into the fissure, vanish, then explode below in a permanent cloud of spray. There is almost always a rainbow. The hiking trail from Baker's Point (accessible from the cruise) climbs to the summit in 45 minutes.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Nile River cruise to the base of the falls (3 hours). From Paraa jetty, the boat travels 17 km upstream past banks thick with hippos, Nile crocodiles and elephants coming down to drink. The cruise — inaugurated in 1959 by the Queen Mother — is one of Uganda's most iconic experiences. At the turnaround point, the spray from the falls reaches you on the deck.

⏱ 180 min

Game drive on the park's northern plains. Murchison Falls is Uganda's largest park (3,893 km²) and one of the few where you can see Rothschild's giraffes — a critically endangered subspecies with fewer than 2,900 individuals remaining worldwide. Elephants, buffalos, kobs, hartebeests and scattered lions complete the scene.

Kidepo Valley

Uganda's most isolated savannah, where the wilderness stretches to South Sudan

★★★★★

Kidepo Valley is probably Uganda's most remote destination — 700 km from Kampala, wedged between South Sudan and Kenya in the semi-arid Karamoja region. Gazetted as a national park in 1962, the area was inaccessible and dangerous until the mid-2000s due to the LRA insurgency and raiding between Karamojong clans. Today the park is stable and safe, but the end-of-the-world feeling is entirely intact. Buffalo herds of over 3,000 animals sometimes sweep across the Narus plain. It is also Uganda's only park with ostriches.

⏱ 30 min
2

A pre-dawn departure is mandatory for tomorrow. Kidepo to Kitale is the most demanding leg of the entire circuit: roughly 680 km and 14 hours on dirt tracks through Kitgum, then Gulu, then the Kenya–Uganda border at Suam or Malaba. Prepare the vehicle the night before — full tank, water for the day, food. Mobile coverage is non-existent across much of the route.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Sunset game drive in the Narus Valley. This is the heart of the park — the only area with permanent water in the dry season, which draws all the wildlife to the remaining pools. Lions, elephants, Rothschild's giraffes, zebras, buffalos, bat-eared foxes — a species almost absent from the rest of Uganda. 476 bird species including several Albertine Rift endemics found nowhere else in Uganda.

This is a full driving day — Kidepo to Kitale — the longest and most demanding leg of the circuit. Leave Apoka before 6am, on red laterite tracks through Kaabong, then Kotido, Moroto and Mbale as the roads gradually improve. The Malaba border crossing (or Busia, depending on your route) comes in the late afternoon, with 1–2 hours of formalities. Arrival in Kitale is realistically around 8pm if things go smoothly. It's a day of Karamojong landscapes and semi-arid plains rolling past your window — an experience in itself, but load up a playlist.

Kitale

Gateway to Mount Elgon and the sitatunga marshes

★★☆☆☆
📍 Kitale Kenya📍 Kitale Museum Kenya📍 Mount Elgon National Park Kenya📍 Saiwa Swamp National Park Kitale
1

Kitale is a quiet farming town nestled in the foothills of Mount Elgon at 1,800 metres above sea level. It serves here as a recovery stop after the gruelling Kidepo tracks. The Kitale Museum gives a decent overview of the local Nandi, Turkana and Pokot peoples — with a small botanical garden thrown in.

⏱ 60 min
2

Saiwa Swamp National Park, 18 km north of Kitale, is Kenya's smallest national park (2 km²) — created specifically to protect the sitatunga, an exceptionally rare semi-aquatic antelope adapted to marshland. Elevated wooden boardwalks thread through the swamp, letting you watch sitatungas from just a few metres without disturbing them.

Iten

The village at 2,400 metres that has produced more Olympic distance champions than anywhere else on Earth

★☆☆☆☆
📍 Iten Kenya Home of Champions📍 Kerio Valley viewpoint Iten📍 Elgeyo Escarpment Iten Kenya📍 High Altitude Training Centre Iten
1

Iten calls itself 'The Home of Champions' — with every right to do so. This small town perched on the Elgeyo escarpment at 2,400 metres has produced a disproportionate share of Olympic long-distance champions over the past 50 years. The road from Kitale follows the edge of the Kerio escarpment — a 700-metre cliff above the Rift Valley floor — with sweeping viewpoints every 5 km.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Walk along the Kerio Valley escarpment viewpoints. The trail at the cliff edge offers 180° views over the Rift Valley 700 metres below — terraced farmland, forested hills and, on clear days, Mount Elgon's peaks to the north. In the early morning, groups of athletes run in silence along the red dirt paths — a strangely majestic scene.

Nyeri

The gateway to the Aberdares and a night watching rhinos from a treetop hide

★★★☆☆
📍 Aberdare National Park Kenya📍 Outspan Hotel Nyeri Baden-Powell📍 Treetops Lodge Aberdare Kenya📍 The Ark Aberdare Kenya
1

Nyeri is the gateway to the Aberdares — a forested massif of 767 km² rising above 3,900 metres between the Rift Valley and Mount Kenya. Robert Baden-Powell, founder of the Scout movement, spent his final years in Nyeri and is buried in the small Saint Peter's cemetery — a brief but touching stop for those who know the story. The town sits in a fertile agricultural region of tea and coffee plantations.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Night at Treetops or The Ark in Aberdare National Park. These lodges built on stilts above a floodlit waterhole are one of East Africa's most distinctive experiences: from your room or the open deck, you watch elephants, buffalos, black rhinos, hyenas and leopards come down to drink through the night. It was at Treetops that Princess Elizabeth learned in 1952 she had become queen on the death of her father.

Samburu Nr

The five endemic northern species you will find nowhere else in Kenya

★★☆☆☆
📍 Samburu National Reserve Kenya📍 Ewaso Ngiro River Samburu📍 Buffalo Springs Reserve Kenya📍 Samburu Special Five Kenya📍 Elephant Watch Camp Samburu
1

Samburu is the reserve to visit if you want species found nowhere else in Kenya. The Samburu Special Five — a local term — names five species endemic to the semi-arid northern plains: Grevy's zebra (finer stripes, large round ears, fewer than 2,000 left worldwide), reticulated giraffe (clean polygon-patterned coat, around 8,500 surviving), gerenuk (a long-necked antelope that rears up on its hind legs to browse high leaves), Beisa oryx and Somali ostrich.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Ewaso Ng'iro River cuts through the reserve in a corridor of doum palms and riparian forest. Elephant Watch Camp, founded by Saba Douglas-Hamilton (daughter of the celebrated researcher Iain Douglas-Hamilton), sits directly on the bank. The river is the focal point of all wildlife movement — crocodiles, elephants, leopards — in the early morning and late afternoon.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Morning and evening game drives in the reserve. Samburu sees far fewer vehicles than the Masai Mara, and guides can often leave the tracks to approach animals — a welcome freedom. As an option, Kenya has reintroduced black rhinos into the adjacent Sera Conservancy — a walking rhino trek can be arranged with advance notice.

Ol Pejeta

The last refuge of Najin and Fatu, the world's only two northern white rhinos

★★☆☆☆
📍 Ol Pejeta Conservancy Kenya📍 Northern White Rhino Ol Pejeta📍 Sweetwaters Tented Camp Ol Pejeta📍 Sweetwaters Chimpanzee Sanctuary Kenya📍 Ol Pejeta black rhino Kenya
1

Ol Pejeta Conservancy is East Africa's largest black rhino sanctuary — over 165 individuals across 360 km² at the foot of Mount Kenya. And there is something else entirely: this is the only place on Earth where you can stand in front of the last two northern white rhinos. Najin and Fatu — mother and daughter — live in a 700-acre enclosure under 24-hour armed protection. The last male, Sudan, died on 19 March 2018. An international scientific team is currently attempting to create embryos via IVF using frozen sperm from the last males to pull the subspecies back from extinction.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Sweetwaters Chimpanzee Sanctuary is the only place in Kenya to see chimpanzees. These are rescued animals — orphans or individuals seized from illegal trafficking — that cannot be returned to the wild. The guided sanctuary visit lasts 90 minutes and includes feeding time.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Game drive in the conservancy. Ol Pejeta is one of the few places in East Africa where seeing all of the Big Five in a single day is a realistic prospect. Cheetahs are particularly active in the late morning. The night game drive is recommended for leopards, hyenas and other nocturnal species that avoid the midday heat.

Amboseli

Big-tusked elephants roaming beneath the snows of Kilimanjaro

★★★☆☆
📍 Amboseli National Park Kenya📍 Enkongo Narok Swamp Amboseli📍 Observation Hill Amboseli📍 Ol Tukai Lodge Amboseli📍 Amboseli elephants Kilimanjaro
1

Amboseli — from the Maasai Empusel, meaning 'salty dusty place' — is the most photographed safari landscape on Earth, for one simple reason: herds of 50 to 100 elephants moving across dusty plains with snow-capped Kilimanjaro as a backdrop. The 392 km² park is home to around 1,800 elephants, the best-studied wild elephant population in the world — the Amboseli Elephant Research Project has tracked individuals continuously since 1972, giving over 50 years of unbroken data. Some of the great old bulls carry tusks so long they nearly graze the ground.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Enkongo Narok and Longinye swamps are fed by Kilimanjaro meltwater filtering underground for 50 km. In the dry season, all wildlife converges on these permanent wetlands: elephants bathing, hippos snorting, crowned cranes dancing, flamingos painting the shallows pink. The ideal time to be there is sunrise — by 9am the clouds typically close in around Kili's summit.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Sunset game drive around the swamps. Observation Hill is the only spot in the park where you can leave the vehicle and walk freely — the view from the summit takes in the swamps, the plains, the elephant herds and, when the mountain cooperates, Kilimanjaro filling the entire southern horizon.

Arusha

The gateway to Tanzania's wild north, in the shadow of Mount Meru

★★★☆☆
📍 Arusha National Park Tanzania📍 Kilimanjaro International Airport JRO Tanzania📍 Mount Meru Arusha Tanzania📍 Arusha town centre Tanzania📍 Cultural Heritage Centre Arusha
1

Kenya–Tanzania border crossing at Namanga. The Amboseli–Arusha road skirts the Kenyan foothills of Kilimanjaro before reaching Namanga (165 km from Nairobi, 270 km from Arusha). The crossing is generally quick for private vehicles (30–45 minutes), though it can back up at midday. A Tanzanian visa is required — best obtained as an e-visa before departure at eservices.immigration.go.tz, or purchased at the border ($50). Your vehicle's COMESA insurance must cover Tanzania.

⏱ 60 min
2

Arusha is the tourism capital of northern Tanzania — the departure point for all Northern Circuit routes (Serengeti, Ngorongoro, Tarangire, Kilimanjaro). It sits at the foot of Mount Meru (4,566 m), Africa's fifth-highest mountain, surrounded by fertile coffee and banana plantations. If you have a full day, Arusha National Park, 30 km to the east, offers giraffes, zebras, black-and-white colobus monkeys and a guided hike on Meru's slopes.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Cultural Heritage Centre in Arusha is the largest craft market in East Africa — a three-storey labyrinth of galleries with Makonde wood sculptures, Tingatinga paintings, Maasai jewellery and fabrics. Prices are fixed and fair. If you want quality souvenirs to bring back, this is the place.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Transfer to Kilimanjaro International Airport (JRO), 50 km east of Arusha (1 hour by road). International return flights depart from JRO. If you're extending your stay in Tanzania, Arusha is the base for Serengeti and Ngorongoro circuits, or for attempting Kilimanjaro itself.
