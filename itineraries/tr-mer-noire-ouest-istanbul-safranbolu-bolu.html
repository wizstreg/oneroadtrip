Istanbul

★★★★★
1

Church, museum, mosque—Hagia Sophia encapsulates Istanbul's entire destiny. Built in the 6th century and reconverted to a mosque in 2020, it now offers a ticketed visitor circuit around its monumental dome. Arrive at opening to avoid the queues that stretch through the morning.

⏱ 90 min
2

Touristy, yes—but the 20,000 blue İznik tiles lining the interior of the Blue Mosque justify the visit. Built between 1609 and 1616, it's the only mosque in Istanbul with six minarets—a scandal at the time, as that number was reserved for Mecca. Entry is free; visits suspended during the five daily prayers.

⏱ 60 min
3

The scent of old leather and incense lingers in the halls of Topkapi Palace once you leave the tour groups behind. The seat of Ottoman power for roughly four centuries, it's structured in successive courtyards. The Harem (separate ticket) is what makes or breaks the visit—budget an extra 45 minutes.

⏱ 120 min
4

Underground, yet you breathe easier than above—the Basilica Cistern (Yerebatan Sarnıcı) offers welcome coolness after a day in the sun. Recently renovated, it now presents a scenographic circuit around its 336 columns. An hour is enough—and it's three minutes on foot from Hagia Sophia.

⏱ 60 min
5

Cross the Hippodrome (Sultanahmet Meydanı) and spot the Obelisk of Theodosius and the Serpent Column: two ancient monuments standing in the middle of the square. Ten minutes is enough to walk around; it's the natural connector between Hagia Sophia, the Blue Mosque, and the Cistern.

⏱ 15 min

Activities

Locals just call it 'the Bazaar'—the Grand Bazaar (Kapalıçarşı), dating from the 15th century, houses over 4,000 shops under vaulted galleries. Best mid-day to avoid the rush. Enter through the Nuruosmaniye Gate and exit toward the Spice Bazaar: the transition from gold to spices is worth the walk.

⏱ 90 min

Take a ferry from Eminönü to cruise the Bosphorus as far as Üsküdar or beyond—it's the cheapest way to see Istanbul from the water. IDO public ferries depart every 15-20 minutes. In the evening, the lights from both shores and the Bosphorus Bridge beat any organized cruise.

⏱ 60 min

Polonezkoy

★★★☆☆
1

A Polish village 25 km from Istanbul—the incongruity is total. Polonezköy (Polish: Adampol) was founded in 1846 by Polish refugees fleeing the partition of their country. Turkey was, at the time, the only European state that refused to recognize the partition. Declared a nature park in 1996, the village retains its wooden houses in the forest.

⏱ 20 min
2

Founded in 1846 by Polish settlers, Polonezköy hosted Franz Liszt in 1847 and Gustave Flaubert in 1850—then Atatürk in 1937 and the future Pope John XXIII in 1941 for a confirmation. The Church of Our Lady of Częstochowa, built in 1914 after a fire, is still active. Its restorers bear distinctly Polish names: Ohotski, Dohoda, Novicki.

⏱ 15 min
3

Life-sized wood sculptures among the oaks—Polonezköy's open-air exhibition brings together works by Turkish and Polish artists, installed in the nature park. Beekeeper Kemal Sayal's honey museum is small but well-documented. Look for it on your way back from the forest walk.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Have the village breakfast (köy kahvaltısı) before tackling the 5 km marked forest trail. That's the main activity here—what Istanbul residents come seeking on weekends. Arrive early to avoid Saturday crowds; on weekdays, the paths are almost deserted.

⏱ 90 min

Sile

★★★☆☆
1

Painted black and white to be visible from afar, Şile Lighthouse (Şile Feneri) was built on 60-meter-high rocks during the reign of Sultan Abdülmecid I. Its 19-meter stone tower houses a small maritime museum. Originally built to guide ships coming from the Black Sea during the Crimean War.

⏱ 30 min
2

Inaccessible, Şile Castle fascinates more from the cliff than if you could enter it. This 10th-century Byzantine fortress occupies an islet separated from the shore—you look at it, imagine it. Take in the view from the promontory facing the lighthouse to grasp the entire coastline.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

If the sea is calm, Şile Beach and Kilimli Bay (Kilimli Sahil), a few minutes east, offer less crowded coves. The Black Sea is cool even in summer—expect 18-22°C in July-August. Avoid summer weekends when Istanbul residents flood the beaches.

⏱ 60 min

Agva

★★☆☆☆
1

Its Latin name means 'village between two streams'—and that's exactly what Ağva is: wedged between the Göksu and Yeşilçay, both emptying into the Black Sea. Until the 1950s, the village shipped charcoal from Istanbul's forests. Today, tourist boats have replaced charcoal barges.

⏱ 30 min
2

The ochre cliffs of Kilimli drop straight into the Black Sea at the end of a short walk from the village. The panorama over both rivers and the coast is excellent—plan for sunset here. It's also a diving spot for the daring who jump from the rocks.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Rent a boat on the Göksu before noon—local captains offer hour-long tours among the reeds and willows. It's the most local way to see Ağva. In the evening, dine on fresh fish on the Yeşilçay side—that's where the most reputed seafood restaurants are.

⏱ 60 min

Until the 1950s, Ağva shipped charcoal to Istanbul from its forests. Canoes and pedal boats are available on both rivers to explore the reed-lined waterways at your own pace. Excursions to Hacıllı Waterfall (12 km into the forest) are done by local minibus.

⏱ 90 min

Kerpe

★★☆☆☆
1

Not spectacular, but these Quaternary-era cliffs dropping into the sea at Kerpe Kayalıkları have something honest about them. Kocaeli municipality has developed the waterfront with a row of cafés before the rocks. Young people dive from heights of 15 to 20 meters—guaranteed spectacle from the promenade.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

If you arrive on a weekday, the bay is nearly deserted—Istanbul and Kocaeli residents only invade the beaches on weekends. The water is calmer here than at Şile. The fine sand beach runs about 200 meters. Good transition stop before the wilder coast that begins at Akçakoca.

⏱ 60 min

Akcakoca

★★★☆☆
1

Listed on the UNESCO tentative list in 2013, Akçakoca's Genoese Castle (Ceneviz Kalesi) dominates the sea from its promontory. Of Roman and Byzantine origin, it was remodeled by the Genoese in the 13th century, then taken by the Ottomans in 1323. Behind it, coastal caves known as 'seal reefs' open at low tide.

⏱ 45 min
2

The blue of Genoese Bay contrasts with the brown of the castle perched above. Kale Beach, at the foot of the fortress itself, has earned Blue Flag status. On either side, caves along the coast lend themselves to coastal caving. Avoid July-August if you're seeking tranquility.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Head down to the fishing harbor at sunset—boats are coming in, fish restaurants come alive. Local cuisine relies on hazelnuts and chestnut honey from the Düzce region. Akçakoca is a transition stop before the wild coast toward Amasra—take advantage to eat well and simply.

⏱ 60 min

Konuralp

★★★★☆
1

They call it 'the Ephesus of the Black Sea'—and the theater of Prusias ad Hypium, 95% excavated, once held some 10,000 spectators. Dating from the 3rd century BC, its semicircular 100-meter tiers still bear relief sculptures of Apollo and Actaeon. Ongoing excavations regularly yield new finds.

⏱ 40 min
2

Founded in 1994, Konuralp Museum houses 1,788 archaeological pieces from Prusias ad Hypium excavations—including a mosaic of Orpheus, one of Achilles and Thetis, and a 1st-century sarcophagus. Small but dense. Closed Mondays; allow 30 to 45 minutes for a complete visit.

⏱ 40 min

Karadeniz Eregli

★★★☆☆
1

Local legend places Heracles' descent to the Underworld here to capture Cerberus. The Cehennemağzı caves—'the Mouth of Hell'—consist of three cavities, two carved by human hands and used as Christian sanctuaries in Roman times. Opened to the public in 2001; the geometric floor mosaics in the first cave are remarkable.

⏱ 50 min
2

An industrial city at first glance, but Heraclea Pontica hides beneath its streets the remains of a Greek colony founded around 560 BC. The harbor ramparts and citadel are partially accessible. Some sections remain in military zones—check before approaching.

Amasra

★★☆☆☆
1

Sea on both sides—that's your first impression arriving in Amasra by the winding road. The town stretches on an isthmus with a small harbor to the west and a large sandy harbor to the east. Founded in the 12th century BC under the name Sesamos, then renamed after the Persian princess Amastris. The castle connects two sections—Sormagir on Boztepe island and Zindan on the peninsula—via the Kemere Bridge.

⏱ 60 min
2

The ramparts reach 8 meters thick in places, reinforced by Roman, Byzantine, Genoese, and Ottoman bastions. The Kemere Bridge linking the two parts of the castle dates from Roman times and was restored in the 9th century. Climb the ramparts late in the day for the light over both harbors.

⏱ 45 min
3

Amasra Museum, opened in 1982, presents collections spanning Hellenistic, Roman, Byzantine, Genoese, and Ottoman periods. Small and honest. It's the logical complement to the ramparts—objects found on site are properly displayed. Allow 30 to 40 minutes.

⏱ 35 min

Activities

Stroll the old harbor on the west side late afternoon—fishing boats return, terraces come alive, the citadel rises above the houses. Amasra has preserved its charm precisely because it's remained off the major routes. Less concrete than elsewhere on the coast.

⏱ 60 min

If you arrive late in the day, the sand beach of the large harbor (east side) is the ideal place to end the stop. The water is calmer here than on the open coast. The beach remains accessible in the evening when the crowds disperse.

⏱ 45 min

Kurucasile

★★☆☆☆
1

Once a supplier of Ottoman galleons, Kurucaşile today shelters eight active shipyards in the villages of Ovatekkeönü and Kapısuyu. Under tarp shelters, master carpenters build yachts and fishing boats in chestnut, pine, and oak—without drawn plans, from memory. In 2021, a yard produced for the first time in 50 years a single-masted Ottoman-style boat. Come in the morning to see the teams at work.

⏱ 45 min
2

The village of Kurucaşile itself deserves a walk: modest fishing harbor, fishermen's houses facing an often-agitated sea, and the rocky coast extending eastward. It's one of the few stretches of the western Black Sea coast still preserved from tourist concrete. The scenic road between Amasra and Kurucaşile (55 km) is among the wildest of the route.

⏱ 30 min

Bartin

★★☆☆☆
1

Not on the tourist circuits, but these Art Nouveau and Baroque façades grafted onto two-story timber structures are unique to Bartın. Local houses (with their 'daraba' fences) reflect regional woodcraft on their pediments. Ancient city mentioned in the Iliad under the name Kromna.

⏱ 40 min
2

The Kemer Bridge, built in 1872, measures 42 meters and spans the Bartın River in the city center. It marks the boundary between the old town and modern quarters. Go down to the banks to see both sides from below—the city reads better from there.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Bartın's old market is worth an hour wandering among stalls of local products. The region is known for its honey and hazelnuts. More authentic than coastal tourist bazaars—locals do their shopping here. Look for chestnut honey vendors in the alleys behind the main mosque.

Safranbolu

★★★★☆
1

The scent of saffron and warm Turkish delight settles in from the first streets of Eski Çarşı—Safranbolu's old bazaar. More than 2,000 Ottoman houses, UNESCO-listed since 1994, shape this exceptional urban ensemble. Built with stone on the ground floor and half-timbered wood on upper floors, they never cast shadow on one another—a layout conceived back in the 17th century.

⏱ 60 min
2

Built in 1645, Cinci Han was a major caravanserai on the trade route between Istanbul and the Black Sea. Its inner courtyard surrounded by two-story arcades is now a boutique hotel, but visiting the building's heart remains free. It's the largest historic structure in town.

⏱ 30 min
3

Kaymakamlar Evi is more than an ordinary museum: this 18th-century house-museum shows the interior of a wealthy Ottoman family home in its original state—furniture, textiles, room organization by rank. Allow 30 to 40 minutes; the stone ground floor contrasts with the airy wooden upper floors.

⏱ 35 min
4

Climb to Hıdırlık Tepesi to see Safranbolu's red roofs spread through the valley, with the Clock Tower (Saat Kulesi, built in 1797) standing out. It's the classic viewpoint—and for good reason. Best early morning or late afternoon when the light is soft.

⏱ 25 min

Activities

If you have time, Cinci Hamamı has been operating since the 17th century. Classic Turkish bath, well restored. Ideal after a long day on the road. Reservations possible on site; allow 1h30 for the full circuit (scrub included).

⏱ 90 min

Copper and leather artisan workshops line the alleys of Eski Çarşı. Local saffron is packaged in small sachets—it's the emblematic souvenir of the town that gave it its name. Avoid shops on the central square, pricier: the best addresses are in side alleys.

⏱ 45 min

Yedigoller

★★★★☆
1

Seven lakes linked by wooden bridges—Yedigöller National Park is perhaps the most photogenic park in the western Black Sea. Declared a national park in 1965, it owes its name to seven basins—Büyükgöl, Deringöl, Séringöl, Nazlıgöl, Sazlıgöl, İncegöl, and Küçükgöl—formed by ancient landslides. Autumn (October-November) is peak season: beech and oak trees turn red and orange.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Follow the main trail that passes each lake and returns in a 2 to 3-hour loop. Artisan bridges span the small waterfalls and fountains between basins. Camping is possible on developed areas (Nazlıgöl)—but without electricity. In winter, the road from Bolu is closed by snow.

Bolu

★★★☆☆
1

Established in 1981, Bolu Museum displays archaeological and ethnographic collections spanning the Bronze Age, Byzantium, and the Ottoman period. Small and well-organized. Ideal for contextualizing the Konuralp and Yedigöller sites seen the day before. Closed Mondays.

⏱ 45 min
2

Five minutes from the center, Lake Gölcük surprises: set at 1,217 meters altitude in a nature park of beech and fir forests, it appears like an alpine version of Bolu. The Yalnız Ev—a solitary house by the water—is the park's iconic image, reproduced in all local tourist guides.

⏱ 50 min

Activities

The Yalnız Ev, a solitary house on the shore of Lake Gölcük, is the focal point of the park's main promenade. The trail around the lake takes about 1h30. Ideal in the morning for mist on the water. Bolu is known for its local gastronomy—Bolu köfte are a regional specialty.

Abant

★★★★☆
1

Deep blue against forests of pines, beeches, and oaks—Lake Abant at 1,328 meters altitude is one of northern Turkey's most celebrated lake landscapes. Formed by ancient tectonic processes, it's fed by springs. In winter, it's snow-covered and the road from Bolu is sometimes closed.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Rent a pedal boat and tour the lake from the main pier—or walk the peripheral trail (about 8 km, 2 hours of easy walking). Lakeside hotels offer breakfast with your feet in the grass. Leave early morning before weekend groups arrive.

Mudurnu

★★★★☆
1

Evliya Çelebi described it as fertile and famous for its vineyards, wooden spoons, and combs. Mudurnu is a Cittaslow (slow city) on the UNESCO tentative list. Hundreds of preserved Ottoman houses rise following the natural slope of the terrain—builders worked with topography, not against it.

⏱ 45 min
2

Yıldırım Bayezid Mosque, built in 1382 by Prince Bayezid (future Bayezid I), is part of a complex with hammam and madrasha from the same year—one of the region's oldest Ottoman ensembles. The hammam is still in service. Free entry to the mosque.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Saturday bazaar is truer than many coastal tourist markets. Local products, wooden crafts, regional cheeses. Ahi culture—a religious and mystical corporation practiced here for centuries—still manifests in a bread-throwing ritual on Fridays.

Goynuk

★★★☆☆
1

Fatih Sultan Mehmet had it built in 1464 for his teacher—Akşemseddin Türbesi honors the spiritual master who accompanied the conquest of Constantinople in 1453. The hexagonal ashlar stone building is in the courtyard of Gazi Süleyman Paşa Mosque. Free entry, open daily from 8:30am to 5:30pm.

⏱ 30 min
2

Half-timbered façades rise in Göynük's green valley like pages from an Ottoman architecture manual. A Cittaslow like Mudurnu, the town has preserved a 14th-century urban texture. Gazi Süleyman Paşa Camii, built between 1331 and 1335 by Orhan Gazi's son, is one of the oldest Ottoman mosques in the region.

⏱ 35 min
3

Slow city (Cittaslow) but history here isn't slow at all—Göynük is nicknamed 'Diyar-ı Akşemseddin'—land of Akşemseddin—and annual commemoration ceremonies in May draw pilgrims from the entire region. Houses from the Anatolian Beyliks period constitute rare testimony from that transition era.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Climb to the Victory Tower (Zafer Kulesi), a three-story wooden tower erected in memory of the Battle of Sakarya (1923). Access is free and the view over the Ottoman houses and valley is the town's best. The climb is steep but short—allow 15 minutes round trip.

⏱ 20 min

Tarakli

★★★☆☆
1

If you're looking for Safranbolu without the tourists, you've found Taraklı. Over 100 registered cultural properties, Cittaslow since 2011, European Destinations of Excellence Award 2013—and almost no one in the cobbled alleys. Traveler Evliya Çelebi in the 17th century already described it as 'fertile, famous for its vineyards, spoons, and combs.'

⏱ 50 min
2

Yunus Paşa Mosque, built in 1517 by Grand Vizier Yunus Paşa and designed by Mimar Sinan, is Taraklı's largest building. Locals call it 'Kurşunlu Camii' (Lead Mosque) because of the lead used in its construction. It had a heating system powered by the adjacent hammam—a rarity for the era.

⏱ 25 min

Activities

The colorful bazaar still sells wooden combs—the very name Taraklı comes from the word 'tarakçı' (comb seller). It's one of the few craft specialties to have survived in its city of origin. Timber houses restored as guesthouses offer the route's most authentic overnight stays.

Sapanca

★★☆☆☆
1

Lake Sapanca: a vast green and blue expanse between Sakarya's hills. It's also one of the Kocaeli region's main drinking water reserves. About 80 bird species have been recorded here. Along its northern shore, the road to Maşukiye is one of the route's greenest.

⏱ 40 min
2

Maşukiye comes from the Arabic word for 'love'—which explains its reputation as a honeymoon destination. The mountain village is wedged between forests and waterfalls, 10 km from Sapanca. Terrace restaurants above streams serve extended Turkish breakfast among the trees.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Lunch facing Mahmudiye waterfalls and breathe—this is the decompression stop before İznik. The waterfalls are accessible from a small parking lot at the hamlet entrance; the path is short but steep. Several overlooking restaurants offer river fish menus.

Iznik

★★★★☆
1

These ramparts have stood for 17 centuries—you won't forget them. İznik's Byzantine fortifications encircle the old town for about 5 km, with 114 towers and four major gates (Istanbul, Lefke, Izmit, Yalova). The inner wall, erected toward the end of the 3rd century under Claudius II, stands 9 meters high. Some sections are under restoration—notably the Istanbul Gate.

⏱ 50 min
2

Orhan Cami was first a 4th-century church—tradition holds that the Seventh Ecumenical Council was held here. Converted to a mosque, then a museum, then back to a mosque in 2020 under the name İznik Hagia Sophia, it retains marble mosaics at the entrance and barely visible fresco fragments. Prayer is central; all around, visitors circulate freely.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Green Mosque's minaret: dark green glazed tiles—the direct ancestors of the İznik ceramics that conquered the Ottoman Empire. Erected on commission by Çandarlı Halil Paşa, vizier to Sultan Murat, it's one of the earliest examples of Ottoman architecture. The elegant white marble porch contrasts with the nave's sobriety.

⏱ 25 min
4

World-famous for its ceramics, but few travelers know the production had almost disappeared. İznik Vakfı workshops relaunched the tradition from the 1990s. The municipal museum displays authentic 16th-17th century Ottoman examples—the quality difference from current copies is immediately visible.

⏱ 35 min

Activities

If you're passing in summer, Lake İznik, the largest in the Marmara region, offers natural access from the shore for swimming. The water is fresh and clean. In 2014, a 4th-century Byzantine basilica was discovered just 20 meters from the shore, collapsed during the 740 earthquake.

⏱ 60 min

Several workshops in town have perpetuated Ottoman İznik ceramics since the 1990s. İznik Vakfı shops are the most reliable for distinguishing real quartz-based ceramics from industrial imitations. An introductory tile-painting session is possible by reservation.

⏱ 60 min
