Muenster

★★★☆☆
1

In 1648, inside the Friedenssaal of the historic Rathaus, delegates from across Europe brought thirty years of war to a close — the room is smaller than you'd expect, and that's precisely what catches you off guard. The 37 portraits of sovereigns and envoys, purchased by the council as early as 1649, still gaze down from oak-panelled walls dating to 1577. Entry €3; closed Monday mornings.

⏱ 45 min
2

The scent of fresh pretzels drifts up from the market at Domplatz, just behind the Prinzipalmarkt. This arcaded street — known to locals as the Gute Stube, the city's front parlour — runs 300 metres between the Rathaus to the south and the Lambertikirche to the north. The Giebelhäuser, destroyed in 1945, were rebuilt in Baumberge sandstone between 1947 and 1958. Come early in the morning, before the delivery vans arrive.

⏱ 40 min
3

Three iron cages hang from the tower of the Lambertikirche — in these late-Gothic cages, the bodies of executed Anabaptist leaders were put on public display in 1536. The church itself is free to enter, and the organ alone is worth a stop. The contrast between the beauty of the space and the darkness of its history is quietly unsettling.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Pick up a bike at the central station: Münster is one of Germany's most cycle-friendly cities. The loop through the Schlossgarten, past Schlaun's princely palace and the botanical garden, takes under two hours. Stay alert on shared paths — local cyclists don't slow down for anyone.

⏱ 120 min

If you're passing through on a Wednesday or Saturday morning, the Domplatz market is unmissable: fruit, Westphalian cheeses, and cured meats right in front of the cathedral. Arrive before 10am for the best picks. Stalls are gone by 1pm.

⏱ 60 min

Havixbeck

★★☆☆☆
1

Locals say she thought like a man and suffered like a woman — Annette von Droste-Hülshoff was born here in 1797, in this Renaissance moated castle, and spent the first thirty years of her life within its walls. Burg Hülshoff is now a literary museum with six original rooms, a library, and family portraits. The restaurant in the vaulted cellars is open from April to November.

⏱ 75 min
2

The seven-kilometre Lyrikweg links Burg Hülshoff to Haus Rüschhaus near Münster, tracing a path Annette von Droste-Hülshoff walked hundreds of times. Outdoor poetry stations, hedgerows, open fields — an open-air museum without gates or crowds. Best walked in the late afternoon.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

After your visit, stop in at Droste Café 1797 in the castle's vaulted cellar — regional cakes and a view over the moat. Check the Burg website for ongoing restoration works: some areas may be closed depending on the phase.

⏱ 30 min

Luedinghausen

★★★☆☆
1

Built in 1271 on a sandbar in the meanders of the Stever river, Burg Vischering is one of the oldest and best-preserved castles in the Münsterland. A 16th-century fire led to a Renaissance rebuild that has remained largely untouched ever since. The museum offers a combined Vorburg + Hauptburg ticket for €7.50.

⏱ 75 min
2

Defensive and austere on the outside — and yet the park between Lüdinghausen's two castles, open since 2018, reveals a bucolic path shaded by eight-hundred-year-old trees, a stork's nest to the north, and tiny bridges crossing the Stever. Ten minutes on foot separates Burg Vischering from Burg Lüdinghausen.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Follow the moat path to Burg Lüdinghausen along the Kapitelweg, lined with busts of churchmen — an atmosphere somewhere between the grotesque and the serene. Allow ten minutes on foot. Burg Lüdinghausen hosts cultural events but its interior is not open to visitors.

⏱ 30 min

Nordkirchen

★★★☆☆
1

You might smile at the nickname 'the Westphalian Versailles' — and then you pull up in front of Schloss Nordkirchen and immediately understand it. This water-encircled Baroque castle, designed by Pictorius and completed by Schlaun, is the largest moated castle in Westphalia. Since 1959 it has housed a finance school of the state of North Rhine-Westphalia. Guided tours run on Sundays and public holidays.

⏱ 60 min
2

Twenty-three radiating avenues fan out from Schloss Nordkirchen through a formal French-style park. The reconstructed Venus Island, the sculptures punctuating the parterres, the right-angled canals — a geometry that borders on the dizzying. The park is free to enter.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Rent a bike at the park entrance: Nordkirchen has marked cycling paths through the Münsterland bocage. A full circuit of the estate takes about an hour. Avoid weekends when open-air concerts bring in the crowds.

⏱ 60 min

Tecklenburg

★★★☆☆
1

The Burgruine Tecklenburg dates to around 1100 — Count Konrad von Tecklenburg-Schwerin expanded it in the 16th century. Today the ruins serve as the stage for the Tecklenburger Freilichtspiele, one of the most popular open-air theatre festivals in northern Germany. On a clear day, the views stretch across the Münsterland for up to 80 kilometres.

⏱ 45 min
2

Pink sandstone lanes wind through Tecklenburg's Altstadt — a town its residents call the northernmost settlement in Germany with an alpine character. The Puppenmuseum, housed in a half-timbered building from 1684, holds one of the richest collections of antique dolls in North Rhine-Westphalia.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

If the Tecklenburger Freilichtspiele are running (June to August), book well in advance — the musicals staged among the ruins sell out weeks ahead. Outside festival season, the Dörenther Klippen and the 'Hockendes Weib' rock formation offer an hour-long hike from the old town.

⏱ 60 min

Osnabrueck

★★★☆☆
1

The Rathaus in Osnabrück was the second seat of the Westphalian Peace negotiations — while Münster hosted talks with the Dutch and French, Osnabrück dealt with Sweden and the Holy Roman Empire. The decisive handshake came here in August 1648, before the formal signing in Münster that October. The ground-floor Schatzkammer displays a replica of the treaties. Open Tuesday to Sunday.

⏱ 50 min
2

Remarque and Nussbaum — two pacifists born here in the last century — give Osnabrück a depth its architecture doesn't always reveal. The Felix-Nussbaum-Haus at the MQ4 museum — designed by Daniel Libeskind and opened in 1998 — presents the work of the Jewish painter who died at Auschwitz. Admission €8.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Download the free 'Entdecke Osnabrück' app before you set out: two 2.5 km routes, guided by historical characters, cover the 23 key sites of this city of peace. The Friedenstour includes the medieval Bucksturm, the synagogue, and the Rathaus.

⏱ 90 min

Detmold

★★★☆☆
1

The LWL-Freilichtmuseum in Detmold is Germany's largest open-air museum: 90 hectares, more than 120 buildings dismantled from their original locations and rebuilt here, spanning 500 years of Westphalian architecture from peasant farmsteads to urban workshops. A 1960s petrol station turned museum piece sits alongside 16th-century farmhouses. Open Easter to October, Tuesday to Sunday.

⏱ 180 min
2

Smoke rises from the blacksmith's forge inside a half-timbered building — at the LWL-Freilichtmuseum, smiths, bakers, and potters work for real in the historic workshops. Daily demonstrations run on no fixed schedule: come in the morning to catch an artisan at work. Senner-Pferde, a rare horse breed, graze freely within the museum grounds.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Residenzschloss in Detmold, built in the Weser Renaissance style, is still the residence of the princely zu Lippe family. Its façade and clock tower dominate the Altstadt skyline. The state apartments can be visited on guided tours by arrangement — the interior is well worth the effort.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Start early and set aside two to four hours at the LWL-Freilichtmuseum — a full circuit of the site covers 9 kilometres. Horse-drawn carriages run between the entrance and the Paderborner Dorf. Three historic on-site restaurants mean you can have lunch without leaving the museum.

⏱ 240 min

If the Saturday market is on, Detmold hosts a weekly market on the Altmarkt with regional produce from the Lippe area. The nearby Strate's Brauhaus has been serving its local beer since 1863 — a solid choice for dinner.

⏱ 60 min

Hermannsdenkmal

★★★★☆
1

Fifty-three and a half metres of triumphant Germanic warrior — the Hermannsdenkmal on the Grotenburg is Germany's tallest statue. Before its inauguration in 1875, it was also the tallest in the Western world. Ernst von Bandel devoted 40 years of his life to the project, completed the year before his death. The viewing platform costs €5 to climb.

⏱ 60 min
2

From the top, the Teutoburg Forest stretches away in every direction — on a clear day, the hills above Bielefeld are visible on the horizon. The monument commemorates Arminius's victory over the Roman legions of Varus in 9 AD, in this very forest. The Waldbühne amphitheatre at the base of the hill hosts concerts from June to August.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Walk back down to the car park via the Hermannsweg footpath from the monument: the dense forest, the reddish sandstone trails, and the geology information boards make this twenty-minute descent a real counterpoint to the monument above. The adjacent Teuto-Kletterpark offers treetop adventure courses for families.

⏱ 45 min

Externsteine

★★★★☆
1

Whether you read them as mystical or purely geological, the Externsteine have captivated every traveller who has passed this way. Thirteen sandstone pillars rise 40 metres above a pond in the Wiembecke valley, and a Romanesque Descent from the Cross relief carved directly into the rock — first documented in 1129 — still stands in place. Carved stone stairways lead to a panoramic platform at the summit.

⏱ 75 min
2

A Romanesque Descent from the Cross carved between 1115 and 1130 into the Externsteine sandstone depicts Christ at life size — one of the rare surviving examples of Romanesque open-air sculpture still in its original position in Germany. Whether the site was a Germanic place of worship, a Christian pilgrimage site, or both remains a matter of scholarly debate.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Arrive early — crowds build significantly around midday and into the afternoon at this popular site. The circular one-hour trail around the rock formations offers the best angles. Climbing ticket €5 for adults, €2.50 for children aged 6 to 17.

⏱ 60 min

★★★☆☆
1

Corvey — Westphalia's only UNESCO World Heritage site to date — was founded in 822 on the orders of Louis the Pious. Its Carolingian Westwerk has stood intact since the 9th century and earned the UNESCO designation in 2014. In the princely library of some 75,000 volumes, Hoffmann von Fallersleben was working as librarian when he wrote the Deutschland-Lied in 1841.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Weser shimmers behind Corvey's Carolingian towers — the site is a two-kilometre walk from the centre of Höxter along the river. Along the way, Höxter's Altstadt lines up its Weser Renaissance half-timbered houses between the stones of the old town walls.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Walk the two kilometres from the town centre to Corvey along the Weser — flat, well-marked, following the Weser-Radweg cycle path. The return by bike takes ten minutes. On site, the medieval Remtergarten herb garden is free to enter.

Bodenwerder

★★★★☆
1

Locals still call him the Lügenbaron — the lying baron. Hieronymus Carl Friedrich von Münchhausen was born here on 11 May 1720, in what is now Bodenwerder's town hall. The Münchhausen-Museum next door tells both stories: the real military man who served the Russian tsarina, and the fictional fabulist whose tall tales were published without his knowledge or consent.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Münchhausen-Museum, housed in the medieval Schulenburg (c. 1300) of the family estate, exhibits first editions of the baron's adventures. The fountain outside the town hall depicts the famous scene of Münchhausen and his half-horse emerging from the swamp — an image so absurd it somehow captures the spirit of the whole town.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

On any Sunday from May to October, catch the Münchhausen-Spiel on the steps of the town hall: local amateur actors have been performing the baron's adventures here on the first Sunday of the month for over twenty years. Free entry, and reliably good fun.

⏱ 60 min

Hameln

★★★☆☆
1

The Pied Piper legend dates to 1284 — and in Hameln, you won't be allowed to forget it. But beneath the commercial folklore lies a genuine town: Hameln's Altstadt is one of the finest concentrations of Weser Renaissance architecture in Germany, with the 1617 Hochzeitshaus (43 metres of unbroken façade) as its centrepiece. The carillon plays daily at 1:05pm, 3:35pm, and 5:35pm.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Rattenfängerhaus, built in 1602, takes its name from an inscription on its flank recalling the events of 1284. It's now a restaurant — but the Weser Renaissance façade makes the exterior more than worth a look. The adjacent Museum Hameln traces the legend through medieval documentary sources.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Follow the line of rat silhouettes set into the cobblestones along Bäckerstraße — a discreet trail that guides you through the entire Altstadt. In summer (mid-May to mid-September), the Rattenfänger-Freilichtspiel on Sundays at noon draws sixty costumed performers. The best way to experience Hameln without getting lost in the tourist crowd.

⏱ 90 min

Bad Karlshafen

★★★☆☆
1

Immaculate white townhouses mirror themselves in the Baroque harbour basin — Bad Karlshafen is a planned town, drafted in one stroke and commissioned in 1699. Landgrave Charles I of Hesse wanted to shelter French Huguenots expelled by Louis XIV, and to bypass the toll at Hann. Münden. The canal intended to link it to the Rhine was never completed: only 17 kilometres were ever dug.

⏱ 30 min
2

Founded in 1699 by Charles I of Hesse under the name Sieburg, Bad Karlshafen sits at the precise confluence of the Diemel and the Weser. The Huguenot Museum, housed in a former cigar factory, traces the exile of French Protestants from 1685 onward. Free admission on the first Sunday of the month.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Head up to the Weser-Skywalk, a 3km walk from the centre: this platform on the Hannoversche Klippen cliffs offers a view across three German states simultaneously — Hesse, North Rhine-Westphalia, and Lower Saxony. It's also accessible by car, with a small car park nearby.

⏱ 60 min

Sababurg

★★★★☆
1

Built in 1334 under the name Zapfenburg to protect pilgrims on the road to Gottsbüren, the Sababurg was later transformed into a hunting lodge by Landgrave William I from 1490 onward. Between 1571 and 1591, a thorn hedge three metres high and five kilometres long encircled it — the very image said to have inspired the Brothers Grimm for Sleeping Beauty. The state of Hesse has been restoring the castle since 2018.

⏱ 45 min
2

A rose garden surrounds the ruined towers of the Sababurg, with rare species and climbing plants weaving through the medieval remains. In summer (Friday to Sunday), open-air theatre performances bring Grimm fairy tales to life in the castle courtyard. The atmosphere is oddly enchanting — part building site, part fairy tale.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The Tierpark Sababurg, open since 1571 and one of the oldest wildlife parks in Europe, is home to European bison, lynx, and wild horses roaming the Reinhardswald. Directly adjacent to the castle, it's the natural companion to your visit. Allow two hours.

⏱ 120 min

Hannoversch Muenden

★★★☆☆
1

Alexander von Humboldt reportedly called it one of the seven most beautifully situated towns in the world — and even if that quote is apocryphal, you understand it immediately here. Hann. Münden is a half-timbered town of more than 700 buildings, ringed by forests and set at the junction of the Werra, the Fulda, and the newborn Weser.

⏱ 60 min
2

On the Tanzwerder island, the Werra and Fulda merge into the Weser at the foot of the 1899 Weserstein — a small obelisk bearing an inscription quoted in every German geography textbook. The Welfenschloss overlooks the confluence from the bank, its Weser Renaissance structure still standing intact.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Take the river boat towards Kassel if the timetable allows: seasonal services on the Weser connect Hann. Münden to Reinhardshagen and sometimes beyond. Otherwise, follow the Weser-Radweg south by bike as far as the wooded banks of Reinhardshagen — 12 kilometres, flat all the way.

⏱ 90 min

Kassel

★★★★★
1

Bergpark Wilhelmshöhe, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2013, is the largest hillside park in Europe at 560 hectares, climbing the slopes of the Habichtswald. The 8.25-metre Hercules statue crowns an octagonal tower at 596 metres, visible from across the city. The Baroque water features — a cascading waterfall descending 250 metres from the Hercules to the palace below — run every Wednesday and Sunday from May to October.

⏱ 120 min
2

Schloss Wilhelmshöhe, at the foot of the park, was the summer residence of the Hessian landgraves and later of German emperors after 1866. Today it houses the Antikensammlung and one of the finest collections of Flemish and Dutch paintings in Germany. Closed Mondays.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Fridericianum dates to 1779 and is widely considered the first purpose-built public museum on the European continent. Since 1955 it has been the centrepiece of documenta, widely regarded as the world's most important contemporary art exhibition. The next edition — documenta 16 — is scheduled from 12 June to 19 September 2027. Even between editions, the temporary shows are worth the visit.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Water rushes down 250 metres of Baroque cascades from the Hercules to the Grand Lake every Wednesday and Sunday from May to October, between 2:30pm and 4pm. The geyser shoots 50 metres into the air mid-show — absurd and magnificent in equal measure. Position yourself near the Hercules at the start to watch the water flow down the entire length.

⏱ 120 min

If the water features aren't running or the weather discourages the climb to the Hercules, the Altstadt quarter and the Karlsaue riverside park offer a full alternative day — the Brothers Grimm museum, the Baroque Orangerie, and walks along the Fulda.

⏱ 120 min
