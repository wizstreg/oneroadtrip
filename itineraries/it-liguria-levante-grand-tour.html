Day by day

1

Genova

Where 42 UNESCO palaces line streets built for emperors and kings
★★★★☆
1

Welcome to Genoa—La Superba, as Petrarch christened it in the 14th century, and the nickname hasn't aged a day. Dive straight into the Porto Antico, redesigned in 1992 by Renzo Piano (who's Genoese) for the 500th anniversary of Christopher Columbus's departure. The Aquarium is Italy's largest: 13,000 animals, 400 species, minimum two hours if you want to do it justice. Buy timed-entry tickets online, otherwise expect 30-minute queues in summer. Next door, Renzo Piano's Biosfera—the glass sphere floating on the water—houses a mini tropical rainforest.

⏱ 150 min
2

San Lorenzo Cathedral is instantly recognizable by its alternating bands of black and white marble and the two stone lions guarding the entrance. Built from the 9th century onward, with a 13th-century Gothic façade featuring a remarkable rose window. Inside the right nave, an unsettling detail: a replica of a 381mm British shell that fell on the church in February 1941 and failed to detonate. The Genoese preserved it as a memorial to the bombings. The Treasury Museum in the basement is also worth visiting: you'll see the Sacro Catino, a green glass dish long believed to be the Holy Grail.

⏱ 60 min
3

Via Garibaldi, formerly Strada Nuova or Via Aurea (Golden Street), is Europe's most magnificent 200-meter stretch. Forty-two palaces inscribed on UNESCO's list in 2006, three of which are open to the public on this street: Palazzo Rosso, Palazzo Bianco, and Palazzo Doria Tursi. Original system: from 1576, by Senate decree, these private mansions were drawn by lottery from lists (rolli) to host the Republic's state guests. Pope, emperor, king: each got a palace according to rank. Rubens lodged here between 1604 and 1606 and published a book of building plans in 1622 featuring the architecture that had impressed him.

⏱ 120 min
4

The caruggi are the medieval historic center—one of the vastest in Europe. Alleys so narrow that two people brush shoulders passing each other, you enter by chance and emerge later than planned. Historic shops with wooden signs, tripe vendors, 19th-century grocers, bookbinding workshops. Use caution at night in very isolated lanes, as in any major port city, but the atmosphere remains surprisingly lively thanks to the mix of students, immigrants, and locals. Look for the Sottoripa, the market arcade along the port dating from 1133—Italy's oldest.

⏱ 120 min
5

Boccadasse is Genoa's best-kept secret: an old fishing village swallowed by the city but never truly absorbed. Small houses in salmon, ochre, and Naples yellow arranged in a crescent around a pebble beach, 20 minutes on foot from the center via the Corso Italia promenade. Gelato is mandatory at Gelateria Amedeo, right on the beach: a century-old tradition. Five minutes uphill, Capo Santa Chiara offers the best panoramic view over Genoa. Go in late afternoon when the light gilds the façades.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Gastronomic tour of the caruggi. Here you taste real mortar-ground pesto, focaccia pulled at 5 a.m., farinata emerging from a wood-fired oven on copper sheet, and walnut pansoti. Several local guides organize tastings in historic shops (around €60 per person, 3 hours, six or seven stops). Antica Friggitoria Carega—fried fish in a paper cone, since 1908. At Pietro Romanengo, confectioner since 1780, buy the crystallized rose petals.

⏱ 180 min

If it's a May or September weekend, you might catch the Rolli Days. Twice yearly, about forty private palaces (which are never otherwise open) throw open their doors—for free. Art history students from the University of Genoa serve as guides. Entering the Galleria Dorata of Palazzo Tobia Pallavicino or the Sala dello Specchio of Palazzo Spinola means seeing the Italy you've always imagined. Calendar at rolliestradenuove.it.

⏱ 120 min

Nervi

A seaside garden district where clifftop promenades meet Belle Époque elegance
★★★★☆
1

The Anita Garibaldi Promenade, 2 km long, runs along the cliff face from Nervi's little port to the Capolungo cove. The Turismo.it portal once called it the most beautiful promenade in the world—an exaggeration, but not by much. Opened in 1862 by Marquis Gaetano Gropallo on a path fishermen and farmers had used since 1823. Renamed for Anita, Garibaldi's wife, on June 19, 1945. Red brick paving, perfectly flat, benches everywhere, stairs descending to rocks where you can swim. Best in late afternoon for sunsets over the Portofino promontory.

⏱ 90 min
2

Halfway along the promenade stands Torre Gropallo, also called Torre del Fieno (Hay Tower)—because hay was burned there to signal Barbary pirate arrivals by smoke. Built in 1547, square base, four stories, lookout terrace at the summit. One of the best-preserved examples of medieval military architecture around Genoa, integrated into the Republic's coastal defense system. Occasionally open for exhibitions, but the panorama from the terrace alone justifies climbing the stairs when possible.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Parks of Nervi, 92,000 m² of gardens between via Aurelia and the sea, are the district's other jewel. Four former private parks (Villa Gropallo, Villa Saluzzo Serra, Villa Grimaldi Fassio, Villa Luxoro) merged into one and passed to the city. Nearly a hundred botanical species, palms, cedars, and the Luigi Viacava rose garden restored in 2012—over 200 varieties, best seen in spring. Avoid midday in July: little shade in the rose garden. Free entry.

⏱ 90 min
4

Four modern art museums sleep within the parks' historic villas. The Galleria d'Arte Moderna (Villa Saluzzo Serra) houses Ligurian and Italian art from the 19th-20th centuries, with emphasis on Impressionism and Futurism. The Raccolte Frugone (Villa Grimaldi Fassio) showcase the collection of the Frugone brothers, Genoese merchants passionate about 19th-century painting. The Wolfsoniana, more unexpected, is devoted to decorative arts between 1880 and 1945—furniture, posters, propaganda. Allow two hours for the whole set. Combined ticket around €10.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Swimming in the promenade's coves. Several staircases descend from the path to rocky platforms: deep water, transparent, pale rock bottom. No sandy beach—it's rockface dropping straight down—but that's exactly what makes the experience local. Italians come with masks and snorkels and towels, have aperitivo at 6 p.m. on the rocks. Some natural pools between the boulders. Plastic shoes recommended.

⏱ 120 min

Aperitivo on the promenade at sunset. Several bar-terraces follow one another, including the historic Bar Anita 17 (local institution). Spritz at €7-8, warm focaccia, view of Portofino silhouetted on the horizon. If the air is clear, you'll see Corsica. Locals come in late afternoon—mass tourism never really invaded Nervi, it's remained an elegant residential district.

⏱ 60 min

Camogli

Trompe-l'oeil towers in sunset hues standing guard over fishing boats
★★★☆☆
1

First glimpse of Camogli from the sea or the corniche: a wall of seven- or eight-story houses painted in Naples yellow, ochre, and terracotta pink, with their feet in the water. Trompe-l'oeil everywhere on the façades—pediments, pilasters, false windows, false balconies—painted to imitate the architectural ornaments of palaces at lower cost (the façades are plaster and brick, not carved stone), and to signal the house to sailors returning from offshore: each family had its palette. The village name supposedly comes from casa moglie, the house of wives: while the men sailed for months, the women ran the village. Park up top at piazza Matteotti, descend on foot to the port in 10 minutes.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Basilica of Santa Maria Assunta has dominated the little port since the 12th century, remodeled several times, current façade from the 19th. Late Baroque interior, polychrome marbles, a silver Saint Fortunato (town patron) carried in procession on the second Sunday of May. The evening before, the village lights traditional fires on the beach representing the old Porto and Pineto quarters. And on Sunday, it's the Sagra del Pesce: three tons of fish fried in a 6-meter diameter pan, entered in the Guinness Book of Records.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Abbey of San Fruttuoso is the day's highlight. Wedged at the back of a cove between Camogli and Portofino, it's accessible only on foot (2 hours' walk from Camogli via San Rocco) or by boat (30 minutes, €15 round-trip). Legend: in the 8th century, Greek monks supposedly brought the relics of Saint Fructuosus of Tarragona, a Catalan bishop burned alive under Valerian in 259, while fleeing the Arab conquest of Spain. Rebuilt around the year 1000 by Adelaide, widow of Emperor Otto I. In the 13th century, the Dorias added their family tomb—the famous tombs in black and white marble bands you see on the ground floor. Medio-Byzantine dome adorned with 17 arcades.

⏱ 90 min
4

At 17 meters depth in the bay of San Fruttuoso rests the Christ of the Abyss. A 2.5-meter bronze statue, arms raised in blessing, submerged on August 22, 1954 at the initiative of diver Duilio Marcante in memory of his friend Dario Gonzatti, the first Italian to use autonomous diving equipment, who died there four years earlier. Sculpted by Guido Galletti, cast from donated bells and naval elements. Restored in 2003-2004 after corrosion tore off a hand. Dive requires Level 1 minimum. Non-divers can see a replica in the church.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hike from Camogli to San Rocco to Punta Chiappa. 1.5 hours one way, moderate elevation gain, through Portofino Regional Park: pine forests, olive groves, old churches like San Nicolò di Capodimonte. Punta Chiappa is a flat tongue of rock jutting into the sea—you swim there, you picnic. A small ferry goes back up to Camogli for those who don't want to walk back. Good shoes, water, early departure in summer.

⏱ 240 min

Taste the focaccia di Recco, just 4 kilometers away. Not the usual Ligurian focaccia—this one's unleavened, two very thin sheets of dough filled with fresh stracchino, baked at 320°C, crispy outside, molten inside. IGP certified since 2015. Manuelina in Recco is the historic institution (since 1885). Expect €12-15 per portion. Better to get there by train from Camogli (5 minutes).

⏱ 90 min

Portofino

The jewel-box harbor where umbrella pines frame Mediterranean dreams
★★★★☆
1

Portofino's Piazzetta is the Riviera's most photographed open-air salon. About twenty tables, historic cafés, Hermès and Dior boutiques behind, and facing them the horseshoe-shaped harbor bordered by tall houses painted in ochre, pink, and terracotta. Yachts on one side, fishing boats on the other. Documented in Roman times as Portus Delphini (port of dolphins)—the center still preserves the Roman grid. Coffee at Sandro's is €10, fair warning. Better to come off-season or at 9 a.m. before the cruise buses.

⏱ 60 min
2

Climb the stairs to the left of the port up to the Church of San Giorgio. Built around the 12th century, supposedly received relics of Saint George from Crusaders—patron of sailors, naturally. Destroyed by bombing in 1944, rebuilt identically in 1950. The small adjacent terrace offers a less-photographed but far more beautiful view than the piazzetta, especially in late afternoon. Five minutes on foot from the port, the bell tower visible from everywhere.

⏱ 30 min
3

Five minutes above San Giorgio, Castello Brown crowns the hill. Medieval Genoese fortress, expanded in the 16th century, purchased in 1867 by Montague Yeats Brown—British consul in Genoa—who transformed it into a private residence. Three years later, on his wedding day, he planted with his own hands the two umbrella pines that still dominate Portofino from its terraces, now the castle's symbol. Repurchased by the municipality in 1961. Today a museum, art exhibitions, weddings. Admission €5. The garden (free) suffices for the photo.

⏱ 60 min
4

At the tip of the promontory, 40 meters above the sea, the Punta del Capo lighthouse. 30 minutes' walk from the castle via an easy path through Mediterranean scrub—pines, agaves, wild rosemary. The lighthouse itself doesn't allow visits, but a tiny bar-terrace at its feet lets you have a drink suspended over the void, view across the entire Gulf of Tigullio. On clear days, you glimpse Corsica. Go in late afternoon for sunsets—you're facing due west.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Snorkeling in the Portofino Promontory Marine Protected Area, classified since 1999. Limestone and conglomerate rock plunge steeply, red gorgonians, posidonia meadows, small groupers, sars, salps. Several coves accessible from the trail (Cala dell'Oro), or guided dive to the Christ of the Abyss from Portofino dive centers (around €80, Level 1 required).

⏱ 180 min

Swimming at Paraggi, 1 kilometer before Portofino on the road from Santa Margherita. Small fine-sand beach (very rare in Liguria) nestled in a semicircular cove, uniquely turquoise water on the coast. Part is free (no charge, narrow), the rest paid via bagni—expect €30 to €80 per lounger depending on establishment. By train + bus 82, or via the Passeggiata dei Baci coastal path from Santa Margherita (1h).

⏱ 180 min

Santa Margherita Ligure

Belle Époque grandeur wrapped around a bay of red shrimp and baroque villas
★★★★☆
1

Villa Durazzo is Genoese Baroque elegance condensed into three hectares of park overlooking the Gulf of Tigullio. Built in the 17th century by the Marquises Durazzo, still furnished with antiques, frescoed ceilings, Murano chandeliers, Ligurian graniglia floors. Look for the Genoese quadreria (painting collection) on the piano nobile. The park itself is free: historic citrus grove with rare varieties—the 18th-century Bizzarria bitter orange, nearly extinct elsewhere in Italy. The English-style Wood, romantic and slightly disordered, conceals the Secret Garden of the Centurione Princes, a 19th-century lovers' refuge. Villa admission €6.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Abbey of Cervara clings to the rock between Santa Margherita and Portofino, above the SP 227. Founded in 1361 by Genoese monk Ottone Lanfranco. Benedictine, then fortified in 1546 against Saracen raids (the Saracen tower is still there, set back out of deference to the sacred). The great historical fact: Francis I of France, captured after the Battle of Pavia in 1525, was detained here one night during his transfer to Spain. Petrarch supposedly also stopped here. Today the only Italian-style garden in Liguria perched over the sea—boxwood trimmed into cones, 17th-century marble fountain with putto. Visits only on 1st and 3rd Sundays of the month from March to October, at 10am, 11am, and noon. Admission €15, reservation required.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Castle of Santa Margherita was built in 1550 by architect Antonio De Càrabo to defend the town from Barbary pirates who terrorized the entire coast. Small, squat, set directly on the seafront, it's taken on a decorative function today. Not much to see inside (reserved for civil weddings), but the silhouette matters: it's the postcard image of Santa Margherita. Photograph at sunrise, facing due east.

⏱ 30 min
4

The Basilica of Saint Margaret of Antioch is the town's religious center, set on the main square. 17th-century Genoese Baroque façade, interior all in polychrome marbles, gilding, and a parvis in risseu—the traditional Ligurian mosaic of black and white pebbles. Risseu appears everywhere in Liguria but this one is among the most accomplished: floral motifs, maritime scenes. The floor work is almost as valuable as the façade.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Walk on the Passeggiata dei Baci (Promenade of Kisses), 8.5 km flat along the seafront from Santa Margherita to Portofino. Umbrella pines, permanent view across the Gulf of Tigullio, sumptuous properties hidden behind gates. Walk out, return by bus 82 or boat (€5 from Portofino). Allow 2 hours without breaks. Very shaded—possible even in midsummer.

⏱ 180 min

Dinner of gambero rosso di Santa Margherita, the large local red shrimp, fished from the submarine canyon beneath the gulf between 400 and 700 meters depth. Sweet, almost raw, eaten with a drizzle of oil and pepper. The town's absolute specialty. Several trattorias around the port serve it—Skipper and Oca Bianca for the classics. Expect €35-50 for a main course.

⏱ 120 min

Rapallo

Where a castle floats on water and Italy's only cable car climbs to sanctuary
★★★★☆
1

Strange castle, this one in Rapallo: it truly has its feet in the water. A small square fortification planted 30 meters from the shore, connected to the promenade by a slender mole. Built in 1549 after the spectacular raid by Ottoman pirate Dragut, who sacked the town, burned houses, and carried off 50 captives. Never again: the Genoese set this castello sul mare as a warning. Every July 3rd, at the end of the Nostra Signora di Montallegro festivities, it becomes the theater for a spectacular firework display—white pyrotechnic cascade falling from the roof into the sea, red smoke pouring from the windows, as if it were burning. Free visit Friday-weekend, 9am-12:30pm and 2pm-5:30pm.

⏱ 45 min
2

Rapallo's cable car, opened in 1934, is the only one in all of Liguria. 600 meters elevation gain, 7 minutes to go from the town center to the Sanctuary of Montallegro at 612 meters altitude. Spectacular view during ascent: olive groves, colorful houses climbing the hillside, Gulf of Tigullio all the way to Portofino. Round-trip around €11. If you have time, ride up by cable car and descend on foot via the signposted forest path (1h30)—permanent panoramic views.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Sanctuary of Nostra Signora di Montallegro was built after July 2, 1557, the day the Virgin supposedly appeared to local peasant Giovanni Chighizola on this hill. White marble façade from 1896, Baroque-Renaissance interior, sailors' ex-votos everywhere on the walls (Rapallo was a great sailing port). Inside, a Byzantine icon that tradition says miraculously appeared in the chapel. One of Liguria's major Marian sanctuaries. On July 3rd, enormous procession and descent by cable car at nightfall, with the castle set ablaze below to close the festival.

⏱ 45 min
4

The Basilica of Saints Gervais and Protais, right in the town center, intrigues with its obviously tilted bell tower. 12th-century building remodeled several times—hence the tilt, due to successive renovations and unstable ground. Ornate Baroque interior, ancient Byzantine paintings, but it's the exterior silhouette that stands out. Not to be confused with the Tower of Pisa: here the tilt is more discreet, almost comical when you spot it.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Stroll on the Lungomare Vittorio Veneto, Rapallo's seafront. Early 20th-century bandstand, palm trees, Belle Époque façades recalling that Rapallo was one of the favorite seaside resorts of Northern writers between the wars. Hemingway stayed here in 1923 with Ezra Pound, whom he joined for drinking and literature talk. Yeats lived here five years. Nietzsche had begun Zarathustra here in 1882. The town retains a discreet elegance contrasting with neighboring Portofino's bling.

⏱ 60 min

Hike from Montallegro toward Monte Pegge or descent to Rapallo via signposted trails. The sanctuary is the starting point for several routes of varying difficulty, some descending toward San Massimo and the medieval ruins of Valle Christi (a former 13th-century Clarissan convent, rare in Italy for its still-standing Gothic arches). Allow 2 to 3 hours, significant downhill elevation.

⏱ 180 min

Chiavari

Three kilometers of medieval arcades sheltering eight centuries of commerce
★★★★☆
1

Chiavari is the city of arcades. Three kilometers of medieval and Renaissance porticoes covering the historic center, expanded century after century without ever losing coherence. Founding act in 1181-1182: the Genoese founded the city to contain the power of the Counts of Lavagna (the Fieschi). Renaissance refoundation in the 15th-16th centuries with palaces. The arcades have had the same function for 800 years: sheltering shops and pedestrians from the weather. The Caruggio diritto (Via Martiri della Liberazione) is the main axis, entirely pedestrian, lined with historic pastry shops, focaccerias, and boutiques.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Cathedral of Nostra Signora dell'Orto dominates the main square of the same name. 12th-century origin (on former vegetable gardens, hence Orto), rebuilt in 1633 after a Marian apparition to a citizen, then profoundly remodeled in the 19th century in a surprising neoclassical style: very Roman colonnaded façade, ornate Baroque interior. Frescoed vault, precious high altar, 14th-century crucifix. It's also a Marian sanctuary where pilgrimages still occur.

⏱ 45 min
3

Of Chiavari's castle, not much remains: the Torrione (keep), two cisterns, the parade ground, and a fragment of wall. Built by the Genoese in the 12th century to lock down the city after retaking it from the Fieschi. What you see today requires a short climb behind the center, but the panorama over the Gulf of Tigullio (from Portofino to Sestri Levante) rewards the ascent. Public domain, free.

⏱ 30 min
4

5 kilometers north, above Lavagna, the Basilica dei Fieschi is a total surprise. Built in 1245 by Pope Innocent IV (Sinibaldo Fieschi, born locally) to celebrate his family. Façade in black and white marble bands—typical Ligurian style but on the scale of a rural cathedral. Latin cross plan, remarkable Gothic rose window. Sober, almost Cistercian interior. Next door, the Fieschi comital palace, heavily restored. See both together, it's one of Liguria's most beautiful medieval ensembles. 15 minutes by car from Chiavari, accessible by bike via the slate cycle path.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Bike on the Slate Cycle Path (Via dell'Ardesia). 33 km flat on the old Val Fontanabuona railway, from Lavagna's seafront to Rivarola. Local slate (ardesia) has been extracted here since the Middle Ages and has roofed half of Liguria. Several old quarries and processing plants visible from the route. Rental in Lavagna, expect €15-20 per day.

⏱ 240 min

Lavagna beach, 2 km from Chiavari. Nearly 4 km of fine sand, one of the longest beaches in Liguria—a rare commodity here, where it's almost always pebbles. Very family-friendly, shallow for the first meters. Free beach widely accessible at both ends, paid bagni in the center. The town of Lavagna itself also deserves a detour: it's the former medieval fief of the Fieschi.

⏱ 180 min

Sestri Levante

The peninsula between two seas that cast its spell on Andersen
★★★★☆
1

Sestri Levante is the town with two seas. A narrow peninsula—the Isola, which wasn't an island until the High Middle Ages—connects the historic center to a wooded promontory, and opens on both sides to two opposite bays. West side, the large Baia delle Favole. East side, the small Baia del Silenzio. The center sits on the isthmus. Colorful houses, paved alleys, 14th-century slate portals. Mandatory glance from the Isola: climb to the San Nicolò church to see both bays at once.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Baia del Silenzio has borne this name since 1919, christened by Sestrese poet Giovanni Descalzo, struck by the strange calm of this cove even when storms shook the neighboring coast. Small golden sand beach sloping gently down, bordered by pastel houses pressed against each other, fishing boats pulled onto the sand. At dawn, it's one of Italy's most beautiful views—a cliché you've seen a thousand times and which still surprises. Better to see it early morning or late afternoon: on a July afternoon, you're packed like sardines.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Baia delle Favole owes its name to another, more illustrious figure. In autumn 1833, caught by a storm, Hans Christian Andersen—then 28 years old, not yet known for his tales—spent a night in an inn in Sestri Levante. He left enchanted. He would later write: 'What a fairy-tale evening I spent in Sestri Levante!' In 1958, on the occasion of creating the Premio Andersen—a literary competition dedicated to children's literature, still active today—the municipality christened the large western bay Baia delle Favole in homage to the Danish storyteller. Golden sand, long beach, livelier than the Baia del Silenzio.

⏱ 45 min
4

At the foot of the Isola, on the Baia del Silenzio, the former Convent of the Annunziata, founded by Dominicans in the 15th century. Little-known fact: it was between Sestri Levante and Levanto that Guglielmo Marconi conducted, in 1932-1934, his decisive experiments on microwave propagation—the signals crossed the 70 kilometers of open sea separating the two towns, demonstrating for the first time that these short waves could carry far. Today the complex hosts events and the panoramic terrace is one of the most beautiful views of the Tigullio.

⏱ 30 min
5

In the heart of the center, Palazzo Fascie in Coppedè style (Italian Liberty, early 20th century) houses the municipal library, historical archives, and above all the MuSeL—Sestri's archaeological museum. Two well-designed floors recounting the area from the necropolis of the Liguri Tigulli (8th-7th century BC) to the 20th century. Section on Ligurian emigration to the Americas, particularly moving. Small museum but complements the walk well.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hike to Punta Manara, from the eastern edge of the center. 2 hours round-trip, moderate elevation gain, signposted trail climbing through olive groves and pine forests to a rocky cape 200 meters above the sea. Plunging view over both bays of Sestri on one side, Riva Trigoso on the other, and the coast stretching to the Cinque Terre on clear days. Ruins of a 16th-century watchtower at the summit. Good shoes, water, early departure in summer.

⏱ 180 min

If you're here in June, it's the Andersen Festival: 6 days of street performances, puppet theater, tales for children and adults, concerts in the alleys. Free programming for many events. Otherwise, in July, the Barcarolata on the Baia del Silenzio—parade of fishing boats decorated with wacky themes, illuminated at nightfall. Local tradition since the 1950s.

⏱ 180 min

Moneglia

A golden crescent reached through five tunnels carved by 19th-century railways
★★☆☆☆
1

Unique feature in Italy: to enter Moneglia from Riva Trigoso, you pass through five alternating one-way road tunnels, controlled by traffic lights that open three times per hour in each direction. Former railway tunnels from the Genoa-Pisa line, dating from 1874, decommissioned in 1970, recycled into a single-lane automobile road. It's narrow, it's lit, it's very Ligurian. Riva Trigoso side, departures from Moneglia at :05, :25, :45 of each hour; Deiva side, every 10 minutes. Check beforehand, otherwise you wait 20 minutes at a red light in the sun. And between tunnels, rocky coves for diving.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Castello di Monleone dates from 1173: it's one of two fortresses built by the Republic of Genoa to defend Moneglia (the other, Villafranca, is in ruins). In the 19th century, transformed into a neo-Gothic private castle. Today a luxury hotel. The climb to reach it (5 minutes on foot from the center) remains public and worthwhile for the panorama: Moneglia, its bay, and the coast stretching toward the Cinque Terre. The castle even appears on the municipal coat of arms.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Castello di Villafranca is the other fortress, opposite the village, on the east side. Reduced to ruins by Allied bombing during World War II, partially restored. What you visit today is mainly its park and botanical garden. Strategic position: it watched for Barbary pirate arrivals from offshore. The tower gives its silhouette to the entire bay.

⏱ 30 min
4

The Church of Santa Croce, former 12th-century parish church, rebuilt in 1726. Inside, two major works: an altarpiece by Luca Cambiaso (Genoese Mannerist painter born in Moneglia in 1527, one of the greats of the Genoese school) and a statue by Anton Maria Maragliano, the Baroque sculptor who dressed almost all Ligurian processions in the 18th century. Next door, the Baroque oratorio dei Disciplinanti preserves a cycle of ancient frescoes. The Church of San Giorgio, higher up, dates from 1396 and keeps a beautiful Franciscan cloister.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Moneglia beach. One kilometer of granular sand, shallow sea for the first meters—one of the rare family beaches truly usable with small children on the Riviera di Levante. Blue flag every year since 1990. Free beach widely accessible from both sides, paid bagni in the middle (€15-25 per lounger). The further east you go (toward the tunnels), the more the coast closes into small coves of pebbles and rocks, perfect for snorkeling.

⏱ 240 min

Hike the Sentiero Verde Azzurro between Moneglia and Deiva Marina, about 6 km, 2 hours. The trail climbs toward Lemeglio then San Pantaleo (high point 325 m), pine forests, olive groves, vertiginous views over the coast. Level reserved for experienced hikers (EE in Italian classification)—some passages are steep. Return train from Deiva to rejoin Moneglia (3 minutes, €1.80).

⏱ 180 min

Levanto

Where Ligurian surfers catch waves at the northern gate to paradise
★★★☆☆
1

Levanto, the northern gateway to the Cinque Terre, but without the crowds. The central beach stretches nearly a kilometer, sand mixed with gravel, and it's one of the very rare surf spots in all of Liguria—the bay is open and receives western swells. Several schools rent boards (€35-50 per day with wetsuit). No monumental big waves, but a relaxed surf vibe that stands out on the coast. Free beach accessible, paid bagni in the center.

⏱ 60 min
2

Levanto's medieval center remains largely pedestrian. 13th-century Loggia Comunale, perfectly preserved—its stone arches still support the old town hall. All around, arcaded alleys recalling those of Chiavari but on a smaller scale. The Church of Sant'Andrea, façade in black and white marble bands from the 13th century, remarkable Gothic rose window, sober interior. The medieval town was fortified—remnants of walls visible in several places.

⏱ 60 min
3

Levanto's castle, at the top of the town, dates from the 16th century. Built to defend the town from Barbary incursions, transformed into a private residence in the 19th century. Not open to visits (private), but the climb through the alleys and the view from the adjacent belvedere are worth it. Five minutes on foot from the center.

⏱ 30 min
4

Punta Mesco marks the cape separating Levanto from Monterosso, first of the Cinque Terre. The trail climbing it (3 hours round-trip, 400 m elevation gain) crosses pine forests and old terraced vineyards. At the summit: ruins of the Church of Sant'Antonio (13th century), lighthouse, and one of the most beautiful views over the Cinque Terre from offshore. Marine protected area below. Doable in half a day if you start early.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

The day's highlight—and one of the most singular things on the Ligurian coast: the Maremonti cycle path Levanto-Bonassola-Framura. 5.5 km flat on the old Genoa-Pisa railway line, opened in 1874, abandoned in 1970 when a new double-track route was built further inland. Lit tunnels (cool in midsummer—18°C inside, 35°C outside), coves accessible by stairs between tunnels, permanent sea views at the openings. Converted to a trail 2010-2011. Bike rental in Levanto (€10-15 per day at Cicli Raso or Onda Sport). Allow 1h round-trip by bike, 2h on foot.

⏱ 180 min

Tasting of Cinque Terre DOC in a Levanto cellar. Vermentino and Bosco, dry white with notes of almond blossom and yellow fruit, aged on terraced vineyards plunging toward the sea. Several local producers open their cellars (around €15-20 for tasting with four wines and focaccia). Also possible to taste Sciacchetrà, very rare straw wine, sometimes up to €80 per half-bottle.

⏱ 90 min
