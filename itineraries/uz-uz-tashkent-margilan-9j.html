Three madrasas around the Registan, Tamerlane's ribbed blue tomb at Gur-e-Amir, and the dazzling tiled avenue of Shah-i-Zinda — the soul of the Silk Road.

★★★★★
1

Registan Square brings together three madrasas built between 1420 and 1660: Ulugh Beg (the oldest), Sher-Dor with its lions above the portal, and Tilya-Kori whose mosque ceiling mimics a gold-leaf dome. A UNESCO World Heritage Site, this is the defining image of Samarkand.

2

Its early 15th-century cupola became the template for all Mughal architecture in India, right up to the Taj Mahal. A slab of black jade marks the exact resting place of the conqueror.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Siab Bazaar, behind the Bibi-Khanym Mosque, is the living market of the old city. Samarkand melons are celebrated across the country — taste them early in the morning, before the tour groups arrive.

⏱ 60 min

The Bibi-Khanym Mosque (1404), built to celebrate Tamerlane's return from his Indian campaign, was once among the largest in the Islamic world. Its sheer scale still impresses, centuries and earthquakes notwithstanding.

Samarkand

Three madrasas around the Registan, Tamerlane's ribbed blue tomb at Gur-e-Amir, and the dazzling tiled avenue of Shah-i-Zinda — the soul of the Silk Road.

★★★★★
1

Shah-i-Zinda — the Tomb of the Living King — is a narrow avenue lined with more than twenty mausoleums spanning the 11th to the 19th century. The finest date from the 14th and 15th centuries: deep-blue tilework, calligraphic inscriptions, carved stalactites. It is said that Qusam ibn Abbas, cousin of the Prophet, came here to preach Islam in the 7th century. Dress modestly. Legend holds that anyone who counts the same number of steps going up and coming down will have their wish granted.

⏱ 90 min
2

Ulugh Beg's observatory (15th c.) has almost nothing left above ground — only the pit of a giant sextant with a 40-metre radius, buried in the hillside. Ulugh Beg, the scholar-sultan, charted more than 1,000 stars here with remarkable precision for his era. The adjacent museum traces his scientific legacy.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The Afrosiyab Museum stands on the hill of the ancient Sogdian city (8th century BC). Its centrepiece is a 7th-century fresco depicting King Varkhuman receiving ambassadors from China, India and Korea — one of the rare pre-Islamic frescoes still visible in Central Asia.

Bukhara

The 1127 Kalon Minaret that outlasted Genghis Khan, a labyrinth of trading domes, and the mulberry-shaded pool of Lyabi-Hauz — a city frozen in amber.

★★★★★
1

The Poi-Kalon complex gathers three monuments: the Kalon Minaret (1127, 47.5 metres), which legend says was the only structure spared by Genghis Khan, the Kalon Mosque with a capacity of 12,000 worshippers, and the Mir-i-Arab Madrasa (1535), still active today with 120 students. Take time to sit facing the minaret as the day winds down.

⏱ 90 min
2

Lyabi-Hauz, a pool dug in 1620, is ringed by mulberry trees planted at the same time and three historic buildings: the Kukeldash Madrasa (1568), the Nadir Divan-Begi Madrasa and a Sufi khanaka. This is the beating heart of Bukhara, with café terraces and a statue of Nasreddin on his donkey.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Ark Citadel, a fortress-residence of the emirs since the 5th century, towers over the square from a height of 20 metres. Palace, prison and place of execution: the entire history of the Emirate of Bukhara is written into its walls.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Wander between the three 16th-century trading domes (taks) — jewellers, hatmakers, money changers — that punctuate the historic bazaar's main axis. Their architecture remains intact beneath the silks and souvenirs. The Samanid Mausoleum (10th century), in the nearby park, is the oldest monument in the city: its intricate fired-brick work creates mesmerising patterns of light and shadow.

⏱ 90 min

Fergana

Gateway to the fertile valley, where the cobalt blues of Rishtan pottery and the shimmering silks of Margilan have been crafted for over a thousand years.

★★☆☆☆
1

Rishtan (25 km from Fergana) is the world capital of blue and white ceramics. Local artisans use a mineral glaze that produces a cobalt blue impossible to replicate industrially — a technique dating back to the 12th century, inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list. In family workshops, you can watch the wheel turning, the brush painting and the firing in brick kilns.

Margilan

The world capital of ikat silk: the entirely hand-operated Yodgorlik factory and the Kumtepa bazaar, alive only on Thursdays and Sundays.

★★★☆☆
1

The Yodgorlik factory (meaning 'souvenir'), founded in 1972, is the only manufacturer in the world where ikat silk is produced entirely by hand, from cocoon to finished fabric. Here you can watch women unreeling cocoons in boiling water, dyeing threads using the ikat resist technique, then weaving on wooden hand looms. 450 employees, 6,000 metres of fabric per month. The guided tour is free.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Said Akhmad-Khoja Madrasa (19th c.) now houses Margilan's Craft Development Centre: the former student cells are occupied by master weavers of atlas and adras textiles.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Kumtepa Bazaar, 5 km west of the town centre, opens only on Thursdays and Sundays. Men in chapan robes negotiate rolls of atlas and ikat fabric over pots of tea, with not a tourist in sight. Arrive between 8am and 11am for peak atmosphere.
