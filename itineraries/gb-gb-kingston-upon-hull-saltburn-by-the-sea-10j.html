Kingston Upon Hull

★★★☆☆
1

The Deep, perched at the tip of Sammy's Point where the River Hull spills into the Humber, ranks among the UK's largest aquariums—5,000 marine species housed in a Terry Farrell iceberg of a building that opened in 2002 for £52 million. The 9-metre tunnel plunges you into the heart of a 2.5-million-litre tank: sharks glide overhead without warning. Allow two hours.

⏱ 120 min
2

Wilberforce House, on High Street in the old town, is the birthplace of William Wilberforce (1759–1833), the MP who spent his life campaigning to abolish slavery across the British Empire—a victory he secured with the Abolition Act of 1807, just months before his death. The museum, free since opening in 1906, is unexpectedly moving. The garden in front bears Nelson Mandela's name.

⏱ 60 min
3

Hull Minster, in the heart of the old town, is one of England's largest parish churches by floor area. Its 14th-century brick tower marks a slice of history: Hull built in brick because it imported materials from the Low Countries, lacking local stone. It was here, in 1642, that the town gathered to refuse opening its gates to King Charles I—an act that helped trigger the English Civil War.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Hull's old town walks itself in 45 minutes—medieval lanes, Georgian facades that survived the Blitz, and Ye Olde White Harte, a 1550 Grade II* pub where legend has it the governor decided to shut the town to the king. The carved oak interior warrants stopping for a pint.

⏱ 60 min

Beverley

★★☆☆☆
1

Spurn Point is Yorkshire's end of the world. This sand-and-shingle spit stretches 5 kilometres between the North Sea and the Humber estuary—in places barely 50 metres wide. In December 2013, a storm destroyed the road that crossed it: since then, the tip has become an island at high tide, accessible only to walkers and cyclists. The Yorkshire Wildlife Trust runs a safari in a converted military Unimog—two hours through the dunes to the 1895 lighthouse, First World War blockhouses, and abandoned lifeboat crew houses. Check tide times before you go—the washover section can cut off access.

⏱ 180 min
2

Spurn Lighthouse, restored in 2015 after decades of abandonment, rises 39 metres and offers 360° views over the estuary and the North Sea. It's a Victorian structure from 1895—Yorkshire Wildlife Trust volunteers recount the history of the lifeboat station that operated here without interruption from 1810, with permanent crews and their families living at the tip of this sand tongue. The last family left in 2012.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The walk from the visitor centre to the tip of Spurn runs about 10 kilometres over sand and shingle—allow three hours. You'll pass the ruins of a Great War coastal battery, shipwrecks half-buried in sand, and in autumn thousands of Scandinavian migrant birds stopping here. Bring binoculars.

⏱ 180 min

Late afternoon, head north to Beverley for the night—50 minutes through the Holderness plains. The old town warrants an evening stroll around North Bar, England's only surviving medieval fortified gateway in a town of this size, dating from 1409.

⏱ 45 min

Beverley

★★☆☆☆
1

Beverley Minster announces itself the moment you turn into Minster Yard: twin limestone towers rising over 40 metres, a west facade so perfect in its proportions that Nicholas Hawksmoor used it as the model for Westminster Abbey's towers. Inside, the Minster stretches 122 metres—longer than many English cathedrals—and houses 68 16th-century misericords, the largest collection of any parish church in the country. Don't miss the carved scene of the fox preaching to geese, and geese hanging the fox—medieval satire was unsparing. Construction began in 1220, finished in 1425, with three Gothic styles layered atop one another. Free entry.

⏱ 90 min
2

St Mary's Church, Beverley, a ten-minute walk away, gets fewer visitors but is nearly as beautiful—its 15th-century painted ceiling depicts the kings of England from the Middle Ages onward. Lewis Carroll, a frequent visitor to Beverley, supposedly found inspiration for the White Rabbit here: a rabbit carrying a watch appears carved in one of the vaults.

⏱ 45 min
3

Beverley Westwood is a vast common at the edge of town where cattle have grazed freely since the 12th century—a grazing right codified by royal charter. It's also the locals' playground: golf, running, picnics. A half-hour walk from the centre to decompress before hitting the road.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Beverley's Saturday Market—or any weekday market in the town centre—is the beating heart of the town. Beverley has exported wool to Ghent and Ypres since the 12th century, and its agricultural market is one of Yorkshire's oldest. Local cheeses, craft beers, independent butchers and cheesemongers holding the line against supermarkets.

⏱ 60 min

Filey

★★☆☆☆
1

Filey Brigg is a blade of Jurassic rock jutting 1.5 kilometres into the North Sea north of town. At low tide, the exposed rocks form a sandstone and limestone platform riddled with pools and crevices of ammonites, bivalves, and burrow fossils 150 million years old. Local legend claims the Brigg is the skeleton of a dragon that villagers lured into the waves with a parkin cake—which also explains the cake's name. At high tide the rocks vanish underwater—check tide times before venturing onto the point.

⏱ 90 min
2

Filey Bay beach stretches 8 kilometres of fine sand between the Brigg to the north and Speeton to the south—one of Yorkshire's longest sandy beaches. Filey itself is a quiet Edwardian seaside town, less crowded than Scarborough, with a promenade and cafes that feel plucked from the 1950s. Perfect for a peaceful fish & chips lunch.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Fossil hunting on the Brigg and Filey's north beach is among the best on England's Jurassic coast—comparable to Dorset, but without the crowds. Local operators like Yorkshire Coast Rock run 2-hour guided outings: a geologist shows you where to search and how to distinguish a real ammonite from a plain pebble. Book online—groups are small.

⏱ 120 min

The walk from Filey Brigg Country Park to the top of Carr Naze—the tip of the peninsula—takes 45 minutes return. At the top, Filey beach spreads on one side and the Flamborough cliffs on the other. Note the site of the Roman signal station discovered here in 1857.

⏱ 60 min

Whitby

★★★★☆
1

Ravenscar is the town that never was. In 1895, property developers bought this headland 200 metres above the North Sea with a grand scheme: build a seaside resort to rival Scarborough. They hired 300 workers, laid out streets, installed sewers, erected a hotel (Raven Hall, still standing), and auctioned off 1,300 plots. Fewer than half found buyers. When the first purchasers arrived to view their land, they understood why: the descent to the sea is nearly vertical, and the beach below barely exists. The company liquidated in 1913, leaving empty streets, intact sewers, and a handful of isolated houses. Today you can walk these phantom avenues and find underfoot bricks stamped 'RAVENSCAR' made for a project that never came to be.

⏱ 75 min
2

Ravenscar's old alum works, managed by the National Trust, are the remains of an industry that dominated the coast for two centuries. Alum—produced by burning cliff shale, then treating it with human urine imported from Newcastle—was used to fix dyes in textiles. The works operated from 1650 to 1862, when the arrival of synthetic dyes made it obsolete overnight. The 17th-century stone structures are still visible on the coastal path.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Cinder Track links Ravenscar to Robin Hood's Bay on the old Scarborough–Whitby railway line, closed in 1965 by the Beeching cuts. This flat 8-kilometre trail follows the cliff edge with plunging views of the coast. From Ravenscar toward Robin Hood's Bay, you pass above the bay looking down at the village's red roofs. One of the coast's finest walks—and you avoid the roads entirely.

⏱ 150 min

Continue to Whitby for the night—30 minutes by car from Ravenscar. If you arrive before sunset, head straight to St Mary's churchyard to see the abbey backlit against the evening sky.

⏱ 45 min

Goathland

★★★☆☆
1

Pickering Castle, perched on its hill above town, is one of England's best-preserved Norman castles—begun in timber in 1069 by William the Conqueror, rebuilt in stone around 1180, and remarkably intact since. What makes it special: the two-bailey plan is nearly unchanged from the 12th century, and the buildings escaped damage in both the Wars of the Roses and the Civil War. Managed by English Heritage. Allow an hour.

⏱ 60 min
2

Pickering's parish church, just below the castle, hides a secret that Protestant reformers nearly erased forever: behind the whitewash applied in the 16th century to purify the church, a series of 15th-century medieval frescoes slept for three centuries before being rediscovered in 1852. The scenes of St Christopher, St George, and St John the Baptist are among the best-preserved in England. Free entry.

⏱ 30 min
3

Goathland, the northern terminus of your steam train journey, is one of North Yorkshire's most photographed villages—and one of the least predictable. Sheep roam freely through the streets as if cars were never invented. The village played Aidensfield in the 1990s TV series Heartbeat, and Goathland station served as Hogsmeade in the first Harry Potter film. Stroll for 30 minutes to see both.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The steam train journey from Pickering to Goathland on the North Yorkshire Moors Railway is this day's centrepiece. The line was laid in 1836 by George Stephenson to link Whitby to the interior—the first wagons were horse-drawn. Closed in 1965 by the Beeching cuts, it was saved by volunteers and reopened in 1973. Today it's one of the UK's busiest heritage lines, with 350,000 passengers a year. The 24 kilometres from Pickering to Grosmont cross moorland, wooded valleys, and Fen Bog peatland—book ahead in summer, absolutely essential.

⏱ 180 min

Mallyan Spout waterfall, a 20-minute walk from Goathland village, plunges some 15 metres into a wooded ravine—the highest fall in North York Moors National Park. The trail runs along the damp, moss-carpeted gorge from the Mallyan Spout Hotel pub. After rain, the flow is spectacular.

⏱ 60 min

Hutton Le Hole

★★★☆☆
1

Hutton-le-Hole is the kind of place you start doubting exists before you arrive. A stream wanders through the village in meandering loops across a broad green lawn edged with white fences, and sheep graze freely between stone cottages. Lonely Planet cites it as one of England's most beautiful villages—hard to argue. The name itself comes from the dips and hollows of the terrain, and the 'le' is a Victorian invention to sound more French. Arrive early morning or after 5pm—during the day coaches unload.

⏱ 60 min
2

Ryedale Folk Museum, at the entrance to Hutton-le-Hole, is a 5-acre open-air museum assembling 25 historic Yorkshire buildings dismantled and rebuilt here: medieval timber-framed cottages, an Elizabethan farmhouse, a Victorian shop, a blacksmith's workshop. The museum traces rural Yorkshire life from the Iron Age to the 1950s. Allow at least two hours.

⏱ 120 min
3

Lastingham, 5 minutes by car, is one of the moors' most discreet and most singular villages. Its St Mary's Church possesses an 11th-century crypt built on the foundations of a monastery founded in 654 by St Cedd—one of the missionaries who Christianised northern England. Descend the old stone steps into the dim light: Norman columns from 1078 surround the saint's tomb. It's one of Yorkshire's oldest and most silent spaces.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The circular walk from Hutton-le-Hole to Lastingham and back across the moors runs 7 kilometres—allow two hours. You cross open moorland with 360° views of the hills before descending into the Lastingham valley. On the return, stop for a drink at the Blacksmith's Arms in Lastingham—an intact village pub serving real Yorkshire ales.

⏱ 120 min

Lealholm

★★☆☆☆
1

Runswick Bay is perhaps the most beautiful coastal village on England's entire north-east coast—regularly ranked among Britain's best beaches by the Sunday Times. Red-tiled cottages huddle against the cliff face, linked by staircases so steep the road descends at a gradient of 1 in 4. What the postcards don't tell you: in 1682 (not 1664 as some sources claim), the entire village slid into the sea during a winter's night. The only thing that saved everyone: villagers were keeping vigil over a corpse and managed to wake the others in time. Coins and a silver spoon were still being found on the beach in 1817. The village was rebuilt a little further south, and the houses still cling to the same unstable clays.

⏱ 90 min
2

Lealholm, in the Esk Valley 35 minutes inland by car, is the perfect antidote after the coast. This stone village threaded by the Esk walks itself in 20 minutes: 18th-century humpback bridge, stepping stones in the riverbed, overflowing gardens above the water. The atmosphere is that of deep Yorkshire unchanged since the late 19th century. The Board Inn, a riverside pub, serves dinner.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The morning at Runswick Bay is ideally spent on the beach. At low tide, the exposed Jurassic shale rocks at Hob Holes—the caves that locals believed were inhabited by a benevolent elf capable of curing children's whooping cough—reveal fossil ammonites and bivalves. The Cleveland Way skirts the bay: an hour's walk toward Staithes to the north or Sandsend to the south is enough to find yourself alone facing the sea.

⏱ 90 min

In the afternoon, climb up the Esk Valley to Lealholm. The road winding from the coast inland crosses open moorland before plunging into the wooded valley—the contrast is striking. In Lealholm, cross the Esk on the stepping stones, walk along the cottage gardens by the water, and end the day riverside.

⏱ 90 min

Saltburn By The Sea

★★★☆☆
1

Boulby cliffs, between Staithes and Saltburn, are the highest on mainland England—203 metres of shale and sandstone dropping straight into the North Sea. From the Cleveland Way coastal path, you look directly down to the sea and out to the offshore wind farms. What makes Boulby truly unique is what's happening beneath your feet: Boulby potash mine descends to 1,400 metres below the surface—Britain's deepest mine—and has housed since the 1990s the Boulby Underground Laboratory, installed at 1,100 metres depth in its galleries. The lab isn't open to the public, but knowing that researchers are down there searching for the universe's secrets while you gaze at these ordinary cliffs from the path is dizzying.

⏱ 75 min
2

Staithes, wedged between two cliffs at the mouth of a narrow valley, remains one of the coast's most authentic fishing villages. Leave your car at the top and walk down into the lanes—some are 45 centimetres wide. In 1745, young James Cook, 16 years old, came here to work as a grocer's apprentice. He lasted only 18 months: fascinated by the boats in the harbour, he left for Whitby to join the navy. The shop was destroyed by a storm, but its materials were reused to build a cottage you'll still find on Church Street.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Cleveland Way walk from Staithes toward Boulby along the cliff is one of the most spectacular on the entire coast. You climb gradually from harbour level to 200 metres, with the sea below and the moors behind. Allow two hours return to the top of Boulby cliffs. Waterproof footwear essential—the path can be muddy.

⏱ 120 min

The Captain Cook and Staithes Heritage Centre, in the former 1880 Methodist chapel, assembles over 200 books and 61 original engravings from Cook's voyages, as well as a reconstruction of the Sanderson shop as it was in 1745—the only one of its kind in the world. Free entry.

⏱ 45 min

Saltburn By The Sea

★★★☆☆
1

Saltburn Cliff Tramway is Britain's oldest water-balanced funicular still in service, opened in June 1884. In 55 seconds it descends 37 metres over 63 metres of track—a gradient of 71% (1 in 1.40), confirmed by the original technical specifications—the principle has scarcely changed in 140 years: a 1,100-litre water tank is filled in the top cabin until its weight exceeds that of the bottom cabin. A brakeman in person controls everything from his post. Ride it both ways and watch the mechanics—it's living Victorian engineering.

⏱ 20 min
2

Saltburn Pier, opened in May 1869 and Grade II* listed, extends 200 metres over the North Sea—the only remaining great pleasure pier on the entire north-east coast of England. Walk to the end: the view of Boulby cliffs to the north and the curving sandy beach to the south is the best possible conclusion to this circuit.

⏱ 30 min
3

Saltburn's Valley Gardens are a cascading Victorian garden tumbling down the valley behind the town—fountains, flower beds, a miniature railway that's been running in circles since 1947. A striking contrast to the cliffs and moors you've crossed: here everything is ordered, colourful, and gently outdated.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Saltburn is a recognized surf spot on the north-east coast. The swell regularly rises from offshore, the bottom is sandy, and the beach is long. Rental operators work on the main beach in season—wetsuit essential, the water barely reaches 15°C even in August. If you don't surf, settle atop the cliffs and watch the surfers battle the tide.

⏱ 120 min

Finish with a sunset walk on the beach: from the foot of the funicular heading north, the sandy beach runs to the Huntcliff cliffs. At low tide, the exposed rocks on the north side offer one last fossil hunt—Jurassic ammonites and oysters that the sea regularly brings back.

⏱ 60 min
