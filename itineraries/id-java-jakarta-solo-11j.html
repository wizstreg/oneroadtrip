📋 Important notes

Ujung Kulon National Park (D3–D4) is accessible only by boat from Tamanjaya or Sumur — plan at least 2 nights. Roads to Ujung Kulon from Jakarta are long (5–6 hours to Tamanjaya). Day 1

Jakarta

A 34-million megalopolis built on the bones of Dutch colonial Batavia, where 17th-century warehouses face the modern sprawl

★★★★★
📍 Kota Tua Old Batavia Jakarta📍 Fatahillah Square Jakarta📍 Jakarta History Museum Fatahillah📍 Wayang Museum Jakarta📍 Sunda Kelapa Harbour Jakarta📍 Kali Besar Canal Jakarta
1

Start with Kota Tua, old Jakarta — one square kilometer of 17th-century Dutch buildings surviving in one of the densest megalopolises on Earth. The VOC (Dutch East India Company) founded Batavia in 1619 under Governor Jan Pieterszoon Coen, who massacred the local population to establish the Asian capital of his pepper empire. At the heart of Kota Tua, Fatahillah Square (Taman Fatahillah) is flanked by the former city hall (now the Jakarta History Museum, built in 1710), the former Court of Justice (now the Fine Arts and Ceramics Museum) and the Wayang Museum housed in a former Dutch church dating from 1640. In the 18th century, this square was the site of colonial public executions.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Bank of Indonesia Museum occupies a 1933 Art Deco building (Nieuwe Zakelijkheid style, a rare example of Dutch modernism in Asia) — the former Nederlandsche Handelsmaatschappij trading house. The interior preserves period counters, monumental safes and stained-glass windows. The contrast between the splendor of this architecture and the chaotic megalopolis outside is striking. Don't miss the stained glass on your way out.

⏱ 60 min
3

Sunda Kelapa harbor, 500 meters north of Fatahillah, was Batavia's maritime gateway since the 16th century. To this day, pinisi — the large two-masted schooners typical of the Indonesian archipelago — still moor here and unload their cargo. It's one of the last places where this type of traditional sailing is still active on Java. Arrive early in the morning to watch the crews at work.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The evening in Jakarta is best spent in the Kemang district or around the national monument Monas (the 132-meter Independence Tower inaugurated in 1975, topped with a gold-plated bronze flame) — surrounded by a park that Jakartans have reclaimed as a nighttime strolling ground. If you have the energy, take the elevator to the observation deck at 115 meters for a view over the sprawl of the city.

Day 2

Jakarta

A 34-million megalopolis built on the bones of Dutch colonial Batavia, where 17th-century warehouses face the modern sprawl

★★★★★
📍 National Monument Monas Jakarta📍 National Museum Jakarta📍 Jakarta Cathedral📍 Istiqlal Mosque Jakarta📍 Glodok Chinatown Jakarta📍 Pasar Baru market Jakarta
1

The National Museum (Museum Nasional), nicknamed the "Elephant Museum" for the statue at its entrance, is the richest in Indonesia. Founded in 1778, it houses extraordinary collections of 9th-century Buddhist bronzes, Tang-era Chinese ceramics, Batak textiles and Balinese masks. This is where you'll grasp just how central Java was to the great Asian trade routes long before the Europeans arrived. Allow 2 hours.

⏱ 120 min
2

Istiqlal Mosque (the "Mosque of Independence") and Jakarta Cathedral face each other across the same square — an architectural symbol of the pluralism championed by Sukarno, who inaugurated Istiqlal in 1978. The mosque is the largest in Southeast Asia, with a capacity of 200,000 worshippers. Across the way, the neo-Gothic cathedral built by the Dutch in 1901 remains fully active. The coexistence of the two buildings is deliberate.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Chinese quarter of Glodok, two kilometers from Kota Tua, is the oldest Chinatown in Indonesia — Chinese merchants settled here in the 17th century under the VOC. The massacre of 1740 (10,000 Chinese killed on orders from the Dutch governor, one of the worst atrocities of the colonial era) left a living memory. Visit the Vihara Dharma Bhakti temple, founded in 1650 and one of the oldest Buddhist temples in Jakarta, still active and thick with incense at all hours.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Prepare for tomorrow's departure to Bandung — 3 hours by road or under 3 hours by Argo Parahyangan train from Gambir station (frequent service, recommended). Wrap up the evening in the Blok M or Kemang district for local restaurants and bars. Jakarta is not an easy city, but it rewards those who dig beneath the surface.

Day 3

Ujung Kulon

UNESCO park at the westernmost tip of Java, sole refuge on Earth of the one-horned Javan rhinoceros

★★★★☆
📍 Ujung Kulon National Park Javan rhino📍 Peucang Island Ujung Kulon📍 Tamanjaya village Ujung Kulon entrance📍 Sunda Strait from Ujung Kulon📍 Krakatoa Anak Krakatau excursion
1

Early departure from Jakarta — count on 5 to 6 hours to reach Tamanjaya, the gateway village to Ujung Kulon at the westernmost tip of Java. The road passes through Serang and Pandeglang before dropping down to the western Banten coast. Ujung Kulon National Park is the last lowland tropical rainforest on Java and the last refuge on Earth of the Javan rhinoceros (Rhinoceros sondaicus). Inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1991, it shelters between 60 and 76 individuals according to the latest surveys — no Javan rhinoceros exists in captivity anywhere in the world.

⏱ 60 min
2

The boat crossing from Tamanjaya to Peucang Island (about 1 hour) is the first powerful experience at Ujung Kulon: dolphins often escort the boats, egrets fly low overhead, and the silhouette of the Krakatoa volcanoes appears in the distance across the Sunda Strait. Peucang Island has a simple wooden lodge and a white-sand beach fringed by primary forest where Javan deer and long-tailed macaques wander freely between the bungalows.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

First forest trek from Peucang with a park ranger: the trails cut through giant dipterocarp jungle and mangroves. Camera traps in this sector have detected rhinos. You probably won't see one — they stay in the most inaccessible zones — but you may find their footprints in the river mud and their tracks through the vegetation. That alone is enough to understand you're walking through the last territory of an animal that has survived for 40 million years.

⏱ 180 min

Day 4

Ujung Kulon

UNESCO park at the westernmost tip of Java, sole refuge on Earth of the one-horned Javan rhinoceros

★★★★☆
📍 Snorkeling Peucang Island Ujung Kulon📍 Handeuleum Island mangroves Ujung Kulon📍 Banteng wild ox Java Ujung Kulon📍 Sunrise Ujung Kulon peninsula📍 Krakatoa Anak Krakatau Sunda Strait excursion
1

Handeuleum Island, east of the peninsula, is the best place to spot banteng — the wild oxen (Bos javanicus) that roam in herds through the park's open areas. These wild cattle with white sock-like markings on their legs are one of Ujung Kulon's flagship species alongside the rhinoceros, and far easier to see. Javan gibbons (Hylobates moloch) sing from the canopy at dawn — a sound that carries for kilometers and serves as the forest's natural clock. Freshwater crocodiles inhabit the peninsula's rivers.

⏱ 120 min
2

The coral reefs around Peucang Island are remarkably intact — the park's remoteness has shielded them from intensive fishing. Green sea turtles, leopard rays and schools of parrotfish glide just a few meters from shore. Snorkeling is easily accessible from the beach with no need for sophisticated diving gear.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

If weather conditions allow, local operators run speedboat excursions to Anak Krakatau — the "child of Krakatoa," born in 1927 inside the caldera of the 1883 eruption that killed 36,000 people and echoed around the world. The island continues to grow and erupt (last major collapse in 2018). From the boat, the view of the smoking cone surrounded by turquoise water in the Sunda Strait is spectacular.

Day 5

Bandung

Indonesia's third city at 768 meters, gateway to the active Tangkuban Perahu volcano and West Java's finest Art Deco boulevard

★★★★☆
📍 Tangkuban Perahu volcano Bandung📍 Kawah Ratu crater Bandung📍 Kawah Domas hot springs Tangkuban Perahu📍 Jalan Braga colonial boulevard Bandung📍 Dutch Art Deco villas Bandung
1

Bandung is Indonesia's third-largest city, perched at 768 meters in the Bandung Caldera — giving it a temperate climate almost unheard of in the tropics. From the city, the flat silhouette of Tangkuban Perahu (literally "the overturned boat" in Sundanese) dominates the northern horizon. The name comes from a legend: Sangkuriang tried to build a boat in a single night to win the heart of Dayang Sumbi, who was in fact his own mother. Tricked by a false sunrise, he kicked the unfinished boat in a rage — forming the mountain. The volcano is active (eruptions in 1969, 1983, 2013 and 2019) and stands at 2,084 meters: it's one of the few volcanoes in the world where you can drive right up to the crater rim.

⏱ 30 min
2

The main crater Kawah Ratu (Queen's Crater) opens just steps from the parking lot — a depression of sulfur and yellow-green smoke enclosed by a wooden railing. Since 1928, a paved road built by the Dutch (Bandung Vooruit) runs directly to the crater. Check the alert level before going: the volcano is closed to visitors when seismic activity rises. Arrive before 9 AM to beat the crowds and the midday fog.

⏱ 60 min
3

Kawah Domas crater, a one-hour walk from the parking area, is the most unusual feature of Tangkuban Perahu: a zone of bubbling geysers and sulfurous mud pools where local vendors offer to boil eggs in the naturally hot volcanic waters at over 90°C. The geological spectacle — steam, ochre and yellow hues, sulfur smell — is striking. Approach only with a guide.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

In the late afternoon, Bandung reveals its other face: the city is known as Indonesia's "fashion capital" and its factory outlets line the avenues. More interesting culturally, Jalan Braga is the best-preserved colonial boulevard on Java — its 1920s–1930s Art Deco facades now house cafes and galleries. In the evening, order a ketan bakar (grilled sticky rice) or a soto bandung (beef and vegetable soup) at a local warung.

Day 6

Pangandaran

West Java's beach resort on the Indian Ocean, with a jungle-covered peninsula and the limestone gorges of the Green Canyon

★★★★★
📍 Pangandaran Beach West Java📍 Pangandaran peninsula nature reserve📍 Cukang Taneuh Green Canyon Java📍 Batu Hiu cliff Pangandaran📍 Pangandaran fish market
1

Pangandaran is West Java's main beach resort — a 530-hectare peninsula of which half is a nature reserve protected since 1978. The coast has two sides: a western shore (calm, ideal for swimming) and an eastern shore (stronger waves, better for surfing). The reserve entrance is walkable from town and leads into dense jungle populated by monkeys, giant squirrels and over 150 bird species. Javan deer (Cervus timorensis) and macaques live on the edge of the village.

⏱ 90 min
2

Cukang Taneuh, known to tourists as the Green Canyon, lies 31 km east of Pangandaran. An emerald-green river has carved gorges through limestone draped in dense tropical vegetation — the walls are vertical, often covered in moss and vines. Local operators offer flat-bottomed boat excursions (about 90 minutes from Cijulang) or river-swim outings with fins and mask to explore the submerged walls. The water is clear but swimming against the current is physically demanding.

⏱ 120 min
3

The Batu Hiu promontory ("shark rock"), 14 km northwest of Pangandaran, offers the most dramatic view of Indian Ocean waves crashing against basalt boulders. It's a remarkable sunset spot when the sky is clear.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The Bandung–Pangandaran road crosses the hills of West Java and the terraced rice paddies around Ciamis — allow 4 hours with photo stops. On arrival, the Pangandaran fish market in the late afternoon is a dive into the fishermen's world: the boats come in loaded and prices are haggled out loud. Order your fresh fish grilled directly on the quay.

⏱ 90 min

Day 7

Garut

Mountain town at 750 meters ringed by five volcanoes, with the Papandayan crater and West Java's only Hindu temple

★★★★★
📍 Kawah Papandayan volcano Garut📍 Cangkuang Lake Hindu temple Garut📍 Kampung Pulo Sundanese village Garut📍 Curug Orok waterfall Garut📍 Dodol confection Garut market
1

Garut is a mountain town at 750 meters in a basin surrounded by five volcanoes — Guntur, Galunggung, Talagabodas, Cikuray and Papandayan. Kawah Papandayan (2,665 m) is the most accessible: a road climbs to 2,000 meters and trails reach the active crater in under an hour. The eruptive zone is lunar — patches of bright yellow sulfur, dead pine forests called Hutan Mati (the "dead forest"), and permanent fumaroles. A 2002 eruption created a new depression visible from the trails.

⏱ 180 min
2

Seventeen kilometers north of Garut, Lake Cangkuang holds the only candi (Hindu temple) still standing in West Java — Candi Cangkuang, a Shaivite temple from the 8th century discovered in 1966 in near-ruin and partially restored. On the same lake island sits Kampung Pulo, a village of six houses whose layout follows strict ancestral rules (odd number of buildings, precise orientation) and whose families have been the temple's guardians for generations. Access is by paddle raft from the lakeshore.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Garut is also famous across Java for two specialties: dodol (a dense confection of glutinous rice, coconut milk and palm sugar, cooked in large copper pots over wood fire) and Garut-breed rams raised for ritual fights. The dodol workshops around Rancabango produce hundreds of kilos a day — stalls overflow with decorated gift boxes. Spend a few hours in the evening browsing the central market.

⏱ 90 min

Day 8

Yogyakarta

Java's cultural capital with an active sultanate, Prambanan, gateway to Borobudur, and birthplace of wayang kulit and Javanese batik

★★★★★
📍 Prambanan Hindu temple Yogyakarta📍 Kraton Yogyakarta sultanate palace📍 Malioboro Street Yogyakarta📍 Borobudur Temple from Yogyakarta📍 Merapi volcano Yogyakarta
1

Yogyakarta ("Jogja" to locals) is the cultural capital of Java and one of the few Indonesian cities to have retained an active sultanate after independence. Sultan Sri Sultan Hamengkubuwono X is both head of the traditional Javanese state and governor of the Yogyakarta Special Region. The Kraton (royal palace), built in 1755, remains the residence of the royal families and the living heart of Javanese culture — gamelan, batik, wayang kulit (shadow puppetry) and court dances. Part of it is open to visitors.

⏱ 90 min
2

Prambanan, 17 km east of Yogyakarta, is the largest Hindu temple complex in Indonesia and one of the most imposing in Southeast Asia. Built in the 9th century by the Sanjaya dynasty, it originally comprised 240 temples. The main temple, Candi Prambanan, dedicated to Shiva, stands 47 meters tall. A UNESCO World Heritage Site, it lay abandoned and partially collapsed when a Dutch officer rediscovered it in 1811 — Thomas Stamford Raffles, once again. Arrive in the late afternoon for the orange light on the basalt spires.

⏱ 120 min
3

Malioboro Street is Jogja's legendary commercial artery — one kilometer of batik shops, wayang puppets, silver filigree and becak (pedicabs) connecting the train station to the kraton. It's touristy, lively and essential for a first evening in Yogyakarta. Yogya batik can be told apart from Solo's by its darker tones and stricter geometric motifs.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

In the evening, attend a wayang kulit performance (traditional Javanese shadow puppetry) at the Kraton or one of Yogyakarta's cultural centers. Wayang kulit draws its stories from the Hindu epics Mahabharata and Ramayana — cut-leather puppets are projected onto a stretched white screen, with a gamelan orchestra as the soundtrack. Full performances run through the night, but shorter tourist-oriented shows are given in the evening.

Day 9

Wonosobo

Central Java mountain town, gateway to the volcanic Dieng Plateau and its 7th-century Hindu temples

★☆☆☆☆
📍 Dieng Plateau Hindu temples Wonosobo📍 Candi Arjuna temple complex Dieng📍 Kawah Sikidang crater Dieng📍 Telaga Warna lake Dieng📍 Sikunir Hill sunrise Dieng📍 Tea plantations Dieng Wonosobo
1

The Dieng Plateau ("Di Hyang" in Sanskrit: the abode of the gods) is a volcanic highland at 2,093 meters, wrapped in mist, rice paddies and plantations of tobacco and vegetables. This is where the oldest stone temples on Java stand: the Candi Arjuna complex, eight Hindu shrines built by the Sanjaya dynasty between the 7th and 8th centuries — remnants of an ensemble Raffles estimated at 400 sanctuaries when he visited in 1815, most of which had been submerged by a volcanic lake. They're modest compared to Prambanan, but their age — several centuries before Borobudur — and their isolation on this fog-shrouded plateau give them an atmosphere like nowhere else.

⏱ 90 min
2

Kawah Sikidang is the most accessible of the plateau's active craters — you can walk around it in 30 minutes on a boardwalk trail above the bubbling mud and sulfur geysers. The colors — yellow, orange, white — and the steam rising from the surface are spectacular. The sulfur smell is intense: bring a mask or a scarf.

⏱ 45 min
3

Telaga Warna ("the color-changing lake") gets its name from hues that shift from emerald green to turquoise blue depending on the angle of the sun and the concentration of dissolved sulfur. In fact two adjacent lakes — Telaga Warna and Telaga Pengilon — separated by a strip of vegetation. From the Batu Pandang Ratapan Angin lookout above, you'll see both lakes at once framed by volcanic cliffs.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

If you're sleeping in Wonosobo or on the plateau, set the alarm for 4 AM and hike up Sikunir Hill (about 1 hour in the dark with a headlamp) for a sunrise over the caldera and the surrounding peaks. On a clear day, the volcanoes Sindoro, Sumbing and Merbabu rise above a sea of clouds. It's one of the most photographed vistas in central Java.

⏱ 150 min

Day 10

Solo

Former Mataram capital since 1745, crossroads of classic batik tulis and the only two active Javanese royal courts

★★★★☆
📍 Kraton Kasunanan Solo Surakarta📍 Pura Mangkunegaran Solo📍 Laweyan batik quarter Solo📍 Pasar Klewer batik market Solo📍 Danar Hadi Batik Museum Solo
1

Solo (Surakarta) is the quiet cultural rival of Yogyakarta — less touristy, more authentic in the eyes of the Javanese themselves. The city was founded in 1745 when Sultan Pakubuwono II moved his court from Kartasura (whose palace had been burned during a Chinese revolt in 1743) to the village of Solo, renamed Surakarta. Twenty years later, the Treaty of Giyanti in 1755 split the Mataram kingdom in two: the Surakarta court in the east, the Yogyakarta court in the west — a schism that still explains the cultural rivalry between the two cities today.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Kraton Kasunanan (established in 1745) is the residence of the Susuhunan — the Sultan of Surakarta — whose current descendant, Pakubuwono XIII, still lives in the complex. Part of the pavilions are open to the public and display gilded royal carriages, ceremonial armor and kris (ritual daggers) set in carved wooden cases. The 2006 earthquake damaged several pavilions; restoration is ongoing. The alleyways surrounding the kraton feature a unique Dutch-Javanese colonial architecture.

⏱ 75 min
3

Pura Mangkunegaran is the palace of Solo's second royal house, founded in 1757 after a succession dispute with the main Kraton. Open to the public since 2023, it's regarded by many as the best-maintained Javanese palace — its collections of dance masks, gold jewelry and antique batik pieces are displayed in rooms still used for royal ceremonies. The Pracimasana restaurant in the courtyard serves Javanese dishes amid the royal pavilions.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

In the afternoon, dive into the Laweyan batik quarter — the oldest and most authentic in Solo. The workshops producing batik tulis (hand-drawn batik using a tool called a canting) have been active here since the 17th century. Solo batik is distinguished by its sogan tones (golden brown derived from a tropical tree) on white or ivory backgrounds and its codified geometric motifs. The Danar Hadi Museum (Jl. Slamet Riyadi) displays a collection of 1,078 batik pieces from every region of Java, including 19th-century royal fabrics — one of the largest private collections in the world. Watch a batik cap (stamped batik) demonstration or sign up for a 2-hour workshop to create your own motif.

⏱ 120 min

Day 11

Solo

Former Mataram capital since 1745, crossroads of classic batik tulis and the only two active Javanese royal courts

★★★★☆
📍 Sangiran archaeological site Java📍 Candi Sukuh erotic temple Solo📍 Pasar Gede central market Solo📍 Adi Sumarmo Airport Solo departure📍 Bengawan Solo River promenade
1

Eighteen kilometers north of Solo, the Sangiran archaeological site has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1996 — one of the most important prehistoric sites in the world, with the remains of 70 Homo erectus individuals (also known as "Java Man") representing over 50% of all known fossils of this species worldwide. The site was identified in 1936 by German-Dutch paleontologist G.H.R. von Koenigswald, who found the first Sangiran Homo erectus fossils there. Eugène Dubois had discovered the species at Trinil, further east, as early as 1891. A modern on-site museum traces human evolution using the artifacts found nearby.

⏱ 90 min
2

Candi Sukuh, perched at 910 meters on the slopes of Mount Lawu 36 km east of Solo, is the most mysterious of all Java's temples. Built in the 15th century — just decades before the fall of Java's last Hindu empire — it's covered in erotic and symbolic sculptures, some of which seem to have absorbed pre-Hindu Batak influences. Its pyramidal shape resembles Mayan temples more than the candis of Prambanan. The road up through the tea plantations is magnificent.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Before heading to Adi Sumarmo Airport (Solo), browse Pasar Gede (Solo's grand central market), a 1930 Art Deco building designed by Dutch architect Thomas Karsten. The main hall, covered by a metal-and-glass canopy, is lined with stalls of exotic fruit, spices, jamu (traditional Javanese herbal medicines) and local snacks like serabi (rice-flour pancakes with coconut milk). It's the best place to pick up last souvenirs of Java before your flight.

⏱ 90 min
