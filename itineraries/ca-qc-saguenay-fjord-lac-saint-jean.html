⏳ How many days should I plan?

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Summer (June-August): Peak tourist season, warmest weather, outdoor activities, highest prices, long daylight hours, festival season.

Fall (September-October): Spectacular autumn colors, comfortable temperatures, harvest season, fewer crowds, excellent photography.

Winter (December-March): Winter sports paradise, Northern Lights, Christmas markets, cold temperatures, budget accommodations, unique experiences.

Spring (April-May): Mild weather returning, fewer crowds, moderate prices, wildflower blooms, shoulder season advantages.

Regional Variations: West Coast mild year-round, Prairies continental extremes, Maritimes maritime climate, North extremely cold.

🌍 What to discover in CA?

Vast wilderness, multicultural cities, and natural wonders from Niagara Falls to Northern Lights. Experience French culture in Quebec, Rocky Mountain adventures, and friendly Canadian hospitality. The country offers incredible outdoor recreation and diverse cultural experiences across massive landscapes. Canada provides safety, beauty, and adventure in North America's northern giant.

📋 Important notes

Loop departing from and returning to Québec City. Route 175 crosses the Laurentides Wildlife Reserve for 200 km with no villages or gas stations—fill up before departure. Two return options offered on day 9: via Mauricie or via Tadoussac. 100% nature and regional terroir circuit.

Day 1

Jacques Cartier

Where a 550-meter glacial valley begins thirty minutes from the city

★★★★★
📍 Parc national de la Jacques-Cartier Québec📍 Vallée de la Jacques-Cartier📍 Sentier Les Loups Jacques-Cartier📍 Sentier Éperon Jacques-Cartier📍 Rivière Jacques-Cartier canoë kayak
1

Route 175 North from Québec City: after 30 minutes, you enter the Laurentides Wildlife Reserve, a vast boreal forest stretching 200 km between Québec and the Saguenay. No villages, very few gas stations along this stretch—fill up before you leave. The four-lane highway cuts through a landscape of lakes, black spruce, and rounded hills sculpted by glaciers.

⏱ 30 min
2

Jacques-Cartier National Park (670 km², established 1981): just 30 minutes from Québec City, the park protects one of Québec's most dramatic glacial valleys. The Jacques-Cartier Valley drops more than 550 meters—the walls plunge from a high mountain plateau down to a meandering river at the bottom. Up top, boreal conifer forest; in the valley floor, deciduous trees (sugar maples, yellow birch) thrive in a sheltered microclimate.

⏱ 30 min
3

Hike the Loups Trail (11 km, 447 m elevation gain, difficult): the park's most spectacular viewpoint, with a bird's-eye view down to the Jacques-Cartier Valley and river far below. Shorter alternative: the Éperon Trail (5.4 km, 250 m gain, moderate) also delivers stunning views over the valley and the river at l'Épaule.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

Canoe or kayak descent on the Jacques-Cartier River: the river alternates between calm stretches and rapids. The park offers boat rentals and a shuttle service for downstream runs (up to 26 km). A unique way to experience the valley from below, framed by forested walls rising on both sides.

⏱ 120 min

Day 2

Saguenay

Chicoutimi's pulpery heritage and the memory of the '96 flood

★★★★★
📍 La Pulperie de Chicoutimi musée📍 Petite Maison Blanche Chicoutimi📍 Rue Racine Chicoutimi📍 Chicoutimi Saguenay centre-ville📍 Déluge du Saguenay 1996
1

Arrival in Chicoutimi, one of three boroughs that form the City of Saguenay (along with Jonquière and La Baie). La Pulperie de Chicoutimi: built between 1896 and 1930, this former pulp mill was once the world's largest. The complex of brick and stone buildings along the Chicoutimi River has been converted into a regional museum showcasing industrial history, contemporary Québécois art, and the house of Arthur Villeneuve—a barber-turned-painter who transformed his entire home into a work of art by covering every surface—walls, ceiling, floors—with naïve paintings.

⏱ 90 min
2

La Petite Maison Blanche (The Little White House): this modest wooden home became a symbol of resilience after the July 1996 Saguenay Flood. For 50 hours, torrential rains dumped approximately 270 mm of water on the region, triggering floods and landslides that killed 10 people, forced the evacuation of 16,000, and caused $1.5 billion in damage. Everything around the Little White House was swept away—but it stood firm, alone in the middle of devastated ground. It's now a museum dedicated to the disaster.

⏱ 45 min
3

Rue Racine, Chicoutimi's main downtown artery: restaurants, cafés, art galleries, and local shops. This is the beating heart of urban Saguenay, with its summer terraces and colorful storefronts. Chicoutimi takes its name from the Innu word 'Ishkutimiu,' meaning 'up to where it is deep'—a reference to the Saguenay River, which becomes navigable at this point.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Evening at Saguenay's microbreweries: the region boasts several craft breweries that highlight boreal ingredients—beers brewed with wild blueberries, boreal forest herbs, or local honey.

⏱ 60 min

Day 3

Anse Saint Jean

A covered bridge and marina where the river meets the fjord

★★☆☆☆
📍 L'Anse-Saint-Jean village Québec📍 Pont couvert L'Anse-Saint-Jean📍 La Baie Saguenay secteur historique📍 Marina L'Anse-Saint-Jean fjord📍 Fjord du Saguenay rive sud
1

La Baie, a Saguenay borough at the head of Ha! Ha! Bay (the name comes from old French 'ha ha,' denoting a dead end or unexpected obstacle—navigators discovered the bay led nowhere). This was once a major timber-shipping port. The historic district preserves buildings from the industrial era and offers views across the bay to the fjord entrance.

⏱ 45 min
2

Route 170 toward L'Anse-Saint-Jean, hugging the fjord's south shore. The road winds through boreal forest punctuated by glimpses of the fjord's dark waters below.

⏱ 30 min
3

L'Anse-Saint-Jean: often ranked among Québec's most beautiful villages, this hamlet of about 1,200 is nestled at the head of a cove where the Saint-Jean River flows into the fjord. Its covered bridge, built in 1929, was so iconic it appeared on the old Canadian $1,000 bill. The marina offers spectacular views of the fjord walls rising on either side.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Sea kayaking on the fjord from L'Anse-Saint-Jean: paddle at the base of cliffs soaring more than 300 meters above the water, in a fjord that reaches 275 meters deep. Surface water is fresh (it comes from Lac Saint-Jean), while salt water from the Atlantic occupies the depths.

⏱ 150 min

Day 4

Riviere Eternite

Where Caps Trinité and Éternité tower above the dark water

★★★★★
📍 Parc national du Fjord-du-Saguenay Baie-Éternité📍 Cap Trinité Saguenay📍 Cap Éternité Saguenay📍 Statue Notre-Dame-du-Saguenay📍 Sentier de la Statue Cap Trinité
1

Baie-Éternité sector of Saguenay Fjord National Park (328 km², established 1983): this is where the fjord reaches its most spectacular dimensions. Two rocky capes face each other across the water: Cap Trinité (518 m) and Cap Éternité (457 m). Their Precambrian rock walls plunge almost vertically into the fjord, which reaches its maximum depth of 275 meters here.

⏱ 30 min
2

Hike the Statue Trail (7.8 km round-trip, 350 m elevation gain, moderate to difficult): the trail climbs through boreal forest to the summit of Cap Trinité, where the statue of Notre-Dame-du-Saguenay stands. This statue made of pine covered with lead sheets, 7.5 meters tall, was installed in 1881 at the cliff's edge. It was commissioned by Charles-Napoléon Robitaille, a traveling salesman from Chicoutimi who had promised to erect it if he survived nearly drowning in the Saguenay's icy waters. Sculptor Louis Jobin created the work in three blocks of white pine, hollowed out to reduce weight. Hoisting it up the cliff face—cut into 14 pieces, raised 180 meters above the water using pulleys and beams—took eight days.

⏱ 210 min
3

The Saguenay Fjord stretches approximately 105 km between Saint-Fulgence and Tadoussac. It's the southernmost navigable fjord in North America and one of the world's longest. Its waters have a unique feature: a thin layer of fresh water (7% of the volume) from Lac Saint-Jean floats atop a mass of salt water that entered from the Atlantic via the St. Lawrence River. This stratification creates a unique ecosystem frequented by about 900 belugas that regularly visit the fjord and its mouth (the entire St. Lawrence estuary population numbers about 1,700 individuals).

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Boat or zodiac cruise on the fjord from Baie-Éternité: navigating at the base of Caps Trinité and Éternité gives you the true measure of these 300-meter cliffs. Your gaze travels upward endlessly. In summer, seals sometimes lounge on rocks at water level.

⏱ 120 min

Day 5

Saint Fulgence

Cliffside via ferrata and treehouses suspended above the fjord

★☆☆☆☆
📍 Sainte-Rose-du-Nord village fjord📍 Parc Aventures Cap Jaseux Saint-Fulgence📍 Via ferrata Cap Jaseux📍 Saint-Fulgence Saguenay📍 Parc national des Monts-Valin
1

Sainte-Rose-du-Nord, nicknamed 'the pearl of the fjord': this village of about 400 is tucked into a narrow cove, framed by two forested rocky capes. You reach it via a winding road that drops sharply toward the fjord. The small Nature Museum displays collections of rocks, minerals, and natural specimens from the region. The observation wharf offers a direct view of the fjord's dark waters and the cliffs on the opposite shore.

⏱ 60 min
2

Parc Aventures Cap Jaseux in Saint-Fulgence: an outdoor adventure site perched on the fjord cliffs. The park offers a via ferrata (climbing route on rock face with cables and rungs bolted to the rock), unusual suspended accommodations in the trees (treehouses and glass domes), and sea kayaking on the fjord. The site overlooks the Saguenay directly—the views from the aerial routes are dizzying.

⏱ 150 min
3

Saint-Fulgence marks the fjord's western end—this is where the Saguenay River ceases to be a fjord and becomes an ordinary river. For experienced hikers, Monts-Valin National Park is accessible 45 minutes north. In winter, its summit is famous for the 'Valley of the Ghosts': frost and snow-covered trees take on spectral shapes.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Sea kayaking at sunset on the fjord from Cap Jaseux: paddle between the fjord's rocky walls as the low-angle light paints the cliffs in orange hues. The silence is broken only by the sound of paddles and the cry of gulls.

⏱ 120 min
La vieille ferme, Écogîte9.8

Day 6

Alma

Gateway to the Lake Tour and Pointe-Taillon's endless beaches

★★★★★
📍 Alma Lac-Saint-Jean📍 Île d'Alma passerelle piétonne📍 Parc national de la Pointe-Taillon📍 Tour du Lac-Saint-Jean véloroute📍 Lac Saint-Jean plage
1

Alma, gateway to the Tour du Lac-Saint-Jean: this city of 30,000 sits where the Grande Décharge River exits the lake to become, farther downstream, the Saguenay River. Île d'Alma, accessible by a pedestrian footbridge, offers a first encounter with the lake's immensity. The Tour du Lac is a marked 263-km circuit—Québec's 19th official tourist route—that makes a complete loop around Lac Saint-Jean.

⏱ 60 min
2

Pointe-Taillon National Park (92 km², established 1985): a flat peninsula jutting into Lac Saint-Jean, bordered by 15 km of sandy beaches. The park also protects bogs and marshes where numerous birds nest. Lac Saint-Jean covers 1,100 km²—43 km long by 34 km wide. It's a true inland sea fed by about ten rivers that drain an immense boreal forest watershed. In summer, its shallow waters warm enough for swimming.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Swimming or cycling on Pointe-Taillon Park trails: the park connects to the Véloroute des Bleuets, a separate 256-km cycling route that circles the lake (not to be confused with the 263-km road Tour du Lac, which follows Route 169 for cars). The fine sand beaches and warm lake water feel more like coastal shores than the interior of the Canadian Shield.

⏱ 120 min

Day 7

Saint Felicien

Ghost villages, wild zoo, and Ilnu heritage at Mashteuiatsh

★★★★★
📍 Village historique Val-Jalbert📍 Chute Ouiatchouan Val-Jalbert📍 Mashteuiatsh Musée Ilnu📍 Zoo sauvage de Saint-Félicien📍 Lac Saint-Jean rive ouest
1

Val-Jalbert Historic Village: an authentic ghost town, not a reconstruction. In 1901, Damase Jalbert founded a pulp mill here at the foot of Ouiatchouan Falls, whose hydraulic power ran the machinery. An entire village grew around it: workers' houses, general store, school, convent, hotel. At its peak, about 80 families lived here. When the mill closed in 1927—due to falling pulp prices and competition—everyone left. The buildings remained as they were, frozen in time. Today you walk through the streets of a 1920s village, past houses with empty windows and a ruined mill.

⏱ 150 min
2

Ouiatchouan Falls, in the heart of Val-Jalbert: 72 meters high—20 meters taller than Niagara Falls. The name comes from the Innu 'Ouiatchouan,' meaning 'rapids with white foam.' A cable car or a 764-step staircase leads to the summit, where the view sweeps across Lac Saint-Jean and the village below. A glass-floor viewing platform lets you stand above the void, right in the heart of the cascade. Upstream, a second waterfall (Maligne Falls, 49 m) is accessible via trail.

⏱ 60 min
3

Mashteuiatsh (formerly Pointe-Bleue): community of the Pekuakamiulnuatsh First Nation, on Lac Saint-Jean's western shore. It's the region's only Indigenous community. The Ilnu Museum, founded in 1977, traces more than 6,000 years of Pekuakamiulnuatsh history—the Innu people of Lac Saint-Jean. The permanent exhibition 'Tshilanu Ilnuatsh' (We, the Ilnuatsh), winner of a Canadian Museums Association Award of Excellence, explores culture and way of life through the seasons, guided by the voices of Kukum (grandmother) and Mushum (grandfather).

⏱ 90 min
4

Zoo sauvage de Saint-Félicien (485 hectares): a zoo that reverses the roles. The 'Sentiers de la nature' trail is experienced aboard a small mesh-enclosed train that crosses vast enclosures of caribou, black bears, wolves, bison, and wapiti—visitors are 'caged,' animals roam semi-free in natural habitats. More than 85 species, mainly boreal, coexist in this zoo that presents itself as a living museum of Canadian Shield fauna.

⏱ 150 min

Activities

Tasting of Lac-Saint-Jean regional products: artisan cheeses (including the famous Perron cheddar, made in Saint-Prime since 1890), tourtière du Lac-Saint-Jean (a slow-cooked meat pie different from tourtière elsewhere in Québec), and local microbrewery beers.

⏱ 60 min

Day 8

Peribonka

Where Maria Chapdelaine lived and wild blueberries blanket the earth

★★☆☆☆
📍 Dolbeau-Mistassini capitale bleuet📍 Péribonka Maria Chapdelaine musée📍 Parc régional des Grandes-Rivières Péribonka📍 Bleuetières Lac-Saint-Jean📍 Louis Hémon Péribonka
1

Dolbeau-Mistassini, which bills itself as the 'wild blueberry capital of the world.' Every August, the Blueberry Festival celebrates this small fruit emblematic of the region. Blueberry fields—endless expanses where wild lowbush blueberries (Vaccinium angustifolium) grow—cover thousands of hectares around the lake. In season (July-August), you can pick your own in fields open to the public.

⏱ 60 min
2

Péribonka: this village on Lac Saint-Jean's north shore houses the museum dedicated to Louis Hémon's novel Maria Chapdelaine. Hémon was a Breton writer who arrived in Canada in 1911 and settled in Péribonka in 1912 as a farmhand with the Bédard family. In a few months he wrote a novel about the lives of pioneer settlers clearing land around Lac-Saint-Jean—the cold, the isolation, the attachment to the land. Hémon left Péribonka in June 1913 and died tragically on July 8, struck by a train in Ontario, at age 33. The novel, published posthumously in 1914, was translated into more than 25 languages and became one of the most famous texts about rural Québec.

⏱ 60 min
3

Parc régional des Grandes-Rivières du lac Saint-Jean: located at the confluence of the Mistassini and Péribonka rivers, two of the most powerful rivers feeding the lake. The park offers hiking trails along the riverbanks, kayaking, and observation points overlooking the rapids. The Péribonka River, made famous by Hémon's novel, flows 451 km from deep in the boreal forest.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Wild blueberry picking in the region's blueberry fields (in season, mid-July to mid-August): lowbush blueberry fields stretch as far as the eye can see, covering the ground in a blue-purple carpet. Lac-Saint-Jean blueberries are smaller and more flavorful than cultivated blueberries—they're used to make pies, jams, wines, and local liqueurs.

⏱ 90 min

Day 9

La Tuque

Deep in the Saint-Maurice Valley where the boreal forest breathes

★☆☆☆☆
📍 La Tuque Mauricie📍 Route 155 vallée Saint-Maurice📍 Rivière Saint-Maurice forêt boréale📍 Parc national de la Mauricie
1

OPTION A - Return via Route 155 (Saint-Maurice Valley): this spectacular 230-km road follows the Saint-Maurice River through nearly pristine boreal forest. The Saint-Maurice River, once the highway for log drivers floating timber down to mills in Trois-Rivières, flows 563 km from the Gouin Reservoir. The road follows its meanders, rapids, and lake-like widenings. The route's most striking moment: when you leave Lac Saint-Jean from Chambord and the immense expanse of water appears suddenly, 'like the sea.'

⏱ 60 min
2

Stop in La Tuque, a small forestry town on the Saint-Maurice—a rest and refueling point midway. The town was an important log-driving center and remains shaped by the forest industry. Félix-Antoine Savard, author of the novel Menaud, Master Logger (1937) celebrating Saint-Maurice log drivers, drew on this region for his work.

⏱ 30 min
3

OPTION B - Return via Chicoutimi → Tadoussac (Route 172 East): this alternative follows the Saguenay Fjord's north shore to its mouth. Stop at the Sainte-Marguerite River, where belugas regularly visit the shallow cove waters (observable from a platform on shore). Arrival in Tadoussac, at the confluence of the Saguenay and St. Lawrence, is one of Québec's most beautiful landscapes. This option is recommended if you haven't done the Eastern Canada road trip and want to discover Tadoussac.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

If option A: stop at La Mauricie National Park (536 km², midway between La Tuque and Trois-Rivières) if time allows—maple forest, lakes, and waterfalls in the Laurentian hills.

Day 10

Quebec

★★★★★
📍 Québec ville Vieux-Québec📍 Trois-Rivières Mauricie📍 Autoroute 40 Québec
1

If option A: Route 155 toward Trois-Rivières, then Highway 40 East toward Québec. The final stretch crosses the St. Lawrence plain, with its farmland and riverside villages. Arrival in Québec City by early afternoon.

⏱ 30 min
2

If option B: Tadoussac → Québec via Route 138 West through Charlevoix (about 3 hours). The road hugs the St. Lawrence River, passing through Baie-Saint-Paul and the rolling landscapes of Charlevoix—a region designated a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve. Arrival in Québec City late afternoon.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Final stroll through Old Québec to close the loop: the 1,200-km circuit through boreal forest, fjord, and lake ends where it began, at the foot of the ramparts of North America's only fortified city.
