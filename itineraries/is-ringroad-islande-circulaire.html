Reykjavik

★★★★☆
1

You'll spot Hallgrímskirkja from afar - this 74.5-meter concrete church dominates all of Reykjavik from atop Skólavörðuhæð hill. Designed by Guðjón Samúelsson in 1937, it took 41 years to complete (1945-1986), financed solely by parishioner donations. Its facade echoes the basalt columns found throughout Iceland. Take the elevator to the top of the tower: the 360° view over the city and Faxaflói bay is the best in the capital. In front of the church stands the statue of Leif Eriksson - gifted by the United States in 1930 for the thousandth anniversary of the Althing.

⏱ 45 min
2

Walk down Skólavörðustígur street toward the harbor until you reach Harpa, the concert hall inaugurated in 2011 after a troubled construction interrupted by the 2008 financial crisis. The south facade features 714 glass panels designed by artist Ólafur Elíasson - each panel unique. The building won the Mies van der Rohe Award in 2013. Entry is free: push through the doors to admire the play of light in the lobby, especially late in the day when sunlight strikes the windows.

⏱ 30 min
3

Continue along the waterfront eastward to reach the Sun Voyager (Sólfarið), this stainless steel sculpture resembling a Viking longship. Installed in 1990, it faces Mount Esja across the bay. It's one of Reykjavik's most iconic photo spots - come late in the day for golden light on the water.

⏱ 20 min
4

Head back toward the center via Laugavegur, Reykjavik's main shopping street. Take time to browse Icelandic design shops, thrift stores, and cafés. If you still have energy, detour to Lake Tjörnin where locals feed swans and ducks - in winter, the lake freezes over and residents skate on it.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

In the evening, head down to the Old Harbour to explore the docks where fishing boats share space with whale-watching vessels. Several seafood restaurants line the harbor - order the plokkfiskur, a traditional Icelandic fish casserole. If you're here in summer, the sun barely sets: enjoy the evening light bathing the bay.

⏱ 75 min

Vik

★★★★☆
1

Marathon day - plan an early departure (before 8am) to see everything. Early morning start for the Golden Circle. First stop: Þingvellir National Park, UNESCO-listed since 2004. This is where Vikings founded the Althing in 930 - the world's oldest parliament still in existence. You'll literally walk between the Eurasian and North American tectonic plates, through a rift valley widening by 2 cm per year. The main trail through Almannagjá gorge runs about 1 km - allow 30 to 45 minutes.

⏱ 60 min
2

Head to Haukadalur, the geothermal area where the Strokkur geyser erupts every 5 to 10 minutes to a height of 15 to 20 meters. Its neighbor Geysir, which gave its name to the phenomenon in all languages, is now nearly dormant. Position yourself opposite the wind to avoid spray - and don't get too close, the water reaches nearly 100°C.

⏱ 40 min
3

Third stop on the Golden Circle: Gullfoss, the 'golden falls.' The Hvítá river plunges in two tiers down 32 meters into a 70-meter-deep canyon. On clear days, the spray creates a permanent rainbow. Take the path descending closest to the lower falls - you'll get soaked, but the spectacle is worth it. The site's history is equally remarkable: in the early 20th century, Sigríður Tómasdóttir threatened to throw herself into the falls to prevent a hydroelectric dam project.

⏱ 45 min
4

Descending toward the south coast, stop at Seljalandsfoss. This 65-meter waterfall has a unique feature: a trail allows you to walk behind the curtain of water. Bring waterproof clothing - you'll get drenched. The spectacle is particularly beautiful at sunset when light shines through the water veil. Just nearby, don't miss Gljúfrabúi, a 40-meter waterfall hidden in a narrow canyon - you must wade through a stream to reach it.

⏱ 40 min
5

Last waterfall of the day: Skógafoss, 30 km further. At 25 meters wide and 60 meters high, it's a perfect wall of water. On sunny days, a double rainbow forms in the spray. Climb the 527 steps of the lateral staircase to reach the viewpoint: the vista plunges across the coastal plain to the ocean and Westman Islands. According to legend, the first Viking settler Þrasi hid a chest filled with gold behind the waterfall - a ring from the chest is now displayed at the Skógar folk museum next door.

⏱ 50 min

Activities

If the day is long in summer (the sun doesn't set before 11pm in June), make a detour to the Sólheimasandur plane wreck. This US Navy DC-3 crashed on the black sand beach in 1973 - the entire crew survived. The fuselage has lain there since, battered by wind. Allow 45 minutes of walking from the parking area on a flat but windy trail.

⏱ 90 min

Vik

★★★★☆
1

Reynisfjara is probably Iceland's most spectacular beach. The sand is deep black - volcanic ash from nearby Katla - and the hexagonal basalt columns at the foot of Reynisfjall mountain form a sort of giant organ 20 meters high. You can enter Hálsanefshellir cave at low tide, but be extremely careful: 'sneaker waves' have caused several deaths here. Never turn your back to the ocean and stay at least 30 meters from the water.

⏱ 60 min
2

Offshore, the Reynisdrangar rock stacks rise from the Atlantic. According to Icelandic folklore, they're trolls petrified by the rising sun while attempting to drag a ship to shore. The site served as a filming location for Game of Thrones and Star Wars. If you're here in summer (May-August), watch for puffins nesting on the cliffs and stacks - they return late in the day.

⏱ 30 min
3

Take the road to Dyrhólaey, the promontory west of Vík. This 120-meter-high natural arch juts into the sea - boats could pass beneath it in the past. From the summit, views sweep the entire south coast, from Reynisfjara to the Mýrdalsjökull and Eyjafjallajökull glaciers. In summer, the area closes in the evening to protect nesting puffins.

⏱ 45 min
4

Finish at the village of Vík í Mýrdal, the southernmost settlement in mainland Iceland with 300 residents. Climb to the little red church Víkurkirkja overlooking the village - from here you'll have the best view of the Reynisdrangar and the black beach below. The village lives under threat from Katla, a subglacial volcano whose last major eruption dates to 1918.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

For hiking enthusiasts, a walk along the Reynisfjall ridge connects Reynisfjara beach to Vík village from above. The trail offers vertiginous panoramas of the coast on both sides. Bring hiking boots and a windbreaker - wind blows hard on the ridge.

⏱ 120 min

Hofn

★★★☆☆
1

Leaving Vík eastward, stop at Fjaðrárgljúfur canyon, 6 km off Route 1. This canyon - 100 meters deep and 2 km long - was carved by glacial erosion about 9,000 years ago. A marked trail follows the rim with several viewing platforms. If the site seems familiar, there's a reason: Justin Bieber shot a music video here in 2015, causing such an influx of visitors that the site had to close temporarily for regeneration.

⏱ 40 min
2

Continue to Skaftafell in Vatnajökull National Park. From there, take the hiking trail to Svartifoss, the 'black falls.' At 20 meters high, it tumbles before an amphitheater of dark basalt columns - this waterfall inspired the architect of Hallgrímskirkja. The trail runs about 5.5 km round trip with 120 meters elevation gain. Allow 1.5 to 2 hours.

⏱ 90 min
3

Jökulsárlón is the highlight of the day. This 18 km² glacial lagoon didn't exist before 1935 - it formed as the Breiðamerkurjökull glacier, a tongue of Vatnajökull (Europe's largest ice cap), retreated. Now 284 meters deep (Iceland's deepest lake), it's filled with blue, white, and black icebergs, some composed of ice over 1,000 years old. From the parking area, walk along the western shore to escape the crowds - in 10 minutes you'll be practically alone facing the icebergs. With luck, you'll see seals lounging on the ice.

⏱ 75 min
4

Just across Route 1, Diamond Beach awaits. Icebergs drifting from the lagoon to sea get tumbled and polished by waves, then wash up on the black sand. The contrast between translucent ice and volcanic sand is striking - come at sunrise or sunset for the best colors. Don't climb on the ice blocks - they can tip over.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Operators offer amphibious boat or zodiac excursions on Jökulsárlón lagoon to navigate between icebergs. The zodiac tour allows closer approach to the glacier front - allow about 1 hour. Book in advance in summer, places fill quickly.

⏱ 60 min

Egilssta Ir

★★★☆☆
1

Before leaving Höfn, take time to tour the harbor. This small fishing town of 2,000 inhabitants is the Icelandic lobster capital (humar, actually langoustine). From the docks, the view of Vatnajökull on clear days is spectacular - the ice cap appears to fall directly into the sea.

⏱ 30 min
2

The road between Höfn and Egilsstaðir crosses the East Fjords, one of Iceland's least visited regions. Route 1 winds between mountains and deep fjords, with fishing villages clinging to the coast. Make regular photo stops - each bend reveals a new panorama. At Djúpivogur, a tiny village of 450 residents, don't miss the series of giant stone eggs along the harbor, reproducing eggs of 34 local bird species.

⏱ 45 min
3

From Egilsstaðir, make the 27 km detour to Seyðisfjörður, the most beautiful village in the East Fjords. The road descends a mountain pass before plunging toward the fjord's end where a colorful artists' village awaits. Spot the little blue church (Bláa kirkjan) at the end of the main street paved with a rainbow path. The village hosts the ferry to Denmark - if a ship is in port, the atmosphere changes completely.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

In Egilsstaðir, a short but rewarding hike leads to Fardagafoss waterfall. The trail runs 2 km one way with moderate elevation gain - allow about 30 minutes climbing. The waterfall tumbles into a rocky amphitheater with valley views. Continue a few minutes beyond to reach a second smaller waterfall, even more peaceful.

⏱ 75 min

If you have time, take a drive around Lake Lagarfljót, Iceland's third-largest lake. At 25 km long and 112 meters deep, it harbors - according to legend - the Lagarfljótsormur, a giant serpent cousin to the Loch Ness monster. In 2012, a video showing a serpentine form in the lake went viral worldwide. Even without a monster, the setting is superb, especially when Hallormsstaður forest (Iceland's largest - which says a lot about the country) borders the shore.

⏱ 45 min

Myvatn

★★★★☆
1

From Egilsstaðir, take Route 862 to Dettifoss, Europe's most powerful waterfall. Water from the glacial river Jökulsá á Fjöllum - fed by Vatnajökull - plunges 44 meters high and 100 meters wide with an average flow of 200 m³/s, which can double in summer. You'll hear the roar well before seeing it, and the mist is visible for kilometers. Approach from the west bank (Route 862, paved): the trail from parking runs 1.5 km. No barriers separate visitors from the canyon edge - stay careful. This is where Ridley Scott filmed the opening scene of Prometheus.

⏱ 60 min
2

One kilometer upstream from Dettifoss, don't miss Selfoss. Smaller (11 meters high) but wider, it forms an arc in the canyon - fewer tourists, more tranquility. The trail connecting the two waterfalls follows the Jökulsá á Fjöllum canyon edge, one of Iceland's most spectacular.

⏱ 30 min
3

If you have time, push on to Ásbyrgi, the horseshoe-shaped canyon 30 km north. At 3.5 km long and 1 km wide, its 100-meter cliffs shelter a birch forest - rare in Iceland. According to Norse mythology, it's the hoofprint of Sleipnir, Odin's eight-legged horse. In reality, the canyon was carved by glacial floods about 10,000 years ago.

⏱ 45 min
4

Arriving in the Mývatn region, detour to Grjótagjá cave. This small lava cave shelters a hot spring naturally heated by geothermal activity. Game of Thrones fans will recognize it - a famous scene between Jon Snow and Ygritte was filmed here. Bathing has been prohibited since Krafla eruptions in the 1970s raised water temperature above 50°C, but the place merits a visit.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Settle in the Mývatn region for the night. If you arrive early enough, drive around the lake (36 km via Route 848) to scout tomorrow's sites. The lake, whose name means 'midge lake,' is Iceland's fourth-largest - in summer, midges feed millions of birds, including 13 breeding duck species. Bring a head net if you're sensitive.

⏱ 45 min

Myvatn

★★★★☆
1

Start with Dimmuborgir, the 'dark castles.' This lava field of improbable shapes formed about 2,300 years ago when a lava flow met a lake - steam sculpted pillars, arches, and caves. The Kirkjuhringur trail (2.6 km, easy) passes through Kirkjan cave whose natural vault resembles a Gothic cathedral interior. This is also where scenes at the Fist of the First Men in Game of Thrones were filmed.

⏱ 60 min
2

Then climb to Hverfjall crater, 15 minutes' walk from Dimmuborgir. This tephra crater formed 2,500 years ago in a single eruption measures 1 km in diameter and 140 meters deep - one of the world's best-preserved circular craters. The ascent is steep (250 m elevation gain, 20-30 minutes) but at the summit, the 360° panorama over Mývatn, lava fields, and Krafla volcanic system is exceptional. The complete rim circuit takes 1 hour.

⏱ 90 min
3

Head to Hverir (Námaskarð), the geothermal field at the base of Námafjall pass. The ground is covered with bubbling gray and yellow mud pots, fumaroles spewing sulfurous steam - and everything gives off a persistent rotten egg smell. The landscape is entirely barren, not a blade of grass, with hues ranging from ochre to bright red. You'd think you were on another planet. The site is right next to Route 1, free and accessible year-round. Stay on marked trails - the earth's crust is thin and water underneath reaches 100°C.

⏱ 45 min
4

Push on to Krafla volcano, 20 minutes north. The Víti crater ('hell' in Icelandic) harbors a striking blue-green lake, formed during an eruption in 1724. Krafla's last eruption series, between 1975 and 1984, profoundly transformed the landscape. From the parking area, the trail to Leirhnjúkur lava field crosses still-steaming flows - some less than 50 years old.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

End this volcanic day with a soak at Mývatn Nature Baths (Jarðböðin). These open-air geothermal baths offer milky blue-green water between 36 and 40°C, with views over the lake and mountains. Less crowded and cheaper than the southern Blue Lagoon, it's the preferred option for travelers who know the country. Open daily until midnight in summer - in June, you can soak under the midnight sun.

⏱ 75 min

Akureyri

★★★☆☆
1

Leaving Mývatn westward, stop at Goðafoss after 45 minutes' drive. This horseshoe waterfall is 30 meters wide and 12 meters high. Its name - 'waterfall of the gods' - dates to the year 1000: when Iceland officially adopted Christianity, chieftain Þorgeir Ljósvetningagoði supposedly threw his statues of Norse gods into the waterfall to mark the break with paganism. Two parking areas provide access to both banks - take the east bank for the best overall view.

⏱ 40 min
2

Akureyri, the 'capital of the north,' has only 19,000 inhabitants but serves as a metropolis in northern Iceland. Climb to Akureyrarkirkja, the church dominating downtown. Designed by the same architect as Reykjavik's Hallgrímskirkja (Guðjón Samúelsson), it houses stained glass from Coventry's old cathedral in England, salvaged after its 1940 bombing. From the forecourt, views plunge over Eyjafjörður fjord, Iceland's longest.

⏱ 30 min
3

Descend to the Lystigarðurinn botanical gardens, the world's northernmost open to the public (at 65°N). Free, they shelter over 7,000 plant species - surprising for this latitude. Open from June to September, it's an unexpected haven of greenery. An on-site café offers cakes and hot drinks overlooking the flowerbeds.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Don't leave Akureyri without visiting the municipal pool (Sundlaug Akureyrar). In Iceland, the pool is a social institution - locals gather to chat and relax. Akureyri's features several heated outdoor pools, waterslides, and a steam bath. Entry costs a few hundred króna and it's one of the best ways to live 'Icelandic-style.'

⏱ 60 min

Stroll the main pedestrian street. Amusing detail: Akureyri's traffic lights are heart-shaped - a 2008 initiative during the financial crisis to lift residents' spirits. Take advantage to taste a kleina (Icelandic doughnut) in one of the downtown cafés.

⏱ 40 min

Siglufjor Ur

★★★☆☆
1

The road to Siglufjörður via the Tröllaskagi peninsula is one of Iceland's most beautiful - and least traveled. Take Route 82 along the coast rather than the tunnel: mountains plunge into fjords, isolated farms dot the landscape, and the road passes through single-lane tunnels carved through rock (yield in the recesses provided for this purpose).

⏱ 30 min
2

Stop at Hofsós for its infinity pool perched on the cliff edge, with direct views over Skagafjörður fjord and Drangey island. The water is 38°C and the pool edge merges with the fjord below - this is probably Iceland's most beautiful pool.

⏱ 60 min
3

Siglufjörður, at the peninsula's end, is a former fishing village nestled at the end of a fjord ringed by mountains. In the 1940s-50s, it was Iceland's most important herring port - the population had climbed to 10,000 during the 'herring boom.' The Herring Era Museum recounts this period with a reconstructed salting factory and fishing boat. The museum won a European Museum Award - astonishing for a village of 1,200 inhabitants.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Enjoy the evening strolling through Siglufjörður. The harbor is surrounded by mountains descending almost vertically into the fjord. In summer, the sun circles the horizon without setting, bathing the village in permanent golden light. A few restaurants serve fresh fish caught that very day.

⏱ 45 min

Borgarnes

★★★☆☆
1

Leaving Siglufjörður westward, detour via the Vatnsnes peninsula to see Hvítserkur. This 15-meter basalt monolith, pierced by two arches, stands in the water a few meters from shore. Its silhouette evokes a rhinoceros lowering its head to drink - Icelanders see it as a troll petrified by the rising sun. Erosion threatened collapse, so its base was reinforced with concrete in 1995. From the parking area, a viewpoint offers a plunging view, but descend to the beach at low tide to approach it. The peninsula is also one of Iceland's best spots for observing seals - watch carefully at Ósar beach.

⏱ 45 min
2

Continue south and stop at Hraunfossar and Barnafoss waterfalls, 70 km before Borgarnes. Hraunfossar is a series of streams gushing directly from a 900-year-old lava field, over nearly a kilometer - water filtered through porous rock and emerges in dozens of crystalline threads tumbling into the Hvítá river. Just next door, Barnafoss (the 'children's falls') owes its name to a legend: two children supposedly disappeared crossing a natural stone arch spanning the waterfall. The arch has since collapsed, but the name remains.

⏱ 40 min
3

End the day in Borgarnes. The Settlement Center recounts in two interactive exhibitions the Viking colonization of Iceland and the Saga of Egill, the most famous Icelandic warrior-poet. It's one of the best introductions to Icelandic history you'll find. Allow 1 hour for both exhibitions.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

If you're a natural hot spring enthusiast, detour to Deildartunguhver, Europe's most powerful hot spring with a flow of 180 liters per second at nearly 100°C. A spa has been developed next door - perfect for relaxing after the long drive from the north.

⏱ 60 min

Stykkisholmur

★★☆☆☆
1

First stop: Kirkjufell, Iceland's most photographed mountain. At 463 meters high, it stands isolated beside the sea, near the fishing village of Grundarfjörður. Its nearly perfect conical shape - sculpted by glaciers - earned it the nickname 'arrowhead mountain' in Game of Thrones (season 7). The best viewpoint is from Kirkjufellsfoss waterfall directly opposite: the three water threads in the foreground with the mountain behind - this is THE Iceland photo. Come early morning or evening to avoid crowds.

⏱ 40 min
2

Descend to the peninsula's south coast to Arnarstapi. The coastal trail between Arnarstapi and Hellnar (2.5 km, 45 minutes) follows natural arches, sea caves, and basalt columns battered by waves. In summer, the cliffs are covered with nests of kittiwakes and fulmars. At Arnarstapi, don't miss Gatklettur, a spectacular stone arch right at the water's edge.

⏱ 60 min
3

Continue to Djúpalónssandur, a black pebble beach at the foot of Snæfellsjökull glacier. Four basalt stones of different weights (23, 54, 104, and 154 kg) once served as strength tests for fishermen: only those who lifted at least the third (104 kg) were allowed to embark. You'll also see the rusted remains of a British trawler wrecked here in 1948.

⏱ 40 min
4

On the return route, stop at Búðakirkja, the little black church of Búðir. Built in 1848, isolated in the middle of a moss-covered lava field, it contrasts strikingly with the landscape. The Snæfellsjökull glacier - the one from Jules Verne's novel 'Journey to the Center of the Earth' (1864) - can be glimpsed at the peninsula's summit when weather is clear.

⏱ 25 min

Activities

If weather permits, operators offer excursions on Snæfellsjökull glacier by snowmobile or on foot. Snæfellsjökull rises to 1,446 meters and constitutes the entry point for the journey imagined by Jules Verne. The guided climb takes about 4 to 5 hours round trip. Book in advance.

⏱ 90 min

Reykjavik

★★★★☆
1

Return to Reykjavik. If you haven't yet visited Perlan, now's the time. This glass dome building, perched on six geothermal hot water tanks, houses an interactive museum on Icelandic nature - with a 100-meter artificial ice cave, planetarium, and 360° observation deck offering one of the best views over the city and mountains.

⏱ 75 min
2

Push through the doors of the National Museum (Þjóðminjasafn Íslands). Its collections cover the country's history from Viking colonization to today. The centerpiece is a 13th-century church door carved with dragons and knights. Allow 1.5 hours for a complete visit.

⏱ 90 min
3

Explore the Grandi district, the old harbor quarter undergoing transformation. You'll find the maritime museum, art galleries, Icelandic design workshops, and a food court with Icelandic and international cuisine stands. It's Reykjavik's liveliest neighborhood for eating and browsing.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

For your last evening, treat yourself to a soak in one of Reykjavik's geothermal pools. Laugardalslaug is the city's largest, with a 50-meter pool, several hot pots at different temperatures, and a waterslide. It's the quintessential Reykjavik socializing spot - sink into the hot water and let go after 12 days on the road.

⏱ 60 min

If you prefer a more exclusive setting, geothermal baths have been developed at the ocean's edge minutes from downtown. Water at 38°C facing the North Atlantic, with views of Mount Esja - a memorable way to conclude your Iceland circuit.

⏱ 75 min
