Cheyenne Wy

★★★☆☆
1

The Wyoming State Capitol's gold-leafed dome has been gilded five times since 1900 - from the north it floats above the city like something European. Inside, the first-floor rotunda has checkerboard marble floors and a cherry-wood staircase shipped from Ohio. The building was finished in 1890, the same year Wyoming became the 44th state, and the second-floor chamber is where delegates voted to enshrine women's suffrage in the state constitution - Wyoming women had held that right since 1869, five decades before the 19th Amendment. Self-guided tours are free, Monday through Friday during business hours.

⏱ 60 min
2

Cheyenne was nothing but open plains until 1867, when Union Pacific drove its tracks west and the city appeared in a matter of months. That story is inside the Cheyenne Depot Museum, housed in the original 1886 railroad station - a National Historic Landmark. The second floor holds a wood-carved Big Boy locomotive model and a full-scale model railroad display; labels cite their sources like footnotes, which is unusual and worth slowing down for. Plan 90 minutes if you read everything.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Cheyenne Frontier Days Old West Museum holds one of the largest collections of horse-drawn carriages in the country and thousands of rodeo artifacts tracing the event to its first day: September 23, 1897, when Union Pacific agent Frederick W. Angier organized a one-day cowboy roundup that drew 4,000 people. By year two, 20,000 showed up. Today it draws 300,000 visitors over 10 days and pays out more than $1 million in prize money. Worth 60-90 minutes even outside rodeo season.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Scattered across downtown Cheyenne are dozens of 8-foot cowboy boots painted by local artists, each depicting a different slice of Wyoming history - the Big Boots art trail. Pick up a location map at the Depot Museum visitor kiosk, or call (307) 316-0067 for a cell phone audio tour where artists explain their own work. The main cluster near the historic district takes 45-60 minutes to cover, and you'll see more of the old town than any guided bus tour would show you.

⏱ 60 min

Curt Gowdy State Park sits 24 miles west in the foothills of the Laramie Mountains - granite outcrops, rainbow trout in Granite Springs Reservoir, pronghorn antelope grazing in sight of the trail, and over 35 miles of mountain biking. It's one of the few spots near Cheyenne where the city genuinely disappears. Day use fee applies.

⏱ 120 min

Laramie Wy

★★☆☆☆
1

Built in 1872 as one of the few federal prisons of its time, the Wyoming Territorial Prison State Historic Site has sandstone walls that still feel oppressive. Of the 44 prisoners taken in during the first two years, 11 escaped. The most famous resident was Butch Cassidy, held here from 1894 to 1896 for grand larceny. After the prison moved to Rawlins in 1901, the University of Wyoming used the building as a livestock experiment station until 1989 - a strange afterlife for a place this grim. The site sits on 197 acres with a nature trail along the Big Laramie River. Fee applies; open Memorial Day through Labor Day.

⏱ 90 min
2

A full Apatosaurus skeleton greets you near the entrance of the University of Wyoming Geological Museum - one of the more complete sauropod specimens on display anywhere in the state. The collection covers Wyoming's rich fossil record including dinosaur trackways and Morrison Formation specimens. Free admission. Allow 45-60 minutes; the historic Romanesque Revival campus buildings are worth a walk afterward.

⏱ 60 min
3

At I-80 Exit 329, sixteen miles east of Laramie, the landscape breaks open into something that looks structurally impossible: 1.4 billion-year-old Sherman Granite stacked in towers up to 500 feet high. The Arapaho called this place 'bito'o'wu' - earth-born - a name popularized by a University of Wyoming drama professor in 1922. Over 900 climbing routes thread the rock; non-climbers can walk the 3.1-mile Turtle Rock Loop through aspen groves with minimal elevation gain. Day use fee applies.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

WY-130 west from Laramie is the day's main event - the Snowy Range Scenic Byway rises to 10,847 feet at Libby Flats, the second-highest mountain pass in Wyoming, designated the second National Forest Scenic Byway in 1988. Lake Marie is the best stop: Medicine Bow Peak (12,013 feet) appears to grow straight out of the water. At Libby Flats, views extend south into Colorado. If you're staying in Laramie tonight, make it an out-and-back; otherwise it's tomorrow's route to Saratoga.

⏱ 180 min

Laramie's historic downtown repays an evening on foot - locally owned shops, colorful murals, and places like Range Leather Co. (handcrafted leather goods) and the Bent and Rusty (antiques and oddities). The town was born in 1868 when Union Pacific arrived; within months it had a population of several thousand. For dinner, try the Crowbar and Grill for hand-tossed pizza or Sweet Melissa for creative vegetarian dishes a block away.

⏱ 90 min

Saratoga

★★☆☆☆
1

In Saratoga's City Park on Walnut Street, a public mineral hot spring sits open 24 hours a day, 365 days a year, free of charge - the Hobo Hot Pool. It ranges from 106°F at its coolest end to 119°F at the 'Lobster Pot.' The current pool was discovered in 1917 after a flood eroded the banks of Spring Creek. Native Americans used the original springs here as neutral territory for centuries; the town was named Saratoga in 1884 after an Iroquois word meaning 'place of miraculous water in the rock.' Restrooms and changing facilities are on site.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Hotel Wolf on Bridge Street has been operating since the late 19th century and remains the social hub of the North Platte Valley. The bar and restaurant serve some of the better steaks in the region. Sit down at lunch and listen to the tables around you - ranchers, outfitters, and visiting anglers talking about water levels and hatch conditions. This is what Saratoga actually is.

⏱ 60 min
3

Five miles north of town on WY-130/230, the Saratoga National Fish Hatchery raises multiple trout species for federal and state hatcheries nationwide and runs a captive breeding program for the endangered Wyoming Toad. Free to visit; a short self-guided tour explains the operation. Context worth having: the North Platte at Saratoga holds over 4,000 catchable wild trout per mile and has not been stocked since 1979.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

A guided float trip on the Upper North Platte is the full-day option - 70 miles of blue-ribbon water holding over 4,000 wild trout per mile (browns and rainbows averaging 13-17 inches), unstocked since 1979. The section from Bennett Peak to Treasure Island to Saratoga runs through canyon scenery and cottonwood-lined banks. Mid-June to early August is peak season when spring runoff clears. Reserve at least a week in advance in summer.

⏱ 480 min

Twenty minutes south of Saratoga lies Encampment, and from there Battle Highway (WY-70) heads west to 'Aspen Alley' - a half-mile stretch of dirt road where tall aspens arch completely overhead, glowing gold in late September. The Grand Encampment Museum covers the 1890s-1900s copper mining boom with over a dozen original buildings; free admission. A 2-3 hour loop that shows a completely different face of Carbon County.

⏱ 150 min

Rawlins Wy

★☆☆☆☆
1

Pull off I-80 in Rawlins for the Wyoming Frontier Prison - Wyoming's first state penitentiary, 80 years in operation from 1901 to 1981, roughly 13,500 inmates including Big Nose George. Guided tours run through the dungeon, solitary confinement cells, and the gas chamber where 14 executions took place. One-hour tours run year-round. The building is on the National Register of Historic Places.

⏱ 60 min
2

Step into the Carbon County Museum a few blocks away - free admission, 30,000 artifacts, and the only place in the world where you can see shoes custom-made from the skin of outlaw Big Nose George Parrot after his 1881 hanging. Exhibits also cover Thomas Edison's visit to the Rawlins area, where he reportedly found inspiration for his lightbulb filament while fishing, and Wyoming women's history. Allow 30-45 minutes.

Lander Wy

★★☆☆☆
1

Six miles southwest of Lander on WY-131, the Middle Fork of the Popo Agie River pulls one of geology's stranger tricks: it disappears into a limestone cavern called 'the Sinks' and reappears a quarter mile downstream at 'the Rise' - taking more than two hours underground to cover that quarter mile. A paved path connects the two sites. At the Rise, a pool holds 8-12 pound rainbow trout protected from fishing since 1919; toss in food and the water churns. Humans have used this canyon for approximately 9,000 years. Free admission.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Fremont County Pioneer Museum in downtown Lander anchors the town's founding logic: Lander was named for Frederick W. Lander, engineer of the Lander Trail used by thousands of westward emigrants. When the Chicago & North Western Transportation Company chose Lander as the western terminus of its 'Cowboy Line' railway in 1906, the phrase stuck - 'where the rails end and the trails begin.' It still fits today. Free admission.

⏱ 45 min
3

Twenty-five miles south of Lander, Wild Iris is one of the best sport-climbing areas in North America - over 300 routes in bone-white dolomite limestone surrounded by aspen groves and wildflower meadows. Todd Skinner and partners first developed it in 1990. Even without climbing shoes, the drive alone is worth it: the meadows are spectacular in June and July, and Lander's International Climbers' Festival, running every July since 1994, is the longest-running climbing festival in the United States.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Canyon Loop Trail in Sinks Canyon crosses the river on a swinging bridge, loops past the Sinks and the Rise, then climbs the north wall for views across the limestone cliffs. The rock type shifts from sandstone to limestone to granite within a few miles as the Wind River Range closes in. Watch for bighorn sheep on the upper cliffs, mule deer in the canyon bottom, and moose near the river in early morning.

⏱ 120 min

Main Street Lander in the evening has a working Western character unusual for a town of 7,500. Wild Iris Mountain Sports at 425 Popo Agie St is worth stopping in - staff have climbed every crag in the region and give genuine advice on the Wind Rivers. For dinner, Lander Brewing Company serves local craft beer alongside bison dishes; the Gannett Grill is the other local anchor.

⏱ 120 min

Thermopolis Wy

★★☆☆☆
1

Hot Springs State Park smells of minerals from the parking lot. The Big Spring - Wyoming's first state park since 1897 - discharges more than 1.8 million gallons of water per day at a constant 128°F, flowing over rainbow-colored travertine terraces in layers of orange, white, and rust. The free public Bath House at the park's core is a condition of a treaty signed that year with the Eastern Shoshone and Northern Arapaho, who sold the land on the condition the waters remain free. The Bath House, cooled to 104°F, limits soaks to 20 minutes. Cross the suspension footbridge over the Bighorn River for the best view of the terraces.

⏱ 90 min
2

In 1993, fossil hunters found dinosaur bones weathering out of the hills on Warm Springs Ranch ten minutes from downtown Thermopolis. The Wyoming Dinosaur Center grew from that discovery and now houses 58 mounted skeletons and over 20,000 bones from the Thermopolis bonebeds - including the Thermopolis Specimen of Archaeopteryx, one of the most complete ever found. Watch scientists at work in the preparation lab year-round; the Dig for the Day program (late May through mid-September) puts you on a real excavation site with actual paleontology tools. Book in advance.

⏱ 120 min
3

Four miles south on US-20, Wind River Canyon closes around the road - sheer walls, the Wind River running alongside, rock layers exposing more than 2.5 billion years of geological history in one of the most complete sequences visible anywhere in Wyoming. Keep your eyes on the upper ledges for bighorn sheep. The canyon runs about 14 miles; allow 20-30 minutes with stops.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

For a longer soak than the 20-minute Bath House limit allows, Star Plunge or Hellie's Tepee Spa charge paid entry but offer waterslides, multiple pools, and no time restrictions - both fed by the same mineral waters. Star Plunge has been operating since 1900. The State Bath House allows a second 20-minute session if you wait two hours between them.

⏱ 90 min

Twenty-one miles northwest of Thermopolis, the Legend Rock Petroglyph Site carries the work of three prehistoric cultural groups on sandstone cliffs spanning roughly 500 AD through the 19th century - hundreds of yards of images. Pick up the gate key at Hot Springs State Park headquarters before leaving town; free to visit. The road turns to gravel for the last few miles. Bring water: it's exposed high desert.

⏱ 90 min

Cody Wy

★★☆☆☆
1

Five museums share one roof at the Buffalo Bill Center of the West, 720 Sheridan Avenue - a Smithsonian Affiliate since 2008 and the oldest museum complex dedicated to the American West, founded in 1917. The five: Buffalo Bill Museum (Wild West show artifacts and personal effects), Cody Firearms Museum (7,000 individual firearms, the most comprehensive collection in the US), Whitney Western Art Museum (Remington, Russell), Draper Natural History Museum (Yellowstone geology and wildlife), and Plains Indian Museum (Arapaho, Lakota, Crow and Cheyenne objects; first curator was George Horse Capture of the A'aninin tribe). Your ticket is valid for two consecutive days.

⏱ 240 min
2

Old Trail Town on the western edge of Cody is 26 historic frontier buildings gathered from original sites across Wyoming by archaeologist Bob Edgar, starting in 1967. The collection includes a fur traders' cabin from 1883, a dance hall, a saloon, and the grave of mountain man John 'Liver-Eating' Johnston. One of the few places in the West where the structures are original, not reconstructed. Paid entry; open May through September.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Irma Hotel on Sheridan Avenue opened in November 1902 - Buffalo Bill Cody's own hotel, built for $80,000 and named after his youngest daughter. At the center of the dining room sits a cherrywood back bar said to have been a gift from Queen Victoria in recognition of a Wild West show command performance at her court; the hotel's current owner acknowledges there is no documentary proof, but the bar has been here since opening day. Walk in for a drink or a meal without booking; the Cody Gunfighters do a free shootout on the front sidewalk at 6 p.m. nightly in summer.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Attend the Cody Nite Rodeo at Stampede Park - every single night from June 1 through August 31 since 1938, making Cody the only town in the United States with a nightly rodeo. Former Wild West show performer Carly Downing founded it, originally calling it the 'Pup Rodeo.' The 6,000-seat arena sits on a terrace above the Shoshone River west of downtown; gates open at 7:00 p.m., rodeo starts at 8:00 p.m. A bus departs from most hotels for ~$7 round-trip. Paid entry; book ahead in peak summer.

⏱ 150 min

West of Cody, US-14/16/20 follows the North Fork of the Shoshone River through 52 miles of Shoshone National Forest that Theodore Roosevelt called 'the most scenic 50 miles in the United States.' Dramatic volcanic formations line both sides. No Yellowstone entrance fee applies if you turn around at the East Gate - back in Cody in 2-3 hours.

⏱ 180 min

Sheridan Wy

★☆☆☆☆
1

Forest Road 12 off US-14A leads to a lower parking lot, from which a 1.5-mile gravel road climbs to the Medicine Wheel: white limestone boulders arranged in a circle 82 feet across, 28 radial spokes extending from a central cairn, six outer cairns at the rim. All of this sits at 9,642 feet and has been visited by Native Americans for at least 7,000 years. Designated a National Historic Landmark in 1970, it remains an active ceremonial site used by tribes from across the Northern Plains - negotiations over the 1996 Historic Preservation Plan involved representatives of 16 tribes. If a ceremony is underway when you arrive, stand back and observe quietly. Access road opens July 1.

⏱ 120 min
2

King's Saddlery at 184 N. Main Street is a working shop, not a museum - leather smell, power tools, artisans building saddles and ropes. Don King opened it in 1963 after learning his trade riding cattle trails through Texas, Arizona and California as a child; his custom saddles are in the Cowboy Hall of Fame and the ProRodeo Hall of Champions. Behind the shop is the Don King Museum - free to enter - with historic saddles, wagons, photographs, and leather-working tools spanning a century of Western craft. Ask the staff to watch them work.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Historic Sheridan Inn at 856 Broadway Street opened in 1893 - built by the Chicago, Burlington and Quincy Railroad and designed by Thomas Kimball after a Scottish hunting lodge. It was the first building in the area with electric lights and bathtubs, and was said at opening to be the finest hotel between Chicago and San Francisco. Buffalo Bill Cody was a part-owner who auditioned Wild West show performers from the wraparound front porch. Reopened in 2015 after a 50-year closure; walk in for a meal or a drink without staying.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The descent into Sheridan via the Bighorn Scenic Byway (US-14) from Burgess Junction to Dayton offers two stops worth pulling over for: Shell Falls, where water plunges 120 feet over granite cliffs with an interpretive walkway, and Steamboat Point, where a steep single-track trail reaches a 360-degree viewpoint over the valley. The full run is 58 miles; allow 90 minutes. US-14 handles all vehicle sizes - US-14A does not.

⏱ 120 min

Sheridan's Main Street in the evening is worth lingering in - the 1923 WYO Theater at 42 N. Main still hosts live performances in a building that newspapers called 'Wyoming's Wonder Picture Palace' when it opened. A few blocks away, the Mint Bar has been a gathering spot since 1907: ranchers, rodeo cowboys, and passing travelers on the same barstools. No performance for tourists; just a working Western town doing what it does.

⏱ 120 min

Casper Wy

★★☆☆☆
1

Pull off I-25 for the National Historic Trails Interpretive Center - free admission, and one of Wyoming's better history museums. Casper sits at the crossing of four western migration corridors: Oregon, California, Mormon Pioneer, and Pony Express trails. The museum covers the 400,000 emigrants who crossed this ground between 1841 and 1868, with hands-on exhibits including a virtual covered wagon ride and a treadmill that simulates pulling a handcart. A 27-minute film addresses US treaty violations with Plains tribes. Allow 75-90 minutes.

⏱ 90 min
2

Fort Caspar Museum sits on the banks of the North Platte River in a reconstructed 1865 military post - named for 2nd Lieutenant Caspar Collins, killed at the Battle of Platte Bridge Station that year. A typo in the 1889 town registration swapped the 'a' for an 'e' in Casper, which is why the town and the fort are spelled differently. The museum covers prehistoric Plains Indians, the frontier army, and the energy industry. Open daily May through September.

⏱ 60 min

Cheyenne Wy

★★★☆☆
1

Thirty minutes north of downtown, the Quebec 01 Missile Alert Facility State Historic Site is the only publicly accessible Peacekeeper Missile Alert Facility remaining in the world. Topside is a self-guided museum of Cold War daily life; underground guided tours take you through the actual launch control room - codes, lockboxes, escape hatches designed to fill with sand after a nuclear blast. The 90th Missile Wing at F.E. Warren Air Force Base still operates Minuteman III missiles today. Free topside; paid entry for the underground tour.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Wyoming Historic Governors' Mansion at 300 East 21st Street is a Colonial Revival building from 1904 that housed 19 Wyoming First Families - among them Nellie Tayloe Ross, the first female governor in United States history, elected in 1924. Docent-led tours move through rooms decorated to represent different eras of occupancy. Open Tuesday through Saturday; free admission.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Nelson Museum of the West downtown holds one of the larger collections of saddles, carriages, and Old West memorabilia in the region - cowboys, lawmen, Native Americans, and soldiers across the frontier period. Free admission for most of the year. Allow 60 minutes and combine with a walk through the historic district nearby.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Cheyenne Street Railway Trolley runs a 90-minute narrated loop of the historic district - railroad quarter, Capitol Hill, historic mansions, Fort D.A. Russell. Quickest way to orient yourself before deciding which buildings to revisit on foot. Runs in summer only; paid entry; departs from Depot Plaza.

⏱ 90 min

Close out the loop at Westby Edge Brewing downtown - local craft beer, the same city where the itinerary started nine days ago. Denver is 90 minutes south on I-25; factor that into your checkout time and don't book a departure before noon.

⏱ 90 min
