Seoul

★★★★★
1

Start at Gyeongbokgung right when it opens—the largest palace of the Joseon Dynasty, covering 100 acres, built in 1395. It was burned by the Japanese in 1592, rebuilt in 1867, then demolished by 80% between 1910 and 1945. Restoration continues to this day. Arrive for the changing of the guard at 11 AM. If you're wearing a hanbok, admission is free.

⏱ 120 min
2

Head north to Bukchon—900 traditional wooden houses from the 1920s-1940s, wedged between two palaces. During Japanese colonization, a Korean developer deliberately built these hanok to prevent Japanese buyers from acquiring the land. The alleys of Gahoe-dong offer the classic view of curved rooftops with N Seoul Tower in the background. Access restricted 10 AM-5 PM since November 2024—speak quietly, people actually live here.

⏱ 75 min
3

Finish at Changdeokgung, the best-preserved of Seoul's five palaces, a UNESCO World Heritage site. Built in 1405, it replaced Gyeongbokgung for 250 years after its destruction. The Secret Garden to the north—80 acres of pavilions, ponds, and forests—can only be visited by reservation in guided groups. Book ahead.

⏱ 90 min
4

The National Palace Museum of Korea, right at the entrance to Gyeongbokgung—collections of royal furniture, court costumes, everyday objects from life inside the Joseon palaces. Free admission, less crowded than the palace itself. A good starting point before your visit.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Rent a hanbok near Anguk Station—free admission to all of Seoul's palaces and better photos in the Bukchon alleys.

⏱ 60 min

Lunch in the Bukchon alleys—the sundubu jjigae restaurants (silky tofu in spicy broth) around Anguk are among Seoul's best.

⏱ 60 min

Evening stroll along the Cheonggyecheon—an urban stream reopened in 2005, running 3.6 miles, illuminated and lively at night. A classic Seoul locals' walk.

⏱ 60 min

Evening in the Jongno-3ga neighborhood—its pojangmacha (heated street tents) welcome night owls over soju and meat skewers or pajeon. The vibe after 9 PM is quintessentially Seoul.

⏱ 90 min

Seoul

★★★★★
1

Begin at Jongmyo Shrine, the quietest and most awe-inspiring place in Seoul. Built in 1395, it houses the funeral tablets of 19 Joseon kings and 30 queens. The main hall stretches 330 feet long—Korea's longest straight wooden building. Every first Sunday in May, the royal Jongmyo Jerye ceremony unfolds here with ancestral costumes, music, and dances, inscribed on UNESCO's list.

⏱ 60 min
2

Head to Gwangjang Market for lunch—opened in 1905, Seoul's oldest covered market still operating. Order the yukhoe (beef tartare), bindaetteok (fried mung bean pancake), or spicy mini rice rolls. Halmeoni in aprons have been cooking here since dawn.

⏱ 75 min
3

In the evening, climb N Seoul Tower on Namsan Mountain—1,571 feet elevation, 360° views over illuminated Seoul. The Namsan fortress walls snake around the hill with sections dating to the 17th century. Take the cable car or hike up the trail (30 minutes).

⏱ 60 min
4

The Dongdaemun Design Plaza (DDP) by Zaha Hadid—over 900,000 square feet without a single right angle, open 24/7. During construction, archaeologists unearthed 1,600 artifacts from a 14th-century fortress. The Heunginjimun Gate, a remnant of Seoul's old ramparts, stands right next door.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

After dinner, take a walk along the Cheonggyecheon—an urban stream reopened in 2005 after 40 years beneath a highway. 3.6 miles of illuminated banks, lively in the evening. A classic Seoul locals' stroll.

⏱ 60 min

In the afternoon, wander through Insadong—antique dealers, calligraphy galleries, tea houses, craft shops. On Sundays, the street is closed to cars.

⏱ 60 min

Korean barbecue in the evening in the Mapo neighborhood—samgyeopsal or galbi grilled over charcoal at your table, lettuce wraps, banchan.

⏱ 90 min

Evening in Hongdae—bars, clubs, food trucks, street musicians. Seoul's liveliest student quarter after midnight.

⏱ 120 min

Seoul

★★★★★
1

Head to Gangnam to see the Starfield Library in the COEX mall—a 43-foot-high hall entirely lined with books, now one of Seoul's most photographed spots. A 10-minute walk away, Bongeunsa Temple (794 AD) offers a striking contrast: 37,000 square feet of Buddhist gardens at the foot of Gangnam's skyscrapers.

⏱ 60 min
2

Then head up to the Seoul Sky observatory at Lotte World Tower, on the 123rd floor—1,821 feet high, the world's 5th tallest tower. The glass floor at 1,640 feet is reserved for thrill-seekers. Views stretch across the entire city, the Han River, and the mountains beyond.

⏱ 75 min
3

The National Museum of Korea, Asia's largest at 3.5 million square feet. Free admission, 12,000 years of Korean history. Don't miss the 7th-century Baekje gilt-bronze incense burner—one of the finest pieces of Korean art.

⏱ 90 min
4

The Apgujeong-Rodeo neighborhood in Gangnam—Korean designer boutiques, luxury cafés, contemporary art galleries. Seoul's most upscale district, the opposite of the historic palace areas. This is where K-beauty and Korean cosmetic surgery have their most visible showcase.

⏱ 60 min
5

Seoul Olympic Park, built for the 1988 Olympics in the Songpa district. Five sports venues surrounded by contemporary sculptures and a velodrome. The site also preserves remnants of a Baekje kingdom fortress (4th century) unearthed during pre-Olympic excavations.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Korean cooking class in Insadong or Hapjeong—kimchi, bibimbap, or tteokbokki depending on the program. About 3 hours, advance booking required.

⏱ 180 min

Visit the MMCA Seoul (National Museum of Modern and Contemporary Art) in the former military barracks near Gyeongbokgung. Paid admission, Korean and international collections.

⏱ 90 min

Korean barbecue in the evening in the Yongsan neighborhood—galbi and naengmyeon in restaurants around the station. Local neighborhood vibe, off the tourist trail.

⏱ 90 min

Evening in the Sinchon neighborhood—student bars, pojangmacha, and grilled fish restaurants. More local and cheaper than Hongdae, just two subway stops away.

⏱ 120 min

Seoul

★★★★★
1

The Third Infiltration Tunnel, discovered in 1978, 240 feet beneath the border. Dug in secret, it runs over a mile long and could have moved 30,000 soldiers per hour toward the South. The walls were painted black by North Korea to make it look like a coal mine—there's no coal in this region.

⏱ 60 min
2

Dora Observatory, the most advanced observation point facing north. On clear days, you can spot Kaesong and Kijong-dong, the North Korean 'propaganda village'—empty buildings with programmed lights. Binoculars are provided on-site. Photography is prohibited in certain zones.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Bridge of Freedom, across which 12,773 prisoners of war crossed south in 1953. The barbed wire lining it is covered with colored ribbons tied by families separated by the border for 70 years. Imjingak Park around the bridge preserves a Korean War tank and a bullet-riddled locomotive found on the Gyeongui railway line.

⏱ 45 min
4

Dorasan Station, the last South Korean station before the border—built in 2002 in hopes of reunification, it has never hosted regular trains to the North. The departure board still displays 'Pyongyang' as a destination. The waiting room sits empty.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Organized tour from Seoul—passport required, minimum 48 hours advance booking. Total duration about 7 hours. JSA/Panmunjom tours have been suspended since 2023.

⏱ 60 min

Evening stroll on Banpo Bridge and the Rainbow Fountain—9,500 nozzles projecting illuminated colored water over the Han. Shows every hour in the evening.

⏱ 60 min

Evening in Itaewon upon returning from the DMZ—restaurants from around the world, lively bars. Seoul's international neighborhood, 10 minutes from downtown by subway.

⏱ 90 min

Dinner in Yongsan on your way back—a stone's throw from the War Memorial, Korean restaurants serving galbi and naengmyeon (cold noodles), local neighborhood atmosphere.

⏱ 90 min

Seoul

★★★★★
1

Start at Deoksugung Palace, the last built during the Joseon era, right in the heart of the modern city center. Emperor Gojong lived here after Queen Min's assassination in 1895. The changing of the guard in medieval costumes happens three times daily at Daehanmun Gate. The street along its walls is called 'Breakup Street'—a local tradition says couples who walk here will split up.

⏱ 60 min
2

In the afternoon, climb the Seoul City Wall around Naksan Mountain—restored sections of the 1396 wall that encircled the Joseon capital for 11 miles. Nighttime views over the old quarters from the rampart walk. Plan for 90 minutes of walking.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Seochon neighborhood and its hanok—artist studios, hidden galleries, tea houses in century-old buildings west of Gyeongbokgung. Poet Yi Sang lived here. More authentic than Insadong, fewer tourists.

⏱ 75 min
4

Cheongdam Bridge and the Cheongdam-dong neighborhood—contemporary art galleries, Korean concept stores, and design cafés in the alleys perpendicular to Apgujeong. Less famous than Gangnam but more interesting for current Korean art and design.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

End the evening in Mangwon or Mapo—local market, grilled fish restaurants, residential neighborhood vibe off the tourist circuit.

⏱ 90 min

Temple stay overnight at Bongeunsa Temple in Gangnam—4:30 AM wake-up, meditation, morning ceremonies, vegetarian breakfast. Advance booking required on the temple website.

⏱ 120 min

Evening walk on the Naksan ramparts—the illuminated wall walk offers views over the rooftops of old neighborhoods. Less than 30 minutes from Seochon.

⏱ 60 min

Late-night shopping in Dongdaemun—the fashion markets open at 10 PM and close at dawn. Wholesalers and independent Korean designers side by side.

⏱ 120 min

Seoul

★★★★★
1

Spend the morning at the National Museum of Korea—3.5 million square feet, 12,000 years of Korean history, free admission. Don't miss the 7th-century Baekje gilt-bronze incense burner, the crown jewel of the collections. Allow at least 2 hours.

⏱ 120 min
2

Stop by Namdaemun Market before you leave—open since 1414, one of Asia's oldest covered markets still operating. Ginseng, artisan noodles, clothing. Sungnyemun Gate stands at the entrance—Seoul's oldest wooden structure, built in 1398, burned down in 2008 and restored by 2013.

⏱ 60 min
3

Gyeonghuigung Palace, the least known of Seoul's five palaces, now partially restored in a quiet residential neighborhood west of Gyeongbokgung. Built in 1617, it served as a secondary residence for Joseon kings. Much less crowded than the other palaces.

⏱ 45 min
4

Han River Park for a final moment by the river—settle on the banks, order ramyeon through a Korean app (waterside delivery is a local tradition). Bikes for rent on-site to ride along the banks to Banpo Bridge.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Final moment at Han River Park—lay out a mat on the banks, order ramyeon through a Korean app (riverside delivery is a local tradition), and watch the city across the water.

⏱ 120 min

Evening in Gangnam—Apgujeong Rodeo Street for Korean luxury boutiques, or Sinchon for a more student-friendly vibe.

⏱ 120 min

Last-minute shopping in Myeongdong—K-beauty cosmetics, street food (tteokbokki, hotteok), fashion boutiques. The neighborhood is pedestrian in the evening.

⏱ 90 min

Final café in a hanok café in Bukchon or Seochon before heading to the airport.

⏱ 60 min
