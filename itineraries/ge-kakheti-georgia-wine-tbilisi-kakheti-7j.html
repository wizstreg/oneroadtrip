Tbilisi

★★★★★
1

First stop: Old Tbilisi, with its balconies of carved wood overhanging narrow cobblestone lanes. The neighbourhood clusters at the foot of the Narikala Fortress, a 4th-century citadel whose walls still trace the ridgeline above the Kura River. The view from up here - city on both banks, Mount Mtatsminda behind, the shiny Peace Bridge below - tells you immediately that this is not a standard European capital. The fortress is free to enter and best at dusk; take the cable car from the Bridge of Peace (6 GEL return) rather than the uphill walk.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Sameba Cathedral (Holy Trinity) catches you off-guard with its scale. Completed in 2004 after 12 years of construction, it rises 87 metres - currently the tallest religious building in the South Caucasus. The interior is all gilded domes and candlelit chapels; the upper terrace offers a 360° panorama that most visitors miss because they stay on the ground floor. No admission charge, but head scarves are required for women inside.

⏱ 60 min
3

The wine bar 8000 Vintages (Erekle II Street) makes an ideal first evening: over 200 Georgian references, rotated by region and style, with knowledgeable staff who speak English. Ask for the 'Saperavi qvevri vs. Saperavi classic' flight to understand what the clay vessel actually does to the same grape. The bar name refers to the 8,000 years confirmed by the 2017 PNAS study - not marketing, actual archaeology.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Dinner at Shavi Lomi ('The Black Lion') in the Abanotubani quarter. The setting - an old townhouse with a courtyard - is reliable, the prices are still reasonable (35-50€ per person with wine), and the khinkali are the real test: fold the dough, bite a small hole, drink the broth before it runs. Eating khinkali with a fork is considered slightly embarrassing.

⏱ 120 min

Walk the Mtkvari river embankment after dinner, from the Peace Bridge south toward the Metekhi cliff church. At night the lights reflect off the water and the old town stacks up the hillside. The 20-minute walk is flat and well-lit - a rare chance in Tbilisi to cover ground without climbing.

Tbilisi

★★★★★
1

The Georgian National Museum (Rustaveli Avenue 3) holds the archaeological argument at the core of the whole trip. In the basement treasury, alongside Scythian gold from Vani (3rd-2nd centuries BC), look for the Neolithic pottery shards from Shulaveris Gora dated to 6000 BC - the very vessels whose residues, analysed by Patrick McGovern's team and published in PNAS in November 2017, confirmed wine production 600-1,000 years earlier than any previous evidence on Earth. Seeing a shard of fired clay and knowing what it proved is oddly moving. Allow 2 hours. Ticket: 7 GEL (~2.50€).

⏱ 120 min
2

The sulphur baths of Abanotubani gave Tbilisi its name - 'tbili' means warm in Georgian. The legend: King Vakhtang Gorgasali was hunting in 458 AD when a pheasant he shot fell into a hot spring and emerged cooked, which convinced him to found a city on the spot. Pouchkine visited in 1829 and wrote that he had never encountered anything more luxurious. The domed brick bathhouses (17th-18th century) are still operating; the most ornate is Chreli-Abano with its blue-tiled facade. A basic soak runs about 30€/hour, a scrub and massage about 60€. Book the private room, not the communal pool.

⏱ 120 min
3

Fabrika, in the Chugureti district, is the converted Soviet textile factory that best shows what Tbilisi's under-40 scene looks like right now: container-bar courtyards, vinyl shops, a natural wine bar (Naturalist Wine Bar, specialised in qvevri), tattoo studios, and a hostel with some of the best design in the city. Worth 45 minutes even if you don't stay.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Take the 1905 funicular up Mount Mtatsminda (727m) at sunset. The Soviet-era amusement park at the top is delightfully eccentric, the views over the city at golden hour are genuinely good, and the ride itself - the funicular creaks along a cliff face - is half the experience. Return ticket: 3 GEL.

⏱ 90 min

Dinner at Barbarestan, based on a real 1873 cookbook by Duchess Barbara Jorjadze - every dish follows a historical Georgian recipe. It is probably the most interesting dinner you will have in the country: not fusion, not reinvention, just documented 19th-century Georgian bourgeois cooking that most Georgians have never eaten. Book at least a week in advance. Budget 50-80€ per person with wine.

Sighnaghi

★★★☆☆
1

Sighnaghi sits on a ridge at 800 metres with the Alazani valley below and the snow-capped Greater Caucasus behind - a view that justifies the drive entirely. King Erekle II built the 4 km of fortified walls and 23 towers in the 18th century to protect the town from Persian raids. Today about 2,000 people live here and the whole centre has been carefully (some say over-carefully) restored. One genuinely odd detail: since 2007, the town has operated Georgia's only 24/7 civil registry office, allowing couples to marry without an appointment at any hour of the night. The scheme to attract tourists worked.

⏱ 60 min
2

Bodbe Monastery, 2 km south of Sighnaghi, is where Saint Nino - the 4th-century woman credited with converting Georgia to Christianity - is buried. She arrived from Cappadocia around 330 AD, convinced Queen Nana and eventually King Mirian III to adopt the faith. The monastery's church, rebuilt in the 9th-11th centuries, holds her tomb and a constant flow of pilgrims who make the steep descent to the spring below the grounds, said to heal the sick. Not touristic in the way Sighnaghi is - still a working pilgrimage site.

⏱ 60 min
3

Pheasant's Tears winery, right in Sighnaghi's centre, is the address that put Georgian natural wine on the global map. Painter John Wurdeman settled in Georgia in the 1990s, learned the qvevri method, and in 2007 launched a biodynamic estate working exclusively with nearly-extinct indigenous varieties: Tavkveri, Aladasturi, Chitistvala, Tsolikouri. The wines are unlike anything produced elsewhere. The attached restaurant serves traditional Kakhetian food paired to the estate's range. Tasting of 6 wines: approximately 25-40€. Reservation required.

⏱ 150 min

Activities

Walk the full circuit of the Sighnaghi ramparts. The path is mostly unguarded and unrailed - which sounds alarming but means you can walk the 4 km of walls at your own pace with views west over the Alazani valley on one side and the town's terracotta rooftops on the other. Allow 1.5 hours; the walls are uneven stone so wear proper shoes.

⏱ 90 min

Evening supra experience at the Pheasant's Tears restaurant or at a local guesthouse. A supra is not a dinner - it is a ritual with a tamada (toastmaster) who conducts a precise sequence of toasts: to the homeland, to ancestors, to women, to children, to peace. Do not drink between toasts. Do not refuse a toast without good reason. This is exactly the kind of thing that sounds awkward in description and becomes one of the clearest memories of the trip.

⏱ 180 min

Telavi

★★★☆☆
1

Alaverdi Cathedral stands alone in the Alazani plain, visible from kilometres away. The first church here was built in the 6th century by an Assyrian monk named Joseph Alaverdeli; the current cathedral was raised by King Kvirike III between 1010 and 1029, reaching 56 metres - for centuries the tallest building in Georgia. But most visitors come for the marani (wine cellar), where monks have been making wine continuously since 1011. The label on every bottle says 'Since 1011'. The cellar holds qvevri from the 8th-10th centuries, with 104 indigenous varieties growing in the monastery vineyards. Tasting with the monks: 30-50€, reservation required. One monk noted: 'Qvevri wines open your heart.' Whether or not that is true, the wine is excellent.

⏱ 150 min
2

Schuchmann Wines, 15 minutes from Telavi at Kisiskhevi, is the counterpoint to Alaverdi: German industrialist Burkhard Schuchmann invested heavily in 2008, built a modern cave with 165 qvevri in active use, added a gastronomic restaurant and hotel-villas on the property. The range covers every style from qvevri amber to méthode champenoise. The Vinoterra Rkatsiteli Qvevri and the Saperavi Reserve are the wines to request. The estate demonstrates that qvevri and modernist architecture are not contradictory.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Lunch at the Schuchmann estate restaurant: a full wine-and-food pairing designed around the property's range. Portions are generous and the setting - glass walls opening onto vineyard - is well done. Budget 40-70€ per person.

⏱ 120 min

Late afternoon walk around Telavi's old town market. Telavi is Kakheti's administrative capital, a workaday market town where the commerce is real and untouched by tourist pricing. The central bazaar sells churchkhela (walnut-and-grape-must strings), dried herbs, adjika paste, and chacha (Georgian grappa) in plastic bottles. Prices are about one-third of what you'd pay in Tbilisi wine shops for the same items.

Kvareli

★★☆☆☆
1

The Khareba Wine Tunnel is 7.7 km of tunnels drilled into the Caucasus rock, opened in 1962 - the year the World Congress of Vine and Wine was held in Georgia. The Soviet engineers built it as a military storage facility; it was converted into a wine cellar after independence and acquired by Winery Khareba in 2006. Temperature inside: a constant 12-14°C year-round, which makes it among the most stable natural cellars anywhere. Currently 30,000+ bottles age here, the oldest over 20 years. Visitors access only the tourist section of the tunnel, but even that stretch gives a clear sense of scale. Guided tour + tasting: from 10 GEL. Touristique? Yes. Worth it? Also yes.

⏱ 90 min
2

Gremi Fortress, on the road from Telavi to Kvareli, is a 16th-century church and tower complex built by King Levan of Kakheti in 1565. The tower stands on a rocky outcrop above the Alazani plain and houses the Church of the Archangels, with frescoes still intact. In 1615, Shah Abbas I of Persia destroyed the town of Gremi - only the church fortress survived. It's on the UNESCO tentative list and draws almost no crowds. Stop for 45 minutes on the way from Telavi.

⏱ 45 min
3

Nekresi Monastery clings to a forested hillside above the Alazani valley, reachable by shuttle jeep from the base (the road is too rough for regular cars). Founded in the 4th century, repeatedly rebuilt through the 8th century, the complex includes a small fire-worshippers' temple at the valley floor - evidence that Zoroastrianism preceded Christianity in this part of Kakheti. The view over the valley from the monastery terrace, particularly in October when the forest turns, is among the best in the region.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Tasting at Kindzmarauli Marani. The Kindzmarauli is a red semi-sweet AOC wine, made exclusively from Saperavi grown in five villages of the Kvareli municipality. Stalin, who was Georgian by birth, had crates shipped to Roosevelt and Churchill during the Second World War. Today it is Georgia's most exported wine style. The factory tour is not glamorous, but the qvevri scale is spectacular and the tasting explains the unusual vinification method - fermentation is stopped by cold to preserve natural residual sugar. 25-40€.

⏱ 90 min

Tsinandali Estate, 10 minutes from Kvareli, is the former property of Prince Alexandre Chavchavadze (1786-1846), the poet who first introduced European winemaking techniques to Georgia in the 19th century. The restored manor house and 18-hectare botanical garden are open for visits. The Tsinandali white (Rkatsiteli-Mtsvane blend, European method rather than qvevri) has been produced here since 1886, making it one of the oldest continuously labelled Georgian wines.

⏱ 75 min

Tbilisi

★★★★★
1

The Dry Bridge Market (Mtkvari embankment, near the Dry Bridge) is Tbilisi's open-air flea market: Soviet badges, icons, Caucasian carpets, old cameras, Soviet-era wine label sets, and genuine antiques mixed with junk in roughly equal proportion. It runs daily but is largest and best stocked on weekends. Even if you buy nothing, an hour here is a better picture of 20th-century Georgian material culture than most museums.

⏱ 60 min
2

Rustaveli Avenue is Tbilisi's central boulevard - but look at the facades rather than the ground floor shops. The Opera House (1880s, Moorish), the Parliament building (Soviet neoclassical), the Rustaveli Theatre (Art Nouveau), and half a dozen ornate apartment buildings carry a century and a half of architectural ambition into a single 1.5 km stretch. The buildings are not pristine - that's part of the point. Crumbling plasterwork next to new boutiques, scaffolding over wedding-cake facades.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Naturalist Wine Bar in Fabrika specialises in qvevri and natural wines from small Georgian producers - the kind of estates too small to be on organised wine tour routes. The list rotates constantly and the staff know every domaine on it. A flight of 4 wines runs 20-35€. This is where the Tbilisi natural wine scene actually happens on a Tuesday evening, not in the Old Town tourist bars.

⏱ 90 min

Final dinner at Café Littera, in the garden of the Georgian Writers' Union building (a handsome 1930s mansion). The kitchen does contemporary Georgian - not fusion, but traditional recipes executed with precision and seasonal produce. The garden setting on a warm evening, under plane trees with string lights, is one of Tbilisi's genuinely pleasant dinner experiences. Budget 40-60€ per person with wine.

Mtskheta

★★★★☆
1

Svetitskhoveli Cathedral dominates the centre of Mtskheta and has served as the spiritual and coronation seat of Georgian kings since it was completed by architect Arsukisdze in 1010-1029. At 54 metres high, it was the tallest building in Georgia until 2004. The name means 'life-giving pillar', a reference to the column said to have grown from the spot where Christ's robe was buried after being brought from Jerusalem in the 1st century by a Mtskhetan Jew. The story sounds like allegory; the cathedral built around it is entirely real. UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1994. Free entry; open 8 AM-8 PM.

⏱ 75 min
2

Jvari Monastery perches on the cliff above the confluence of the Kura and Aragvi rivers, exactly where Saint Nino planted a wooden cross in the 4th century after converting the Georgian kingdom to Christianity. The current domed church dates to the late 6th century - one of the earliest intact examples of Georgian ecclesiastical architecture. Mikhail Lermontov set the opening of his poem 'Mtsyri' here. Drive up rather than walk; the road is steep and cars park at the monastery gate.

⏱ 60 min
3

Samtavro Monastery (a short walk north of Svetitskhoveli) holds the grave of King Mirian III, the first Christian monarch of Georgia, and his queen Nana - both canonised saints. The small 4th-century domed church is the oldest surviving part of the complex. Unusually for a Georgian monastery, it is administered by nuns rather than monks, and the interior is always lit by candles rather than electric light.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Lunch at Salobie restaurant in Mtskheta, a long-standing local address known for its lobiani (bean-filled flatbread), traditional khachapuri, and grilled meats. Tourist-adjacent but legitimately good; the lobiani here is better than most versions you will find in Tbilisi. Budget 15-25€ per person.

⏱ 75 min

Wine souvenir stop before the airport: the best picks to carry home in your suitcase are a Pheasant's Tears Rkatsiteli or Tavkveri (~25-40€ at the estate, if you bought direct), an Alaverdi Monastery Cellar Saperavi (~35€), and a Schuchmann Vinoterra Kisi Qvevri. EU customs allows 6 bottles in hold luggage duty-free; pack them in the padded wine carriers most Kakheti estates sell at the door. The Tbilisi airport duty-free has a decent but overpriced Georgian selection as a backup.
