Extreme Hokkaido Road Trip: Arctic Nature and Volcanoes

📅 20 days📍 20 stops🚗 1600 km⏱ 22h55

Hakodate → Shakotan → Sapporo → Hokuryu → Furano → Biei → Asahidake Onsen → Sounkyo → Tomamu → Kamishihoro → Kushiro → Teshikaga → Shiretoko → Abashiri → Saroma → Wakkanai → Rishiri → Rebun

In 18 days looping around Hokkaido, this road trip chains together northern Japan's wildest landscapes: Mount Asahidake's smoking cliffs, 7,000-year-old Lake Mashu's cobalt blue waters, UNESCO-listed Shiretoko's primeval forest. From Otaru's romantic canal to Abashiri's drift ice from Siberia, Furano's lavender fields to Teuri Island's rhinoceros auklet colonies, Hokkaido delivers a concentration of arctic nature unmatched in Japan.

✨ Highlights
  • Hakodate night view ranked among Japan's three most beautiful with Nagasaki and Kobe
  • Lake Mashu: among the world's most transparent lakes, 211 meters depth, unique Mashu blue color
  • Shiretoko UNESCO-listed 2005: intact primeval forest sheltering 80% of Hokkaido's brown bear population
  • Teuri Island: 400,000 rhinoceros auklet breeding pairs, world's largest colony of this species
📆 Best period

June, July, August, September, October

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 17 and 21 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Spring (March-May): Cherry blossom season, perfect weather, mild temperatures, iconic sakura viewing, peak tourist season, advance booking essential, magical atmosphere.

Fall (September-November): Autumn colors, comfortable weather, clear skies, beautiful foliage, excellent touring, fewer crowds, ideal photography, pleasant conditions.

Summer (June-August): Hot humid weather, rainy season, festival season, vibrant atmosphere, mountain hiking, beach activities, air conditioning essential.

Winter (December-February): Cold weather, snow sports, illuminations, onsen season, fewer crowds, winter festivals, cozy atmosphere, skiing opportunities.

🌍 What to discover in JP?

Unique culture with ancient traditions, modern innovation, and incredible attention to detail. Experience temples, technology, and authentic Japanese lifestyle. The country offers cultural immersion with natural beauty and culinary excellence. Japan provides transformative cultural experiences with historical depth and modern sophistication.

Day 1

Hakodate

where the historic port traces a luminous hourglass between two seas

★★★★☆
📍 Mount Hakodate Observatory Hakodate📍 Goryokaku Fort Hakodate📍 Motomachi District Hakodate📍 Kanemori Warehouses Hakodate📍 Asaichi Morning Market Hakodate
1

From the summit of Mount Hakodate at 334 meters, the city traces an hourglass silhouette of lights between the Tsugaru Strait and the bay - ranked among Japan's three most beautiful night views alongside Nagasaki and Kobe. The ropeway, operating since 1958, reaches the upper station in 3 minutes. Arrive 30 minutes before sunset to watch the city gradually illuminate: the effect peaks during the "blue hour." In summer, beware of frequent fog; in autumn, clear skies provide ideal conditions. Private vehicles are banned on summer evenings from 5pm to 10pm.

⏱ 90 min
2

Fort Goryokaku, built between 1857 and 1866 following the model of Vauban's bastioned citadels, was Japan's first Western-style fort. Originally designed to repel Russian advances on Hokkaido, it never saw Russo-Japanese conflict. Instead, it became the site of the final battle of the Boshin War in 1869: 2,000 shogunate soldiers took refuge here before surrendering to imperial forces. From the 107-meter Goryokaku Tower (90m observation deck), the five-pointed star shape is striking, especially in spring when 1,660 cherry trees bloom around the moats.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Motomachi district, at the foot of Mount Hakodate, bears witness to the port's opening to foreigners in 1859 - simultaneously with Yokohama and Nagasaki. Stroll the sloping streets along Hachiman-zaka, lined with Westernized buildings: the old Russian Orthodox church, the former British Consulate, and several missions dating from the Meiji era. The contrast with traditional Japanese architecture is striking and unique in northern Japan.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Take the ropeway to the summit in 3 minutes from Sanroku Station, accessible in 10 minutes on foot from the Jujigai tram stop. Ascend 30 minutes before sunset - that's when the city begins to sparkle and crowds have already gathered. The panoramic restaurant at the summit accepts highly sought-after reservations for tables on the night-view side.

⏱ 90 min

Head down to Asaichi morning market from 5am, steps from JR Hakodate Station. It's one of Hokkaido's liveliest fish markets: vendors offer tastings of shrimp, sea urchin, and king crab directly from their stalls. The local specialty is kaisendon - a rice bowl topped with ultra-fresh seafood that you choose yourself from the displayed products.

⏱ 60 min

In the evening, stroll along the red-brick Kanemori warehouses dating from 1909, converted into shops and restaurants at the harbor's edge. Their reflections in the harbor water at night create one of Hakodate's iconic images. If you're looking for local beer to enjoy on the quays, Hakodate Beer brews in one of the old warehouses.

⏱ 60 min

Day 2

Shakotan

where Shakotan Blue waters rival the purest tropical seas

★☆☆☆☆
📍 Cape Kamui Shakotan📍 Charenka Trail Shakotan📍 Shimamui Coast Shakotan📍 Cape Shakotan
1

Cape Kamui juts into the Sea of Japan on the Shakotan Peninsula, within Niseko-Shakotan-Otaru Kaigan Quasi-National Park. Ainu legend tells that Charenka, daughter of an Ainu chief in love with the warrior Minamoto no Yoshitsune, threw herself into the sea when he abandoned her - her spirit supposedly petrified into the Kamui rock, 40 meters high, visible at the cape's tip. Following this, women were historically forbidden access until the Meiji era. You can still see the "Gate Forbidden to Women" at the Charenka trail's beginning.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Shimamui coast, a few kilometers north of Cape Kamui, offers maximum concentration of the famous "Shakotan Blue" - this deep turquoise hue resulting from exceptionally clear waters. Less crowded than the cape itself, it deserves a stop for photographers. The view from the lookout overlooking the cliffs ranks among the peninsula's finest.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hike the 770-meter Charenka trail to the cape's tip: allow 20 minutes one way, 40 minutes round trip. The trail is developed but rugged in places. At the point, enjoy 300-degree panoramic views of the Sea of Japan's curved horizon. Bring a windbreaker - gusts are powerful. The cape is accessible from spring to autumn only, the gate being closed off-season.

⏱ 60 min

In early summer (June-July), Ezo daylilies (Ezo Kanzou) bloom in orange-yellow clusters along the trail, contrasting with the sea blue below. This ranks among Hokkaido's most photographed panoramas. Shakotan Blue is particularly intense in sunny weather between 10am and 2pm, when the sun is high.

⏱ 45 min

The Shakotan Peninsula is renowned for its sea urchins (uni), considered among Japan's finest. Seaside restaurants serve rice bowls covered with fresh urchins caught that morning. The season runs from May to August - don't miss this opportunity if you're there in summer.

⏱ 60 min

Sapporo

where the northern capital has brewed beer since 1876 and parties in Susukino

★★★★★
📍 Odori Park Sapporo📍 Clock Tower Sapporo📍 Former Hokkaido Government Office Sapporo📍 Nijo Market Sapporo📍 Sapporo Beer Museum
1

Odori Park is Sapporo's heart: a 1.5 km boulevard-park divided into 13 sections running east to west, built in 1871 as the city's firebreak. At its eastern end, the TV Tower (147 m) offers a bird's-eye view of the city's orthogonal grid - modeled on North American cities during Hokkaido's colonization in the Meiji era. The park hosts all the city's major events: Snow Festival in February, beer garden in summer, autumn food festival in September.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Former Hokkaido Government Office - the Akarenga ("red brick") - is an American neo-Baroque edifice from 1888, emblematic of the island's colonization period. Its red bricks, green dome, and gardens make it one of Sapporo's most photographed buildings. The interior houses an exhibition on Hokkaido's history and admission is free.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Clock Tower (Tokeidai), built in 1878, is one of Sapporo's three oldest historic buildings. Oddly squeezed between modern buildings downtown, it sometimes disappoints visitors expecting more grandeur - and that's precisely what makes it amusing to see. The interior is a small museum on the city's history.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Start the day at Nijo Market from 7am. It's Sapporo's kitchen: hairy crabs (kegani) and king crabs, sea urchins, scallops, and Hokkaido salmon line the stalls. Order a sanshoku-don - a rice bowl topped with sea urchin, salmon roe, and crab - at one of the restaurants bordering the market. It's the city's best value.

⏱ 60 min

In the evening, dive into Susukino - the largest entertainment district north of Tokyo. Izakaya, beer bars, late-night ramen: the atmosphere electrifies from 8pm. Don't miss Ramen Yokocho, the ramen alley with 17 restaurants packed together - this is where Sapporo miso ramen was popularized in the 1950s. Choose randomly: all addresses are good.

⏱ 120 min

Day 4

Sapporo

where the northern capital has brewed beer since 1876 and parties in Susukino

★★★★★
📍 Sapporo Beer Museum📍 Mount Moiwa Sapporo ropeway📍 Hokkaido Shrine Sapporo📍 Maruyama Park Sapporo📍 Hitsujigaoka Observation Hill Sapporo
1

The Sapporo Beer Museum occupies the old red-brick brewery from 1890 - Japan's first modern brewery, founded by Seibei Nakagawa upon his return from studies in Germany. The building crowned with a five-pointed red polar star has become a city symbol. The tour explains how Sapporo became one of Japan's three most consumed beers. Tasting available on-site at tour's end.

⏱ 75 min
2

Mount Moiwa (531 m) is reached by ropeway from downtown. Its observatory offers the most complete night view of Sapporo: the city's illuminated grid stretches to snow-capped mountains on the horizon. Ranked among Japan's 100 best night views. Ascend 30 minutes before sunset to witness the transition from day to night.

⏱ 90 min
3

Hokkaido Shrine in Maruyama Park is Hokkaido's principal Shinto shrine, founded in 1869 to watch over the island's colonization. The Maruyama primeval forest surrounding it - one of the rare intact ancient forests within a major Japanese city - shelters squirrels and birds. In spring, 1,400 cherry trees bloom around the shrine.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Climb to Hitsujigaoka Observation Hill ("sheep hill") where a statue of Dr. William Clark, founder of Hokkaido Agricultural University, points toward the horizon with his famous phrase: "Boys, be ambitious!" The view of Sapporo and the Daisetsuzan mountains in clear weather ranks among the city's best. Sheep actually graze on the site.

⏱ 45 min

Genghis Khan (jingisukan) is THE Sapporo specialty you won't easily find elsewhere in Japan: lamb grilled on a Mongolian helmet-shaped grill, accompanied by sautéed vegetables and soy sauce. It's the typical meal for residents' celebrations and summer barbecues. Look for a restaurant with a queue - that's usually a good sign.

⏱ 75 min

Day 5

Hokuryu

where two million sunflowers flood 23 hectares of hills with blazing gold

★★☆☆☆
📍 Hokuryu Sunflower Fields Himawari no Sato📍 Hokuryu Sunflower Maze📍 Hokuryu Sunflower Village
1

Hokuryu counts only 1,575 inhabitants - fewer than the number of visitors on certain summer weekends. Yet the town hosts Japan's largest sunflower fields: 2 million flowers covering 23 hectares of gentle hills. The Himawari no Sato festival has existed since 1979, initially cultivated by local farmers to valorize difficult land. The World Sunflower Corner displays 24 distinct varieties of sunflowers, from giants to dwarfs, from golden yellow to burgundy. Blooming extends from late July to mid-August.

⏱ 90 min
2

The elevated observation point, accessible from the fields, offers a bird's-eye view of the yellow sea stretching to the horizon. In clear weather, the rolling hills evoke the Pre-Alps in miniature. Field admission is free; only the giant sunflower maze and bike rentals are paid.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Get lost in the giant sunflower maze, whose pathways change configuration each year. The challenge can take 20 to 45 minutes depending on your sense of direction. Late morning, the flowers are still fresh and the sun hasn't yet bent the heads - it's the best time for photography.

⏱ 45 min

At the Himawari information center, taste sunflower ice cream flavored with seed oil, and local roasted seeds. Tourist tractors traverse the fields for those who don't want to walk. Outside sunflower season (before mid-July or after mid-August), the fields are green and uninteresting - check dates before making the detour.

⏱ 60 min

Evening thermal bath at Sunflower Park Hokuryu Onsen, steps from the fields. This non-touristy public bath lets you relax after a day on the road and mingle with residents of this deep Hokkaido countryside.

Day 6

Furano

where Farm Tomita's lavender waves perfume the valley each July

★★★★☆
📍 Farm Tomita Nakafurano Furano📍 Furano lavender farm📍 Flower Land Kamifurano📍 Furano Cheese Factory
1

Farm Tomita, founded in 1903, became Japan's most famous lavender farm almost by accident. In the 1960s, imported low-cost lavender threatened Hokkaido producers. Farm Tomita held on, and a photo of its fields published in a JR Railways calendar in the 1970s triggered a tourist wave that never stopped. Today, five lavender varieties succeed each other from late June to early August, peaking in mid-July. The Irodori field, in multicolored horizontal bands on a hillside (purple lavender, red poppies, white gypsophila, yellow daisies), is Furano's most reproduced image. Admission is free.

⏱ 90 min
2

Flower Land Kamifurano, a few kilometers further north, offers a different perspective: backed by the Tokachi and Ashibetsu peaks, the farm cultivates successive blooms from April to November. Tractor buses tour the fields for visitors wanting an elevated view of the colored bands. The atmosphere is calmer than at Tomita.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Arrive at Farm Tomita before 9am in peak season (July): after 10am, parking lots and pathways saturate. Lavender fragrance is more intense in the morning, before heat evaporates essential oils. The shop sells products distilled on-site (essential oil, soaps, candles) - in 1990, the farm's oil won first place at an evaluation conference organized in France.

⏱ 60 min

Climb to the top of Hokusei Hill via the ropeway (paid, ~400 yen) from the neighboring station to overlook the lavender fields with the Tokachi mountains as backdrop. The view from above is that of Hokkaido's iconic photographs. In July, groups of local hikers use the same starting point for alpine trails.

⏱ 60 min

The Furano cheese factory offers free tastings and butter or cheese-making workshops. The region's pastures produce milk of richness recognized throughout Japan. Combine with a tasting at Chateau Furano Winery, set on a hill east of town: light, fruity wines, panoramic view of the valley.

⏱ 75 min

Day 7

Biei

where the Blue Pond guards its cobalt color born from a volcanic accident

★★★☆☆
📍 Blue Pond Shirogane Biei📍 Patchwork Road Biei📍 Shikisai no Oka Biei📍 Panorama Road Biei📍 Shirahige Falls Biei
1

The Blue Pond of Shirogane (Aoiike) is a curiosity born by accident: built in 1988-1989 as a mudflow control reservoir related to Mount Tokachi eruptions, it accumulated waters laden with colloidal aluminum hydroxide from the nearby Shirahige waterfalls. This aluminum diffracts light and produces the unreal cobalt blue color. The pond's bottom, whitened by sulfide, further accentuates the effect. Color varies throughout the day and with weather: in calm wind and clear sky early morning, the blue is most saturated. In 2012, Apple chose a photo of this pond as wallpaper for OS X Mountain Lion - since then, its fame is global. Dead larches and birches stand in the water, swimming is forbidden (water laden with aluminum).

⏱ 75 min
2

The Patchwork Road, northwest of Biei, takes its name from the appearance of agricultural fields seen from the heights: potatoes, wheat, beets, and corn form rectangles of textures and colors changing with seasons. In winter, the snowy undulations of these hills are unmatched. At the summit of Hokusei Hill, the observation pyramid offers 360° views of this entire landscape. The Panorama Road, further south, runs along flower gardens open from spring to autumn.

⏱ 60 min
3

Shikisai no Oka, a flower park at the entrance to Panorama Road, displays dozens of floral varieties in colored bands on a south-facing hill. Tractors and quads offer tours of the estate. From May to October, blooms succeed without interruption - lavender, cosmos, dahlias, sage.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Go to the Blue Pond early morning and in calm weather: it's the sine qua non condition for maximum color and perfect reflection in ripple-free water. Tourists will flood in from 9:30-10am. Parking is paid on-site. In winter (November to April), nighttime illuminations transfigure the site - ice blocks under spotlights create an almost surreal effect.

⏱ 75 min

Drive the Patchwork Road stopping at improvised lookouts on the ridges. Bike rental shops in Biei offer mountain bikes and electric bikes to explore secondary paths between fields - it's the most authentic way to taste this rolling countryside reminiscent of central European hills.

⏱ 90 min

Detour to Shirahige Falls, 2.5 km from the Blue Pond: water surges directly from volcanic rock fissures and plunges into the Biei River. The phenomenon is visible from a bridge in the Shirogane hot spring village. The whitish water that gushes turns blue upon joining the river - this is where the aluminum coloring the Blue Pond comes from.

⏱ 30 min

Day 8

Asahidake Onsen

where Hokkaido's rooftop smokes beneath the island's highest volcano's alpine tundra

★★★☆☆
📍 Asahidake Ropeway📍 Mount Asahidake Daisetsuzan📍 Sugatami Pond Asahidake📍 Asahidake Fumaroles
1

Mount Asahidake peaks at 2,291 meters - it's Hokkaido's rooftop and the island's highest point. It belongs to the Daisetsuzan volcanic group, called "Kamui-mintara" ("playground of the gods") by the Ainu. The ropeway from Asahidake Onsen ascends in 10 minutes to Sugatami Station at 1,600 meters - a large 101-passenger gondola. There begins a world of alpine tundra devoid of trees: sulfurous fumaroles, crater ponds, and a 1-hour circular trail accessible even to non-hikers. In summer, Aleutian dryas and mountain blue heather briefly bloom on the rocky slopes.

⏱ 90 min
2

Sugatami Pond, visible from the ropeway station, reflects the smoking silhouette of Asahidake's summit in its calm waters. It's one of the park's most photographed sites. Asahidake is also Japan's first place to display autumn colors - mid-September generally - several weeks before the rest of the archipelago.

⏱ 60 min
3

For experienced hikers, the summit ascent from the ropeway station takes about 2 hours. The trail is rocky and exposed to wind; hiking boots and warm clothing are essential even in August. Once at the summit, the view extends across the entire Daisetsuzan range. Beware of brown bears: a bell and noise are recommended.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Take the ropeway early morning, ideally at opening. The 30 to 60-minute circular trail around Sugatami Station passes active fumaroles releasing sulfurous vapors - a reminder that you're on a living volcano. The crater ponds Kagami-ike and Suribachi-ike are accessible on this circuit.

⏱ 90 min

Observe Hokkaido deer and chipmunks (small striped squirrels) frequently visible around the upper trails, especially early morning. Pikas (whistling lagomorphs) inhabit the rocky scree above the tree line - a rare species difficult to observe elsewhere in Japan.

⏱ 60 min

Enjoy the onsen at Asahidake hot spring resort in the evening. This village counts only a dozen buildings including wooden lodges and a few large hotels offering hot spring baths. It's one of Hokkaido's most isolated and wildest onsen - steps from the national park trails.

⏱ 60 min

Day 9

Sounkyo

where the 24-kilometer gorge ignites in red and gold from mid-September

★☆☆☆☆
📍 Sounkyo Gorge📍 Ginga no Taki Falls Sounkyo📍 Ryusei no Taki Falls Sounkyo📍 Kurodake Ropeway Sounkyo📍 Obako Kobako Sounkyo
1

The Sounkyo Gorge - whose name comes from Ainu and means "river with many waterfalls" - stretches 24 kilometers along the Ishikari River. The basaltic cliffs reach 100 meters in height, formed by magma that cooled millions of years ago. The Obako and Kobako formations, where the walls narrow into a tight corridor, are most spectacular. Sounkyo is one of Japan's first sites to display autumn colors - from mid-September at Mount Kurodake's summit, then progressively toward the valley floor until mid-October.

⏱ 90 min
2

Ginga no Taki ("Milky Way") and Ryusei no Taki ("Shooting Star") Falls are 3 km east of the village. They drop directly from 100-meter cliffs in two opposing styles: Ginga is thin, silvery, and spread like hair - the "female fall" - while Ryusei is powerful, compact, and direct - the "male fall." A viewpoint allows seeing both simultaneously.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Rent an electric bike in the village to ride up the gorge to the falls and Obako-Kobako formations. The road follows the Ishikari River for about 8 km one way. Rental shops are near downtown Sounkyo. In peak color season (mid-September to mid-October), the contrast between dark basaltic cliffs and red-and-gold foliage is striking.

⏱ 150 min

Take the Kurodake ropeway from Sounkyo visitor center to access the national park heights. A chairlift extends the journey to within an hour's walk from Mount Kurodake summit (1,984 m). From the top, view the steam plumes of Asahidake on the horizon. A natural onsen is accessible during descent.

⏱ 120 min

The public onsen Kurodake no Yu in downtown is Sounkyo's communal hot spring bath - separate indoor and outdoor baths, modest rate, mountain view from the outdoor bath. In winter (late January to late March), the town hosts the Ice Waterfall Festival (Hyobaku Matsuri) with giant snow and ice sculptures.

Day 10

Tomamu

where Unkai Terrace floats above an ephemeral and unpredictable sea of clouds

★★☆☆☆
📍 Unkai Terrace Tomamu Hoshino Resorts📍 Cloud Walk Tomamu📍 Mount Tomamu
1

The Unkai Terrace at Hoshino Resorts Tomamu, opened in 2006, perches at 1,088 meters altitude. "Unkai" means "sea of clouds" in Japanese - the phenomenon occurs when precise conditions converge: clear night, rapid nocturnal cooling in the valley, humid air condensing at ground level while heights remain clear. The probability of observing the sea of clouds is about 30 to 40% during the opening period (May to mid-October). The terrace offers six observation points including the Cloud Walk (cloud-shaped walkway jutting into the void), a Cloud Pool, and a Cloud Bar.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Wake before dawn - the gondola begins rotations around 5am in high season. Sea of clouds probability is highest on mornings following a warm sunny day preceded by a cool night, typically July to September. The site publishes a next-morning forecast each noon on its official website. Bring extra layers: it's noticeably colder at 1,088 m than on the plain, even in midsummer.

⏱ 120 min

On the Unkai Terrace, three types of sea of clouds are possible depending on conditions: the "Pacific sea of clouds" carried by marine flow, the "Tomamu sea of clouds" by radiative cooling, and the "bad weather sea of clouds" during disturbances. A cloud guide (Kumokai Guide) explains in real time the observable phenomenon that morning.

⏱ 60 min

If the sea of clouds is absent that morning, the view of the Hidaka and Tokachi mountains from 1,088 meters remains beautiful. Descending, explore the Hoshino Resorts domain which offers outdoor summer activities, horseback riding, and hot air ballooning above the meadows.

⏱ 60 min

Day 11

Kamishihoro

where the phantom Taushubetsu Bridge emerges and vanishes with the seasons beneath the lake

★★☆☆☆
📍 Taushubetsu Bridge Kamishihoro📍 Lake Nukabira Kamishihoro📍 Taushubetsu Observatory Kamishihoro📍 Mikuni Pass Hokkaido
1

The Taushubetsu Bridge is a concrete arch bridge 130 meters long, 10 meters high with 11 arches, built in 1937 to allow the former Shihoro railway line to cross the river of the same name. In 1955, construction of the Nukabira Dam flooded the line and bridge. Since then, the bridge emerges and disappears under water according to reservoir level - hence its nickname "phantom bridge." It's generally visible from January to August, its arches recalling a Roman aqueduct. The forest road leading there is closed to the public: access is exclusively by guided tour organized by the local guide center (reservation mandatory). In winter, snowshoe hikes on the frozen lake allow approaching it.

⏱ 90 min
2

Mikuni Pass, at 1,139 meters altitude, is Hokkaido's highest road pass. It marks the border between Kamikawa and Tokachi subprefectures and offers views of dense forest of Ezo spruce and Sakhalin fir. Autumn foliage is spectacular from late September to early October. Several developed lookouts line the road.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Book the Taushubetsu Bridge guided tour in advance through the Higashi-taisetsu Nature Guide Center. Tours generally depart in the morning and last 2 to 3 hours. In summer (July), the bridge reflects in the lake's calm waters on windless mornings - it's the image most sought by photographers. The bridge deteriorates year by year: each freeze-thaw cycle erodes the concrete, portions have collapsed. Visit it while it still stands.

⏱ 150 min

Lake Nukabira, Hokkaido's second-largest artificial lake, is bordered by Nukabira Onsen hot spring resort. In winter, the frozen lake hosts ice smelt fishing and snowshoe walks. Summer, trails follow the shores through the forest. Horoka Onsen hot spring, nearby, gushes in open nature.

⏱ 60 min

Day 12

Kushiro

where Japan's largest wetland shelters a thousand red-crowned cranes

★★★☆☆
📍 Kushiro Shitsugen Wetlands📍 Hosooka Observatory Kushiro📍 Norokko Train Kushiro marshlands📍 Red-crowned Crane Park Kushiro📍 Akan International Crane Center Kushiro
1

Kushiro Shitsugen National Park covers 268 km² and constitutes Japan's largest non-degraded wetland. Listed under the Ramsar Convention in 1980 - Japan's first site - and established as a national park in 1987, this expanse of reeds and sinuous rivers shelters more than 700 plant species and 1,300 animal species. The Japanese crane (tancho-zuru), considered extinct in the early 20th century, was rediscovered here in 1926 at about twenty individuals. Thanks to conservation efforts, their population now exceeds 1,700 individuals. This crane - white and black with a bright red crown, 150 cm tall and up to 250 cm wingspan - is classified as a Special Natural Treasure of Japan.

⏱ 90 min
2

Hosooka Observatory overlooks the marsh from a forested ridge. A boardwalk and developed lookouts allow 360° views of the wetlands' green-russet carpet, the meandering Kushiro River, and mountains on the horizon. Wings of Steller's sea eagles, silhouettes of sika deer, and sometimes cranes are distinguishable in the vegetation.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Board the Norokko tourist train (April to early October) from Kushiro Station to Toro, running along the Kushiro River for 45 minutes through the marsh. From the windows, you regularly spot sika deer, Ezo red foxes, and sometimes red-crowned cranes. The train slows at the most picturesque spots. Seats on the river side (left heading to Toro) offer the best views.

⏱ 100 min

In winter (November to March), the crane feeding stations at Tsurui and Akan gather large groups of birds. The Akan International Crane Center, open year-round, overlooks the feeding meadows - by far the best configuration for photographing cranes in their spectacular courtship dances without disturbing them.

⏱ 90 min

Canoe down the Kushiro River through the marsh's heart: local providers offer half-day excursions from April to October. The river is slow and calm, perfect for silent navigation that doesn't disturb wildlife. It's the most immersive way to approach the wetlands.

Day 13

Teshikaga

where Lake Mashu, the gods' lake, guards some of the world's clearest waters

★★☆☆☆
📍 Lake Mashu Teshikaga📍 Lake Mashu Observatory 1📍 Lake Mashu Observatory 3📍 Mount Io Teshikaga📍 Lake Kussharo Teshikaga📍 Kawayu Onsen
1

Lake Mashu is a volcanic caldera formed over 7,000 years ago. At 211 meters depth and visibility extending to 20-30 meters in clear weather, it ranks among the world's most transparent lakes - only Lake Baikal can rival it. Its water, fed solely by rain and groundwater (no rivers enter or exit), maintains a remarkably stable level. The Ainu call it "Kamuito" - the lake of the gods. Its deep blue color, called "Mashu blue," varies with light and sun angle. Mist envelops it more than 100 days a year, adding mystical atmosphere. Access only from observatories - the lakeshore is in fully closed special protected zone.

⏱ 90 min
2

Mount Io (Atosanupuri), a few kilometers away, is an active volcano whose Ainu name means "naked mountain" - and it's the right word. Thousands of fumaroles spew sulfurous steam from the bare flanks, creating a yellow and gray lunar landscape. It's a former sulfur extraction site, abandoned. You can approach on foot from the parking lot.

⏱ 60 min
3

Lake Kussharo, about fifteen kilometers away, is Japan's largest crater lake. In winter, whooper swans migrate from Siberia and stay in non-frozen zones warmed by geothermal springs. A hot sand bath (Sunayu) is accessible on the south shore - dig the beach sand and a natural onsen gushes.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Visit Lake Mashu in clear weather - check local forecast before departure. Observatory No. 1 offers the most direct view of the lake and the volcanic islet Kamuishu at its center. Observatory No. 3, higher up, gives a panoramic view of the entire caldera and Mount Mashu. Observatory No. 1 parking is paid (410 yen). If fog covers the lake, Ainu legend says the show is still worth the trip.

⏱ 90 min

Bathe at Kawayu Onsen, one of Japan's most acidic hot springs (pH between 1.6 and 1.9). This sulfurous water, originating from Mount Io's activity, is renowned for relieving skin problems. Free foot baths (ashiyu) line the town streets - the contrast between boiling water and Hokkaido's fresh air is invigorating.

Day 14

Shiretoko

where the peninsula at the world's end protects Japan's highest brown bear density

★★☆☆☆
📍 Shiretoko Five Lakes Goko📍 Furepe Falls Shiretoko📍 Shiretoko Pass📍 Shiretoko Cruise Utoro📍 Cape Puyuni Shiretoko
1

The Shiretoko Peninsula - "sir-etok," the end of the world in Ainu - has been listed as a UNESCO World Heritage site since 2005. No road penetrates the final 50 kilometers of the peninsula, which concentrate 80% of Hokkaido's brown bear population. The Shiretoko Five Lakes (Shiretoko Goko) are five small volcanic lakes formed hundreds of years ago by an eruption of Mount Io, connected by primeval Ezo spruce forest. A free elevated 800-meter boardwalk provides access to Lake Ichiko with views of the Sea of Okhotsk. Ground trails of 1.6 km to 3 km allow completing the five-lake loop (90 minutes) - but guide mandatory mid-May to late July due to high bear activity.

⏱ 120 min
2

Furepe Falls plunges directly from a cliff into the Sea of Okhotsk from a 2 km coastal trail from the nature center. The Shiretoko mountain range reflects in the calm lakes at the waterfall's foot - one of the peninsula's most refined compositions. Brown bears sometimes descend to the pebble beaches below.

⏱ 75 min
3

Shiretoko Pass (Shiretoko Toge) at 740 meters connects Utoro (west coast) to Rausu (east coast). The view of Mount Rausu (1,661 m), the peninsula's highest point, and in clear weather the Russian island of Kunashiri across the Nemuro Strait, is striking. It's also one of Japan's first places to see autumn colors - early October.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Book a boat cruise from Utoro Port (April to November) that runs along the peninsula's wild coast. Brown bears on beaches, seals on rocks, white-tailed sea eagles in the air - the wildlife concentration is exceptional. The 3-hour cruises to Kashuni no Taki (waterfall falling into the sea) are most complete.

⏱ 180 min

Five Lakes hike: if you're there between mid-May and late July, book a certified guide (about 5,000 yen, groups of 10 maximum, 3h duration). Outside bear season, the complete 3 km loop is freely accessible with a brief safety orientation. Bring a bear bell, carry no food on the trail.

Day 15

Abashiri

where pack ice from Siberia grips the Okhotsk coast each winter

★★☆☆☆
📍 Aurora Icebreaker Abashiri📍 Abashiri Prison Museum📍 Mount Tento Abashiri📍 Okhotsk Ryuhyo Museum Abashiri📍 Ryuhyo Norokko Train Abashiri
1

Each winter, the Sea of Okhotsk off Abashiri covers with drifting ice plates (ryuhyo) pushed from the Amur River mouth in Siberia - a journey of over 1,000 kilometers. These plates reach Japanese coasts from mid-January to late March. The Aurora icebreaker, with 450-passenger capacity, cleaves these plates with its weight and power during an hour-long cruise. The physical sensation of the ship ramming against ice is unique. Seals laze on the plates, white-tailed sea eagles soar above.

⏱ 75 min
2

The former Abashiri Prison, built in the Meiji era to clear Hokkaido with convicts, is now a high-quality open-air museum. Five original wooden buildings, classified heritage, recount prison life at the world's edge. Neighboring Mount Tento (207 m) offers from its summit a panoramic view of the city, Lakes Abashiri and Notoro, and the Sea of Okhotsk.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

In season (mid-January to early April): board the Aurora icebreaker from Ryuhyo-Kaido pier, 5 minutes by car from JR Abashiri Station. The ship makes 4 to 5 crossings daily. Bundle up well - sea wind on deck is biting. If conditions are exceptional and ice thick, the ship's shocks against pack ice can make you lose balance.

⏱ 90 min

Off-season or in addition, the Ryuhyo Norokko panoramic train (January to March) runs along the coast for 1 hour between Abashiri and Shiretoko-Shari, with views of the ice sea from the windows. An extended stop at Kitahama Station, the Japanese rail network's closest to the Sea of Okhotsk, allows seeing ice plates meters away.

⏱ 90 min

Outside ice season, Abashiri remains interesting for its prison-museum, seafood market (specialties: scallops, king crab, Abashiri Zangi - marinated and fried salmon), and Mount Tento hikes. The Okhotsk Ryuhyo Museum, at the mountain's foot, recreates an ice room at -15°C.

Day 16

Saroma

where Japan's third-largest lake edges its shores with scallops and swans

★☆☆☆☆
📍 Lake Saroma Hokkaido📍 Cape Kimuanepp Saroma📍 Mount Horoiwa Observatory Saroma📍 Wakka Garden Saroma📍 Wakka Nature Park Saroma
1

Lake Saroma is Hokkaido's largest lake and Japan's third (154 km²), separated from the Sea of Okhotsk by a sand spit. It's a brackish lake - a mixture of fresh water and seawater entering through two openings in the spit. From Mount Horoiwa observatory (376 m), accessible in 1 hour hiking, the view of the lake's blue expanse and coastal spit provides scale of what is actually a gigantic marine lagoon. The sunset from this lookout - sky and lake merging in orange and purple hues - is one of Hokkaido's most beautiful.

⏱ 90 min
2

Cape Kimuanepp juts into Lake Saroma from the north shore, at the coastal spit's end. In autumn (mid-September to early October), a glasswort variety (akkeshiso) reddens and covers the ground with a crimson carpet - a rare phenomenon also visible at Notsuke and around Lake Toro. The contrast between the coral grass's flaming red and the lake's blue background is photogenic.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hike or bike in Wakka Garden (Wakka Gensei-kaen), at the sand spit's eastern end, one of Japan's richest dune floras with over 300 plant species. The Nature Center lends bikes for free (season April to mid-October). The trail runs along the Okhotsk on one side and Lake Saroma on the other - views of both water bodies simultaneously.

⏱ 90 min

Lake Saroma is renowned for its farmed scallops and oysters, whose quality is recognized throughout Japan. Lakeside restaurants serve shell-on scallops, grilled or raw as sashimi, with a flavor made incomparable by proximity to the sea. A specialty not to miss if passing through.

⏱ 60 min

In winter, Lake Saroma partially freezes and hosts ice smelt fishing. Whooper swans migrate from Siberia and rest on non-frozen zones - up to several hundred birds simultaneously. The sunset over the lake with swan silhouettes is an unparalleled winter spectacle.

Day 17

Wakkanai

where Japan's northernmost point faces Russia across the strait

★★★☆☆
📍 Cape Soya Wakkanai📍 Soya Hills Wakkanai📍 White Shell Road Wakkanai📍 Breakwater Dome Wakkanai📍 Cape Noshappu Wakkanai
1

The Soya Hills, behind Cape Soya, are a periglacial undulating landscape formed 20,000 years ago during the last ice age by repeated cycles of ground freezing and thawing. Their rolling form, devoid of trees since fires in the mid-Meiji era, has been classified Hokkaido heritage since 2004. The 57 wind turbines turning there have powered Wakkanai city with electricity for several years. The White Road (Shiro no Michi), opened in 2011, winds for 3 kilometers through these hills - paved with crushed scallop shells, a local specialty, giving it a dazzling white hue contrasting with the grass green.

⏱ 90 min
2

Cape Soya is Japan's northernmost accessible point - latitude 45.31°N. 43 km north, in clear weather, you can make out the Russian island of Sakhalin. A triangular monument points north, and a statue honors Mamiya Rinzo, the Japanese explorer who mapped Sakhalin in the 19th century. Cape Soya is also linked to France: Comte de La Pérouse was the first European to navigate between Sakhalin and Hokkaido during his worldwide expedition in the 1780s.

⏱ 45 min
3

Wakkanai's Breakwater Dome, built in 1936, is a 427-meter-long structure supported by 70 Greek-style columns 13.6 meters high - a baroque architectural feat in this far-north context. It protects the port against waves from Siberia and serves as a panoramic promenade facing the sea and the islands of Rishiri and Rebun.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walk the White Road through the Soya Hills. The trail exists in 3 km version (about 1h) or 5 km (about 2h). Choose a clear day to enjoy the panorama of the Sea of Japan on one side and the Sea of Okhotsk on the other. Wind is constant and sometimes violent - bring a windbreaker. Soya mountain cattle graze peacefully among the wind turbines.

⏱ 120 min

From Cape Noshappu, 10 minutes by bus from Wakkanai, watch the sunset over the Sea of Japan with the silhouettes of Rishiri and Rebun islands in the background - Rishiri Island with its conical volcano resembles a northern Fuji. It's one of northern Hokkaido's most appreciated twilight spectacles.

⏱ 60 min

Wakkanai is at Russia's edge - Cyrillic signs intersperse downtown, recalling Russian territorial claims on the Kuril Islands. Taste specialties from Fukuko covered market: crab, grilled scallops, and hotate ramen (scallop ramen), local specialty.

Day 18

Rishiri

where the solitary volcano rises from the Sea of Japan like a northern Fuji

★★★★☆
📍 Mount Rishiri Rishiri Island📍 Hime-numa Pond Rishiri📍 Otatomari Pond Rishiri📍 Cape Peshi Rishiri📍 Oshidomari Port Rishiri
1

Mount Rishiri (1,721 m) is a solitary stratovolcano rising from the Sea of Japan, ranked among Japan's 100 famous mountains and nicknamed "Rishiri-Fuji" for its perfect conical shape. From the ferry, the snow-capped cone emerging from blue waters recalls Mount Fuji - but surrounded by water on all sides. Its name comes from Ainu and means "high island." In summer, endemic alpine flowers grow on its flanks, including the Rishiri poppy (Rishiri-hinageshi) that grows nowhere else in the world. The complete ascent takes 10 to 12 hours round trip - reserved for experienced hikers and in good weather only.

⏱ 90 min
2

Hime-numa Pond, in the island's north near Oshidomari, is an ornithological observation site nestled at the volcano's foot. In calm weather, Mount Rishiri's perfect silhouette reflects in its waters - the island's most photographed image. Otatomari Pond, in the south, offers the same composition in a wilder setting, surrounded by Sakhalin spruce forest.

⏱ 60 min
3

Cape Peshi, next to Oshidomari port, is reached in 15 minutes walking from the ferry terminal. From this 93-meter promontory, the view of the port, the Sea of Japan, and Mount Rishiri's cone in the background is striking. It's also one of the island's best spots for sunset.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Leave your car in Wakkanai - bringing a vehicle on the ferry costs 22,000 to 30,000 yen one way. Rather rent a bike or car on the island upon arrival at Oshidomari. The dedicated bike path runs along the north coast for 20 km with spectacular views of the Sea of Japan. For the complete island circuit (60 km), plan a full day by bike.

⏱ 180 min

If you're fit, Mount Rishiri ascent is the island's experience. Departure before 5am from the Oshidomari trailhead, allow 10 to 12 hours round trip. From the summit, 360° views of the island, Rebun, Hokkaido, and in clear weather to Sakhalin in Russia. The climbing season runs from late June to early October. Filing a climbing plan is recommended.

⏱ 600 min

Don't leave Rishiri without tasting sea urchins (uni) and konbu (kelp seaweed) - both reputed among Japan's finest, nourished in the cold rich waters around the island. Oshidomari port restaurants offer rice bowls with fresh morning urchins. A sea urchin harvesting experience is organized at Cape Kamui for visitors.

⏱ 60 min
Day 19

Rebun

where the floating island of flowers harbors 300 alpine plants growing at sea level

★★★★☆
📍 Rebun Island Hokkaido📍 Cape Sukoton Rebun📍 Momoiwa Observatory Rebun📍 Momoiwa Trail Rebun📍 Cape Kanedano Rebun
1

Rebun Island measures 29 km north to south and 8 km wide - it's Japan's northernmost island excluding uninhabited islets. Its nickname is the "floating island of flowers": about 300 alpine plant varieties grow here at sea level, a phenomenon unique in the world due to the island's particular arctic climate. Among them, the Rebun-atsumoriso - a pale lady's slipper growing nowhere else on Earth, flowering from late May to mid-June. Cape Sukoton, at the northern tip, offers views of the uninhabited Todo islet where sea lions laze, and in clear weather to Sakhalin.

⏱ 75 min
2

Momoiwa Observatory, in the island's south, is accessible via the 7 km trail running along the west coast from Kafuka port. This 3h30 route crosses cliff meadows covered with alpine flowers with Mount Rishiri's silhouette permanently visible behind you over the sea. Endemic Rebun edelweiss (Rebun-usuyukiso, Leontopodium discolor), the island's official flower, carpets the slopes. Momoiwa rock ("peach rock," 250 m) and Neko rock ("cat rock") animate this coastal landscape.

⏱ 210 min

Activities

The Momoiwa trail is Rebun's emblematic hike: 7 km west coast, 3h30 walk, accessible to all levels. It departs from Kafuka ferry terminal, crosses the Momoiwa promontory, runs along Motochi cliffs, passes before Jizo rock (50 m, resembling a bodhisattva in prayer) and reaches Shiretoko village in the south. Bus returns to port. Prefer a windless day - cliffs are exposed.

⏱ 210 min

For ambitious hikers, the 8-hour route connects Cape Sukoton (north) to Shiretoko (south) over 20 km, crossing the entire island via ridges. Simultaneous views of both coasts at pass summits. Early morning departure mandatory, bring provisions.

⏱ 480 min

Kita no Canaria Park, in the island's north, preserves the wooden school filmed for the Japanese feature film "Northern Canaries" (2012). The view from the café veranda of Mount Rishiri rising from the sea is one of the island's most romantic. Rebun sea urchins and hoke (local mackerel) as sashimi are specialties to taste at Kafuka port restaurants.

⏱ 60 min

Wakkanai

★★★☆☆
📍 Wakkanai Ferry Terminal Port📍 Wakkanai JR Station📍 Wakkanai Airport
1

Return ferry day from Rebun (or Rishiri) to Wakkanai. The crossing from Rebun takes about 2 hours; from Rishiri, 1h40. Wakkanai has an airport with flights to Tokyo (2h) and Sapporo (1h), as well as a JR station with trains to Sapporo (5h30). For travelers ending their road trip here, it's the car return point - or the opportunity to take the scenic train southward.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

If you have time before departure, head down to Fukuko Ichiba covered market for a final far-north seafood meal: crabs, sea urchins, and fresh Sea of Okhotsk scallops. Hotate ramen (scallop ramen) is the local specialty to order.

⏱ 60 min

For the most adventurous travelers: Teuri Island, 195 km south (Haboro), is accessible by ferry from Haboro port (1h30 crossing). It shelters the world's largest rhinoceros auklet colony - 400,000 breeding pairs. Nocturnal June visits allow observing birds returning to their burrows at sunset. Plan a night in Haboro or on the island. This detour is independent of the rest of the itinerary.
