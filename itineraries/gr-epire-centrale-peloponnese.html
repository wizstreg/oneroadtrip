Road Trip Greece: Epirus, Peloponnese and Athens

📅 7 days📍 7 stops🚗 825 km⏱ 29h45

Riza → Preveza → Marathias → Delphi → Athenes → Kalavryta → Patras

Riza → Preveza → Marathias → Delphi → Athenes → Kalavryta → Patras

The road begins beneath the eucalyptus trees of Epirus, on a wild beach that tour buses haven't discovered yet. It winds down through the Roman ruins of Nicopolis and Delphi's mysterious oracle, before plunging into Athens' tumultuous history. Then it heads back into the Peloponnese and its scarred mountain villages, all the way to Patras and the ferry to Italy. An itinerary that blends the Ionian coast, the great ancient sites, and the interior landscapes of mainland Greece.

Riza

★★★★☆
1

Welcome to Greece. The coastal road from Igoumenitsa to Riza Beach, about an hour south, sets the tone perfectly: long stretches of sandy shore following one after another, crystalline waters, and eucalyptus trees lining the roadside. Riza Beach remains refreshingly unspoiled—a sandy sweep frequented mainly by campervan travelers and Greek families. There's no big tourist infrastructure, which is precisely what makes it ideal for your first evening. If you're arriving via the Igoumenitsa ferry, allow at least 30 minutes to clear the port before hitting the road.

⏱ 60 min
2

En route from the port, make a detour through the village of Plataria, 12 km from Igoumenitsa, where green mountains meet the blue Ionian Sea in beautiful harmony. If you've got time before settling in, push on to Sivota Bay—about fifteen kilometers south of Plataria—where small verdant islands rise from impossibly blue water. It's one of the most stunning natural anchorages in Epirus.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Swimming and unwinding at Riza Beach—you've earned some downtime after arriving. The taverna at Acrogiali camping, just steps from the shore, makes a fine choice for dinner: Greek specialties made with fresh ingredients in a straightforward, welcoming setting. Time to order your first tzatziki and souvlaki in peace.

Preveza

★★★☆☆
1

The Necromanteion of Acheron at Mesopotamos ranks among Greece's most mysterious sites. Identified in 1958 as a sanctuary dedicated to Hades and Persephone, this was renowned throughout antiquity as the place where the living could speak with their dead—the nearby Acheron River was believed to be a tributary of the Styx itself. Homer mentions it in the Odyssey: this is where Odysseus comes to consult the shade of the prophet Tiresias. Ancient pilgrims underwent a ritual lasting several days (fasting, purification, narcotic plant preparations) before descending into an underground labyrinth to await the appearance of the deceased. The visit takes about 30 to 45 minutes—don't miss the underground tunnels, which still send shivers down your spine.

⏱ 60 min
2

Eight kilometers north of Preveza, the site of Nicopolis (the "city of victory") stands as one of the most significant Roman foundations in Greece. Octavian Augustus created it from nothing in 31 BC, on the site of his encampment following his decisive naval victory at Actium over Mark Antony and Cleopatra. The city, which housed tens of thousands at its peak, sprawls across roughly 13,000 hectares today—making it the largest archaeological zone in Greece. Start with the archaeological museum, inaugurated in 2009, which traces the site's history from the battle through the Byzantine era. Then head up to the Byzantine ramparts, the Basilica of Doumetios, and the odeon, which still hosts a summer festival. Lesser-known fact: it was at Nicopolis that Epictetus, the great Stoic philosopher, taught his philosophy until 125 AD, after being expelled from Rome by Domitian. Wear comfortable shoes and bring water—the site is vast and offers little shade.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Drive through the Aktio-Preveza undersea tunnel, inaugurated in 2002, which connects Preveza on the north shore of the Ambracian Gulf to the Aktio peninsula in the south—the very spot where the Battle of Actium unfolded. At 4.7 km total length, with 1.57 km beneath the seabed, it's Greece's only undersea tunnel. The drive takes just a few minutes, but the detour is worthwhile for the historical context. From the far shore, you get a beautiful view back across to Preveza and the ancient site of Nicopolis.

⏱ 45 min

Marathias

★★☆☆☆
1

The Etoliko lagoon, along the coastal road between Preveza and Marathias, makes for an unexpected stop that differs dramatically from the beaches you've already seen. Here there's no sand, but instead a vast coastal marsh—one of Greece's most important—where flamingos, herons, and numerous wading species make their home. If you're a birder, pack binoculars and pull over to observe. The landscape, with village houses suspended between sky and water, has a singular quality all its own.

⏱ 45 min
2

Marathias is a small fishing village on the Ionian Sea, little-known to foreigners and visited mainly by Greeks. It's far from the tourist bustle: a fishing harbor, a handful of hotels, and tavernas serving the catch of the day. What really makes it worthwhile is the view from the seafront: across the water, the Peloponnese mountains rise in bluish silhouette. In the evening, locals gather along the waterfront for dinner and sometimes informal performances—ask when you arrive if there's anything happening tonight.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Settle in along Marathias' seafront at sunset. With the Peloponnese ridges closing the horizon, the late-day light is often spectacular. It's also the perfect opportunity to try a pita gyros with a sea view—local tavernas serve them at very modest prices, with fresh ingredients. To round out the evening, a short stroll along the harbor is enough to discover the unhurried rhythm of this village that lives at half-speed.

⏱ 90 min

Delphi

★★★★★
1

Delphi is the most-visited site in Greece after the Parthenon—and with good reason. Perched at 570 meters altitude on the slopes of Mount Parnassus (which peaks at 2,459 m), the Sanctuary of Apollo served for over a thousand years as the spiritual and political heart of the Greek world. From the 6th to 4th centuries BC, kings, generals, and entire city-states came to consult the Pythia—the priestess charged with transmitting the god's oracles—before any major decision. The fascinating detail: her answers were deliberately ambiguous. Croesus, king of Lydia, asked whether attacking the Persians was wise; the oracle replied that if he crossed the Halys River, a great empire would fall. He launched the campaign—and it was his own empire that collapsed. The entrance ticket (€12 full price, €6 reduced) covers both the sanctuary and the museum. Arrive at opening time, 8 AM: the tour buses roll in after 10.

⏱ 150 min
2

The Archaeological Museum of Delphi houses one of Greece's finest collections. Don't miss the Charioteer of Delphi, a 5th-century BC bronze statue of astonishing precision—the eyelashes, the veins in the arms, the eyes in glass and ivory remain intact after 2,500 years. The museum also holds friezes from the Treasury of the Siphnians, the Omphalos (the sacred stone marking the "navel of the world"), and a large 6th-century BC kouros. Allow at least 90 minutes if you want time to read the labels properly.

⏱ 90 min
3

Just below the main sanctuary, don't miss the Sanctuary of Athena Pronaia and its Tholos—a circular 4th-century BC building with Doric columns whose function remains a mystery to archaeologists. It's one of the most photographed images in Greece, and for excellent reasons: the three standing columns framed by the cliffs of the Phaedriades rocks and the landscape plunging toward the Kirra plain are simply magnificent. Access is free and the site is often less crowded than the main sanctuary.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Before leaving the site, climb up to Delphi's ancient stadium—it's the highest point of the sanctuary and often less crowded than the Temple of Apollo. The stone seats and packed-earth track are remarkably well preserved. In the evening, from the modern village, settle onto a terrace to watch the Phaedriades rocks change color with the light—from dazzling white to deep ochre at sunset. The view from the main street across the valley and olive groves descending toward the Gulf of Corinth, fifteen kilometers away, is among the most beautiful in all of central Greece.

⏱ 60 min

Athenes

★★★★☆
1

The Acropolis of Athens sits atop a rocky plateau 156 meters above the city. The Parthenon—built between 447 and 432 BC under Pericles by the architects Ictinus and Callicrates—is the centerpiece: a Doric temple of Pentelic marble that once housed a 12-meter-tall chryselephantine statue of Athena. What's less often mentioned is that the complex served successively as a Christian church, a mosque, and then a Turkish arsenal—before partially exploding in 1687 when the Venetians bombarded the Ottoman powder magazine stored inside. What you see today is an assemblage of successive restorations since the 19th century. Book your ticket online in advance, especially in July-August: standby queues without reservations are endless. Arrive at 8 AM opening time or late afternoon for better light and cooler temperatures.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Acropolis Museum, reopened in a modern building in 2009, is essential for understanding what you've just seen. It houses the Parthenon friezes that weren't carried off by Lord Elgin in the early 19th century—the famous "Elgin Marbles" that Greece still claims from the British Museum. The building itself is an engineering feat: constructed in glass on pillars, it reveals archaeological excavations below. The Parthenon Gallery on the third floor is aligned with the temple and bathed in natural light. Allow at least 90 minutes.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Ancient Agora, below the Acropolis on the north side, often receives fewer visitors than the sacred rock—but it deserves a stop. This is where Athens' political and commercial life unfolded during the classical era, where Socrates debated in the streets, and where Saint Paul came to preach in the 1st century AD. The Temple of Hephaestus, on the northwest side of the site, is one of the best-preserved Greek temples in the world—roof and columns virtually intact. The ticket is separate from the Acropolis admission.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Get lost in the Plaka district, at the foot of the Acropolis—it's Athens' old center, with its cobbled lanes, colorful neoclassical houses, and flowered terraces cascading down in steps. To escape the tourist crowds, climb into the tiny Anafiotika quarter, tucked against the Acropolis flank: whitewashed houses, narrow stairways, geraniums in the windows—you'd swear you were on a Cycladic island in the heart of Athens. In the evening, head to Monastiraki for dinner with a direct view of the illuminated Parthenon—the effect from certain terraces is breathtaking.

⏱ 120 min

Kalavryta

★★★☆☆
1

The Corinth Canal, which you'll cross leaving Athens, is one of Greece's most striking sights. This corridor carved through a limestone isthmus—inaugurated in 1893—links the Gulf of Corinth to the Aegean Sea over 6.4 km in length, at 79 meters depth. The width is only 25 meters, making it impractical for modern large vessels. Stop on the bridge that spans it: the vertical view down to the turquoise water below is dizzying. The idea for the canal dates back to antiquity—Nero himself launched construction in 67 AD, with thousands of slaves, before abandoning the entire project.

⏱ 45 min
2

Kalavryta, at 735 meters altitude in the Achaea mountains, is one of the Peloponnese's most moving villages. On December 13, 1943, soldiers of the Wehrmacht's 117th Jäger Division encircled the village and executed the entire male population aged 12 and over—1,436 people by estimates—in reprisal for the deaths of 81 German soldiers. The women and children, locked in the school, managed to escape during the massacre. Only 13 men survived. Since then, the clock on the Orthodox church has been stopped at 2:34 PM, the exact time of the executions. A memorial at the massacre site bears the names of all the victims—it's understated, without theatrics, and all the more powerful for it.

⏱ 90 min
3

Six kilometers from Kalavryta, the Monastery of Agia Lavra (the Holy Lavra), founded in 961, is a place charged with dual history. It was here that on March 25, 1821, Archbishop Germanos of Patras raised the banner of revolt against the Ottomans—a gesture that sparked the Greek War of Independence and made March 25 the national holiday. The monastery was burned by the Germans in the aftermath of the 1943 Kalavryta massacre and has since been rebuilt. It houses a small collection of relics and historical objects related to the revolution.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Kalavryta is also a pleasant mountain resort, perched at 735 m altitude in the Chelmos-Vouraikos National Park, designated a UNESCO Global Geopark. If you have energy left in the late afternoon, a short hike from the village to the hamlet of Zachlorou follows the river through a superb green corridor—allow 1 hour each way. For lovers of local products, the pedestrian street 25is Martiou has groceries where you'll find Kalavryta PDO feta, award-winning mountain honey, and chestnut specialties.

⏱ 90 min

Patras

★★★★☆
1

The Kastria Cave, nicknamed the "Cave of the Lakes," is one of the Peloponnese's most surprising natural wonders. It stretches 1,890 meters in total and contains a succession of 13 terraced underground lakes—three of which are accessible during the guided tour (500 m route). The blue-green water reflects stalactite and stalagmite formations in very soft light, and 10 bat species coexist discreetly on the ceiling. The adjacent museum displays fossils found on site, including European hippopotamus bones—yes, hippos once lived in Greece. Arrive in the morning if possible: the cave stays at 14°C year-round, which is pleasant in summer. The guided tour lasts about 30 minutes.

⏱ 75 min
2

Patras, Greece's third-largest city and the Peloponnese's largest, is known mainly as the departure port for Italy—but it deserves a stop. From the Aghiou Nikolaou pedestrian street, 192 steps climb to the old town and its colorful neoclassical mansions. At the top, Patras' medieval castle, built in the 6th century AD on the ruins of the ancient Acropolis, offers sweeping views across the gulf and the Rio-Antirrio bridge—the cable-stayed suspension bridge completed in 2004 that links the Peloponnese to mainland Greece over 2.8 km. It's one of the world's longest cable-stayed bridges. Descending toward the port, the seafront promenade invites one last Mediterranean pause before boarding.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

In late afternoon, catch the ferry from Patras port to Bari in Italy—the crossing takes approximately 16.5 hours, with cabin or airline-seat options depending on budget. Companies serving the route operate regular departures. Book well in advance for July-August. Onboard: bar, restaurant, and usually outdoor deck space. It's an elegant way to close this road trip, watching the Greek coast fade into the sunset.
