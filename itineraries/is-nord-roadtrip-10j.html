📆 Best period

June, July, August

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 2 and 6 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Summer (June-August): Midnight sun, warmest weather, lupine flowers, highland access, peak tourist season, highest prices, advance booking essential.

Winter (December-February): Northern Lights season, ice caves, winter activities, darker days, lowest prices, fewer crowds, magical atmosphere.

Shoulder Season (April-May & September-November): Aurora viewing possible, fewer crowds, moderate prices, transitional weather, excellent value, variable conditions.

Spring (March-May): Lengthening days, awakening nature, moderate temperatures, bird nesting, good weather, fewer tourists, excellent photography.

Fall (September-November): Aurora season begins, autumn colors, comfortable weather, harvest season, good value, stunning landscapes.

🌍 What to discover in IS?

Nordic island with dramatic landscapes, geothermal wonders, and unique culture. Experience Northern Lights, pristine nature, and authentic Icelandic lifestyle. The country offers natural phenomena with cultural authenticity and progressive values. Iceland provides life-changing natural experiences with cultural depth and environmental leadership.

📋 Important notes

4x4 vehicle recommended for the Westfjords (gravel roads, mountain passes). Roads accessible June to September only. Last gas station before Látrabjarg is in Flókalundur - fill up there. The Baldur ferry (Stykkishólmur → Brjánslækur) must be booked in advance during summer. Warning: gravel roads in the Westfjords often require speeds of 30-40 km/h. Actual driving times are often much longer than Google Maps suggests. /// WHAT MAKES THIS DIFFERENT: this itinerary does NOT circle the island. It focuses on the Snæfellsnes peninsula and the Westfjords - a region only 10% of visitors discover. No Jökulsárlón, no Mývatn, no south coast. If you want the greatest hits, choose the 12-day Ring Road circuit or the 17-day Highlands route. This circuit is for those who want wild Iceland, the puffin cliffs of Látrabjarg, deserted fjords, and hot springs with nobody around.

Stop 2

Stykkisholmur

Colorful harbor gateway to the Westfjords ferry crossing

★★★★☆
📍 Hraunfossar Waterfalls📍 Barnafoss Waterfall📍 Kirkjufell Mountain Grundarfjörður📍 Arnarstapi Cliffs Snæfellsnes📍 Djúpalónssandur Beach
1

Morning drive northwest. First stop: Hraunfossar waterfalls, where dozens of streams pour directly from a 900-year-old lava field over nearly a kilometer. Right next door, Barnafoss (the "children's waterfall") takes its name from two children said to have disappeared while crossing a natural stone arch over the falls.

⏱ 35 min
2

Turn onto Route 54 toward the Snæfellsnes peninsula. Stop before Kirkjufell (463 m), Iceland's most photographed mountain, with Kirkjufellsfoss waterfall in the foreground. It's the "arrowhead mountain" from Game of Thrones (season 7). The nearby fishing village of Grundarfjörður has 900 inhabitants.

⏱ 40 min
3

Head down to Snæfellsnes' south coast. The coastal trail between Arnarstapi and Hellnar (2.5 km, 45 min) runs along natural arches and basalt columns. Don't miss the Gatklettur arch at Arnarstapi. Further along, Djúpalónssandur is a black pebble beach with four test stones that fishermen had to lift to prove their strength (23, 54, 104, and 154 kg).

⏱ 75 min
4

On your way back up to Stykkishólmur, stop at Búðakirkja, the little black church from 1848 standing alone in a lava field. The Snæfellsjökull glacier (1,446 m) - the one from Jules Verne's "Journey to the Center of the Earth" - dominates the peninsula on clear days.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

In the evening, explore the colorful harbor of Stykkishólmur. Climb to the Súgandisey lighthouse for views over Breiðafjörður fjord and its thousands of islets. If you still have energy, try the local mussels at one of the harbor restaurants.

Stop 3

Patreksfjordur

Where fishing boats meet the world's largest puffin cliffs

★★☆☆☆
📍 Baldur Ferry Stykkishólmur📍 Flatey Island Breiðafjörður📍 Rauðisandur Red Sand Beach Westfjords
1

Board the Baldur ferry at Stykkishólmur (departs 9am in summer). The 2.5-hour crossing passes through Breiðafjörður fjord, dotted with thousands of islets. If you take the first ferry, you can make a stopover on Flatey island - a village of 5 permanent residents where all the houses have been restored in 19th-century style. Cars aren't allowed on the island; a crew member will park yours on the other side. Catch the next ferry to Brjánslækur.

⏱ 150 min
2

From Brjánslækur, take the road toward the southern Westfjords. Make a detour to Rauðisandur, a 10-km beach of red and golden sand - a striking contrast to Iceland's usual black beaches. The road down is steep and unpaved but manageable. With luck, you'll spot seals on the shore.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

On the way, stop at Hellulaug, a natural hot spring on the fjord's edge. The small pool carved into the rock offers water at about 38°C with a direct view of mountains and fjord - free and open year-round, with no facilities whatsoever. Bring a towel.

⏱ 45 min

Stop 4

Patreksfjordur

Where fishing boats meet the world's largest puffin cliffs

★★☆☆☆
📍 Látrabjarg Bird Cliffs Westfjords📍 Breiðavík Beach Westfjords📍 Patreksfjörður Harbour
1

A day dedicated to the Látrabjarg cliffs, Europe's westernmost point. These cliffs stretch for 14 km and reach 440 meters high. They shelter the world's largest colony of Atlantic puffins - millions of birds nest here from mid-June to late July. Razorbills, guillemots, fulmars, and kittiwakes complete the spectacle. The puffins aren't shy: lie down on the ground a meter from the edge (no closer!) to photograph them up close. Warning: there are no barriers, the ground is riddled with burrows and can collapse under your weight.

⏱ 150 min
2

On the way, stop at Breiðavík beach, a stretch of golden sand - rare in Iceland - with turquoise water (but freezing cold). The contrast with the dark cliffs is magnificent. A few isolated farms dot the landscape.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

In the evening, wander around the small harbor of Patreksfjörður (650 inhabitants). It's one of the few Westfjords villages with services (gas, grocery, restaurants). The fjord is surrounded by mountains and the atmosphere is that of an inhabited end of the world.

⏱ 40 min

Stop 5

Isafjordur

A mountain-ringed town perched on a sandbank at the world's edge

★★★★★
📍 Dynjandi Waterfall Westfjords📍 Arnarfjörður Fjord📍 Þingeyri Village Westfjords
1

A spectacular day's drive north through the fjords. The road is long and often gravel - expect 30-40 km/h on some sections, don't underestimate the travel time. The major stop: Dynjandi (also called Fjallfoss), considered by many to be Iceland's most beautiful waterfall. Standing 100 meters tall, it widens from 30 meters at the top to 60 meters at its base - it looks like a bridal veil unfurled down the mountain. It's actually a series of 7 tiered waterfalls, each with its own name. The trail from the parking lot (800 m, 20 minutes) passes the first six before reaching Dynjandi at the top. Protected natural monument since 1981. Come late in the day for the best light - in the morning it's backlit.

⏱ 75 min
2

Along the way, Arnarfjörður fjord is one of the most beautiful and widest in the Westfjords. Route 60 winds along its shores with constant panoramas of snow-capped mountains and the fjord's calm water. Make photo stops.

⏱ 30 min
3

If you have time, stop in Þingeyri, a village of 250 inhabitants at the head of Dýrafjörður fjord. It's one of the oldest trading posts in the Westfjords. The Old Blacksmith's Workshop (Svartasmiðjan) has been restored and now serves as a cafe - a surprising spot in the middle of nowhere.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The road between fjords crosses mountain passes with dizzying views. Some sections are single-lane roads - yield in the pullouts. Drive slowly, enjoy the scenery, and keep in mind that in the Westfjords, the journey IS the destination.

⏱ 30 min

Stop 6

Isafjordur

A mountain-ringed town perched on a sandbank at the world's edge

★★★★★
📍 Ísafjörður Old Town Westfjords📍 Bolungarvík Village📍 Ósvör Maritime Museum📍 Flateyri Village Westfjords
1

Ísafjörður (2,600 inhabitants) is the "capital" of the Westfjords - the town nestles on a triangular sandbank at the head of Skutulsfjörður fjord, hemmed in by mountains that rise steeply. The old quarter (Neðstikaupstaður) preserves 18th-century wooden houses, among Iceland's oldest. Stroll through the colorful streets and visit the Westfjords Heritage Museum (Vestfjarðasafn) housed in a warehouse from 1784.

⏱ 60 min
2

Take the excursion to Bolungarvík, 15 minutes away. This fishing village is the northernmost in the Westfjords. The Ósvör maritime museum, a reconstruction of a traditional fishing station with turf huts and fish-drying racks, gives a glimpse of the harsh life of Icelandic fishermen. From the Bolafjall viewpoint (accessible by car), the view over the Arctic Ocean and the Greenland coast on clear days is striking.

⏱ 60 min
3

On the other side of the fjord, the village of Flateyri (200 inhabitants) deserves a detour. It's the largest village in Önundarfjörður fjord, with a historic bookstore (Gamla bókaverslunin, opened 1914) whose decor has barely changed since. In 1995, a devastating avalanche struck the village, killing 20 people - an imposing protective wall has been built since.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

If you're adventurous, outfitters in Ísafjörður offer kayak excursions in the fjords - a unique way to see the coast from the water, between cliffs and bird colonies. In summer, boat trips to the Hornstrandir nature reserve (Iceland's wildest and most uninhabited region) are also possible.

⏱ 120 min

Stop 7

Holmavik

Where witchcraft legends linger on Iceland's loneliest coast

★★★☆☆
📍 Drangsnes Hot Pots Westfjords📍 Museum of Icelandic Sorcery Hólmavík📍 Strandir Coast Westfjords
1

Long drive southeast along the Strandir coast, one of Iceland's most isolated regions. The desolate, wind-battered landscapes have fueled centuries of witchcraft legends. In Hólmavík, the Museum of Icelandic Sorcery and Witchcraft (Galdrasýning á Ströndum) is as strange as it is fascinating: it traces the 17th-century witchcraft trials in Iceland, where 21 people were burned (including 20 men, unique in Europe). The museum's most famous object is a reconstruction of "nábrók," trousers made from human skin said to bring wealth to the wearer.

⏱ 60 min
2

Stop at Drangsnes, a tiny fishing village on the Strandir coast. The three hot pots by the sea are free and open 24/7 - you bathe in 40°C water with a direct view of Grímsey island in the fjord. It's one of Iceland's most authentic baths, far from any tourist infrastructure.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The Strandir coast is arctic fox territory, Iceland's only native land mammal. Keep your eyes open while driving - they're sometimes spotted along the road, especially early morning. An arctic fox research and rescue center operates in the region.

Stop 8

Reykjavik

Where colorful streets meet geothermal pools and Nordic creativity

★★★★★
📍 Borgarfjörður Valley Iceland📍 Deildartunguhver Hot Spring📍 Reykjavik City Centre
1

Drive back to Reykjavik via Borgarfjörður. Along the way, stop at Deildartunguhver, Europe's most powerful hot spring: 180 liters of water at 100°C gush out every second. A spa has been built next door for those who want to soak (in cooled water, rest assured).

⏱ 40 min
2

If you didn't have time to visit Perlan in Reykjavik, this is your last chance. This glass dome perched on geothermal tanks houses a 100-meter artificial ice cave and a 360° panoramic terrace.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Enjoy your last evening in Reykjavik. The Grandi district (former industrial harbor) brings together restaurants, galleries, and the food court with Icelandic and international cuisine. A relaxed way to conclude 8 days in Iceland's wildest regions.
