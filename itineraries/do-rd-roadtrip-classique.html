Dominican Republic Road Trip: North Coast & Central Highlands

📅 7 days📍 6 stops🚗 495 km⏱ 9h20

Santo Domingo → Santiago De Los Caballeros → Puerto Plata → Cabarete → Jarabacoa

Six days in a loop from Santo Domingo to discover the Dominican Republic beyond the resorts: colonial streets of the capital, the country's second city with its controversial monument, Atlantic beaches of Puerto Plata and Cabarete, and the mountain waterfalls of Jarabacoa in the Central Highlands.

✨ Highlights
  • Santo Domingo's Zona Colonial, the oldest European quarter on the American continent
  • The Isabel de Torres cable car, unique in the Caribbean, with sweeping Atlantic coast views
  • Cabarete and its trade winds: kitesurfing, windsurfing, and surfing on the same coast
  • The waterfalls and rafting of Jarabacoa in the cool Central Highlands
📆 Best period

December, January, February, March, April

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 5 and 9 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Peak Season (December-April): Perfect weather, minimal rainfall, ideal beach conditions, highest prices, most crowded, advance booking essential.

Shoulder Season (May-June, November): Good weather with occasional showers, fewer crowds, moderate prices, excellent value.

Hurricane Season (July-October): Higher humidity, potential storms, lowest prices, lush landscapes, September-October peak risk.

Baseball Season (October-February): Winter baseball league, cultural experience, passionate fans, authentic Dominican atmosphere.

🌍 What to discover in DO?

Caribbean's most diverse destination with pristine beaches, merengue music, and colonial heritage. Experience Santo Domingo's history, Punta Cana resorts, and authentic Dominican culture. The country offers excellent value with diverse landscapes and warm hospitality. Dominican Republic provides complete Caribbean experiences with cultural authenticity and beach paradise.

Day 1

Santo Domingo

Where the New World's first European streets still echo with five centuries of history

★★★★★
📍 Zona Colonial Santo Domingo📍 Catedral Primada de América Santo Domingo📍 Alcázar de Colón Santo Domingo📍 Fortaleza Ozama Santo Domingo📍 Parque Colón Santo Domingo📍 Calle Las Damas Santo Domingo📍 Puerta del Conde Santo Domingo📍 Malecón Santo Domingo
1

Start with the Zona Colonial, a UNESCO World Heritage site since 1990—this is where the first European quarter in the New World took root, founded in 1498 by Bartolomé Colón. From this grid of cobblestone streets, the Spanish orchestrated the conquest of an entire continent. Walk slowly: the facades date back to the early 16th century, and it's rare to find this density of monuments packed into a single city block.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Catedral Primada de América, built between 1514 and 1540, is the oldest cathedral in the Americas. It rises above Parque Colón, where a statue of Christopher Columbus stands at the very center—a detail that still sparks debate here. Inside, you'll find Columbus's presumed tomb: his remains were transferred from this very cathedral to the Faro a Colón in 1992—but the controversy is far from settled. DNA analyses published in 2024 by the University of Granada conclude that Columbus actually rests in Seville. Allow 45 minutes to take it all in without rushing.

⏱ 60 min
3

Head to Calle Las Damas, the oldest paved street in the New World, laid out in 1502. It runs alongside the Alcázar de Colón, the former palace of Christopher Columbus's son, Diego. Built in 1510, the building has the unusual distinction of being constructed entirely from coral stone without a single nail. Inside is a museum displaying period furniture and colonial decorations. Don't walk past without going in.

⏱ 60 min
4

As you make your way toward Puerta del Conde, stop at Fortaleza Ozama—the oldest fortress still standing in the Americas, built between 1502 and 1508 to guard the mouth of the Ozama River. The fortress walls are up to 3 meters thick. The view from the ramparts over the river and port is worth the stop.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Late afternoon, head down to Avenida El Malecón at sunset. This 9-kilometer boulevard hugs the Caribbean Sea and comes alive around 5pm: Dominican families, coconut vendors, street musicians. This is the hour not to miss if you want to feel the real rhythm of the city, far from the tourist circuits.

⏱ 60 min

After dark, the Zona Colonial transforms. The ruins of the San Francisco convent regularly host open-air bachata and merengue concerts—a scene frequented as much by locals as visitors. Ask your hotel if there's an event scheduled for tonight.

⏱ 90 min

Day 2

Santiago De Los Caballeros

Where tobacco fortunes built monuments and carnival traditions run deeper than anywhere else

★★★★☆
📍 Monumento Héroes Restauración Santiago de los Caballeros📍 Parque Duarte Santiago de los Caballeros📍 Centro León Santiago de los Caballeros📍 Catedral de Santiago Santiago de los Caballeros📍 Mercado Modelo Santiago de los Caballeros📍 Fortaleza San Luis Santiago de los Caballeros
1

You'll spot the Monumento from afar as you arrive—this 67-meter tower dominates Santiago from a central hilltop. Its history is worth knowing: commissioned in 1944 by dictator Trujillo under the name 'Monument to the Peace of Trujillo'—legend has it that the number of interior stairs symbolizes the 365 days of the year, as if to signal his daily benevolence toward the people. After his assassination in 1961, the government renamed the structure to honor the heroes of the Restoration War (1863-1865) against Spain. Inside, five floors of museum chronicle this independence war with life-sized dioramas and 20 frescoes by exiled Spanish painter José Vela Zanetti. Climb the stairs late in the day for the best light over Santiago.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Centro León, built with the fortune of the León Jiménez family (tobacco and beer), is the finest Dominican art museum in the country—and one of the most serious in the entire Caribbean. The collections cover pre-Columbian Taíno art, the colonial period, and contemporary Dominican artists. Allow at least 1.5 hours to see it properly. Open Tuesday through Sunday.

⏱ 90 min
3

Walk down to Parque Duarte, the historic heart of Santiago, framed by the cathedral built between 1868 and 1895 in a blend of Gothic and Neoclassical styles, and the former city hall which now houses a small museum on the city's history. If you're here in February, this is where the Santiago Carnival parades kick off—one of the oldest carnivals in the Caribbean.

⏱ 45 min
4

Stop by the Mercado Modelo to observe local life: spices, tropical fruits, handicrafts, tafia (artisanal rum). They also sell hand-rolled cigars here—Santiago sits at the heart of the Cibao tobacco region, which produces some of the world's finest cigars. Only buy what you can carry on the plane.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The drive from Santo Domingo crosses the plains of the Cibao, the country's agricultural heartland. If you leave early, make a stop in La Vega, halfway along, to see its cacao plantations and buy local chocolate. The main highway (Autopista Duarte) is good, but trucks are plentiful—drive carefully.

⏱ 60 min

In the evening in Santiago, head to Calle del Sol or the area around the Monumento for dinner and try Cibaeño cuisine: sancocho (a stew of meats and root vegetables), mofongo (mashed fried plantain), or chicharrón de cerdo. Local restaurants around Parque Duarte are far more authentic than the tourist chains.

⏱ 90 min

Day 3

Puerto Plata

Where a Spanish fortress guards the shore and a cable car soars above the clouds

★★★☆☆
📍 Téléphérique Isabel de Torres Puerto Plata📍 Fortaleza San Felipe Puerto Plata📍 Musée de l'Ambre Puerto Plata📍 Parque Central Puerto Plata📍 Malecón Puerto Plata📍 Playa Dorada Puerto Plata
1

Take the cable car—the only one of its kind in the Caribbean—up to the summit of Mount Isabel de Torres at 793 meters altitude. The cabin climbs through 10 minutes of dense tropical forest before emerging onto a 360° view of Puerto Plata, the bay, and the Atlantic. At the summit, you'll find a botanical garden with 600 plant species and a 16-meter Christ the Redeemer statue inaugurated in 1975—a gift from Brazil, modeled after the famous Christ statue on Rio de Janeiro's Corcovado. The cable car operates from 8:30am to 5pm, but cabins only depart on demand. Arrive early: the summit is often shrouded in clouds by midday.

⏱ 120 min
2

Fortaleza San Felipe occupies a promontory jutting into the ocean at the western end of the Malecón. Commissioned by Philip II of Spain in 1564 and completed in 1577, it's the only intact 16th-century vestige in Puerto Plata—the rest of the old town having been destroyed during the Restoration wars. You can visit a small military museum inside, and the cell where Juan Pablo Duarte, independence hero, was imprisoned in 1844. The view over the bay from the ramparts is especially beautiful at sunset.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Amber Museum, housed in a Victorian mansion in the town center, presents one of the finest collections of Dominican amber—a fossilized resin tens of millions of years old, extracted almost exclusively from the north of the island. Some pieces contain perfectly preserved insects or plants, frozen since the Tertiary era. Don't confuse it with larimar, the blue stone also sold in the Dominican Republic: both are geological curiosities unique to the island.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In the afternoon, head to Playa Dorada, a few kilometers east of downtown. It's a wide golden-sand beach protected by a reef barrier, with calm waters near shore. Access is free, even though it's bordered by resort complexes. Plan at least an hour to enjoy the water before heading back.

⏱ 90 min

In the evening, stroll along Puerto Plata's Malecón on foot—this waterfront is where local families gather after nightfall, with food stalls, children's games, and sometimes live music. An atmosphere very different from the tourist zones.

⏱ 60 min

Day 4

Cabarete

Where Atlantic trade winds sculpt waves and fill the sky with kites year-round

★★★★★
📍 Plage de Cabarete📍 Kite Beach Cabarete📍 Playa Encuentro Cabarete📍 Parc national El Choco Cabarete📍 Laguna Gri-Gri Rio San Juan📍 Bozo Beach Cabarete
1

Cabarete was built on a trade wind: the one that blows across the bay from the Atlantic at 20 to 25 knots from December to August, making this coastal village one of the world's kitesurfing and windsurfing capitals. The bay is protected by a reef about 600 meters offshore, creating a relatively flat lagoon near the beach—reassuring for beginners—and short but consistent waves at the reef for advanced riders. Even if you don't kite, the spectacle of dozens of colorful kites filling the sky is worth the trip.

⏱ 60 min
2

A few minutes from the village, El Choco National Park offers a total contrast to the beach: limestone caves, inland lagoons, trails through dense jungle of cacao and mango trees. The solenodon, an endemic mammal among the rarest in the Caribbean, is protected here. You can visit on foot or by bike from Cabarete.

⏱ 90 min
3

Half an hour east of Cabarete, the Laguna Gri-Gri in Rio San Juan is a mangrove lagoon traversed by boat under vaults of vines and roots. Herons, iguanas, and land crabs roam freely. The excursion often ends at the virgin beach of Caletón, accessible only by water.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

At Playa Encuentro, a 5-minute drive west of Cabarete, the waves are better than on the main bay: several surf schools are set up on site and offer lessons for all levels. Watch out for sea urchins on the rocky bottom and currents during heavy swell—check conditions before entering the water.

⏱ 120 min

In the evening in Cabarete, the main beach transforms into a long line of beachfront bars and restaurants. The cuisine is surprisingly international for a village this size—Dominican seafood, pizzas, fusion cooking. Dine early to get a table with a sea view, then enjoy the musical atmosphere that often lasts late into the night.

Day 5

Jarabacoa

Where mountain rivers cascade through cloud forest and the air stays spring-cool all year

★★☆☆☆
📍 Salto de Jimenoa Jarabacoa📍 Salto Baiguate Jarabacoa📍 Rancho Baiguate Jarabacoa📍 Río Yaque del Norte Jarabacoa📍 Parque La Confluencia Jarabacoa📍 Reserva Científica Ébano Verde Jarabacoa
1

Jarabacoa, at 529 meters elevation in the Central Highlands, is nicknamed 'The City of Eternal Spring'—temperatures hover between 16 and 22°C year-round, a sharp contrast to the humid heat of the coast. Its name comes from Taíno and means 'place where water flows': four rivers converge here, including the Yaque del Norte, the longest river in the Caribbean at nearly 300 kilometers. Start by crossing through Parque La Confluencia, at the intersection of the Yaque del Norte and Jimenoa rivers, to feel the coolness of the mountain water.

⏱ 45 min
2

Salto de Jimenoa Uno is Jarabacoa's iconic waterfall: a roughly 75-meter drop into a basin enclosed by moss-covered cliffs, accessible via a trail and two suspension bridges from the road leading to the dam. The pool below is ideal for swimming, but don't get too close to the base of the falls because of the currents. The site's discovery dates back to the early 20th century, when a team of British engineers working on the construction of a hydroelectric plant on the Jimenoa River stumbled upon this cascade—the ruins of the old plant chimney are still visible on site. Wear closed-toe shoes for the trail.

⏱ 90 min
3

Salto Baiguate is a second waterfall of over 25 meters, different in atmosphere: more isolated, surrounded by cilantro fields and mountain forests, accessible by a farm road. An emerald natural pool forms below. Locals come here on weekends to swim—a sign that the spot is good.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Rafting on the Yaque del Norte is Jarabacoa's signature activity. The Class II to III rapids are accessible to beginners accompanied by a guide, and the setting—wooded gorges, limestone cliffs, royal palm forests—is exceptional. Local agencies organize 2 to 3-hour outings from downtown. Indicate your level to the agency before departing.

⏱ 180 min

Late afternoon, climb to the natural viewpoint of Monte El Mogote, accessible on horseback or on foot from the fincas around town. The view over the La Vega valley, coffee plantations, and the Central Highlands is worth the effort. You can also simply stroll through the flowering fields of Rancho Baiguate and buy locally roasted coffee on site.

⏱ 90 min

Day 6

Santo Domingo

Where the New World's first European streets still echo with five centuries of history

★★★★★
📍 Faro a Colón Santo Domingo📍 Acuario Nacional Santo Domingo📍 Jardín Botánico Nacional Santo Domingo📍 Aéroport Las Américas Santo Domingo
1

The drive back from Jarabacoa takes about 2.5 hours via the Autopista Duarte—a decent road that crosses the Cibao plains before reaching the capital. If you have time before your flight, detour to the Faro a Colón (Columbus Lighthouse), a cross-shaped monument erected in 1992 for the 500th anniversary of Columbus's arrival in America. It houses the navigator's official mausoleum and a museum. The structure is visible from afar and opinions are divided on its integration into the urban landscape—some Dominicans find it monumental, others excessive.

⏱ 60 min
2

If your flight is in the evening, the Jardín Botánico Nacional, with its 2 million square meters of classified tropical vegetation, is a peaceful place to spend your final hours. You can explore by small train or on foot among orchids, endemic palms, and bamboo groves. A good antidote to Santo Domingo's intense traffic before heading to the airport.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Before returning the car, fill up at a gas station before entering the capital—prices are lower outside Santo Domingo. Las Américas Airport (SDQ) is about 30 kilometers east of downtown, allow 45 minutes to 1 hour depending on traffic, more during rush hour.

⏱ 60 min
