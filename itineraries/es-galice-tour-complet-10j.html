Santiago De Compostela

★★★★☆
1

Santiago Cathedral rises at the end of the Praza do Obradoiro and takes your breath away even if you're a convinced atheist. The first stones date from 1075, under Alfonso VI's reign, built over the supposed tomb of the Apostle James discovered by the hermit Pelayo in 813. The current Baroque façade dates from 1750—Fernando de Casas Novoa placed it like a mask over the original Romanesque structure. Pilgrims who've walked the Camino still kiss the statue of St. James above the high altar and descend to the crypt to see the silver reliquary. Allow at least 1h30, more if you catch the noon mass with the giant botafumeiro.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Pórtico da Gloria, behind the western façade, is arguably Europe's finest Romanesque portal—sculpted by Master Mateo between 1168 and 1188, with over 200 polychrome figures restored in 2018 after a ten-year project. The original colouring, nearly erased by centuries of smoke and touch, has been partially revealed—greens, reds, golds. Guided visit compulsory, €18, 30-minute slots, limited to 25 people. Booking essential 2-3 days ahead, especially during pilgrimage season. The cathedral's absolute highlight—see this before anything else.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Cathedral Museum occupies the ground floor and the cloister at the rear of the cathedral. Three floors of medieval goldwork, Goya tapestries copied by Bayeu, and the star piece: the 1851 botafumeiro (53 kg, 1.6 m tall), the giant censer that swings across the transept crossing at 70 km/h on feast days, suspended 25 m high. Rope-and-pulley system operated by 8 tiraboleiros. Origin legend: to mask the stench of filthy medieval pilgrims. Still true today? More than ever. Allow 1h, €12 combined ticket.

⏱ 75 min
4

The Praza do Obradoiro, the vast cobbled square west of the cathedral, is the backdrop for the iconic photo of exhausted pilgrims collapsing on the ground after 800 km. Flanked by the Hostal dos Reis Católicos (1499, former pilgrims' hospital, now a 5-star parador), the Pazo de Raxoi (city hall) and the Colexio de San Xerome. Continuous action: musicians, gaiteros (bagpipers), tearful pilgrims, reunions of strangers who became friends. Not just a place—a stage. Best in late afternoon when the light gilds the granite stone.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The Mercado de Abastos, Santiago's second attraction after the cathedral, buzzes Tuesday to Saturday from 8am to 2pm. Neo-Romanesque building from 1937 with eight themed halls. Fish stands: live octopus in tanks, percebes (goose barnacles) at €80/kg, navajas (razor clams) piled high. Several market restaurants cook what you buy on the spot—€5 extra per item. Mariscadas Abastos 2.0 remains the reference, expect €30-40 per person. Booking advised Saturday lunch.

⏱ 90 min

For dinner, the rúa do Franco packs 50 metres of tapas bars—crowded, noisy, blackboard-based. Pulpo a feira (paprika and olive oil octopus) for €12, empanadas de zamburiñas (scallop pastries) at €3 per piece, white ribeiro in ceramic bowls at €2. Casa Manolo offers a pilgrim's menu at €12—hearty, unpretentious. The ritual: tour 3-4 bars, one dish per stop, finish with the queimada (flamed eau-de-vie) with a spirit-conjuring incantation—theatrical and effective.

⏱ 150 min

Santiago De Compostela

★★★★☆
1

The Mosteiro de San Martiño Pinario, just north of the cathedral, is Spain's second-largest monastery after El Escorial—20,000 m², 110 m-long façade. Benedictine since 912, rebuilt between 1590 and 1750 in restrained Baroque. The church altarpieces, signed by Casas Novoa and Romay, line up gilding, chubby angels and Solomonic columns. Today an active seminary and pilgrims' hostel. Entry €4, allow 1h. Church accessible separately from the main visit, free at certain times.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Museo do Pobo Galego, in the former Santo Domingo de Bonaval convent, tells 2,000 years of Galician culture—Celtic rock carvings, Galician fest-noz costumes, ancient gaitas (bagpipes), reconstructed peasant interiors. The architectural highlight: the triple spiral staircase, carved in granite by Domingo de Andrade in 1700—three helical ramps with no central pillar, a technical feat unique in Europe. Restored in 2019. Entry €3, allow 1h30. Perfect for rainy days.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Colexiata de Santa María a Real do Sar, 1 km south of the centre, is a 12th-century Romanesque church out of a nightmare—columns tilted 15° outward, as if the building's leaning backwards. Cause: foundations laid on loose ground sank from the 15th century, but the structure's held for 800 years with emergency flying buttresses added externally. Dizzying effect inside. Adjacent cloister with 12 sculpted capitals. Entry €2, almost never crowded. Strange and magnificent.

⏱ 45 min
4

The Cidade da Cultura de Galicia, perched on Monte Gaiás 4 km from the centre, is Spain's most controversial architectural UFO—Peter Eisenman's project launched in 1999, never completed. Six buildings with granite wave roofs reproducing the old town's topography, budget ballooning from 100 million to over a billion euros. Construction abandoned in 2013 after partial inauguration. Library, archives and museum function. Unexpected urban stroll, sunset view over Santiago. Bus C11 from centre, €1.20.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Parque da Alameda, just west of the centre, is Santiago's green lung—90,000 m² of century-old oaks, eucalyptus, magnolias, and viewpoints offering the postcard view of the cathedral at sunset. The statue of the 'Dúas Marías' (two eccentric sisters from Franco's dictatorship who became local icons) attracts selfies. Flat 30-minute loop walk, or an easy 1h with a café stop at the historic kiosk. Free concerts Tuesday at 7pm in July-August.

⏱ 60 min

If you're there between 24 June and 25 July, the Apostle's feast is in full swing—fireworks against the cathedral façade on 24 July at midnight ('Os Fogos do Apóstolo'), solemn procession on the 25th with incense, bagpipes and the king's presence every 7 years (Holy Year Compostelana). Outside the festival, the queimada at La Bodeguilla de San Roque is worth the trip: flamed grape eau-de-vie with lemon, coffee and sugar, accompanied by a 2-minute ritual incantation against witches. Touristy but genuine.

⏱ 90 min

A Coruna

★★★★☆
1

The Tower of Hercules, planted on a cliff north of La Coruña, is the only Roman lighthouse still in service in the world—built under Trajan around 110 AD, it's guided sailors to the port of Brigantium for 1,900 years without interruption. Current height 55 m (the Roman core is 34 m, the rest added in 1791). UNESCO-listed in 2009. Climb 234 steps for panoramic bay views. The surrounding park, 47 ha, houses contemporary sculptures and Phoenician tombs from the 4th century BC. Entry €3, closed in high winds (rare but possible).

⏱ 90 min
2

The Praza de María Pita, La Coruña's main square, celebrates the local heroine who killed an English standard-bearer in 1589 during Drake's siege—a gesture that galvanised the defence and drove off the English. Statue in her likeness at the centre. The 10,000 m² square is bordered by the neo-Baroque municipal palace (1908) and three rows of arcaded buildings. Antique book market on Saturdays, packed terraces in the evening. Allow 30 minutes. Ideal tapas at bars around the perimeter, around €3 per piece.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Castelo de Santo Antón, a 16th-century fort planted on a harbour islet (connected by causeway), houses the provincial archaeological museum. Celtiberian collections from Galician castros, pre-Roman gold metalwork, and the Leiro helmet (6th century BC)—a unique piece in Europe. The castle itself served as a political prison under Franco—visiting the cells is chilling. Bay views from the ramparts. Entry €2, closed Mondays. Allow 1h.

⏱ 75 min
4

The Pescadería quarter, between the Praza de María Pita and the port, retains La Coruña's authentic maritime character—white gallery façades with glazed windows ('miradoiros'), a 19th-century local invention to enjoy the sun without the Atlantic wind. The Paseo Marítimo, 13 km along the seafront from the port to the Tower of Hercules, is Europe's longest. Tourist tram runs along it in summer, €4 return. Sunset walk sublime from Riazor beach.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

At noon, the Plaza de Lugo market (calle Plaza de Lugo, 600 m from María Pita) cooks the day's catch upstairs. Stand A Pulpeira de Pasados serves Galician-style octopus (cooked in a copper cauldron, sweet paprika, olive oil and salt) at €18 per portion—the city's truest version. Order an Albariño white in a Galician bowl (€2.50). Otherwise, in the bars of calle Estrella: Adega O Bebedeiro for empanadas, La Bombilla for grilled sardines. Crowded, functional, good.

⏱ 90 min

Riazor beach, right in the city centre, is one of Europe's rare urban spots where you can surf 100 m from a tapas bar. 600 m of fine sand, medium waves in autumn (up to 2 m). Prado Surf school rents board and wetsuit for €30/day, group lesson €35 for 2h. Water at 17°C in summer, 13°C in winter—thick wetsuit essential. Ideal for beginners, summer lifeguards. Changing rooms €1.

⏱ 180 min

Fisterra

★★★☆☆
1

Cape Finisterre—Fisterra in Galician—is the Romans' end of the world, who called the place Finis Terrae ('land's end'). Granite cliff rising 138 m into the Atlantic, constant winds, the feeling of standing at the edge of a cosmic precipice. The 1853 lighthouse marks the unofficial finishing point of the Camino—many pilgrims continue from Santiago on an extra 90 km to reach it. Controversial tradition of burning your socks at sunset on the rocks: a gesture of rupture, journey's end. Now forbidden but still practised in secret.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Pedras Santas, 200 m below the lighthouse, are rounded granite rocks, a Celtic-Roman solar cult site predating Christianity—hence the nickname Costa da Morte (coast of death). Pilgrims came here for purification before Christianity, a ritual later co-opted by the Church. The main flat rock still serves for end-of-Camino ceremonies. Feasts, meditation, sometimes symbolic weddings—tolerated as long as nature isn't degraded. Strange atmosphere, more pagan than Christian.

⏱ 30 min
3

The igrexa de Santa María das Areas, in Fisterra village 3 km from the cape, is the official spiritual stop for pilgrims—a 12th-century Romanesque sanctuary housing the Cristo de la Barba Dourada, a medieval Christ with a gold-painted beard that supposedly floated ashore according to legend. Church open only during mass times, usually noon and 7pm. Small Atlantic cemetery attached—gravestones facing the ocean, some very recent. Allow 30 minutes.

⏱ 45 min
4

The Costa da Morte, stretching 200 km from Malpica to Fisterra, owes its ominous name to over 600 recorded shipwrecks since antiquity. Winds, fog, invisible cliffs; HMS Serpent in 1890 lost 172 English sailors at Camariñas. Cemiterio dos Ingleses (Cemetery of the English) 30 km north of Fisterra, moving 1h walk. Mar de Fora beach, 2 km from Fisterra, inadvisable for swimming (killer currents) but sublime on foot. Perfect at dusk.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

For dinner, Fisterra's port serves what the trawlers brought in that afternoon. O Centolo (calle del Puerto) has been the institution since 1957—percebes (goose barnacles) at €60 per 200g portion, whole spider crab (centola) at €30/kg, white wine clams at €18. Booking essential at weekends. For limited budgets, A Marina on the port serves a fisherman's menu at €18 with the day's fish dish. Bottle of Pazo de Señorans Albariño at €22.

⏱ 120 min

For a morning off the beaten track, the Lighthouse Way (Camiño dos Faros)—200 km route from Malpica to Fisterra along the coast—offers an easy stage between Fisterra and O Pindo (12 km, 4h, green-marked). Cliffs, deserted beaches, lost hermitages, sacred Mount Pindo ('Celtic Olympus'). Taxi return €25 from Fisterra. Perfect in the morning before the wind picks up. Walking poles useful, water and windbreaker essential.

⏱ 240 min

★★☆☆☆
1

Cambados presents itself as the capital of Albariño—6,000 inhabitants, port backed onto the Arousa ría, vineyards all around. The Praza de Fefiñáns, the main square, is one of Galicia's finest: 5,000 m² of cobbles, framed by the 17th-century Pazo de Fefiñáns (still private property, tasting possible), the 18th-century Church of Saint Benedict and the northern arcades. Sunday morning market, music festivals in July. Allow 45 minutes for the square and adjacent lanes. Noble and maritime atmosphere, without tourist hordes.

⏱ 60 min
2

The ruínas de Santa Mariña Dozo, 800 m from the centre, are one of Galicia's most mysterious images: a 15th-century Gothic church, abandoned in 1840, whose walls and round arches still stand in the middle of a still-active cemetery. Collapsed roof, sky visible through the ogives, ivy on the stones. Century-old cypresses, fishermen's graves with repeated names. Free access, photogenic at dusk. Allow 30 minutes. Avoid visits during funerals (often Friday-Saturday mornings).

⏱ 45 min
3

The mosteiro de Armenteira, 12 km east of Cambados, is a Cistercian monastery founded in 1149 by Don Ero—a knight turned monk according to legend, who supposedly slept for 300 years in the forest listening to a bird sing. The 12th-century Romanesque church, 17th-century conventual buildings and a marked meditative path in the surrounding woods ('Ruta da Pedra e da Auga', 8 km, 3h, gentle slopes) merit the stop. Monks still present, simple welcome. Free entry, donations appreciated.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Albariño DO Rías Baixas is one of Europe's great whites—grape planted here for 800 years (origin legend: Cistercian monks brought the vine from the Rhine), harvested late August, aged on lees. 4,800 hectares, 180 wineries. Visit + tasting at Martín Códax (best known, easy parking, €12), Pazo de Señoráns (superior quality, €20) or Bodegas Granbazán (17th-century noble architecture, €15). Booking advised. Combining with lunch on-site is the perfect option.

⏱ 150 min

If you catch the first Sunday in August, the Festa do Albariño is in full swing—100,000 visitors over 5 days, tastings at €1 per glass in the streets, gaita concerts, seafood cooked in the open air. Off-season, Cambados keeps the maritime vibe—port 2 km from the centre, mussel farms visible from the jetty (boatmen offer tours in traditional dorna boats at €20 for 1h). The marisqueada (6-seafood platter) at Yayo Daporta (1 Michelin star, €75) or A Esmorga (€35) remains the local reference.

⏱ 120 min

Pontevedra

★★★★☆
1

Pontevedra is Europe's pioneering car-free city—since 1999, Mayor Lores has pedestrianised the entire historic centre (300,000 m²), reduced road fatalities to zero, and made the provincial capital a textbook case. Direct consequence: strolling possible everywhere, terraces open late, children playing in the streets. The granite old town (commercial centre), entirely pedestrian, merits the detour alone. Compact centre, allow 1h for the tour of squares. Perfect between 10am and 1pm.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Basílica de Santa María a Maior, perched on Pontevedra's highest hill, is one of the finest examples of Galician Gothic-Renaissance—built between 1480 and 1559 by the sailors' guild ('mareantes'). Western façade carved like a stone altarpiece, signed by Cornelius of Holland, with maritime and apostolic scenes. Inside, polychrome altarpiece from 1610 and private chapels of great families. Free entry, closed 1pm-4pm. Allow 30 minutes.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Capela da Peregrina, right in the centre on the square of the same name, is unique for its scallop shell-shaped plan—symbol of the Portuguese Camino that passes through Pontevedra. Built in 1778 by Antonio Souto, curved Baroque façade, two symmetrical bell towers. Small circular interior with vault. Portuguese Camino pilgrims make an obligatory stop here. Free entry, irregular opening. See mainly for the exterior, the most surprising part. Allow 15 minutes.

⏱ 25 min
4

The Museo de Pontevedra, spread across six buildings in the historic centre, is one of Spain's oldest provincial museums—founded in 1927. Celtiberian goldwork collections (the Caldas treasure, 2nd century BC), paintings by Castelao (key 20th-century Galician artist-writer), reconstructions of 17th-century noble interiors. The Sexto Edificio, modern building opened in 2013, exhibits contemporary Galician art. Free entry. Allow 1h30 for the main buildings.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

At noon, the Praza da Leña, granite-paved, lined with tapas bars—Pontevedra's most local vibe. Casa Fidel serves bacalao à galega (cod, potatoes, caramelised onions) at €14 since 1900. Ribadelle for warm meat empanadas, €2.80 per piece. Bar Mercado for Galician-style octopus. Expect €12-18 per person with a glass of wine. Avoid 1:30-2pm Saturday, queue forms. Perfect buzz for watching the car-free city in action.

⏱ 90 min

Pontevedra is the last major stop on the Portuguese Camino before Santiago (60 km further north). If you're tempted, the half-day walk to Caldas de Reis (20 km, 5h, yellow-marked) follows the historic route—medieval stones, Roman bridges, rural hamlets. Bus return at €4, departures every hour. Otherwise, simply stroll along the Lérez leaving the centre towards the Illas dos Loureiros (river park)—4 km flat, shaded, pushchair-accessible.

⏱ 90 min

Vigo

★★★☆☆
1

Vigo, Galicia's largest city (300,000 inhabitants), isn't the most beautiful at first glance—industrial port, tower blocks, traffic. But the Casco Vello (old quarter) sloping down to the port, with its cobbled lanes and oyster bars, retains a rough charm. Rúa Pescadería, called 'calle de las Ostras', is where women ('ostreiras') have sold oysters directly from the fish market since 1860—€12 a dozen, opened in front of you, no fuss. Allow 1h for the quarter and tasting.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Castelo do Castro, on the hill of the same name at 147 m, dominates the entire Vigo bay. 17th-century fortress built on remains of a Celtic castro (Iron Age fortified village). Ramparts, moats, ruins of Civil War bunkers. The surrounding park offers the most complete panoramic view of the ría: bridges, islands, mussel farms in parallel lines. Free access, free parking. Perfect at sunset. Allow 1h.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Museo do Mar de Galicia, 3 km from the centre in a former Alcabre sardine cannery, tells 2,000 years of Galician maritime history—13th-19th century whaling (Vigo was a major whaling port), evolution of sardine canneries, Galician emigration to the Americas (over a million departures between 1850 and 1960). Basement aquarium with ría species. Allow 1h30, €3. Perfect for rainy days.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Vigo's key attraction is the Illas Cíes—an archipelago of three islands 14 km offshore, designated national park since 2002. Rodas beach (1.5 km of white sand, turquoise water) voted the world's most beautiful beach by the Guardian in 2007. Access only by boat (Mar de Ons, departure from Estación Marítima de Vigo, €19 return, 45 minutes). Quota of 1,800 visitors per day in summer—authorisation mandatory, request 5-7 days ahead at autorizacionsillasatlanticas.xunta.gal. Marked trails on the island, western lighthouse with Atlantic views. Full day.

⏱ 360 min

If Cíes is full or off-season, the gastronomic tour of O Berbés (old port, under the metal bridge) remains the best evening in Vigo. Tabernas Otilio (rúa Carral) for zorza (paprika-marinated pork) at €8, Bar Eligio (rúa Cesteiros) for oysters at €1.50 each and red ribeiro in bowls. Expect €20-25 per person with 4-5 bars. Avoid the tourist chains on rúa Velazquez Moreno. Rúa Real, parallel, is more authentic.

⏱ 150 min

Ourense

★★★☆☆
1

Ourense is Galicia's thermal capital—and probably Spain's most underrated city. As Burgas, hot springs right in the centre, have gushed at 67°C since Roman times (mentioned by Pliny the Elder in the 1st century). Three freely accessible fountains in the heart of the city, steam visible in winter. Free developed baths at the rear ('piscinas das Burgas')—water arrives at 41°C. No proper changing rooms, family atmosphere, totally free. Unusual and moving.

⏱ 60 min
2

Ourense Cathedral (Igrexa Catedral de San Martiño) dates from the 12th-13th centuries—late Catalan Gothic on Romanesque foundations, western façade carved copying Santiago's Pórtico da Gloria (Mateo trained the Ourense workshop around 1180). Inside, the Cristo de la Expiración, 14th-century polychrome sculpture with real beard and hair—venerated by locals, restored in 2019 (the hair was from a 12th-century woman, carbon-dated). Allow 1h, entry €5.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Ponte Romana (Ponte Vella), 370 m long, has spanned the Miño since AD 75 (Vespasian's order)—Roman foundations still in place, central medieval arch (1230) rebuilt by Bishop Pedro Yáñez after flooding. Seven arches, pronounced hump, photos from the south bank. The bridge has been pedestrian since 1989. At sunset, the Miño turns golden and the bridge lights up. Allow 30 minutes for the return walk.

⏱ 45 min
4

Ourense's Praza Maior, the entirely pedestrian main square, is a large cobbled square bordered by 17th-18th century arcaded buildings. The Museo Arqueolóxico Provincial in the Pazo do Bispo (12th-century episcopal palace transformed) reopens in 2026 after 18 years of work—collections of Celtiberian and Roman goldwork. Café Vino Tinto under the arcades has been the local institution since 1925, tinto de verano at €3. Continuous action, the city's beating heart.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The Termas de Outariz, 5 km west of the centre along the Miño, are Galicia's best-known free outdoor thermal baths—9 tiered pools along the river, water at 41°C, Japanese onsen vibe. Open 24/7 except Tuesday (cleaning). Basic changing rooms, bring swimsuit and towel. Combine with the 'Ruta Termal': free electric shuttle along 16 km of riverbank serving 7 different thermal sites. Avoid weekends after 6pm, packed.

⏱ 120 min

For lunch, rúa Viriato (old town) clusters the best tapas bars. Tapería A Taberna serves calamari bocadillos at €4.50, Restaurante Sanmiguel for cocido galego (thick hotpot) at €16 in winter. Ourense is also in DOP Ribeiro zone—one of Spain's oldest vineyards (mentioned since the 13th century), dry mineral white treixadura wine, €2.50 a glass in all bars. Expect €18-22 for a full meal.

⏱ 90 min

Monforte De Lemos

★★☆☆☆
1

Monforte de Lemos, capital of Ribeira Sacra, keeps inland Galicia's most impressive castle—a donjon-tower called 'Torre da Homenaxe' from the 13th century, 30 m high, among Spain's tallest. Built by the Counts of Lemos, a dynasty that married Pedro Fernández de Castro to a daughter of Charles V in 1574. Today a 4-star parador, 360° views from the top over the Cabe valley. Courtyard open to the public during the day. Allow 1h.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Colexio do Cardeal, a 17th-18th century Jesuit school south of the castle, houses one of Galicia's most precious religious art collections—works by El Greco ('St Francis in Meditation', two versions), Andrea del Sarto (school), and a polychrome altarpiece by Francisco de Moure (1620). Historic library of 7,000 volumes, some from the 16th century. Building itself: 80 m-long golden stone façade. Entry €4, allow 1h. Often empty, quasi-private experience.

⏱ 75 min
3

The mosteiro de Santo Estevo de Ribas de Sil, 30 km northwest of Monforte, perched 700 m above the Sil canyon, is one of Ribeira Sacra's three great Benedictine monasteries (with Oseira and Samos). Founded in the 9th century, enlarged 10th-18th centuries, transformed into a parador in 2003. Three superimposed cloisters (Romanesque, Gothic, Renaissance), plunging views over the canyon. Visit entry: €4 if not a guest. Coffee at the parador even without staying—the terrace is worth every penny.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Sil canyon, natural border between Lugo and Ourense, is Galicia's other landscape—500 m cliffs plunging into the river, practically vertical terraced vineyards, cultivated by hand since Roman times. AOC Ribeira Sacra, 1,200 hectares, red mencía and white godello, so-called 'heroic' viticulture (average slope 60%). The catamaran ride from Doade or Belesar port (1h30, €14) crosses the heart of the canyon—French narration available, departures 11am, 1pm and 4pm depending on season.

⏱ 120 min

The mirador dos Balcóns de Madrid, at Parada de Sil (45 min from Monforte), is Ribeira Sacra's most striking view—a metal walkway cantilevered 500 m above the canyon, dizzying void beneath your feet. Free access, parking 200 m away. Combine with the Ruta dos Cavorcos (8 km, 3h, marked), a high-altitude trail along the canyon. Exceptional sunset, but frequent autumn fog—check the weather. Walking poles useful, no refreshments.

⏱ 180 min

Lugo

★★★☆☆
1

Lugo's Roman Wall is the only Roman rampart in the world preserved along its entire original length—2,117 m circuit, 85 round towers, 4 original gates plus 6 opened in the 20th century, height 10-15 m. Built around AD 270 under Aurelian to defend Lucus Augusti against the Suebi. Local schist stone. UNESCO-listed in 2000. Complete walk along the top walkway (4-7 m wide), 45 minutes at a gentle pace. Free 24/7 access via 10 stairways. Given the monument's uniqueness, absolutely unmissable.

⏱ 60 min
2

Santa María de Lugo Cathedral, inside the wall, mixes four periods: 12th-century Romanesque nave (begun 1129), 14th-century Gothic chapels, 19th-century neoclassical façade, two asymmetrical bell towers. Peculiarity: it holds the privilege of perpetual exposition of the Blessed Sacrament since the Council of Toledo in 569—one of only two in the world (with St Peter's in Rome). Allow 1h. Entry €6, combined ticket with diocesan museum. Remarkable acoustics.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Domus do Mitreo, 200 m southwest of the wall, is a 2nd-3rd century Roman house discovered under Lugo University in 2002—entirely underground, accessible by glass staircase. Mosaics in place, hypocaust (underfloor heating), mural painting workshop under study. The Mithraeum room (sanctuary to Mithras, Eastern god) is unique in northwest Hispania. Guided visit compulsory, €3, limited slots. Allow 45 minutes. For archaeology enthusiasts.

⏱ 60 min
4

The Museo Provincial de Lugo, in the former 13th-century San Francisco convent, preserves the finest pieces from Celtic-Roman archaeology of the northwest—pre-Roman gold metalwork, coins, mosaics, and the Galician ethnographic collection (ceramics, textiles, farming tools). Preserved Gothic cloister. Small garden at the rear. Free entry. Allow 1h30. Perfect for classic Galician rainy days.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

For your final lunch, rúa Nova and rúa da Cruz in the old town (intramuros) cluster the best tapas bars—Lugo has the peculiarity of offering a free tapa with each drink, a tradition still strictly observed (unwritten local law). Bar Verruga (calle da Cruz) serves Galician-style octopus free with a ribeiro at €3. Mesón A Taberna for lacón con grelos (ham hock, turnip greens, potatoes) at €12. Expect €15-20 per person for a full meal. Perfect, economical, genuine.

⏱ 120 min

To finish the trip, the evening walk on the ramparts at sunset. Raking floodlights on the round towers, views over old town roofs inside, countryside outside. The Praza Maior, inside the ramparts, hosts free concerts in July-August on Thursday and Saturday evenings. To conclude: caña galega (grape eau-de-vie) at €3 in any bar in the centre. By the time you leave, you'll understand why Galicians say 'Galicia, calidade de vida'.
