✨ Highlights
  • Portobelo: UNESCO-listed forts since 1980, scene of raids by Francis Drake (died here in 1596) and Henry Morgan
  • Emberá Chagres: dugout canoe on the river that feeds the Panama Canal, musical welcome and vegetable ivory handicrafts
  • El Caño: pre-Columbian necropolis 8th-11th century, ongoing excavations by El Caño Foundation - National Geographic calls it one of the most important discoveries in the Americas
  • Las Perlas: over 200 Pacific islands, humpback whales July-September, exploited for their pearls until 1530
📆 Best period

January, February, March, April

⏳ How many days

To make the most of it, plan between 3 and 7 days.

☀️ Weather

Dry Season (December-April): Peak tourist season with minimal rainfall, comfortable temperatures (24-32°C), and excellent conditions for outdoor activities and beach visits. Highest prices and crowds.

Shoulder Season (May, November): Good weather with brief afternoon showers, moderate prices, and fewer tourists. Excellent value while maintaining pleasant conditions for most activities.

Green Season (May-November): Higher rainfall with lush landscapes, significantly lower prices, and fewer crowds. Morning activities work well before afternoon rains arrive.

Regional Variations: Caribbean coast wetter year-round, Pacific coast has distinct dry/wet seasons. Mountain areas cooler with different precipitation patterns than lowlands.

🌍 Discover

Central American bridge with canal engineering, biodiversity, and cultural diversity. Experience traditional cultures, incredible nature, and historical significance. The country offers cultural immersion with natural beauty and engineering marvels. Panama provides diverse experiences with natural wonders and cultural authenticity.

📋 Notes

POSSIBLE OPTIMIZATIONS: (1) Day 5 can be shortened by skipping the overnight in Panama City - do Portobelo → Emberá (Chagres Park, on the way) → Chitré directly (~5h total drive, long day but doable with a 6:30am departure). This saves one transit day. (2) El Caño and Natá (currently D6) can be visited in the morning departing from Chitré southbound, before Las Tablas and Pedasí - they're on the route. (3) The Las Perlas ferry (D11-D12) doesn't run daily in low season: verify operating days before fixing your dates. If the ferry is unavailable, the excursion can be replaced with Taboga Island (1h ferry, more frequent departures). (4) An SUV is recommended for Santa Fé (mountains, dirt roads) and some beach access points in Azuero. (5) If you pass through Penonomé (between Panama City and Chitré), it's the best place to buy a sombrero pintao, the traditional UNESCO-listed woven hat.

⚠️ SAFETY NOTE: Panama is generally safe in tourist areas, though standard precautions apply regarding petty crime in cities. Avoid displaying valuables, use official transportation, and stay in well-established tourist zones. Some remote border areas with Colombia should be avoided due to security concerns.

Panama City

Where Gehry's bold architecture meets the engineering marvel of the canal

★★★★☆
1

On the Amador Causeway, right at the Pacific entrance to the canal, prepare to be surprised by the architecture of the Biomuseo - a building designed by Frank Gehry, his only work in Latin America, inaugurated in October 2014 after 15 years of construction and $100 million. The undulating multicolored roofs tell a simple but staggering story: it was the Isthmus of Panama that altered the biodiversity of the entire planet. Its eight permanent galleries trace how the closure of the isthmus a few million years ago split one ocean into two, created the Great American Biotic Interchange, and possibly even influenced human evolution by drying out the forests of East Africa. Allow at least 2 hours to explore it thoroughly.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Amador Causeway was built with rocks excavated during the canal's construction - an engineering gesture recycled into a promenade. It connects the city to three small islands over 6 km and offers the best possible views of the canal entrance and Panama City's skyline. Late in the day, take a bike ride: rental shops are set up at the base of the causeway, and the low-angle light on container ships gliding toward the Miraflores locks is worth the trip alone.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Interoceanic Canal Museum in Casco Viejo traces the entire history of the waterway from the first Spanish projects of the 16th century through to the handover of the canal to Panama on December 31, 1999. Take your time - the scale models of the locks and photographic archives from the American construction period (1904-1914) are remarkable. Plan for 1.5 hours.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Bike ride on the Amador Causeway in late afternoon. Rental shops offer bikes on site. Allow 1 hour round-trip for the 6 km causeway with views of the canal.

Panama City

Where Gehry's bold architecture meets the engineering marvel of the canal

★★★★☆
1

The Gatún Locks, on the Atlantic side of the canal, are less visited than those at Miraflores but far more spectacular: here you watch ships traverse three chambers in succession to climb 26 meters above sea level and reach Lake Gatún. The lake itself covers 425 km² - when it opened in 1913, it was the world's largest artificial lake. You can observe ships passing from the viewing platform at the Agua Clara visitor center, a recent and well-designed facility. Arrive early in the morning to have the ships in full light.

⏱ 90 min
2

12 km from the locks, on a rocky promontory at the mouth of the Río Chagres, the ruins of Fort San Lorenzo have been watching the horizon since 1590. The Spanish built it from carved coral - as hard as granite, they say - to protect access to the Chagres River, the transit route for Peruvian gold. Francis Drake attacked it in 1596, Henry Morgan took it in 1671 before sailing upriver with 1,200 men to sack Panama City. Listed as a UNESCO site since 1980, the fort is now overgrown with jungle, cannons lie in rows among the grass. Don't miss the view of the river mouth from the ramparts.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Boat tour on Lake Gatún: operators offer excursions on the lake from the locks area. You'll encounter howler monkeys and capuchins in the trees overhanging the water, crocodiles sunning on the banks, and possibly toucans. The lake flooded an entire valley when it was filled between 1907 and 1913 - church steeples still emerge in places at low water.

Portobelo

Spanish ramparts echoing with tales of Drake, Morgan and Caribbean corsairs

★★★★☆
1

Portobelo was founded on March 20, 1597 - the Spanish saw it as the ideal port for transferring gold from the Americas to Spain. For more than two centuries, galleons waited here for their cargo: Peruvian gold, Bolivian silver, tobacco, cocoa. The well-protected bay was the heart of the empire's transatlantic trade. You get a sense of this seeing the forts San Jerónimo, San Fernando and Santiago de la Gloria that still encircle it - listed as UNESCO sites in 1980 as exceptional examples of 17th and 18th-century military architecture. Walk along the walls: cannons lie in the grass on their rusted carriages, the jungle is reclaiming its territory. Allow 1.5 hours to tour the forts.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Real Aduana - the royal customs house - is the two-story building with tall columns that dominates the village. This is where gold and pearls from Central and South America were stored, counted and weighed before being shipped to Spain. Destroyed by English cannons in 1744, then by an earthquake in 1882, it was restored in the 1990s. Its large halls now host temporary exhibitions. Note that during restoration, the Spanish used coral from the nearby reef for the walls - which caused near-total destruction of the reef.

⏱ 45 min
3

In the San Felipe church, look for the Cristo Negro - a dark wooden statue, dressed in a purple velvet robe, object of intense devotion. Every October 21, thousands of pilgrims converge on Portobelo for the Black Christ festival, some on their knees for the final kilometers. The statue's origin remains mysterious. If you're not there in October, you can still see the Cristo Negro in its chapel and understand why this figure has become the symbol of Afro-Panamanian religious syncretism.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Walk through the village to discover Congo culture - descendants of African slaves who built the forts and inhabited Portobelo have preserved their dances and music of origin, listed as UNESCO intangible heritage. Strolling around, you might catch rehearsals or performances in the village, especially on weekends.

⏱ 60 min

A short boat crossing from the coast north of Portobelo, Isla Grande and Isla Mamey live to the rhythm of fishing and coconut palms. No cars, a few families, a relaxed Caribbean vibe. 2

The water is cool, the natural pool below is perfect for swimming - bring good shoes, the path gets muddy in rainy season.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Diving or snorkeling in Portobelo Bay: the waters shelter several coral reefs and galleon wrecks. The dive sites are less crowded than classic tourist spots. Local operators offer snorkeling trips from the beach.

Boat excursion in the mangroves around Portobelo Bay. The vegetation is dense, egrets and herons abound, and if you're lucky, you'll spot manatees in the calm waters. Allow 1.5 hours.

Panama City

Where Gehry's bold architecture meets the engineering marvel of the canal

★★★★☆
1

Chagres Park is on the return route to Panama City - a detour of a few kilometers from the Pan-American Highway. Once at the port, you board a motorized dugout canoe to go upriver. The Chagres River is also the water source for Lake Gatún and thus for the canal - the Emberá have been its guardians for decades. Several families have been settled on the riverbanks since the 1950s, coming from the Darién. Allow 30 minutes of navigation to reach the village.

⏱ 60 min
2

In the Emberá village - stilted huts, palm-leaf roofs - you're welcomed with music by women in brightly colored skirts and children. The program: traditional dances and music with flutes and drums, presentation of medicinal plants in the forest, handicrafts (baskets woven from chunga fiber, sculptures in cocobolo wood and vegetable ivory called tagua). A traditional meal of grilled fish and plantains is included in most packages. Book in advance - the excursion requires at least two people and an early morning departure.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

Swimming in the Río Chagres at the waterfall that Emberá guides visit along the way. The water is cool and clear. Bring your swimsuit under your clothes - there are no changing rooms on site.

Chitre

Gateway to Azuero's living folklore and pre-Columbian mysteries

★★★★☆
1

On the route to Chitré, stop at Natá de los Caballeros, founded May 20, 1522 by Pedrarias Dávila - the oldest continuously inhabited town on the Pacific coast of the Americas. Its Santiago Apóstol church is also the oldest still-active church on the entire continental Pacific side. Walking in the small central plaza, your feet touch the same cobblestones as the conquistadors did five hundred years ago. When Gonzalo de Badajoz arrived here in 1515, he described an indigenous village so populated that it frightened him.

⏱ 45 min
2

10 km from Natá, the El Caño archaeological site is a pre-Columbian necropolis discovered in 1925 and excavated by the El Caño Foundation since the 2010s. Archaeologists have unearthed tombs of chiefs from the 8th to 11th century AD, containing gold ornaments, painted ceramics and objects of such sophistication that National Geographic ranked these discoveries among the most important in the Americas. The museum renovated in 2019 presents the objects and funeral rituals of this still poorly known civilization. Free admission.

⏱ 75 min
3

In Chitré, capital of Herrera province, the regional museum presents pre-Columbian ceramics and objects from the central region, as well as pieces from Azuero folklore - polleras, diablicos masks, traditional musical instruments. Allow 45 minutes.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The village of La Arena, 4 km from Chitré, has been renowned for centuries for its red clay pottery. Workshops open to the public allow you to see artisans working by hand or on the wheel. You'll find pieces to buy directly from the potters, much cheaper than in Panama City shops.

⏱ 60 min

Pedasi

Where Pacific waves meet pristine reefs and turtle-nesting beaches

★★★★☆
1

Las Tablas is the capital of Panamanian carnival - every year in February or March, the town divides into two rival camps, Calle Arriba and Calle Abajo, who compete with floats, brass bands and crowned queens since 1906. Off-season, the town keeps traces of this passion: hand-embroidered polleras (each dress represents several months of work) are displayed in shop windows at the artisans', and the main street is lined with carnival photos. UNESCO inscribed the pollera on the intangible heritage list in 2022.

⏱ 60 min
2

In Los Santos, the Museum of Nationality occupies an 18th-century colonial house. This is where Panama's first declaration of independence from Spain was signed in 1821 - a local act that preceded Panama City's decision by a few days. The colonial church San Atanasio across the street is also worth a look.

⏱ 45 min
3

Pedasí is a fishing village with colorful colonial houses - clean streets, ice cream kiosks, horses tied up in front of the church. It's the hometown of Mireya Moscoso, Panama's first female president (1999-2004), who financed parks and infrastructure there. It's also the ideal base for excursions in the area. Take time to wander before dinner.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Playa Venao, 34 km southwest of Pedasí, is one of Panama's best surf beaches: waves break in both directions and suit all levels. Surf schools, beachfront hostels and a bohemian vibe have set up shop there. Even if you don't surf, the beach is beautiful and uncrowded on weekdays.

Pedasi

Where Pacific waves meet pristine reefs and turtle-nesting beaches

★★★★☆
1

Isla Iguana is a 53-hectare nature reserve declared in 1981, a 20-minute boat ride from Playa El Arenal. The island shelters the largest coral reef in the Gulf of Panama, hundreds of free-roaming iguanas, pelicans and frigatebirds. During World War II, it served as a target for American training aircraft - you can still find shell fragments in the rocks. Vegetation has covered everything since. Bring your picnic: there are no services on the island. Between July and October, humpback whales pass offshore.

⏱ 180 min
2

Sarigua National Park, an hour's drive north of Pedasí, always surprises: it's a nearly desert-like landscape in a Panama you imagine as entirely tropical. Deforestation since colonial times has transformed this area into semi-desert. But it's also the country's oldest pre-Columbian site - human occupation dating back 11,000 years has been documented here. Stunning views of a landscape that runs counter to everything you'll have seen until now.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Snorkeling at Isla Iguana on the coral reef. The water is clear and shallow on the north side of the island, perfect for beginners. Bring your equipment from Pedasí - rental shops offer it in town. Access may be closed in bad weather between January and March.

⏱ 120 min

If you're traveling between July and November, Isla Cañas (1.5 hours drive south) is one of the main Pacific Panamanian sea turtle nesting sites - five species nest there. Community guides organize nighttime tours with red lights to avoid disturbing the turtles. Book the day before.

Santa Fe

Cloud forest refuge where quetzals glide and waterfalls cascade

★★★★☆
1

The Pedasí-Santa Fé route takes 5 hours via Santiago - a crossing of the Pan-American Highway then a climb into the hills of Veraguas up to 500 meters altitude. Santa Fé was founded in 1557 by Captain Francisco Vásquez to support the coastal gold mines. Today it's a peaceful end-of-the-world: a few hundred inhabitants, coffee fincas in the hills, the Río Santa María - the country's third-longest river - flowing through the village. After Santiago, the road is good until arrival. Take your time in Santiago for fuel and groceries.

⏱ 60 min
2

Once settled, first stroll through Santa Fé village. Surrounding farms cultivate coffee, sugarcane, and more than 1,200 species of orchids grow in the region - including the Espíritu Santo, Panama's national flower. Local nurseries sell plants. Ask residents or local guides about tomorrow's trails.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Evening walk along the Río Santa María at the edge of the village. The water is crystal clear and natural pools are ideal for cooling off after the long drive.

Cloud forest refuge where quetzals glide and waterfalls cascade

★★★★☆
1

Santa Fé National Park protects 72,636 hectares of rainforest on the Central Cordillera since 2001. Leave early with a local guide - trails are poorly marked, tracks in the mud are your best compass. The trail to the Alto Piedra waterfalls (easy, ~4 km round-trip) crosses humid tropical forest and opens onto natural pools where swimming is essential. If you want more challenge, the trail to Las Golondrinas waterfalls (further and less frequented) is considered by many as the most beautiful in the region.

⏱ 240 min
2

In the hills around Santa Fé, families from the local coffee cooperative welcome visitors to their fincas. You'll see the transformation of coffee from cherry to cup, with a tasting. It's one of Panama's most unknown high-altitude coffees, and also one of the best.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Bird watching in cloud forest: Santa Fé park is an ornithological paradise with over 100 species recorded, including quetzals, toucans, hummingbirds and harpy eagles. Local guides know the passage corridors. Leave at dawn - that's when birds are active.

Where Gehry's bold architecture meets the engineering marvel of the canal

★★★★☆
1

The Santa Fé-Panama City route takes 4 hours via Santiago. Leave as early as possible in the morning to have the afternoon free in the capital. If the Pearl Islands ferry leaves the next morning (which is often the case), take advantage of the afternoon to check into your accommodation and rest.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Ferry excursion to the Pearl Islands archipelago from Flamenco Marina on the Amador Causeway - if the day's schedule permits. The crossing takes 1.5 hours by catamaran to Contadora or Saboga. Before 1530, annual production reached 800,000 piastres. Three seasons of the American Survivor were filmed there. If the excursion doesn't happen today, it will be tomorrow (D12).

Panama City

Where Gehry's bold architecture meets the engineering marvel of the canal

★★★★☆
1

If the Pearl Islands excursion didn't happen the day before, now's the time. From Flamenco Marina on the Amador Causeway, the ferry takes 1.5 hours to reach Contadora or the Saboga stop. Contadora (1.4 km²) is the most touristy of the islands - white sand beaches, snorkeling on pristine reefs, relaxation. Saboga, the ferry's first stop, is more authentic: residents live from fishing and the village sees few visitors. Between July and September, you might spot humpback whales. If you already did the Pearls yesterday, free day in Panama City.

⏱ 360 min
2

Metropolitan Natural Park is a 232-hectare tropical forest right in the heart of the city. It's one of the last urban natural spaces of its kind in the world - 10 minutes from downtown, you can encounter sloths, capuchin monkeys and agoutis on easy marked trails. Plan for 1.5 hours late morning.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Balboa craft market before the return flight - molas from the Guna (multi-layered embroidered fabric), Emberá baskets, sombrero pintao, Azuero pottery. It's the best place in Panama City for artisan souvenirs at reasonable prices.
