Quito

★★★★★
1

Start at Plaza Grande, the beating heart of Quito's UNESCO-listed historic center—the very first cultural site inscribed in 1978, alongside Krakow. Surrounding the square: the Palacio de Carondelet (presidential residence since the 16th century), the Metropolitan Cathedral, and the Archbishop's Palace. On Tuesdays at 11am, catch the changing of the guard—it's highly photogenic. Grab a bench, order fresh juice from a street vendor, and watch daily life unfold around the fountain.

⏱ 40 min
2

Step inside the Iglesia de la Compañía de Jesús, a five-minute walk away. Built between 1605 and 1765 from gray volcanic stone, its gold-leaf interior will leave you speechless—it's South America's finest example of Baroque art. Construction took 160 years, which explains the blend of styles: Mudéjar, Churrigueresque, Neoclassical, and dominant Baroque. The main altarpiece is considered the masterpiece of Quito's colonial art school. Allow a full hour here.

⏱ 60 min
3

Continue to Plaza de San Francisco and its massive Franciscan convent, founded in 1537 atop the ruins of an Inca palace. With 40,000 m² of buildings—thirteen cloisters, three churches—it's nicknamed the "Escorial of the New World." Inside, over 3,500 works of colonial art and Legarda's famous Winged Virgin (1734) on the main altar. This is the original sculpture whose giant replica stands atop El Panecillo.

⏱ 60 min
4

Late afternoon, wander down Calle La Ronda, old Quito's most picturesque pedestrian street. Cobblestones, colorful facades, artisan workshops, and cozy cafés line the way. Come in the evening and the vibe shifts: street musicians, open galleries, and the scent of canelazo (hot cinnamon drink spiked with aguardiente). A perfect spot to end the day.

⏱ 45 min
5

If time allows early or late in the day, make a detour to Mitad del Mundo, 25 km north of Quito. The monument marks the Equator as determined in 1736 by Charles-Marie de La Condamine's expedition. You can plant a foot in each hemisphere—the classic photo op. Right next door, the Inti Ñan ethnographic museum offers fun experiments on the effects of latitude 0° (water flow, balancing an egg). Allow 1.5 hours on-site.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Stroll through the Rocafuerte neighborhood to sample an almuerzo (daily set menu) and encounter traditional herbalists, chocolatiers, and restorers of religious statues—a living Quito you won't find in guidebooks.

⏱ 45 min

Quito

★★★★★
1

The Basílica del Voto Nacional dominates Quito's skyline—the building rises 115 meters high, with towers exceeding 78 m. It's the largest neo-Gothic basilica in the Americas. French architect Emilio Tarlier drew inspiration from Bourges Cathedral when he drafted plans in 1890, but the first stone wasn't laid until 1892, and construction spanned nearly a century. Fun detail: the gargoyles aren't medieval chimeras but iguanas, tortoises, and armadillos—local fauna. Quiteños say that when the basilica is finally finished, the world will end. Climb the towers—wear good shoes, the final meters involve metal ladders. The view over the historic center is Quito's best.

⏱ 75 min
2

Take a taxi to El Panecillo, the "little bread loaf" hill rising to 3,016 m. At the summit, the Virgin of Quito—41 meters including the base, assembled from 7,400 aluminum plates by Spanish sculptor Agustín de la Herrán Matorras, installed in 1976. It's the world's tallest aluminum statue, taller than Rio's Christ the Redeemer. And it's the world's only winged Virgin—inspired by a passage from Revelation. The 180° view over Quito is superb. Pro tip: take a taxi up, don't walk—the neighborhood around the stairs isn't safe.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Afternoon at the TelefériQo: this cable car lifts you in 18 minutes from 3,117 m to 3,947 m on the slopes of Pichincha volcano—one of the world's highest gondolas. From Cruz Loma, the view over Quito is dizzying: the city stretches 40 km long but only 10 km wide. On clear days, you'll spot Cotopaxi, Cayambe, and Antisana, all snow-capped. Bring a windbreaker—it's chilly up here. If well-acclimatized, continue on foot to Rucu Pichincha summit (4,696 m, 3h one-way)—the trail is steep and sandy in the final section, but the view from the top sweeps the entire Avenue of Volcanoes.

⏱ 180 min

Otavalo

★★★★☆
1

Head north to Otavalo, 95 km from Quito via the Panamericana—count 2 hours with Cayambe and Imbabura volcanoes as your backdrop. On arrival, make straight for Plaza de los Ponchos, South America's most famous artisan market. Its roots stretch back to pre-Columbian times, when the Otavaleños already traded textiles and goods. Open daily, it explodes on Saturdays with hundreds of stalls selling hand-woven ponchos, alpaca wool blankets, silver jewelry, and hammocks. Haggling is expected—it's part of the game. On weekdays, prices are better and the vibe more relaxed.

⏱ 90 min
2

Loop around Parque Bolívar and Calle Sucre. Otavalo sits at 2,530 m at the foot of Imbabura (4,630 m) and Cotacachi (4,944 m) volcanoes. Otavaleños are recognizable by their long black braids and traditional clothing—a cultural pride remarkably preserved across centuries. If you're here on Saturday, don't miss the livestock market 1 km from the plaza—cows, pigs, chickens, and guinea pigs change hands from 6am onward.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Late day, swing by San Pablo Lake, 10 km away at the foot of Imbabura volcano. Stop at the El Lechero viewpoint: the millennial tree standing there is a sacred site where indigenous people from across the country come for purification rituals. The 360° view over the lake, volcano, and patchwork farmland is well worth the detour.

⏱ 60 min

Otavalo

★★★★☆
1

Set out early for Cuicocha Lagoon, 30 minutes from Otavalo. This crater lake formed 3,000 years ago during an eruption of Cotacachi volcano—3 km in diameter, 200 m deep, and two islands shaped like guinea pig backs in the center (hence the Quechua name: "guinea pig lake"). The waters shift from deep blue to emerald green. You can take a boat tour around the islets (25 min) to admire the colors up close.

⏱ 45 min
2

On the way back, stop at Peguche village, 3 km from Otavalo. Its 18-meter waterfall is sacred to Otavaleños—every June 21st during Inti Raymi (Festival of the Sun), locals come for ritual nocturnal purification baths in the cold water. Outside this period, it's a peaceful walk through eucalyptus forest (20 min). The village specializes in weaving—step into a workshop to watch traditional looms in action.

⏱ 60 min
3

Passing through Cotacachi, 20 minutes from Otavalo, stop in this small town renowned for leather craftsmanship—jackets, bags, belts, and boots line the main street at very reasonable prices.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Hike the Gorki Campuzano trail around Cuicocha Lagoon: 14 km of ridgeline between 3,100 and 3,500 m elevation, with stunning views over the crater and—on clear days—Imbabura, Cayambe, and Pichincha volcanoes. The trail is marked, rolling but without technical difficulty. Bring water and snacks—there's nothing along the route.

⏱ 270 min

Mindo

★★★★☆
1

Long drive from Otavalo to Mindo (roughly 4h via Nanegalito). The landscape shifts dramatically: you leave the dry highlands and plunge into the Andean cloud forest of the Chocó bioregion at 1,250 m elevation. Mindo is a former farming village turned naturalist's paradise—Birdlife International designated it an Important Bird Area. Ecuador harbors 130 of the world's 340 hummingbird species, and many live here. On arrival, take advantage of feeders at your lodging to observe these colorful rockets.

⏱ 30 min
2

Head to the Mariposario, 2.5 km from the center. Under a massive glass greenhouse, over 1,000 butterflies of 25 species fly freely around you. Place a piece of banana on your fingers—they'll come feed on it. With luck, you'll witness a butterfly emerging from its chrysalis. At the entrance feeders, hummingbirds put on a constant show.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Take the tarabita (metal gondola suspended from a cable) that crosses the canopy to access the waterfall sanctuary. Three trails: Nambillo cascade (15 min one-way, easy), La Reina cascade (50 min, most impressive), or the 5-waterfall circuit (2h round-trip). Paths are narrow and muddy—bring hiking shoes, rain gear, and a swimsuit if you want to bathe at the falls' base.

⏱ 150 min

Late afternoon, join a chocolate-making workshop. Ecuadorian cacao ("fino de aroma" variety) ranks among the world's finest, and several Mindo producers offer the complete process from bean to bar, tasting included.

⏱ 90 min

Latacunga

★★★☆☆
1

Big driving day: from Mindo, loop back through the foothills of Pichincha then head due south toward Cotopaxi National Park (count 4-5h total). Cotopaxi awaits with its nearly perfect snow-capped cone at 5,897 m—one of the world's highest active volcanoes. Its last major eruption in 1877 sent mudflows that destroyed Latacunga and several valley towns. Pack warm clothes: the park is windy and you'll be above 3,800 m.

⏱ 30 min
2

Stop at Limpiopungo Lagoon, at 3,800 m elevation at the volcano's foot. On clear days, Cotopaxi's snow-capped cone mirrors in the calm waters—Ecuador's classic postcard shot. The trail loops the lake in 1h to 1h30, easy-going and ideal for spotting Andean ducks, Andean gulls, and—with luck—a carunculated caracara. Walk slowly—altitude makes itself felt.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

If well-acclimatized, drive up to the parking lot at 4,500 m then hike to the José Rivas refuge at 4,864 m—half an hour of steep climbing through volcanic sand. From the refuge, you can continue 45 min to the glacier edge at 5,000 m. Shortness of breath guaranteed, but the view over the ice cap and Avenue of Volcanoes will take what's left. Grab a hot chocolate at the refuge on your way down.

⏱ 120 min

Banos

★★★★☆
1

Leave Latacunga for Quilotoa Lagoon, roughly 2h west through Andean farmland. Quilotoa is a volcano whose last confirmed eruption dates to 1280. It left behind a 3-km-diameter, 250-m-deep crater filled with turquoise water that shifts color with the light. At 3,914 m elevation, it's one of Ecuador's most photogenic sites. From the crater rim, the view is striking. Descend to the lake if you've got energy—30 min down steep, but the climb back (40-50 min) is taxing at this altitude. For the weary, mules are available for the ascent.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

After Quilotoa, drive toward Baños de Agua Santa (3-4h). You descend from the high plateaus into the Río Pastaza valley, dropping from 3,900 m to 1,815 m—the climate warms noticeably and vegetation turns tropical. Arriving in Baños, loop the central square to see the Basílica de la Virgen de Agua Santa, a pilgrimage site where frescoes depict miracles attributed to the Virgin. Then climb to the Bellavista viewpoint for your first glimpse of the town dominated by Tungurahua volcano (5,023 m), active since 1999.

⏱ 60 min

Banos

★★★★☆
1

The Waterfall Route (Ruta de las Cascadas) is Baños' star attraction: nearly 20 km toward Puyo, with some sixty waterfalls along the Río Pastaza valley. Rent a bike—the road is almost entirely downhill. First stop: Manto de la Novia cascade ("Bridal Veil"), which you can fly over via tarabita above the gorge. Continue to Río Verde and Pailón del Diablo ("Devil's Cauldron")—80 meters high, a force that soaks you in 30 seconds up close. Passages carved through rock let you slip behind the cascade. It's one of South America's most impressive waterfalls.

⏱ 90 min
2

Heading back up to Baños, stop at Casa del Arbol, a former Tungurahua volcano observation post turned attraction. Its famous swing suspended over the void at 2,660 m delivers vertigo-inducing photos with the volcano backdrop—if weather cooperates. The site is small, count 30 minutes.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Bike the waterfall route from Baños to Río Verde. The descent is easy and landscapes shift from Andean mountain to tropical vegetation. On return, local pickup trucks load bikes and cyclists to haul everyone back to Baños—very handy.

⏱ 180 min

Late afternoon, unwind in Baños' thermal baths. The hot, sulfurous waters flow directly from Tungurahua volcano. Several pools at varying temperatures (18 to 54°C). The local specialty to try on your way out: melcocha, a sugarcane candy stretched by hand right in front of you.

⏱ 90 min

Tena

★★★☆☆
1

From Baños, continue the descent toward the Amazon heading to Puyo (1h30). You drop even more elevation—heat and humidity rise, vegetation turns dense and tropical. Make a stop at Mirador Indichuris, 45 minutes south of Puyo: perched at 1,000 m, this viewpoint run by the indigenous Indichuris community ("children of the sun" in Quechua) offers panoramic views over the confluence of the Puyo and Pastaza rivers and the endless Amazon canopy. There are also caves adorned with carved faces and a swing over the abyss.

⏱ 60 min
2

Don't leave without visiting Paseo de los Monos, a nonprofit refuge tucked deep in the rainforest. Volunteers—sometimes French-speaking—guide you among monkeys, coatis, parrots, and tortoises rescued after illegal trafficking seizures. Some animals, too habituated to humans or injured, can never return to the wild. The place is moving and the welcome very warm.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Canoe excursion on Río Puyo or Río Napo to reach a Kichwa Amazonian community. You'll be welcomed with traditional achiote body paint (red plant pigment), can join ritual dances, and discover the use of jungle medicinal plants. It's a brief but authentic immersion into the culture of the Oriente peoples.

⏱ 120 min

Quito

★★★★★
1

Spectacular climb from Tena back to the highlands via the Papallacta road (3-4h). You ascend from 500 m to over 4,000 m at the pass, then descend to Papallacta at 3,300 m. The landscape shifts from suffocating jungle to wind-swept Andean páramo—a striking contrast in a few hours. Enjoy panoramas over high-altitude lagoons and tall grasses of the páramo before your hot springs stop.

⏱ 30 min
2

Before driving back to Quito, take the short walk around Papallacta's lagoons in the páramo. The air is crisp, silence total, and tall grasses ripple in the wind. It's an end-of-the-world landscape at 3,500 m, populated by Andean ducks and mountain rabbits. Count 45 minutes for the loop.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Relax at Papallacta hot springs, Ecuador's finest. Perched at 3,300 m elevation, the natural hot-water pools (between 36 and 40°C) are outdoors, surrounded by mountains and mist. After 10 days of driving, hiking, and altitude, it's the perfect stop to loosen muscles before the return to Quito (1h30 drive).

⏱ 120 min
