Hamburg

★★★★★
1

Opened in January 2017 after ten years of construction and 866 million euros, the Elbphilharmonie delivered on every scandalous promise: the acoustics steal the show from the architecture. Herzog & de Meuron set a glass wave atop a red-brick warehouse in the port — the result is both improbable and inevitable. The panoramic Plaza is free and open daily, but online tickets for morning slots sell out fast — book the evening before.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Speicherstadt feels like a city within a city: seven kilometres of canals, dark red-brick warehouses built between 1883 and 1927, listed as UNESCO World Heritage in 2015 alongside the Kontorhausviertel. It once stored coffee, cocoa and tobacco. Today, designers' studios and small museums have taken their place. Avoid Sundays — everything shuts.

⏱ 90 min
3

Walk up to Hauptkirche St. Michaelis, the great white Michel that has dominated the quays since the 17th century. The Baroque interior is restrained; the view from the tower is sweeping. Budget €5 for the lift, or three times as many steps for the thrifty.

⏱ 60 min
4

The HafenCity waterfront smells of fresh concrete and salt water mixed together — it is Europe's largest urban waterfront redevelopment project, right next door to the Speicherstadt. Contemporary buildings sit alongside the red warehouses without apparent friction. Am Sandtorkai square, directly in front of the Elbphilharmonie, offers the best vantage point over the whole ensemble.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

If you arrive on a Sunday morning before 9:30am, the Altona Fischmarkt is still in full cry — a Hamburg tradition since 1703. Smoked eels and grey shrimps go fast. After 9:30, it's over.

⏱ 60 min

The scent of hops and malt drifts through the Schanzenviertel from late afternoon. Biergärten and craft breweries line the Schulterblatt. The neighbourhood is also the best spot for dinner before a concert at the Elbphilharmonie.

⏱ 120 min

Deichstraße is home to the last surviving 17th-century merchants' houses in Hamburg — narrow half-timbered façades overlooking the Nikolaifleet canal. The classic view is from Hohe Brücke, two minutes' walk from the Speicherstadt. Free, accessible at any hour.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 55 km · 50min

Luneburg

★★★★☆
1

Lüneburg grew rich on salt for a thousand years, then nearly sank into the ground: the city is built on a pillar of salt that is slowly dissolving beneath its foundations. The result is Gothic façades tilted like exhausted dominoes — and the charm comes precisely from that instability. The salt works closed in 1980, but the houses are still standing.

⏱ 60 min
2

Built from 1405, Lüneburg's Altes Rathaus is an accumulation of styles spanning six centuries — Gothic, Renaissance and Baroque — stacked together without contradiction. The councillors' chamber with its 16th-century glazed tiles is accessible on a guided tour. Check with the tourist office for opening times.

⏱ 75 min
3

Head down to the Stintmarkt, the old harbour on the Ilmenau, where the medieval wooden crane from the 14th century still stands on the quay. It was used to load barges with salt bound for Lübeck. Today the terraces have taken over from the cargo — views across half-timbered warehouses included.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The Deutsches Salzmuseum is worth two unhurried hours: housed in the former salt works that closed in 1980, it traces the salt industry from antiquity to the 19th century. Giant extraction vats, horse-powered wheels, the long rivalry with Lübeck — all meticulously reconstructed.

⏱ 120 min

The weathered brickwork and tilting houses of the old town are best seen in the evening: the gabled façades of Am Sande and Grapengießerstraße are lit up and the day-trippers are gone. End with a glass of Lüneburger Pils at one of the Kneipen on the Stintmarkt.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 50 km · 50min

Wilseder Berg

★★★★☆
1

The locals simply call it 'the summit' — at 169 metres, Wilseder Berg is the highest point on the north-west German plain. On a clear day, Hamburg is visible 40 km away. You arrive on foot from Niederhaverbeck (about 4.5 km) or from Wilsede: cars are banned throughout the nature reserve.

⏱ 90 min
2

Wilsede, a car-free village off-limits to tour coaches, looks like a fairy-tale set: half-timbered houses, sandstone walls, chickens roaming freely on cobbled paths. The Heidemuseum Dat ole Huus preserves 19th-century moorland peasant life — the attached Milchhalle serves a decent buckwheat cake.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Walk the marked heathland trails around Wilseder Berg along the Heidschnuckenweg, the long-distance path that crosses the entire reserve. The curly-fleeced Heidschnucken sheep have been the moorland's gardeners for centuries: without them, the heather would revert to forest. Best time: August, when everything turns violet.

⏱ 150 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 10 km · 15min

Totengrund

★★★★☆
1

In the morning, junipers rise from the mist in dark clumps across this bowl-shaped hollow — a scene that draws nature photographers from across Germany. Totengrund owes its brooding name to old local legends whose meaning no one quite agrees on. What is certain: the hollow, likely formed at the end of the last ice age, looks unlike anything else in the region.

⏱ 45 min
2

This 30-hectare hollow was bought in 1906 by Pastor Wilhelm Bode using donor funds — a founding gesture that gave rise to Germany's first major nature reserve, established in 1921. A memorial stone to investor Andreas Thomsen marks the viewpoint at the rim of the hollow.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Walk the loop around Totengrund on the marked paths that trace the rim of the hollow — descending into the bowl itself is prohibited to protect the vegetation. The full circuit takes about 45 minutes. Keep an eye out for snipe and northern lapwings in the wetland areas.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 110 km · 1h35

Bremen

★★★★☆
1

The Bremen Roland has stood sword in hand on the market square since 1404 — the largest free-standing medieval statue in Germany, over 10 metres tall. It symbolised the city's rights against the bishop. Together with the Rathaus, it has been UNESCO-listed since 2004 as an exceptional testament to civic autonomy in Europe. The Rathaus itself, built between 1405 and 1408, has never been destroyed or structurally altered since.

⏱ 60 min
2

If you step into the Ratskeller, head straight down to see the barrels: the Rathaus cellar has stored German wines since the 15th century — a few century-old bottles sit in stone niches. It is one of the oldest wine cellars in Germany still in operation. Booking recommended for lunch.

⏱ 60 min
3

Touristy by reputation, the Schnoorviertel holds its own: this 15th-century fishermen's quarter — with its lanes barely wide enough for two people to pass, red half-timbered houses and small craftsmen's shops — has not been spoiled. Arrive before 11am and you'll have it almost to yourself.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Follow the Bremen Town Musicians — donkey, dog, cat and rooster — from Gerhard Marcks' statue to the municipal theatre. The Grimm tale was born here, even though the brothers never set foot in Bremen. The original statue dates from 1951; the donkey's hoof is polished smooth by millions of hands.

⏱ 30 min

The Schlachte promenade runs along the Weser for a kilometre of terraces and floating bars — the old merchant waterfront of the medieval port, now a riverside walkway. In the evening, the restaurant boats on the water light up. Choose the riverside side for a view of the barges.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 75 km · 1h05

Bremerhaven

★★★☆☆
1

Five continents in a day: the Klimahaus Bremerhaven follows the 8th meridian East from Niger to Alaska, taking in the Swiss Alps and Samoa along the way. The temperature changes with each room — you go from 35°C desert heat to Antarctica in twenty minutes. A concept unique in the world, open since the late 2000s.

⏱ 180 min
2

The Deutsches Auswandererhaus (German Emigration Center) hits harder than a conventional museum: located on the quay from which more than seven million Europeans departed between 1830 and 1974, it hands each visitor a 'boarding pass' bearing the real biography of an emigrant. Winner of the European Museum of the Year Award in 2007.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Stroll the Havenwelten quays and watch container ships pass within arm's reach of the embankment. Bremerhaven is one of Europe's top container ports — the yellow cranes are visible for kilometres. In the evening, a handful of fresh fish restaurants line the basin.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 80 km · 1h15

Cuxhaven

★★★☆☆
1

Standing in wood on the Elbe embankment since the 18th century, the Kugelbake has guided ships into the river mouth for centuries — it marks the northernmost point of Lower Saxony. The current structure dates from 1945 (the previous one was destroyed during the war), stands nearly 30 metres tall, and has featured on Cuxhaven's coat of arms since 1913.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Wattenmeer mudflats around Cuxhaven stretch as far as the eye can see at low tide: a flat tidal plain churned by worm burrows, scattered with shells and lit by shifting colours. Listed as a UNESCO World Natural Heritage Site, it hosts millions of migratory birds.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Set off on a Wattwanderung at falling tide from Duhnen beach — twice a day the sea gives up kilometres of mudflats to be crossed barefoot. Grey shrimps and crabs survive in rock pools. Get back before the tide turns: it comes in fast.

⏱ 120 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 175 km · 2h35

Friedrichstadt

★★★☆☆
1

It is hard to believe: Amsterdam has an enclave in Schleswig-Holstein. Friedrichstadt was founded in 1621 by Duke Friedrich III to receive Dutch settlers — Remonstrants fleeing religious persecution. They brought their bricks, their stepped gables and their canals. The town council minutes were written in Dutch until the 18th century.

⏱ 60 min
2

Founded in 1621 by Duke Friedrich III of Schleswig-Gottorf, the planned town is laid out around the Mittelburggraben — the central canal — on a grid of perpendicular streets. The Gevelsteen, carved and painted stone reliefs above doorways, bear the coats of arms of the original Dutch families and some date back to the town's founding.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Take a rowing boat out on the Mittelburggraben for a tour of Friedrichstadt's grachten — departure from the Tourist-Information at Am Markt 9. Red-brick gabled houses mirror themselves in the water. One hour is enough for the main canals.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 30 km · 30min

Husum

★★★☆☆
1

Theodor Storm called his hometown 'the grey city by the sea' — and he was right. North Sea skies press down hard on Husum. The Theodor-Storm-Zentrum, installed in the house where the writer lived, displays manuscripts and personal objects belonging to the master of Nordic short fiction. Storm is the author of Der Schimmelreiter — read it before you come.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Schloss vor Husum is worth visiting less for its interior rooms than for its park: in spring, more than four million violet crocuses carpet the lawn beneath the trees — the spectacle lasts two weeks in March and April. It is the only castle on the North Sea coast in Schleswig-Holstein, built between 1577 and 1582.

⏱ 60 min
3

At the Binnenhafen, ochre and white façades reflect in the still water of the inner basin — the very image of a Nordic postcard. This is the marina today. The real fishing harbour, with its crab cutters, lies at the Außenhafen, 500 metres further towards the sea.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walk through the Nordfriesland Museum to understand the history of the North Frisians — a seafaring people settled on this coast since the Middle Ages, whose language is still spoken in a handful of villages. Fishing implements, storm charts and traditional costumes sum up a thousand years of coastal life.

⏱ 90 min

If it is April, the castle park erupts in crocuses for two weeks — now or never. Outside the flowering season, the Herzoginnengarten and Schlosspark take about twenty minutes to walk through. The smoked herring Brötchen stalls on the Binnenhafen quay open from 8am.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 25 km · 30min

Westerhever

★★★★☆
1

Built between 1906 and 1908 on an artificial mound four metres above the flats, the Leuchtturm Westerheversand stands 40 metres tall and its beam carries 39 kilometres — as far as Helgoland on a clear day. Since the 1970s it has appeared on Jever Beer bottles, earning it a fame its solitary setting would never suggest.

⏱ 60 min
2

Sheep graze around the red-and-white tower in complete indifference — an absurd and entirely real scene. The Salzwiesen salt marshes at the foot of the lighthouse are a protected biotope within the Schleswig-Holsteinisches Wattenmeer National Park.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Climb the 157 steps to survey the Eiderstedt peninsula from the platform at 37 metres: on a clear day you can count twenty church spires across the peninsula, along with the halligen islands to the north. The tower is only accessible on a guided tour, Wednesday to Monday, 11am–4pm, from Easter to late October.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 75 km · 1h20

Westerland Sylt

★★★☆☆
1

Westerland is not pretty — it is functional: Sylt's 'capital' concentrates hotels, shops and the Hindenburgdamm rail terminal. It is a logistical base, not a destination in itself. The Weststrand that stretches for kilometres north and south, however, is genuine wilderness: wind, waves, and few crowds outside high season.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Hindenburgdamm, the 11-km railway causeway that has connected Sylt to the mainland since 1927, is the island's only land link. Cars are loaded onto flat wagons — the car-train. Watching one pass from the beach at Morsum or the port at List gives a vivid sense of Sylt's deliberate isolation.

⏱ 30 min
3

St. Severin in Keitum is the real Romanesque church of Sylt — built in the 12th century in local sandstone, flanked by a mariners' churchyard with granite crosses. Keitum is also the best-preserved village on the island, with its thatched houses. It is 5 km east of Westerland by bike or bus line 1.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Cycle the Weststrand from Westerland towards Wenningstedt or Kampen — the cycling paths follow either the Wattenmeer on the east side or the sea on the west. Hire a bike in town: €15–20 a day. The eastern route through the Braderup heath is less known and wilder.

⏱ 150 min

The North Frisian wind tears words from your mouth and snaps the flags — a constant on Sylt. Use it as an excuse to watch kitesurfers at Brandenburger Strand, 3 km north of Westerland: conditions are consistent there and the riders are spectacular.

⏱ 60 min

Book a car-train ticket on the Hindenburgdamm to cross to Niebüll on the mainland — a 40-minute passage at rail level with the dunes and the Wattenmeer, perfect at sunset. Online booking is mandatory via DB Sylt Shuttle, especially at weekends in season.

⏱ 80 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 10 km · 15min

Kampen Sylt

★★★☆☆
1

Kampen carries the steepest price tag on Sylt — €8 breakfasts, luxury boutiques, villas tucked behind hedgerows. But the beach and the cliff are free. The Rotes Kliff owes its name to the iron-rich clay that turns blood-red in the late afternoon light — 30 metres of cliff face stretching 4 km between Kampen and Wenningstedt.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Rotes Kliff runs 4 km along Sylt's west coast between Kampen and Wenningstedt: the 30-metre cliff crumbles steadily into the sea — keep 5 metres back from the edge. A wooden boardwalk has run along the top since 1979, designated a nature reserve. Kampen Lighthouse dates from 1855 and stands 40 metres tall.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Climb the Uwe-Düne via 110 wooden steps from Kampen: at 52.5 metres, it is Sylt's highest natural point. The wooden platform gives a 360° view over the island, from List in the north to Hörnum in the south. Free and accessible at any time.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 15 km · 20min

List Sylt

★★☆☆☆
1

At the Ellenbogen — Sylt's 'forearm', the island's northern tip — the wind cuts in from both sides at once: North Sea to the west, Wadden Sea to the east, Denmark visible 12 km away. It is private land that the owner opens for a €8 car toll; walkers and cyclists pass freely on the dune paths.

⏱ 60 min
2

Lister Hafen, at the island's northern end, runs ferries to Rømø (Denmark) and to the halligen islands. Fishing boats sell Sylt oysters directly at the dock — Sylt is one of the very few places in Germany where oyster farming still survives.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Take a ferry to Rømø from List — 45 minutes to the Danish island. Rømø has the widest beaches in Scandinavia at low tide: up to 2 km of dry sand. The wind drives the land yachts.

⏱ 120 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 35 km · 45min

Hornum Sylt

★★☆☆☆
1

Hörnum Odde is Sylt's very end: a sand spit that narrows towards the south and is half-swallowed by winter storms. The island loses several metres of ground here each year on the North Sea side. There is a rare feeling of impermanence — the land is literally slipping away underfoot.

⏱ 45 min
2

Leuchtturm Hörnum, brought into service in 1907, is the only lighthouse on Sylt accessible on a guided tour — the other four do not open to visitors. Built in cast iron on a 17-metre dune, it rises nearly 50 metres above sea level. Views from the gallery take in Föhr, Amrum and the halligen.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Walk to Hörnum Odde point along the marked dune path from the village: 2 km each way, flat terrain, but a headwind can double the effort. Seals often rest on the sandbanks visible from the tip — binoculars are useful.

⏱ 75 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 110 km · 2h10

Flensburg

★★★☆☆
1

Flensburg has been importing rum from the Danish West Indies since 1755, when the city's merchants supplied Saint Croix, Saint Thomas and Saint John. Of the era's 200 rum houses, two active distilleries remain: Johannsen and Braasch. The Rum Museum inside the Schifffahrtsmuseum traces this colonial trade that most Germans know nothing about.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Museumshafen with its old sailing vessels is worth visiting even without a festival: the traditional wooden boats moored along the stone quay are accessible on certain days. The former West Indies warehouse stands directly on the basin.

⏱ 60 min
3

In the inner courtyards of the Norderstrasse, hints of molasses and tobacco seep from the old warehouses. The Kaufmannshöfe — merchants' courtyards — conceal secret gardens behind gates that are often left open. The Marienburg is the best known.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Follow the Rum & Zucker Meile from the Nordertor to the harbour: this marked trail links the old warehouses, merchants' courtyards and the two last active distilleries. Tastings are offered at Johannsen Rum — let them know in advance.

⏱ 90 min

If you arrive on a Thursday morning, the Südermarkt market is at its liveliest: fruit, vegetables, Danish cheeses, smoked fish. Flensburg is officially bilingual German-Danish — you hear both languages at the stalls.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 35 km · 35min

Schleswig

★★★★☆
1

The Nydam Boat, a 4th-century rowing vessel excavated in 1863, is displayed in the Nydamhalle at Schloss Gottorf: 23 metres long, 28 oars, built from clinker-laid oak with no keel. It was probably a Germanic war vessel. No other archaeological find of this size and age is as well preserved anywhere in Northern Europe.

⏱ 90 min
2

Dom St. Petri dominates the Schleswig skyline from every angle — its 112-metre spire towers over the Schlei fjord. Inside stands the Brüggemann Altarpiece, a masterwork of early 16th-century wood carving composed of 392 individual figures.

⏱ 60 min
3

Schloss Gottorf looks like a pleasure palace but is in fact a transformed medieval fortress — the Dukes of Schleswig-Gottorf ruled from here between the 12th and 18th centuries. Now the state museum of Schleswig-Holstein, it also houses a reconstruction of the Gottorfer Globus, a Baroque globe-planetarium 3 metres in diameter that rotated slowly for its 17th-century visitors.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Walk through the reconstructed Baroque garden of Gottorf, a terraced garden descending to the Schlei: clipped hedges, formal parterres and a water view reproduce the 17th-century original. Free and open year-round, it is often empty off-season.

⏱ 45 min

The banks of the Schlei smell of seaweed and the tar of old wooden boats — the brackish-water scent unique to Baltic fjords. The Lollfuß promenade follows the waterfront from the old town harbour to the cathedral: twenty minutes on foot, with sailing boats in view.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 5 km · 10min

Haithabu

★★★★☆
1

The Wikinger Museum Haithabu, inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List in 2018 alongside the Danevirke, is one of Germany's most important archaeological museums. Between the 8th and 11th centuries, Haithabu was one of Northern Europe's largest trading ports — around 3,000 people are estimated to have lived here in about 950 AD. The semicircular earthwork that enclosed the town is still visible in the landscape today.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Danevirke, a 30-km wall that cut across the Jutland peninsula from the Schlei to the North Sea, was built to defend the Viking kingdom against the Franks. It shares its UNESCO listing with Haithabu. A restored section of the earthwork wall can be visited a few kilometres to the north.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walk the boardwalk paths of the Haithabu open-air museum, where seven Viking longhouses have been reconstructed full-size within the ancient rampart. Craftspeople in period costume work here in season. A reconstructed longship is moored at the jetty — boat trips are offered in summer.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 45 km · 45min

Eckernforde

★★★☆☆
1

The smell of smoke and salt announces Eckernförde well before you reach the harbour: the town has been smoking sprats and eels in wood-fired kilns since the 19th century. The Alte Fischräucherei Eckernförde museum traces this craft. At the harbour, a few artisan smokers still sell directly at the quayside.

⏱ 60 min
2

St. Nicolaikirche, plain and Gothic, surprises with the quality of its interior woodwork — 17th-century altars and pews inside a building raised in the 13th century. 200 metres from the harbour, usually empty, free entry.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Buy smoked sprats directly from fishermen on the quay in the morning — Eckernförder Sprotten, smoked and marinated in vinegar, are the local speciality. On the first Sunday of the month, the fish market sets up along the harbour promenade.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 30 km · 30min

Kiel

★★★☆☆
1

Kiel was 80% destroyed in 1944, but its waterfront was rebuilt with care around the Kieler Förde — the 17-km fjord that opens onto the Baltic. The Kiellinie, a 4-km promenade along the fjord's west bank, is the meeting point of joggers, cyclists and families. In June, Kieler Woche turns it into one of the world's largest sailing regattas.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Kiellinie runs 4 km along the Förde from the city centre to the botanical garden: Scandinavian ferries, cargo ships and racing yachts pass within metres. At the far end, the Düsternbrooker Gehölz is a waterside woodland park.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Take the free ferry between the two banks of the Kieler Förde — the Schlepp- und Fährgesellschaft runs crossings every 15 minutes. Commercial port on the east side, marina and sailboats on the west. Five minutes on the water, panorama included.

⏱ 30 min
