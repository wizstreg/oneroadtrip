⏳ How many days should I plan?

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Winter (November-March): Perfect weather, comfortable temperatures, ideal for sightseeing, peak tourist season, higher prices.

Spring/Fall (April-May, September-October): Good weather, moderate crowds, reasonable prices, still hot but manageable.

Summer (June-August): Extremely hot, challenging conditions, lowest prices, Red Sea diving still excellent, indoor attractions preferred.

Red Sea Season: Year-round excellent diving, summer best underwater visibility, winter more comfortable surface conditions.

Egypt Journey: Pyramids, Nile Temples & Pharaohs' Treasures

From the Grand Egyptian Museum—100,000 objects including the complete Tutankhamun collection—to the colossi of Ramesses II carved into the Abu Simbel cliff, from Karnak's forest of 134 columns to the feluccas gliding silently between Aswan's granite islands, this 10-day circuit travels up the Nile from Cairo to the Nubian border, passing through the most extraordinary sites on the planet.

Stop 1

Cairo

Where Fatimid mosques and the 1382 souk pulse alongside contemporary Cairo life

★★★★★
📍 Al-Azhar Mosque Cairo📍 Khan el-Khalili souk Cairo📍 Al-Muizz Street Islamic Cairo📍 Sultan Hassan Mosque Cairo📍 Saladin Citadel Cairo📍 Muhammad Ali Mosque Citadel Cairo
1

Start at the Citadel of Saladin, perched on the Mokattam heights 75 meters above the city. Built from 1176 by Saladin to defend Cairo against the Crusaders, it served as the seat of Egyptian power for nearly 700 years. Inside, step through the doors of the Muhammad Ali Mosque—nicknamed 'the Alabaster Mosque' for its interior alabaster cladding rising 11 meters high. The central dome peaks at 52 meters and its Ottoman silhouette dominates Cairo's skyline. From the terrace, the view sweeps across Old Cairo, the minarets, and on clear days, the Giza pyramids on the horizon. Plan for 90 minutes on site.

⏱ 90 min
2

Descend next to the Sultan Hassan Mosque, just below the Citadel—one of the masterpieces of Mamluk architecture. Built between 1356 and 1363 under Sultan An-Nasir Hassan, it measures 150 meters long and its south minaret reaches 68 meters, the tallest in medieval Cairo. The interior stuns with its proportions: the central courtyard opens onto four iwans (vaulted halls), each dedicated to one of the four schools of Sunni jurisprudence. Across the way, Al-Rifai Mosque (1912) forms a striking contrast—same Mamluk style but built 550 years later. It houses the tombs of the last Shah of Iran and several members of the Egyptian royal family.

⏱ 60 min
3

Al-Azhar Mosque, founded in 970 by the Fatimids, is Cairo's oldest mosque and home to the world's second-oldest continuously operating university. Its 12,000 square meters blend Fatimid, Mamluk, and Ottoman architectural layers—spot the three minarets, built respectively in 1340, 1469, and 1510, each in a different style. The white marble courtyard dates from the original construction. If you're here late morning, you might see students attending Quranic interpretation classes beneath the arcades, as they have for over a thousand years.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Dive into Khan el-Khalili souk, founded in 1382 by Emir Djaharks al-Khalili on the former cemetery of the Fatimid caliphs. More than 900 stalls crowd into a labyrinth of covered alleyways—goldsmiths, spice vendors, hammered copper, perfumes. Walk up Al-Muizz Street, one of Cairo's oldest (10th century), lined with 29 UNESCO-listed Islamic monuments. Don't miss mint tea at El-Fishawy Café, open continuously since 1773. The souk is at its best in late afternoon when the heat subsides and the alleyways come alive.

⏱ 150 min

Dinner in Old Cairo—Egyptian street food is worth the trip: koshari (lentils, rice, pasta with spicy tomato sauce), ful medames (slow-cooked fava beans), ta'ameya (Egyptian falafel made from favas rather than chickpeas). The hole-in-the-wall joints around Khan el-Khalili serve generous portions. If you prefer a more relaxed experience, several restaurants with terraces overlook Al-Hussein Square.

Stop 2

Giza

Where four-and-a-half millennia of stone meet the world's newest museum

★★★★★
📍 Grand Egyptian Museum GEM Giza📍 Pyramid of Khufu Giza📍 Pyramid of Khafre Giza📍 Pyramid of Menkaure Giza📍 Great Sphinx Giza📍 Panoramic Point Nine Pyramids Giza
1

The Grand Egyptian Museum (GEM), inaugurated November 1, 2025, is the world's largest archaeological museum dedicated to a single civilization—500,000 square meters total area, 100,000 objects displayed across 12 permanent halls covering seven millennia of history. It sits 2 km from the pyramids on the Giza plateau. Right at the entrance, the colossal statue of Ramesses II (11 meters, 83 tons, 3,200 years old) welcomes you in the atrium. The Grand Staircase, spanning six levels and 6,000 square meters, is lined with some sixty monumental statues with direct views of the pyramids through the glass facade. The complete Tutankhamun collection—5,398 pieces, many displayed for the first time—occupies 7,500 square meters alone. Count on a minimum of 4 hours, ideally 6 for a complete visit. Tickets are no longer sold on-site—book online.

⏱ 300 min
2

After the museum, head to the Giza plateau 2 km away—a pedestrian bridge now connects the two sites. The Pyramid of Khufu, sole survivor of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World, has stood for more than 4,500 years. The numbers are dizzying: 230 meters per side, 146 meters tall originally (138 today), 2.3 million limestone blocks averaging 2.5 tons each, for a total weight of 5 million tons. It was the world's tallest building for 3,800 years. If you go inside (extra fee), expect a narrow, steep corridor leading to the King's Chamber—10.5 m by 5.2 m in solid granite. It's physical and claustrophobic, but the experience is worth it. Arrive at opening (8am) to avoid crowds and heat.

⏱ 120 min
3

The Sphinx, carved directly from a rocky outcrop on the plateau, measures 73.5 meters long and 20 meters high—the largest carved monolith from antiquity. Its face, probably that of Pharaoh Khafre (4th Dynasty, around 2500 BC), looks due east toward the sunrise. Don't miss the Panoramic Point (Nine Pyramids viewpoint) on the road that curves around the southern plateau—this is where you'll get all three great pyramids and the three smaller queens' pyramids aligned in a single frame.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Sunset over the pyramids from the Panoramic Point or from one of the terrace restaurants on Fayoum Road that runs along the plateau. The golden light on the pyramids in late afternoon is the best photographic moment—tour buses have left and the heat is bearable. Some organize camel or horse rides around the plateau in late afternoon.

⏱ 90 min

Stop 3

Luxor

The world's greatest open-air museum stretched between Karnak and the Valley of Kings

★★★★☆
📍 Step Pyramid of Djoser Saqqara📍 Bent Pyramid Dahshur📍 Red Pyramid Dahshur📍 Imhotep Museum Saqqara📍 Luxor Temple illuminated📍 Avenue of Sphinxes Luxor
1

Early departure to Saqqara, 30 km south of Cairo—the necropolis of ancient Memphis. The Step Pyramid of Djoser, built around 2660 BC by vizier Imhotep, is Egypt's oldest pyramid and humanity's first monumental stone structure. Six stacked mastabas of decreasing size form its 62 meters of height. The entrance through the colonnade—40 pillars sculpted like papyrus—is striking in its modernity. Beneath the pyramid, a network of 5 km of underground corridors descends 28 meters deep to the burial chamber. It's possible to visit part of the underground complex for an extra fee. The site is far less crowded than Giza—take advantage of it.

⏱ 120 min
2

Continue to Dahshur, 15 km south of Saqqara—an even quieter site. Here you'll see the evolution of construction techniques: the Bent Pyramid (105 meters, around 2600 BC), recognizable by its change of slope at mid-height—Sneferu's architects had to reduce the angle during construction to avoid collapse. Right next to it, the Red Pyramid (104 meters), the first successful smooth-sided pyramid in history. It served as the direct model for Khufu's Great Pyramid. You can enter the Red Pyramid via a 63-meter corridor—the ammonia smell is strong, but the three interior chambers in red stone are worth the trip.

⏱ 90 min
3

Stop by the Imhotep Museum at Saqqara before leaving—small but remarkable. The blue faience tiles found inside Djoser's pyramid, the mummy of Merenre I, and Imhotep's wooden coffin are displayed here. It's a good complement for understanding the necropolis.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Cairo → Luxor flight in late afternoon (1 hour flight, several carriers offer daily departures). On arrival, settle on the east bank of the Nile. If your flight arrives before sunset, head straight to Luxor Temple—it's magnificent at night, illuminated. The pylon of Ramesses II, its two seated colossi of 15 meters, and the remaining obelisk (the second has been in Paris's Place de la Concorde since 1836) form the entrance. The processional colonnade of Amenhotep III, 20 meters high, gives you chills under the floodlights. The avenue of human-headed sphinxes, restored and reopened in 2021 after decades of work, connects Luxor Temple to Karnak over 2.7 km—you can walk it in the cool evening air.

Stop 4

Luxor

The world's greatest open-air museum stretched between Karnak and the Valley of Kings

★★★★★
📍 Karnak Temple Luxor📍 Hypostyle Hall Karnak📍 Sacred Lake Karnak📍 Hatshepsut Obelisk Karnak📍 Avenue of Sphinxes criosphinxes Karnak📍 Luxor Museum Nile corniche
1

Karnak is the largest religious complex ever built—more than 2 square kilometers of temples, pylons, obelisks, and sanctuaries erected over 2,000 years by dozens of pharaohs, from Senusret I (around 2000 BC) through the Ptolemies. Arrive at opening (6am) to have the Great Hypostyle Hall almost to yourself. This forest of 134 columns, built by Seti I and completed by Ramesses II, measures 103 meters long by 52 meters wide—it could contain Notre-Dame de Paris. The 12 central columns rise to 23 meters with capitals 3.5 meters in diameter. In the raking morning light, the reliefs carved in stone take on striking depth. Count on a minimum of 2.5 hours for Karnak, 3 if you want to explore the corners.

⏱ 180 min
2

Don't leave Karnak without circling the Sacred Lake (120 m × 77 m), where priests purified themselves before ceremonies. Hatshepsut's obelisk, the tallest still standing in Egypt (29 meters, carved from a single block of Aswan pink granite), rises between the 4th and 5th pylons. At the end of the main axis, the sanctuary of the Barque of Amun—the spiritual heart of the temple—preserves remarkably well-preserved wall paintings. At the entrance, 40 ram-headed sphinxes (criosphinxes) line the processional avenue that once led to the Nile.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Luxor Museum, on the Nile corniche between Karnak and Luxor temples, is a remarkably well-designed small museum—far more pleasant than the old Cairo museum. Among the major pieces: two royal mummies (Ahmose I and Ramesses I), a colossal head of Amenhotep III in granite, and the talatat wall—283 painted sandstone blocks from the temple of Aten that Akhenaten had built at Karnak before his successors demolished it. Count on 1 to 1.5 hours.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Late afternoon stroll along the Nile corniche between Luxor Temple and downtown. Luxor's east bank comes alive at sunset—feluccas glide on the river, restaurant terraces light up. If you haven't yet seen Karnak at night, the sound and light show (every evening, in multiple languages) lets you wander through the illuminated temple—it's kitschy but the atmosphere in the hypostyle hall under the spotlights is worth it.

Stop 5

Luxor

The world's greatest open-air museum stretched between Karnak and the Valley of Kings

★★★★★
📍 Hot air balloon Luxor west bank📍 Valley of the Kings Luxor west bank📍 Tomb Ramesses IV KV2 Valley of the Kings📍 Tomb Ramesses IX KV6 Valley of the Kings📍 Colossi of Memnon Luxor📍 Artisan village Deir el-Medina Luxor
1

Wake at 4:30am for a hot air balloon flight over the west bank—one of Egypt's most spectacular experiences. The balloon takes off at sunrise and floats over the Valley of the Kings, Hatshepsut's temple, and the sugarcane fields along the Nile for 45 minutes to an hour. Book the day before—flights are canceled in strong winds.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Valley of the Kings holds 63 tombs carved into rock between 1539 and 1075 BC—New Kingdom pharaohs had abandoned pyramids in favor of underground burials to escape looters. Your ticket includes 3 tombs of your choice. The most impressive for their paintings: Ramesses IV (KV2), Ramesses IX (KV6), Merenptah (KV8). Seti I's tomb (KV17), considered the valley's most beautiful, requires a substantial extra fee. Tutankhamun's (KV62), discovered in 1922 by Howard Carter, is small and its paintings less spectacular than others—the 5,398 objects from the treasure are now at GEM in Giza. Arrive before 7am—there's no shade in the valley and the heat quickly becomes unbearable.

⏱ 150 min
3

The Colossi of Memnon, two seated statues of Amenhotep III rising 18 meters each, guard the entrance to what was Luxor's largest mortuary temple—now vanished. Carved from 720-ton blocks of quartzite each, transported from Gebel el-Ahmar quarries near Cairo 675 km away. In antiquity, the north colossus emitted a sound at sunrise (probably due to dilation of stone cracked by an earthquake in 27 BC)—Greeks and Romans came from afar to hear it. The stop is quick—15 to 20 minutes suffices.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Free afternoon to recover from the 4:30am wake-up. If you still have energy, detour to Deir el-Medina, the village of artisans who built and decorated the Valley of the Kings tombs for 500 years. The small tombs of the workers—notably those of Sennedjem and Inherkhau—are paradoxically better painted than some royal tombs, with colors of astonishing freshness. The site is little visited and gives a glimpse into the daily life of the royal tomb builders.

Luxor

The world's greatest open-air museum stretched between Karnak and the Valley of Kings

★★★★☆
📍 Temple Hatshepsut Deir el-Bahari Luxor📍 Medinet Habu temple Ramesses III Luxor📍 Valley of the Queens Luxor📍 Tomb Nefertari QV66 Valley of the Queens📍 Ramesseum temple Ramesses II Luxor
1

Hatshepsut's mortuary temple at Deir el-Bahari backs against a 300-meter cliff that serves as its natural backdrop—the architectural spectacle is unique in Egypt. Three monumental terraces connected by ramps rise against the rock wall. The first measures 120 meters deep by 75 wide. The temple, built in 15 years by architect Senenmut, recounts on its walls the trading expedition to the Land of Punt (present-day Somalia/Eritrea)—you can see the frankincense trees transported by boat—and the queen's divine birth. Hatshepsut, the only woman to have reigned as pharaoh for 22 years, saw most of her representations hammered out by her successor Thutmose III after her death in 1458 BC. Arrive early—the temple is in full sun from 9am.

⏱ 90 min
2

Continue to Medinet Habu—Ramesses III's mortuary temple, less crowded than Karnak but whose painted reliefs are the best preserved on the entire west bank. The battle scenes against the Sea Peoples (around 1178 BC) still retain their original colors—blue, red, yellow. The ceiling of the second portico preserves traces of vivid pigments after 3,000 years. The outer enclosure, inspired by a Syrian fortress (the Migdol), is unique in Egyptian temple architecture. If you only see one 'off the beaten path' temple at Luxor, this is it.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Ramesseum, Ramesses II's mortuary temple, deserves a stop for its monumental remains—notably a broken colossus of Ramesses II that weighed over 1,000 tons and stood about 18 meters tall. This is the colossus that inspired Shelley's 1818 poem Ozymandias. The hypostyle hall still preserves part of its ceiling with astronomical paintings. The site is less restored than Karnak, giving it a romantic ruin charm.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

If your budget allows, visit the Valley of the Queens and the tomb of Nefertari (QV66)—considered the most beautiful tomb in all of Egypt for the vividness of its wall paintings. Every inch of wall is painted with exceptional finesse—Nefertari guided by the gods, scenes from the Book of the Dead, starry ceiling of deep blue. The extra fee is high and visitor numbers limited, but it's a singular moment. The Valley of the Queens holds about a hundred tombs, several of which are open with the standard ticket.

Stop 7

Aswan

Where the Nile meets Nubia in a shimmer of pink granite and white sails

★★★★☆
📍 Edfu Temple Horus Egypt📍 Kom Ombo Temple Sobek Haroeris📍 Aswan Nile corniche📍 Felucca Aswan sunset📍 Aswan souk spices henna
1

Drive from Luxor to Aswan (220 km) with two major stops. First stop: the temple of Edfu, dedicated to the falcon god Horus, is the best-preserved Ptolemaic temple in Egypt—and the second largest after Karnak. Built between 237 and 57 BC, it took 180 years to complete. Its entrance pylon, 36 meters high, bears reliefs showing Ptolemy XII smiting his enemies before Horus. Inside, the hypostyle hall with 18 columns is intact, with its ceiling still in place—which is extremely rare. Plan 1 to 1.5 hours for the visit.

⏱ 90 min
2

Second stop: the temple of Kom Ombo, spectacularly perched on a promontory overlooking the Nile. Its unique feature: it's a double temple, dedicated simultaneously to two deities—Sobek (the crocodile god) on the south side and Haroeris (Horus the Elder) on the north. Everything is symmetrical: two entrances, two sanctuaries, two series of reliefs. On the outer wall, don't miss the panel showing surgical instruments—one of the rare representations of ancient medicine. The small crocodile museum attached displays mummies of Nile crocodiles found in the area. Count on 45 minutes to an hour.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Arrival in Aswan in late afternoon. The city has a slower rhythm and a more Nubian atmosphere than Luxor—the border with historical Nubia passes here. Stroll through Aswan's souk, Egypt's most colorful: spices in pyramids (cumin, saffron, henna), woven Nubian baskets, fabrics and perfumes. Vendors are less aggressive than in Cairo. The souk stretches for several hundred meters parallel to the Nile.

⏱ 90 min

First felucca ride on the Nile at sunset—Aswan is where this experience is most beautiful. The river is dotted with granite islands, golden light falls on the sand dunes of the west bank, and white felucca sails glide in silence. Count on 1 to 1.5 hours. Negotiate the fare before boarding—feluccas depart from the south corniche.

Stop 8

Aswan

Where the Nile meets Nubia in a shimmer of pink granite and white sails

★★★★☆
📍 Philae Temple Agilkia Island Aswan📍 Trajan's Kiosk Philae📍 Unfinished Obelisk Aswan quarries📍 Aswan High Dam Lake Nasser📍 Elephantine Island Aswan Nubian village📍 Botanical Garden Kitchener Island Aswan
1

The temple of Philae, dedicated to the goddess Isis, is one of Egypt's most beautiful Ptolemaic temples—and its setting is unique. Built between 380 BC and the 3rd century AD, it stands on an island accessible only by water taxi (10-15 minutes from the Shellal dock). The temple's recent history is as extraordinary as the ancient: threatened with submersion by rising waters after construction of the Aswan High Dam, it was entirely dismantled stone by stone then reassembled on neighboring Agilkia Island between 1972 and 1980 as part of a UNESCO international campaign. Trajan's Kiosk, with its 14 columns all with different capitals, is Aswan's most photographed structure. Plan 2 to 2.5 hours including the boat crossing.

⏱ 150 min
2

The Unfinished Obelisk, in the pink granite quarries of Aswan south of the city, is a giant obelisk abandoned during carving—probably commissioned by Hatshepsut around 1450 BC. Had it been completed, it would have measured 42 meters and weighed 1,200 tons, making it Egypt's largest obelisk. A crack discovered in the rock forced the carvers to abandon it. The site is fascinating for understanding ancient Egyptian extraction techniques: you'll see tool marks, trenches carved with chisels, and traces of wooden wedges used to split the stone. Quick visit—30 to 45 minutes suffice.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Aswan High Dam (Sadd el-Aali), built between 1960 and 1970 with Soviet Union assistance, measures 3,600 meters long, 111 meters high, and 980 meters wide at the base. It created Lake Nasser, one of the world's largest artificial lakes (500 km long). The dam ended the millennial Nile floods that had regulated Egyptian agriculture since the pharaohs. The view from the top looks over Lake Nasser on one side and the Nile on the other. The stop is short—20 minutes—but the perspective is impressive.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Felucca crossing to Elephantine Island, in the middle of the Nile facing Aswan's corniche. The island hosts a Nubian village with brightly painted houses—blue, yellow, orange—and ruins of a temple of Khnum dating from the 30th Dynasty. The island's Nilometer, a graduated stairway plunging into the river, was used to measure the annual Nile flood and calculate taxes since pharaonic times. If you have time, push on to Kitchener Island (Aswan Botanical Garden), just next door—a tropical garden created by Lord Kitchener in the 19th century, pleasant for a shaded break.

Aswan

Where the Nile meets Nubia in a shimmer of pink granite and white sails

★★★★★
📍 Abu Simbel Temple Ramesses II📍 Nefertari Temple Abu Simbel📍 Lake Nasser Abu Simbel📍 UNESCO relocation Abu Simbel 1968
1

Departure at 3am from Aswan for Abu Simbel—280 km of desert road (3 to 3.5 hours), in organized convoy. Arrival at sunrise at the temples, when the raking light sets fire to the four colossi of Ramesses II. The Great Temple, carved directly into the cliff around 1265 BC, strikes with its dimensions: the facade measures 33 meters high and 38 wide. The four seated statues of Ramesses II reach 20 meters each. Inside, a hypostyle hall of eight Osirid pillars leads to the innermost sanctuary, 63 meters into the rock. Twice a year, at the equinoxes (February 22 and October 22), the sun's rays penetrate to the back of the sanctuary to illuminate three of the four seated statues—an astronomical alignment calculated 3,200 years ago.

⏱ 120 min
2

Right next door, the Small Temple of Nefertari—one of the rare Egyptian temples dedicated to a queen—is dedicated to Nefertari and the goddess Hathor. Six 10-meter statues adorn the facade: four represent Ramesses II and two Nefertari. Inside, the wall paintings showing Nefertari crowned by the goddesses are among the best preserved in Egypt. The fact that Ramesses built a temple of this scale for his wife is unprecedented in Egyptian history.

⏱ 45 min
3

The story of the temples' relocation is as extraordinary as the temples themselves. In 1964-1968, facing the rising waters of Lake Nasser, an unprecedented UNESCO operation cut the two temples into 1,042 blocks of 20 to 30 tons each, then reassembled them 65 meters higher and 210 meters back on an artificial hill. The total cost—financed by 50 countries—was considerable. Look carefully at the joints between blocks on the colossi: the cut lines are barely visible. Explanatory panels trace the operation behind the temples.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Return to Aswan in the afternoon. Enjoy the rest of the day relaxing—after the 3am wake-up, you'll have earned it. A second felucca at sunset, iced Nubian tea (karkadé) on the corniche, or dinner at a Nubian restaurant on Elephantine Island are good options for this last evening in Aswan.

Stop 10

Cairo

Where Fatimid mosques and the 1382 souk pulse alongside contemporary Cairo life

★★★★☆
📍 National Museum Egyptian Civilization NMEC Cairo📍 Hanging Church Old Cairo Coptic📍 Ben Ezra Synagogue Old Cairo📍 Fortress of Babylon Old Cairo📍 Cairo International Airport CAI
1

Morning flight Aswan → Cairo (1h15). Use this last day to explore Coptic Old Cairo, a neighborhood most tours neglect. The Fortress of Babylon, built by the Romans in the 1st century, houses within its walls the Hanging Church (Al-Moallaqa)—one of Egypt's oldest churches, whose nave is literally suspended above a passageway of the fortress. Its carved wooden ceilings and Coptic icons date from the 13th century. Right next door, Ben Ezra Synagogue (9th century) occupies the spot where, according to tradition, Moses was found in the Nile reeds.

⏱ 90 min
2

The National Museum of Egyptian Civilization (NMEC), opened since 2021 in the Fustat district, is the perfect complement to GEM. Where GEM focuses on pharaonic Egypt, NMEC covers all of history—from early humans to the 20th century, passing through Coptic, Islamic, and modern periods. The Royal Mummies Hall, in the basement, presents 22 pharaoh mummies (including Ramesses II, Seti I, Hatshepsut) in optimal conservation conditions—soft lighting, controlled temperature. The experience is more intimate and respectful than at the old Cairo museum. Count on 2 hours.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Transfer to Cairo International Airport (CAI) for the return flight. The airport is 30-45 minutes from downtown depending on traffic—which can be chaotic in Cairo during rush hours. Allow plenty of time. The airport offers direct flights to Paris CDG, Lyon, Marseille, as well as connections to Europe and the rest of the world.
