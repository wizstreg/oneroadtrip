Sevilla → Carmona → Cordoba → Medina Azahara → Jaen → Baeza → Ubeda → Granada → Lanjaron → Pampaneira → Bubion → Capileira → Trevelez

Ten days to hold both threads of Al-Andalus: the caliphal and Nasrid legacy of Córdoba and Granada, and Vandelvira's Iberian Renaissance in Úbeda and Baeza. Day 1

Sevilla

1

Built from 1402 onward atop the great Almohad mosque, Seville Cathedral is the world's largest Gothic cathedral—135 meters long, roughly a city block and a half. The Giralda, its bell tower, is the former 12th-century Almohad minaret extended into a Renaissance tower: 104 meters high, 35 levels of ramps (no stairs—riding up on horseback was possible). Book ahead and avoid midday in summer.

⏱ 120 min
2

One of Europe's oldest working royal palaces: the Real Alcázar remains the official Seville residence of the Spanish royal family. Its foundations date to the 11th century, expanded by Pedro I in the 14th in pure Mudéjar style, accumulating architectural layers across several centuries. Booking recommended. Allow at least two hours.

⏱ 120 min
3

The Museo de Bellas Artes de Sevilla occupies a former 17th-century convent and holds one of the country's most important collections of Spanish Baroque painting—Zurbarán, Murillo, Valdés Leal. Less crowded than the cathedral, often empty in the morning. Free entry for EU citizens.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The scent of orange blossoms, the heat of limestone alleys, laundry hanging from windows—the Santa Cruz quarter delivers pre-boulevard Seville. Two hours of free wandering between the cathedral and the Alcázar. In April, the flowers and the Feria make it the year's most intense moment.

⏱ 90 min

The Triana Market (Mercado de Triana), set in a repurposed 19th-century castle on the left bank of the Guadalquivir, is the best spot to taste local products: olives, cheeses, Andalusian cured meats, tapas standing up. Open mornings until 3pm, closed Sundays.

⏱ 60 min
Day 2

Sevilla

1

Backdrop of an empire, now a galactic film set: the Plaza de España, built in 1928 for the 1929 Ibero-American Exposition, is a 50,000-square-meter half-moon adorned with tiled benches representing each Spanish province. George Lucas shot Naboo scenes here for Star Wars Episode II. In the morning, before 10am, it's nearly empty.

⏱ 60 min
2

Sevillanos say Triana isn't really Seville—and they're right in the best sense. The far bank of the Guadalquivir has its own character: historic cradle of flamenco, ceramicists' quarter, covered market, balconies heavy with flowers. A morning is enough to grasp its tone.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

If you only see one flamenco show the entire trip, choose the Casa de la Memoria in Seville over a tourist tablao. Intimate room, local artists, no commercial staging. Booking essential—seats sell out days ahead.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 35 km · 40min
Day 3

Carmona

1

The red clay of the ramparts burns in late-morning light. Carmona has perched on a promontory since the Bronze Age. Its two monumental gates—Seville to the west, Córdoba to the east—have Punic foundations reinforced by Rome, then modified by the Umayyads. Three civilizations stacked in the same stone.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Alcázar del Rey Don Pedro, at the eastern tip of the promontory, is an Arab-origin fortress transformed by Pedro I of Castile in the 14th century. Now a luxury hotel, the mirador entrance is still free—the view from the battlements over the Sevillian plain is worth the stop alone.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Two centuries before Christ, Carmona buried its dead outside the walls in underground chambers carved into bedrock, some fitted with niches for cinerary urns. The 1st-century Roman necropolis, which yielded the Tomb of the Elephant and the Tomb of Servilia, is one of the peninsula's best-preserved Roman burial complexes. Allow 90 minutes. Closed Mondays.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 105 km · 1h10
Day 4

Cordoba

1

To enter the Mezquita is to enter two centuries of layered construction. Abd ar-Rahmân I launched the works in 785 atop the ruins of a Visigothic church. The result: 856 columns of marble, jasper, and granite with their red-and-white bicolor arches. In 1523, a Gothic-Renaissance cathedral was inserted at its heart. Charles V, after the fact: 'You have built here what could be built anywhere, and you have destroyed what was unique in the world.' Online ticketing advised. Allow 2 hours.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Alcázar de los Reyes Cristianos, a stone's throw from the Mezquita, is a 14th-century fortress with spectacular irrigated gardens—pools, trimmed cypresses, water jets. Here Christopher Columbus presented his project to Ferdinand and Isabella before the 1492 departure. Modest entry fee, open mornings.

⏱ 60 min
3

Córdoba's Roman bridge, 247 meters long on sixteen arches over the Guadalquivir, dates to the Augustan era and has been rebuilt several times. Free, accessible all hours. The view from the bridge's midpoint onto the Mezquita and Torre Calahorra is the postcard everyone's looking for.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Get lost in the Judería before the midday crowds. The medieval Jewish quarter, tucked against the Mezquita, keeps its whitewashed alleys and geranium-filled patios. The Calleja de las Flores leads to the classic postcard shot—wrought iron, flowers, minaret in back. Allow an hour of free wandering.

⏱ 60 min

An olive oil tasting in one of the many specialized shops in Córdoba's historic center—Jaén province produces a significant share of Spain's olive oil, and local varieties (picual, hojiblanca) deserve a stop. Some workshops offer 30-minute comparative sessions.

⏱ 30 min
Day 5

Cordoba

1

Cordobans simply call it 'the palace of patios': the Palacio de Viana, built in the 14th–15th centuries and expanded over five centuries, concentrates twelve interior courtyards, each different in style and flora. Orange trees, bougainvillea, geraniums. The interior preserves original furnishings from the noble families who succeeded each other here. During the May patio festival, the waiting list grows long.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Synagogue of Córdoba, built in 1315, is one of three medieval synagogues preserved in Spain along with those in Toledo. It's tiny—barely 70 square meters of prayer hall—but its Mudéjar stuccowork and Hebrew inscriptions are intact. Free entry for EU citizens.

⏱ 20 min
3

The Torre de la Calahorra, a 13th-century Islamic fortress at the entrance to the Roman bridge, houses a multimedia museum on the coexistence of three cultures in caliphal Córdoba. Conventional but effective for contextualizing what you've just seen. Paid entry, around €5.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In May for the patio festival (UNESCO Intangible Heritage), the Palacio de Viana's courtyard rivals the private patios of the neighborhood—residents open their homes for the competition. Outside festival time, the walk through the northern streets of Córdoba toward Santa Marina is far less crowded than the Judería.

⏱ 90 min

The Mercado Victoria, in a rehabilitated 1877 modernist kiosk, is the safest bet for dinner in Córdoba: about twenty stalls offering tapas, salmorejo, rabo de toro, local cheeses. Open daily until midnight. Stand-up or seated drinks.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 8 km · 15min
Day 6

Medina Azahara

1

Founded in 936, razed in 1010, rediscovered in 1911, inscribed as UNESCO in 2018—the trajectory of Medina Azahara sums up the fragility of the Córdoba caliphate. Abd ar-Rahmân III had an entire city built here as the caliphate's seat and a symbol of his power against the Abbasids. Mandatory shuttle from the visitor center. Allow 2 hours on site.

⏱ 120 min
2

The site museum, in the Medina Azahara visitor center, displays the best-preserved pieces unearthed since 1911—carved capitals, marble panels with vegetal decoration, caliphal ceramics. Allow 30 minutes before or after the archaeological site visit. Included in the entry ticket.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The Salón Rico (the caliph's reception hall), partially reconstructed from excavations, gives an idea of Medina Azahara's refinement at its peak: polychrome marbles, geometric stuccos, mercury pools that reflected light on the walls. Ask the guide to point out the marble scars—they show where 11th-century looters did their work.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 105 km · 1h15
Day 7

Jaen

1

Vandelvira had a problem: Jaén Cathedral is too large for the city that contains it. The architect's last great 16th-century work, it became the formal template for many Latin American cathedrals. On the UNESCO indicative list. Its twin-towered façade, 63 meters high, dominates from any point in the city.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Castillo de Santa Catalina, an Arab fortress dominating Jaén from 330 meters elevation, is now partly a parador. The terrace is freely accessible and offers the widest view over the sea of olive trees covering the province—millions of trees as far as the eye can see. Drive up recommended, 15 minutes from downtown.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Beneath the slabs of a 16th-century palace, 450 square meters of interconnected rooms—cool, dark, perfumed with damp stone. The Arab baths of the Palacio de Villardompardo, built in the 11th century, are the most extensive you can visit in Spain. A viceroy of Peru buried them beneath his palace in the 16th—a fortunate mistake. Free entry. Closed Mondays.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 50 km · 40min
Day 8

Baeza

1

Inscribed as UNESCO in 2003 along with Úbeda, Baeza was primarily an ecclesiastical and university center in the 16th century. The Plaza del Pópulo is the opening act: repurposed Roman fountain, former grain market, Gothic-Mudéjar façade. The cathedral, once again by Vandelvira, juxtaposes three styles over five centuries. Allow 2 hours for a stroll.

⏱ 120 min
2

The University of Baeza, founded in 1538 and now the seat of the Universidad Internacional de Andalucía, is one of the Iberian Peninsula's first higher-education institutions. Its Renaissance courtyard can be freely visited. Antonio Machado taught French here from 1912 to 1919—his classroom is preserved.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Wander to the Plaza de Santa María, Baeza's quiet heart—terraces, Renaissance palaces in golden sandstone, 16th-century fountain. Tourist activity stays light compared to Úbeda: take advantage for a peaceful lunch before hitting the road again.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 9 km · 15min
Day 9

Ubeda

1

Lost in Jaén province, one of Europe's finest Renaissance squares waits to be found. The Plaza Vázquez de Molina, composed between 1530 and 1580, is Vandelvira's and his patrons' urbanistic manifesto. Carved from local ochre sandstone, it has the coherence of an open-air room.

⏱ 60 min
2

Francisco de los Cobos had ambition: a funerary chapel for himself alone, on the town's best square. The Sacra Capilla del Salvador, commissioned from Vandelvira by Charles V's secretary, is the most accomplished monument of Iberian humanism. The sacristy alone summarizes Spanish Renaissance sculpture.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Casa de las Torres, a 16th-century Plateresque palace two minutes from the main square, is Úbeda's most exuberant example of fantastical decoration—façade covered with grotesque motifs, medallions, and atlantes. Now an art school, the patio is freely accessible during the day.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Dubbed the Andalusian Escorial, the Hospital de Santiago—completed after Vandelvira's death in 1575 by his students—closes out Úbeda's monumental ensemble away from the main square. Now a performance hall and congress palace: you can freely enter the double-gallery patio. A few minutes' walk from the square.

⏱ 30 min

Úbeda is also Spain's capital of green-glazed artisan pottery—a tradition dating back to the Islamic period. The potters' quarter, around Calle Valencia, groups about ten workshops open for visits. Juan Tito, one of the best-known names, has run his workshop-boutique for three generations.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 145 km · 1h45
Day 10

Granada

1

The arabesque unfolds to the point of vertigo: stucco, azulejos, carved cedar wood—the Nasrid Palaces are the culmination of five centuries of Arab-Andalusian architecture. Built primarily in the 14th–15th centuries, they comprise the Mexuar, the Comares Palace, and the Palace of the Lions. Booking mandatory weeks in advance, time slot imposed for the Nasrid section. Allow 4 hours minimum for the entire site.

⏱ 240 min
2

If you only have one sunset slot over the red fortress, it's the Mirador de San Nicolás in the Albaicín. Arrive 30 minutes before sunset to find space. The alleys below are worth exploring for Nasrid civilian architecture still visible in the cármenes.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Palace of Carlos V, a 1527 Renaissance construction at the very heart of the Alhambra, is the only circular palace inscribed in a square in Spanish architecture—30 meters in interior diameter, two superimposed galleries. It now houses the Museo de la Alhambra (Nasrid ceramics) and the Museo de Bellas Artes. Included in the site ticket.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Locals say 'Jannat al-Arif,' the architect's garden: the Generalife, the Nasrid sultans' summer palace, is as famous for its pools and cypress hedges as for its architecture. A ticket separate from the Nasrid Palaces gives access to the gardens and the Alcazaba. Climb via the Paseo de los Cipreses on the way back.

⏱ 90 min

A hammam in the Albaicín quarter completes the Nasrid day—the Baños Árabes Hammam Al Andalus, set in a house in Granada's historic center, offers 90-minute thermal circuits in pools of varying temperatures. Booking mandatory. Sober, authentic atmosphere.

⏱ 90 min
Day 11

Granada

1

Isabella the Catholic wrote in her will how the chapel where she wished to rest should be completed. Built from 1506 onward in Isabelline Gothic style, the Capilla Real houses the white marble effigies of Isabella, Ferdinand, Joanna the Mad, and Philip the Handsome, sculpted by Domenico Fancelli. The sacristy preserves the rulers' personal crowns, scepter, and sword. Allow 45 minutes.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Alcaicería, the former Nasrid silk market rebuilt in the 19th century after a fire, is now a covered street of craft shops between the cathedral and Plaza Bib-Rambla. Less authentic than in earlier times, but the original bazaar's urban fabric is still legible in the layout of the alleys.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Head up to Sacromonte in the evening for a cave show: the caves carved into tuff have sheltered Granada's gypsy families and their flamenco for centuries. The setting bears no resemblance to a downtown tablao. Book directly with neighborhood groups to avoid overloaded tourist packages.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 45 km · 50min
Day 12

Lanjaron

1

Lanjarón water has a taste: light, cold, slightly mineral—the water sold in bottles all over Spain comes from this town's springs at the gateway to the Alpujarras. The ruined castle on the northern promontory, called the 'Moors' castle,' offers a panorama over the valley. According to legend, its last defender threw himself from the tower rather than surrender to the Catholic Monarchs in 1490. An hour is enough here.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Balneario de Lanjarón, a thermal establishment open since the 19th century, uses water from the town's six sulfurous and bicarbonate springs. A day pass to the baths (basic thermal circuit) is offered without an overnight stay requirement—a good break before climbing into the Alpujarras.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Mirador del Castillo, a few minutes' walk from the center, gives the clearest overview of Lanjarón and the Guadalfeo valley toward the sea. A marked trail from the main street climbs to the castle ruins in 20 minutes—rocky path but no difficulty.

⏱ 45 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 18 km · 30min
Day 13

Pampaneira

1

Pampaneira is touristy. We know it, and we don't care: at 1,058 meters in the Poqueira gorge, this Morisco village classified as a historic ensemble keeps its flat roofs in slate slabs, its tubular chimneys, and its Mudéjar church Santa Cruz, the finest of the three gorge villages. The architecture resembles Rif and Atlas villages—same Berber heritage.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Santa Cruz church in Pampaneira, built in the 16th century in Mudéjar style, has preserved its coffered ceiling in mulberry wood. Inside, sober and cool. The San Antonio fountain on the square, called the bachelors' fountain, is said to help those who drink from it find a partner—the tradition is still cited seriously by elders.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Shops selling jarapas (Alpujarran weavings made from recovered fabric scraps) line Pampaneira's alleys. It's not high craft, but it's authentically local and cheap. The Hilacar workshop, in operation for several decades, is one of the few still weaving on traditional looms—visits are free.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 3 km · 8min
Day 14

Bubion

1

Less visited than its neighbors, Bubión has woven its rugs for centuries. At 1,296 meters, the quietest village in the gorge lived for a long time on textile craft—the jarapas alpujarreñas, colorful weavings from recovered fabric scraps, are still made here. Its 16th-century Mudéjar church is more sober than Pampaneira's. An hour, time for a coffee and a tour of the workshops.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Casa Alpujarreña de Bubión, a small house-museum in a restored traditional dwelling, shows how Morisco then Christian families lived in these villages: slate roofs, wood-fire kitchen, chestnut furniture. Symbolic entry fee, often staffed by local volunteers.

⏱ 25 min

Activities

The ridge trail between Bubión and Capileira (about 45 minutes uphill) offers the best views of the Poqueira gorge—the ravine below, the Sierra Nevada's residual glaciers across. Marked path, no technical difficulty. Start early morning for light and cool air.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 3 km · 8min
Day 15

Capileira

1

At 1,436 m, Capileira is the highest starting point for Mulhacén—3,479 meters, the Iberian Peninsula's summit, reachable on foot from the village in a long day. The Museo Etnológico Pedro Antonio Alarcón, a small collection of Morisco tools and costumes, gives the cultural key to the entire region in 30 minutes.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Capileira mirador, at the village's northern tip, is the highest viewpoint over the Poqueira gorge accessible without hiking—direct view over the three villages below, the ravine, and on clear days to the Almería sea. Ten minutes' walk from the main square.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

The PR-A 70 trail links the three villages via the ridges in 4 to 5 hours: descent from Capileira to Bubión (45 min), then Bubión to Pampaneira (45 min), with permanent views over the gorge and the Sierra Nevada glaciers. It's the most sensible way to visit all three villages—start at the top and work down.

⏱ 270 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 25 km · 50min
Day 16

Trevelez

1

Here, the air itself smells of ham. At 1,476 meters, Trevélez is continental Spain's highest village—the altitude, cold, and dry Sierra Nevada air create ideal conditions for curing PGI jamón serrano for over a century. The village is built on three stacked barrios (quarters); the path from bottom to top is a workout.

⏱ 60 min
2

The church of San Antonio de Trevélez, in the barrio alto, is a small 18th-century church preserving a statue of the Virgin carried in procession every August 5th to celebrate the Mulhacén festival—hundreds of hikers climb at night to reach the summit at dawn. The church terrace gives the best view over the three barrios.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Don't resist the tabla de jamón at lunch in a bodega in the lower quarter: Trevélez PGI jamón, bread with oil, local wine. Simple and right. Guided tours of the secaderos (curing houses) are offered by the Vallejo family—the oldest in the business—and last about an hour.

⏱ 90 min
