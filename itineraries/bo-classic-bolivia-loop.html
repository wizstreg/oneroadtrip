Bolivia Road Trip: Uyuni Salt Flats and Andean Route

📅 11 days📍 10 stops🚗 2067 km⏱ 25h35

Discover Lake Titicaca and Isla del Sol, vertiginous La Paz at 11,800 feet, the Amazon jungle from Rurrenabaque, gastronomic Cochabamba, white Sucre, the mines of Potosí, and the immensity of the Uyuni salt flats. Bolivia in one journey.

✨ Highlights
  • Isla del Sol: north-to-south trek across the Incas' sacred island on Lake Titicaca
  • Death Road: 40-mile bike descent from 15,420 feet into cloud forest
  • Rurrenabaque: pink river dolphins and alligators in the Yacuma pampas
  • Potosí: tour the Cerro Rico mines at 13,353 feet elevation
  • Salar de Uyuni: sunset over the world's largest salt desert
📆 Best period

May, June, July, August, September

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 9 and 13 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Dry Season (May-October): Best weather overall, minimal rainfall, clearer skies, ideal for Salar de Uyuni, peak tourist season.

Wet Season (November-April): Heavy rains, challenging road conditions, Salar de Uyuni becomes mirror-like, lush landscapes, fewer tourists.

Shoulder Seasons (April-May, October-November): Good weather transitions, moderate crowds, reasonable prices, excellent for most activities.

High Altitude Considerations: La Paz and Altiplano best visited during dry season, less altitude sickness risk with clear weather.

🌍 What to discover in BO?

World's highest capital and salt flats create otherworldly landscapes alongside ancient Incan heritage. Experience authentic indigenous culture in dramatic Andean settings with incredible value for money. Lake Titicaca's floating islands and death road adventures thrill visitors. Bolivia offers authentic South American experiences with stunning natural phenomena.

Day 1

Copacabana

Where pilgrims gather between the sacred lake and snow-capped Andes

★★★☆☆
1

The Basilica of Our Lady of Copacabana stands gleaming white on Plaza 2 de Febrero. Built in 1601 in Mannerist style, it houses the Dark Virgin of Copacabana, patron saint of Bolivia, installed in 1583 within a richly decorated altar.

⏱ 45 min
2

Head to the basilica forecourt to witness the blessing of the cars. Bolivian families decorate their vehicles and have them sprinkled with holy water to gain the Virgin's protection—a daily ritual unique in the world.

⏱ 20 min
3

Climb Cerro El Calvario: 400 feet of elevation gain marked by crosses and votive altars. At the summit, 12,990 feet above sea level, Copacabana Bay and the immensity of Lake Titicaca spread before you, with the snow-capped peaks of the Cordillera Real as backdrop.

⏱ 60 min
4

Stroll along the lakefront and its wooden piers. Local fishermen unload their trout; beachside restaurants cook them with garlic, butter, or as ceviche. Lake Titicaca sits here at 12,507 feet elevation—the world's highest navigable lake.

⏱ 30 min
5

Copacabana's market occupies the corner of Pando and Jauregui streets. Aymara Cholitas in layered skirts and bowler hats sell fresh produce, coca, and local handicrafts. An essential stop before heading to the boat docks.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Kayak or pedal boat excursion on Lake Titicaca from the harbor. The water, an intense blue at this altitude, offers a unique perspective on the bay and mountains.

⏱ 60 min

Hike along the Yampupata peninsula: take a taxi to Sampaya (20 min), then walk the ridges overlooking the lake for 2 to 3 hours to the village of Yampupata, where boats depart for Isla del Sol.

⏱ 60 min

Sample lake trout at small waterfront restaurants. Trout with garlic, butter, or as ceviche—the fish are pulled from the water that very morning. Ispis, tiny fried fish sold on the Tiquina docks during the crossing, are worth trying too.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 22 km · 1h30
Day 2

Isla Del Sol

Where the Inca sun god was born on an island of stone and legend

★★★★★
1

In the island's north, the Chinkana Labyrinth is Isla del Sol's great ceremonial complex. Its stone corridors reportedly served as a training center for Inca priests. Nearby, the Sacred Rock shaped like a puma is the legendary site where, according to Inca mythology, Manco Cápac and Mama Ocllo emerged to found the empire.

⏱ 50 min
2

At Yumani, the Inca Stairway counts 206 steps carved into the rock. It leads to the Sacred Fountain, a three-spouted water source the Incas considered a font of eternal youth and spiritual purity.

⏱ 30 min
3

The palace of Pilkokaina clings to the island's southern point. Built by Inca Tupac Yupanqui, only one floor remains of this two-story palace—but the trapezoidal doorways and wall niches characteristic of Inca architecture are intact. The terraces plunge toward the lake.

⏱ 40 min
4

The island holds about a hundred ruins scattered among Inca agricultural terraces, pastures, and eucalyptus-scented paths. The silence is total: no cars, no motors—just wind and lapping waves.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

North-to-south trek: take the morning boat to Challapampa in the north, then walk to Yumani in the south along 6 to 7 miles of ridgeline. The route passes ruins and offers 360° panoramas of the lake and snow-capped Andean peaks. Allow 3 to 5 hours depending on your pace.

⏱ 60 min

Sunset from the heights of Yumani. At 12,470 feet, the low-angle light turns the lake copper and the Andean peaks violet silhouettes. Arrive at least 30 minutes early.

⏱ 60 min

Overnight on the island in simple accommodation in Yumani or Challapampa. At 12,470 feet, the night sky is pitch black. The southern hemisphere stars, without light pollution, are breathtaking.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 155 km · 5h
Day 3

La Paz

A city that climbs, breathless and beautiful, into the clouds

★★★★★
1

Mirador Killi Killi, perched on Santa Bárbara hill, offers the most complete panorama of La Paz. Below, red roofs stack up in the canyon down to El Alto on the plateau. In the distance, the snow-capped peaks of the Cordillera Real close the horizon.

⏱ 30 min
2

Mercado de las Brujas (Witches' Market) stretches along Calle Linares between Sagarnaga and Santa Cruz streets. Stalls overflow with dried llama fetuses, magic stones, medicinal herbs, and mysterious powders: offerings to Pachamama and Aymara remedies. The yatiris, wearing black hats, read the future in coca leaves.

⏱ 50 min
3

San Francisco Church dominates the square of the same name. Its 18th-century mestizo baroque façade blends indigenous motifs with Spanish ornamentation. The interior nave houses gilded sculptures and religious paintings. The bell tower offers stunning views over La Paz.

⏱ 40 min
4

Plaza Murillo is the political heart of Bolivia. The neoclassical Government Palace, Legislative Palace, and 19th-century metropolitan cathedral frame it. Illuminated in the evening, the square bustles with newspaper vendors, masked shoe-shiners, and pigeons.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Ride the Mi Teleférico network, the world's largest urban cable car system with 18 miles of cable. The red line connects downtown to El Alto; the yellow line descends toward Sopocachi. From the cabins, La Paz reveals itself in all its enormity: the colorful Chualluma neighborhood and its street art murals, terraced markets, snow-capped summits.

⏱ 60 min

Walking tour of the Chualluma neighborhood: its facades covered with giant murals rival Medellín's Comuna 13. Descend from the Cementerio cable car station and climb back up through the sloping alleys on foot.

⏱ 60 min
Day 4

La Paz

A city that climbs, breathless and beautiful, into the clouds

★★★★★
1

Calle Jaén is La Paz's best-preserved colonial street. Its ochre and blue façade houses several small museums: the Museo del Litoral, Museo de Metales Preciosos, and Museo de la Coca. A peaceful interlude amid the urban whirlwind.

⏱ 45 min
2

Valle de la Luna sits 6 miles from downtown. Erosion has sculpted the clay and sandstone into needle formations, miniature canyons, and pinnacles reminiscent of a lunar surface. Marked trails allow a 45-minute circuit through these otherworldly landscapes.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Bike descent of the Yungas Road, nicknamed 'Death Road.' 40 miles of elevation drop from 15,420 feet to the Amazon foothills, along vertiginous cliffs, waterfalls, and cloud forest. An experience for adrenaline seekers.

⏱ 60 min

Cholitas Wrestling show in El Alto, every Sunday afternoon. These women in layered skirts and long braids battle in a wrestling ring in a colorful spectacle. Beyond the show, it's a powerful message of Bolivian women's empowerment.

⏱ 60 min

Feria 16 de Julio in El Alto, every Thursday and Sunday, is one of South America's largest open-air markets: electronics, textiles, medicinal herbs, auto parts across several miles of streets. Combine it with the Cholitas on Sunday.

⏱ 60 min

Hike in Valle de las Ánimas, 30 minutes from downtown. This phantasmagoric canyon harbors rock formations shaped like obelisks and pre-Columbian worship sites. According to local beliefs, the protective spirits Achachilas reside here.

⏱ 60 min
Day 5

La Paz

A city that climbs, breathless and beautiful, into the clouds

★★★★★
1

Before leaving La Paz for the jungle, head back up to Mirador Killi Killi one last time. A farewell view of the canyon and stacked roofs tumbling to the bottom—the contrast with tomorrow's Amazonian vegetation will be striking.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Museo Nacional de Etnografía y Folklore (MUSEF), Calle Ingavi, occupies an 18th-century baroque palace. Ritual textiles, Tiwanaku carnival masks, and pre-Columbian ceramics trace 3,000 years of Andean cultures before you embark for the jungle.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Transfer La Paz → Rurrenabaque: TAM flight (~45 min) or overnight bus via the Yungas road (~18h). The flight is highly recommended—Rurrenabaque's landing strip, framed by jungle and the Beni River, is renowned as one of the world's most spectacular.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 420 km · 50min
Day 6

Rurrenabaque

Gateway to pink river dolphins and the wild heart of Madidi

★★☆☆☆
1

Rurrenabaque sits on the Beni River's bank, a 45-minute flight from La Paz. This small town's laid-back tropical atmosphere contrasts radically with the Altiplano. It's the jumping-off point for two wild nature regions: Madidi National Park to the west (dense jungle) and the Pampas del Yacuma to the east (open wetlands).

⏱ 30 min
2

Madidi National Park is one of the world's most biodiverse areas. Accessible in 2.5 hours by dugout canoe from Rurrenabaque, it spans 4.5 million acres of primary rainforest, Andean valleys, and grasslands. Over 1,000 bird species have been recorded here.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

3-day excursion into Madidi Park. Boat journey on the Beni and Tuichi rivers to a community ecolodge. The program: hikes on themed trails (medicinal plants, giant trees), spotting spider monkeys, peccaries, and macaws, and building a balsa raft on the Tuichi River. Jungle nights echo with howler monkeys.

⏱ 60 min

3-day excursion into the Pampas del Yacuma. Dugout canoe navigation on the Yacuma River and its tributaries to observe wildlife in broad daylight: caimans lounging on the banks, pink river dolphins surfacing, capybaras at the water's edge, anacondas hunted on foot through the wetlands. The difference from the jungle: here, the grassy vegetation allows you to see far.

⏱ 60 min

Piranha fishing in the Pampa del Yacuma lagoons. Local guides provide lines and explain techniques. These carnivorous fish, despite their reputation, only pose real danger if you have an open wound in the water.

⏱ 60 min

Sunrise from the dugout canoe on the Yacuma River. At 5:30am, howler monkeys announce the day from riverside trees, followed by macaw and parrot calls. The pink light on the calm river and mist rising from the water offer an unforgettable silent spectacle.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 420 km · 50min
Day 7

Cochabamba

Where Bolivia's soul is served on a plate beneath eternal spring

★★★★☆
1

Cristo de la Concordia stands atop San Pedro hill 870 feet above the city. At 130 feet total height (statue + base), it surpasses Rio's Christ the Redeemer and is South America's tallest. The cable car ride up from Avenida Heroínas offers progressive panoramas across the entire fertile Cochabamba valley and Tunari mountain range. On Sundays, you can go inside the statue.

⏱ 60 min
2

Palacio Portales is the masterpiece of French architect Eugène Bliault, built between 1915 and 1927 for tin baron Simón Patiño—one of the world's richest men in his time. Marble imported from Europe, precious woodwork, French-style gardens: a demonstration of Bolivian mining opulence. Patiño never lived there. In 1964, the palace hosted General de Gaulle on an official visit.

⏱ 60 min
3

Plaza 14 de Septiembre is the colonial heart of Cochabamba. The metropolitan cathedral, begun in 1542 as a stone and adobe structure, has been rebuilt and expanded over the centuries. Its arcades and bell tower frame a lively square where chess players and shoe-shiners coexist under palm trees.

⏱ 40 min
4

La Cancha market sprawls across several avenues south of the city. South America's largest open-air market, according to local sources, overflows with fruits and vegetables from the valley, textiles, handicrafts, and ritual objects like llama fetuses. The bustle peaks on Wednesdays and Saturdays.

⏱ 60 min
5

Convento de Santa Teresa, from the 17th century, is a baroque complex with a grand central patio and tower. The guided tour (45 min) provides access to cells, the chapel, and colonial religious art collections—a striking contrast to the nearby market's commotion.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Lunch in the Recoleta neighborhood or on El Prado: Cochabamba is renowned as Bolivia's culinary capital. Silpancho (breaded beef, rice, potatoes, egg), pique macho (fries, beef, sausages, egg, peppers), or chankao de pollo—portions are generous.

⏱ 60 min

Ascent to Cristo de la Concordia on foot via 2,000 steps if you're fit, or by cable car Tuesday through Sunday. The summit offers 360° views of the fertile valley framed by mountains.

⏱ 60 min

Chicha in Tiquipaya: 9 miles from Cochabamba, this village is the heart of chicha production, fermented corn beer sacred since Inca times. Chicherías fly a white or red flag to signal they're open.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 340 km · 8h
Day 8

Sucre

The white city where independence was born in baroque splendor

★★★★★
1

Plaza 25 de Mayo is Sucre's heart. The 16th-century metropolitan cathedral and immaculate colonial buildings frame it. In the evening, locals gather in traditional or modern dress—an authentic scene of Bolivian life not to be missed.

⏱ 30 min
2

Casa de la Libertad is Bolivia's most important historical monument. It was in the chapel of this former Jesuit monastery built in 1621 that Bolivia's independence was proclaimed on August 6, 1825. The Independence Hall preserves the original document, portraits of liberators, and two of Bolívar's swords. The guided tour is essential to grasp the 50 presidents of the Republic parading across the next room's walls.

⏱ 75 min
3

San Felipe Neri church allows you to climb onto the city's rooftops. From there, the panorama over ochre tile roofs, white bell towers, and the green hills surrounding Sucre is exceptional—one of the city's finest views.

⏱ 40 min
4

La Recoleta convent dominates the southern heights of the city. Its colonial galleries, flowering patios, and a 1,400-year-old cedar tree proclaimed a Bolivian historical monument make it a peaceful haven. The Recoleta viewpoint on Plaza Pedro de Anzúrez offers spectacular sunset views.

⏱ 50 min
5

ASUR Museum (Arte y Cultura de Textiles) exhibits hand-woven textiles by indigenous communities from the Chuquisaca region. Each piece tells a cosmology: the Jalq'a geometric patterns from the north represent the chaos of the underworld; those from Tarabuco in the south illustrate victory against the conquistadors. A masterpiece of Bolivian textile art.

⏱ 45 min
6

Parque Cretácico Cal Orck'o, 3 miles from downtown, displays 5,000 dinosaur footprints on a vertical limestone wall 260 feet high. It's the world's largest dinosaur track site, with 294 identified species.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Tarabuco artisan market, 40 miles from Sucre, every Sunday morning. Yampara communities sell their textiles with unique warrior motifs, musical instruments, and traditional dishes. Arrive early to encounter peasants in traditional costume descended from the villages.

⏱ 60 min

Hike to the Maragua crater in the Cordillera de los Frailes, 31 miles from Sucre. A full day's walk (7h round trip) leads to the edge of this spectacular geological crater, among corn fields, dinosaur tracks, and Andean landscapes.

⏱ 60 min

Stop at chocolate artisans in downtown Sucre: the city produces some of Bolivia's finest chocolate from yungas cacao. Workshops on Arenales street let you watch production and taste bars, pralines, and singani truffles.

⏱ 60 min

Morning at Sucre's Central Market: fresh-pressed fruit juices, grilled chorizos chuquisaqueños, and hot salteñas. Arrive before 9am—salteñas sell out before 10am.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 155 km · 4h
Day 9

Potosi

Where mountains bled silver and built empires

★★★★☆
1

Arrival in Potosí (13,353 ft) from Sucre (9,153 ft): an ascent of 4,200 feet in ~3h by bus. Take the first two hours to acclimatize at the hotel before any activity. Coca tea, hydration, no alcohol or intense physical effort before afternoon.

⏱ 30 min
2

Casa Nacional de la Moneda was built in 1572 to mint silver coins destined for the Spanish Empire. Its colonial wooden machinery, heavy mills, and baroque sacred art galleries form one of South America's richest museums. Between 1545 and 1810, Potosí's mines produced some 150,000 tons of silver. The guided tour lasts 2 hours. ⚠️ The gallery visit is physically demanding: temperatures of 113°F in some tunnels, silica dust, crawling passages. Reserved for people in good physical shape without respiratory issues. Travelers preferring to skip the mine will find in Casa de la Moneda a first-rate cultural alternative.

⏱ 120 min
3

Cerro Rico (Rich Mountain) dominates Potosí at 15,689 feet. Its flank is riddled with gallery entrances—miners still work there today extracting tin and zinc, in the same tunnels dug since 1545. At night, the small lights of miners' lamps slowly descend the mountain.

⏱ 30 min
4

San Lorenzo church (18th century) is the jewel of Andean baroque in Potosí. Its carved pink stone façade blends indigenous and Christian motifs with exceptionally rich detail. From the terraces of the neighboring San Francisco convent, the view of Cerro Rico and the old town's colorful roofs is striking.

⏱ 40 min
5

Santa Teresa convent, founded in 1685, opens for guided tours. Its silent cloisters, colonial sacred art collections—including a Virgin by Murillo—and gilded altars evoke Potosí's extreme wealth at its peak.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Guided tour of Cerro Rico's active mines. Enter the galleries where miners still extract tin and zinc under extreme conditions. Before descending, stop at the miners' market to buy offerings for the workers: coca leaves, dynamite, sugarcane juice. Inside, meet El Tío, the devilish figure who protects men of the underworld.

⏱ 60 min

Walk through the UNESCO-listed historic center. Cobblestone streets, colonial houses with carved wooden balconies, and 36 baroque churches testify to the city's splendor in the 16th century, when Potosí had 160,000 inhabitants—as many as Paris or London at the time.

⏱ 60 min

Thermal bath at Ojo del Inca, a natural hot spring a few miles from downtown. A welcome break after physical exertion at 13,000 feet altitude.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 205 km · 4h30
Day 10

Uyuni

Where earth becomes mirror and the horizon dissolves in salt

★★★☆☆
1

The train cemetery sits 2 miles from Uyuni. Some fifty locomotives and cars from the early 20th century rust on abandoned tracks. These trains hauled ore from the Andean mines to Chilean ports—a grand Bolivian railway network project that never came to be.

⏱ 40 min
2

In Colchani, the village at the salar's gates, artisans work salt extracted from the flats: decorative objects, salt plates, raw furniture. A small artisan refinery explains the salt transformation stages. Just in front, the Ojos de Sal—underground water resurgences piercing the crust—reveal the geothermal activity beneath the desert.

⏱ 30 min
3

Incahuasi Island rises from the salar's absolute whiteness at its center. A remnant of volcanic formation covered in petrified coral, it's colonized by candelabra cacti 30 to 40 feet tall. From the viewpoint at the summit, the 360° view across the salt immensity is breathtaking.

⏱ 45 min
4

Salar de Uyuni spans 4,086 square miles, the world's largest salt flat, at 12,001 feet elevation. Its white crust, formed about 10,000 years ago when an inland sea evaporated, conceals beneath its halite polygons 40% of the world's lithium reserves.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

3-day 4x4 circuit through the salar and Sud Lipez. Day 1: train cemetery, Colchani, Incahuasi, overnight in salt hotel. Day 2: Laguna Colorada and its pink flamingos, Arbol de Piedra, Sol de Mañana geysers, Polques hot springs. Day 3: Laguna Verde and Dali desert, return to Uyuni. The 2nd night is spent in a refuge near Laguna Colorada (14,042 ft): nighttime temperatures regularly drop below 14°F between May and October, refuges are unheated and water freezes. Sleeping bag rated to 5°F essential.

⏱ 60 min

Perspective photography on the salar. The perfectly flat surface tricks the eye: play with miniature objects in the background to create optical illusions. The best angles come in late afternoon when low-angle light tints the white pink.

⏱ 60 min

Stargazing from the salar on a moonless night. At 12,001 feet elevation, with zero light pollution, the Milky Way is visible to the naked eye. New moon nights between May and September offer the best conditions.

⏱ 60 min
