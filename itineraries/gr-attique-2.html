Delphi

★★★★★
1

The Sanctuary of Apollo clings to the slopes of Mount Parnassus at 2,000 feet—a site that functioned as an oracle for over a thousand years, from the late 7th century BC through the 4th century AD. According to tradition, this is where the omphalos stood, the stone Zeus designated as the center of the world. Don't expect a peaceful museum experience: tour buses arrive early in summer. Plan your ascent for opening time (8 AM) to catch the site while it's still quiet.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Temple of Apollo you see today dates from the 4th century BC—actually the sixth temple built here, after an earthquake in 373 BC destroyed its predecessor. A Doric structure measuring 197 feet long by 79 feet wide, with six columns on the facade and fifteen along the sides, it housed the Pythia seated on her tripod inside the cella. This is where the maxim Socrates would make famous was carved: "Know thyself." Three columns still stand—they give you a sense of the original building's scale.

⏱ 60 min
3

The theater, built in the 4th century BC and restored by the Romans in the 2nd century, seated 5,000 spectators during the Pythian Games, held every four years in honor of Apollo. Climb to the upper tiers: the view over the Pleistos valley, the olive groves, and the Gulf of Corinth in the distance is one of the finest at the entire site. Take time to examine the proscenium's bas-reliefs depicting the labors of Heracles before moving on.

⏱ 45 min
4

Don't rush past this small building in Parian marble: the Athenian Treasury, built between 510 and 480 BC to celebrate the victory at Marathon, is one of the few Delphi monuments completely reconstructed—French archaeologists reassembled it stone by stone between 1903 and 1906. Its friezes depict Theseus, Heracles, and an amazonomachy. And on the exterior walls, carved into the marble, you can still see two hymns to Apollo with their musical notation—among the only sonic documents to survive from antiquity.

⏱ 30 min
5

The stadium, at the very top of the site, is the least visited of Delphi's monuments—yet one of the best preserved in Greece. Built in the 5th century BC then remodeled in the 2nd century AD by the benefactor Herodes Atticus, who replaced the earthen stands with stone seating, it measures 584 feet long with capacity for about 6,500 to 7,000 spectators. The runners' starting line is still visible in the ground. Allow 15 minutes to climb from the theater.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Take your time walking slowly along the Sacred Way from the sanctuary entrance to the temple. On either side once stood dozens of statues and treasuries offered by Greek city-states competing for prestige. The empty pedestals you see today once bore bronzes long since melted down. This gradual ascent with the oracle at the end creates an atmosphere that even the crowds can't quite dispel.

⏱ 45 min

The Delphi Archaeological Museum deserves half a day on its own. Its centerpiece is the Charioteer (Hêniokhos): a 6-foot bronze discovered in 1896 beneath the Sacred Way, buried during the earthquake of 373 BC. Cast around 470-466 BC to commemorate the tyrant of Gela's victory at the Pythian Games, it's one of only five major classical bronzes preserved worldwide. Look closely at the eyes: irises in brown chalcedony, pupils in obsidian, tear ducts in pink coral—a staggering level of detail for a work 2,500 years old.

⏱ 90 min

In late afternoon, from the heights of the site, the panorama over Parnassus (8,061 feet according to official sources) and the valley of olive trees descending toward the Gulf of Corinth is at its finest. The oblique light brings out the nuances of the temple's honey-colored stone. If you're staying in Delphi or Arachova, you can walk down via the trail along the ravine—allow 30 minutes and wear sturdy shoes.

⏱ 45 min

Formaela from Arachova, a PDO sheep's milk cheese, can be purchased in shops in the neighboring village. Grill it and drizzle with local honey—a combination you won't find everywhere in Greece. Arachova is 6 miles from Delphi via a road offering some beautiful valley viewpoints.

⏱ 30 min

Kleftiko lamb—slow-cooked in a paper parcel with herbs and garlic—is the mountain specialty of the region. You'll find it in most tavernas around Delphi and Arachova. The name comes from the "klephtes," brigands who cooked meat underground so the smoke wouldn't give them away.

⏱ 45 min

Agiorgitiko, a red wine from Nemea (Peloponnese), pairs beautifully with the region's mountain cuisine. Full-bodied with soft tannins, it drinks young. You'll find it by the carafe in local tavernas.

⏱ 20 min

Delphi

★★★★★
1

The Castalian Spring lies in the ravine between the two Phaedriades rocks, just before entering the sanctuary. Every pilgrim had to purify themselves here before consulting the oracle—washing their hands, or their entire head for first-time visitors. Today it's a basin carved from rock, with water still flowing. The setting, between cliffs rising over 650 feet, has a stark beauty.

⏱ 20 min
2

Below the main sanctuary, the Sanctuary of Athena Pronaia is often less visited—wrongly so. Its most famous monument is the tholos, a 4th-century BC circular building whose exact function remains unknown. Three Doric columns and three interior Corinthian columns have been re-erected—they give the site an almost photogenic appearance. Allow 20 to 30 minutes to descend from the main site entrance.

⏱ 40 min
3

The gymnasium at Delphi, midway between the tholos and the main sanctuary, was the training ground for athletes competing in the Pythian Games. You can still make out the running track (xystos) and the cold bath basins. A quiet spot, often deserted, that you may have entirely to yourself.

⏱ 25 min
4

Arachova (3,150 feet elevation) deserves at least an hour's stop. Its steep alleyways open onto craft shops—hand-woven rugs, cheeses, local liqueurs—and viewpoints over the valley. In winter it's a lively little ski resort; in summer the village is considerably calmer. Look for the overlook above the main church for the best view of the surroundings.

⏱ 60 min
5

Make the detour to Hosios Loukas, 23 miles from Delphi—it's an hour's drive well worth the displacement. This monastery founded in 945 by the hermit Saint Luke of Steiris has been UNESCO-listed since 1990 and preserves 11th-century gold-ground mosaics among the best preserved in the entire Byzantine world. The great katholikon, built in 1011, introduced an architectural plan—octagonal inscribed cross—that would become standard throughout Byzantine Greece. Inside, look up at the dome: Christ Pantocrator gazes down at you. The site is open mornings; check hours before departing as midday closures often surprise visitors.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The walk through the Pleistos Gorge, the river flowing below Delphi, is accessible from the village and takes 1.5 to 2 hours round trip. The trail winds through thousand-year-old olive groves—some trees are said to be over a millennium old. The quiet contrasts with the bustle of the archaeological site. Hiking shoes recommended; the path can be slippery.

⏱ 120 min

In the village of Arachova, step into the craft shops lining the main street. Hand-woven rugs and kilims are the village specialty going back several generations—red and black geometric patterns you'll only see here. Local cheeses (formaela, fresh goat cheese) are sold directly from producers.

⏱ 45 min

On the route to Hosios Loukas from Delphi, stop in the village of Distomo—known for the 1944 tragedy when the SS massacred 218 residents in reprisal. A sober memorial marks the village center. This context adds another dimension to the monastery visit that follows, a place of cultural continuity and resistance for a thousand years.

⏱ 60 min

Hilopites—square dried pasta typical of the region—are eaten in soup or baked with cheese. A simple, hearty dish you'll find in mountain tavernas around Arachova.

⏱ 30 min

Feta saganaki, pan-fried in olive oil and served with honey and sesame seeds, is a classic appetizer in the region's tavernas. The sweet-salty balance works particularly well with a glass of dry white wine.

⏱ 30 min

Tsipouro from Thessaly, a grape marc eau-de-vie sometimes lightly anise-flavored, is served ice-cold with mezze—olives, cheese, mountain charcuterie. It's the aperitif drink throughout central Greece. Don't confuse it with ouzo: tsipouro is more concentrated and without added sugar.

⏱ 30 min

Kalambaka

★★★★★
1

When you arrive in Kalambaka and see these sandstone pillars rising from the plain for the first time, you understand why monks chose this place in the 11th century. These rocks formed 60 million years ago from river sediments, uplifted then sculpted by erosion into pinnacles over 1,300 feet tall. At their peak in the 15th century, 24 monasteries occupied the summits—built without roads, with materials hoisted in nets by pulley. Six remain active today. A 10.5-mile circuit from town allows you to drive the loop with stops; count on a full day.

⏱ 30 min
2

Start with the Great Meteoron—the largest, oldest, most visited, and for good reason. Founded in the 14th century by Saint Athanasios the Meteorite, its main church was rebuilt between 1545 and 1582 on the Mount Athos model, with frescoes from the Cretan school covering the walls floor to ceiling. Plan on good shoes: you'll climb 300 steps carved into rock from the parking lot. Arrive at opening (9 AM in season) or late afternoon—between 11 AM and 2 PM tour buses unload hundreds of people simultaneously. Don't leave without glancing at the museum of manuscripts and icons from the 14th through 20th centuries.

⏱ 75 min
3

Varlaam Monastery, perched at 1,224 feet on its pinnacle, is Meteora's second-largest. Founded in the 16th century by the brothers Theophanes and Nectarios Apsarades, it preserves in its katholikon frescoes painted in 1548 by Frangos Katelanos, one of the great masters of post-Byzantine painting. Don't miss the old winch system and the net—still on display—that were used to hoist people and provisions before the stairs were built. And look at the enormous 16th-century wooden barrel: 3,170-gallon capacity.

⏱ 60 min
4

Roussanou is a convent of nuns founded in the 16th century, built on a narrow rock and accessible by bridge. Its Cretan school frescoes, still vividly colored, are among Meteora's finest. The garden extending to the cliff edge is well-tended and peaceful. This is often the least crowded monastery on the loop—visit it last, when the tour buses have departed.

⏱ 45 min
5

Agios Nikolaos Anapafsas is the first monastery on the route from Kastraki, the smallest and often least visited. Founded in the late 14th century, it's famous for its frescoes created in 1527 by Theophanes the Cretan, considered the founder of the Cretan school of post-Byzantine painting. The interior space is tiny but the paintings are of exceptional quality. The terrace offers one of the best views of the rock pillars.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The Meteora scenic road runs 10.5 miles and connects all the monasteries with spectacular viewpoints between each stop. Certain overlooks let you see several monasteries simultaneously silhouetted against the sky. In late afternoon, the golden light on the sandstone rocks is photographically remarkable. The village of Kastraki, at the foot of the rocks, is calmer and more photogenic than Kalambaka for overnight stays.

⏱ 120 min

Hiking trails connect some monasteries via ancient paths—those the monks used before roads were built. One of the most interesting descends from the Great Meteoron to Kastraki (about 1 hour). Warning: some passages run along cliffs without guardrails. Maps available at the Kalambaka tourist office.

⏱ 60 min

In Kalambaka and Kastraki, artisans offer icon painting workshops following traditional Byzantine technique—gold background, natural pigments, egg-based binder (tempera). These workshops often work on commission but sometimes accept visitors. Ask at your hotel or the tourist office.

⏱ 60 min

Kontosouvli, pork skewers spit-roasted over charcoal, is Thessaly's meat specialty. You'll find it in Kalambaka tavernas, often served with country bread and Greek salad. A simple dish but particularly successful in establishments that build their own fire.

⏱ 45 min

Boureki is a savory pie with zucchini, potatoes, and feta, enclosed in crispy phyllo pastry. It varies from house to house depending on cheese proportions and herbs used. In Thessaly, it's often made thicker than in Crete, where it's also popular.

⏱ 30 min

Xinomavro from Naoussa, a red wine from northern Greece, is tannic and full-bodied with aromas of black cherry and spice. It pairs well with roasted meats and aged cheeses. Producers in the Naoussa region often age their vintages several years in oak barrels.

⏱ 20 min

Kalambaka

★★★★★
1

Agios Stefanos is the most accessible of the Meteora monasteries—no stairs, just a bridge spanning the gap between parking and entrance. Founded in the 14th century, it was converted to a female convent in 1961 after suffering significant damage during World War II. The nuns have carefully restored the spaces. The view from the terrace across the Thessaly plain is clear and impressive.

⏱ 40 min
2

Agia Triada (Holy Trinity) is the most difficult monastery to access: 147 steps carved into rock to reach a narrow pinnacle where gardens advance right to the void's edge. This is where the 1981 James Bond film "For Your Eyes Only" was shot—filming equipment had to be helicoptered in. The view from the gardens is vertiginous. If you only do one difficult monastery, make it this one.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Cathedral of the Dormition of the Virgin in Kalambaka is often ignored by visitors rushing straight to the monasteries. Built on the foundations of an ancient temple, it preserves 12th-century Byzantine frescoes and a marble pulpit placed in the center of the nave—a unique arrangement in Greece. Allow 30 minutes and it's a fine introduction to the region's religious art.

⏱ 30 min
4

The Kalambaka Natural History Museum presents Meteora's geology and explains how these sandstone pillars formed over 60 million years from river deposits then were sculpted by erosion. A clear, well-illustrated exhibition, particularly useful if you have children. Allow 30 to 45 minutes.

⏱ 35 min
5

The Pineios Gorge, east of Kalambaka, is accessible by car and offers preserved nature very different from Meteora's mineral landscape. The river flows between wooded walls, with several old Ottoman stone bridges along the route. Traditional villages hide in perpendicular valleys. A half-day excursion to get off the classic circuits.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Sunrise at Meteora is an experience unto itself. Several viewpoints below the monasteries (notably near Kastraki) let you watch the rocks emerge from valley mist with golden, low-angle light. Plan to be there before 7 AM—in summer the sun rises early. It's the ideal moment for photography and absolute silence.

⏱ 60 min

The Kalambaka market (Saturday mornings) offers products from the monasteries and neighboring farms: mountain honey, dried herbs from the Pindus, olive oil, green walnut preserve. Shops around the main square also sell icons and handcrafted items.

⏱ 60 min

If you have a car, leave early morning to explore the 31-mile loop departing Kastraki toward the mountain villages of Agios Dimitrios, Gavros, and Vlachava at 3,280 feet elevation. The ridge road offers a panorama over Meteora from above—a completely different perspective from below. Along the way: beekeepers, goat herds, cave chapels, and vineyards from Liakou estate.

⏱ 180 min

Galaktoboureko is central Greece's signature pastry: vanilla-scented semolina custard, enclosed in phyllo pastry and drenched in lemon syrup. You'll find it in all Kalambaka pastry shops, often still warm late morning.

⏱ 20 min

Stifado, a beef stew with pearl onions, red wine, cinnamon and clove, simmers for hours and is served in an earthenware pot. It's a seasonal dish that Kalambaka tavernas often cook in the evening. Order it ahead if you want to be sure to find it.

⏱ 60 min

Retsina Malamatina is a resinated white wine produced in Attica following a tradition dating back to antiquity—originally pine resin was added to amphorae to preserve them. Highly distinctive, it rarely pleases on first tasting but grows on you with rich Greek cuisine. Regional tavernas always serve it by the carafe.

⏱ 20 min

Ioannina

★★★★☆
1

Take the boat from the pier facing the fortress—the crossing takes five minutes and departures are regular. Nisi Island, in the middle of Lake Pamvotis, is smaller than it appears but concentrates several Byzantine monasteries and a gripping history: it was in the monastery of Agios Panteleimon that Ali Pasha took refuge in 1822, hunted by Ottoman troops who had withdrawn the sultan's confidence. He was executed there, at age 82, and his decapitated body buried within the grounds. His head was sent on a silver platter to Constantinople. Walking around the island takes 20 minutes.

⏱ 75 min
2

Ioannina's fortress occupies a rocky promontory jutting into Lake Pamvotis—three sides out of four are surrounded by water, making it a naturally defensible position. Justinian had laid the first stones in the 6th century; Ali Pasha almost entirely rebuilt it in the early 19th century, completing the work in 1815. He made it what you see today. Wander the alleyways of the old town inside the walls: at the entrance to the plane tree where the Turks tortured rebels, cafes and silversmiths' shops coexist with the ruins of Ali Pasha's palace. One of the rare still-inhabited Ottoman town centers in Greece.

⏱ 75 min
3

In the fortress's northeast citadel, don't miss the Aslan Pasha Mosque—built in 1618 on the site of a Byzantine church after expulsion of the Christian population. It now houses the Municipal Ethnographic Museum, documenting the cohabitation of Ioannina's three communities: Christian, Muslim, and Jewish. What few people know is that the city was long one of the great Sephardic Jewish communities of the Balkans. The costumes, silver jewelry, and weapons on display give a concrete picture of what the city was before the 20th century.

⏱ 45 min
4

Three miles from the center, Perama Cave is worth the detour. Discovered by chance in 1940 by villagers seeking shelter during the war, it extends over more than half a mile of galleries covering 159,000 square feet. Archaeologists found the skeleton of a cave bear inside—the species is extinct, no worries. The visit is done in guided groups (Greek and English), lasts about 45 minutes. Bring a sweater: even in midsummer, 59°F inside surprises. Last entry around 5:30 PM.

⏱ 60 min
5

Head down to the alleyways of the old bazaar, around the fortress, and step into a goldsmith's workshop if you find one open. Ioannina was nicknamed "city of silversmiths"—an Epirote tradition dating back at least to the Ottoman period and whose techniques a few artisans still perpetuate old-school. The silverwork museum, inside the citadel, displays worked silver pieces from the Ottoman period that give an idea of the region's craftsmanship. The bazaar street is also the best place to buy homemade pita to go.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Late in the day, settle on a terrace by Lake Pamvotis and order local fish: smoked eel, carp, trout—fishermen supply the lakefront tavernas directly. Ioannina is also known for its frogs, exported as far as France. Stay until sunset: the lake reflects the mountains turning violet, and the fortress silhouette stands out against the water.

⏱ 90 min

The walk along the fortress ramparts lets you follow the walls lakeside with a spectacular view over Pamvotis and the mountains beyond. In the evening, the reflection of the city lights on the water is striking. The cafe set up in the Its Kale acropolis, up high, is a good place to pause.

⏱ 60 min

Bakaliaros skordalia, fried cod served with garlic-potato purée, is the signature fried dish of Greek popular cuisine. You'll find it in traditional tavernas around the fortress, often on Fridays—a legacy of the Orthodox calendar.

⏱ 40 min

Ioannina's pita is different from industrial flaky pitas: a thick homemade phyllo pastry pie, filled with spinach and local cheeses, baked in a wood-fired oven. Every taverna has its recipe. This is THE specialty eaten standing or seated by the lake, wrapped in paper.

⏱ 30 min

Epirus tsipouro differs from Thessaly's by the total absence of anise: it's dry, almost neutral, and gives center stage to the mezze. In the kafeneion (traditional cafes) around the fortress, you'll be served a small carafe accompanied by a few slices of cheese and mountain charcuterie—a local institution.

⏱ 40 min
