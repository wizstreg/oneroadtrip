Rimouski

★★☆☆☆
1

The Pointe-au-Père Maritime Historic Site unfolds around three focal points. The Empress of Ireland Museum chronicles the sinking of May 29, 1914: in just 14 minutes, the Canadian Pacific liner went down after being rammed by the Norwegian collier Storstad in thick fog, taking 1,012 of the 1,477 people aboard with her. This remains Canada's worst maritime disaster, though it was quickly overshadowed by the outbreak of World War I two months later. The wreck still rests at 45 meters depth off the coast of Sainte-Luce.

⏱ 60 min
2

The HMCS Onondaga submarine, an Oberon-class vessel, stretches 90 meters long. Launched in 1965 and decommissioned in 2000, it's the only submarine in Canada open to visitors. You descend through narrow hatches into the vessel's belly to discover the torpedo room, command post, and stacked bunks where 70 submariners slept.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Pointe-au-Père Lighthouse, built in 1909, is an octagonal reinforced concrete tower standing 33 meters tall—one of Canada's tallest lighthouses. It guided navigation through the St. Lawrence estuary for nearly a century. Climb the stairs for panoramic views of the river, which spans 40 km wide at this point.

⏱ 25 min
4

The Musée régional de Rimouski occupies eastern Quebec's oldest stone church, built in 1824. Exhibitions alternate between contemporary art, science, and regional history of the Bas-Saint-Laurent. The building itself, with its neoclassical façade, is worth the visit.

⏱ 45 min
5

Maison Lamontagne, built in 1744, is designated a national heritage building. It's one of the rare surviving examples of New France architecture still standing in the region. Its construction blends colombage pierroté (half-timbering with stone infill) and pièce sur pièce (horizontal log construction), both typical techniques of the colonial era.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Stroll along the Promenade de la Mer at sunset. Rimouski was named Quebec's 'City of Happiness' in 2013, and its estuary sunsets rank among the province's most spectacular.

⏱ 60 min

Cross by kayak or zodiac to Île Saint-Barnabé, a 4-km-long island facing Rimouski. A hermit named Toussaint Cartier lived there alone from 1728 to 1767. Hiking trails traverse the island through forest and shoreline.

⏱ 210 min

Le Bic

★★★☆☆
1

Bic National Park, established in 1984, covers 33.2 km² between Route 132 and the St. Lawrence estuary. This territory has been inhabited for over 8,000 years, as evidenced by some thirty Indigenous archaeological sites. Samuel de Champlain anchored here in 1603 and gave his name to the peak dominating the park. The landscape consists of rock ridges folded by the Appalachians, forming an intricate lace of bays, coves, headlands and islands along 30 km of coastline.

⏱ 45 min
2

Pic Champlain rises to 346 meters elevation—the highest point in the natural region of the estuary's southern shore. The trail climbs through a maple grove before opening onto a panorama embracing the islands, Ha! Ha! Bay, and the 34-km-wide estuary. The park harbors the continent's highest concentration of North American porcupines and harbor seal colonies observable from Anse aux Bouleaux Ouest.

⏱ 30 min
3

Le Grand Rassemblement in Sainte-Flavie marks the 'gateway to Gaspésie.' In 1986, sculptor Marcel Gagnon began placing life-sized concrete statues in the St. Lawrence waters. Today, about 120 figures emerge from the river with the tides, in a long silent procession toward land. The artwork transforms with light and water level—at low tide, the silhouettes rise in full; at high tide, only heads protrude.

⏱ 30 min
4

The Reford Gardens in Grand-Métis have been a National Historic Site of Canada since 1995. Elsie Reford, a self-taught horticulturist who didn't take up gardening until age 54 following an appendix operation, transformed between 1926 and 1958 a fishing camp inherited from her uncle George Stephen (founder of Canadian Pacific Railway) into English-style gardens. The 18-hectare estate shelters 3,000 plant species and varieties, including the famous Himalayan blue poppy. The microclimate created by the confluence of the Mitis River and the St. Lawrence allows cultivation of species found nowhere else in Canada. Villa Estevan, built in 1887, is a historic 37-room house.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Hike the Grand Tour at Bic Park: this 8.7-km loop follows the rocky shoreline and is only passable at low tide. Start about two hours before the lowest tide to explore the rock formations and observe seals on the rocks.

⏱ 210 min

Sea kayaking excursion through the Bic archipelago, weaving between islands and headlands. Harbor seal colonies bask on the rocks and common eider ducks nest on the islets. In the background, the setting sun illuminates the cliffs.

⏱ 150 min

Sainte Anne Des Monts

★★☆☆☆
1

Gaspésie National Park, established in 1937, protects 802 km² in the heart of the peninsula. It contains more than 25 peaks exceeding 1,000 meters, including 7 of Quebec's 10 highest. The Chic-Choc Mountains—from the Mi'kmaq word meaning 'impassable barrier'—form a 95-km-long range parallel to the river. Mont Albert, at 1,154 meters, is composed of serpentine, a rock usually buried in the Earth's mantle and rarely exposed at the surface. Its summit plateau harbors alpine tundra with Arctic plants, at a latitude where you'd normally find boreal forest.

⏱ 60 min
2

Lac aux Américains is a glacial cirque formed about 10,000 years ago, nestled at the foot of steep walls. The 2.4-km trail (round-trip) crosses a boreal forest of balsam firs before opening onto this natural basin of dark waters. The name recalls the Americans who came to fish for Atlantic salmon in the Sainte-Anne River in the 19th century.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Martre Lighthouse, en route to Sainte-Anne-des-Monts, is one of Quebec's rare wooden lighthouses. Painted red, it was built in 1906 and designated a heritage building. It dominates the Haute-Gaspésie shoreline and houses a small interpretation center on St. Lawrence lighthouses.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Ascent of Mont Albert: the 11.6-km loop with 860 meters of elevation gain crosses boreal forest, then alpine tundra. At the summit, the serpentine plateau offers panoramic views of the Chic-Chocs. The park was originally created to protect the Gaspésie caribou herd—estimated at fewer than 50 individuals—the last remaining south of the St. Lawrence.

⏱ 360 min

For a more accessible option, the Mont Ernest-Laforce trail (5.2 km loop) offers viewpoints over valleys and summits without the difficulty of alpine ascent.

⏱ 120 min

Perce

★★★☆☆
1

Percé Rock is a limestone monolith 433 meters long and 88 meters high, pierced by a natural arch 15 meters wide. The vertical strata composing it belong to the Forillon Formation and date from the Lower Devonian, between 394 and 418 million years ago. They contain fossils of brachiopods and trilobites. Until 1845, the rock had two arches—the second collapsed. Marine erosion drops several tons of rock each year, and access to the base of the rock is prohibited for safety reasons.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Percé UNESCO Global Geopark offers 18 km of hiking trails through 500-million-year-old geology. The Mont Sainte-Anne trail leads to a natural cave, a spectacular crevasse, and a forest of cedars twisted by wind, nicknamed the 'magic forest.' At the top of the climb, a glass platform suspended at 200 meters altitude overlooks the village and Percé Rock—Gaspésie's most photographed spot. The immersive Tektonik exhibition in the welcome pavilion takes you back through Percé's geological time.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Promenade de la Grave runs along the village waterfront. Percé takes its name from Basque and Breton fishermen who frequented the bay as early as the 16th century for cod. The village was an important fishing center under the regime of Jersey companies, including Charles Robin & Company, which dominated the dried cod trade for over a century.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Hike the Geopark trails: the Mont Sainte-Anne loop (about 4 km) passes through the crevasse, cave, and magic forest before reaching the suspended glass platform.

⏱ 150 min

Sunset walk along La Grave and Cap Mont-Joli for the best views of Percé Rock bathed in golden light.

⏱ 60 min

Perce

★★★☆☆
1

Bonaventure Island and Percé Rock National Park protects exceptional natural, historical, and geological heritage. Bonaventure Island, covering 4.16 km², harbors the world's most accessible Northern Gannet colony—about 110,000 birds nest each summer on the red conglomerate cliffs of the eastern coast. The Northern Gannet is a seabird with white plumage, an orange-tinted head, and black wings, with a wingspan reaching up to 2 meters. It dives like an arrow at over 100 km/h to catch fish.

⏱ 60 min
2

The island was inhabited by fishing families until 1971. The historic village, with its houses and vigneaux (wooden structures for drying cod), testifies to a vanished way of life. Le Boutillier House, the former fishing manager's residence, is open to visitors free of charge and displays period furniture.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Emerald River Falls, in Percé's backcountry, lives up to its name: water cascades into a natural basin of intense turquoise. A short forest trail leads there for a refreshing break away from the coast.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Cruise to Bonaventure Island: the boat first circles close to Percé Rock, then runs along the island's cliffs where thousands of birds nest—black-legged kittiwakes, cormorants, razorbills, and Atlantic puffins. Once landed on the island, take the Sentier des Mousses (3.1 km) going and the Chemin du Roy returning for varied landscapes. At the end, the Northern Gannet colony extends just meters from the observation boardwalk.

⏱ 300 min

Whale watching cruise: seven whale species frequent the waters off Percé, including minke whales, fin whales, and occasionally humpback whales.

⏱ 150 min

Gaspe

★★★☆☆
1

Gaspé is the birthplace of French America. On July 24, 1534, Jacques Cartier planted a 30-foot cross here to claim the territory in the name of King François I—a gesture that prompted protests from Iroquoian chief Donnacona. The Musée de la Gaspésie on the boulevard presents the permanent exhibition 'Le Grand Large,' which tells the peninsula's story through 15 sailing vessels, from Viking drakkars to Mi'kmaq ocean-going canoes. In front of the museum, six bronze steles by artist Gérard Leblanc compose the monument commemorating the encounter of two civilizations.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Christ-Roi Cathedral in Gaspé, consecrated in 1969, is one of the only cathedrals built entirely of wood in North America. Its red cedar structure and modern stained glass windows make it a singular architectural monument. It replaced a previous church destroyed by fire.

⏱ 25 min
3

The Gespeg Mi'gmaq Culture Interpretation Site recreates a 17th-century Mi'kmaq village, at the time of the first Europeans' arrival. Birchbark wigwams, fire circles, utilitarian objects, and traditional crafts immerse visitors in this millennia-old culture. The word 'Gespeg' means 'land's end' in Mi'kmaq—it gave its name to the town and the entire peninsula.

⏱ 45 min
4

The Cap-des-Rosiers Lighthouse, erected in 1858, is Canada's tallest lighthouse at 34 meters. It stands at the entrance to Forillon National Park, on a rocky cape battered by gulf waves. Its name comes from explorer Samuel de Champlain, who noted the abundance of wild roses on this point in 1632.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Walk the Jacques-Cartier Promenade along the York River and Gaspé Bay. Period houses and shops line the route, and on some summer evenings, a projection show recounts Cartier's arrival on a giant screen mimicking his ship's sails.

⏱ 90 min

Mackerel fishing from the Gaspé wharf: the activity is open to all, with or without experience, and equipment is easy to find on-site.

⏱ 90 min

★★★☆☆
1

Forillon National Park, created in 1970, spans 245 km² at the northeastern tip of the Gaspésie Peninsula—where the Appalachians end their 3,200-km journey in the St. Lawrence waters. Its creation forced the expropriation of 225 families whose villages were razed, a painful episode in local history. The park's rocks—limestone, sandstone, and mudstone—date from the Ordovician to the Devonian (488 to 359 million years ago). Ten superimposed geological formations in bands are exposed here, a rare phenomenon.

⏱ 45 min
2

Cap-Bon-Ami offers one of the park's most striking panoramas: vertical limestone cliffs plunge into turquoise water, with a pebble beach at the base. In spring, the Raoul-Roy lookout allows observation of raptor migration. The sector is also the starting point for Mont Saint-Alban, whose observation tower at 285 meters altitude provides 360-degree views—on clear days, you can glimpse Percé Rock and Bonaventure Island in the distance.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Grande-Grave Heritage Site, on the park's southern coast, preserves buildings from the cod fishing era. The Hyman & Sons general store, built in the 1860s, functioned as a trading post where fishermen exchanged their catch for goods. Anse-Blanchette recreates the life of a farming-fishing family from the early 20th century. Fort Péninsule, near Penouille, was fortified during World War II to defend Gaspé Bay against German submarines.

⏱ 45 min
4

The Les Graves trail leads to Cap-Gaspé, where you'll find 'the end of the world': a red and white lighthouse and a lookout perched above the sea. This is where the Gaspésie Peninsula ends in a rocky point plunging into gulf waters. Whales are regularly observed offshore from this point.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Hike Mont Saint-Alban: 7.8-km loop starting from Cap-Bon-Ami. The climb is steep for the first kilometer, then the trail crosses boreal forest before reaching the observation tower at 285 meters.

⏱ 180 min

Hike to Cap-Gaspé via the Les Graves trail: 8-km round trip from Anse-aux-Amérindiens, following the Gaspé Bay coast between forest and shoreline.

⏱ 210 min

Sunset sea kayaking in Gaspé Bay, departing from Cap-aux-Os beach. The 3-hour guided outings allow observation of grey seals, seabirds, and the sun descending behind Forillon's cliffs.

⏱ 180 min

Carleton Sur Mer

★★☆☆☆
1

The route between Gaspé and Carleton-sur-Mer follows the Baie des Chaleurs, named by Jacques Cartier in July 1534 because of the summer heat prevailing there. This is where he made the first documented trade exchanges between the French and Mi'kmaq—furs for European objects. This arm of the Gulf of St. Lawrence benefits from a microclimate that makes the water noticeably warmer than the rest of the estuary.

⏱ 20 min
2

Stop at Bonaventure en route: the Bioparc de la Gaspésie presents the peninsula's five ecosystems—the bay, river, boreal forest, tundra, and shoreline—with indigenous fauna in semi-liberty (black bears, lynx, caribou, otters). The Musée acadien du Québec, on the same street, traces the history of Acadians deported during the Great Upheaval of 1755, some of whom fled to Gaspésie with Mi'kmaq help.

⏱ 90 min
3

Mont Saint-Joseph rises to 555 meters elevation, just 5 km from the shore. A paved road climbs to the summit, but the hiking trails (30 km total) are more rewarding. At the summit, the view plunges over the Baie des Chaleurs, Carleton's triangular barachois, the Gaspésian backcountry, and the New Brunswick coasts across the bay. Notre-Dame Oratory, a Breton-style chapel built in 1935 by the local community, is notable for its interior mosaics and colored glass windows.

⏱ 60 min
4

The Carleton-sur-Mer barachois is a brackish body of water separated from the sea by a sand spit—Pointe Tracadigache. This type of coastal lagoon shelters rich biodiversity of birds and fish. The 6-km bike path that loops around it offers a unique perspective on the junction between sea and mountain. As early as the mid-19th century, city dwellers and pilgrims came to enjoy this landscape, making Carleton one of Gaspésie's oldest resort destinations.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Hike Mont Saint-Joseph: the Taguine trail (5 km) followed by the Cap-Ferré extension (3.6 km) offers an 8.6-km round trip to the summit with progressive views over the Baie des Chaleurs.

⏱ 210 min

Bike tour around the barachois (6 km) late in the day, with a stop at Pointe Tracadigache for sunset and, on some summer evenings, open-air storytelling.

⏱ 60 min

Carleton Sur Mer

★★☆☆☆
1

Miguasha National Park, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1999, protects a 380-million-year-old fossil site. In the Mi'kmaq language, 'Miguasha' means 'red land,' referring to the color of the cliffs. The Escuminac Formation, 120 meters thick, contains 21 fossil fish species representing 5 of the 6 major groups that lived in the Devonian—only sharks and rays are missing. The site has yielded more than 18,000 specimens since its discovery in 1842 by geologist Abraham Gesner.

⏱ 45 min
2

Eusthenopteron foordi, nicknamed the 'Prince of Miguasha,' was a fish equipped with lungs and bony structures in its fins, illustrating the transition from aquatic to terrestrial life. But the museum's current star is Elpistostege watsoni—'Elpi' for short—a 1.6-meter fossil discovered in 2010, unique in the world, considered the closest relative of the first tetrapods. It's a key piece for understanding how vertebrates conquered dry land.

⏱ 45 min
3

The town of Bonaventure has the world's longest aluminum pedestrian walkway. The Circuit des Bâtisseurs, a bike path lined with illuminated interpretation panels at night, tells the story of the last century in the Baie des Chaleurs.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Visit the Miguasha natural history museum and descend to the fossil cliff with a naturalist guide. The 'Through the Geologist's Eye' activity allows you to observe sedimentary layers in situ and understand how fossils form. The 'Evolution of Life' trail (3.5 km) then offers a wooded hike with views over the Restigouche estuary.

⏱ 150 min

Kayak or canoe descent on the Bonaventure River, renowned for its emerald-colored transparent waters. The river, 125 km long, flows through forests and red sandstone cliffs. Guided descents last from 2 to 7 hours depending on the chosen route.

⏱ 300 min

Rimouski

★★☆☆☆
1

The Matapédia Valley, taken on the return route between Carleton and Rimouski, is a glacial valley carved between the Notre-Dame Mountains. The Matapédia River, 73 km long, is one of Quebec's most renowned for Atlantic salmon fishing. Route 132 then Route 6 wind between dense forests and forestry villages.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Canyon des Portes de l'Enfer in Saint-Narcisse-de-Rimouski takes its name from the deep gorges carved by the Rimouski River. The suspension bridge spanning the canyon peaks at 63 meters height—Quebec's highest suspension bridge. A 300-step staircase, baptized 'la descente aux enfers' (the descent into hell), reaches the river level below. The trails also pass by the Grand Sault waterfall and a tribute to the log drivers, those men who floated timber down the river in the forestry era.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hike at Canyon des Portes de l'Enfer: several hiking trails offer 2 to 12 km of routes between lookouts, bridges, and waterfalls. Crossing the 63-meter suspension bridge is the highlight.

⏱ 180 min

Sunset over the river from the Rimouski promenade, to close this Gaspésie loop.

⏱ 60 min
