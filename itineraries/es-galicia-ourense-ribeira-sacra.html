Ourense

★★★☆☆
1

Ourense is Galicia's 'thermal capital'—and thermal waters have been the very reason for its existence for 2,000 years. Start at As Burgas, right in the historic centre: two monumental fountains (the 17th-century Burga de Arriba and the 19th-century Burga de Abaixo) from which water gushes out at 67°C. The Romans built baths here and worshipped the 'nymphs of the springs.' Local legend claims the springs originate beneath the Santo Cristo chapel inside the cathedral. You can touch the water (careful—it's scalding) and even drink it, though its sulphurous taste isn't particularly pleasant; its curative properties, however, are well documented. The modern, free-entry As Burgas Interpretation Centre explains the geology and history of the site.

⏱ 45 min
2

Walk up to the Cathedral of San Martín, a national monument begun in the 12th century. Its hidden treasure is the Pórtico del Paraíso (Paradise Portico), a polychrome Romanesque sculptural ensemble that has retained its original colours—an exceptionally rare thing. It's the 'cousin' of Santiago's famous Pórtico de la Gloria, but here the medieval paintwork remains intact. In the Chapel of San Cosme and San Damián, there's a quirky feature: a permanent nativity scene displayed all year round, with figures dressed in Galician costume. Exit via the Praza Maior, surrounded by arcades, where you'll find the Casa Consistorial and the archaeological museum housed in the old episcopal palace.

⏱ 60 min
3

Descend towards the Miño through the old town—medieval lanes, Renaissance palaces, shady squares. The Ponte Vella (Old Bridge), of Roman origin but rebuilt in the 13th century, spans the river on seven stone arches. On the far side, the ultra-modern Ponte do Milenio (2001) creates a spectacular contrast with its millennial neighbour.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

In the afternoon, treat yourself to the thermal circuit along the Miño—a 3 km riverside walk with four open-air thermal stations. Start at A Chavasqueira (water emerges at 41°C, free pools), then O Tinteiro (43°C, good for skin conditions), the Pozas de Muíño das Veigas (65–72°C at source, four pools), and finally Outariz (61°C, the largest thermal area, six pools). All are free and open year-round. The experience is magical in winter, when steam rises into the cold air, but it's pleasant in any season. Bring your swimsuit and a towel.

⏱ 150 min

In the evening, Ourense transforms into a tapas and wine town—locals love going out. Rúa do Vino and the streets around Praza Maior form the heart of the nightlife. Try the pulpo á feira (Galician-style octopus), pimientos de Padrón, and a crisp, chilled Ribeiro white. Ourense's cuisine is among the finest in Galicia.

⏱ 60 min

Ribadavia

★★☆☆☆
1

First stop: Celanova, 30 minutes south of Ourense. The Monastery of San Salvador, founded in 936 by San Rosendo (a Galician bishop and saint), is one of the most important in Galicia. The church, rebuilt in Baroque style in the 17th–18th centuries, impresses with its monumental façade and regular cloister. But Celanova's true treasure hides behind the monastery: the Chapel of San Miguel, a tiny 10th-century Mozarabic oratory, one of the few pre-Romanesque buildings in all of Galicia. Its horseshoe arches and miniature proportions transport you to an era when Christians and Muslims shared a common architectural vocabulary.

⏱ 60 min
2

Continue to Allariz, 20 minutes north. This medieval village on the Arnoia River won the European Prize for Urban Planning in 1994 for its exemplary restoration. Stroll along the river, cross the stone bridges, and climb into the historic centre where granite lanes preserve the atmosphere of a medieval Galician town. The 12th-century Romanesque church of Santiago and the Convent of Santa Clara (which houses a rare medieval rock-crystal crucifix) are worth a stop. Allariz is also a gastronomic village, renowned for its artisan bakeries and the summer bica (traditional cake) festival.

⏱ 60 min
3

End the day at Ribadavia, the former capital of the medieval province of Ribeiro. The historic centre preserves one of Galicia's best-preserved Jewish quarters—the judería is organized around narrow lanes, with noble houses bearing coats of arms and the former synagogue (identifiable by its floor plan). After the expulsion of the Jews in 1492, the quarter was gradually absorbed, but the architectural traces remain visible. The castle of the Counts of Ribadavia dominates the village, and the Praza Maior, lined with granite arcades, is the perfect place to enjoy a glass of Ribeiro—Galicia's oldest white wine, produced here since Roman times.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

If you're visiting Ribadavia during the last weekend of August or the first of September, you'll stumble upon the Festa da Istoria—a medieval re-enactment where the entire village dresses up, the streets transform into a medieval market, and life in the judería is recreated. It's one of the most authentic historical festivals in Galicia.

⏱ 60 min

Sample Ribeiro wine at the bars around Praza Maior—it's a light, fresh white, perfect with fish tapas and Galician empanadas. Several estates in the region offer tours and tastings.

⏱ 45 min

Ribeira Sacra

★★★★☆
1

Welcome to the Ribeira Sacra, one of Spain's most spectacular yet least-known regions. Canyons 500 metres deep carved by the Sil and Miño rivers, Romanesque monasteries clinging to cliffsides, and terraced vineyards so steep that harvesters sometimes work roped to the vines. The name Ribeira Sacra (Sacred Shore) comes from the concentration of monasteries founded between the 6th and 12th centuries along these gorges. Begin at the Mirador de Balcones de Madrid, one of the most vertiginous viewpoints: you're suspended more than 300 metres above the Sil canyon, with terraced vineyards plunging towards the river like a natural amphitheatre.

⏱ 30 min
2

Descend to the Monastery of Santo Estevo de Ribas de Sil, founded in the 6th century and rebuilt in the 12th–13th centuries. Its three superimposed cloisters—Romanesque, Gothic, and Renaissance—are a lesson in architectural history in a single place. The monastery has been converted into a national Parador (state-run hotel)—even if you're not staying, you can enter to visit the cloisters and have coffee in the gallery overlooking the canyon. The Romanesque church preserves the tombs of nine bishops who retired here in the Middle Ages.

⏱ 90 min
3

Push on to the Monastery of San Pedro de Rocas, the oldest monastic site in Galicia (founded in the 6th century). Three chapels are carved directly into the granite rock—a unique case in Galicia. Inside, a rare medieval mappamundi (world map) painted in fresco is partially preserved. The place is isolated, deep in a chestnut forest, and the silence is total. The three stone bell towers emerging from the rock are the iconic image of the Ribeira Sacra.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The catamaran cruise through the Sil canyons is the signature experience of the Ribeira Sacra. For 1.5 to 2 hours, you glide up the gorge between 500-metre walls covered with oaks and terraced vineyards. Several embarkation points exist (Santo Estevo, Abeleda). Book ahead in summer—places fill up quickly.

⏱ 120 min

If you're a wine lover, visit a Ribeira Sacra bodega. The terraced vineyards, some with gradients of 50–60%, produce reds based on Mencía—an indigenous grape variety that yields elegant, fruity wines. Harvesting is done by hand, sometimes with ropes to prevent slipping. It's one of the most heroic vineyards in Europe.

⏱ 90 min

Monforte De Lemos

★★☆☆☆
1

Monforte de Lemos is the capital of the Tierra de Lemos and the most important town in the interior of Lugo province. Dominating the town from its hilltop, the monumental complex of San Vicente del Pino brings together a Benedictine monastery (founded in the 10th century, rebuilt in the 16th–17th), the Torre del Homenaje (a 30-metre keep, one of the largest in Galicia, 13th century), and the ruins of the palace of the Counts of Lemos. From the tower's summit, the view sweeps across the entire Cabe valley and the surrounding mountains.

⏱ 60 min
2

Monforte's star monument is the Colegio de Nuestra Señora de la Antigua, nicknamed the 'Escorial of Galicia' for its monumentality. This Jesuit college was founded in 1593 by Cardinal Rodrigo de Castro, Archbishop of Seville and a Monforte native. The building, in Herreran style (the same as Madrid's Escorial), is immense: an austere granite façade, a church on a Greek-cross plan with a retable by Francisco de Moure, and above all an extraordinary collection of paintings including works attributed to Andrea del Sarto and the school of El Greco. The college's picture gallery is one of the most important in Galicia.

⏱ 90 min
3

Head back down to the centre for the Puente Viejo over the Río Cabe, of Roman origin, and wander through the lanes of old Monforte—noble houses with coats of arms, seigneurial palaces, and quiet little squares. The church of Santa María de A Régoa, former church of the Franciscan convent, preserves a Gothic portal and a venerated Romanesque Virgin.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Monforte's gastronomy is that of deep Galicia: empanada (tuna or meat pie), lacón con grelos (pork shoulder with turnip greens), caldo gallego (thick cabbage and bean soup). For dessert, filloa (Galician crêpe) and bica de Monforte, a light almond-paste cake.

⏱ 60 min

If time allows, take an excursion into the wine villages around Monforte—Doade, Sober, Chantada—to see the terraced vineyards up close and visit a cellar. Ribeira Sacra's Mencía is starting to gain international recognition.

⏱ 90 min

O Cebreiro

★★★☆☆
1

The road from Monforte to O Cebreiro crosses the mountainous heart of Lugo province—chestnut forests, meadows, stone hamlets where time seems to have stopped. O Cebreiro (1,300 m altitude) is the gateway to the Camino de Santiago in Galicia for pilgrims coming from Castile on the Camino Francés. The village has barely a handful of houses, a pilgrims' hostel, and a church—but the atmosphere is extraordinary, especially when fog rises from the valley and wraps around the pallozas.

⏱ 30 min
2

The pallozas are the very reason for visiting O Cebreiro. These pre-Roman dwellings—circular or oval stone houses with conical thatched roofs—were inhabited until the mid-20th century. They descend directly from pre-Roman Celtic constructions (the castros). Several have been restored, and one houses a small ethnographic museum showing how peasant families lived: a single room shared with animals, a central hearth with no chimney (smoke escaped through the thatched roof). These structures are unique to this mountainous zone of Galicia and the Bierzo.

⏱ 45 min
3

The church of Santa María la Real in O Cebreiro, pre-Romanesque (9th century), is one of the oldest in Galicia still standing. It's also the site of the 'Miracle of O Cebreiro': in the 14th century, a priest who doubted transubstantiation saw the bread and wine literally transform into flesh and blood during Mass. The chalice and paten from the miracle are still preserved in the church—and it's precisely these that are said to have inspired the legend of the Holy Grail in Wagner's Parsifal. The simple, moving Romanesque chalice is displayed in a case to the left of the altar.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Walk a section of the Camino de Santiago from O Cebreiro—even if you're not a pilgrim, the descent to the village of Liñares then Hospital da Condesa (4 km, 1h) gives you a taste of what walkers experience: an earth path between stone walls, birch forests, views of the Lugo mountains. You'll encounter pilgrims of all nationalities carrying their scallop shells and walking sticks.

⏱ 60 min

If you continue by car to the Alto do Poio (1,337 m), the highest point of the Camino de Santiago in Galicia, you'll cross a landscape of moorland and peat bogs that recalls Scotland or Ireland. It's also excellent territory for spotting wild Galician horses (garañóns). In the evening, dine at O Cebreiro on caldo gallego and empanada—Galician mountain cuisine is simple and comforting.

⏱ 60 min

Las Medulas

★★★★☆
1

From O Cebreiro, you descend into the Bierzo, leaving Galicia behind. The road passes through Villafranca del Bierzo, a charming staging town on the Camino de Santiago with Romanesque churches and a medieval bridge. Stop for coffee on the Plaza Mayor before continuing to Ponferrada. Ponferrada's Templar Castle (12th–13th century) is one of the largest Templar fortresses in Spain—its towers and ramparts dominate the town and the Río Sil. It was built to protect pilgrims on the Camino de Santiago and enlarged in the 15th century by the Counts of Lemos.

⏱ 75 min
2

End your itinerary at Las Médulas, the ancient Roman gold mines listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site. This site is already covered in another ORT itinerary—it's your connection point with Castile. The landscape of red earth pinnacles sculpted by Roman hydraulic engineering (the ruina montium, which involved exploding the mountain with water pressure) is spectacular from the Mirador de Orellán.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

If you have time, the trail through the Roman galleries of Las Médulas (La Cuevona and La Encantada) is accessible and takes about 1h30 return. It's a chance to walk inside tunnels carved by the Romans 2,000 years ago.

⏱ 90 min

Before leaving the Bierzo, try the botillo—the region's emblematic charcuterie, a casing stuffed with pork ribs and smoked tail, served with potatoes. It's the Bierzo's winter dish, hearty and delicious. Bierzo wine, based on Mencía like in the Ribeira Sacra, rounds out the picture perfectly.

⏱ 60 min
