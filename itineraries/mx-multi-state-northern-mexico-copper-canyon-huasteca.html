San Luis Potosi

★★★☆☆
1

San Luis Potosí's Plaza de Armas is over 430 years old—it's the beating heart of the historic center. Surrounding it: the baroque cathedral built between 1670 and 1730, with its rose cantera stone façade and sculptures of the twelve apostles carved in Carrara marble, and the Palacio de Gobierno, where Benito Juárez established his government in 1863 during the French occupation. Take time to sit on the plaza benches in late afternoon.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Jardín de San Francisco is the centro's second major garden. The adjacent baroque church houses a remarkable sacristy and colonial paintings. Right next door, the Museo Regional Potosino occupies a former convent—its Aranzazú chapel on the second floor is one of the most elaborately decorated baroque spaces in the city.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Teatro de la Paz, inaugurated in 1894 on Jardín Colón, is one of Mexico's finest neoclassical theaters. Its gray cantera stone façade is worth seeing even if you don't catch a performance. Step into the lobby if it's open—the ceiling frescoes have been beautifully restored.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Enchiladas potosinas are San Luis's unmissable specialty. Unlike enchiladas you might know, these are red corn tortillas (chile ancho worked into the dough) stuffed with fresh cheese and fried, served with cream and salsa. They bear no resemblance to Tex-Mex versions. Look for a cocina económica around the Mercado de la República—that's where they're at their best.

⏱ 75 min

Real De Catorce

★★★☆☆
1

Real de Catorce exists only because silver was abundant here. At its peak around 1890, the town counted 40,000 inhabitants. When silver prices collapsed after 1900, only 1,500 remained. Today the cobblestone streets are half-deserted, colonial façades crumbling. That's the charm: a town frozen in time. The parish church of the Purísima Concepción in the town center dates from the 18th century—its baroque altars and 19th-century pipe organ are still in place.

⏱ 90 min
2

To enter Real de Catorce, you drive through the Ogarrio tunnel—2.3 km carved through rock in 1901, hewn from an old mine shaft, lit in yellow light, and one-way. You wait your turn at the entrance. When the gatekeeper opens the barrier, you enter the darkness. It's the best decompression airlock ever invented.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Descend into the Wirikuta desert by Willys jeep or on horseback with a local guide. This is the sacred land of the Wixárika (Huichol) people, who make a pilgrimage here every spring from Nayarit—several hundred kilometers—to harvest peyote as part of shamanic ceremonies. On rocks and bushes, you'll see offerings of colored wool shaped like diamonds—nierika, portals between worlds. Don't touch them. The sacred mountain Cerro El Quemado dominates the desert.

⏱ 180 min

Ciudad Valles

★★☆☆☆
1

The descent from Real de Catorce toward the Huasteca Potosina is one of Mexico's most spectacular landscape transitions. In 4 hours of driving, you drop from 2,750 meters elevation in a cactus desert to 100 meters in dense tropical jungle. The vegetation changes every half hour. Keep your eyes open on the road: it snakes through gorges, crosses rivers, and passes through villages where women still wear the embroidered huipil of the Teenek people.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Arrive in Ciudad Valles in late afternoon—it's the logistical base for the Huasteca. The town itself holds little interest, but it concentrates activity providers, Huastecan cuisine restaurants, and hotels. Get settled and organize tomorrow's excursions: Xilitla and Tamul can be combined in a single day from Ciudad Valles with a 7am departure.

⏱ 90 min

Ciudad Valles

★★☆☆☆
1

Las Pozas in Xilitla is the mad project of Edward James—eccentric British poet, patron of the surrealists, friend of Dalí and Magritte. In 1962, after frost killed his 29,000 orchids, he began building concrete structures in the middle of the jungle. Staircases leading to the sky. Doors opening onto nothing. Flower-shaped columns 10 meters high. He continued until his death in 1984 without ever finishing. The garden covers 37 hectares—9 of sculptures, the rest primary forest. Declared a national artistic monument in 2012. Book your time slot in advance: daily visitor numbers are capped.

⏱ 150 min

Activities

In the afternoon, take a lancha from the Tanchachin dock to reach Tamul waterfall by ascending the Santa María river. Tamul is the highest waterfall in San Luis Potosí state—105 meters dropping into a setting of limestone cliffs covered in vegetation. The boat trip takes about 45 minutes each way. In dry season (November-May), the water is turquoise.

⏱ 240 min

Ciudad Valles

★★☆☆☆
1

Puente de Dios—the Bridge of God—is a natural limestone arch beneath which a river flows into a turquoise pool. The water filters through the rock and takes on an otherworldly blue-green hue. You swim beneath the vault. It's accessible on foot from a parking area—count on 20 minutes of trail.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Cascadas de Micos are a series of nine waterfalls cascading over roughly 500 meters through tropical vegetation. Each pool is swimmable—some allow jumps from rocks 3 to 8 meters high. The water is warm (27-28°C in summer). Allow 2 hours to explore the entire site.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

If you have an extra half-day, the Sótano de las Golondrinas is one of Mexico's most mesmerizing natural phenomena. It's a vertical sinkhole 376 meters deep, 135 meters wide at the opening. Every morning at sunrise, tens of thousands of white-collared swifts spiral upward—a living vortex rising for several minutes. Access from Ciudad Valles takes 2 hours—it's an excursion to schedule very early in the morning.

⏱ 180 min

Zacatecas

★★★☆☆
1

Zacatecas sits between two hills—Cerro del Grillo and Cerro de la Bufa—at 2,450 meters elevation. All its streets are built in pink cantera, the local volcanic stone. Its historic center has been UNESCO-listed since 1993. Take the cable car up on your first evening: the gondola crosses the city diagonally, 200 meters above the rooftops. Views over the cathedral, towers, and narrow alleys.

⏱ 60 min
2

Zacatecas Cathedral, built between 1729 and 1752, is considered the masterpiece of Mexican Churrigueresque baroque—the most ornamental style there is. Its main façade is a wall of sculptures: angels, saints, vases, drapery in relief, all carved in pink cantera. Look for details on the top level—the arabesques interweave until not a square centimeter of stone remains bare.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

First evening in Zacatecas: wander down Avenida Hidalgo, the centro's main artery, and settle into a restaurant on Plaza de Armas. Callejoneadas—nighttime wanderings with tamborazo brass bands through the alleys—often depart from the plaza in late evening. A group of musicians with kettledrums and brass, a donkey loaded with mezcal, and everyone follows.

⏱ 120 min

Zacatecas

★★★☆☆
1

The Mina El Edén was worked from 1586 to 1960. Indigenous slaves extracted silver, gold, copper, zinc here—up to five deaths per day from tuberculosis and silicosis combined. Today a small tourist train takes you into the lit galleries. The walls still show tools from the era. At the back, an underground lake. And in the evening, a nightclub has opened in the main chamber—probably the strangest dance club in Mexico.

⏱ 90 min
2

Climb up to Cerro de la Bufa, the hill overlooking Zacatecas. At the summit: the Museo de la Toma de Zacatecas, dedicated to the June 1914 battle during which Pancho Villa seized the city by defeating Huerta's federal troops. This victory marked a turning point in the Mexican Revolution. In front of the museum, equestrian statues of Villa, Felipe Ángeles, and Pánfilo Natera. 360° views over the city.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Museo Pedro Coronel is housed in a former 17th-century Jesuit college. The Zacatecan painter Pedro Coronel bequeathed his personal collection here: works by Picasso, Miró, Chagall, pre-Columbian masks, African and Oceanic pieces. It's one of Mexico's best-endowed provincial museums.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Evening callejoneada with tamborazo—the real deal. Tamborazo zacatecano is a 19th-century tradition born in the mines: miners who returned Saturday night with their pay and paraded through the alleys with brass bands until dawn. Join a group departing from Plaza de Armas and follow the procession through lantern-lit callejones.

⏱ 120 min

Durango

★★★☆☆
1

Durango is a lesser-known colonial city than its neighbors, and that's precisely its appeal. Its historic center is intact, its Plaza de Armas large and open, its 17th-century baroque cathedral sober and elegant. The city sits at 1,880 meters elevation in a basin surrounded by the Sierra Madre—the air is dry, nights cool. Wander through the centro without a set plan: the street markets and colonial portales deserve lingering.

⏱ 90 min
2

The open-air film studios in the desert around Durango have served as sets for dozens of westerns—Mexican and American—since the 1950s. Productions by Sergio Leone, John Wayne, Anthony Quinn shot here. Several sets still stand a few kilometers from town: saloons, sheriff's offices, dusty streets. Some offer daytime tours.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

If you stumble upon an escaramuza charra, don't pass it by. Mazatlan

★★★☆☆
1

The Durango → Mazatlán road is one of North America's most spectacular highway stretches. Highway 40D, inaugurated in 2013, crosses the Sierra Madre Occidental with 115 bridges and 61 tunnels. The most striking: Puente Baluarte, a 1,124-meter-long cable-stayed bridge whose roadway is 403 meters above the canyon floor—the highest road bridge in the Americas. ⏱ 30 min
2

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Mazatlán's malecón stretches 21 km—the longest in the world according to locals. Rent a bike in late afternoon and ride along the Pacific coast until sunset. Pacífico, the local beer brewed in the city since 1900, is best drunk cold on the old centro's terraces.

The El Fuerte → Bahuichivo/Cerocahui journey by Chepe train takes about 6 hours. Sit on the right side (if departing from Los Mochis/El Fuerte): the best canyon views are on this side. Activities

Divisadero

★★★★☆
1

Divisadero is THE viewpoint of the Barrancas del Cobre. From the train station, you look down into three canyons simultaneously: Urique, Tararecua, and Cobre, with a 1,800-meter vertical drop. The Barrancas del Cobre form a system of six canyons whose total area is four times larger than the American Grand Canyon. The Chepe stops here for a few minutes—step out onto the platform even if you're not staying.

⏱ 120 min
2

At the canyon's edge, Tarahumara women sell their handicrafts: woven grass baskets, fabric dolls, pine bowls. The Rarámuri—their name in their own language, meaning 'foot runners'—live in the mountains and caves of the sierra. They're the ultra-marathon runners in leather sandals whom the world knows from the book Born to Run. Buy directly from them.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Parque Aventura Barrancas del Cobre offers the ZipRider—2.5 km of zipline over the canyon, descending at 80 km/h, starting at 2,400 meters elevation. It's one of the longest ziplines in the world. The park also offers a circuit of seven linked ziplines with rope bridges, and a via ferrata. Book upon arrival—slots fill quickly in high season.

⏱ 180 min

Chihuahua

★★★☆☆
1

The Chepe train from Divisadero → Chihuahua takes about 5 hours. Upon arrival, Quinta Luz—Pancho Villa's former residence—is the unmissable museum. The 48-room house remains as it was at Villa's death in 1923. In the courtyard: the black Dodge riddled with bullets in which he was assassinated in Parral—the bullet holes are original. Inside: his saddles, pistols, photos, bedroom. His legal widow, Luz Corral, lived here until her death in 1981.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Palacio de Gobierno in Chihuahua houses in its basement cell the room where the father of Mexican independence, Miguel Hidalgo, was imprisoned for 86 days before his execution in 1811. A monumental 1960s fresco by Aarón Piña Mora covers the inner courtyard walls and recounts the history of Chihuahua state from its origins. The baroque cathedral on Plaza de Armas, built between 1725 and 1826, is one of northern Mexico's finest.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Return flight to Mexico City or your destination from Chihuahua International Airport (CUU). Domestic flights to CDMX take 1h30. The airport is 20 minutes from the centro by taxi.
