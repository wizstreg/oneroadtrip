Stop 1

Hanoi

Where thousand-year-old guild streets meet French colonial heritage on the banks of the Red River

★★★★★
📍 Old Quarter Hanoi📍 Hoan Kiem Lake Hanoi📍 Ngoc Son Temple Hanoi📍 Long Bien Bridge Hanoi📍 Dong Xuan Market Hanoi
1

Hanoi's Old Quarter has existed since Emperor Lý Thái Tổ founded the capital Thăng Long in 1010 - its trading alleys carry over a thousand years of history. Nicknamed the 'Quarter of the 36 Guilds,' it actually counted more than 70 streets, each specializing in a specific trade: Hàng Bạc for silversmiths, Hàng Gai for silk, Hàng Mã for votive paper objects. Some streets have lost their original craft, but Hàng Gai still sells its fabrics and Hàng Mã its paper decorations. Set out on foot early in the morning - before 8am, you'll cross paths with bicycle delivery riders and phở vendors, not yet the tourist groups.

⏱ 90 min
2

Hoan Kiem Lake - 'Lake of the Returned Sword' - takes its name from a 15th-century legend: King Lê Lợi is said to have returned to a divine turtle the magical sword that had allowed him to drive out the Ming invaders. This 12-hectare lake, quite shallow (1.2 m on average), connects the Old Quarter to the French colonial district. The Ngoc Son Temple sits on an islet at the north end of the lake, accessible via the red The Huc bridge - 45 meters of lacquered wood. On weekends, the streets around the lake become pedestrian-only and Hanoians gather there to play chess, practice tai chi, or simply stroll. Plan to pass by in the evening when the lights come on - the reflection of the red bridge on the water is one of Vietnam's most photographed scenes.

⏱ 60 min
3

Built between 1898 and 1902 by the French company Daydé & Pillé, the Long Bien Bridge spans the Red River for 1,682 meters and was one of the four longest bridges in the world at its inauguration. American aviation bombed it repeatedly between 1967 and 1972, destroying part of its original nineteen spans. Nine remain today, the others having been rebuilt with a different structure - this mix of period spans and modern replacements is visible to the naked eye. You can cross it on foot or by bike. The sunset from the middle of the bridge, with the Red River and banana fields, is the favorite spot for Hanoian photographers.

⏱ 45 min
4

Built during the French colonial era in 1889, Dong Xuan Market is Hanoi's largest covered market. Its three-gabled facade is recognizable from afar. Inside, five floors of galleries offer wholesale and retail: textiles, clothing, kitchenware, spices, dry foods. Behind the main building sits a continuous street food market with stalls selling pho, bun cha, and bánh mì. Beware of pickpockets during rush hours - keep your belongings in front of you in the crowd at the stalls.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Don't leave Hanoi without tasting cà phê trứng - egg coffee. The recipe originated in the 1940s when a barman at the Sofitel Métropole replaced milk, extremely rare during wartime, with egg yolks beaten into foam with sugar and condensed milk. The result: a thick cream sitting atop a strong robusta, served in a bowl of hot water to maintain temperature. Old Quarter houses offer this specialty upstairs in ancient wooden dwellings, which you reach by climbing a nearly vertical staircase - the effect is worth it.

⏱ 30 min

When evening comes, the Old Quarter's alleys transform into a succession of stalls spilling onto the sidewalks. Don't miss: bún chả (rice vermicelli, grilled pork, and vinegar broth), bánh cuốn (steamed rice crepes stuffed with filling), and nem rán (crispy fried spring rolls). Choose a plastic stool near the open-air kitchens - the liveliest restaurants with a rapid turnover of local customers are the best indicator of freshness. Tạ Hiện Street, nicknamed 'Beer Street,' is the nerve center of nighttime activity, with its draft beers at 5,000 VND per glass served directly on the sidewalk.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 2

Ba Be

Vietnam's largest natural lake, where emerald waters mirror limestone cliffs and Tay villages on stilts

★★☆☆☆
📍 Ba Be Lake Bac Kan Vietnam📍 Puong Cave Ba Be📍 Pac Ngoi Village Ba Be📍 Dau Dang Waterfall Ba Be📍 Ba Be National Park
1

Ba Be Lake - 'Slam Pé' in the Tay language, meaning 'three lakes' - is actually three interconnected basins: Pé Lầm, Pé Lù, and Pé Lèng. It stretches 8 km long, covers 650 hectares, sits at 145 m altitude, with an average depth of 20 to 25 m. It's Vietnam's largest natural freshwater lake and one of twenty freshwater lakes worldwide considered worthy of preservation. In the middle of the emerald waters, two small islets emerge: An Ma Island, where locals have built a small temple venerating the spirits of feudal war, and Ba Goa Island. The national park surrounding the lake, created in 1992, shelters more than 417 plant species and 299 vertebrate species. Embark early in the morning - around 7am - for a boat ride; the low-angle light on the limestone cliffs is particularly beautiful at this hour.

⏱ 120 min
2

Puong Cave is a natural tunnel carved by the Nang River through the limestone mountain of Lung Nham: 300 m long, 30 m high, 30 m wide. You enter it by boat on the river - the sound of tens of thousands of bats (23 recorded species) greets you from the entrance. The limestone walls are covered with strangely shaped concretions, and the rays of light piercing through at the other end of the tunnel create a striking effect. Once you've crossed the cave, you can disembark and walk to the small Tay villages on the riverbank. Don't come at noon - the bats fly out at dusk by the thousands, a spectacle not to be missed if you're staying in the park.

⏱ 60 min
3

Pac Ngoi is the most accessible and best-preserved Tay village at Ba Be Lake, with about 80 households still living from fishing and rice cultivation. Wooden stilt houses dominate the shore, built high to withstand seasonal floods. The Tay are renowned for crafting their doc moc canoes, carved from a single tree trunk and so narrow they glide between rocks effortlessly. In the evening in the homestays, it's not uncommon to hear the Dan Tinh - a three-stringed lute - accompanying traditional Hat Then songs. If you stay overnight here, your host will prepare smoked pork and forest vegetables picked that very morning.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Dau Dang Waterfall is the most spectacular point of the Nang River, 3 km from the lake. The water cascades down three successive levels between rocky walls, then calms into a series of pools where catfish (ca chien) can exceed 10 kg - a rare species protected in the park. The classic excursion combines crossing Puong Cave then ascending the Nang to the falls, with lunch on site in a small shelter by the water. Bring a light rain jacket even in the dry season - the waterfall's spray is quite effective. Count at least half a day for this loop.

⏱ 180 min

Kayaking is the best way to explore Ba Be at your own pace, skirting the limestone cliffs and gliding under the branches of forests overhanging the shore. Outfitters in Pac Ngoi village offer kayak rentals to reach Puong Cave or simply follow the shores of the main lake. The lake is deep (35 m at maximum) - wear the life jacket even if you're a strong swimmer. Swimming is only recommended at An Ma Island, where the depth is more moderate.

⏱ 120 min

Stop 3

Cao Bang

Where Ban Gioc thunders at the Chinese border and Nguom Ngao Cave holds underground rivers

★★☆☆☆
📍 Puong Cave Ba Be Nang River📍 Dau Dang Waterfall Ba Be📍 Phong Nam Valley Cao Bang📍 Khuoi Ky Village Cao Bang📍 Cao Bang City Vietnam
1

On the road between Ba Be and Cao Bang, the Phong Nam Valley is one of the finest stops in this little-visited northeast. Tay villages line up low houses built of gray limestone, topped with black tiles - an architecture radically different from the wooden stilt houses you just left at Ba Be. Corn and tobacco crops follow one another in the valley floors. Stop to walk 30 minutes through the alleys - locals rarely receive foreigners who are just passing through, and the curiosity is usually mutual.

⏱ 60 min
2

Khuoi Ky may be the most unique village in Cao Bang province: its stilt houses are built entirely of limestone - a technique unique in Vietnam. The raised foundations protect the dwellings from floods of the nearby river, but unlike what you see everywhere in the north, here it's stone and not wood that supports the whole structure. The village has about fifteen houses inhabited by the Tay ethnic group, some dating back several generations. If you pass by mid-morning, you'll encounter women returning from the nearby periodic market with their baskets loaded with vegetables and dried fish.

⏱ 60 min
3

The road between Ba Be and Cao Bang crosses several passes offering plunging views over the karst valleys of the northeast. Stop at the marked viewpoints to photograph the terraced rice paddies and limestone formations piercing through the forest. In September-October, golden rice paddies in the valley floors create a striking contrast with the gray cliffs. The road is good but narrow in places - passing logging trucks requires vigilance.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

In this region straddling Ba Be and Cao Bang, several periodic markets bring together the Tay, Nung, Dao, and Hmong ethnicities from surrounding villages. These markets - Nam Cuong, Cho Ra, or Quang Khe - are not tourist markets: people come to buy piglets, sell dried corn, negotiate indigo-dyed textiles. Costumes vary dramatically from one ethnic group to another - the Dao wear headdresses embroidered with red threads, the Hmong Flower people wear clothing covered with colorful patterns. Arrive before 8am to see the maximum activity; after 10am, people head back to their villages.

⏱ 60 min

Cao Bang is a quiet provincial town, crossed by the Bang Giang River. In the evening, the surroundings of the central market come alive with street stalls offering local specialties: bánh áp chao (fried rice pancake stuffed with duck meat), Cao Bang bánh cuốn reputed to be more delicate than Hanoi's, and Nung dried lạp xưởng. It's also a good opportunity to ask the restaurant or your accommodation to organize the next day's visits to Ban Gioc - departures are early in the morning.

Stop 4

Cao Bang

Where Ban Gioc thunders at the Chinese border and Nguom Ngao Cave holds underground rivers

★★☆☆☆
📍 Ban Gioc Waterfall Cao Bang China border📍 Nguom Ngao Cave Trung Khanh📍 Phuc Sen Village Nung An forge📍 Thang Hen Lake Cao Bang📍 Phia Thap Village incense Cao Bang
1

Ban Gioc is the largest waterfall in Southeast Asia and the fourth in the world among those marking a national border, after Iguaçu, Victoria, and Niagara. Formed by the Quay Son River 90 km from Cao Bang, it unfolds over about 100 m wide and 30 m high, divided into three separate falls by wooded rocky spurs. The eastern part of the waterfall belongs to China, where it's called Detian - you'll see Chinese tourists on the other bank from the Vietnamese shore. In the rainy season (August-September), the flow is maximum and the mist creates permanent rainbows. In the dry season, golden rice paddies at the foot of the waterfall add a picturesque touch. Outfitters offer to approach the falls by flat-bottomed boat - don't miss it.

⏱ 120 min
2

Three kilometers from Ban Gioc, Nguom Ngao Cave - 'Tiger Cave' in the Tay language - was discovered in 1921 by French officials during an inspection at the falls. According to a 1995 survey by the British Speleological Association, it extends over a total of 2,144 m with three entrances: Nguom Ngao, Nguom Lom, and Ban Thuon. The stalactites and stalagmites display an unusual color palette due to the presence of various minerals in the limestone - ochre, white, and slightly pinkish tones that change depending on the angle of light. Since late 2021, a new gallery (Ban Thuon) has been open to the public with illuminated walkways and a boat section. Allow at least 1h30 to tour it completely.

⏱ 90 min
3

On the return road toward Cao Bang, the village of Phuc Sen deserves a 30 to 45-minute stop. Inhabited by the Nung An ethnic group, this village has practiced artisanal forging for over 300 years. With nearly 160 forges still in operation, it produces knives, sickles, and agricultural tools sold throughout the province - and renowned for their durability far superior to imported equivalents. You'll hear the sound of hammers before even arriving. The artisans work without particular protection in the heat of braziers, in the old-fashioned way, with impressive efficiency.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

To truly get close to Ban Gioc, board the flat-bottomed boats that local outfitters offer at the foot of the falls. In a few minutes, you find yourself under the spray, a few meters from the Chinese border, with the curtain of water falling right in front of you. Swimming is prohibited at this point - the current is strong even when it appears calm. The best time is early morning (before 9am) when the light is still low and tourists are rare.

⏱ 30 min

If you have time after Ban Gioc and Nguom Ngao, Thang Hen Lake - 'bee tail' in Tay - deserves the detour. This system of 36 lakes connected by underground caves peaks at nearly 1,700 m altitude, surrounded by mirrored karst formations. The whole is classified as a national natural site. The road to climb there is narrow and winding - count 45 min from Ban Gioc. On weekdays, you'll often be alone facing the landscape.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 5

Ha Giang

Gateway to the Dong Van karst plateau, where the Happiness Road winds through clouds

★★☆☆☆
📍 Ha Giang City Vietnam📍 Me Pia Pass Cao Bang Ha Giang📍 Heaven's Gate Quan Ba Ha Giang📍 Fairy Mountains Quan Ba📍 Meo Vac Ha Giang
1

The road from Cao Bang to Ha Giang via Meo Vac is one of northern Vietnam's great crossings - it crosses the Me Pia Pass (Mèo Pì Lèng on the Bao Lac side) in a landscape of karst ridges and embedded valleys that few travelers take in this direction. It's a preview of the Ha Giang loop you'll start tomorrow, but from the back, with fewer people. Stop at viewpoints overlooking the Bao Lac gorges - the contrast between the green forest on the slopes and the gray limestone of the summits is particularly striking after rains.

⏱ 30 min
2

From the Heaven's Gate viewpoint (Cổng Trời), at the entrance to Quan Ba district, you get your first view of the two limestone peaks called 'Fairy Mountains' or 'Fairy Breasts' - two karst cones about 500 m high emerging from a bowl of rice paddies and colorful houses. The Hmong legend tells that a fairy, touched by the inhabitants' misery, gave them her milk to water their fields - the peaks would be the imprint of her body. The viewpoint is at 1,500 m altitude; the 360° view over the geopark opens here for the first time. Arrive in late afternoon for the light.

⏱ 60 min
3

Ha Giang is Vietnam's northernmost provincial capital, 300 km north of Hanoi. The city itself is lively but modest - most of the interest lies in the surrounding districts. On the evening of your arrival, tour the night market to taste regional specialties: thắng cố (horse or pork offal stew, traditional Hmong dish served in mountain markets), mèn mén (corn flour pancake), and corn wine. Organize the logistics for tomorrow's loop here - car, driver, or motorbike - according to your preference.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Before starting the Ha Giang loop, a few practical points to settle upon your arrival in town. In recent years, Vietnamese authorities have required a special permit (giấy phép vùng biên) to travel in the border districts of Dong Van and Meo Vac - local accommodations and Ha Giang agencies provide it in less than an hour, sometimes included in the service. If you take a driver or guide, they'll handle it for you. Also check road conditions in case of recent rains - in the wet season, some sections between Meo Vac and Bao Lac can be cut by landslides.

⏱ 60 min

The Lo River, which crosses Ha Giang, offers a pleasant waterfront stroll after a long day on the road. The quay is the locals' living space at the end of the day - anglers, children swimming, fruit vendors. It's the ideal spot to observe the city before plunging tomorrow into the country's most spectacular landscapes. A few river fish restaurants line the banks; grilled cá lăng (river catfish) is the house specialty.

⏱ 45 min

Stop 6

Dong Van

A village carved into karst cliffs at 1,600 m, where the Hmong King's 64-room palace still stands

★★★★★
📍 Dong Van Old Quarter Ha Giang📍 Hmong King's Palace Sa Phin Dong Van📍 Lung Cu Flag Tower Vietnam📍 Tham Ma Pass Ha Giang📍 Sung La Valley Ha Giang
1

The road from Ha Giang to Dong Van, called the 'Happiness Road' (Con đường Hạnh Phúc), was built between 1959 and 1965 by thousands of young volunteers. It crosses several successive passes: Bac Sum (1,200 m), Cổng Trời (Heaven's Gate), Tham Ma Pass with its 700 m of elevation gain in tight hairpins. In the Sung La Valley, white clay Hmong houses are scattered among flowering gardens - in October-November, buckwheat flowers cover the hills in white and pink. This is the landscape you see in all Ha Giang photos. Stop regularly at viewpoints - the panoramas change at each turn and the road itself is as beautiful as the destinations.

⏱ 60 min
2

Fifteen kilometers from Dong Van, in the Sa Phin Valley, the Vuong family palace is one of northern Vietnam's most astonishing architectures. Vuong Chinh Duc, a Hmong chief who became powerful through opium trade in the region's four districts, had this palace built between 1898 and 1907 for an estimated cost of 15,000 piastres of the time. Designed by artisans from Yunnan (China), the 64-room edifice distributed over three interior courtyards blends Manchurian architecture, Hmong motifs, and French colonial touches. The walls are 50 to 60 cm thick; the columns and floors are made of imported precious wood. Classified as a national monument in 1993, the palace is open daily from 7:30am to 5pm.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Lung Cu tower marks Vietnam's northernmost point, at the summit of Dragon Mountain (Lung Cu) at the Chinese border. The current tower, 33 m high, was renovated in 2010; its foundations date back to the 11th-century Lý dynasty, when General Lý Thường Kiệt planted the national flag there to mark Vietnamese sovereignty. The name Lung Cu means 'corn valley' in Hmong but also 'king's drum' in another reading - the eight Bronze Age Dong Son drums are carved on the tower's base. To reach the summit, you must climb 389 steps in two sections. The view over the karst valleys and Hmong and Lolo villages on both sides of the border is the best panorama of the entire loop.

⏱ 75 min
4

Dong Van's old quarter, built during the colonial era in the early 20th century, is a small ensemble of wooden and stone houses with black tile roofs, set at the foot of the karst plateau's limestone cliffs. The style is a mix of Chinese and Vietnamese shop houses, with canopied facades that protect from the elements. Every Sunday morning, one of northern Vietnam's liveliest ethnic markets is held here, bringing together Hmong, Tay, Nung, and Lo Lo from surrounding villages - women in traditional costume arrive on foot after several hours of walking. If you're not there on a Sunday, the evening stroll through the lantern-lit alleys remains a beautiful experience.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

At the end of the day in Dong Van, seek out one of the old quarter's small cafes that overlook the limestone ridges. Several historic houses dating from 1912, with stone walls and dark wooden beams, have been transformed into rest spaces. Order local tea or corn wine - produced artisanally by Hmong families from the villages. The sunset light on the cliffs takes on an orange tint that lasts about twenty minutes and colors the entire plateau in gold. It's the loop's most photographed moment, and for good reason.

⏱ 45 min

If your visit coincides with the 14th, 15th, or 16th days of the lunar month, Dong Van's night market transforms the old quarter into a popular festival. Craft, embroidery, and local specialty stalls invade the alleys, accompanied by Hmong folk performances. Musicians play the kèn lá (leaf flute) and đàn môi (jaw harp). It's one of the only authentic ethnic night markets on the karst plateau - it has nothing to do with Sapa's tourist markets.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 7

Ha Giang

Gateway to the Dong Van karst plateau, where the Happiness Road winds through clouds

★★☆☆☆
📍 Ma Pi Leng Pass Dong Van Meo Vac📍 Tu San Canyon Nho Que River Ha Giang📍 Meo Vac Sunday Market Ha Giang📍 Nho Que River Viewpoint Ha Giang📍 Happiness Road Ha Giang
1

Ma Pi Leng is the highest and most spectacular point of the Ha Giang loop, classified as a national monument in 2009. The pass peaks at about 1,500 m altitude over 20 km of winding road between Dong Van and Meo Vac - the most vertiginous portion of the Happiness Road, whose construction lasted from 1959 to 1965. From the viewpoints arranged at the summit, your gaze plunges more than 700 m toward Tu San Canyon and the Nho Que River whose emerald waters snake between vertical walls - Southeast Asia's deepest canyon. The geological formations visible on the road's flanks date from 330 to 545 million years ago, with fusulinid fossils directly visible in the limestone. Plan at least 1h30 on site to enjoy the viewpoints - and avoid foggy days.

⏱ 90 min
2

Meo Vac is a town nestled at the bottom of a karst valley, 23 km southeast of Dong Van. Its Sunday morning market is one of northern Vietnam's most authentic, bringing together the Hmong Flower, Black Hmong, Lo Lo, and Nung from the district's remote villages. People come on foot, by motorbike, or on horseback from sometimes several hours away. They negotiate pigs, goats, medicinal herbs, and colorful textiles. Hmong elders smoke long metal pipes in a corner, young girls display their finest costumes. Arrive early - around 7am - to see the gradual arrival of locals.

⏱ 60 min
3

From Ta Lang village or the Xin Cai port, local outfitters offer a 30 to 60-minute cruise in Tu San Canyon on the Nho Que River. You glide between vertical limestone walls 700 to 800 m high - the cliffs are so straight and close together that the sun only reaches the canyon floor at noon. The water, of an intense emerald green, owes its color to the particular mineral concentration of the rocky bottom. It's one of the rare ways to see the canyon from the inside - different and complementary to the plunging view from Ma Pi Leng.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The return road from Meo Vac to Ha Giang via Yen Minh and Quan Ba is less spectacular than the outbound loop but offers different viewpoints, notably over the terraced rice paddies of Yen Minh district and the Hmong Flower villages with brightly painted houses. Take a break at Quan Ba to see the Fairy Mountains from a different angle - in the afternoon, the light is better on the western slope. If you have time, the small Yen Minh market (on Wednesdays) is less known than Meo Vac and quieter.

⏱ 90 min

Buckwheat wine (rượu tam giác mạch) is the region's alcoholic specialty, distilled from buckwheat grain - the same plant whose flowers cover the hills in autumn. It's a clear, strong alcohol (often 40°), with a slightly floral aftertaste. Local producers offer tastings directly at their shop or in guesthouses. To bring back as a souvenir - 500 ml artisanal bottles pack easily and handle the journey.

⏱ 30 min

Stop 8

Hanoi

Where thousand-year-old guild streets meet French colonial heritage on the banks of the Red River

★★★★★
📍 Temple of Literature Hanoi📍 Vietnam Museum of Ethnology Hanoi📍 West Lake Ho Tay Hanoi📍 Tran Quoc Pagoda Hanoi📍 Noi Bai International Airport Hanoi
1

If you have a few hours in Hanoi before your departure, the Temple of Literature is the visit that complements a circuit through the mountainous north well. Founded in 1070 under the Lý dynasty to honor Confucius, it was the seat of Vietnam's first national university from 1076 to 1779. The 82 stone steles perched on marble tortoises - inscribed in the UNESCO Memory of the World register in 2010 - list the names of 1,307 doctorate recipients between 1442 and 1779. The complex of five interior courtyards is one of the rare architectural sites in Vietnam to have remained intact for several centuries. Arrive early - after 9am school groups flood the alleys.

⏱ 60 min
2

After encountering Hmong, Tay, Nung, and Lo Lo in mountain villages, Hanoi's Museum of Ethnology offers valuable context. Inaugurated in 1997 with help from Paris's Museum of Mankind, it presents the 54 officially recognized ethnicities in Vietnam through costumes, tools, musical instruments, and ritual objects. In the outdoor garden, eight traditional full-scale houses have been reconstructed identically - Tay stilt house, Ede longhouse, Bahnar communal house. Count between 1h30 and 2h to tour it seriously. Take a taxi from the center - the museum is in the Cau Giay district, 15 min away.

⏱ 90 min
3

Tran Quoc Pagoda is Hanoi's oldest, founded in the 6th century under the Lý Nam Đế dynasty on the banks of the Red River, then moved to its current promontory on West Lake in the 17th century. Its eleven-story stupa and jade boddhisattva make it one of northern Vietnam's most venerated Buddhist sites. The surrounding West Lake (Ho Tay) is Hanoi's largest lake - 500 hectares - and its shores are lined with flower villages and cafes. In late afternoon, locals come to fish from the pontoons or bike along the dike. It's the city's calmest and greenest spot.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Before hitting the road to the airport, plan a final Vietnamese sit-down meal - it's harder to replicate abroad than you'd think. Phở bò (beef noodle soup) served with its fresh herbs, hoisin sauce, and chopped chili on the side, or bun cha (grilled pork, vermicelli, and vinegar broth) constitute the best culinary memory of northern Vietnam. Old Quarter addresses open from 6:30am and serve these dishes until sold out - and stock often runs out before 10am at the best places.

⏱ 45 min

Noi Bai International Airport is located about 30 km north of downtown Hanoi. Allow plenty of time - traffic during rush hours (7am-9am and 5pm-7pm) can turn this trip into an hour and a half. Official taxis, express bus 86 from Hoan Kiem Lake, or ride-hailing platforms offer the transfer. For international flights, arrive at least 3h before departure. Noi Bai Airport has two terminals - T1 for domestic flights, T2 for international flights, connected by a free shuttle.

⏱ 60 min
