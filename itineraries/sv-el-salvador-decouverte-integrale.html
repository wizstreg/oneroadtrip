✨ Highlights
  • Joya de Cerén, El Salvador's only UNESCO site, inscribed in 1993: a Maya village buried around 600 CE under 5 meters of ash—the 'Pompeii of the Americas,' discovered accidentally in 1976 during silo construction
  • Volcán Ilamatepec (Santa Ana), 2,381 meters, the country's highest peak—its turquoise sulfuric crater lake is visible from the rim, without barriers, surrounded by active fumaroles since the last eruption in 2005
  • The Ruta de las Flores: 35 kilometers of scenic road connecting 5 villages between 700 and 1,225 meters—Juayúa's Feria Gastronómica every weekend for over 20 years
  • El Mozote: on December 11, 1981, the Atlácatl Battalion massacred 978 civilians including 541 children—the memorial lists each name; a trial is underway since 2025
  • Alegría and its Laguna de Alegría, nicknamed 'the Emerald of the Americas' by Chilean poet Gabriela Mistral (1945 Nobel Prize)—sulfurous emerald-green water at the bottom of Volcán Tecapa crater (1,603 meters)
📆 Best period

November, December, January, February, March

⏳ How many days

10 days suggested. 7 days would technically be enough, but we prefer to let each stop breathe.

☀️ Weather

Dry Season (November-April): Perfect weather (22-30°C) with minimal rainfall and ideal conditions for outdoor activities. Peak season with higher prices but optimal beach and volcano conditions.

Wet Season (May-October): Heavy afternoon rains, high humidity, but lush landscapes and fewer crowds. Lower prices, challenging road conditions, dramatic waterfalls.

🌍 Discover

Central American authenticity with volcanic landscapes, traditional culture, and warm hospitality. Experience beautiful nature, traditional crafts, and genuine Central American experiences. 📋 Notes

Joya de Cerén is closed on Mondays—plan Day 2 for another day. The hike to Volcán Ilamatepec (Santa Ana) requires an official guide from Cerro Verde, morning departures only. El Mozote is visited from Perquín, 15 kilometers on partially unpaved road.

⚠️ SAFETY WARNING: High crime rates including gang violence, armed robbery, and kidnapping. Avoid displaying valuables, stay in tourist areas, use reputable transportation, and be particularly cautious in San Salvador and rural areas. Some regions controlled by gangs with restricted access.

San Salvador

★★★★☆
1

Plaza Gerardo Barrios forms the historic heart of San Salvador. The Metropolitan Cathedral—consecrated in 1999 as the third iteration of this temple (the previous two destroyed by earthquakes and fire)—houses the tomb of Archbishop Óscar Romero, assassinated during Mass in 1980 and canonized in 2018. Across the square, step inside the Palacio Nacional: government headquarters until 1976, now a museum showcasing marble staircases, ornate chambers, and portraits of 19th-century presidents.

⏱ 90 min
2

Just steps away, Iglesia El Rosario demands a visit. The unassuming brutalist concrete façade gives no hint of what's inside—then you enter: sculptor Rubén Martínez designed the plans in six days in 1962, with construction completed in 1971. The pillar-free arched structure is lined with stained glass that floods the nave with a rainbow of light. Come mid-morning for maximum illumination. Free entry.

⏱ 45 min
3

Parque Cuscatlán contains the Memorial al Mártir y al Héroe Civil, a somber installation etching the names of thousands of civil war victims (1980-1992). It's a place of reflection frequented by Salvadoran families searching for a name on the stone wall.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Dive into Mercado Central for 30 minutes before leaving: hot pupusas, tropical fruit, handicrafts, and cooking aromas. Stay alert around the market edges, especially late afternoon—mornings are safer.

⏱ 60 min

Santa Ana

★★★☆☆
1

Joya de Cerén is El Salvador's only UNESCO site and one of Central America's most extraordinary archaeological finds. In 600 CE, the Loma Caldera volcano buried this Maya farming village under 5 meters of ash—residents fled, leaving behind houses, kitchens, manioc gardens, and even a temazcal (steam bath), the only intact example ever found in the Maya world. Discovered accidentally in 1976 during silo construction, the site reveals the daily life of Maya peasants rather than kings—which makes it unique. Allow 90 minutes on the wooden walkways above the excavations. Closed Mondays.

⏱ 90 min
2

Five kilometers from Joya de Cerén, San Andrés presents the complementary face of the same civilization: not peasant houses but the political and ceremonial center. Between 600 and 900 CE, San Andrés served as capital of a Maya polity controlling the Zapotitán valley, with an estimated population of 12,000. The Acropolis was constructed by filling an ancient plaza with more than 100,000 adobe blocks. The main pyramid (Structure 1) rises 15 meters. Trade connections with Copán (Honduras) and the Guatemalan Highlands have been proven through ceramics and pyrite mirrors found on site.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Arriving in Santa Ana by evening, stroll around Parque Central: the neo-Gothic cathedral (built 1906-1954) and the Teatro Nacional (1910) face each other. Check the theater's schedule—performances are often pay-what-you-wish. Dine on the square, one of the country's most beautiful.

⏱ 60 min

Santa Ana

★★★☆☆
1

Volcán Ilamatepec (Santa Ana), at 2,381 meters, is El Salvador's highest peak and one of Central America's most spectacular hikes. At the summit, the crater reveals a sulfuric acid lake of surreal turquoise—the color shifts with the light. The last eruption in 2005 hurled car-sized boulders 1.5 kilometers. Mandatory guided departure from Cerro Verde starting at 8:30am. 7-kilometer round trip, 465 meters elevation gain, moderate difficulty. Arrive early—after 10am, clouds often obscure the crater.

⏱ 210 min
2

On the descent, stop at the Tazumal site in Chalchuapa, 25 kilometers east. The main pyramid, 24 meters high, was occupied from 100 CE until the Spanish conquest—over 1,400 years of Pipil and Maya history. Allow an hour on site.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Between the volcano and Tazumal, pause at Lake Coatepeque, a caldera filled with turquoise water at 745 meters elevation. The water stays around 25°C in dry season—swim from lakeside restaurant docks, or simply enjoy the view from the road above.

⏱ 90 min

Concepcion De Ataco

★★★☆☆
1

The Ruta de las Flores winds 35 kilometers through five villages between 700 and 1,225 meters elevation. Start with Nahuizalco, the least touristy: its candlelit night market (open until 10pm) has sold rattan and tule handicrafts for generations. The Nahuatl name means 'the four Izalcos'—the village was founded by four families from Izalco.

⏱ 60 min
2

Concepción de Ataco is the route's most colorful village. Since 2004, when residents participated in the 'Pueblos Vivos' program, hundreds of murals cover the walls, telling Pipil history. Step into the workshops: weavers still work traditional treadle looms, producing hand-embroidered textiles and garments. The village was founded by Pipils in the heart of the Apaneca cordillera.

⏱ 90 min
3

Apaneca, 5 kilometers from Ataco, is the highest village (1,225 meters) and the heart of the coffee region. The Bourbon (64%) and Pacas (26%) varieties, shade-grown on these volcanic soils, rank among the world's most prized coffees. A finca will show you the complete chain, from red cherry to cup.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Operators in Apaneca offer guided tastings with artisanal roasting demonstrations. Allow 90 minutes. You can also combine this with a zipline above the coffee plants—local operators run a 420-meter course above the tropical forest.

⏱ 90 min

Juayua

★★★☆☆
1

Juayúa—'river of purple orchids' in Nahuatl—is the Ruta de las Flores' pivotal village. Its church houses the Cristo Negro, a Black Christ statue carved in the 16th century, object of an annual pilgrimage drawing thousands of faithful. Wander the parque central and side streets—this is the route's liveliest village, with authentic local life.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Los Chorros de la Calera waterfalls lie 2 kilometers from downtown. Water tumbles in several successive falls into natural pools. Hire a guide from your accommodation—the path crosses isolated areas and some incidents have been reported in recent years. Allow 2 hours round trip.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

If you're there on a weekend, the Feria Gastronómica de Juayúa in the parque central is unmissable. Every Saturday and Sunday for over 20 years, dozens of stalls take over the square: gourmet pupusas, grilled shrimp, iguana, specialty coffee. Come hungry—portions are generous and prices ridiculously low.

⏱ 90 min

El Tunco

★★★☆☆
1

El Tunco is the Salvadoran Pacific coast's surf hub. The black volcanic sand beach is dominated by a pig-shaped rock. The waves at El Sunzal, 1 kilometer away, host WSL and ISA competitions—watch the surfers from the rocky point before heading to bed: the evening spectacle, with silhouettes on the swells, is striking.

⏱ 60 min
2

The seafood market in La Libertad, 5 kilometers east, is one of the country's most vibrant. Lobsters, shrimp, fresh mariscos—vendors cook directly on the port. Go early morning, before 9am, when boats return.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Take a surf lesson in El Tunco—operators offer beginner sessions, board and instructor included. The waves are consistent, suitable for beginners, but currents can be strong. Follow instructions carefully. Allow 2 hours.

⏱ 120 min

Walter Thilo Deininger National Park, 12 kilometers east, is Central America's oldest national park (founded 1921). A tropical dry forest sheltering deer, coatis, and iguanas. The main trail takes 2 hours.

⏱ 120 min

El Zonte

★★★☆☆
1

El Zonte, 5 kilometers west of El Tunco, is calmer and more family-friendly. Nicknamed 'Bitcoin Beach' since 2019, when an anonymous donor launched a Bitcoin ecosystem in this fishing village—the experiment directly inspired the 2021 law making Bitcoin legal tender in El Salvador. Between 2015 and 2024, the price per square meter jumped 134.8%. Swim, watch surfers from the river mouth, or explore the sea caves under cliffs accessible at low tide.

⏱ 90 min
2

The coastal walk from El Zonte to El Tunco on foot (5 kilometers, 40 minutes) follows black volcanic cliffs, passing rock formations and isolated coves. Leave at low tide to access rocky passages. It's one of the country's wildest coastal walks.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Tamanique waterfalls lie 15 kilometers inland—several falls into natural pools surrounded by dense tropical forest. Local operators offer transfers from El Zonte. Allow 2.5 hours including travel.

⏱ 150 min

From July to November, community programs organize nighttime baby sea turtle releases. Nestings occur at night and releases at dawn. Ask your accommodation for dates.

⏱ 120 min

Alegria

★★☆☆☆
1

Alegría, at 1,250 meters elevation in the Usulután sierra, is nicknamed the 'Garden of El Salvador' for its tropical flower nurseries. Chilean poet Gabriela Mistral (1945 Nobel Prize for Literature) baptized its volcanic lagoon 'the Emerald of the Americas.' Stroll the white cobblestone streets—the pace here is deliberately slow. The village takes its name from a 19th-century Latin professor, Presbyter José Miguel Alegría, whose remains rest in the crypt of the main church.

⏱ 60 min
2

Laguna de Alegría sits 2 kilometers from downtown, in the crater of Volcán Tecapa (1,603 meters). Its emerald-green sulfurous waters—impossible to swim in, the acid is too concentrated—bubble in places: active fumaroles heat the water from below. Circle the crater on foot (1.5 kilometers, slippery terrain in spots). Locals collect the mineral-rich clay from the banks, using it as a cosmetic treatment.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Mirador de las Cien Gradas ('hundred steps viewpoint'), above the village, offers panoramas across the Tecapa sierra and the coastal plain to the Pacific on clear days. Climb late afternoon for golden light on the coffee plantations below.

⏱ 60 min

Perquin

★★☆☆☆
1

The Museo de la Revolución Salvadoreña in Perquín is one of Central America's most moving museums. The guides are former FMLN guerrillas—they show you the downed helicopter that killed Colonel Domingo Monterrosa in 1984, the clandestine radios of Radio Venceremos, the guerrillas' improvised weapons. A living memorial to the 12-year civil war (1980-1992) that left 75,000 dead and 8,000 disappeared.

⏱ 90 min
2

El Mozote, 15 kilometers from Perquín, is an essential memorial site. On December 11, 1981, the Atlácatl Battalion killed 978 civilians—220 men, 200 women, 541 children, most under 12 years old. The memorial lists each name by family. Descendants of the victims will greet you on site. A criminal trial was initiated in late 2025.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Morazán mountains around Perquín conceal waterfalls in a pine forest at 1,200 meters elevation. Local guides organize 3-4 hour hikes to La Olomina and Las Pilas. The cool climate (often below 18°C) contrasts sharply with the rest of the country.

⏱ 180 min

Suchitoto

★★★☆☆
1

Suchitoto is often called El Salvador's most beautiful town. Its cobblestone streets, pastel adobe colonial houses, and Santa Lucía church—built in 1853 on the ruins of a 17th-century straw temple—make it feel timeless. Founded in 1528 by the Spanish as the country's first capital, it grew wealthy on indigo, nicknamed 'blue gold.' Get lost in the side streets in the morning, before the weekend influx of local visitors.

⏱ 90 min
2

Los Tercios waterfall sits 1 kilometer from downtown in the quebrada Las Ánimas. What makes it unique: a vertical wall of perfectly aligned hexagonal basalt columns—10 meters high, as if hand-carved. It flows during rainy season (June-November); in dry season, the geological formation alone justifies the trip. Three miradors also offer views over Lake Suchitlán.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Lake Suchitlán, created in 1976 by the Cerrón Grande dam, spans 135 square kilometers. Take a boat from San Juan port to Isla de los Pájaros—a sanctuary with over 170 recorded bird species—and Isla del Ermitaño. Allow 90 minutes on the water. Leave early morning for birdwatching.

⏱ 90 min

Late afternoon before returning to San Salvador (50 kilometers, 1 hour), climb to the miradors overlooking the lake from downtown. Sunset over Suchitlán from the colonial house terraces is one of El Salvador's most photographed scenes.

⏱ 60 min
