Vilnius

★★★★☆
1

Inscribed on UNESCO's World Heritage List in 1994, Vilnius Old Town covers 3.5 km² of medieval streets and blends Gothic, Baroque, and Renaissance—all on a street plan that has barely changed in seven centuries. It's the largest surviving baroque old town north of the Alps, and by far the most easterly. Allow half a day to wander its lanes without map or agenda.

⏱ 120 min
2

Touristy, yes—but St. Anne's Church deserves the stop. This red-brick Gothic gem from the 15th century so captivated Napoleon that, legend has it, he wanted to carry it back to Paris in the palm of his hand. The adjacent Bernardine Church forms a striking ecclesiastical ensemble with it. Best seen early morning for the light on the facade.

⏱ 45 min
3

A whisper of rosaries, the scent of incense: the Gate of Dawn concentrates all of Lithuania's Catholic devotion within a chapel six meters high. This 16th-century gate is the sole survivor of the nine original gates in the medieval city wall. Come on a Sunday morning to witness the procession of pilgrims.

⏱ 30 min
4

Walk Pilies Street from north to south: it connects the cathedral to the Gate of Dawn by cutting through the heart of the old town, past baroque cafés and amber stalls. It's Vilnius's liveliest artery—street musicians included. Take time to spot the hidden courtyards behind the portals.

⏱ 45 min
5

Lithuanians simply call it 'the castle': Gediminas Tower has dominated Vilnius from its hill since the 14th century. Gediminas, legendary founder of the capital, is said to have dreamed here of an iron wolf howling—interpreted as the sign of a great city to be born. The climb is worth it for the 360° view over the baroque rooftops.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

If it's your first day in Vilnius, join an evening walking tour: local guides reveal the medieval, Soviet, and post-independence layers that the facades don't tell. Count on 2 hours, around €15-20. Book ahead in high season.

⏱ 120 min

Vilnius

★★★★☆
1

Excessively baroque or quietly restrained? The Palace of the Grand Dukes of Lithuania plays both hands: the underground ruins of the original 15th-century palace sit alongside upper floors reconstructed with period furniture and tapestries. A virtual reality experience plunges visitors into medieval Vilnius. Allow 1.5 hours, closed Mondays.

⏱ 90 min
2

Founded in 1579, Vilnius University was shut down by Russian tsars in 1832 and didn't reopen for a century. Its thirteen interconnected courtyards traverse four centuries of architectural styles. St. John's Church within the complex conceals an interior of rare baroque richness—and its tower offers the finest view over the old town's rooftops.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Head down to the Užupis quarter, the self-proclaimed 'Artists' Republic' established in 1997 with its own constitution engraved on plaques in twenty languages. This bohemian micro-state of a few streets beside the Vilnia River is home to all the capital's eccentrics. Terrace cafés, ephemeral galleries: plan an hour of aimless wandering.

⏱ 60 min

Trakai

★★★☆☆
1

Red brick on green water: Trakai Castle rises from its island in the middle of Lake Galvė like a Gothic apparition. Built by Duke Kęstutis in the late 14th century and completed by Vytautas the Great around 1409, it served as the royal residence and state treasury of the Grand Duchy of Lithuania. After centuries of ruin, restoration in the 1950s-1960s returned its medieval bearing.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Rent a kayak from the Karaite village dock to circle the castle from the water: the perspective from the lake is incomparable. Lake Galvė has 20 islands—the full loop by kayak takes 1.5 hours. Avoid July weekends when boats compete for space.

⏱ 90 min

Kernave

★★★★☆
1

Five mounds in a misty valley: few know that Kernavė, inscribed on UNESCO's list in 2004, concentrates 10,000 years of human occupation on 194 hectares. It's the former political capital of the Grand Duchy of Lithuania, abandoned in the 14th century after Teutonic Knight raids and never rebuilt. This sudden disappearance preserved intact this unique archaeological landscape.

Kaunas

★★★☆☆
1

In 1920, with Vilnius occupied by Poland, Kaunas became Lithuania's provisional capital. In twenty years, this provincial town sprouted more than 12,000 modernist buildings—Art Deco, neoclassical, functionalist—to the point of being inscribed on UNESCO's list in 2023 as the only European city representing large-scale interwar urbanization. The medieval Old Town and the modernist New Quarter coexist effortlessly.

⏱ 90 min
2

Kaunasians call it the 'Swan': the white Gothic Town Hall has dominated Town Hall Square for centuries, surrounded by merchants' facades and hidden churches. Kaunas Castle from the 14th century, built to repel the Teutonic Knights, stands two steps away, partly ruined but evocative. Lively square in the evening, markets on weekends.

⏱ 60 min
3

Provincial yesterday, UNESCO-listed in 2023 today: Kaunas's modernist quarter (Naujamiestis and Žaliakalnis) is an absolute surprise. Buildings from 1919-1939 with sober yet elaborate facades reveal an architectural optimism that Soviet occupation nearly erased. Birutė Hill and its staircases offer a panoramic view over the rooftops.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Walk the 1.6 km of car-free Laisvės Avenue: this pedestrian boulevard links the Old Town to the New Quarter along a line of cafés and Lithuanian boutiques. It's the country's longest pedestrian street. Go late afternoon to see local life in full swing.

⏱ 60 min

Siauliai

★★☆☆☆
1

Tens of thousands of crosses in the wind, the rattle of rosaries in the silence of the plains: the Hill of Crosses (Kryžių kalnas) is one of the strangest and most moving sites in the Baltics. The first crosses appeared after the anti-Russian uprising of 1831. The Soviets bulldozed the site three times—and every night, Lithuanians returned to plant new ones. Arrive early morning to avoid the tour buses.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Take time to absorb the profound atmosphere of this unique sacred site, where faith and history intertwine in the most tangible way imaginable. Many visitors choose to add their own cross to the collection, perpetuating a tradition spanning more than two centuries. The Hill of Crosses stands as testimony to the indomitable spirit of the Lithuanian people and their unwavering commitment to religious freedom.

⏱ 60 min

Klaipeda

★★☆☆☆
1

Founded by the Teutonic Knights in 1252 as Memelburg, Klaipėda was Prussian, French, Swedish, and Soviet before becoming Lithuanian again in 1923. This composite history reads in its half-timbered lanes and Theatre Square, where Richard Wagner lived and worked early in his career. The old town was rebuilt after the 1854 fire on a grid plan—more rectilinear than its Baltic sisters.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

If weather permits, take the ferry to the peninsula facing the old town: Klaipėda's aquarium sits there in a baroque building by the water, 1.8 km from shore. It's also the embarkation point for the Curonian Spit. The crossing takes 5 minutes and costs a few euros—essential for the next leg of the itinerary.

⏱ 60 min

Nida

★★★☆☆
1

No other place in Europe resembles the Curonian Spit, inscribed on UNESCO's list in 2000: 98 km of dunes between the Baltic Sea and the Curonian Lagoon, just 400 meters wide at its narrowest point. Legend says a giantess named Neringa formed it while playing on the beach. Reality is stranger still: entire villages were swallowed by the dunes in the 17th-18th centuries.

⏱ 60 min
2

At 52 meters above sea level, Parnidis Dune dominates both the lagoon and the Baltic Sea—a breathtaking view of both faces. A sandstone sundial marks the summit. The dune has lost nearly 10 meters of height in thirty years: every visitor who climbs displaces sand. The Parnidis educational trail starts from Nida village.

⏱ 90 min
3

White sand slips underfoot between dense pines and Nordic sky: the village of Nida has attracted artists and writers for more than a century. Thomas Mann spent several summers here in the 1930s in a house that's now a museum. Jean-Paul Sartre made a memorable stop in 1965—a statue commemorates it. Look for the painted wooden weathervanes typical on the roofs.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Take the Grey Dunes trail in the heart of the Naglių nature reserve to see the landscapes that swallowed villages. These 'dead' dunes, stabilized by centuries of pine reforestation, create a surreal environment of pale sand and twisted trunks. The Nagliai educational trail is about 4 km—avoid the heat of the day in summer.

⏱ 120 min

Palanga

★★★★☆
1

A 19th-century palace in a botanical park to showcase amber: Palanga's Amber Museum is as unusual as it is successful. Housed in the Tiškevičius Manor, it displays more than 4,500 pieces of Baltic gold, including insect inclusions tens of millions of years old. The adjacent park—designed by French landscape architect Édouard André—is worth the visit alone.

⏱ 90 min
2

Iodized air and pine resin mingled: Palanga and its 470-meter pier extend into a surprisingly calm Baltic Sea. The town is Lithuania's main seaside resort, with 16,000 winter inhabitants and summer crowds. The pedestrian Jono Basanavičiaus Street concentrates amber shops, restaurants, and old wooden villas. Sunset on the pier is mandatory.

⏱ 60 min

Kuldiga

★★★★☆
1

At 249 meters wide, the Venta Rapid is Europe's widest waterfall—but only 1.8 meters high. That's Kuldīga's paradox: a cascade that impresses by breadth, not power. In spring, salmon leap upstream; in the 17th century, they were caught in baskets suspended above the current. Free entry, accessible year-round.

⏱ 45 min
2

Kuldīga looks like a postcard—and it's true. Its leaning wooden cottages from the 1700s, cobbled lanes, and elegant baroque St. Catherine's Church form one of Latvia's best-preserved historic centers. The town prospered under the Duchy of Courland and had no major industry to disfigure it. Avoid July weekends: buses arrive in groups.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Push on to the Riezupe sandstone caves for a striking contrast with yesterday's dunes: these tunnels carved in sandstone by natural erosion create a cool, mysterious underground labyrinth. Accessible on foot from the Venta riverbank. Flashlight useful, modest entrance fee.

Ventspils

1

Founded in the second half of the 13th century, Ventspils Castle of the Livonian Order is one of Latvia's best-preserved medieval fortresses—it has kept its original footprint for 700 years. Fortress, residence, garrison, school, prison: the castle has done it all before becoming the Ventspils Museum in 2001, featuring an award-winning digital exhibition.

⏱ 75 min
2

Flower beds at every intersection: Ventspils claims the title of Latvia's floral capital and earns it every summer. The city adds floral sculptures and parterres to its promenades from spring onwards. The market square and Skolas Street show the mix of civic pride and historic architecture that distinguishes this port city.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Stroll the Blue Flag-certified beach of Ventspils before heading to Jūrmala: the city has invested massively in its coastal amenities and the result is worth seeing. Harbor promenade, monumental 23-ton anchor, little 1916 steam train: Ventspils is also an unexpected playground. Take the tourist train to reach the beach from the center.

The 'Latvian Riviera' has earned its credentials: Jūrmala offers 33 km of fine sandy beach bordered by dunes and pine forests. Brezhnev and Khrushchev came here to take the waters. Activities

Well-maintained boardwalk trails wind through this unique ecosystem, allowing visitors to explore without disturbing the fragile environment. The park offers exceptional birdwatching opportunities with numerous rare species inhabiting these atmospheric wetlands.

Riga

★★★★★
1

The cobblestones of Riga's old town echo beneath Gothic, Baroque, and Neoclassical facades that succeed one another without logic—that's the beauty of the place, UNESCO-listed since 1997. The reconstructed House of the Blackheads presides over Town Hall Square; St. Peter's Church and its soaring spire organize the panorama. Allow half a day for the lanes and a pause in one of the square's many cafés.

⏱ 120 min
2

More than 800 Art Nouveau facades in Riga's so-called 'Quiet Center'—the largest concentration in Europe. Alberta Street concentrates the most spectacular creations of Mikhail Eisenstein (father of the filmmaker): howling masks, winged women, and giant medallions on seven different buildings. Built between 1901 and 1908, no two are alike. Visit the Art Nouveau Museum at no. 12, closed Mondays.

⏱ 90 min
3

Riga's Central Market is spectacular: five former zeppelin hangars converted into halls where Riga Black Balsam rubs shoulders with local cheeses and smoked meats. It's one of Europe's largest covered markets and the place where Riga has done its shopping since the 1930s. Arrive before 10 a.m. to see stalls at full capacity and prices before the tourist influx.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

If you arrive on a weekday, the market is less crowded and vendors more available to explain their products. Taste the local craft beer and grey peas with smoked bacon, a Latvian specialty. The halls generally close at 5 p.m.; best to spend the morning there and devote the afternoon to the Art Nouveau district.

⏱ 60 min

1

42 meters of granite, neither toppled nor moved under Soviet occupation: the Freedom Monument (Brīvības piemineklis) is one of the rare statues to have survived the Soviet era in place. Latvians affectionately call it 'Milda.' Erected in 1935, it represents a woman holding three golden stars symbolizing the country's historic regions. Permanent honor guard.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Latvian Ethnographic Open-Air Museum gathers more than 100 historic buildings—farms, windmills, workshops—transferred from across the country into a forest park on Riga's edge. It's rural Latvian life across several centuries made visible in one walk. Seasonal events with traditional crafts and folk dances make the visit immersive.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Take the tram to Mežaparks park to leave the center and see the residential Riga of the 1920s-1930s: Art Nouveau villas and private gardens in a quarter the guides avoid. The park itself overlooks Lake Ķīšezers—good option for a picnic. Return by tram to the center in 20 minutes.

In autumn, the forest turns red and gold—the best season for this section. Bobsleigh and luge activities available in winter.

In 1577, 300 besieged defenders blew themselves up with gunpowder rather than surrender to Ivan the Terrible: Cēsis Medieval Castle has a history to match its imposing ruins. Built from the early 13th century, it was for two centuries the residence of the Grand Master of the Livonian Order. Today you visit the towers by candlelight provided at the entrance—a theatrically effective touch.

⏱ 90 min
2

The cobbled streets of Cēsis and its white-and-yellow painted wooden houses form one of Latvia's best-preserved historic centers. St. John's Church contains the tombs of Knights of the Livonian Order. Rozu Square and May Park below create a tranquil setting to finish the journey before returning to Riga.
