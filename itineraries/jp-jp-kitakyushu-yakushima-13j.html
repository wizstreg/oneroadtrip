Kitakyushu

★★★☆☆
1

The Mojiko Retro district is a walk through Meiji-era Japan without the artificial reconstruction. Moji Port opened in 1889 as an international trade hub, and its Western-style buildings survived - a rarity in Japan. Mojiko Station (1914), the first station in Japan designated an Important Cultural Property, sets the tone with its neo-renaissance facade. Two minutes on foot, the Mitsui Club (1921) is a half-timbered mansion built by Mitsui & Co. to host merchants and their clients: Albert Einstein stayed here in 1922, and the room he occupied remains in its original state. Wander down to the waterfront to see the Blue Wing Moji, a 108-meter pedestrian drawbridge that opens every hour to let boats pass - a spectacle in itself.

⏱ 90 min
2

Kokura Castle has dominated the city since 1602, when Hosokawa Tadaoki had it built following the Battle of Sekigahara. The original structure burned in 1866 during inter-clan warfare, and the current keep - reconstructed in concrete in 1959 - adopts a karazukuri style where the top floor is larger than the one below, giving it its distinctive silhouette. From the top, 360° views over Kitakyushu. The park is lined with 300 cherry trees in spring. The small historical detail absent from most guides: in August 1945, Kokura was the primary target for the second atomic bomb. That morning, thick fog blanketed the city. After three unsuccessful passes, the B-29 diverted to its secondary target - Nagasaki.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Mojiko's signature dish is yaki curry: rice and curry baked together in the oven with cheese until a golden, crispy crust forms. Every restaurant in the district has its own recipe. Expect a 30-minute wait on weekends. In winter (late November to mid-February), over 300,000 lights illuminate the historic buildings for Mojiko's Roman Illumination - one of Kyushu's most spectacular night displays.

⏱ 60 min

From Mojiko Port, a small ferry crosses in 5 minutes to Karato Market in Shimonoseki (Honshu) - perfect for a fresh fugu (pufferfish) sashimi break, the city's specialty. An international crossing just steps from your overnight stay.

⏱ 60 min

Kitakyushu

★★★☆☆
1

Hidden in the forested hills south of Kitakyushu lies Kawachi Fujien, a private garden opened in 1977 by the Higuchi family. In 2015, CNN selected it among Japan's 31 most beautiful sites - and attendance exploded. The main attraction: two wisteria tunnels, one 80 meters and the other 110 meters long, under which violet, white, and pink cascades fall like curtains. The large pergola covers over 3,000 tsubo (roughly 10,000 sq ft) of trellis draped with 22 wisteria varieties. Peak viewing is late April to early May - advance online booking mandatory during peak season, tickets sell out fast. In autumn (mid-November to early December), maples take over.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

To reach the garden, take the train to Yahata Station, then Nishitetsu bus #56 to the Kamishigeta terminus (25 minutes). From the stop, it's about 45-55 minutes on foot - bring comfortable shoes. By car, the journey is much simpler (free parking). Outside the wisteria bloom peak, entry is free and the garden nearly empty: another way to discover it at your own pace.

⏱ 30 min

Fukuoka

★★★★☆
1

Dazaifu Tenmangu is Japan's largest Tenjin shrine - the kami of learning - and it's built directly over the grave of Sugawara no Michizane (845-903). Michizane was a brilliant scholar at the Heian imperial court, exiled to Dazaifu by the jealous Fujiwara clan. He died two years later, and disasters immediately struck the capital. The official interpretation: his vengeful spirit. The shrine was erected to appease him, and he was deified as Tenjin. The current main hall (honden) dates from 1591, Momoyama style - it's been under renovation since 2023 for the first time in 124 years (reopening scheduled for 2027). A temporary plant-based pavilion designed by contemporary architect Sou Fujimoto serves as replacement. The grounds hold 6,000 plum trees, including the Tobiume - the plum tree that allegedly flew from Kyoto to follow Michizane into exile.

⏱ 90 min
2

Kushida Shrine, in Hakata's historic quarter, has been the city's protective deity since the 8th century. This is where Fukuoka's largest festival, Hakata Gion Yamakasa, begins each year. Inside, the permanent exhibition displays the 14 festival floats (yamakasa) used during the race: these wooden and bamboo structures covered with samurai and mythological hero figurines reach several meters high. The 1,000-year-old ginkgo tree in the grounds deserves an upward glance.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Ask your accommodation where to find the nearest yatai: these small covered street stalls set up each evening on Fukuoka's sidewalks, serving tonkotsu ramen (creamy pork broth), gyoza, and yakitori skewers. Fukuoka is the only major Japanese city where yatai culture still thrives - about a hundred stalls operate nightly in the Nakasu, Tenjin, and Nagahama districts. There's no menu and you eat elbow-to-elbow - that's the essence.

⏱ 90 min

If you have time, the Kyushu National Museum, just above Dazaifu Tenmangu, traces cultural exchanges between Japan and Asia from prehistory - one of Japan's four national museums. Its undulating glass-covered architecture, designed by Kikutake Kiyonori, is spectacular in itself. The permanent collection covers the maritime route between China, Korea, and the Japanese archipelago.

⏱ 90 min

Itoshima

★★★☆☆
1

40 minutes west of Fukuoka, Sakurai Futamigaura beach in Itoshima offers one of Kyushu's most photographed views: a pristine white torii standing in the Genkai Sea, and behind it, two rocks connected by a 30-meter sacred shimenawa rope weighing about a ton. These Meoto Iwa (literally "wedded rocks") represent the deities Izanagi and Izanami from Japanese mythology - the couple who created Japan's islands. The rope is replaced annually during a high-tide ceremony in April or May. The male rock measures 11.8 meters, the female 11.2 meters. At low tide, you can walk to the torii's base. Come late afternoon: Sakurai Futamigaura is known as "the sunset Futamiura" - unlike Ise's, which is famous for sunrise.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Sunset Road runs along Itoshima's west coast for several kilometers, lined with cafes and restaurants with sea views - a route to cycle or drive at sunset. Itoshima is also known for its artisan salt (shio) producers: some workshops offer visits and direct sales. Local cheeses (rare for Japan), Ago Bay oysters, and organic vegetables attract Fukuoka residents to the weekend market.

⏱ 60 min

Karatsu

★★☆☆☆
1

Karatsu Castle, also called the "dancing crane castle" (Maizurujō), is one of the rare Japanese castles using the ocean as a natural moat - its stone walls emerge directly from the bay. Built between 1602 and 1608 by Terasawa Hirotaka using materials salvaged from the dismantled Nagoya Castle, it was then surrounded by a pine forest that, viewed from neighboring Mount Kagamiyama, resembled a crane spreading its wings. The forest has nearly disappeared on the west side, but the Nijino Matsubara pine grove - over a million black pines planted along 5 km of coast by Terasawa to protect farmland from sea winds - survives on the east, ranked among Japan's three most beautiful pine groves. The current keep dates from 1966 (concrete) but the 5th floor offers superb panoramic views over the bay, city, and pine forest.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Hikiyama Exhibition Hall, facing Karatsu Shrine, displays year-round the 14 floats from the Karatsu Kunchi festival (November 2-4). These lacquered wooden and bamboo structures - red lions, samurai helmets, giant fish - were crafted between 1819 and 1876. If you're not there in November, it's the only way to see these artisan masterpieces up close.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

If you pass through Yobuko port, 30 minutes by bus north of Karatsu, don't miss the Asaichi morning market: 200 meters of fresh seafood stalls run by local fisherwomen, open from 7:30 am. The specialty is ikizukuri squid - squid caught that morning, prepared alive as sashimi, tentacles still moving on the plate. The experience isn't for the faint-hearted, but the taste is unparalleled.

⏱ 60 min

Karatsu is one of Japan's major pottery centers (karatsu-yaki). The tradition dates back to the 16th century and today counts about 70 workshops in and around the city. Several galleries let you watch potters at work and buy directly. If ceramics interest you, plan a good hour to explore the potters' quarter.

⏱ 60 min

Sasebo

★★★☆☆
1

Huis Ten Bosch ("House in the Forest" in Dutch) is Japan's largest theme park by area: 153 hectares, 6 km of canals, 400,000 trees planted during its construction. Opened in 1992 on a reclaimed island in Omura Bay, it recreates a 17th-century Dutch town with full-scale buildings. The name comes from Huis ten Bosch palace in The Hague, one of the Dutch royal family's residences. The location is no accident: for over two centuries, the Dutch were the only Europeans authorized to trade with Japan, via their trading post in Hirado then Dejima in Nagasaki, 35 km from here. The park has 11 attractions, 12 museums, 53 restaurants, and several hotels including one staffed by robots. Peak season is the spring tulip festival, but the nighttime illuminations (nearly 13 million LEDs) are worth the trip any season.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

Rent a bike or electric boat to explore the canals at your own pace - the best way to see the park without rushing. Canal cruises (about 30 minutes) give a different perspective on the buildings. Around 7 pm, when illuminations light up the entire artificial city, the atmosphere transforms dramatically: plan to stay for the evening.

⏱ 120 min

If Huis Ten Bosch doesn't occupy your whole day, Hirado Island, 45 minutes north of Sasebo, deserves a half-day: this is where the first Dutch landed in 1609, and the Catholic church (1931) visible from the feudal castle makes a very particular photographic composition - symbol of the historical coexistence between clandestine Christianity and feudal power.

⏱ 90 min

Nagasaki

★★★★☆
1

On August 9, 1945 at 11:02 am, the "Fat Man" bomb exploded 580 meters above the Urakami district. Kokura was the primary target - but the city was covered in smoke that morning, result of bombing the previous day in Yahata. After three unsuccessful attempts over Kokura, the B-29 set course for its secondary target. Nagasaki's fate was linked to that of Kitakyushu, your departure city. Peace Park, inaugurated in 1955, sits next to the hypocenter. The Peace Statue - 10 meters of bronze, right hand pointing toward the nuclear threat, left hand extended toward peace - was created by sculptor Seibō Kitamura, a Nagasaki native. Reflect here before visiting the museum.

⏱ 60 min
2

Mount Inasa (333 meters), west of the city, ranks among Japan's three most beautiful night views. Nagasaki city, built in a basin surrounded by hills, lights up at nightfall in a unique way - houses tier up the slopes to the water. It's nicknamed "the 10-million-dollar view." The cable car departs from the port's west bank and reaches the summit in 5 minutes. Ascend 30 minutes before sunset to watch the transition.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Shinchi district is one of Japan's three main Chinatowns, founded in the 17th century by Chinese merchants trading through Nagasaki port. Specialties to try: champon (vegetable and seafood noodle soup, created here in the 19th century) and kakuni manju (steamed rice buns stuffed with braised pork). The artificial island of Dejima, five minutes on foot, was the only point of contact between closed Japan and the Western world between 1641 and 1853: a 15,000 m² enclosure where the Dutch were authorized to trade. It's been reconstructed and can be visited.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Take Nagasaki's streetcar - lines in service since 1914, fixed-price tickets - to reach the Atomic Bomb Museum from Peace Park. The museum, opened in 1994 for the 50th anniversary, presents objects recovered from the site, photographs, and testimonies from hibakusha (survivors). The visit is difficult. Allow at least 1.5 hours, and know that certain rooms are particularly harrowing.

⏱ 90 min

Glover Garden, on a hill overlooking the bay south of the city, was the property of Scottish merchant Thomas Blake Glover (1838-1911), one of the first industrialists to modernize Japan. Glover participated in creating the country's first railway depot and brewery. The view from its terraces over Nagasaki Bay and distant Sakurajima is splendid, especially at sunset.

⏱ 60 min

Kumamoto

★★★☆☆
1

Kumamoto Castle is one of Japan's three most admired castles along with Himeji and Osaka. Its construction began in 1601 under orders from Kato Kiyomasa, and the fortress then occupied 98,000 m² with 3 large towers, 49 turrets, 18 gates, and 29 portals. The April 2016 earthquake series (magnitude 7) severely damaged the grounds - one-third of the stone walls, or 23,500 m², were compromised, and reconstruction is scheduled until 2052. The main keep reopened in June 2021 after five years of work. The damage still visible on the ramparts is part of the visit: you understand the catastrophe's power, and the stones carefully numbered and catalogued to be replaced one by one tell another story - that of Japanese patience.

⏱ 90 min
2

Suizenji Jojuen sanctuary, 15 minutes by streetcar from the castle, is one of Kyushu's most renowned Japanese gardens. Created in 1632 by the Hosokawa clan who had just settled in Kumamoto, it reproduces in miniature the 53 stages of the Tokaido route linking Tokyo to Kyoto - including notably a 10-meter-high grass Mount Fuji. The natural water spring at the garden's depths is renowned for its purity.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

From the 14th floor of Kumamoto City Hall (free access), the plunging view over the castle grounds gives exceptional scale to the site - you grasp the fortress's immensity and its gardens. Don't miss the Sakura-no-baba Josaien complex, facing the castle: this is where Kumamon mascot (the black and red bear emblem of the prefecture) is sold in every possible variation.

⏱ 45 min

Kumamoto's culinary specialty is basashi - raw horse carpaccio served with soy sauce, ginger, and garlic. If you're curious and not too squeamish, it's a taste experience found only in this region. Several izakaya in the Shimotori district serve it, along with other specialties like karashi renkon (lotus stuffed with mustard and fried).

⏱ 60 min

Beppu

★★★★☆
1

Beppu is Japan's hot spring capital. The city produces more hot spring water than any other onsen resort in the country. Its signature attraction is the Jigoku Meguri - the Hell Circuit. These aren't baths for bathing, but extreme-temperature springs (over 100°C) that create unique geothermal spectacles. Umi Jigoku ("sea hell") is the most impressive: a steaming cobalt-blue basin with giant water lilies capable of holding a child. Chinoike Jigoku ("blood pond hell") - blood-red due to iron oxide in the clay - is the oldest hell, documented for over 1,300 years. Tatsumaki Jigoku is a natural geyser that erupts every 30 to 40 minutes and propels 150°C water up to 30 meters.

⏱ 120 min
2

Kamado Jigoku ("cooking pot hell") is the most diverse hell: several different-colored basins, a demon cook statue, foot and hand baths, and snacks steamed using volcanic heat. It's the best if you're only visiting one. Oniishibozu Jigoku ("monk head hell") gets its name from the gray mud bubbles that surface and resemble shaved skulls. It's also the only hell where you can actually bathe in the attached public bath, fed by the same spring.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The hells are grouped in two zones: five in the Kannawa district to the north, two in Shibaseki 2 km away. Buy the combination pass to see all seven official ones. Count a full day to see them all - or choose the four essential ones (Umi, Kamado, Oniishibozu, Chinoike) for 4 to 5 hours. Start early morning to avoid Japanese tour groups that arrive after 10 am.

⏱ 240 min

After the hells, the sand bath (sunamushi) at Kanawa Onsen is Beppu's most singular thermal experience: you're buried up to your neck in sand naturally heated to 50°C by underground springs, for about 10 to 15 minutes. Bring a towel. It's recommended for muscle pain and circulation. Different establishments offer this experience in the Kannawa district.

⏱ 60 min

Takachiho

★★★☆☆
1

Takachiho Gorge cuts 7 kilometers through a V-shaped valley carved by the Gokase River through solidified lava flows from Mount Aso, 270,000 years old. The columnar basalt cliffs reach 80 to 100 meters. The water is an emerald green that justifies the legend: according to the Kojiki (Japan's oldest collection of myths), Manai Falls - 17 meters high - was moved here by a god to supply water to a region that lacked it. Designated a national scenic spot in 1934, the gorge is accessible on foot via a 1 km trail along the cliff edge. The other option - the best - is the rental boat for 30 minutes, maximum 3 people: you find yourself at the base of the walls, within arm's reach of the waterfall. Book early morning in high season, spots go fast.

⏱ 90 min
2

Amano Iwato Shrine, 8 km from the gorge, is Takachiho's most sacred mythological site. This is where the sun goddess Amaterasu allegedly took refuge in a cave after a dispute with her brother Susanoo, plunging the world into darkness. The other gods gathered before the cave and organized a banquet - a goddess began dancing so spectacularly that all laughed uproariously. Amaterasu, curious, partially opened her cave stone to look, and the gods prevented her from returning inside. This is the first Kagura dance - ritual art still practiced every winter night in Takachiho in 33 tableaux. Amano Yasukawara Cave, 10 minutes on foot from the shrine along the river, is covered with thousands of cairns left by visitors making wishes.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

The Kagura dance performance at Takachiho Jinja Shrine happens every evening at 8 pm for about 1 hour - a condensed version of the full night's 33 tableaux. Dancers wear traditional masks and long-sleeved costumes, accompanied by flute, drum, and cymbals. It's one of Japan's oldest forms of sacred theater. Modest entry fee, sober and genuinely inhabited performance - not to be missed if you stay overnight.

⏱ 60 min

Takachiho is difficult to access - since 2005, no train serves the village following a typhoon. You must arrive by bus from Kumamoto (2h) or Miyazaki (2.5h), or by car. Plan to stay overnight to have time to see the gorge, shrine, and evening performance. Village ryokan serve local specialties like mountain taro and Takachiho beef.

⏱ 30 min

Aso

★★★☆☆
1

Mount Aso is Japan's largest volcanic complex, with a caldera 25 km north to south and 18 km east to west - 100,000 people live inside. At the center, Nakadake (1,506 m) is the main active crater: 600 meters in diameter, 160 meters deep, and a green acid lake at the bottom. Since the 1990s, concrete bunkers have been built near the crater to shelter from projectiles in case of sudden eruption. Access depends on the day's volcanic activity - consult aso-volcano.jp before departing. The last major eruption was October 2021. If access is closed, the volcano museum broadcasts live crater images.

⏱ 90 min
2

Kusasenrigahama grassland, between Mounts Kishima and Eboshi, is a one-kilometer-diameter basin where horses and cows graze freely facing Nakadake's smoke - one of Kyushu's most photographed landscapes. In autumn, pampas grass (susuki) turns golden. Daikanbo Observatory, on the caldera's northern periphery, offers the most spectacular panoramic view of Aso's five peaks - their recumbent profile evokes a reclining Buddha. It's also one of the best spots for the sea of clouds in autumn and winter, when morning mist fills the caldera.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Komezuka, a perfectly symmetrical 50-meter volcanic cone covered in green grass, is one of Aso's iconic symbols. Locals say it resembles an upside-down bowl of rice - "kome" means rice. It photographs ideally from the road crossing the caldera, stop at the designated parking area.

⏱ 30 min

For those denied crater access, helicopter flights from the launch zone south of the caldera offer a plunging view over Nakadake and its fumaroles otherwise unobtainable. Count about 7 minutes of flight. Hot springs in the Aso region - notably those at Kurokawa Onsen, 1 hour north - are among Kyushu's most renowned for their traditional ryokan with outdoor baths.

⏱ 60 min

Kagoshima

★★★★☆
1

Sengan-en Garden, created in 1658 by the 19th lord of the Shimazu clan, is the masterpiece of shakkei - the art of "borrowed scenery": the garden is designed to integrate Sakurajima volcano and Kinko Bay as natural backdrop, creating the illusion that the composition extends infinitely. Across 50,000 m², this park of the Shimazu family villa houses historic rooms open to the public (Edward VIII and Nicholas II were received here), a shrine, and the Shoko Shuseikan museum - installed in Japan's first modern factory, built by the Shimazu in the 1850s with techniques learned from a Dutch manual. In 2015, the site was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in the category "Sites of Japan's Meiji Industrial Revolution."

⏱ 90 min
2

35 km southwest of Kagoshima, Chiran is a dual town. On one side, an Edo-period samurai quarter with seven classified dry gardens, nestled behind high stone walls. On the other, the Peace Museum dedicated to kamikaze pilots, built in 1975 on the site of the former airbase. Of the 1,036 Special Attack Corps pilots who died during the Battle of Okinawa, 439 departed from Chiran. The museum preserves their farewell letters, personal effects, photographs, and four original aircraft including a Mitsubishi Zero recovered from the sea in 1980. The visit is silent and heavy. Allow 1.5 hours.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Take the 15-minute ferry from Kagoshima port to reach Sakurajima - one of the world's most active volcanoes, with minor eruptions several times a week for decades. Yunohira Observatory, at 373 meters altitude on the volcano's flanks, offers the closest authorized view of Minamidake crater. The Arimura Cape trail lets you walk on solidified lava flows. Keep car windows closed - fine ash infiltrates everywhere.

⏱ 120 min

Shiroyama Observatory, on the city's heights, offers the classic panoramic view over Kagoshima and Sakurajima at sunset. The hill is also the site of Saigo Takamori's last stand - the "last samurai" - during the 1877 Satsuma Rebellion. A plaque marks the exact spot where he died. The walk through Shiroyama forest (30 minutes) is pleasant and accessible.

⏱ 60 min

Yakushima

★★★★☆
1

Yakushima is a 505 km² island designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1993 - one of Japan's first natural sites to receive the honor. Seventy-five percent of its area is covered in forest, and a local proverb says "it rains 35 days a month" - not far from truth, with over 8,000 mm of annual precipitation in the highlands. This humidity has created a unique ecosystem: 1,900 plant species, moss on everything stationary for more than 10 minutes, and thousand-year-old Japanese cedars (yakusugi) rooted since the Jomon era. Shiratani Unsuikyo forest, accessible from Miyanoura port, is the one Hayao Miyazaki traversed in the 1990s before creating the world of Princess Mononoke - when you walk among the twisted roots and moss thick as cushions, you recognize the film.

⏱ 180 min
2

The Jomon Sugi, Yakushima's most famous cedar, is estimated between 2,300 and 7,000 years old depending on calculation methods. It measures 25 meters high and 16 meters in circumference. To reach it requires 8 to 10 hours round trip over 22 km from the Arakawa trailhead - a long hike but without technical difficulty, on old forest rails from the era when cryptomeria wood was cut to roof temples throughout Japan. The observation platform 10 meters from the tree dates from 1996, installed to reduce soil compaction by tourists. Plan a 5 am departure from the port (Arakawa shuttle bus) to return before 5 pm.

⏱ 240 min

Activities

From Kagoshima, the jetfoil (hydrofoil) reaches Miyanoura in 1h40 or Anbo in 2h. In high season, book ahead. There are also 35-minute flights from Kagoshima airport. Once on the island, local buses serve the main trailheads, but having a car gives much more freedom. Bring complete waterproof gear: rain can arrive anytime, even in fine weather in town.

⏱ 60 min

For non-hikers, the Yakusugi Land trail (starting at 1,000 m altitude) offers 30 to 150-minute loops through thousand-year-old cryptomerias on wooden boardwalks - accessible to all. Oko-no-taki waterfall (88 meters high) is Yakushima's tallest, visible from the road. Nagata beach (west coast) is the North Pacific's most important loggerhead turtle nesting site - females arrive May to July, at night.
