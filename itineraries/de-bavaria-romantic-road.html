Germany's Romantic Road: Fairytale Castles and Medieval Towns of Bavaria

📅 7 days📍 7 stops🚗 290 km⏱ 3h40

Wurzburg → Rothenburg Ob Der Tauber → Augsbourg → Fussen

Wurzburg → Rothenburg Ob Der Tauber → Augsbourg → Fussen

The Romantic Road winds 400 kilometers from Franconia to the Bavarian Alps, from Würzburg's UNESCO-listed Baroque palace—home to the world's largest single-piece fresco painted by Tiepolo—to Neuschwanstein Castle, the fairytale fortress inscribed as a World Heritage Site in 2025. In between, Rothenburg ob der Tauber preserves its 4 kilometers of intact medieval ramparts and 42 towers, Dinkelsbühl unfolds its half-timbered houses spared by all wars, and Augsburg shelters the Fuggerei, the world's oldest social housing complex where rent remains €0.88 per year. Seven days of dense history, changing landscapes, and discoveries around every bend.

Wurzburg

★★★★★
📍 Würzburger Residenz Würzburg📍 Hofgarten Würzburg📍 Hofkirche Würzburger Residenz📍 Alte Mainbrücke Würzburg📍 Dom St. Kilian Würzburg
1

The Würzburg Residence, built between 1720 and 1744 under the patronage of the Schönborn prince-bishops, stands as Europe's most harmonious Baroque palace according to UNESCO, which inscribed it as a World Heritage Site in 1981. Architect Balthasar Neumann, just 33 years old at the time, directed construction with assistance from specialists brought in from Vienna, Paris, Italy, and Flanders. The grand staircase houses the world's largest ceiling fresco painted in a single piece (32 × 19 meters), created by Venetian master Tiepolo in just 210 days between 1752 and 1753: it depicts the four known continents paying homage to the prince-bishop. The prince-bishop had initially hired a Milanese artist, but his work proved so poor that Tiepolo was summoned with a promise of 10,000 florins—three times his Venetian salary.

⏱ 90 min
2

Step through the doors of the Hofkirche, the Residence chapel tucked into the southwest wing. Despite its modest dimensions, it's considered one of the most perfect achievements of 18th-century sacred architecture. Five oval domes rise in succession above twisted pink marble columns crowned with gilded Corinthian capitals. Tiepolo painted the altarpieces here, while Antonio Bossi crafted the stuccowork. After the chapel, stroll through the Court Gardens (Hofgarten), laid out in terraces between 1765 and 1780, adorned with sculptures, putti, and symmetrical flower beds—they too are part of the UNESCO listing.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Alte Mainbrücke, Würzburg's oldest bridge, has connected the city to Marienberg Fortress since the 15th century. An earlier stone bridge existed as early as 1133, but floods in 1342 and 1442 destroyed it. The current bridge, stretching 185 meters across eight limestone arches, was reconstructed between 1476 and 1703. Since the 1720s, twelve Baroque sandstone statues standing 4.5 meters tall have adorned the pillars: you'll recognize Saint Kilian, the city's patron saint martyred in 689, and Charlemagne. Until 1886, this was the only way to cross the Main at Würzburg. Now pedestrianized, the bridge perpetuates a beloved local tradition: residents gather here at day's end to drink glasses of Franconian Silvaner, the region's signature grape varietal, served in the distinctive flat Bocksbeutel bottles.

⏱ 45 min
4

St. Kilian's Cathedral (Dom), Germany's fourth-largest Romanesque church by dimension, has dominated the urban landscape since the 11th century. It bears the name of the city's patron saint, an Irish missionary martyred in 689 along with his companions Colman and Totnan. Inside, works by sculptor Tilman Riemenschneider stand alongside Ottonian elements from the early 11th century and Gothic additions. The Schönborn family's funerary chapel, designed by Balthasar Neumann in 1736, can be visited in the transept.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Settle onto the Alte Mainbrücke in late afternoon to savor a glass of Franconian Silvaner as the sun sets over Marienberg Fortress and the vineyard-clad hills—a tradition locals celebrate each summer during the Brückenweinfest, the bridge wine festival.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 2

Wurzburg

★★★★★
📍 Festung Marienberg Würzburg📍 Mainfränkisches Museum Würzburg📍 Käppele Würzburg📍 Juliusspital Würzburg📍 Falkenhaus Würzburg📍 Marienkapelle Würzburg
1

Marienberg Fortress, perched on the left bank of the Main, served as the residence of Würzburg's prince-bishops for five centuries before the Residence was built. Its origins trace back to 706 CE, when a first church was erected on the site—one of the oldest in Germany. The current fortifications blend medieval walls with Renaissance bastions. Inside, the Museum of Franconia (Mainfränkisches Museum) houses the world's largest collection of sculptures by Tilman Riemenschneider, master of the late Gothic period who worked in Würzburg between 1483 and 1531, whose faces carved from limewood achieve a striking expressiveness.

⏱ 90 min
2

Climb the 247 steps of the Stations of the Cross leading to Käppele, the Pilgrimage Church of the Visitation, built by Balthasar Neumann between 1748 and 1750 atop Nikolausberg hill. It all began in 1640, when a Main River fisherman placed a pietà in his vineyard during the Thirty Years' War; miraculous healings were reported years later, drawing pilgrims. The Stations of the Cross, Germany's largest, is punctuated by 14 chapels containing 77 life-size statues sculpted by Johann Peter Wagner. The church's Rococo interior is adorned with frescoes by Matthäus Günther. The church miraculously survived the March 16, 1945 bombing that destroyed 90% of the city—a German soldier reportedly pretended to be out of ammunition to avoid shelling it.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Juliusspital, a hospital founded in 1576 by Prince-Bishop Julius Echter von Mespelbrunn "for the indigent and the sick," remains operational today. It derives its income from about a hundred hectares of vineyards it has owned since the 16th century, making it one of Würzburg's three great historic wine estates alongside the Bürgerspital (founded by wealthy burghers). The arcaded inner courtyard, the 17th-century buildings restored after the war, and the bistro where you can taste Würzburger Stein are worth a stop.

⏱ 45 min
4

On the market square, the Marienkapelle (St. Mary's Chapel) is a Gothic church recognizable by its white and red sandstone façade. Across from it, the Falkenhaus, with its magnificent yellow and white Rococo façade adorned with stucco, houses the tourist office. Franconia's first Baroque collegiate church, St. John im Haug (1670), designed by Italian architect Antonio Petrini, stands out with its two towers flanking a tall façade and a powerful dome—a Crucifixion by Tintoretto (1583) surmounts the high altar.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Descend into the vaulted cellars of the Bürgerspital or Juliusspital for a tasting of Franconian wines. The Franconia wine region, one of Germany's 13 wine-growing regions, is distinguished by its dry white wines—Silvaner, Bacchus, Müller-Thurgau—served in the flat Bocksbeutel bottle, exclusive to Franconia. The terraced vineyards lining the Main and overlooking the city form an integral part of the landscape.

⏱ 75 min

Stop 3

Rothenburg Ob Der Tauber

Medieval perfection frozen in time behind 42 towers and unbroken ramparts

★★★☆☆
📍 Plönlein Rothenburg ob der Tauber📍 Stadtmauer Rothenburg ob der Tauber📍 Marktplatz Rothenburg ob der Tauber📍 Rathaus Rothenburg ob der Tauber📍 St. Jakobskirche Rothenburg ob der Tauber📍 Burggarten Rothenburg ob der Tauber📍 Röderturm Rothenburg ob der Tauber
1

Rothenburg ob der Tauber ranks among Europe's best-preserved medieval towns. The town, perched on a plateau above the Tauber River, has remained virtually unchanged since the Thirty Years' War (1618-1648), which bankrupted the city but paradoxically preserved it: too poor to modernize, it kept its medieval face intact. Its 13th and 14th-century ramparts still stand for 4 kilometers, punctuated by 42 towers and gates. The wall walk (Turmweg) is freely accessible and offers a walk of about 90 minutes at 6 meters height, with plunging views over the red-tiled roofs and the valley. After the March 31, 1945 bombings destroyed more than a third of the old town, international aid enabled faithful reconstruction—donors' names are engraved on the restored rampart stones.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Plönlein—from the Latin planum, "little square"—is Rothenburg's most photographed corner and one of Germany's most reproduced images. At the intersection of two sloping streets, a slightly crooked yellow and red half-timbered house stands between the Siebers Tower (1385) on the left and the Kobolzeller Gate on the right, which descends toward the Tauber valley. This setting inspired the village in Disney's Pinocchio. Come early in the morning to avoid crowds and enjoy the low-angle light. From there, climb the Röder Tower or the town hall belfry for a 360° panorama of the town.

⏱ 60 min
3

St. James's Church (St. Jakobskirche), built between 1311 and 1484, dominates the Marktplatz with its Gothic silhouette. It houses the Altarpiece of the Holy Blood, a masterpiece by sculptor Tilman Riemenschneider. The neighboring town hall, blending 13th-century Gothic elements with a Renaissance façade added in the 16th century, guards a mechanical secret: each day at set hours, the clock on the façade comes alive and an automaton depicts the legendary scene of the Meistertrunk—the "Master Draught"—where Mayor Nusch supposedly downed 3.25 liters of wine in one go to save the town from destruction in 1631.

⏱ 75 min
4

The Burggarten, at the western tip of town, occupies the site of the Hohenstaufen castle built in 1142, from which King Conrad III ruled the Holy Roman Empire. Of the double fortress, only St. Blaise's Chapel remains, now converted into a memorial. This terraced garden offers Rothenburg's finest panorama: unobstructed views of the Tauber valley, vineyards, town rooftops, and the double row of ramparts.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Join the Night Watchman (Nachtwächter) tour, departing each evening from the Marktplatz. In medieval costume, lantern in hand and halberd on shoulder, the watchman guides visitors through the old town's dark alleys, recounting Rothenburg's legends, scandals, and anecdotes—a tradition dating back to the Middle Ages when watchmen patrolled to prevent fires and disturbances.

⏱ 90 min

📍 Mittelalterliches Kriminalmuseum Rothenburg📍 Deutsches Weihnachtsmuseum Rothenburg📍 Reichsstadtmuseum Rothenburg ob der Tauber📍 Dinkelsbühl Altstadt📍 Münster St. Georg Dinkelsbühl📍 Wörnitztor Dinkelsbühl
1

The Medieval Crime Museum (Kriminalmuseum), housed in the former commandery of the Order of St. John, displays over a thousand years of legal history across four floors. Public humiliation masks, dunking cages, "gossips' violins" for punishing talkative women: these instruments reveal the brutal realities of medieval law enforcement. In complete contrast, the German Christmas Museum (Deutsches Weihnachtsmuseum) celebrates German holiday traditions year-round through thousands of decorations, nutcrackers, and historic Advent calendars—Rothenburg hosts one of Germany's oldest Christmas markets.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Imperial City Museum (Reichsstadtmuseum), housed in the former Dominican convent founded in 1258, preserves an intact medieval monastic kitchen, Rothenburg's Stations of the Cross (1494)—a cycle of twelve panels painted by Martinus Schwarz—and Mayor Nusch's tankard, the famous 3.25-liter cup adorned with the emperor surrounded by the seven prince-electors (1616), which illustrates the Meistertrunk legend.

⏱ 60 min
3

Fifty kilometers south of Rothenburg, Dinkelsbühl is the forgotten "little sister" of the Romantic Road—and that's precisely its charm. This former Free Imperial City, fortified by Emperor Henry V, has kept its ramparts intact thanks to an edict by King Ludwig I of Bavaria and was spared from bombings in both world wars. The walls feature about twenty towers, the oldest dating from 1370. The Wörnitztor, the town's oldest gate, opens onto streets lined with colorful gabled patrician houses, the Wine Market square (Weinmarkt), and St. George's Church, one of the finest Gothic hall churches in southern Germany, built between 1448 and 1499, with its net vaulting and St. Aurelian's reliquary.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Try a Schneeball (snowball), Rothenburg's specialty: puff pastry rolled into a ball, fried, and dusted with powdered sugar or coated in chocolate. This Franconian pastry comes in dozens of variations at bakeries along Schmiedgasse and Herrngasse.

⏱ 30 min

Stop 5

Augsbourg

Two thousand years of history where Fugger bankers once financed emperors

★★★★★
📍 Augsburger Rathaus Augsburg📍 Goldener Saal Augsburg📍 Perlachturm Augsburg📍 Fuggerei Augsburg📍 Maximilianstraße Augsburg📍 Augustusbrunnen Augsburg📍 Merkurbrunnen Augsburg📍 Herkulesbrunnen Augsburg📍 Dom Mariä Heimsuchung Augsburg📍 Schaezlerpalais Augsburg
1

Augsburg, founded in 15 BCE by Emperor Augustus's legions, is one of Germany's oldest cities. During the Renaissance, it became one of Europe's financial capitals: the Fugger and Welser dynasties of merchants and bankers lent money to Emperors Maximilian I and Charles V. It was in Augsburg that the Augsburg Confession, the founding charter of Lutheranism, was read in 1530, and where the Peace of Augsburg was signed in 1555, officially recognizing the coexistence of religions within the Empire. The city is also the birthplace of Leopold Mozart, the composer's father, and writer Bertolt Brecht. It's the only city in Germany with its own public holiday: on August 8th, the Peace Festival commemorates the end of Protestant oppression after the Thirty Years' War.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Fuggerei, founded in 1521 by Jakob Fugger—considered the richest man of his time—is the world's oldest social housing complex still in operation. Behind its walls and blue-and-white-striped portal, 67 ochre-façaded houses contain 142 apartments of 60 square meters each. The annual rent, fixed in 1521 at one Rhenish guilder, has never changed: today it equals €0.88. In return, residents must be Catholic, native to Augsburg, in financial need, and recite three daily prayers for the founders. A curfew remains in effect at 10 PM. Mozart's great-great-grandfather, mason Franz Mozart, lived here from 1681 to 1694—a commemorative plaque marks the spot. A model apartment and a 1943 bunker can be visited.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Town Hall (Rathaus), designed by architect Elias Holl and completed in 1620, is considered the most important secular Renaissance building north of the Alps. It was the world's first building with more than six stories. Its Golden Hall (Goldener Saal), finished in 1643, impresses with its 14-meter-high coffered ceiling covered in gold leaf, murals, and marble floor. Everything was destroyed by 1944 bombings but faithfully reconstructed. Right next door, the Perlach Tower (Perlachturm), standing 70 meters tall, offers panoramas across the entire city and, on clear days, to the Alps.

⏱ 75 min
4

Stroll down Maximilianstraße, the city's finest avenue, laid out along the ancient Roman Via Claudia Augusta. Three monumental bronze fountains punctuate the promenade: the Augustus Fountain (1588-1594) by Dutch sculptor Hubert Gerhard, the Mercury Fountain (1596-1599), and the Hercules Fountain (1597-1600) by Adriaen de Vries. These fountains are part of Augsburg's water management system, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2019—an ensemble of 22 hydraulic monuments spanning more than 700 years of history. At No. 36, the Fugger Palace features a Renaissance arcaded courtyard; at No. 46, the Schaezler Palace (1770) houses the most important gallery of German Baroque art and a Rococo ballroom.

⏱ 75 min
5

Augsburg Cathedral (Dom), whose oldest parts date back to 994, blends Romanesque and Gothic architecture. It preserves the world's oldest known figurative stained glass windows, dating from the 11th century: the Prophets—Daniel, Jonah, Hosea, Moses, and David—installed in the nave's south windows. The Romanesque bronze door, considered the cathedral's oldest work of art, is displayed in the adjoining diocesan museum.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Settle into one of the downtown breweries to try Käsespätzle, gratinated fresh cheese noodles, a Swabian specialty from Augsburg. The city, on the border between Bavaria and Swabia, offers cuisine drawing from both traditions—complete your meal with Leberkäse (meatloaf) or Zwetschgendatschi (plum tart).

⏱ 75 min

Attend a performance at the Augsburger Puppenkiste, the famous marionette theater founded in 1943. Housed in the former Holy Spirit Hospital, this string puppet theater stages fairy tales for children as well as adult productions, with an attention to detail that has made it renowned throughout Germany.

⏱ 90 min

Stop 6

Fussen

Alpine gateway to the Romantic Road beneath Neuschwanstein's fairy-tale towers

★★★★★
📍 Schloss Neuschwanstein Schwangau📍 Marienbrücke Schwangau📍 Schloss Hohenschwangau📍 Alpsee Hohenschwangau
1

Neuschwanstein Castle, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2025, is Germany's most visited castle with over one million entries annually. King Ludwig II of Bavaria, crowned in 1864 at age 18, commissioned its construction in 1869 on a rocky spur 200 meters high. He had 8 meters of rock dynamited to clear the foundations and, between 1879 and 1880, transported some 465 tons of Salzburg marble and 400,000 bricks. The construction site employed the most modern techniques of the era: central heating, running water on every floor, and a steel structure to support the throne hall. Ludwig II lived here only a few months in 1884 before being declared unfit to govern and dying mysteriously in Lake Starnberg on June 13, 1886. The castle was then unfinished—and never would be completed.

⏱ 90 min
2

Walk up to the Marienbrücke, suspended 90 meters above the Pöllat Gorge and its 45-meter waterfall. This bridge, originally built in wood in the 1840s by Ludwig II's father as a birthday gift for Queen Marie, then reconstructed in iron in 1866, offers THE classic photo of Neuschwanstein: the castle stands out in its entirety against a backdrop of forests and Alpine peaks. This silhouette inspired Walt Disney for Sleeping Beauty's castle at the first Disneyland, opened in 1955 in California—it later became the Disney studios logo. The walk up from the Hohenschwangau parking lot takes about 40 minutes.

⏱ 60 min
3

Hohenschwangau Castle, below Neuschwanstein, is the residence where Ludwig II spent his childhood. His father Maximilian II had it restored starting in 1832 in neo-Gothic style. The rooms, richly decorated with tapestries illustrating medieval legends—including that of Lohengrin, the swan knight—nourished the future king's imagination from his earliest years. Seeing Wagner's opera Lohengrin in 1861 at age 15 was Ludwig's revelation: the swan would become his emblem, omnipresent throughout Neuschwanstein. The castle overlooks the Alpsee, whose turquoise waters invite a walk on the trail circling it.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Walk the circuit around the Alpsee, a 5-kilometer trail hugging turquoise waters framed by conifer forests and the rocky faces of the Alps. The panorama of the two royal castles from the south shore is spectacular. The trail is flat and accessible, perfect for unwinding after visiting the castles.

Stop 7

Fussen

Alpine gateway to the Romantic Road beneath Neuschwanstein's fairy-tale towers

★★★★★
📍 Hohes Schloss Füssen📍 Kloster St. Mang Füssen📍 Altstadt Füssen📍 Lechfall Füssen📍 Forggensee Füssen📍 Reichenstraße Füssen
1

The Hohes Schloss (High Castle) of Füssen, summer residence of the prince-bishops of Augsburg since the late 15th century, is one of the largest and best-preserved Gothic castles in Swabia. Its trompe-l'oeil painted façades—false windows, false battlements, false oriels—are unique in Germany and date from the reign of Bishop Friedrich von Zollern (1486-1505). The Rittersaal (Knights' Hall) and its carved coffered ceiling merit the climb. The Bavarian State Gallery, installed in the former reception rooms, displays late Gothic paintings and sculptures from the 15th and 16th centuries.

⏱ 60 min
2

Below the castle, St. Mang's Benedictine Monastery (Kloster St. Mang), founded in the 8th century and rebuilt in Baroque style between 1697 and 1726, houses one of Europe's finest collections of antique lutes and violins—Füssen is considered one of the cradles of European luthiery. The heritage museum also presents Jakob Hiebeler's "Dance of Death" (1602), Bavaria's oldest mural representation of a danse macabre. The monastery's splendid Baroque halls offer a glimpse of the institution's former wealth.

⏱ 60 min
3

Wander through Füssen's pedestrianized old town along Reichenstraße, lined with painted-façade houses, which follows the route of the ancient Via Claudia Augusta, the Roman road connecting Italy to Germania. The luthier's fountain recalls the town's historic role in stringed instrument making. On Schrannenplatz, the former grain hall, historic pageants enliven the town in summer. The town, perched at 800 meters altitude at the foot of the Alps, has over 2,000 years of history—its colorful Baroque houses, alleys, and mountain views make it a spectacular terminus for the Romantic Road.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walk down to the Lechfall, the Lech Falls, 10 minutes from Füssen's center. The Lech River, which originates in Austria, plunges here through a narrow gorge in a series of natural cascades. The bridge spanning the gorge offers spectacular views of the turquoise waters and erosion-sculpted rocks.

⏱ 45 min

Embark on a cruise on the Forggensee, Bavaria's fifth-largest lake by surface area. Boats operate from early June to mid-October and offer panoramas of Neuschwanstein and Hohenschwangau castles, Füssen's old town perched on its hill, and the surrounding Alpine peaks. Evening cruises at sunset are particularly recommended.

⏱ 90 min
