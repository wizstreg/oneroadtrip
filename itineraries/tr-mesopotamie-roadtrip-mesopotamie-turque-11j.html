Gaziantep

★★★☆☆
1

Gaziantep wears its baklava capital nickname lightly: it's also the capital of pistachios, isot pepper, eggplant kebab, and one of the densest cuisines in the world. UNESCO designated it a Creative City of Gastronomy in 2015, alongside Lyon and Parma. Start with the historic center, restored across 5.5 km around the castle. Skip the crowded noon terraces—locals eat earlier than you'd think.

⏱ 60 min
2

At Bakırcılar Çarşısı, the hammering of copper has set the rhythm of the day for four centuries. Workshops line the cobbled alleys below the castle. You'll watch trays, teapots, and kebab skewers forged by hand right in front of you. Haggle without hesitation—it's expected. Best prices are in the morning, before tour buses arrive.

⏱ 60 min
3

Built in the Bronze Age, Gaziantep Castle has dominated the old town from its hilltop perch. The February 2023 earthquake damaged it severely, and restoration is ongoing. You may not be able to climb inside, but the circuit around the base of the ramparts remains one of the finest viewpoints over the city. Check access on the day with the tourist office.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Locals start their day with beyran: a lamb broth simmered all night, served with rice and plenty of chili. Try it at the Coppersmiths' Bazaar from 8am, in a nameless diner. This isn't a dish for delicate stomachs, but it's the essence of Antep cuisine.

⏱ 60 min

Push open the door of Zincirli Bedesten in late afternoon: this restored Ottoman covered market gathers spices, kutnu fabrics, and Antep pistachios under a timbered roof. It's less touristy than the copper bazaar, with a more mixed atmosphere. Look for isot, the local smoked pepper with its own protected geographical indication.

⏱ 60 min

Gaziantep

★★★☆☆
1

One mosaic steals the show from all the rest: the Gypsy Girl at the Zeugma Museum. In a dark room, only her eyes are lit, and they follow you. The rest of the museum is vast: 2,448 m² of Roman mosaics rescued from the Birecik dam, across three floors. Budget two full hours.

⏱ 120 min
2

Opened in 2011, the Zeugma Museum protects floor mosaics from the Roman villas of the ancient city of Belkis-Zeugma, founded around 300 BC. Don't miss the Ocean and Tethys mosaic, or the bronze Mars. The twelve panels recovered in 2018 from Bowling Green University have finally rejoined the original work. Avoid weekends—it's overrun with school groups.

⏱ 60 min
3

The smell of hot sugar and clarified butter draws you in from the entrance of the Baklava Museum, housed in a restored Ottoman mansion. The displays show the 40 layers of phyllo pastry, thin as a page, and the role of Antep pistachios in baklava Gaziantep, which has been EU-protected since 2013. Small museum, big technical lesson.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

If you love pastry, book two hours at İmam Çağdaş, operating since 1887. Order the fıstıklı baklava and katmer with creamy pistachio. Service is quick, no fuss. It's the local reference, cited in every guide and always packed with Antep residents. Skip the copycat terraces downtown—the real deal is here.

⏱ 60 min

Taste menengiç, the coffee made from roasted wild pistachios rather than coffee beans. Tahmis Kahvesi next to Zincirli Bedesten has served it since the 17th century. Paired with a bite of halva, it's sweet and earthy.

⏱ 45 min

Halfeti

★★★☆☆
1

Touristy but striking: only the minaret emerges from the waters of Halfeti since 2000, when the Birecik dam submerged 40% of the old village. The rest lies beneath the surface—mosque, stone houses, orchards. Halfeti joined the Cittaslow network in 2013. Skip the jet-skis that turn the experience into a theme park.

⏱ 60 min
2

Grown only here, the black rose of Halfeti draws its color from a very particular microclimate along the Euphrates bank. The buds are almost black in spring, turning deep crimson as they mature. The gardens that still produce them can be visited near the old village. It's inspired several perfumes since the 2010s.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Board a boat on the Euphrates for a 90-minute loop to Rumkale, an ancient Armenian fortress at the confluence with the Merzimen. Boats depart from the old village quay from 9am, without fixed schedule. Expect 100 to 150 TL per person. Choose a collective boat over a jet-ski: the silence of the river makes half the experience.

⏱ 90 min

Sanliurfa

★★★☆☆
1

Locals simply call it Balıklıgöl, the pool of fish. Islamic legend places Abraham's miracle here: Nemrod threw him into a fire, which turned into water, and the wood into carp. The carp are protected, never fished. The site is free, very busy in late afternoon with local families. Avoid midday and the scorching sun.

⏱ 60 min
2

Built in 1211 by the Ayyubids, the Halil-ur-Rahman Mosque borders directly onto the sacred pool. Its shaded courtyard is one of the few places in Şanlıurfa where you can breathe in summer. The square minaret recalls Syrian models. Cover your shoulders and legs before entering; scarves provided at the entrance for women.

⏱ 30 min
3

The air smells of isot and rubbed copper in Kazaz Bedesten, a covered market built in 1562. It's the oldest bazaar in Şanlıurfa, dedicated to fabrics and traditional outfits. All around: Sipahi for Kurdish carpets, the coppersmiths' bazaar, the pepper market. Haggle—it's expected.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

If you climb to Urfa Castle in late afternoon, the view over the pool and old town is worth the effort. Legend has it that Nemrod catapulted Abraham from those two Corinthian columns you still see at the top. Count on 20 minutes' walk from Balıklıgöl. The stairs are steep, closed at 7pm in summer.

⏱ 60 min

Buy a packet of pellets to feed the sacred carp of Balıklıgöl, about 5 TL from vendors around the pool. The fish rush in a compact shoal. According to locals, spotting a white carp brings luck. Don't bring your own food—it's forbidden to preserve the carp population.

⏱ 30 min

Gobekli Tepe

★★★★★
1

Older than Stonehenge by several millennia, the Göbekli Tepe site has been dated between 9600 and 8200 BC by UNESCO. It was built by hunter-gatherers, before the invention of agriculture. This overturns our understanding of the Neolithic: the sacred preceded sedentism, not the other way around. Less than 5% of the site has been excavated.

⏱ 75 min
2

Inscribed on UNESCO in 2018, the Göbekli Tepe site features 5.5-meter T-shaped limestone pillars, carved with bas-relief sculptures: wild boars, foxes, vultures, scorpions. A protective hall covers the main enclosures. You walk around them on walkways, without being able to descend. Klaus Schmidt, the German archaeologist who revealed it all, died on site in 2014.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Arrive before 9am to avoid Turkish tour groups coming by bus from Şanlıurfa. The visitor center opens at 8am; a 10-minute film explains the context before climbing to the site. You can cover everything in 1.5 hours. The midday sun is merciless—no shade on the hill. The French audio guide is recommended.

⏱ 30 min

Sanliurfa

★★★☆☆
1

The Şanlıurfa Archaeology Museum is by far the most important in Anatolia for the Neolithic. Opened in its current building in 2015, it displays nearly 10,000 pieces: original T-pillars from Göbekli Tepe, Karahantepe sculptures, Amazons mosaics from Haleplibahçe. Budget two hours minimum. Closed Tuesday mornings.

⏱ 120 min
2

Standing 1.80 meters tall, the Urfa Man dates from around 9000 BC and is believed to be the world's oldest life-size statue. Found in 1993 during construction work in Yeni Mahalle, it's limestone, with obsidian eyes. No mouth, which intrigues archaeologists. It has its own display case at the museum—don't miss it on arrival.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Mevlid-i Halil cave smells of dampness and incense. Muslim tradition places Abraham's birth here. Now part of a mosque complex adjoining Balıklıgöl, the cave can be visited for free. Men and women enter separately. Cover your hair and shoulders. The atmosphere is that of an active sanctuary, not a museum.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Dine at Cevahir Konukevi, a restored 19th-century caravanserai serving Urfa cuisine: hand-prepared çiğ köfte, thin lahmacun, ali nazik with smoked eggplant. The inner courtyard is cooled with misters in summer. Moderate prices, and it's one of the few places still serving mırra, strong Arabic coffee.

⏱ 90 min

The Urfalis call their city the city of prophets: tradition holds that Job, Abraham, and Elijah all left their mark here. Stroll through the Eyyübiye district at sunrise, when worshippers arrive at Balıklıgöl and the heat hasn't yet crushed you. The golden light on the stone walls is worth the early wake-up.

⏱ 60 min

Harran

★★★★☆
1

The dry silence of the Harran plain strikes you first. 44 km southeast of Şanlıurfa, a few kilometers from the Syrian border, the village remains one of the oldest known inhabited sites, mentioned in Genesis as Abraham's stop on the way to Canaan. The local language is still Arabic for many inhabitants, alongside Turkish and Kurdish.

⏱ 45 min
2

Built between 744 and 750 by Marwan II, the Ulu Cami of Harran is considered the oldest mosque in Anatolia. Only the square minaret remains—atypical in Turkey—along with arches and foundations. The site also included the first Islamic university. All around, ruins of baths and cisterns. Access is free, ruined but open.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Enter a beehive house: 4 to 5 meters high, in mud brick without a single wooden beam, with conical domes that insulate against heat. The Harran Kültür Evi tourist complex recreates the traditional way of life. Hot drink and flatbread offered, but plan for a tip. Allow 30 minutes; the visit is brief.

Karahantepe

★★★★☆
1

Less known than Göbekli Tepe, Karahantepe is still worth the archaeological detour: it's a twin site, also dated around 9400 BC, part of the Taş Tepeler (stone hills) network. Excavations began in 2019 and are advancing quickly. You can watch the workers at work from the walkways.

⏱ 60 min
2

Under the protective hall, the eleven anthropomorphic pillars of Karahantepe's central sanctuary surround a relief figure: a human face carved directly into the rock, which doesn't exist at Göbekli Tepe. The elliptical building measures 28 meters wide. Less than 5% of the site has been cleared. Everything is still fresh; the effect is striking.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

If you've seen both sites, return to the Şanlıurfa Archaeology Museum: major pieces from Göbekli Tepe and Karahantepe are displayed there, including a replica of Sanctuary D and carved human heads. It's more complete than an on-site audio guide, especially for understanding the iconography of the reliefs.

Viransehir

★★☆☆☆
1

Viranşehir means "ruined city" in Turkish, a name that sums up its eventful history. It was Constantia under the Romans and Byzantines, Tella d'Mawzlat for the Syriacs. Rebuilt by Constantine I in the 4th century after destruction by the Persians, it was a strategic lock on the Byzantine frontier. Today it's a large Kurdish cotton town, more useful as a stopover than a destination.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Han El-Burur, a medieval caravanserai noted near Viranşehir, testifies to the town's role on the old caravan routes. The remains are modest, accessible at the eastern exit of the town. Allow 20 minutes for a photo stop, no more. It's mainly an opportunity to break the long stretch between Şanlıurfa and Mardin without forcing the stage.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Take a meal break in Viranşehir before tackling the stretch to Mardin. Several lokantas lined up on the main street serve pistachio kebab and local çiğ köfte. Expect around 150 TL for a full meal with ayran. Avoid prayer times on Friday; many shops close for 45 minutes.

Mardin

★★★★☆
1

The honey-colored stone of Mardin heats in the sun and gives the old town its unique glow. Perched at 1,100 meters on a rocky spur, it dominates the Mesopotamian plain that unfolds toward Syria. The alleys climb and descend steeply, cobbled, sometimes in steps. Sandals not recommended; closed shoes essential.

⏱ 90 min
2

Dating from 1385, the Zinciriye Medresesi (or Sultan Isa Medrese) remains one of the finest examples of Artuqid architecture in Mardin. Its carved portal, two ribbed domes, and inner courtyard deserve your time. The terrace roof overlooks the Mesopotamian plain, accessible via a discreet staircase on the west side.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Church of the Forty Martyrs is worth the detour: it honors the Forty Martyrs of Sebaste, Roman soldiers executed in the 4th century for their Christian faith. Also known as Kırklar Kilisesi, it dates back to the 6th century and still hosts a weekly mass in Aramaic, the language spoken by Jesus. Inside, Byzantine icons and an ancient baptismal font. The local Syriac Orthodox community is a minority but vibrant.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Have breakfast in a konak in the old town: local cheeses, black olives from Hatay, pistachio honey, flatbread with molasses, and dibek coffee pounded in a mortar. Several mansion-restaurants line up their tables on terraces overlooking the plain. Expect around 200 TL per person; better to book on weekends.

⏱ 90 min

If you like terraces, reach the Kasimiye Medresesi on foot from the lower old town, about 25 minutes downhill. The central basin represents the Sirat bridge of Islamic tradition. The accessible roof offers the view featured on all postcards. Go at sunset—it's free but monitored.

⏱ 75 min

★★★★☆
1

A Byzantine crypt beneath a Syriac monastery: the basement of Deyrulzafaran houses an ancient temple dedicated to the Mesopotamian sun god Shamash, older than the Christian building. The transition between the two cults is visible in the stone, without rupture. That's what makes the place so dense with history.

⏱ 60 min
2

Founded in the 5th century, Deyrulzafaran stands about 6 km east of Mardin, on a site once devoted to sun worship. It served as the seat of the Syriac Orthodox patriarch from 1160 to 1932. The visit is guided, the tour lasts 30 minutes, departures every half hour. Visits are in groups, sometimes bilingual English.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Allow 30 km to reach Dara, the ancient Byzantine city southeast of Mardin. Its vaulted underground cisterns, rock-cut necropolis carved into the limestone, and 6th-century fortifications are worth half a day. The site is free access, poorly signposted, few visitors. Bring a flashlight for the cisterns.

⏱ 120 min

Midyat

★★★☆☆
1

Midyat should be savored slowly, alley after alley. The old town of Eski Midyat features medieval Syriac mansions in golden stone, hidden inner courtyards, bell towers of still-active churches. The Tur Abdin region was inscribed on UNESCO's tentative list in 2021 for its medieval churches and monasteries.

⏱ 90 min
2

Founded in 397, the Mor Gabriel Monastery is the world's oldest still-active Syriac Orthodox monastery. 20 km east of Midyat, it's home to around twenty monks and nuns. The liturgy is in Syriac, a language derived from Aramaic. Organized visits all day; plan for a donation at the exit.

⏱ 60 min
3

At the Konukevi, the smell of old wood and cardamom mingles in the vaulted rooms of this large 19th-century Syriac house, now the official guesthouse. You can visit even if you're not staying overnight; the hall and courtyard are accessible. It's one of the symbols of Midyat's Syriac heritage, restored by the state.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Artisans call their art telkari: silver filigree worked into tiny lace patterns, passed from master to apprentice for centuries in Midyat. Several workshops can be visited in the old town. Prices seem high, but the work is measured in hours, not grams. Look for the Turkish 925 hallmark to authenticate the silver.

⏱ 60 min

Taste Syriac wine in a stone cellar: Midyat's Christian community has cultivated vines for centuries, and several cellars serve local vintages in 19th-century stone houses. The reds are full-bodied, the whites rarer. Expect 200 to 300 TL for a tasting with a platter.

⏱ 75 min

Hasankeyf

★★★★☆
1

A 12,000-year-old town swallowed in a few months: the Ilısu Dam began flooding Hasankeyf in 2020. The 12th-century Artuqid bridge, the inhabited caves, the bazaar: everything is underwater now. What remains is a melancholy visit, almost an act of memory.

⏱ 60 min
2

Relocated in 2017, the Zeynel Bey mausoleum is one of eight major monuments rescued from the dam and reassembled at the new cultural park of Yeni Hasankeyf, on a hill above the lake. The Persian tomb in blue and turquoise bricks of an Aq Qoyunlu prince is universally acclaimed. Small modern museum displays the relocated pieces.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Climb up to Hasankeyf Citadel: spared by the waters because perched too high, it remains accessible and offers a heartbreaking view of the lake that covers the old town. Allow 30 minutes of climbing, steep but short. Bring water; no service at the top. The old cave dwellings still visible on the cliff side are worth a stop.

⏱ 90 min

Batman

★★★☆☆
1

Batman surprises: an oil town, flat, with no obvious charm, it's mainly a practical stop before Diyarbakır. Born in the 1940s with the discovery of oil, it only urbanized in the 20th century. Its archaeology museum deserves an hour for the pieces rescued from the Tigris valley before the Hasankeyf flood.

⏱ 60 min
2

Dating from 1146-1147, the Malabadi Bridge spans the Batman Su with a single stone arch nearly 40 meters wide, one of the widest in the medieval world. Built by the Artuqids, it still serves as an arch for pedestrians. It's 30 km east of the city, on the road to Silvan, and can be visited for free.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Eat a yoğurtlu leben, Batman-style yogurt soup thickened with fine bulgur and flavored with dried mint. Several lokantas in the center serve it at lunch, about 70 TL per bowl. It's the regional dish less known than Antep's beyran, but just as characteristic. Served with fresh flatbread.

⏱ 45 min

Diyarbakir

★★★★☆
1

UNESCO-listed in 2015, the black ramparts of Diyarbakır form a 5.8 km ring of volcanic basalt around the Sur district. It's one of the longest medieval enclosures still standing in the world. The ramparts date largely from the Byzantine era, 4th-6th centuries, with Roman and Islamic additions.

⏱ 60 min
2

The basalt heats under your fingers, dark and pitted. The local volcanic rock is so present that the city was nicknamed Kara Amid (black Amida) until the 16th century. The ramparts count 82 bastions and four main gates (Harput, Mardin, Urfa, Yeni), from the Romans to the Ottomans. The Mardinkapı and Urfakapı gates are the best preserved.

⏱ 45 min
3

Locals say Kara Amid, black Amida. The Sur district, within the walls, suffered from the 2015-2016 clashes; some blocks remain inaccessible or under reconstruction. Check before venturing into certain alleys. The tour along the top of the ramparts remains safe and marked, in good condition on the southern half.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Walk 4 km on the rampart walkway on the Mardinkapı side: it's the best-preserved section for views over the Tigris and the Hevsel gardens below. Three hours on foot with photo stops. Access is from the Mardin gate, paid ticket at the entrance. Avoid the hot summer hours; no shade on the wall.

⏱ 180 min

If you come in spring, descend to the Hevsel gardens: 700 hectares of vegetable gardens irrigated by the Tigris, UNESCO-listed with the ramparts. Many bird species nest there, including herons and egrets. Marked trail from the Mardin gate, allow 1.5 hours round trip on foot.

⏱ 90 min

Diyarbakir

★★★★☆
1

The Ulu Camii beats the Umayyad Mosque of Damascus for age in Anatolia: it's considered the oldest great mosque still standing in Turkey. Its northern façade, with reused Corinthian columns and reliefs of a bull attacked by a lion, looks like no other. Women: scarf and covered legs mandatory.

⏱ 45 min
2

Rebuilt in 1091 by the Seljuks, the Ulu Camii adopts the plan of the Umayyad mosque of Damascus, transposed into black basalt and white limestone. The adjoining Mesudiye Medresesi, completed in 1198 by the Artuqids, is one of the region's first theological schools. Black-and-white chevron decoration, very distinctive.

⏱ 45 min
3

In the Sur bazaar, the smell of tütün and hot tobacco mingles with stalls of dried fruit and copper. The market stretches from the Ulu Camii to the Mardin gate, along Gazi Street. Less touristy than those of Urfa or Antep, more authentic. Look for Kurdish passementerie and black filigree jewelry, a city specialty.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Follow Gazi Street to the Cahit Sıtkı Tarancı house, a Kurdish-Turkish poet born in Diyarbakır in 1910. His family home, a magnificent example of Sur's vernacular architecture in basalt and wood, now serves as a museum. Modest entry; allow 45 minutes. The black-and-white checkerboard inner courtyard is typical of the region.

⏱ 60 min

The Dengbêj are Kurdish storytellers: their house in Diyarbakır, opened in 2007 in Sur, hosts their traditional improvised songs, accompanied by tea. Sessions take place in the afternoon, free. You won't understand the lyrics, but the deep voice grain and oral rhythm are enough to tell a story.

⏱ 60 min
