Bristol

★★★★☆
1

Bristol takes you by surprise. The city wears its street art and maritime past with equal pride, refusing to choose between them. Clifton Suspension Bridge, Brunel's masterpiece opened in 1864, spans the Avon Gorge 75 metres above the water. Walking is free; vehicles pay a toll. After dark, the bridge lights up spectacularly.

⏱ 60 min
2

The smell of old timber and rope hits you the moment you board the SS Great Britain. Launched in 1843, she was the first transatlantic liner with an iron hull and screw propeller, designed by Brunel. The tour takes you below the waterline into a climate-controlled dry dock engineered to slow corrosion. Skip weekends if you can—queues stretch in summer.

⏱ 120 min
3

Banksy grew up here, and it shows. Stokes Croft concentrates his best-known pieces, including Well Hung Lover on Park Street. Take a guided street art tour if you want the backstory, or just wander and look up. Walls change fast—some pieces have vanished in recent years.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

For lunch before hitting the road, head to Wapping Wharf: repurposed shipping containers turned independent eateries and bars, right next to the SS Great Britain. Reckon about 2 hours' drive to Salisbury, your halfway stop before Dorset.

⏱ 60 min

Salisbury

★★★★☆
1

Salisbury slots perfectly into the route between Bristol and Dorset. Its cathedral, built in a single campaign between 1220 and 1258, boasts the tallest spire in Britain—123 metres, or 404 feet. In the chapter house sits one of four surviving original copies of the 1215 Magna Carta, foundational document of Anglo-Saxon law.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Cathedral Close, England's largest, is ringed by Georgian townhouses and small museums. Constable painted this landscape from the meadow. Salisbury itself is a city of water: five rivers converge here, offering easy riverside walks along the Avon.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Stonehenge lies 20 minutes away by car if you want to double up. Otherwise, lunch at the cathedral's Refectory Restaurant for direct views of the spire. Then back on the road towards Corfe Castle, about 1.5 hours via back lanes.

⏱ 60 min

Corfe Castle

★★★☆☆
1

First glimpse of Corfe Castle, you understand why it held out so long. Ruins perched on a limestone mound, guarding the only natural gap through the Purbeck Hills. Built by William the Conqueror in the late 11th century, it was among England's first stone castles. Parliamentarians slighted it in 1646. Allow an hour and a half, longer if you take the audio guide.

⏱ 90 min
2

To break up the visit, drop into the village nestled below the castle. Purbeck stone houses, England's smallest town hall now turned into a free mini-museum (iguanodon footprints included). Tea room in an 18th-century cottage for the obligatory scone.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

If you arrive before 11am, the Swanage Railway steam train passes beneath the ruins—guaranteed photo opportunity. Otherwise, use the National Trust car park opposite the castle (free for members), or the cheaper Norden park-and-ride in high season.

⏱ 30 min

Lulworth Cove

★★★★☆
1

Lulworth Cove looks like someone punched the coast. Almost-circular cove, carved roughly 10,000 years ago when the sea breached the Portland limestone and eroded the softer layers behind. The visitor centre explains everything (free), then climb to Stair Hole to see the Lulworth Crumple—spectacular rock folds frozen in stone.

⏱ 60 min
2

The cove forms part of the Jurassic Coast, England's first natural UNESCO site inscribed in 2001. Ninety-five miles from Exmouth to Studland, exposing 185 million years of rock. Water's surprisingly clear and sheltered in summer for a quick dip.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Parking costs £5-10 depending on duration near the visitor centre. Avoid August weekends—it overflows. The Boat Shed Café overlooks the cove for a breather. Reckon 30 minutes' walk via the coast path to Durdle Door if you're skipping the car.

⏱ 30 min

Durdle Door

★★★★☆
1

Touristy, yes, but the arch delivers. Portland limestone pierced by the sea, taking roughly 10,000 years to punch through. The name Durdle derives from Old English 'thirl', meaning to pierce. From the car park, it's 900 metres downhill then wooden steps to the beach—wear proper shoes.

⏱ 60 min
2

Next door, surprise: Man O' War Beach sees fewer visitors despite rivalling the view. Bay sheltered by rocky stacks, water turning turquoise in full sun. Sea deepens quickly, so watch children—no lifeguards on duty.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Arrive at car park opening (8am) to catch Durdle Door crowd-free. Coach parties descend around 9.30am. Parking has climbed to £20 per day, card or JustPark app only, no cash. No drones permitted anywhere on the Lulworth Estate.

⏱ 30 min

Old Harry Rocks

★★★★☆
1

Local legend claims Old Harry takes its name from Harry Paye, Poole pirate who died in 1419—or perhaps from the Devil's English nickname. Three chalk stacks detached from Handfast Point by erosion, marking the eastern end of the Jurassic Coast. The first 'wife' of Old Harry collapsed in 1896.

⏱ 45 min
2

Walk from Studland village, about a mile (20 minutes) on flat path along the South West Coast Path. On clear days you spot the Needles on the Isle of Wight in the distance—remnants of the same chalk ridge that once connected Purbeck to the island. Cliffs are unstable; keep children close.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Bankes Arms in Studland serves local ales in a sprawling garden with sea views, perfect lunch stop before reclaiming the car. South Beach car park is free for National Trust members, otherwise paid.

⏱ 60 min

Bournemouth

★★★☆☆
1

Seven miles of golden sand and a Victorian pier: Bournemouth owns its seaside postcard status. The pier, opened in 1880, has reinvented itself (RockReef, Vertical Slide, terraces) without losing its penny arcades and candyfloss smell. ⏱ 60 min
2

Visit the Oceanarium for sharks, turtles and rays across three floors—handy when weather turns. For space from the crowds, walk 15 minutes west to Durley Chine or Alum Chine, Blue Flag beaches quieter than the central stretch.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

If you want to save your legs, hop on one of two red land trains running between the pier and Alum Chine or Boscombe. More affordable than you'd think. For the evening, scout pubs on the East Cliff for guaranteed sea views.

★★★★☆
1

Three chalk stacks lined up at sea, crowned by a red-and-white lighthouse: the Needles tick every British iconography box. The name comes from a fourth, sharper needle that collapsed during a storm in 1764. The current lighthouse dates to 1859.

⏱ 45 min
2

Around twenty distinct shades of sand compose Alum Bay's cliffs—quartz, feldspar, mica—a rare geological curiosity in Europe. Small bottles sold on-site let you layer your own gradient souvenir. ⏱ 45 min
3

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Portsmouth

★★★☆☆
1

Five hundred years of Royal Navy concentrated in one place—that's Portsmouth Historic Dockyard. HMS Victory, Nelson's flagship at Trafalgar in 1805, is undergoing its biggest conservation project since construction, started in 2022. Still viewable: you board and watch shipwrights at work, an experience that'll vanish once the job finishes around 2032.

⏱ 120 min
2

Sunk in 1545 in the Solent, the Mary Rose, Henry VIII's favourite ship, was raised in 1982 with 19,000 objects. Fragile timbers sprayed for 30 years to stabilize, displayed behind glass in a climate-controlled hall. World's largest Tudor artefact collection.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Royal Navy's first iron-hulled warship, HMS Warrior 1860, never fired a shot in anger. Her mere existence rendered every wooden fleet obsolete. Visit the lower decks with costumed interpreters sometimes.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Grab the Ultimate Explorer ticket (around £45) covering everything plus a harbour tour of about 45 minutes, valid one year. Allow a full day, don't rush it. Mary Rose Café or Camber Lights for breaks, with views of Royal Navy frigates on exercise.

Chichester

★★★☆☆
1

Chichester wears its 2,000 years lightly. The cathedral, founded in 1075 by decision to shift the episcopal seat from Selsey, is the only English cathedral with a medieval detached bell tower still standing. Entry free, £7 donation suggested. John Piper tapestry from 1966 behind the high altar, Chagall window, and Roman mosaic beneath glass flooring.

⏱ 75 min
2

Among the best-preserved in southern England, Chichester's Roman walls run 1.5 miles around the town, with multiple access gates. The four-street grid plan (North, South, East, West) dates to the Romans, who called the town Noviomagus Reginorum.

⏱ 60 min
3

Built over the remains of a Roman bathhouse visible at ground level, the Novium Museum displays mosaics, coins and local finds. Adult £5, family £12. The Bishop's Palace Garden adjacent to the cathedral offers a green pause before continuing the journey.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Stop at the Market Cross, built around 1501 at the four-street junction, still a meeting point. Independent shops towards The Hornet and Needlemakers. Goodwood is 15 minutes by car for motor racing or horse racing fans.

Eastbourne

★★☆☆☆
1

At 162 metres, it's Britain's highest chalk sea cliff. The red-and-white striped lighthouse built in 1902 by Trinity House replaced the older Belle Tout, moved several times to escape erosion. Trail from Eastbourne (about 7 miles return) or drive directly to the plateau car park for the short version.

⏱ 90 min
2

In 1999, Belle Tout, the cliff's old lighthouse (1832), was moved 17 metres to escape the sea. Now converted into a B&B (rare), visible from the path. The Beachy Head Pub offers panoramic views and honest fish and chips.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

To see the lighthouse at the foot, descend at Birling Gap (National Trust staircase), low tide essential (3 km on shingle). Check tide times first. At the top, updrafts attract paragliders and peregrine falcons.

⏱ 60 min

Brighton

★★★★☆
1

The Royal Pavilion defies good taste with aplomb. Palace commissioned by George IV, redesigned by John Nash between 1815 and 1823, mixing Mughal-Indian domes outside with Chinese fantasy inside. Adult £19.50, reckon 2-3 hours for building and garden. Audio guide £2, worth every penny.

⏱ 120 min
2

Opened in 1899, the Palace Pier is the sole survivor of Brighton's three historic piers (the other two burned or collapsed). Rides, arcades, fish and chips, Grade II* listed. Access free, except peak periods when an admission charge applies.

⏱ 60 min
3

Smells of leather, second-hand vinyl, antique dealers' perfumes: The Lanes, maze of medieval alleyways repurposed into antique shops, jewellers and cafés, contrasts with North Laine, its bohemian cousin slightly north (vintage, vinyl, vegan). Five minutes' walk from the Pavilion.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Ride the BA i360, 162-metre observation tower on the seafront, 360° views over the city and coast on clear days. Volk's Electric Railway, world's oldest operating electric railway (1883), runs along the seafront between the pier and the marina. Direct trains to London Victoria, about an hour.

⏱ 90 min
