Lima

★★★★★
1

Drop your bags and head straight out - Lima rewards those who dive in from the first evening. Make your way to Miraflores, the residential district perched on 70-meter cliffs above the Pacific. Head down to the Costa Verde in late afternoon: the low-angled light from the Humboldt Current paints the sea a strange gray-green you won't see anywhere else in South America. Parque del Amor, with its Gaudí-inspired mosaic tilework and ocean views, is the best spot to take the city's pulse - go around 5pm, before couples and families swarm in for sunset.

⏱ 60 min
2

Huaca Pucllana is an adobe pyramid built between 400 and 700 AD by the Lima culture, sitting right in the middle of Miraflores' residential neighborhood - creating a rather stunning temporal disconnect. The structure rises 22 meters, its seven superimposed platforms built using a technique called "bookshelf style": bricks placed vertically with spaces between them to absorb earthquakes. Of the original 20 hectares, only 6 remain - the city literally swallowed the rest. If you go in the evening (night visits from 7pm to 10pm), the pyramid is illuminated and you can dine at the on-site restaurant with views of the floodlit ruins.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Stroll through Miraflores' streets in the late afternoon: the terraced gardens along the clifftops, Parque Kennedy with its stray cats fed by locals, and the neighborhood's numerous ceviche restaurants make this first evening a proper introduction to Peruvian gastronomy. Order a pisco sour - this is where you should taste it for the first time.

⏱ 120 min

Huaraz

★★★★☆
1

About 200 km north of Lima, the Caral archaeological site sprawls across 66 hectares in the arid Supe River valley. It's the oldest city in the Americas: radiocarbon dating places it between 3000 and 1800 BC, contemporary with Egypt's pyramids and the first Mesopotamian cities - and 1,500 years older than other American urban centers. The complex includes six pyramids, a circular amphitheater, ceremonial plazas, and the oldest quipus (knotted cords) ever discovered in Peru. Remarkably, no weapons or fortifications have been found on the site - archaeologists see this as a civilization that governed through religion rather than force. Inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage site in 2009. Plan at least 2 hours for the visit, preferably with a guide.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

After Caral, continue toward Huaraz (~4h from the Supe valley). The ascent into the Cordillera Blanca is gradual but spectacular: you pass from coastal desert to Andean vegetation, the mountain passes cross above 4,000m, and the first snow-capped peaks appear. In Huaraz itself, the town sits at 3,091m altitude - take time to acclimatize as soon as you arrive. Avoid alcohol and exertion, drink plenty of water, and if you can find it, coca tea.

Huaraz

★★★★☆
1

Huascarán National Park, a UNESCO site, covers more than 340,000 hectares and shelters over 400 mountain lakes. Huascarán itself peaks at 6,768m - Peru's highest summit and South America's third tallest. On the road to Cebollapampa, a stop at the Llanganuco lagoons (Chinancocha and Orconcocha) is practically mandatory: their emerald-green waters at the foot of Huandoy's snow-covered walls set the tone for the day.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Laguna 69 is the Cordillera Blanca's most famous trek - and it deserves the reputation. Starting from Cebollapampa (3,900m), it's 14 km round-trip, 700m elevation gain, arriving at 4,600m before a turquoise lake ringed by glaciers. Count on 6 to 7 hours total. The difficulty is moderate to high depending on your altitude adaptation: leave early (agencies in Huaraz organize departures from 5am), bring at least 2.5 liters of water and snacks. If you're not yet acclimatized, postpone this trek to D4 and first do a short outing around Huaraz. Alternative: Pastoruri glacier (5,240m), accessible by car from Huaraz, less physically demanding but visibly receding each year.

⏱ 420 min

Huaraz

★★★★☆
1

86 km east of Huaraz (3h drive through mountain passes), Chavín de Huántar is one of the Andes' oldest and most influential pre-Columbian religious centers. The site functioned as a pilgrimage destination between 1500 and 300 BC - designated UNESCO as early as 1985. What strikes you immediately is the underground architecture: a labyrinth of galleries carved into the thickness of the walls, with ventilation and acoustic ducts so precise that voices carry several meters as if the person were right beside you. At the heart of this maze, in the deepest gallery of the Old Temple, stands the Lanzón: a 4.53m granite monolith representing the principal deity - a hybrid being part human, part feline, with serpent hair, left hand pointing down and right hand raised skyward, as if connecting the underworld to celestial spheres. In 2017, archaeologist John Rick's team discovered in an underground chamber bone tubes used to inhale hallucinogenic substances (tobacco and vilca, confirmed by chemical analysis) - direct proof that Chavín rituals involved altered states of consciousness. Allow 2 to 3 hours on site. The gallery visit may be inadvisable for the claustrophobic.

⏱ 150 min

Activities

On the return road to Huaraz, Lake Querococha (3,980m) deserves a photo stop: its calm waters reflect the surrounding snow-capped peaks. Take advantage of the evening in Huaraz to visit the Áncash Archaeological Museum (Plaza de Armas), which displays Recuay stelae and Chavín ceramics - a good complement to the site visit.

⏱ 90 min

Trujillo

★★★★☆
1

After six hours on the road from the Andes - the coastal desert, the trucks, the heat returning - Trujillo feels like a colonial film set appearing from nowhere. Its Plaza de Armas is one of northern Peru's most carefully maintained: the yellow and white facades gleam under late afternoon light, and you understand upon arrival why the city is nicknamed the "City of Eternal Spring." The cathedral dates from the 17th century; step inside, the frescoes and baroque organ are worth ten minutes. Then circle the adjacent streets to see the carved wooden balconies jutting out over the sidewalks - this is typical Trujillo architecture, unlike anything else in Peru. The Casa de la Emancipación, on the plaza, is the building where the region's independence was proclaimed in 1820 - free entry.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

In the evening, Trujillo's historic center comes alive. The marinera, Peru's national dance, was born here: if you're lucky, you'll see a demonstration in one of the plaza's restaurants. Trujillo is also known for its shambar, a thick soup of grains and legumes that local restaurants serve on Mondays. For the curious, the central market (two streets from Plaza de Armas) is worth an evening visit.

⏱ 90 min

Trujillo

★★★★☆
1

Chan Chan, 5 km from Trujillo on the Huanchaco road, is the largest adobe city in the Americas: at its peak in the 15th century, it sprawled across 20 km² with 9 royal citadels and housed up to 60,000 inhabitants. The Chimú empire that built it from 850 AD controlled the entire northern coast of Peru up to Ecuador, before being conquered by the Incas in 1470. The only fully restored palace open to visitors is Nik An (meaning "the house of the center" in the Muchik language): its 10 to 12m high walls are covered with bas-reliefs depicting fish, otters, birds, and waves - water and the sea were sacred to the Chimú. A ritual pool at the palace center served fertility ceremonies. UNESCO-listed in 1986. Allow 1.5 to 2 hours.

⏱ 120 min
2

Huaca de la Luna, 8 km south of Trujillo, is the ceremonial center of the Moche culture (100-700 AD). What distinguishes it from all other sites in the region is the preservation state of its frescoes: since 1990, archaeologists have uncovered seven construction levels superimposed over six centuries - each generation built a new temple on top of the old one, sealing the frescoes in adobe. The omnipresent figure is Ai Apaec, "the decapitator god," with protruding fangs and serpent hair, often depicted holding a severed head. On the site's ritual plazas, excavations have uncovered the remains of more than 70 sacrificed warriors. Start with the site museum before the visit - it gives meaning to what you're about to see.

⏱ 120 min
3

Huanchaco, the fishing village 12 km from Trujillo, deserves a late-day stop. This is where caballitos de totora are still used - 3 to 4m long reed boats that fishermen straddle - a 3,000-year-old practice depicted on Moche ceramics. A handful of fishermen still perpetuate this tradition on the main beach. In 2012, UNESCO inscribed this navigation art on its intangible heritage list.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Huanchaco is also a good address for lunch or dinner facing the sea: the ceviche is fresh and the promenade restaurants offer fish dishes at prices well below Lima. If you enjoy surfing, regular waves attract a small community of local surfers.

⏱ 90 min

Chiclayo

★★☆☆☆
1

Chiclayo presents itself without frills: no spectacular colonial plaza, no archaeological site in the city center. What makes it worth stopping is the Mercado Modelo and, at the back of its aisles, the Mercado de Brujos. Don't expect superficial exoticism - this is a working market, frequented by locals who come to buy their medicinal herbs and ritual preparations like others do their pharmacy shopping. You'll find dried lizard heads next to flower sachets for luck baths, snake oil vials, clay statuettes representing coastal deities, and San Pedro beans - the hallucinogenic cactus used in shamanic ceremonies still practiced in the region. Some curanderos offer on-site consultations. Walk through slowly, without cameras brandished - the atmosphere is normal and genuine, and stays that way as long as you don't behave like a tourist.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Chiclayo is known for one of northern Peru's most renowned regional cuisines. The cabrito a la norteña (kid goat simmered with spices), arroz con leche (rice pudding), and especially ceviche of fresh coastal fish should be tasted in one of the many restaurants around the Plaza. The city comes alive in the evening: the plaza's cafés animate and it's an opportunity to observe the daily life of a Peruvian provincial city far from the usual tourist circuits.

Huaca Rajada, an hour's drive from Chiclayo, is one of the 20th century's most important archaeological discoveries. In 1987, Peruvian archaeologist Walter Alva uncovered the intact tomb of the Lord of Sipán - a high-ranking Moche dignitary buried around 300 AD with 451 objects in gold, silver, gilded copper, turquoise, and lapis lazuli. The tomb was intact: the site had nearly been looted (huaqueros had started digging a few weeks earlier), but Alva arrived in time to secure the location. This discovery is often compared to Tutankhamun's tomb, not for size but for the wealth and exceptional preservation state. The originals are in the Lambayeque museum, but the site itself allows you to understand the excavation context.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Tumbas Reales de Sipán museum in Lambayeque (~15 min from Chiclayo) opened its doors in 2002 in a red truncated pyramid-shaped building - a direct reference to Moche architecture. This is where the originals are displayed: the Lord's gold headdress, his ear ornaments shaped like golden spiders with human bodies, his pectoral of shells and metal, as well as the skeletons of eight people sacrificed to accompany him to the afterlife. The staging reproduces the tomb's three levels from most recent to oldest. Count at least 2 hours.

⏱ 120 min
3

Túcume, 33 km north of Chiclayo, is nicknamed the Valley of the Pyramids. The site groups 26 adobe pyramids built by the Lambayeque (Sicán) culture from the 11th century, then successively occupied by the Chimú and Incas. The largest, Huaca Larga, measures 700m long, 280m wide, and 30m high - making it South America's largest adobe structure. From the Cerro Purgatorio hill, the view of the entire complex is striking: you realize the site's true extent. Thor Heyerdahl, the Norwegian navigator author of The Ra Expeditions, led excavations here in the 1990s - the site museum preserves his finds.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

If you have energy after Sipán and Túcume, Pimentel beach (~15 km from Chiclayo) is the local seaside spot: a 19th-century wooden pier still standing, a few fresh ceviche restaurants by the sea, and an authentic Peruvian coastal town atmosphere, far from Lima's crowds.

The Karajía sarcophagi are one of northern Peru's most striking images. Nestled in a cliff ledge 200m above ground, six 2.5m tall anthropomorphic sarcophagi have watched the valley for over 500 years. Kuélap is the Chachapoyas culture's most monumental citadel, perched at 3,000m altitude on a rocky spur dominating the Utcubamba valley. The main platform is 584m long, the limestone walls reach 20m high in places - archaeologists have calculated that the stone volume used to build the enclosure represents three times that of the Pyramid of Khufu. Inside, about 420 circular stone buildings are organized on several levels, with geometric friezes carved at their base. The site was occupied between the 5th and 16th centuries, before being discovered in 1843 by a local judge who had gotten lost. The Incas never succeeded in storming it - a natural defense aided by funnel entrances that allow passage to only one person at a time. To access it: 1 hour drive from Chachapoyas to Nuevo Tingo, then 20 minutes by cable car (Peru's first cable car, opened in 2017). Allow 2 to 3 hours visiting the site. Sleep in Chachapoyas, not at Kuélap.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

If time permits, the cable car ascent offers spectacular views of the cloud forest and valley below - it's already the show before even entering the citadel. On site, a local guide is highly recommended: the structures without interpretation don't speak for themselves, but with a guide, the story of the Kuélap massacre (over 100 skeletons found in the 1990s, probably victims of tribal reprisals at the time of the Conquest) gives a very different dimension to the visit.

The Gocta waterfall drops 771m in two leaps (231m + 540m) into a gorge covered with cloud forest - it's one of the world's highest waterfalls according to accepted measurement criteria. Its existence was known to all Cocachimba inhabitants since time immemorial, but legends about a guardian mermaid of the place discouraged approaching it. It wasn't until 2002 that German explorer Stefan Ziemendorff spotted it from the opposite mountain. Only Telésforo Santillán, a villager, agreed to take him there. The official measurement was published in 2006. From Cocachimba (1 hour drive from Chachapoyas), the hike is about 10 km round-trip through the forest and takes 5 hours total. You encounter orchids, toucans, and, if you're lucky, the cock-of-the-rock (gallito de las rocas), Peru's national bird with flaming red plumage. Sleep in Chachapoyas and not at Gocta.

⏱ 300 min

Activities

The hike to Gocta can be done autonomously from Cocachimba (well-marked trail) or with a local guide from Chachapoyas - agencies offer early morning shared transport departures. Horses are available in Cocachimba for the steepest sections if you want to save your energy. In the rainy season (December to March), the waterfall is at maximum flow and photos are spectacular, but the path becomes muddy. In the dry season (May to October), flow is reduced but the hike is more pleasant.

Leymebamba

★★☆☆☆
1

The Mallqui museum, 2.5 km from Leymebamba center (10 min by car), is one of Peru's most astonishing museums - and one of the least visited considering what it contains. In 1997, peasants plowing fields near the Laguna de los Condores stumbled upon a Chachapoyas necropolis. Archaeologists Sonia Guillen and Adriana von Hagen organized an emergency rescue operation: 219 mummies and over 2,000 objects were extracted from the cliff at 10 hours walking from the village. The villagers themselves participated in the excavations, discovering their own history. The museum, inaugurated in June 2000, presents the mummies in a climate-controlled room under glass - same temperature and humidity conditions as the original mausoleum. The bodies, in fetal position, still have their teeth and hair. Among the 2,000 objects: 31 Inca quipus, Chachapoyas ceramics, textiles, and wooden objects. Allow 2 hours.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Revash funerary site, about 45 minutes from Leymebamba, is a collection of painted mausoleums clinging to a limestone wall. These small 1 or 3-story houses, with walls covered in red ocher, served as tombs for the Chachapoyas elite - only chiefs, priests, and important warriors were buried there. Access from San Bartolo village requires about 1 hour walking. The contrast between the austere cliff and the small painted houses is striking. If you don't have time to go, the Mallqui museum offers reconstructions.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Leymebamba itself deserves an hour's stroll: it's a quiet small Andean village, with a tree-lined Plaza de Armas, cobbled streets, and a few restaurants serving local specialties like queso Chachapoyas, a fresh mountain cheese renowned throughout the region. The evening is peaceful and conditions are ideal for resting before the next day's long drive.

★★☆☆☆
1

The Cuarto del Rescate - the Ransom Room - is where the Conquest's most dramatic moment unfolded. On November 16, 1532, Francisco Pizarro and 168 men ambushed Atahualpa, the last Inca sovereign, in the Plaza de Armas. The Inca, accompanied by 7,000 unarmed followers, was captured and his army massacred. Imprisoned in this 6.70m by 5.18m room, Atahualpa made an unprecedented offer: fill the room with gold up to the height of his raised arm (a red line is still visible on the wall at 2.75m), and twice with silver the adjacent rooms. His subjects brought 84 tons of gold and 164 tons of silver in three months. Pizarro accepted the gold, melted down the offerings, divided the treasure with his men - and had Atahualpa executed on July 26, 1533. The king, to save his body from cremation and be reincarnated according to his beliefs, agreed to convert to Christianity. He was strangled. The room is austere, small, and perhaps that's why it strikes so hard.

⏱ 60 min
2

Cumbe Mayo, 20 km from Cajamarca (30 min drive), is a pre-Inca site of absolute strangeness. At 3,500m altitude, in a wind-swept pampa landscape, a 9 km aqueduct carved into volcanic rock drains waters from both slopes of the Andes. Built around 1000 BC by the Cajamarca culture - 500 years before the Incas - it's one of South America's oldest hydraulic constructions. The channel, hand-carved into basalt, measures between 30 and 45 cm wide. On the walls, petroglyphs depicting jaguars and four-pointed stars. Around the channel, a forest of rocks eroded into shapes of hooded monks (the frailones) gives the place a timeless atmosphere.

⏱ 120 min
3

The Baños del Inca, 6 km from Cajamarca center, are thermal springs used since Inca times. Water emerges from the ground at 72°C - the public pools are tempered to bearable levels. Atahualpa himself regularly stayed here (his residence at the springs is partially visible). If you want a break after the day's visits, this is the ideal spot.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Cajamarca's cathedral, on the Plaza de Armas, is one of Peru's rare never-completed ones: the two towers were left without bells by the Jesuits to avoid paying royal taxes levied on finished religious buildings. The interior of blond cut stone (local tuff) and gilded baroque altarpieces are worth 30 minutes. The neighboring San Francisco church houses a colonial religious art museum and catacombs.
