  • Gallipoli Peninsula: 50 Turkish and 31 Commonwealth cemeteries on a single battlefield, still marked by 1915 trenches
  • Troy (UNESCO): 9 layers of civilisation stacked over 4,000 years, ongoing excavations with recent evidence of warfare
  • Ephesus (UNESCO): one of the world's best-preserved Roman cities, Library of Celsus and 25,000-seat Great Theatre
  • Pergamon (UNESCO): steepest theatre in antiquity (70°) and the Asclepieion, ancestor of modern hospitals
  • Lake Bafa: former gulf of the Aegean converted to a lake by the Maeander, Byzantine monasteries on islets

April, May, September, October

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 5 and 9 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Spring (April-May): Perfect weather (15-25°C) with blooming landscapes, comfortable temperatures, and excellent conditions for sightseeing and outdoor activities. Ideal for Istanbul, Cappadocia, and coastal regions.

Autumn (September-October): Excellent temperatures after summer heat with stable weather and fewer crowds. Perfect for all regions, cultural exploration, and outdoor adventures.

Summer (June-August): Hot temperatures (25-40°C) with peak tourist season and crowded attractions. Excellent for Mediterranean and Aegean coasts, challenging for inland areas.

Winter (December-February): Cool temperatures (5-15°C) with occasional snow but lowest prices and authentic experiences. Good for Istanbul and covered attractions, limited beach activities.

🌍 What to discover in TR?

Cultural bridge between Europe and Asia with ancient heritage, stunning landscapes, and vibrant traditions. Experience Byzantine and Ottoman treasures, incredible cuisine, and authentic Turkish hospitality. The country offers cultural immersion with natural beauty and historical significance. Turkey provides transformative cultural experiences with natural wonders and culinary excellence.

📋 Important notes

This road trip follows Turkey's Aegean coast without touching Istanbul or Cappadocia—think of it as an alternative Turkish route. It connects three UNESCO sites (Troy, Pergamon, Ephesus) with lesser-known treasures: Assos and its Doric temple overlooking Lesbos, Lake Bafa—a former gulf now silted inland—and Gümüşlük, built atop the submerged ruins of ancient Myndos. It's a linear north-south route: Gallipoli → Bodrum, easily combined with the Turquoise Coast.

⚠️ SAFETY WARNING: Generally safe but avoid areas near Syrian border due to conflict spillover and terrorism risks. Exercise caution regarding petty crime in tourist areas, political tensions occasionally, and earthquake risks. Monitor current political situation and regional developments.

Eceabat

★★★★★
📍 Anzac Cove Gallipoli Turkey📍 Lone Pine Cemetery Gallipoli📍 Chunuk Bair Memorial Gallipoli📍 Çanakkale Martyrs Memorial Morto Bay📍 57th Regiment Memorial Gallipoli📍 Kabatepe War Museum Gallipoli📍 Eceabat waterfront Turkey
1

At dawn on 25 April 1915, soldiers of the ANZAC corps stormed this pebble beach. They expected to land on flat terrain—they found cliffs instead. A simple stone memorial bears the words Atatürk addressed to Australian and New Zealand mothers in 1934: your sons now lie here, in friendly soil. Walk down to the beach: the water is clear, almost peaceful. It's precisely this disconnect that makes the place so haunting.

⏱ 45 min
2

Among the 31 Commonwealth cemeteries maintained by the CWGC across the peninsula, Lone Pine is the most poignant on the ANZAC side. The memorial lists the names of Australian and New Zealand soldiers with no known grave. Take time to read a few inscriptions: many were under twenty. The pine tree that gives the site its name was replanted from seeds brought back to Australia—the original was destroyed during the fighting.

⏱ 40 min
3

Climb here to understand the strategic stakes of the entire campaign. The Allies needed to seize this summit to control the strait. The New Zealanders held it for 36 hours in August 1915 before being driven off. From the top, the view takes in the Aegean on one side, the Dardanelles and the Asian coast on the other—geography explains everything.

⏱ 30 min
4

At the southern tip of the peninsula, this 42-metre-high monument honours the approximately 253,000 Ottoman soldiers who died at Gallipoli. In front of it, rows of symbolic glass tombs arranged by Ottoman province. The most famous inscription is Colonel Mustafa Kemal's order to his ammunition-starved regiment on 25 April 1915: 'I am not ordering you to attack. I am ordering you to die.' This future founder of modern Turkey is omnipresent on this peninsula—it's here that his legend began.

⏱ 60 min
5

This war museum preserves objects found on the battlefield: false teeth, belt buckles, sniper shields, photographs. Ordinary objects that make death tangible. Panels are in Turkish and English—allow a good hour and don't rush through: this is where the abstract numbers of war take on a human face.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

This marked 2-kilometre trail links the peninsula's 11 main ANZAC sites. Audio guides are available on-site. Walk early in the morning—before 9 a.m., you'll often have the cemeteries to yourself, and the low-angled light on the white crosses is striking. Closed-toe shoes required: the terrain is hilly.

⏱ 120 min

The peninsula stretches roughly 30 km north to south and 25 km east to west. A car is essential to connect the ANZAC sites in the north with the Ottoman memorial in the south in a single day. Allow about 3 hours of driving for the full circuit, not including stops.

Canakkale

★★★★☆
📍 Archaeological Site of Troy Canakkale Turkey📍 Troy Museum Tevfikiye Canakkale📍 Kilitbahir Castle Dardanelles📍 Canakkale waterfront kordon📍 Aynali Carsi bazaar Canakkale📍 Canakkale Clock Tower📍 Cimenlik Castle Canakkale
1

Bring a guide or at minimum a solid reference book—without context, Troy's stones remain mute. The site is organised around nine layers of civilisation stacked over 4,000 years, from 3000 BC to late Roman times. Layer Troy VIIa, destroyed by fire around 1180 BC, is the one archaeologists link to Homer's Trojan War. In 2024-2025, excavations uncovered thousands of sling stones and arrowheads concentrated in front of the palace: evidence of a violent assault, not gradual decline. Enter from the right and follow the marked circuit—layers Troy III-V offer the best view of the strait in the distance.

⏱ 150 min
2

Opened in 2018, this museum 500 metres from the site is one of the best introductions to Troy's excavation. More than 2,000 objects cover all periods of occupation. The originals of 'Priam's Treasure' discovered by Schliemann are in Moscow's Pushkin Museum and Berlin's Neues Museum—the replicas displayed here help you understand what the archaeologist actually found. Visit the museum before the site: the stratigraphy is better explained here.

⏱ 90 min
3

Cross the Dardanelles by ferry from Çanakkale—the crossing takes 20 minutes. On the other side, the fortress built by Mehmed II in 1452 just before the fall of Constantinople literally locks the passage: Kilit-ül-Bahr means 'lock of the sea'. Suleiman the Magnificent added a seven-storey tower in 1541. Climb to the top for a full view of the strait—and spot Çimenlik Castle on the opposite shore, which closed the passage on the Asian side.

⏱ 75 min
4

The promenade displays cannons, anchors, and models of warships—an open-air museum of the Dardanelles battle. Look for the Ottoman Clock Tower, built in 1897 with funds from an Italian consul. Nearby, a replica of the Trojan horse made for the 2004 film dominates the quay. It's kitsch, it's the obligatory photo stop, and it reminds you that the Troy myth still feeds popular imagination.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The crossing between Çanakkale (Asian coast) and Kilitbahir (European coast) takes 20 minutes, but allow 45 minutes including the wait. Position yourself on the outer deck: the parade of tankers through one of the world's busiest straits reminds you that these waters have been decisive since antiquity.

⏱ 45 min

The Mirror Bazaar dates from the 1890s. Its name comes from the mirrors that once adorned the upper galleries, used during wedding preparations. Lively covered alleys: Ottoman ceramics, olive oil soaps, evil-eye jewellery. A good place to buy a few souvenirs without tourist traps.

⏱ 60 min

Tour operators offer guided visits in French or English departing from Çanakkale or directly on-site. A guide radically transforms the experience: without one, the numbered strata Troy I to Troy IX remain abstract. Book in advance during high season (June-August).

⏱ 90 min

Stop 3

Behramkale

★★☆☆☆
📍 Temple of Athena Assos Behramkale Turkey📍 Assos ancient harbour Turkey📍 Assos city walls Turkey📍 Assos agora theatre Turkey📍 Behramkale village stone houses📍 Kadirga beach Assos Turkey
1

Climb all the way up—235 metres of elevation from the village, allow 20 minutes on foot. At the summit, reconstructed andesite column shafts give an idea of the temple built around 530-520 BC. It's the only known archaic Doric temple in Asia Minor. Aristotle came here to teach in 348 BC, invited by King Hermias, married his niece, and left for Lesbos three years later. The island you see 15 kilometres to the southwest is precisely Lesbos. The original friezes—Heracles against the centaurs—are scattered between the Louvre, Istanbul, and Boston.

⏱ 75 min
2

The fortified wall measures 3.1 kilometres long and reaches 12 metres high in places—one of the best-preserved defensive complexes from Greek antiquity in Turkey. The walls were raised in several phases starting in the 4th century BC and reinforced by 8 towers. The west gate, flanked by two large Hellenistic towers, still stands. The site has been on the UNESCO tentative list since 2017.

⏱ 45 min
3

Between the acropolis and the harbour, successive terraces reveal the agora with its two porticoes, the bouleuterion (council chamber), and the theatre carved into the southern slope—orientation chosen to offer spectators the view across to Lesbos. The Roman baths are partially excavated. Excavations continue each summer under the direction of Çanakkale University.

⏱ 40 min
4

Walk down to the harbour: it was already active in Greek times and functioned continuously until the 18th century, exporting valonia oak bark from local trees to the European leather industry. Today a protected zone, all new construction is banned. The Apostle Paul embarked here during his third missionary journey, mentioned in the Acts of the Apostles.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The Ottoman village of Behramkale, built after the Turkish conquest in the 14th century, developed on the slope opposite the ancient city. Grey andesite houses, cobbled lanes, carved lintels. Olive oil soap shops cluster around the central square. Arrive early or late afternoon—between 11 a.m. and 4 p.m. in summer, tour buses arrive.

⏱ 60 min

Return to the Temple of Athena late in the day. The sun sets behind Lesbos and bathes the andesite columns in orange light. It's one of the most striking panoramas on the entire Aegean coast—and often quieter than the midday visit. Bring an extra layer: the wind picks up in the evening on the acropolis.

⏱ 60 min

Three kilometres from the village, Kadırga beach flies the Blue Flag. The water is clear and shallow, in a cove protected from northern winds. Ideal for a midday swim between the morning acropolis visit and the harbour in late afternoon.

⏱ 90 min

Ayvalik

★★★☆☆
📍 Taksiyarhis Church Museum Ayvalik📍 Ayvalik old town Greek houses Turkey📍 Cunda Island Alibey Ayvalik📍 Rahmi M Koc Museum Cunda📍 Seytan Sofrasi viewpoint Ayvalik📍 Sarimsakli Beach Ayvalik📍 Ayvalik olive oil mill Turkey
1

Built in 1844, the Taksiyarhis is the symbol of Greek presence in Ayvalık. Unlike most surrounding churches converted into mosques, it functions as a secular museum. After the Ottoman victory in 1922, the Greek population left the town overnight as part of the population exchange under the Treaty of Lausanne. The church served as an olive oil factory in the 1940s before being abandoned—its restoration and reopening in 2018 uncovered part of the original mosaic flooring.

⏱ 45 min
2

More than 1,000 historic buildings have been counted in the old town. Neoclassical houses built by Greek olive oil merchants in the late 19th century feature local stone walls, carved window frames, and coloured shutters. Unlike Alaçatı, many lanes remain residential—you'll encounter more locals than shops. Look for the Macaron quarter for the best-preserved alleys.

⏱ 60 min
3

Cunda is linked to Ayvalık by a bridge built in 1964—Turkey's first island-mainland bridge—and by ferries from the harbour. Its real name is Moschonisi, 'the perfumed island', but everyone calls it Cunda. Cobbled streets, Greek stone houses, and fish restaurant terraces. The Taksiyarhis Rahmi M. Koç Museum, in a restored Orthodox church, displays a collection of vintage cars, motorcycles, and toys—one of the finest collections of its kind in Turkey.

⏱ 120 min
4

The Devil's Table is a rocky promontory overlooking the Ayvalık archipelago of 22 islands and coastal olive groves. Legend says the devil, cast out of paradise, left the imprint of his foot in the rock. Clear views of Lesbos, Cunda, and the olive-covered hills. Arrive 30 minutes before sunset to secure a spot—it's the town's most sought-after photo location.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Ayvalık is surrounded by 2.5 million olive trees, some several centuries old. The region is considered by many professionals as one of the world's finest extra-virgin oil production zones. Tour operators offer mill visits with guided tastings. Plan to bring back a few bottles: the best local oils are found in the mills, not supermarkets.

⏱ 60 min

Local operators offer island-hopping tours among the 22 islands of the archipelago. Most are uninhabited. Hidden coves between islets offer turquoise waters for swimming. The morning half-day departure allows you to combine the boat tour with an afternoon visit to Cunda.

⏱ 180 min

The weekly market gathers local producers and artisans. Marinated olives, fig jams (the local eşme), artisanal olive oil soaps, and regional cheeses. Allow an hour for the full circuit and taste the products offered for sampling.

Bergama

★★☆☆☆
📍 Pergamon Acropolis Bergama Turkey📍 Asclepion Bergama Turkey📍 Red Basilica Bergama Turkey📍 Pergamon theatre Bergama📍 Bergama archaeological museum📍 Altar of Zeus Pergamon Bergama
1

Founded in the 3rd century BC as capital of the Attalid dynasty, Pergamon is a UNESCO site since 2014. The site dominates the Bakırçay plain from a 335-metre-high hill. A cable car connects the lower town to the acropolis—take it up and walk down. At the top, the Temple of Trajan (2nd century AD) is the only Roman building on the acropolis. Below, the Altar of Zeus, whose foundations measure 36 × 33 metres, was one of the masterpieces of the Hellenistic era: its 113-metre-long Gigantomachy frieze is now in Berlin's Pergamon Museum (closed for renovation until 2027).

⏱ 150 min
2

Built in the Hellenistic period under Eumenes II, this 10,000-seat theatre is considered the steepest in antiquity—its 70° angle gives it a dizzying profile. From the top of the 78 rows of seats, the view plunges to the plain and mountains. The stage was wooden, dismantled after each performance so as not to obstruct the permanent valley view.

⏱ 30 min
3

Founded in the 4th century BC around a sacred spring, the Asclepieion was antiquity's second medical centre after Epidaurus. Treatments included mud baths, dream-analysis psychotherapy, medicinal herbs, and theatre—yes, there was a 3,500-seat theatre for convalescing patients. Galen, born in Pergamon in 129 AD, studied here at sixteen and became court physician to the emperor. Recent analyses showed the sacred spring was slightly radioactive. Walk through the 70-metre underground tunnel that linked the dormitories to the healing temple.

⏱ 90 min
4

In the heart of modern Bergama, this massive red-brick basilica was built in the 2nd century AD for the cult of the Egyptian gods Serapis and Isis. It's one of the largest Roman religious structures in Asia Minor. One of its two lateral towers now houses a mosque—a mosque inside a former Roman temple tower, itself built on a Greek sanctuary. Pergamon's layers are everywhere.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The cable car links the lower town to the acropolis in a few minutes, with panoramic views of the tumuli (royal tombs) scattered across the plain. Ride up by cable car, descend on foot via the Antik Yol (ancient path) to enjoy the landscape and encounter less-visited ruins.

⏱ 20 min

The town museum holds more than 8,700 archaeological objects and 7,300 coins from local excavations. A good introduction to Pergamon's chronology before or after the site. The most important pieces were taken to Berlin in the 19th century, but recent discoveries remain here.

⏱ 60 min

Cesme

★★★★★
📍 Izmir Roman Agora Turkey📍 Konak Square Izmir Turkey📍 Cesme castle Turkey📍 Cesme thermal springs Turkey📍 Alacati village Cesme📍 Cesme peninsula beaches📍 Izmir clock tower Konak
1

Right in the heart of Izmir, this 2nd-century AD agora—rebuilt by Marcus Aurelius after an earthquake—is one of the few Roman civic centres accessible in an urban setting in Turkey. The vaulted basements (cryptoporticus) housed storerooms and shops. Don't miss the frescoes still visible on the northern portico walls. The entrance is discreet: look for the sign between the Konak district buildings.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Ottoman Clock Tower of Konak, built in 1901 for the 25th anniversary of Abdülhamid II's reign, is Izmir's visual emblem. The square around it overlooks the waterfront—the kordon—lined with café terraces. This is where you'll see everyday Izmir, far from tourist circuits: strolling couples, grilled corn vendors, students reading on benches.

⏱ 30 min
3

Built by the Ottomans in the late 15th century and reinforced under Suleiman the Magnificent, this well-preserved castle now houses a museum. It guards the harbour entrance, and facing it eight kilometres away you can see the Greek island of Chios. In 1770, the Ottoman fleet was destroyed here by the Russians at the Battle of Çeşme—commemorative plaque visible on the ramparts.

⏱ 45 min
4

Eight kilometres from Çeşme, Alaçatı is a restored Greek-architecture village with grey stone houses with coloured shutters, palm-lined lanes, and craft shops. Much more touristy than Ayvalık, but the old centre houses, built in the 19th century by Greek merchants, are genuinely well-maintained. Alaçatı is also known for its windsurfing and kitesurfing spots—the Çeşme channel winds are among the most consistent in the Mediterranean.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Çeşme peninsula is renowned for its natural thermal springs, whose waters reach 35 to 45°C. Thermal establishments have been set up around these springs, some directly on the beach. A 1.5-hour session late in the day, after morning visits, is ideal for recovery.

⏱ 90 min

Çeşme's waterfront lines up cafés and fish restaurants facing the castle and the ferry port to Chios. In the evening, the vibe is lively without being aggressive. It's a good place to dine while watching sunsets over the Greek islands on the horizon.

Selcuk

★★★★☆
📍 Ephesus archaeological site Selcuk Turkey📍 Library of Celsus Ephesus Turkey📍 Great Theatre Ephesus Turkey📍 House of Virgin Mary Ephesus Turkey📍 Sirince village Selcuk Turkey📍 Ephesus Terrace Houses Turkey📍 Temple of Artemis Selcuk Turkey
1

Enter from the top (south entrance) and descend towards the north exit—all gently downhill, your legs will thank you. In the 2nd century AD, Ephesus was one of the four largest cities in the Roman Empire with an estimated population of 250,000. Allow 3 to 4 hours for the main highlights and, if budget permits, add 45 minutes for the Terrace Houses (separate ticket): their preserved mosaics and frescoes are among the finest in the Roman East. Arrive before 9 a.m.—groups of cruise passengers from Kuşadası invade the site mid-morning.

⏱ 210 min
2

Built between 110 and 135 AD by consul Tiberius Julius Aquila in honour of his father—whose sarcophagus rests in the building's basement—the Library of Celsus is the third-largest library of antiquity, with approximately 12,000 scrolls. Its two-level façade, with Corinthian columns and four statues symbolising Sophia, Arete, Ennoia, and Episteme, is Turkey's most photographed image. The visible statues are copies—the originals were transferred to Vienna's Ephesos Museum in 1910.

⏱ 30 min
3

This open-air theatre could seat 25,000 spectators—considered the largest in antiquity. From the upper tiers, the view plunges down the ancient Arcadian Way (which led to the ancient harbour, now silted up) is breathtaking. This is where the Apostle Paul sparked a riot by preaching against the cult of Artemis, according to the Acts of the Apostles.

⏱ 30 min
4

Eight kilometres from Selçuk, Şirince is a former Greek Orthodox village founded in the 2nd century AD. It was once called Çirkince—'ugly'—a name chosen to discourage outsiders from settling there. White houses with red-tiled roofs climb hills surrounded by fruit vineyards. After the 1924 population exchange, Greeks were replaced by Turks from Greece who perpetuated the fruit wine tradition—apple, peach, mulberry—still sold in village shops.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

This Christian and Muslim pilgrimage site is located 9 kilometres from Ephesus, in an oak forest. The house was located in the 19th century thanks to descriptions from a vision by German nun Anne Catherine Emmerich, confirmed by the Lazarist Fathers. Pope Paul VI visited in 1967, John Paul II in 1979. The fountain at the entrance is reputed for its beneficial virtues.

⏱ 60 min

Şirince shops offer locally produced fruit wines—apple, peach, mulberry, pomegranate. These aren't grape wines but fruit fermentations inherited from the village's Greek tradition. Taste before buying: quality varies. The best producers are often those who cultivate their own orchards on the surrounding hills.

Stop 8

Didim

★★☆☆☆
📍 Priene ancient city Turkey📍 Priene temple Athena Turkey📍 Miletus ancient city theatre Turkey📍 Temple of Apollo Didyma Turkey📍 Didim Altinkum beach Turkey📍 Sacred Way Didyma Miletus Turkey
1

Priene is a lesson in urban planning: a city founded in the 4th century BC on a Hippodamian grid (regular perpendicular streets), it's one of the best illustrations of the Greek city ideal. Alexander the Great financed the Temple of Athena here—a commemorative plaque confirms it, preserved in the British Museum. The theatre, carved into the slope, offers views over the Maeander plain. The city was never rebuilt nor invaded by a modern town: it's this isolation that makes it an exceptional site.

⏱ 90 min
2

Miletus' Great Theatre could seat 15,000 spectators. Miletus was one of the most influential Ionian cities in antiquity, birthplace of Thales, Anaximander, and Anaximenes—the first philosophers to seek natural (not mythological) explanations for the world. The city was gradually strangled by the alluvium of the Maeander river, which silted up its harbour over centuries. Also visit the Baths of Faustina (2nd century AD) and the bouleuterion portico.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Temple of Apollo at Didyma is the fourth-largest temple in Greek antiquity, measuring 109 × 51 metres. Its construction began around 300 BC and was never completed—even in the 4th century AD, columns and capitals remained unfinished. The Didyma oracle was second in importance after Delphi: Alexander the Great consulted it after liberating Miletus from the Persians in 334 BC, and the oracle declared him son of Zeus. An 18-kilometre Sacred Way linked the sanctuary to Miletus—processions followed it during major festivals. Three monumental columns still standing give an idea of the project's scale. The Medusa head adorning the temple threshold has become the symbol of the town of Didim.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

These three sites are 30 minutes' drive from each other. Tour operators offer organised circuits from Selçuk or Kuşadası. By private car, allow a full day starting early (8 a.m.): 2 hours at Priene, lunch in Söke or on-site, 1.5 hours at Miletus, 2 hours at Didyma. The three sites together don't have Ephesus's crowds—that's precisely their charm.

⏱ 360 min

Two kilometres from the Temple of Apollo, Altınkum (Golden Sand) is a fine-sand beachfront with shallow water ideal for swimming. Late in the day, after visits, an hour at the beach allows you to decompress before heading to Bodrum.

Bafa

★★★★★
📍 Lake Bafa Kapikiri Turkey📍 Heraclea Latmos ancient city Turkey📍 Byzantine monasteries Bafa Lake Turkey📍 Seven Brothers Monastery Latmos Turkey📍 Endymion sanctuary Heraclea Turkey📍 Bafa Lake bird watching Turkey
1

The village of Kapıkırı is built amid the ruins of a Hellenistic city founded in the 3rd century BC. Columns and foundations emerge between current residents' houses: column shafts serve as garden tables. The 6.5-kilometre ramparts—still partly standing at 6 metres high and reinforced by 65 towers—are an exceptional example of Hellenistic fortification. Look for the Sanctuary of Endymion, carved into rock beside the lake: legend says the moon goddess Selene fell in love with this sleeping shepherd and asked Zeus to grant him eternal youth.

⏱ 120 min
2

This 60-square-kilometre lake was 2,000 years ago a gulf of the Aegean Sea. Alluvium deposited by the Maeander river progressively filled the maritime entrance, transforming the gulf into an inland lake. This natural phenomenon explains why ancient port cities like Miletus and Heraclea now find themselves tens of kilometres from the sea. The lake water is slightly salty, a legacy of its marine past.

⏱ 60 min
3

In the 7th century, monks from Sinai settled in caves and on Lake Bafa's islets, transforming Mount Latmos into a holy mountain comparable to Athos. By the end of the Byzantine era, thirteen monasteries and churches had been built in the labyrinth of rocks. The most famous is the Monastery of the Seven Brothers (Yediler), accessible on foot from the village with a local guide. Local boats offer crossings to the islets where monastery ruins stand—with swimming possible in the slightly salty waters.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Local fishermen offer crossings to the lake's monastic islets. On the Twin Islands (Ikizce Adaları), the Byzantine walls of a former cloister still stand. The lake water is clear and slightly salty—ideal for swimming. Negotiate duration with the boatman on arrival.

⏱ 120 min

The Carian Trail traverses Heraclea over 800 kilometres along the Carian coast. The section around Lake Bafa winds between rock formations, prehistoric rock paintings, and monastery ruins. Take a local guide from the village—the landscapes are magnificent but signage is insufficient to navigate alone.

Bodrum

★★★☆☆
📍 Bodrum Castle St Peter Turkey📍 Bodrum Museum Underwater Archaeology📍 Mausoleum of Halicarnassus Bodrum📍 Bodrum marina Turkey📍 Bodrum old town market📍 Bodrum waterfront kordon
1

The Knights of Saint John began building this castle in 1402, reusing stones from the Mausoleum of Halicarnassus—one of the Seven Wonders of the ancient world—for the foundations. Look closely at the walls: you'll see ancient marble and reliefs embedded in the medieval masonry. The castle was ceded to the Ottomans in 1523, converted into a mosque, then into a prison in 1895. Since 1964, it has housed the world's largest underwater archaeology museum. The Uluburun shipwreck hall (14th century BC) is fascinating: the wreck carried copper from Cyprus, tin from Central Asia, Egyptian glass, Anatolian cereals, and a gold scarab bearing Nefertiti's name. Bronze Age globalisation, reconstructed from the seabed.

⏱ 150 min
2

Today only an archaeological site with excavated foundations and a few scattered marble blocks remains—but the place's history justifies the visit. The Mausoleum of Halicarnassus (around 350 BC) was the monumental tomb of Mausolus, satrap of Caria, and his sister-wife Artemisia II. Fifty metres high, decorated with 400 sculptures and topped with a quadriga, it gave its name to all the world's monumental tombs (mausoleum). Most of the friezes were taken to the British Museum by Charles Newton in 1856.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Bodrum's waterfront stretches between the two bays framing the castle. The marina gathers gulets (typical Turkish wooden boats) and luxury yachts. In the evening, the kordon terraces come alive. This is also where you'll find the town's best fresh fish restaurants—fishermen unload their catch directly at the harbour market in the morning.

⏱ 90 min

Tour operators offer traditional hammams in Bodrum's old town. Scrub, massage, and steam bath: a good way to recover after several days walking archaeological sites. Book in advance—evening slots fill quickly in high season.

Gümüşlük is the most authentic village on the Bodrum peninsula. It's built on the ruins of ancient Myndos, a 4th-century BC Carian city. Part of the ancient city is submerged in the bay—the underwater walls are still visible at shallow depth. The place is protected as an archaeological zone: no new construction, no large-capacity hotels. Result: an almost intact fishing-village atmosphere, waterside fish restaurants, and some of the best seafood dining on the entire Turkish coast.

⏱ 90 min
2

A submerged stone causeway—the ancient walls of Myndos—links Gümüşlük beach to Rabbit Island. At low tide, the water doesn't exceed waist height. On the islet, wild rabbits (which gave it its name) coexist with the ruins of a Hellenistic tower. Archaeological excavations are occasionally active on the site, which may be partially closed some seasons. Bring water shoes: the submerged stones are slippery.

⏱ 45 min
3

Ten kilometres from Gümüşlük, Turgutreis is the westernmost point of the Bodrum peninsula. Its sunsets over the Greek islands—Kos, Kalymnos, Leros on the horizon—are renowned along the entire coast. The town is newer and less picturesque than Gümüşlük, but the Saturday market is one of the peninsula's liveliest: local producers, fabrics, spices, and handicrafts.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Gümüşlük's fish restaurants line their tables directly on the water, some on floating pontoons. Octopus drying on lines, the day's catch displayed on chalkboards, your gaze resting on Rabbit Island—it's the image of a simple, delicious coastal Turkey. Arrive around 7:30 p.m. to catch the sunset from your table.

⏱ 120 min

The Bodrum peninsula can be covered in a day via the coastal road: Bodrum → Gümüşlük (west) → Turgutreis (tip) → return via Yalikavak (north) or through the villages of Gündoğan and Türkbükü. The road runs along coves, bays, and olive groves. Avoid the main road crossing the interior in summer—coastal roads are slower but far more beautiful.
