1

Asunción's historic center unfolds on foot from Plaza de Armas, the colonial heart of the capital founded in 1537. Dominating the square stands the Cabildo—the former seat of government now transformed into the cultural center of the Republic—worth visiting as much for its architecture as for its permanent exhibitions on Paraguay's history. The Catedral Metropolitana, on the opposite side of the plaza, ranks among South America's oldest cathedrals; take time to walk around the square rather than driving through it, as the atmosphere shifts dramatically depending on the hour.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Panteón Nacional de los Héroes stands as Paraguay's most symbolically charged building. Commissioned in October 1863 by President Francisco Solano López as an oratory to the Virgin, designed by Italian architect Alessandro Ravizza, it remained unfinished for over 70 years because of the War of the Triple Alliance—the most devastating conflict in South American history, which killed between 60 and 90 percent of Paraguay's male population. Only in 1936, after the Chaco War, did Colonel Rafael Franco have the building completed and declared it a national mausoleum. You'll see the tombs of Mariscal Francisco Solano López, his father Carlos Antonio López, and Chaco War hero José Félix Estigarribia. Practical detail: the building also serves as kilometer zero for all Paraguayan national routes—eight major highways radiate from here.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Palacio de López ranks among the most romantic constructions in colonial America. Francisco Solano López began its construction in 1857 on land inherited from his godfather, in neoclassical style tinged with Palladian influences, under the direction of English architect Alonso Taylor. Materials came from all over Paraguay: stone from Emboscada quarries, timber from Yaguarón, bricks from Tacumbú. In 1868, the Brazilian fleet bombarded it from the river and Allied forces ransacked it—European furniture, statues, and mirrors were carted off to Brazil. López never lived there. Admire it from the Costanera esplanade, river side: the view of the illuminated Palacio at sunset, with the Paraguay River below, is the finest in the city.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Climb up to the Loma San Jerónimo neighborhood for an entirely different image of Asunción. Perched on a hill above the river, this old working-class barrio is one of the city's oldest: its narrow lanes, ochre and turquoise houses, and stairways that open onto unexpected panoramas of the river make it one of the capital's best street art spots. Go in late afternoon—the golden light on the facades and the view down to the Palacio de López below are worth all the postcards.

⏱ 60 min

The Museo del Barro—officially Centro de Artes Visuales—is undoubtedly Paraguay's most singular museum. Founded in 1979 by Carlos Colombino, Ticio Escobar, and Osvaldo Salerno during the Stroessner dictatorship, it brings together three collections under one roof: peasant folk art (over 4,000 pieces including 17th-18th century Franciscan and Jesuit carvings), indigenous art (over 2,500 objects from all the country's ethnic groups—ceramics, feather ornaments, Kamba Ra'anga masks), and contemporary Ibero-American art (around 3,000 works). What makes this place unique is its choice to present these three art forms on equal footing, without hierarchy. Allow at least 90 minutes to appreciate it—entry is free on Fridays and Saturdays.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Jardín Botánico y Zoológico of Asunción occupies the former López family estate in the northern part of the city. While the zoo section isn't its strong suit, the historic López greenhouses—built in the late 19th century—and tree-lined avenues with century-old specimens make the visit worthwhile as a shaded rest between museums. The botanical section preserves a fine collection of Paraguayan palms and subtropical species.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Manzana de la Rivera comprises seven restored 19th-century colonial houses aligned along El Paraguayo Independiente street facing the Palacio de López. Each house represents a different architectural period—from colonial dwelling to fin-de-siècle bourgeois mansion. Inside, the cultural center hosts temporary exhibitions on Paraguayan history and arts. It's one of the rare coherent urban sequences of the city's old colonial fabric: don't pass by without entering.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In late afternoon, head to the Costanera de Asunción for a sunset stroll. This redeveloped riverfront runs along Bahía de Asunción for several kilometers: Asuncenos gather here with their families, tereré and street food vendors set up from 5 PM onward, and the view of the illuminated Palacio de López from the river side is one of the city's finest. Arrive around 6 PM to settle in before the sky turns orange.

Mercado Municipal N°4 is Asunción's most popular covered market and one of the best places to dive into everyday Paraguayan life. Vendors of manioc, peppers, fresh cheese, passion fruit, and chicha have rubbed shoulders here since dawn. Arrive early in the morning to see the activity at its peak—after 10 AM the crowds disperse. It's also the cheapest place in town for breakfast: a hot chipá and a tereré to start the day right.

2

The Iglesia de la Encarnación, in the neighborhood of the same name, is one of the city's oldest parishes. Its sober white architecture contrasts with the bustle of the surrounding Villa Morra shopping district—a neighborhood in full transformation where local designer boutiques sit alongside trendy restaurants.

⏱ 25 min

Activities

Avenida Palma is Asunción's historic pedestrian boulevard, lined with shopping arcades and craft boutiques. Here you'll find the best selections of ao po'i—the fine embroidered cloth characteristic of Paraguay—and ñandutí, the spider-lace of Guaraní craftsmanship. Prices are more negotiable than at the airport, especially if you seek out small family shops rather than tourist chains.

⏱ 60 min

End the day with a tasting of tereré and Paraguayan street food. Tereré is the cold infusion of yerba mate with medicinal herbs: it's both the national drink and a social ritual you'll see everywhere. Accompany it with sopa paraguaya—despite its name, it's a corn flour and cheese cake, not a soup—and empanadas de carne. The many kiosks along the Costanera or in the center's plazas are the best places for this experience.

Thirty kilometers from Asunción via Route 2, Itauguá is the world capital of ñandutí—Paraguayan spider-lace. The Guaraní word literally means "tela de araña," spider web, and perfectly describes these discs of thread woven on wooden frames in radiating circles, imitating a web's geometry. The craft isn't of pure Guaraní origin: it derives from a Spanish lace from the Canary Islands, the "soles de Tenerife," introduced to Paraguay during colonization and transformed by mestiza women until it became something unique. In 2019, it was declared Intangible Cultural Heritage of the Nation. Enter the artisans' workshops—most downtown houses have their studios open to the street—and watch the tejedoras work: a single piece can take several days depending on its size and the complexity of the patterns.

⏱ 75 min
2

Atyrá, founded in 1580 under the name "aty-há" (gathering place) by Franciscans Luis de Bolaños and Alonso de San Buenaventura, is one of the first Franciscan reductions of the Río de la Plata. This flowery Cordillera village preserves its 18th-century church and central plaza typical of the period, with polychrome altarpieces and carved images in the Hispanic-Guaraní baroque style. The atmosphere is more authentic than in Itauguá: fewer shops, more neighborhood life.

⏱ 50 min

Activities

The return to Asunción via the scenic Cordillera route—the "Guaraní hills" that stretch between Asunción and Caacupé—is one of the country's most beautiful drives. The red sandstone hills contrast with subtropical vegetation, estancias alternate with small white villages, and views regularly open onto the plain stretching to the Río Paraguay. Take the opportunity to stop at a local farm and buy artisanal cheese or caña paraguaya.

Encarnacion

Gateway to UNESCO missions and Paraguay's wildest carnival, facing Argentina across the Paraná
Basilique Caacupé ParaguayFranciscan church Yaguarón ParaguayEncarnación Costanera
✈️ Book a flight🚗 Rent a car
1

Caacupé—"behind the yerba wood" in Guaraní—is Paraguay's spiritual capital. The Basilica of Our Lady of Miracles, first inaugurated on December 8, 1765, and rebuilt in its current form starting in 1945, houses the Virgencita Azul, a 50 cm wooden statuette carved around 1600 by a Guaraní who had promised this offering if he survived a Mbayá ambush. Legend has it that the small statue was carried off by the flooding of Lake Ypacaraí in 1603, then miraculously found floating in a small chest—since then, it's been called "Virgen de los Milagros." In July 2015, Pope Francis elevated the sanctuary to the rank of minor basilica. Every December 8, hundreds of thousands of pilgrims converge here on foot from all over Paraguay.

⏱ 50 min
2

The church of San Buenaventura de Yaguarón is one of Paraguay's most overlooked treasures. Built between 1755 and 1772 under the direction of priest Carlos Penayo de Castro—construction finished just in time for him to be buried there—it presents a striking contrast: an austere exterior facade in white adobe with wooden columns that gives no hint of the baroque jewel hidden inside. The main altarpiece, standing 14 meters high by 6 meters wide and 3 meters deep, is the work of Portuguese carver José de Souza Cavados, who arrived from Porto via Buenos Aires and Luján: it dominates the choir in a swirl of gilding, polychromy, and baroque sculptures with French rococo influence, with the archangels Michael, Gabriel, and Raphael in their niches. The ruins of Santísima Trinidad del Paraná are the largest and best-preserved Jesuit mission ruins in Paraguay—and probably on the entire continent. Founded in June 1706 and designed by Jesuit architect Juan Bautista Prímoli, it housed up to 3,000 Guaraníes by 1728. The complex covers 8 hectares: a gigantic central plaza surrounded by the main church, college, workshops, indigenous houses, and a cemetery. Look for the frieze of musician angels in the church ruins—a row of stone bas-reliefs depicting angels playing harpsichord, pipe organ, trumpets, Paraguayan harp, and flute. It's the only known visual testimony to the musical sophistication of the missions before the discovery of the Moxos scores in Bolivia. Listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1993 along with Jesús. Plan at least 90 minutes for the visit; the interpretation center in the former sacristy is worth the detour.

⏱ 120 min
2

Ten kilometers north of Trinidad, the ruins of Jesús de Tavarangüe harbor the finest architectural surprise of the Paraguayan missions. Founded in 1685 on the banks of the Río Monday, the church—designed by Spanish architect Antonio Forcada—was never completed: the expulsion of the Jesuits in 1767 abruptly stopped construction. This unfinished character is precisely what makes it unique: you see the walls open to the sky, the trilobed Mudéjar arches (Christian-Arab style, rare in the missions), the carved doors with finely worked frames. Nowhere else in the Jesuit reductions of the Río de la Plata do you find this blend of baroque and Mudéjar styles. Listed as UNESCO in 1993 with Trinidad. Its name Tavarangüe means in Guaraní "the village that could have been."

San Cosme y Damián is the smallest of the Paraguayan Jesuit missions, but it possesses a unique asset: the reconstructed astronomical observatory of Jesuit father Buenaventura Suárez, the only one the missions ever built. Suárez, born in Santiago del Estero (Argentina) in 1679, spent 40 years in this reduction and made astronomical observations of remarkable precision, publishing his "Lunario de un siglo"—ephemerides for the 18th century—without access to any modern European instruments. The restored college today houses a museum on mission life and a planetarium. Allow half a day including the journey from Encarnación.

The Costanera of Encarnación runs along the Río Paraná for several kilometers and has become Paraguay's finest urban promenade since its renovation in the 2010s. Fine sandy beaches have been developed along the shore—a rare thing in Paraguayan cities—and bars and restaurants have multiplied. In summer, the place resembles a miniature Brazilian beach resort; in low season, the atmosphere is quieter but the sunset over the Paraná, with the lights of Posadas across the way (on the Argentine side), is spectacular.

Activities

Concepción Costanera río ParaguayCentre historique Concepción ParaguayCathedral Concepción Paraguay

Concepción, the "Pearl of the North," was founded on May 25, 1773, by colonial governor Agustín Fernando de Pinedo under the name Villa Real de la Concepción—in homage to Charles III of Spain, a devotee of the Conception. Initially a military villa for 40 years, it quickly became Paraguay's second commercial port, controlling the trade in yerba mate, timber, and cattle from the entire northern region. Its 25-block casco histórico preserves colonial casonas and 19th-century European-style bourgeois mansions, testifying to the prosperity of the Italian, Syrian-Lebanese, and Catalan immigrants who arrived in the late 19th century.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Costanera of Concepción runs along the Río Paraguay on the left bank—25 meters high thanks to a retaining wall built to protect the city from flooding. The river port is one of the country's busiest for commercial river traffic: barges and lighters loaded with goods constantly move up and down the river to Asunción or the Brazilian Mato Grosso. It's one of the rare places in Paraguay where you still feel the economic presence of the great river.

⏱ 45 min
3

The cathedral of Concepción and Calle Palma constitute the nerve center of the city. The neoclassical cathedral dominates the main plaza from which radiate the straight streets of this urban plan inherited from the military foundation. Some beautiful colonial house facades remain in the adjacent streets—look for carved wooden portals and interior courtyards.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

In late afternoon, go down to the banks of the Río Paraguay to watch the sunset from the port area. The immense river—nearly a kilometer wide at this point—takes on hues of gold and rust when the sun sets behind the Chaco on the opposite shore. Local fishermen return at this hour with their catches of dorados and surubís—the two most sought-after fish of the Río Paraguay.

Piribebuy—"fresh air" in Guaraní, because of its numerous streams—is one of Paraguay's most history-laden towns. Founded in 1636 by captain-general Martín Ledesma de Valderrama, it became the third provisional capital of the Republic during the War of the Triple Alliance. On August 12, 1869, an Allied army of 20,000 men besieged the town defended by only 1,600 soldiers, women, and children. Commander Pedro Pablo Caballero, summoned to surrender, replied: "I am here to fight and die if necessary, but not to surrender." Paraguayan cannons were loaded with broken glass and stones for lack of ammunition. The Dulce Nombre de Jesús church—consecrated in 1753—was the theater of the final resistance. The adjacent historical museum presents weapons and objects found in the village soil during construction work. Allow 30 minutes for the church and 30 minutes for the museum.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

After Piribebuy, head to the Cordillera hills around Paraguarí for a hike or walk in the cerros. Cerro Peró (660 m) and Cerro Acahay are the most accessible—marked trails allow you to climb in one to two hours from villages at the base. The views of the plains of Paraguay stretching toward the river and toward Argentina are among the widest in the country. Local operators offer guides for more challenging hikes.

⏱ 180 min

The return to Asunción via the scenic route of the southern Cordillera is a beautiful conclusion to this excursion. The last light of day on the red hills and interior villages constitutes one of the most memorable images of this rural Paraguay that conventional circuits never show.
