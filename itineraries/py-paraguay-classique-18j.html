Asuncion

Where 19th-century palaces meet the broad waters of the Río Paraguay

★★★★☆
📍 Plaza de Armas Asunción📍 Cabildo Asunción📍 Catedral Metropolitana Asunción📍 Panteón Nacional de los Héroes Asunción📍 Palacio de López Asunción📍 Loma San Jerónimo Asunción
1

Asunción's historic center unfolds on foot from Plaza de Armas, the colonial heart of the capital founded in 1537. Dominating the square stands the Cabildo—the former seat of government now transformed into the cultural center of the Republic—worth visiting as much for its architecture as for its permanent exhibitions on Paraguay's history. The Catedral Metropolitana, on the opposite side of the plaza, ranks among South America's oldest cathedrals; take time to walk around the square rather than driving through it, as the atmosphere shifts dramatically depending on the hour.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Panteón Nacional de los Héroes stands as Paraguay's most symbolically charged building. Commissioned in October 1863 by President Francisco Solano López as an oratory to the Virgin, designed by Italian architect Alessandro Ravizza, it remained unfinished for over 70 years because of the War of the Triple Alliance—the most devastating conflict in South American history, which killed between 60 and 90 percent of Paraguay's male population. Only in 1936, after the Chaco War, did Colonel Rafael Franco have the building completed and declared it a national mausoleum. You'll see the tombs of Mariscal Francisco Solano López, his father Carlos Antonio López, and Chaco War hero José Félix Estigarribia. Practical detail: the building also serves as kilometer zero for all Paraguayan national routes—eight major highways radiate from here.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Palacio de López ranks among the most romantic constructions in colonial America. Francisco Solano López began its construction in 1857 on land inherited from his godfather, in neoclassical style tinged with Palladian influences, under the direction of English architect Alonso Taylor. Materials came from all over Paraguay: stone from Emboscada quarries, timber from Yaguarón, bricks from Tacumbú. In 1868, the Brazilian fleet bombarded it from the river and Allied forces ransacked it—European furniture, statues, and mirrors were carted off to Brazil. López never lived there. Admire it from the Costanera esplanade, river side: the view of the illuminated Palacio at sunset, with the Paraguay River below, is the finest in the city.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Climb up to the Loma San Jerónimo neighborhood for an entirely different image of Asunción. Perched on a hill above the river, this old working-class barrio is one of the city's oldest: its narrow lanes, ochre and turquoise houses, and stairways that open onto unexpected panoramas of the river make it one of the capital's best street art spots. Go in late afternoon—the golden light on the facades and the view down to the Palacio de López below are worth all the postcards.

★★★★☆
📍 Museo del Barro Asunción📍 Jardín Botánico Asunción📍 Manzana de la Rivera Asunción📍 Costanera de Asunción
1

The Museo del Barro—officially Centro de Artes Visuales—is undoubtedly Paraguay's most singular museum. Founded in 1979 by Carlos Colombino, Ticio Escobar, and Osvaldo Salerno during the Stroessner dictatorship, it brings together three collections under one roof: peasant folk art (over 4,000 pieces including 17th-18th century Franciscan and Jesuit carvings), indigenous art (over 2,500 objects from all the country's ethnic groups—ceramics, feather ornaments, Kamba Ra'anga masks), and contemporary Ibero-American art (around 3,000 works). What makes this place unique is its choice to present these three art forms on equal footing, without hierarchy. Allow at least 90 minutes to appreciate it—entry is free on Fridays and Saturdays.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Jardín Botánico y Zoológico of Asunción occupies the former López family estate in the northern part of the city. While the zoo section isn't its strong suit, the historic López greenhouses—built in the late 19th century—and tree-lined avenues with century-old specimens make the visit worthwhile as a shaded rest between museums. The botanical section preserves a fine collection of Paraguayan palms and subtropical species.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Manzana de la Rivera comprises seven restored 19th-century colonial houses aligned along El Paraguayo Independiente street facing the Palacio de López. Each house represents a different architectural period—from colonial dwelling to fin-de-siècle bourgeois mansion. Inside, the cultural center hosts temporary exhibitions on Paraguayan history and arts. It's one of the rare coherent urban sequences of the city's old colonial fabric: don't pass by without entering.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In late afternoon, head to the Costanera de Asunción for a sunset stroll. This redeveloped riverfront runs along Bahía de Asunción for several kilometers: Asuncenos gather here with their families, tereré and street food vendors set up from 5 PM onward, and the view of the illuminated Palacio de López from the river side is one of the city's finest. Arrive around 6 PM to settle in before the sky turns orange.

1

Mercado Municipal N°4 is Asunción's most popular covered market and one of the best places to dive into everyday Paraguayan life. Vendors of manioc, peppers, fresh cheese, passion fruit, and chicha have rubbed shoulders here since dawn. Arrive early in the morning to see the activity at its peak—after 10 AM the crowds disperse. It's also the cheapest place in town for breakfast: a hot chipá and a tereré to start the day right.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Iglesia de la Encarnación, in the neighborhood of the same name, is one of the city's oldest parishes. Its sober white architecture contrasts with the bustle of the surrounding Villa Morra shopping district—a neighborhood in full transformation where local designer boutiques sit alongside trendy restaurants.

⏱ 25 min

Activities

Avenida Palma is Asunción's historic pedestrian boulevard, lined with shopping arcades and craft boutiques. Here you'll find the best selections of ao po'i—the fine embroidered cloth characteristic of Paraguay—and ñandutí, the spider-lace of Guaraní craftsmanship. Prices are more negotiable than at the airport, especially if you seek out small family shops rather than tourist chains.

⏱ 60 min

End the day with a tasting of tereré and Paraguayan street food. Tereré is the cold infusion of yerba mate with medicinal herbs: it's both the national drink and a social ritual you'll see everywhere. Accompany it with sopa paraguaya—despite its name, it's a corn flour and cheese cake, not a soup—and empanadas de carne. The many kiosks along the Costanera or in the center's plazas are the best places for this experience.

★★★★☆
📍 Itauguá ñandutí ateliers📍 Atyrá church Cordillera📍 Route panoramique Cordillera Paraguay
1

Thirty kilometers from Asunción via Route 2, Itauguá is the world capital of ñandutí—Paraguayan spider-lace. The Guaraní word literally means "tela de araña," spider web, and perfectly describes these discs of thread woven on wooden frames in radiating circles, imitating a web's geometry. The craft isn't of pure Guaraní origin: it derives from a Spanish lace from the Canary Islands, the "soles de Tenerife," introduced to Paraguay during colonization and transformed by mestiza women until it became something unique. In 2019, it was declared Intangible Cultural Heritage of the Nation. Enter the artisans' workshops—most downtown houses have their studios open to the street—and watch the tejedoras work: a single piece can take several days depending on its size and the complexity of the patterns.

⏱ 75 min
2

Atyrá, founded in 1580 under the name "aty-há" (gathering place) by Franciscans Luis de Bolaños and Alonso de San Buenaventura, is one of the first Franciscan reductions of the Río de la Plata. This flowery Cordillera village preserves its 18th-century church and central plaza typical of the period, with polychrome altarpieces and carved images in the Hispanic-Guaraní baroque style. The atmosphere is more authentic than in Itauguá: fewer shops, more neighborhood life.

⏱ 50 min

Activities

The return to Asunción via the scenic Cordillera route—the "Guaraní hills" that stretch between Asunción and Caacupé—is one of the country's most beautiful drives. The red sandstone hills contrast with subtropical vegetation, estancias alternate with small white villages, and views regularly open onto the plain stretching to the Río Paraguay. Take the opportunity to stop at a local farm and buy artisanal cheese or caña paraguaya.

San Bernardino

A German lakeside retreat where fin-de-siècle villas face tranquil Ypacaraí

★★★★★
📍 Areguá centre historique céramique📍 Lake Ypacaraí Paraguay📍 San Bernardino centre Paraguay
1

Areguá is a town on the shore of Lake Ypacaraí, whose historic center climbs a hillside flanked by forest. Known as Paraguay's ceramics capital, the town counts dozens of workshops where artisans shape figurative pieces from local red clay—from frogs to saints, including characters from rural daily life. The mirador at the hilltop offers a fine view of the lake below. If you have time, walk up from the main plaza: the path runs alongside colonial houses, several dating from the 19th century.

⏱ 75 min
2

Lake Ypacaraí, 40 km east of Asunción, is Paraguay's largest freshwater body at 60 km² with a maximum depth of barely 3 meters. It's famous throughout the country thanks to the song "Recuerdos de Ypacaraí," composed in 1952, which sings of its melancholic beauty. The shores are bordered by subtropical vegetation and fishing villages. Walk to the water's edge and observe the egrets and jacanas on the water.

⏱ 40 min
3

San Bernardino, on the eastern shore of Lake Ypacaraí, was founded in the late 19th century by German immigrants and quickly became the resort of Asunción's elite. The center preserves some traces of this Germanic architecture—half-timbered houses and gabled facades—mixed with modern constructions. The atmosphere is relaxed on weekdays, very lively on summer weekends. Stroll along the lakefront and watch the boats.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Spend the afternoon on a lake excursion or relaxing on the shores of Ypacaraí. Operators offer boat or kayak outings from San Bernardino, allowing you to paddle along the wooded shore and spot waterbirds—cormorants, ibis, and herons are common. The light on the lake in late afternoon, when the water turns golden, is particularly beautiful.

Encarnacion

Gateway to UNESCO missions and Paraguay's wildest carnival, facing Argentina across the Paraná

★★★☆☆
📍 Basilique Caacupé Paraguay📍 Franciscan church Yaguarón Paraguay📍 Encarnación Costanera
1

Caacupé—"behind the yerba wood" in Guaraní—is Paraguay's spiritual capital. The Basilica of Our Lady of Miracles, first inaugurated on December 8, 1765, and rebuilt in its current form starting in 1945, houses the Virgencita Azul, a 50 cm wooden statuette carved around 1600 by a Guaraní who had promised this offering if he survived a Mbayá ambush. Legend has it that the small statue was carried off by the flooding of Lake Ypacaraí in 1603, then miraculously found floating in a small chest—since then, it's been called "Virgen de los Milagros." In July 2015, Pope Francis elevated the sanctuary to the rank of minor basilica. Every December 8, hundreds of thousands of pilgrims converge here on foot from all over Paraguay.

⏱ 50 min
2

The church of San Buenaventura de Yaguarón is one of Paraguay's most overlooked treasures. Built between 1755 and 1772 under the direction of priest Carlos Penayo de Castro—construction finished just in time for him to be buried there—it presents a striking contrast: an austere exterior facade in white adobe with wooden columns that gives no hint of the baroque jewel hidden inside. The main altarpiece, standing 14 meters high by 6 meters wide and 3 meters deep, is the work of Portuguese carver José de Souza Cavados, who arrived from Porto via Buenos Aires and Luján: it dominates the choir in a swirl of gilding, polychromy, and baroque sculptures with French rococo influence, with the archangels Michael, Gabriel, and Raphael in their niches. Don't miss the polychrome wooden lectern or the carved confessional flanking the nave.

⏱ 50 min

Activities

The drive to Encarnación via RN1 crosses the agricultural heart of Paraguay: soybean and sugarcane fields stretch as far as the eye can see between red earth villages and estancias with long avenues of palms. Count on about 5.5 hours of driving from San Bernardino—plan to leave early to have time to stop at Caacupé and Yaguarón without arriving in Encarnación after dark.

Encarnacion

Gateway to UNESCO missions and Paraguay's wildest carnival, facing Argentina across the Paraná

★★★☆☆
📍 Ruines jésuites Trinidad del Paraná Paraguay📍 Ruines Jesús de Tavarangüe Paraguay📍 Centre interprétation Trinidad Encarnación
1

The ruins of Santísima Trinidad del Paraná are the largest and best-preserved Jesuit mission ruins in Paraguay—and probably on the entire continent. Founded in June 1706 and designed by Jesuit architect Juan Bautista Prímoli, it housed up to 3,000 Guaraníes by 1728. The complex covers 8 hectares: a gigantic central plaza surrounded by the main church, college, workshops, indigenous houses, and a cemetery. Look for the frieze of musician angels in the church ruins—a row of stone bas-reliefs depicting angels playing harpsichord, pipe organ, trumpets, Paraguayan harp, and flute. It's the only known visual testimony to the musical sophistication of the missions before the discovery of the Moxos scores in Bolivia. Listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1993 along with Jesús. Plan at least 90 minutes for the visit; the interpretation center in the former sacristy is worth the detour.

⏱ 120 min
2

Ten kilometers north of Trinidad, the ruins of Jesús de Tavarangüe harbor the finest architectural surprise of the Paraguayan missions. Founded in 1685 on the banks of the Río Monday, the church—designed by Spanish architect Antonio Forcada—was never completed: the expulsion of the Jesuits in 1767 abruptly stopped construction. This unfinished character is precisely what makes it unique: you see the walls open to the sky, the trilobed Mudéjar arches (Christian-Arab style, rare in the missions), the carved doors with finely worked frames. Nowhere else in the Jesuit reductions of the Río de la Plata do you find this blend of baroque and Mudéjar styles. Listed as UNESCO in 1993 with Trinidad. Its name Tavarangüe means in Guaraní "the village that could have been."

⏱ 90 min

Activities

If you visit Trinidad at the end of the week, don't miss the sound and light show "Luces, Sonidos e Imágenes" offered on Friday and Saturday evenings starting at 7 PM (8 PM in summer). This video mapping projected onto the ruins of the great church recreates the encounter between Jesuit and Guaraní civilization, with projections of children, animals, and musicians that bring the ruins to life. It's an experience that perfectly complements the daytime visit and completely changes your perception of the site.

Encarnacion

Gateway to UNESCO missions and Paraguay's wildest carnival, facing Argentina across the Paraná

★★★☆☆
📍 San Cosme y Damián mission jésuite Paraguay📍 Encarnación Costanera beaches Paraguay
1

San Cosme y Damián is the smallest of the Paraguayan Jesuit missions, but it possesses a unique asset: the reconstructed astronomical observatory of Jesuit father Buenaventura Suárez, the only one the missions ever built. Suárez, born in Santiago del Estero (Argentina) in 1679, spent 40 years in this reduction and made astronomical observations of remarkable precision, publishing his "Lunario de un siglo"—ephemerides for the 18th century—without access to any modern European instruments. The restored college today houses a museum on mission life and a planetarium. Allow half a day including the journey from Encarnación.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Costanera of Encarnación runs along the Río Paraná for several kilometers and has become Paraguay's finest urban promenade since its renovation in the 2010s. Fine sandy beaches have been developed along the shore—a rare thing in Paraguayan cities—and bars and restaurants have multiplied. In summer, the place resembles a miniature Brazilian beach resort; in low season, the atmosphere is quieter but the sunset over the Paraná, with the lights of Posadas across the way (on the Argentine side), is spectacular.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

End the evening on the Costanera of Encarnación for the sunset over the Paraná. If you're there during the few weeks of Encarnación's carnival (usually January-February), prepare for one of Paraguay's liveliest celebrations: the "Carnaval Encarnaceno" is considered the country's largest carnival, with float parades, Brazilian dances, and a festive atmosphere that invades the entire riverfront.

Ciudad Del Este

Where three borders converge in a buzz of commerce beneath the thunder of Monday Falls

★★★★★
📍 Cataratas del Monday Presidente Franco Paraguay📍 Ruta 6 Paraguay plantations📍 Ciudad del Este centro
1

The Saltos del Monday are eastern Paraguay's great natural surprise. Just 10 km from Ciudad del Este, in the Presidente Franco district, three main falls plunge about 40 meters over black basalt rocks, creating a permanent mist that nourishes luxuriant vegetation. The Río Monday, a tributary of the Paraná, pours an average flow of 100 m³ per second here. The 9-hectare municipal park is well developed: trails, viewpoints, and a 40-meter panoramic elevator inaugurated in 2015—the tallest in Mercosur—allow you to descend to the foot of the falls. If you're coming from seeing Iguazú, don't underestimate this place: the solitude and proximity to the water are incomparable.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

The drive from Encarnación to Ciudad del Este via Ruta 6 crosses the agricultural heart of Alto Paraná, one of the world's most important soy-producing regions. Thousands of hectares of soybean plantations stretch as far as the eye can see, alternating with yerba mate groves and red hills. Count on 4.5 hours of driving—a stop at the Cataratas del Monday is ideal to break the journey in two.

📍 Parc national Iguazú Argentine circuit supérieur📍 Circuit inférieur Iguazú Argentine📍 Isla San Martín Iguazú📍 Garganta del Diablo Iguazú passerelle
1

Iguazú National Park on the Argentine side can be explored in a day via two main circuits. Start with the upper circuit: a walkway runs along the top of the falls and offers plunging views of the cascades tumbling into the red gorges. Then the lower circuit takes you to the foot of the falls—prepare to get soaked. Isla San Martín, accessible by inflatable boat, offers the closest views from across the way.

⏱ 180 min
2

The Garganta del Diablo—"Devil's Throat"—is the climax of the visit on the Argentine side. A 1,100-meter walkway over the Río Iguazú brings you right to the edge of the system's largest drop: 80 meters of free fall across 150 meters wide, in a roar and mist that make the air unbreathable within ten meters. Go in late morning when the light falls from across the way.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Cross-border crossing Paraguay → Argentina: from Ciudad del Este, take the Friendship Bridge to Foz do Iguaçu in Brazil (Brazilian side of the triple border), then the Tancredo Neves Bridge to Puerto Iguazú in Argentina. Plan for customs formalities on both sides—count on 30 to 60 minutes depending on traffic. Dedicate at least 5 to 6 hours in the Argentine park to see all the circuits.

Iguaçu National Park on the Brazilian side offers a radically different perspective from the day before: from the opposite bank, you contemplate the complete amphitheater of the falls in a single panoramic view. The main walkway runs along the shore for 1.2 km and ends facing the Garganta del Diablo—from this side, you see it head-on and in profile simultaneously, from angles impossible from Argentina. The vegetation is more lush on the Brazilian side, and birds more easily observable: toucans, parrots, and giant kingfishers are common.

⏱ 180 min
2

Returning to Ciudad del Este, take a tour of Lago de la República in the city center. This artificial lake with its fountains and gardens is one of the few green spaces in this highly commercial city. The atmosphere of "zona franca"—a free zone since 1961, Paraguay's second-largest city—is worth observing alone: tens of thousands of merchants and Brazilian and Argentine shopping tourists cross paths daily in a permanent bazaar.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Cross-border crossing Paraguay → Brazil via the Friendship Bridge. The Brazilian park can be visited in 3 to 4 hours; it's more concentrated than the Argentine side but the overall panorama it offers of the falls makes it an essential stop. Then return to Ciudad del Este to enjoy border life: the shops on Avenida San Blas and Curupayty street overflow with electronics, perfumes, and all kinds of articles at prices lower than in neighboring countries.

Concepcion

Colonial river port where Italian facades overlook the mighty Paraguay flowing north to the Chaco

★★☆☆☆
📍 Coronel Oviedo cathedral Paraguay📍 Paysages ruraux centre Paraguay estancias📍 Concepción río Paraguay arrivée
1

Coronel Oviedo, halfway between Ciudad del Este and Concepción, is the crossroads city of central Paraguay—all national routes meet here. It's worth a stop for its cathedral and lively central plaza, and to observe the life of this commercial city in Paraguay's "heart," without tourist artifice. It's also a good time for a typical lunch: look for a local market or a plaza fondita to eat a bo'i chipa or homemade sopa paraguaya.

⏱ 40 min
2

Arriving in Concepción in the evening, head down to the port area for a first impression of the Río Paraguay. The river port of the "Pearl of the North" is one of the country's busiest: barges loaded with cattle and yerba mate dock here all day long, and at nightfall the river lights create a particular atmosphere. Walk along the Costanera a few hundred meters—the upper city rises above the retaining wall, and the humid river air contrasts with the dry heat of the road.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The long drive from Ciudad del Este to Concepción—about 460 km, 7 to 8 hours—crosses central Paraguay from end to end. It's one of the most revealing routes in the country: ruta 7 then secondary roads northward unfold landscapes of estancias, zebu herds, red hills, and sleepy villages where nothing seems to have changed in decades. Leave early in the morning to arrive in Concepción before nightfall and enjoy the sunset over the river.

Concepcion

Colonial river port where Italian facades overlook the mighty Paraguay flowing north to the Chaco

★★☆☆☆
📍 Concepción Costanera río Paraguay📍 Centre historique Concepción Paraguay📍 Cathedral Concepción Paraguay
1

Concepción, the "Pearl of the North," was founded on May 25, 1773, by colonial governor Agustín Fernando de Pinedo under the name Villa Real de la Concepción—in homage to Charles III of Spain, a devotee of the Conception. Initially a military villa for 40 years, it quickly became Paraguay's second commercial port, controlling the trade in yerba mate, timber, and cattle from the entire northern region. Its 25-block casco histórico preserves colonial casonas and 19th-century European-style bourgeois mansions, testifying to the prosperity of the Italian, Syrian-Lebanese, and Catalan immigrants who arrived in the late 19th century.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Costanera of Concepción runs along the Río Paraguay on the left bank—25 meters high thanks to a retaining wall built to protect the city from flooding. The river port is one of the country's busiest for commercial river traffic: barges and lighters loaded with goods constantly move up and down the river to Asunción or the Brazilian Mato Grosso. It's one of the rare places in Paraguay where you still feel the economic presence of the great river.

⏱ 45 min
3

The cathedral of Concepción and Calle Palma constitute the nerve center of the city. The neoclassical cathedral dominates the main plaza from which radiate the straight streets of this urban plan inherited from the military foundation. Some beautiful colonial house facades remain in the adjacent streets—look for carved wooden portals and interior courtyards.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

In late afternoon, go down to the banks of the Río Paraguay to watch the sunset from the port area. The immense river—nearly a kilometer wide at this point—takes on hues of gold and rust when the sun sets behind the Chaco on the opposite shore. Local fishermen return at this hour with their catches of dorados and surubís—the two most sought-after fish of the Río Paraguay.

On the return road to Asunción, plan a stop in Tobatí in the Cordillera department. This Franciscan village founded in 1583 by Luis de Bolaños is one of the cradles of Guaraní-Franciscan culture: the colonial church preserves polychrome wood carvings and the central plaza with its historic well gives a fine idea of the urban planning of the reductions. It's also here that, according to legend, one of the two Virgin statues carved by the Guaraní Indian José was born—the big sister of the Virgencita de Caacupé.

⏱ 45 min
2

Arriving in Asunción in late afternoon, take a tour of the Recoleta neighborhood in the north of the center, which the itinerary hasn't yet covered. Its tree-lined avenues, art galleries, and lively plaza make it one of the capital's most pleasant neighborhoods to settle into after the drive. The Museo Nacional de Bellas Artes, housed in a 19th-century villa, is worth a quick look if you still have energy.

⏱ 35 min

Activities

The RN5/RN3 road from Concepción to Asunción crosses some of Paraguay's most authentic rural landscapes: Cordillera hills declining into red earth valleys, estancias with wooden fences, zebu herds on dry pastures in the dry season. Count on about 5 hours of driving for 330 km—leave early in the morning to arrive in Asunción in the afternoon and enjoy one last sunset on the Costanera.

Cerro Lambaré is the hill that dominates the southern suburbs of Asunción, river side. From the mirador at the summit, on clear days, you see on one side the urban panorama of Asunción spread out to the Palacio de López, and on the other the meanders of the Río Paraguay disappearing toward the Chaco. It's not a very developed tourist site—no cafés or shops—which makes it a good place for quiet sunrises and sunsets.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Iglesia Santísima Trinidad in the Trinidad neighborhood, northeast of downtown Asunción, is a beautiful neighborhood church with a sober baroque facade and an interior decorated with 18th-century wood carvings. It's also the name of one of the capital's liveliest neighborhoods, where restaurants and bars cluster—a good base for dinner.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The artisan market that sets up around Plaza de los Héroes—steps from the Panteón—is the best place for last-minute purchases: embroidered ao po'i, hand-tooled leather, palo santo wood sculptures. Prices are often more negotiable than in shops and the artisans present can explain their technique.

⏱ 60 min

Last tereré on the Costanera—do it according to the rules by asking for a "guampa" (the horn or wood container) and let the street vendors prepare the medicinal herbs according to local traditions. Tereré is drunk by passing the guampa from neighbor to neighbor on the river bank, watching the boats and egrets.

1

Piribebuy—"fresh air" in Guaraní, because of its numerous streams—is one of Paraguay's most history-laden towns. Founded in 1636 by captain-general Martín Ledesma de Valderrama, it became the third provisional capital of the Republic during the War of the Triple Alliance. On August 12, 1869, an Allied army of 20,000 men besieged the town defended by only 1,600 soldiers, women, and children. Commander Pedro Pablo Caballero, summoned to surrender, replied: "I am here to fight and die if necessary, but not to surrender." Paraguayan cannons were loaded with broken glass and stones for lack of ammunition. The Dulce Nombre de Jesús church—consecrated in 1753—was the theater of the final resistance. The adjacent historical museum presents weapons and objects found in the village soil during construction work. Allow 30 minutes for the church and 30 minutes for the museum.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

After Piribebuy, head to the Cordillera hills around Paraguarí for a hike or walk in the cerros. Cerro Peró (660 m) and Cerro Acahay are the most accessible—marked trails allow you to climb in one to two hours from villages at the base. The views of the plains of Paraguay stretching toward the river and toward Argentina are among the widest in the country. Local operators offer guides for more challenging hikes.

⏱ 180 min

The return to Asunción via the scenic route of the southern Cordillera is a beautiful conclusion to this excursion. The last light of day on the red hills and interior villages constitutes one of the most memorable images of this rural Paraguay that conventional circuits never show.

The Centro Cultural de la República El Cabildo, on Plaza de Armas, is the former seat of colonial government and one of the rare 18th-century constructions still standing in the capital. Its temporary exhibitions on Paraguayan political, cultural, and artistic history offer a different perspective on the country—often more critical and less official than that of the national museums. Check the program in advance on social media.

⏱ 60 min
2

Barrio Sajonia, in the east of downtown Asunción, is one of the city's liveliest working-class neighborhoods, with murals and street art spanning dozens of facades. Founded by German and Italian immigrants in the 19th century, the neighborhood preserves some beautiful houses from that era alongside modern buildings. Wander on foot between Oliva street and Avenida Colón.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Last shopping session: if you haven't yet stocked up on crafts, Avenida Artigas and Mercado Central offer good selections of ao po'i, tooled leather, guatambú and palo santo wood sculptures. Avoid carandaí wood products which can pose customs problems in some countries.

⏱ 75 min

Farewell evening at a restaurant with live Paraguayan music: the Paraguayan harp—the national instrument—and polca paraguaya (nothing to do with the Central European dance, it's a particular 3/4 syncopated rhythm) deserve to be heard in a good atmosphere. Downtown establishments offer harp and guitarra concerts in the evening, especially on weekends—inquire at your accommodation.

Depending on your flight time, one last walk in the center or at the Jardín Botánico is in order. The Jardín Botánico is the quietest place in the city at dawn—hundreds of tropical birds wake up in the trees and loros are visible from the earliest hours. If you're more in town, walk around Plaza de Armas in the morning when it still belongs to Asuncenos and not to tourists.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Transfer to Silvio Pettirossi International Airport, 15 km northeast of downtown Asunción. Count on 30 to 45 minutes from the center without traffic, 60 to 90 minutes during rush hour. Plan to arrive at least 2.5 to 3 hours before departure for international flights.
