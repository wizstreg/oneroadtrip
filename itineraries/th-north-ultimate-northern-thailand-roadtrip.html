Trip info

✨ Highlights▾
  • 309 steps to reach the 24-meter golden chedi of Doi Suthep, perched at 1,073 meters
  • 1,864 certified curves on the Mae Hong Son Loop, the country's most winding mountain road
  • Wat Si Chum at Sukhothai: a 15-meter Buddha glimpsed through a narrow slit in the brickwork
  • Golden Triangle: the confluence of the Mekong and Ruak rivers, where Thailand, Laos, and Burma meet
☀️ Weather▾

Best period: November to February, temperatures 20-28°C (cool mountain nights, 10-15°C). Avoid March-April (scorching heat up to 40°C + agricultural burning pollution) and June-October (monsoon, daily rains). Mountain roads can be slippery during rainy season.

📋 Notes▾

The Mae Hong Son Loop (routes 108 and 1095) is a winding mountain road—expect double the time compared to the distance. Avoid the rainy season (June-October) for the Doi Inthanon and Mae Hong Son sections. The Kew Mae Pan trail at Doi Inthanon is closed from June 1 to October 30.

Northern Thailand: Lanna Temples, Mountains and the Golden Triangle

📅 13 days📍 12 stops🚗 1270 km⏱ 25h

Chiang Mai → Lampang → Chiang Rai → Doi Inthanon → Mae Hong Son → Pai → Sukhothai

Chiang Mai → Lampang → Chiang Rai → Doi Inthanon → Mae Hong Son → Pai → Sukhothai

Northern Thailand means 13 days between six-century-old Lanna temples, mountain roads with 1,864 curves, and the ruins of the first Thai kingdom. We start with the three major cities—Chiang Mai and its 350 temples, Lampang the city of horse carriages, Chiang Rai and its colorful temples—before diving into the Mae Hong Son Loop, the country's most challenging mountain circuit. We finish at Sukhothai, founded in 1238, birthplace of the Thai alphabet and a UNESCO World Heritage site.

Chiang Mai

★★★★☆
1

Enter the old city through the square moat and walk up Ratchadamnoen Road to Wat Chedi Luang. The chedi was built starting in 1391 to an original height of 85 meters—an earthquake in 1545 reduced it to about 60 meters, never fully repaired since. Sculpted elephants guard its base. Look for the city pillar—the Lak Mueang planted here in 1800 is believed to protect the city.

⏱ 60 min
2

Continue on foot for ten minutes west to Wat Phra Singh. This temple, founded in 1345 to house the ashes of King Fu Kham, is home to 700 resident monks. Inside the Viharn Lai Kham, the smallest building in the complex, examine the murals from 1820—they depict daily life in Chiang Mai two centuries ago. The Phra Singh Buddha statue behind them is one of three identical replicas in the country.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

On Sunday evening, the Walking Street takes over the entire length of Wualai Road: hundreds of street food stalls, weavers, wood carvers. Order khao soi—the red curry and coconut milk soup with crispy noodles is the northern specialty.

⏱ 90 min
$$$9.4POR Thapae Gate

Chiang Mai

★★★★☆
1

Climb the 309 steps of Wat Phra That Doi Suthep, guarded by two seven-headed nagas. At the top, the 24-meter golden chedi gleams in the morning sun—and the view plunges across the entire Chiang Mai plain, 600 meters below. Legend tells that in 1383, a white elephant carried a Buddha relic all the way here, trumpeted three times, then collapsed. The king built the temple on that exact spot. Arrive before 8:30am to have the chedi to yourself.

⏱ 90 min
2

In the afternoon, turn toward Wat Umong, at the foot of Doi Suthep, in the forest. This 13th-century temple is nicknamed the 'tunnel temple'—Umong means tunnel in Thai. Enter the galleries carved into the brick mound: Buddhist monks still meditate inside. Outside, Buddhist quotations are engraved on plaques suspended from the trees.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Book a Thai cooking class at one of the many schools in the Nimmanhaemin neighborhood. Most offer a morning market visit followed by preparation of 4 to 5 traditional northern dishes—khao soi, larb, pad see ew—which you'll eat on site.

⏱ 180 min
$$$9.4POR Thapae Gate
🚗Drive to next stop: 100 km · 1h40

Lampang

★★☆☆☆
1

Twenty kilometers south of Lampang, Wat Phra That Lampang Luang sits on an artificial hillock surrounded by a 17th-century fortified wall—the Burmese used it as a stronghold. The Viharn Luang, built in 1476, is considered the oldest standing wooden building in Thailand. The interior pillars are decorated with lai kham, golden motifs on black lacquer. At the back of the viharn, the golden ku houses the Phra Chao Lan Thong Buddha, venerated since the 16th century.

⏱ 90 min
2

In town, push through the door of Wat Phra Kaew Don Tao on the bank of the Wang River. This temple housed the Emerald Buddha for 32 years (1436-1468) before the statue departed for Chiang Mai and then Bangkok. What remains here is the Phra Kaew Don Tao Buddha in crystal, enshrined in a golden niche of the Mondop. The adjacent chedi dates from the 12th century.

⏱ 40 min

Activities

Ride in a horse-drawn carriage to tour the old town—the drivers know all the temples in the center. This is the only place in Thailand where this mode of transport is still in tourist operation.

⏱ 60 min
$$$9.9Khwanlom House
🚗Drive to next stop: 200 km · 3h

Chiang Rai

★★★★☆
1

Thirteen kilometers south of Chiang Rai, Wat Rong Khun—the White Temple—is entirely covered in white plaster inlaid with thousands of mirror fragments. Approach via the Sukavadee Bridge: on each side, hands reaching skyward emerge from the ground, symbols of souls who haven't reached nirvana. Artist Chalermchai Kositpipat funded and built it himself, with work begun in the late 1990s and still ongoing. Inside, the frescoes blend Buddhist scenes with pop culture characters—Batman, Neo from The Matrix. Photos are forbidden in the main building.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Baan Dam Museum, 10 km north of the city center, is the exact opposite of the White Temple. Forty dark wooden buildings arranged in a garden—the work of national artist Thawan Duchanee. He used animal skins, skulls, buffalo horns. The ensemble explores themes of death and the cycle of life in Buddhist philosophy.

⏱ 60 min
3

End the day at Wat Rong Suea Ten, the Blue Temple, 6 km north of the center. The main hall is entirely painted in a shade between sapphire and cerulean, with a large white seated Buddha at the back. The name means 'temple of the dancing tiger'—tigers allegedly once leapt through the abandoned ruins.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In the evening, wander through Chiang Rai's Night Bazaar, south of the city center. Karen tribe stalls, Hmong embroidery, mountain coffee. Order naem ngiao—the spicy pork and tomato soup, a northern specialty often missing from Chiang Mai menus.

⏱ 90 min
$$$9.4MORA Boutique Hotel
🚗Drive to next stop: 70 km · 1h30

Chiang Rai

★★★★☆
1

Head down to the confluence of the Mekong and Ruak rivers at Sop Ruak. This is where the Thai, Burmese, and Laotian borders meet—the historic Golden Triangle. Thailand eradicated poppy cultivation on its territory as early as the 1970s-80s through royal substitution programs. Across the border in Myanmar, production continued well beyond. A golden Buddha statue stands on a boat-shaped platform anchored at the edge of the Mekong. From the bank, watch Laos to the right and Burma to the left.

⏱ 45 min
2

Two kilometers north of Sop Ruak, the Hall of Opium traces the history of the drug in the region, from its 19th-century medicinal uses to its eradication. The museum is one of the most modern in the country—allow two hours. It's closed on Mondays.

⏱ 120 min
3

Back in Chiang Rai, push through the door of Wat Phra Kaew—this is where the Emerald Buddha was discovered in 1434 inside a stucco-covered chedi. Lightning had split the stucco, revealing the green jade. The statue then departed for Lampang, Chiang Mai, and finally Bangkok. A replica remains here.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Embark on a short longtail boat trip on the Mekong from Sop Ruak—you'll cruise along the Burmese shore and approach the Laotian island of Don Sao. No visa needed to disembark for a few minutes on the Lao side.

⏱ 60 min
$$$9.4MORA Boutique Hotel
🚗Drive to next stop: 180 km · 3h30

Doi Inthanon

★★★★☆
1

First stop climbing toward the summit: Wachirathan Waterfall, whose name means 'diamond stream.' Water plunges 70 meters down a granite escarpment. In good weather, a permanent rainbow forms at the base. This is the most powerful waterfall in the park.

⏱ 30 min
2

At 2,560 meters altitude, the twin pagodas dominate the surrounding valleys. The first, Naphamethinidon, was erected in 1987 for King Bhumibol's 60th birthday. The second, Naphaphonphumisiri, in 1992 for Queen Sirikit's 60th birthday. Their flowered gardens change color with the seasons—Siamese cherry blossoms from late January to early February.

⏱ 60 min
3

Continue to Thailand's highest point: 2,565 meters. In January, it averages 6°C here. The Ang Ka trail takes you along a 360-meter boardwalk above a marsh of moss and orchids. This is one of the few places in Thailand where you can spot Himalayan bird species.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

If you're here between November and March, take the Kew Mae Pan trail—a 3 km loop through conifer forest and highland savanna, with views across the Burmese ridges. Bring a jacket: temperatures can drop to 5°C in the morning.

⏱ 120 min
$$$9.7Doi Inthanon Waterfall Garden
🚗Drive to next stop: 180 km · 5h

Mae Hong Son

★★★☆☆
1

Descending toward Mae Hong Son from Doi Inthanon, stop at the Doi Mae U Kho viewpoint if you're here between mid-November and mid-December. The hills are then covered with bua tong—wild yellow sunflowers that bloom on the slopes without being cultivated. Several kilometers of bright yellow against a green mountain backdrop. Outside this period, continue without stopping: the road itself is the spectacle.

⏱ 30 min
2

Climb up to Wat Phra That Doi Kong Mu, perched on the hill overlooking the entire town. Two white Burmese stupas built in 1860 gleam at the summit. Below, Mae Hong Son bathes in a sea of morning mist. The spectacle is most striking at dawn.

⏱ 45 min
3

At the edge of Nong Kham Lake, the twin temples—Wat Chong Kham (1827) and Wat Chong Klang (1867-71)—reflect their gilded tiered roofs in the water. These are Burmese Shan-style temples, very different from the Lanna wats seen elsewhere. Inside Wat Chong Klang, look for the glass paintings illustrating scenes from the Buddha's life.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

In the cool season (October-March), a small night market sets up along Nong Kham Lake. Order khao tom—Thai rice soup for the evening—or try grilled river fish. The atmosphere is more local than touristy.

⏱ 75 min
$$$9.5Baantonmai Farmstay
🚗Drive to next stop: 110 km · 3h20

Pai

★★★★☆
1

Before leaving Mae Hong Son province, stop at the Su Tong Pae Bridge. This 500-meter bamboo bridge built in 2012 by monks and villagers crosses the rice paddies between Mae Hong Son and a neighboring monastery. Early in the morning, Buddhist novices cross it in single file to collect alms—a calm image, without any artifice.

⏱ 30 min
2

Arrive in Pai and head straight to Pai Canyon, 5 km south of the village. Red-ochre rock formations with sharp ridges—the trails without guardrails run along crests sometimes barely a meter wide. Around 5pm, the sun grazes the walls and everything turns orange.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Pai's Walking Street comes alive every evening on the main street. Live musicians, pad thai stands, grilled sai ua sausages, fruit yogurt. Pai blends backpackers, hill tribe villagers, and Thai families—the atmosphere is warmer than cosmopolitan.

⏱ 90 min
$$$9.4Paihappyvillage

Pai

★★★★☆
1

Rent a scooter and set out early for Mo Paeng Waterfall, 8 km from Pai. It consists of several natural pools with rocky slides. The water is soft and cool, the setting surrounded by jungle. It's noticeably quieter than the attractions in the village center.

⏱ 75 min
2

On the way back, make a detour to the Land Split—a crack in the earth a few meters wide and a hundred meters long, opened suddenly during a landslide in 2008. It's surrounded by terraced rice paddies. Not essential, but it only takes ten minutes.

⏱ 20 min
3

In late afternoon, climb up to Yun Lai viewpoint. The sun sets behind the Burmese ridges and the Pai valley fills with golden mist. It's also the best spot at sunrise—come at 5:30am if you're staying in the area.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Plunge into the Tha Pai hot springs, 7 km from the center. A cold stream mixes with geothermal springs over rocks in the open air. In the cool season (November-February), it's particularly pleasant. Entry is paid.

⏱ 90 min
$$$9.4Paihappyvillage
🚗Drive to next stop: 430 km · 7h

Sukhothai

★★★★☆
1

Long drive south from Pai. Arrive in Sukhothai in late afternoon and enter the historical park directly—it's open until 6pm. This is where the first independent Thai kingdom was founded in 1238, breaking free from the Khmer empire. Its king Ramkhamhaeng invented the Thai alphabet here in 1292. Start with Wat Mahathat, when the light turns low.

⏱ 75 min
2

Wat Sa Si, a few hundred meters away, sits on a small island connected by a footbridge. A Sri Lankan-style chedi and a standing brick Buddha reflect in the water lilies. This is one of the most photographed spots in the park, especially at sunset.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Rent a bicycle at the park entrance—30 to 50 baht per day. The terrain is flat and the paths are paved. It's the most pleasant way to move between the different zones of the site.

⏱ 120 min
$$$9.9The Yard Sukhothai Old Town

Sukhothai

★★★★☆
1

Leave early for Wat Si Chum, in the northern zone. Approach the narrow slit in the brick mondop's facade: the Phra Achana Buddha—'the one who knows no fear'—stares at you from 15 meters high. Its fingers alone measure 1.3 meters. This is the largest seated stone Buddha in the park. An inscription found here in 1293 is the oldest written document in the Thai alphabet.

⏱ 45 min
2

Return to the central zone for Wat Si Sawai. Its three Khmer-style prangs—square-based towers—date from the 12th century, even before the kingdom's founding. This was a Hindu sanctuary dedicated to Shiva, transformed into a Buddhist temple during the expansion of Theravada Buddhism.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Ramkhamhaeng National Museum, just at the entrance to the central zone, displays the 1292 stele with the first Thai alphabet inscriptions, bronze Sukhothai-style Buddhas, and Sawankhalok ceramics. Allow 1.5 hours to tour it properly.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Cycle to the temples in the western zone, less visited. Wat Saphan Hin is built on a 200-meter hill—a long stone pathway leads to a tall standing Buddha overlooking the surrounding rice paddies. Practically no one after 4pm.

⏱ 120 min
$$$9.9The Yard Sukhothai Old Town

Sukhothai

★★★★☆
1

The northern zone also houses Wat Phra Phai Luang—three 12th-century Khmer prangs surrounded by moats. The site is overgrown with vegetation in places and practically deserted most of the time. A very different atmosphere from the restored central zone.

⏱ 45 min
2

If you have half a day to spare, push on to Si Satchanalai, 50 km north. This was Sukhothai's twin city—just as large, just as well preserved, with ten times fewer visitors. Wat Chang Lom there is surrounded by sculpted elephants in the round. The atmosphere is that of a forgotten archaeological site.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Before leaving Sukhothai, buy Sawankhalok ceramics at the old town market. These blue-gray celadon-style pottery pieces, manufactured in the region since the 13th century, are the oldest Thai craft objects.

⏱ 45 min
$$$9.9The Yard Sukhothai Old Town
