Grottammare

★★★★☆
1

Piazza Peretti — the beating heart of the borgo antico, framed by the town hall, the Chiesa di San Giovanni Battista and the Teatro dell'Arancio. From the theatre's loggia, the view sweeps all the way down to the Riviera delle Palme.

⏱ 45 min
2

Teatro dell'Arancio — built in the final decade of the 18th century by Ticinese architect Pietro Maggi, this intimate theatre takes its name from an orange tree that once stood in the square, whose care was entrusted each year to a local family in exchange for a tax exemption. During the Spanish flu epidemic (1918–1920), with no wood available for coffins, the interior woodwork was stripped out entirely. Restored in 2003, it now houses the Gianni Ottaviani museum space.

⏱ 45 min
3

Torrione della Battaglia — a 16th-century fortification tower commanding sweeping views of the coastline. Its rooms hold 250 works by local sculptor Pericle Fazzini (1913–1987), the man behind the monumental silver Resurrection in the Vatican's Nervi Hall.

⏱ 45 min
4

Museo Sistino — housed inside the Chiesa di San Giovanni Battista on Piazza Peretti, it brings together sacred objects from the village's churches, including a chalice donated by Pope Sixtus V, who was born in Grottammare in 1521.

⏱ 30 min
5

Resti del Castello — at the hilltop, the ruins of a fortress built between the 8th and 9th centuries by the monks of Farfa as a defence against Saracen raids. The surrounding park offers the highest viewpoint over the sea.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Wander the alleyways of the borgo antico between brick houses and orange trees, following two marked trails: one tracing the footsteps of Pope Sixtus V, the other leading to Fazzini's sculptures scattered throughout the village.

⏱ 60 min

Taste the brodetto alla sambenedettese — the iconic fish soup of the Picene coast — and the frustingo, a Christmas cake made with dried figs, walnuts, raisins and cooked wine.

⏱ 60 min

Monteprandone

★★☆☆☆
1

Piazza dell'Aquila — the village's main square, dominated by the Palazzo Comunale and the 16th-century Palazzo Campanelli, recognisable by the sundial painted on its façade.

⏱ 30 min
2

Santuario di San Giacomo della Marca — a Franciscan convent founded in 1449 by San Giacomo himself, following special authorisation from Pope Nicholas V. Below the chapel altar rests the saint's body, repatriated from Naples in 2001. The 19th-century cloister is decorated with a tempera fresco cycle by Emidio Tegli (1848) depicting key moments of his life in vivid popular scenes.

⏱ 60 min
3

Museo Civico dei Codici di San Giacomo — preserves 61 manuscripts from the saint's personal library, a collection that once held over 180 codices in this small convent, including Latin and Greek classics and an excerpt from the Qur'an. A remarkable library for a 15th-century Franciscan friar.

⏱ 45 min
4

Collegiata di San Niccolò — the village's main church, rebuilt in the 19th century on a foundation dating to the year 1000. The neoclassical interior is the work of Pietro Maggi — the same architect behind Grottammare's Teatro dell'Arancio.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Walk along the 15th-century town walls with a triple panorama stretching across the Adriatic, the Sibillini Mountains and the Gran Sasso.

⏱ 45 min

Sample the olives all'ascolana — the Piceno region's signature dish: large olives stuffed with meat, breaded and fried. Monteprandone is one of their historic heartlands.

⏱ 45 min

Offida

★★☆☆☆
1

Chiesa di Santa Maria della Rocca — built in 1330 on the ruins of a Lombard fortress, perched on a spur with sheer cliff faces on all sides. This brick Romanesque-Gothic church conceals a low-vaulted crypt below, its chapels covered in 14th-century frescoes by the Maestro di Offida. Enter from the rear: the main portal leads directly down into the crypt.

⏱ 60 min
2

Piazza del Popolo — the central square, anchored by the 11th–12th-century Palazzo Comunale with its arcaded loggia. Inside is the Teatro Serpente Aureo, a small 19th-century baroque theatre whose name evokes a local legend about a golden serpent said to haunt the surrounding hills.

⏱ 45 min
3

Museo del Merletto a Tombolo — set inside the Palazzo De Castellotti-Pagnanelli. Bobbin lace has been made in Offida since the 15th century; the oldest known examples are the camail collars of San Giacomo della Marca, now held at Monteprandone. A gown entirely crafted by Offida lacemakers was worn by Naomi Campbell at a London fashion show.

⏱ 45 min
4

Mura Castellane — the 12th-century walls encircling the historic centre. At their base stands the Monumento alle Merlettaie (1983) by sculptor Aldo Sergiacomi, depicting three women from three generations at work — a tribute to the transmission of a living craft.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Wine tasting at the Enoteca Regionale delle Marche, installed in the cellars of a 14th-century former Franciscan convent. Offida's territory is the most vine-planted in the entire province: Offida Pecorino DOCG and Rosso Piceno Superiore DOC find their finest expression here.

⏱ 60 min

Watch the lacemakers at work in the village: stroll along Corso Serpente Aureo and Via del Merletto, where women still sit in summer on their doorsteps, the tombolo cushion on their knees, the wooden bobbins clicking in an unbroken rhythm.

⏱ 45 min

Montefiore Dell Aso

★★★★☆
1

Polo Museale di San Francesco — housed in a 14th–16th-century former convent, this museum holds Carlo Crivelli's triptych, painted around 1471–1475 for the Chiesa di San Francesco. Originally a five-panel polyptych, it was sold off piece by piece in the mid-19th century — the Franciscan fathers accepted over a thousand scudi, ostensibly to fund convent repairs. The central Pietà now hangs in the National Gallery in London. The six remaining panels include a figure of the Maddalena widely regarded as one of the most beautiful Crivelli ever painted.

⏱ 75 min
2

Chiesa di San Francesco — built between 1247 and 1303 through alms collected by the friars. It was for this church's altar that Crivelli created his polyptych. Adjacent to the museum, the church retains several 16th-century chapel works.

⏱ 30 min
3

Collegiata di Santa Lucia — the main church of the village, dominating the historic centre. Its interior decoration was enriched during the 1900 Holy Year by artists of the Fermana school: a coffered stucco ceiling, frescoes by Luigi Fontana depicting the life of Saint Lucy, and a 17th-century walnut choir.

⏱ 30 min
4

Belvedere De Carolis — a panoramic viewpoint over the Valdaso valley, named in honour of Adolfo De Carolis (1874–1928), the Symbolist painter born in Montefiore and D'Annunzio's celebrated illustrator. The Valdaso still holds the intact hillside and farmhouse landscapes that Renaissance painters once used as backdrops in their works.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Wine tasting from the Valdaso: Falerio DOC and Rosso Piceno, in a territory where vineyards descend in terraces to the valley floor.

⏱ 60 min

Petritoli

★★★☆☆
1

Tre Archi — the village gateway: three neo-Gothic arches built in 1874, flanked by two 15th-century cylindrical towers. Local tradition holds that the name Petritoli comes from the merging of three ancient castles — Petrosa, Petrania and Petrollavia — represented by the three blue lozenges on its coat of arms.

⏱ 20 min
2

Torre Civica and Piazza della Rocca — a tower with a square base, hexagonal body and cylindrical top, at the village's highest point. From here the view stretches from the Sibillini to the Adriatic. At the tower's foot, look for the porta del morto: a small opening in the wall reserved exclusively for the passage of the deceased, so their spirit would not linger in the house.

⏱ 30 min
3

Convento delle Clarisse — built between the 16th and 17th centuries, now the town hall. The building retains several rooms with artworks and a council chamber of elegant, restrained beauty.

⏱ 30 min
4

Chiesa di Santa Maria dei Martiri — built at the end of the 16th century with an adjoining Franciscan convent. Inside: a 17th-century wooden octagonal coffered ceiling painted in tempera on Franciscan themes, and a Callido organ from the 18th century — one of the finest instruments in the province.

⏱ 30 min
5

Teatro dell'Iride — a small 19th-century Italian-style theatre with gilded wooden boxes and balconies, still in use today. Across the street, notice the Palazzo Vitali Rosati with its Venetian-inspired façade decoration, built in the late 19th century by the Vitali family.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Excursion to the Santuario della Madonna della Liberata, a few kilometres from the village on a wooded hillside. Inside, a votive painting from 1529 by Giovanni Battista Morale gives thanks to the Virgin for protecting the inhabitants from French troops and plague. The holy water stoup is carved from a Roman funerary cippus.

⏱ 60 min

Taste the olive oil of the Valdaso: Petritoli is surrounded by olive groves and mulberry trees, at the heart of a little-industrialised farming landscape.

⏱ 45 min

Moresco

★★★☆☆
1

Torre Eptagonale — a 25-metre 12th-century tower with seven sides, unique in Europe. Today it houses the TOMAV (Torre Moresco Arti Visive), a contemporary art centre. From the top, on a clear day, the eye travels from Monte Conero to the Gran Sasso — and all the way to the Croatian coast. The Ghibelline battlements visible today were rebuilt in the early 20th century after the original arabesque spire collapsed.

⏱ 45 min
2

Piazza Castello — the village's only square, triangular in shape, with a 16th-century portico that is all that remains of the left nave of the demolished Chiesa di Santa Maria in Castro. Under the groin vaults, a fresco by Vincenzo Pagani depicts the Madonna con Bambino.

⏱ 30 min
3

Torre dell'Orologio and ex-Chiesa di Santa Sofia — a 14th-century tower marking the original village gateway, flanked by a 16th-century portico. Passing under the tower leads to the former Chiesa Santa Sofia, now a performance space nicknamed 'lu teatrì'. The church retains a fresco from the school of Carlo Crivelli (15th century).

⏱ 30 min
4

Sala Consiliare of the Palazzo Comunale — preserves an altarpiece by Vincenzo Pagani (1540) depicting the Madonna in Gloria con i Santi Lucia, Lorenzo, Rocco e Nicolò. Pagani, born in Monterubbiano around 1490, is the author of both frescoes visible in the village.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Servigliano

★★★☆☆
1

The three gates and the quadrilateral — Servigliano is a rarity in Europe: a town built entirely from scratch in 1772, after a landslide destroyed the hilltop village above. Pope Clement XIV entrusted the project to architect Virginio Bracci. The result is an almost perfect rectangle of 144 × 137 metres, crossed by a cardo and decumanus in the ancient Roman fashion, with three monumental gates (Porta Clementina, Porta Pia, Porta Santo Spirito). Walking through any one of them is enough to grasp the whole idea.

⏱ 30 min
2

Piazza Roma — Collegiata di San Marco and Palazzo Municipale — the collegiata was built between 1774 and 1778 by Luigi Paglialunga (the same architect as Grottammare's Teatro dell'Arancio). It holds the reliquaries of San Servigliano Martire and San Gualtiero, including one in 1403 Marchigian goldsmithery, and an 18th-century Fedeli organ. Opposite the church, the Palazzo Municipale (1784–1789) was built with stones salvaged from the demolished old village. Its courtyard shelters a century-old magnolia.

⏱ 60 min
3

Parco della Pace — outside the walls, a vast grassy enclosure ringed by a brick wall: this is the former internment camp, built in 1915 and used during both World Wars. More than 50,000 people passed through it. A memorial (Casa della Memoria) housed in the old railway station documents its history.

⏱ 45 min
4

Santuario di Santa Maria del Piano — outside the quadrilateral, a former convent of the Observant Friars Minor, built over the ruins of a 1st-century Roman villa. It preserves a wooden altar and a wooden crucifix — the only traces of ancient occupation.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

⏱ 45 min

Try the serpe di cioccolato — a local shortbread shaped like a snake and filled with chocolate — and vincisgrassi al forno, the rich mixed-meat ragù lasagne that is the defining dish of the Macerata kitchen.

Montecosaro

★★★☆☆
1

Chiesa di San Rocco — built in 1447 on an octagonal plan, on the path leading up to the historic centre. It holds magnificent frescoes attributed to Simone de Magistris (1576), a Marchigian painter trained under Lorenzo Lotto. In the apse, the village of Montecosaro itself appears as a backdrop in a religious scene — a rare 16th-century visual document of how the village once looked.

⏱ 40 min
2

Complesso Agostiniano (Palazzo Comunale) — a medieval complex comprising the Chiesa di Sant'Agostino and a former convent now serving as the town hall. The church, though restructured in 1773, retains a gilded Byzantine-style reliquary and a Gaetano Callido organ (1792). ⏱ 45 min

The adjoining Teatro delle Logge, inaugurated in 1809 (stalls and three horseshoe tiers of iron boxes), was the site in 1568 of the execution of Enea Galizia and twelve conspirators, hanged from the railings after their failed uprising against the Cesarini family.

⏱ 45 min
4

The bell tower's campana carries the date 1425 and the oldest known municipal coat of arms.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Piazza del Comune — Palazzetto del Podestà and Torre Civica — the 14th-century Palazzetto with its five-arch loggia and pointed Gothic windows houses the Pinacoteca Civica 'Corrado Pellini' on the upper floor. Palazzo dei Priori — a Gothic 14th-century building dominating the Piazza Unità d'Italia, with crenellated crown, a banditore balcony and a grand connecting arch to the Palazzo Compagnucci. San Ginesio

★★★☆☆
1

Collegiata di Santa Maria Assunta — founded in 1090 on an early Christian place of worship, considered the only true masterpiece of ornate Romanesque-Gothic architecture in Le Marche. The façade is divided in two registers: the lower in travertine with a richly carved portal, the upper in modelled fired brick (cotto) across five compartments, completed in the 15th century by master builder Enrico l'Alemano. The civic tower flanks the façade — note that this is not the campanile, which stands on the transept flank. Damaged in the 2016 earthquake and undergoing priority restoration. The square bears the name of Alberico Gentili (1552–1608), a jurist from San Ginesio at the court of Elizabeth I of England, considered one of the founding fathers of international law and author of the treatise De Jure Belli.

⏱ 45 min
2

Porta Picena and Ospedale dei Pellegrini — the 14th-century Porta Picena is the main gateway, with a wide esplanade in front. The Ospedale dei Pellegrini (1295), built beside it in Romanesque style with a characteristic portico, once served as a staging post on the Rome–Loreto pilgrimage route. ⏱ 25 min
3

The Museo Gentili, in the adjacent former convent (now the town hall), brings together works from the 15th to 17th centuries: a canvas attributed to Ghirlandaio's workshop, Simone de Magistris' Pietà, the Pala della Battaglia tra i Ginesini e i Fermani by Nicola d'Ulisse (15th century), and communal parchments and manuscripts going back to 1199 — one of the best-preserved medieval archives in Le Marche. Partial closure post-earthquake: check access before visiting.

⏱ 60 min

Colle Ascarano — the 'balcony of the Sibillini'. Via Capocastello leads to this viewpoint beyond the walls, where the eye sweeps from the Gran Sasso to Monte Conero, from the Sibillini to the Majella — and on clear days, all the way to the Adriatic. The hill's name recalls the betrayal of the 'Ascari' during a medieval siege, explained by a plaque at Porta Ascarana.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

⏱ 50 min

Taste the Polentone di San Ginesio — oven-baked polenta served with meat ragù or sausage and pecorino cheese — and the award-winning Ciauscolo 'Il Campagnolo' from San Ginesio, which has claimed first prize at the national competition four years running.

1

Piazza Alta — the highest point and original historic nucleus of the village, built entirely in cotto (fired brick) in an elliptical form. The square brings together all the buildings of medieval power: the Palazzo del Popolo (converted into the 150-seat Teatro della Vittoria in 1831, with three horseshoe tiers of boxes), the Palazzo dei Priori, the Palazzo del Podestà with its civic tower, and the Chiesa di Santa Maria Assunta founded in 1265 with an even older campanile. A stone egg — an archaeological marker of Celtic origin and unknown purpose — sits beside the Palazzo del Podestà.

⏱ 60 min
2

Chiesa di Santa Maria Assunta — Romanesque façade, single-nave interior. The incised stone portal leads to 15th–16th-century frescoes, including the Incoronazione della Vergine, Bambino ed angeli musicanti by Lorenzo d'Alessandro, the master of Marchigian Renaissance painting. The 11th-century bell still rings today.

⏱ 35 min
3

Pinacoteca Civica (former convent of Santa Chiara) — brings together works recovered from churches across the territory, including the Madonna adorante il Bambino con angeli musicanti by Vittore Crivelli (late 15th century), the polyptych Compianto sul Cristo deposto by Vincenzo Pagani (1529), the Crocefissione by Stefano Folchetti (1513), and the Madonna della Misericordia by Pietro Alemanno (1494). Partial closure since the 2016 earthquake: check access before visiting.

⏱ 50 min
4

Chiesa di San Francesco — early 14th century, marking the memory of a halt made by Saint Francis between 1214 and 1216. Remodelled in the neoclassical style, it preserves in the adjacent former convent (now the town hall) the Biblioteca Comunale with ancient illuminated codices.

⏱ 25 min

Activities

Walk the spiral streets climbing toward Piazza Alta from the historic Porta Brunforte, retracing the layout of the original medieval castrum. Sarnano is built entirely in cotto: the warm colour of its rooftops, walls and paving stones shifts with the light throughout the day.

⏱ 50 min

Try Sarnano's soft ciauscolo (spreadable pepper salami) with fresh pecorino, polentone baked with sausage and mushrooms, and the crostata al torrone sarnanese — a purely local speciality made with candied and dried fruits.

⏱ 60 min

Visso

★★★★☆
1

Piazza dei Martiri Vissani — one of the finest medieval squares in Le Marche. Together with the adjacent Piazza Capuzi, it forms a C-shaped space bordered by 15th and 16th-century arcaded palazzetti, carved by the local petraioli vissani stonecutters. Art historian André Chastel cited it as a prime example of the Italian genius for urban scenography.

⏱ 30 min
2

Collegiata di Santa Maria — built in the 11th–12th century in Romanesque-Gothic style and substantially remodelled in the 13th and 14th centuries. The portal preserves an Annunciation fresco by Paolo da Visso in the lunette. The single-nave interior was reworked in the 17th century. Miraculously spared by the 2016 earthquake.

⏱ 30 min
3

Ex-Chiesa di Sant'Agostino (Museo Civico) — a 14th-century Gothic building to the left of the collegiata. Before the earthquake it held around 200 paintings and sculptures from the 12th to 18th centuries, including works by Paolo da Visso and, most remarkably, the original manuscripts of six Idylls by Giacomo Leopardi, among them L'Infinito. These manuscripts have since been moved to Bologna for safekeeping. Partially closed since the earthquake.

⏱ 45 min
4

Santuario della Madonna di Macereto — 9 km away, on a high plateau at 1,000 metres at the foot of Monte Bove, one of the most singular Renaissance buildings in Le Marche: an octagonal plan in white stone, built in the first half of the 16th century in the Bramantesque tradition. Isolated among high-altitude pastures, it was built, according to tradition, on the site of a Marian apparition. Partially closed since the earthquake.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Walk the medieval ramparts and through the four gates — the walls date to the 13th century, the gates to the following century. The local stone portals are engraved with family coats of arms and Latin mottoes, evidence of a trading town that once controlled commerce between Le Marche and Umbria.

⏱ 50 min

Taste the ciauscolo dei Sibillini and local cheeses — Visso is nicknamed 'the Norcia of Le Marche' for the quality of its cured meats, complemented by black truffle, mountain lentils and Sibillini honey.

⏱ 60 min

Esanatoglia

★★★☆☆
1

Pieve di Santa Anatolia — the oldest building in the village, Romanesque in origin and documented as early as 1180. Its 13th-century stone portal is one of the most complete Romanesque examples in the province of Macerata. Embedded in the base of the bell tower is a 1st-century Roman inscription, proof of Augustan-era occupation. The interior, entirely rebuilt in the 18th century, retains the baptismal font and the altar of the patron saint.

⏱ 40 min
2

Palazzo Varano (Town Hall) — a 14th-century fortified residence of the Da Varano lords of Camerino. During restoration after the 1997 earthquake, medieval frescoes were uncovered depicting a cavalry parade with Giulio Cesare da Varano in old age and his son Giovanni Maria — the only chivalric fresco cycle of its kind in Le Marche. The entire municipal council chamber is decorated with this 15th-century sequence.

⏱ 40 min
3

Casa delle Tre Porte and the Fonte del Borgo — a medieval house still inhabited today on Corso Vittorio Emanuele II, with three arches serving three distinct purposes: the everyday door, the bridal door, and the 'porta del morto' (walled up and reopened only for the passage of a coffin). The 13th-century village fountain in river stone has two separate basins — one for people, one for animals.

⏱ 30 min
4

Il Roccone and Eremo di San Cataldo — the Roccone is a 13th-century polygonal watchtower with angular spurs on the slopes of Monte Corsegno, reachable on foot from the centre. The Eremo di San Cataldo, at 766 metres, built over a medieval defensive tower, is dedicated to the Irish saint the community proclaimed as patron in the 17th century. Legend holds he stopped a boulder threatening the village — a large rock bearing the 'imprints of the saint' is visible along the path.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Stroll through the 'filetta' — Esanatoglia's nickname for its spindle-shaped elongated form. Corso Vittorio Emanuele II runs from Porta Sant'Andrea at the top to Porta Panicale at the bottom, with side streets branching off perpendicularly toward the seven bell towers that punctuate the village skyline.

⏱ 50 min

Try the Frostingo — an ancient sweet made with nuts, dried fruits and spices — along with local specialities: cotiche with beans, tagliatelline al tartufo nero and artisan honey (Esanatoglia is known as the 'City of Honey').

⏱ 60 min

Pergola

★★☆☆☆
1

Museo dei Bronzi Dorati della Città di Pergola — the major archaeological treasure of Le Marche. In 1946, farmers near Cartoceto di Pergola unearthed while ploughing four gilded bronze statues from the 1st century BC: an equestrian warrior, a noblewoman and two divine or priestly figures, in exceptional condition. Their origin is still debated: some historians identify the equestrian group as depicting Publius Quinctilius Varus, governor of Syria. They are the only gilded equestrian bronzes from Roman antiquity preserved anywhere in the world. Displayed since 1999 in a museum purpose-built for them.

⏱ 90 min
2

Duomo di Santa Maria Assunta and historic centre — the cathedral was rebuilt in the 19th century on a medieval foundation, with a freestanding 12th-century campanile. The interior holds a Crucifixion attributed to Raffaellino del Colle, a pupil of Raphael. The Piazza Municipale is lined with Renaissance palaces, including the Palazzo dei Priori with its loggia.

⏱ 45 min
3

Convento di San Francesco — founded in the 13th century, the church preserves 15th-century frescoes and works recovered from rural churches across the Pergola area. The cloister is one of the few intact Franciscan ensembles surviving in the province of Pesaro-Urbino.

⏱ 35 min

Activities

Walk through the historic centre to admire the arcaded houses typical of the Pergola area, and look for Roman milestones repurposed as door lintels in several façades — traces of the Via Flaminia that once ran nearby.

⏱ 45 min

Taste the formaggio di fossa from Pergola — cheese aged in tufa pits — and the crescia al formaggio, a wood-fired leavened bread with pecorino and eggs, an Easter speciality throughout the Pesaro area.

⏱ 60 min

Matelica

★★★☆☆
1

Piazza Enrico Mattei — the heart of Matelica. Four palaces of entirely different styles face each other here, making it visually unique. On the left, the Palazzo Ottoni (1472), built by architects Costantino and Giovan Battista da Lugano for the Ottoni family — with its loggia and rear courtyard linked by an aerial walkway to another family property. Opposite, the Palazzo del Governo (1271), extended by the Loggia d'Ascanio Ottoni, so shallow it serves no practical purpose — pure urban ornament. To the right, the Palazzo Comunale, acquired from the Scotti di Narni family by the municipality in 1606. At the centre, the Fontana Ottagonale (1587), in white stone, by Lattanzio Ventura — the same architect who worked on the Santa Casa at Loreto.

⏱ 60 min
2

Museo Piersanti — one of the finest museums in Le Marche. Monsignor Venanzio Filippo Piersanti, born in Matelica in 1688, began assembling a collection of art and historical objects in his palazzo in the 17th century. The museum spans three ground-floor rooms and sixteen on the upper floor. The palace has kept its domestic spaces intact: kitchen, cellar with winemaking equipment, bread tools, curing implements. An 1763 inventory documents everything that existed then, livestock included. The museum currently displays the Globo di Matelica — on loan since the closure of Palazzo Finaguerra after the 2016 earthquake: a 29-cm sphere of Greek marble found in 1985 during consolidation works. Dated to the 1st–2nd century AD, it is engraved with lines calculating hours of daylight, solstices, equinoxes and the passage of the sun through the zodiac constellations. One of the rarest objects in the world — a spherical sundial that is simultaneously astronomical and astrological, and whose commissioner and purpose remain unknown.

⏱ 60 min
3

Teatro Piermarini — inaugurated in 1812, designed by Giuseppe Piermarini, the architect of Milan's La Scala. Excavations beneath the stage have uncovered Iron Age dwelling remains and the ruins of a 1st–2nd century Roman bath complex.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Verdicchio di Matelica tasting — Matelica produces one of Le Marche's two Verdicchio DOC/DOCG wines. Different from the Jesi version, it is more structured and mineral, from an inland valley between the Sibillini and Monte San Vicino. The largest producer is the Cantina Belisario cooperative. Pair it with local honey: Matelica is nicknamed the 'City of Honey' for its chestnut, acacia and especially oak honeydew honeys — bitter, rare and prized by connoisseurs.

⏱ 60 min

Treia

★★☆☆☆
1

Piazza della Repubblica — Treia's finest square and one of the most theatrical in all of Le Marche. Its horseshoe shape opens eastward onto a wide panoramic terrace behind a late 18th-century balustrade. On clear days the view reaches Monte Conero and the Adriatic. The square is framed by three remarkable buildings: the Palazzina dell'Accademia Georgica (designed by Valadier, 19th century), which holds 15,000 volumes and the 1,196 parchments of the municipal historical archive; the Palazzo Comunale (the result of merging two medieval palaces, restructured in the Renaissance), which houses the Pinacoteca with portraits of Treia's nobility and paintings by Pietro Tedeschi, Antonio Balestra and Agostino Bonisoli; and the Cattedrale della SS. Annunziata, designed by Andrea Vici, a pupil of Vanvitelli — one of the largest churches in Le Marche by floor area. In the sacristy: a lunette by Vincenzo Pagani (Deposition of Christ) and a panel by Giacomo da Recanati.

⏱ 60 min
2

Porta Vallesacco and Teatro Condominiale — Porta Vallesacco is the most celebrated of Treia's seven medieval gates — declared a national monument because it was here in 1263 that the Montecchiesi (the inhabitants of what is now Treia) repelled the troops of Corrado d'Antiochia. Just beyond it, the Teatro Condominiale (1801, architect Lucatelli) is a rare example of a theatre with entirely wooden balconies. Its stage curtain, painted by Silverio Copparroni in 1865, depicts exactly this Battle of Vallesacco. The theatre also houses the Dolores Prato Study Centre, dedicated to the great novelist born here — her autobiographical novel Giù la piazza non c'è nessuno, published when she was 87, is considered a masterwork of 20th-century Italian literature.

⏱ 60 min
3

Museo Civico Archeologico (Convento San Francesco) — housed in the former San Francesco convent, this museum holds sculptural and architectural fragments from Roman Treia, along with an exceptional collection of statuettes and cult objects of Isis and other Egyptian deities of the Ptolemaic period — unearthed in the early 20th century on the site of the ancient Roman city, where the Santuario del SS. Crocifisso now stands. The outline of an amphitheatre is still visible in the vegetation of a nearby field. Key pieces include two portrait heads, a headless togated statue and a small head of Mercury.

⏱ 60 min
4

Torre Onglavina — a 12th-century tower on a polygonal plan, the only surviving remnant of one of the three castles that once formed Treia under the name Montecchio (del Cassero, dell'Elce and dell'Onglavina). Restored in the 1950s, it offers the best view in the village: the Sibillini, the Majella, the Gran Sasso, the Conero and the Adriatic all in one sweep. Not to be confused with the Torre San Marco, another crenellated 12th-century torrette still standing near Porta Palestro.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Disfida del Bracciale (event, first Sunday of August) — Treia is the Italian capital of the Pallone col Bracciale — a team sport once as popular as football, with neighbourhood sides competing in an annual tournament dating back to 1978. Carlo Didimi, the greatest player of the 19th century, was born here. For ten days before the event, the village transforms: taverns, costumed craftsmen, street performances. A Museo del Gioco del Pallone col Bracciale presents trophies, bracelets and 19th-century balls.

⏱ 60 min

Calcione and Treia specialities — the calcione is Treia's savoury-sweet speciality: a flaky pastry filled with eggs, pecorino, sugar and olive oil. Eaten as a snack or dessert. Annual festival in May (Sagra del Calcione). Best paired with the quintessential vincisgrassi and ciauscolo from the Macerata province.

⏱ 60 min

Cingoli

★★★☆☆
1

Balcone delle Marche and Palazzo Castiglioni — Cingoli perches at 631 metres atop Monte Circe, and its nickname of 'Balcony of Le Marche' is immediately justified when you step onto the medieval rampart terrace: on clear days the panorama spans the Adriatic, Monte Conero and the Apennines. A bronze statue of Tito Labieno (by sculptor Nazzareno Rocchetti), Caesar's lieutenant who founded the city at his own expense in the 1st century BC, keeps watch over the view. Nearby, the Palazzo Castiglioni is the birthplace of Francesco Saverio Castiglioni, who became Pope Pius VIII (1761–1830). Built in the late 17th century by merging two noble residences previously separated by the demolished Chiesa di San Giuliano, it retains 19th-century murals and a library that once held 17,500 volumes.

⏱ 60 min
2

Palazzo Comunale — Sala degli Stemmi and Lorenzo Lotto's Madonna del Rosario — the Palazzo Comunale dates to the 13th century in its oldest section; its façade was redone in 1531 on the orders of governor Egidio Canisio da Viterbo. Inside, the Sala degli Stemmi has a vaulted ceiling decorated with the coats of arms of Cingoli's noble families, with medallions in the corners evoking the city's Roman origins. On display here (moved from the earthquake-closed Chiesa di San Domenico) is Lorenzo Lotto's altarpiece Madonna del Rosario (mid-16th century): a work remarkable for the posture of the Christ Child, the 16 narrative medallions across the top, and the presence of a perfect scale model of the village of Cingoli painted within the composition itself. The Museo Archeologico Statale on the upper floor and in the basement presents prehistoric and Roman finds from the Piano di Fonte Marcosa excavations.

⏱ 60 min
3

Collegiata di Sant'Esuperanzio — outside the walls, one of the most important religious buildings in Cingoli and a fine example of Marchigian Gothic architecture. Its origins go back to around the year 1000; the current structure is already documented in 1139. The 1295 portal is decorated with bas-reliefs of the saint. Inside, a single nave formed by six pointed arches on rectangular pilasters; in the crypt, the relics of Sant'Esuperanzio, rediscovered during works in 1495. The nearby Chiesa di San Filippo Neri in the village offers a good example of Marchigian baroque: a single nave with lateral absidioles, stucco decoration and vault frescoes by Pier Simone Fanelli and Paolo Marini.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Walk the medieval ramparts and watch the sunset over Lago di Castreccioni — the well-preserved walls allow a circular walk around the perimeter with hillside views. Late afternoon, the lake (a few kilometres away, the largest swimmable artificial lake in central Italy) is ideal for an early dinner at a local trattoria, with grilled crescia marchigiana stuffed with local ingredients.

⏱ 60 min

Offagna

★★★★☆
1

Rocca medievale di Offagna — in 1454, Ancona obtained from Pope Nicholas V the small village of Offagna, positioned on an arenite spur 300 metres above sea level. In just two years, the maritime republic built a quadrangular fortress with a massive central mastio rising more than 30 metres over five floors, built entirely in brick. The entrance is at the fourth level — making the stronghold virtually impregnable. Declared a National Monument in 1902. Today the Rocca houses the Museo delle Armi Antiche (a collection spanning prehistory to the modern era, born from a collaboration between the Accademia di Oplologia e Militaria di Ancona and the State Museums of San Marino) and allows visitors to climb to the walkway and mastio terrace for a 360° view over the countryside, the Sibillini and the Conero.

⏱ 60 min
2

Piazza del Maniero and underground gallery — the village's main square, dominated by the Rocca. On its flank stands the Chiesa San Tommaso Apostolo (17th century, fired brick), with an altarpiece depicting San Bernardino da Siena offering the village to the Madonna. Beneath the Torre dell'Orologio, a dark gallery cuts through the arenite rock: a tunnel carved in the 17th century, attributed by local legend to the secretive Giacomini sect, said to be haunted by a ghost guarding a hidden treasure. To the east of the square, via del Monastero leads to the former convent of Santa Zita and the Chiesa del SS. Sacramento (late 18th century), both built by architect Andrea Vici.

⏱ 60 min
3

Museo di Scienze Naturali Luigi Paolucci — located on via del Monastero, this museum preserves part of the collection of Ancona naturalist Luigi Paolucci (1849–1935), who claimed to have catalogued every species of fauna and flora in Le Marche. The rooms display now-extinct species, 19th-century scientific instruments and an exhibition documenting the Liberation of Ancona in 1944, with photographs and weaponry from the Italian Campaign.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Feste Medievali d'Offagna (last week of July) — one of the most celebrated medieval re-enactments in Le Marche. For several days the village is transformed: horsemen and ladies in the streets, costumed merchants and craftsmen, falconry, jugglers, medieval taverns with period banquets. The Contesa tra i Rioni pits the neighbourhoods against each other in various contests. The 'dolce della Contesa' with Moretum (blackberry and honey wine) is the festival's signature treat. Year-round, the crescia — a grilled flatbread filled with local cured meats — can be sampled throughout the village. Offagna even created an Accademia della Crescia to protect the traditional recipe.

⏱ 60 min

Morro D Alba

★★☆☆☆
1

Camminamento di Ronda 'La Scarpa' — Morro d'Alba claims a record unique in Italy: the only fortified village whose entire inner walkway is covered and lined with arcades along its full length. The irregular pentagonal wall circuit with six bastions dates to the 13th–15th centuries. The covered inner walkway, known as 'La Scarpa' (from the scarped inclination of the walls), runs 300 metres, punctuated by six watchtowers and a long row of windows opening onto the countryside — the Sibillini on one side, the Conero and the sea on the other. Two panoramic towers bear the names Torre Teodorico (east, hillside views, now a cultural venue) and Torre Federico I (west, sunset views).

⏱ 60 min
2

Sotterranei di Morro d'Alba — beneath the village houses, inhabitants carved out from the 14th to 15th centuries a labyrinth of caves and galleries on multiple levels — an entire underground city. These spaces served to preserve food, shelter livestock in winter and take refuge during attacks. In the galleries, the oldest known bombarda (primitive firearm) in Italy was discovered — the original is now at the Museo Storico Nazionale di Artiglieria in Turin. Part of the galleries houses the Museo Utensilia, which documents the life of Marchigian sharecroppers (mezzadri) through seven reconstructed environments: a press, weaving workshop, cellar, barn and farmyard, with scythes, baskets, barrels, ploughs and agricultural tools.

⏱ 60 min
3

Piazza Tarsetti and Torre Civica — the village's main square. The fountain bears a graffito by Maestro Enzo Cucchi, born in Morro d'Alba in 1949, one of the leading figures of Italian Transavanguardia. The clock tower preserves a 1504 bas-relief of a rampant lion — the symbol of Jesi, to which Morro d'Alba still belonged at that time. Opposite, the Chiesa di San Gaudenzio (1763), recently restored, holds several paintings including Claudio Ridolfi's altarpiece (1630) Incoronazione della Vergine e Santi, and a 17th-century canvas depicting San Michele Arcangelo, the village's patron saint.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Lacrima di Morro d'Alba DOC tasting — Lacrima is a red wine with violet hues, fruity and lightly tannic, from an ancient grape variety whose skin is so fragile it tears at full ripeness, allowing the juice to seep out — hence the name. More than ten wineries offer visits and tastings: the Antica Cantina Sant'Amico (founded 1850, one of the oldest in Le Marche) is an ideal starting point. Morro d'Alba also falls within the production zone of Verdicchio dei Castelli di Jesi DOC.

⏱ 60 min

Corinaldo

★★★☆☆
1

Medieval walls and towers — a 912-metre fortified circuit, the best-preserved in Le Marche according to the Touring Club Italiano. Built between the 14th and 15th centuries by Francesco di Giorgio Martini after the village was razed in 1360 by the papal army of Galeotto Malatesta. Three access gates: Porta Santa Maria del Mercato (the best preserved, with traces of a drawbridge), Porta San Giovanni to the north, Porta Nova to the west (late 15th century). Four towers punctuate the circuit: Torre dello Scorticatore (inhabited until World War II by a sheep skinner who lived there in exchange for guarding the gate), Torre dello Sperone (18 m, the highest point of the walls), Torre del Calcinaro and Torre della Ronda. In 1517, the inhabitants held out for twenty days against the siege of Francesco Maria Della Rovere — a feat that earned the village the title of Città, granted by Pope Leo X.

⏱ 60 min
2

Via La Piaggia — the Cento Scale — a stairway of 109 steps cutting the historic centre in two, from Porta Santa Maria del Mercato down to the heart of the borgo. Halfway, the Pozzo della Polenta: according to legend, a farmer once dropped a sack of flour into the well — it mixed with the water and fed the entire village through a whole winter. At the bottom right, the Casa di Scuretto: only the façade exists, complete with street number. The cobbler Gaetano (nicknamed Scuretto) drank away the money his son had sent from America to build a house. When the son asked for a photograph of the works, Scuretto had a façade erected and was photographed at the window. The house was never built.

⏱ 60 min
3

Santuario Diocesano di Santa Maria Goretti and Casa Natale — Maria Goretti was born in Corinaldo in 1890, martyred in 1902 at the age of 11, and canonised on 24 June 1950 by Pope Pius XII — one of the rare cases in Church history where a mother attended the canonisation of her own child. The sanctuary holds on its left the remains of her mother Assunta (who died in Corinaldo in 1954), and on its right those of Alessandro Serenelli, the murderer who, released after 27 years in prison, asked for forgiveness. On the central white Carrara marble altar, a silver urn contains the bone of the arm with which the saint tried to defend herself. The casa natale, a few minutes from the centre, displays the kitchen with its fireplace, the birth room with period furniture and the loom used by her mother.

⏱ 60 min
4

Raccolta d'Arte Civica Claudio Ridolfi — a museum in the former Augustinian convent on the main street. Collection of 16th and 17th-century sacred art, including works by local painter Claudio Ridolfi. One room preserves the elaborate costumes worn each year for the Contesa del Pozzo della Polenta historical re-enactment.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Full circuit of the walls along the outer path — walk all 912 metres of the circuit from the outside: a linden-lined avenue runs alongside the walls on the eastern side. From the towers, the view embraces the Marchigian countryside, the rolling green hills and, on clear days, the Apennines with the Monti Azzurri.

⏱ 60 min

Down the Cento Scale and the legends of the borgo — descend the 109 steps of Via La Piaggia from Porta Santa Maria, pause at the Pozzo della Polenta to read the carved legend, then find the façade of the Casa di Scuretto to the right at the bottom of the stairs (50 metres past the well, in the first side lane). The entire borgo can be walked in about an hour and a half.

⏱ 60 min

Contesa del Pozzo della Polenta (third Sunday of July) — a historical re-enactment in period costume commemorating the 1517 victory over Della Rovere's army. Jugglers, ladies, archers, flag-throwers and acrobats fill the village. The four Rioni compete in various trials. Considered the oldest festival in the province of Ancona by local tradition.

⏱ 60 min

Mondolfo

★★★★☆
1

Double ring of walls — Mondolfo is one of the few villages in Le Marche with two concentric circuits of walls. The inner ring dates in part to the 6th century (partially visible from the Byzantine period), the outer to the 15th-century medieval construction. The 19th-century Porta Fanestre and Porta Santa Maria give access to the outer enclosure. The walkways along the walls lead to a belvedere from which, on a clear day, the Croatian coast is visible.

⏱ 60 min
2

Palazzo Comunale and Torre Civica — a neo-Gothic building from 1931, rebuilt with bricks salvaged from the original structure destroyed by an earthquake the same year. Three symbols decorate the façade: the municipal coat of arms in gold and azure (the colours of fields and sea), the campanone San Gervaso, and a stone escutcheon held in a lion's mouth — the city's heraldic device. The central square bears the name of Piazza Mario del Monaco.

⏱ 60 min
3

Bastione di Sant'Anna and Belvedere — a former military bastion converted into an Italian-style garden with a limonaia (orangery). The belvedere at the top offers a panorama over the valley, the coast at Marotta and Monte Conero to the south. On clear days the Croatian coastline is visible on the horizon.

⏱ 60 min
4

Galleria senza soffitto — an open-air museum spread throughout the village. Murals, black-and-white photographs by Mario Giacomelli — a photographer from nearby Senigallia, one of the major figures in 20th-century Italian photography, whose series Io non ho mani che mi accarezzano il volto was inspired by a poem by David Maria Turoldo. The Scalinata del Pianoforte is one of the village's most photographed landmarks.

⏱ 60 min
5

Chiesa di Sant'Agostino — a large single-nave church with barrel vault and twelve gilded carved wooden altars. Two 17th-century paintings stand out: Madonna del Rosario and Madonna col Bambino. Above the entrance, a 1776 organ by Gaetano Callido, one of the greatest Venetian organ builders of the 18th century. The campanone known as 'Cristo Re' is the one that rings on Sundays in a concert of bells.

⏱ 60 min
6

Abbazia di San Gervasio di Bulgaria — a 5th–6th-century abbey a few hundred metres from the centre, founded on the Via Flaminia. Its basilica plan is directly inspired by the great basilicas of Ravenna. In the crypt, the largest Ravenna-style sarcophagus in Le Marche, dating to the 6th century, decorated with Christian symbols, crosses, laurel wreaths and two peacocks symbolising immortality. One of the oldest archaeological sites in the province.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Walk the camminamenti di ronda to the belvedere — follow the walls from inside the village along the old walkways to the highest panoramic point. Along the way, an old cannon recalls the bastion's defensive past.

⏱ 60 min

Museo Civico — the 1858 clock mechanism — installed in the cloister adjoining the Chiesa di Sant'Agostino. The centrepiece: an 1858 clock movement, still working, wound by hand, chiming every 15 minutes. Exhibition on accordion makers — a craft tradition of the region.

⏱ 60 min

La Cacciata — historical re-enactment (summer) — a Renaissance living history event featuring the pallone col bracciale (a ball game played with a leather brace, once as popular in 16th-century Italy as football is today), sword tournaments, archery and flag-throwing. Banquets with local products.

⏱ 60 min

Mondavio

★★★☆☆
1

Rocca Roveresca — masterpiece of Renaissance military architecture — Built between 1482 and 1492 on the orders of Giovanni Della Rovere — nephew of Pope Sixtus IV and son-in-law of Federico da Montefeltro, Duke of Urbino — by Sienese architect Francesco di Giorgio Martini. The mastio is octagonal with a broad base, its ten irregular trapezoidal faces tapering upward in a helical spiral: a geometry designed to deflect cannonballs by presenting oblique angles rather than flat walls. Martini himself wrote in his treatise of 'geometrie che disegno mostrar non può' — forms that drawing alone cannot convey. A torricino di rinfianco protects the walkway leading to a semi-circular torrione, connected by an entrance ravelin. Seen from above, the ensemble traces a crossbow — Martini's habitual signature. The rocca was never struck by a single cannonball. Converted into a prison from 1631 until the 19th century after the Della Rovere line died out. Left unfinished following the simultaneous deaths of both Martini and Giovanni Della Rovere in 1501. In the moat, since 2000, reconstructed war machines built after Martini's original drawings.

⏱ 60 min
2

Museum of historical re-enactment — 5 levels — From the basement to the top floor of the mastio: an armoury in the ravelin (a mascolo — the terminal section of a bombard weighing more than six quintals — along with moschettoni, falconetti, spingarde, halberds and ornately decorated bladed weapons), then four floors of reconstructed Renaissance scenes: a wedding banquet, a prison and torture chamber, an oven, a munitions store. Period-costumed mannequins inhabit every room. Single ticket including the Museo Civico and the village theatre.

⏱ 60 min
3

Piazza Della Rovere and Porta Cavour — the main square was created in the 17th century by vaulting over the rocca's northern moat. It still bears the name of the commissioning family. Porta Cavour, the village gateway, is a well-preserved circular cassero.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Full visit of the Rocca Roveresca — walk all five levels of the mastio, through the narrow passages and spiral staircases that give a concrete sense of life in a 15th-century military castle. End on the camminamenti di ronda to observe from outside the mastio's ten irregular faces and the war machines in the moat.

⏱ 60 min

Caccia al Cinghiale — historical re-enactment (13–15 August) — Three days of Renaissance living history: Mondavio's historical archers in period costume, tournaments, crossbow competitions, banquets with local products in a village transformed into a 15th-century settlement.

Fiorenzuola Di Focara

★★★☆☆
1

Village gateway and the Dante plaque — the only true promontory on the northern Adriatic between Trieste and Ancona's Monte Conero, 177 metres above the cliff. The village appeared between the 10th and 13th centuries as part of a defensive system alongside Casteldimezzo, Gradara and Granarola, at the crossroads between the jurisdictions of Ravenna and Pesaro, later between the Malatesta of Rimini and those of Pesaro. At the village entrance, a stone plaque engraves the lines from Canto XXVIII of Dante's Inferno (vv. 88–89): Guido da Montefeltro foretells that the winds of Focara would be so deadly that no prayer or vow would suffice. 'Focara' derives either from the fuochi — fires lit at the summit to guide sailors by night — or from the fornacelle where bricks were fired (from the Romagnol fuchèr).

⏱ 60 min
2

Pentagonal wall circuit and Sant'Andrea campanile — of the original five bastions, three survive. The walls along the cliff edge are being gradually eroded by the sea below. 17th-century doorways with inscriptions decorate the lanes. The church of Sant'Andrea dates to the 12th century — demolished by the 1916 earthquake, only its bell tower remains standing, its clock still working, striking the hour in this near-silent village.

⏱ 60 min
3

Belvedere on the cliff — Sentiero dell'Amore — The village lanes describe concentric semicircles converging toward the cliff's edge, then rising back toward the old bell tower. From the belvedere at the wall's edge, the drop to the sea is sheer and vertiginous. The Sentiero dell'Amore is a path laid along the old rampart walkway, running along the cliff face.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Descent to the beach at Fiorenzuola — the wild beach of Fiorenzuola di Focara is accessible on foot from the village via the Strada della Marina. A must on any day spent at Focara. Turquoise water, shallow seabed, within the Parco Naturale del Monte San Bartolo: ranked among the 40 most beautiful beaches in Europe by The Guardian in 2019. In summer, a shuttle connects the village to the beach.

⏱ 60 min

Hiking in the Parco del Monte San Bartolo — from Fiorenzuola, several trails in the Parco Naturale Regionale del Monte San Bartolo head north toward Casteldimezzo or south toward Pesaro. Trail 151 runs along the coast, alternating between Mediterranean scrubland and open views over the Adriatic.

Gradara

★★★★☆
1

Rocca Malatestiana — State museum — A 12th-century medieval fortress with a square plan and a powerful polygonal torrione to the northeast, reached by drawbridge. The castle passed from the Malatesta to the Sforza and then the Della Rovere families, and is now a State museum with 14 rooms furnished with antiques and works of art. Key highlights: the Camera di Francesca — with the lectern that held the 'galeotto' book (the love story of Lancelot and Guinevere that the two lovers were reading when discovered), and the trapdoor through which Paolo tried in vain to flee (his cloak caught on a nail); the Camerino di Lucrezia Borgia, covered in frescoes attributed to Amico Aspertini depicting Lucrezia as the goddess Fortuna; the Sala della Giustizia with the altarpiece by Giovanni Santi (Raphael's father), Madonna in Trono con Bambino e Santi; and the chapel with an Andrea della Robbia glazed terracotta from the 1480s. The rampart walkways are open to visitors and offer views over the hills and Adriatic coast.

⏱ 60 min
2

The legend of Paolo and Francesca — Francesca da Polenta, daughter of the lord of Ravenna, was promised to Gianciotto Malatesta (known as 'lo zoppo' — the lame). To avoid a possible refusal, the Malatesta sent his brother Paolo (known as 'il Bello') as proxy suitor. Love blossomed between Francesca and Paolo. Gianciotto surprised and killed them both — probably in September 1289, though some scholars date the events between 1283 and 1285. Dante immortalises them in Canto V of the Inferno among the lustful, elevating their story to a universal symbol of pure love. Boccaccio, Petrarch, Silvio Pellico and D'Annunzio later made their own works from this tragedy.

⏱ 60 min
3

Medieval village and Torre dell'Orologio — the borgo spreads between two rings of walls over roughly 800 metres of perimeter. Corso Umberto I runs through the village from the Torre dell'Orologio to the rocca, lined with craft shops, restaurants and inns. No cars pass inside the walls. The village was voted 'Borgo dei Borghi' in 2018.

⏱ 60 min
4

Museo Storico and Grotte — beneath the village, a network of 16 caves carved into the rock for more than 1,500 years — probably used for secret gatherings and as Byzantine places of worship under the influence of nearby Ravenna. Ten are accessible, one is included in the museum visit. Exhibition of torture instruments, medieval weapons and objects of daily life.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Guided tour of the Rocca Malatestiana — a theatrical journey through 14 furnished rooms following the story of Paolo and Francesca. The guided visit lasts approximately 1h30 and includes the rampart walkways. Entry: €11 (mastio and walkway access). Advance booking recommended in high season.

⏱ 60 min

Passeggiata degli Innamorati — circular path — a 3-kilometre walk that circles the entire village outside the walls, following the old rampart paths. Named after Paolo and Francesca. The loop starts and ends at the Torre dell'Orologio, with continuous views over the Marchigian hills.

⏱ 60 min

Assedio al Castello (summer — August) — re-enactment of the 43-day siege of 1446, during which Francesco Sforza, allied with Federico da Montefeltro, deployed cannons, bombards and schioppi around the rocca before being forced to withdraw. Period costumes, historical procession, drums, jugglers. The village transforms into a medieval encampment.

Montefabbri

★★★★☆
1

The gateway — the only entrance to the castello — Montefabbri first appears in documents in 1216, having developed from the 9th–10th century around the pieve of San Gaudenzio. The name comes from Monte Fabrorum — the mountain of the Fabbri family, the earliest feudal lords. In 1578, the Duke of Urbino granted the village as a fief to Francesco Paciotti, a civil and military architect who brought a long period of prosperity and ceramic craftsmanship. The gateway is the only opening in the enclosure wall. The medieval arch once supported a drawbridge — the niches where the bridge arms rested are still visible in the vestibule. On the valley-facing façade above the arch, a small 15th-century sandstone carving depicts a Madonna lattante. On the inner façade, the white marble arms of Count Francesco Paciotti preside over the entrance. In 1799, insurgents from the surrounding countryside captured Napoleonic soldiers here who had sought refuge on the walls after the sack of Urbino.

⏱ 60 min
2

Pieve di San Gaudenzio — the original nucleus of the village — one of the four oldest pievi in the Archdiocese of Urbino, founded between the 7th and 8th centuries and dedicated to the first bishop of Rimini, martyred in 360. Rebuilt and extended in 1570, as recorded by a tablet at the back of the church. The interior preserves 16th–17th-century works and a collection of scagliole — stucco decorations considered the oldest in Le Marche. The Gothic chapel of Santa Marcellina (12th century) has held the saint's remains since 2 November 1666. The baptismal font is carved from an ancient Roman marble cippus. The 15th-century bell tower (25 m) dominates the village.

⏱ 60 min
3

Belvedere over the Foglia and Apsa valleys — at 319 metres on the ridge separating the two valleys, Montefabbri has remained virtually unchanged since the 15th century — 70 inhabitants, no new construction for decades, only careful restorations. The lanes radiate from the pieve like spokes, with tightly packed fired-brick houses pressing against the walls. From the village's high points, the view spans one side across the Foglia valley toward Pesaro, and the other across the Apsa valley toward Urbino.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Full walk through the village — Montefabbri can be explored in 30 to 45 minutes. Enter through the single gateway, walk the inner walls, visit the pieve and its scagliole, then step outside along the outer enclosure to see the Madonna lattante. The village, with its 70 residents, flowering balconies and deep quiet, is one of the most intimate stops along the entire itinerary.

⏱ 60 min

A cena in famiglia — storytelling dinner (seasonal event) — the association 'Un paese cento storie' opens the homes of Montefabbri residents to strangers for a shared dinner: the grandparents tell the history of the village over a meal. Seasonal initiative — contact the Comune di Vallefoglia for details.

Macerata Feltria

★★★☆☆
1

The Castello — early medieval nucleus — Macerata Feltria is built on the ruins of Pitinum Pisaurense, a Roman municipium founded in the 3rd century BC. 'Pitinum' from the Greek for 'pine' (the surrounding forests), 'Pisaurus' the old name for the Foglia river. Pliny listed it among the cities of the sixth Augustan region. Destroyed by the Goths of Vitige in 552 and rebuilt by the Lombards on the adjacent hill — the present Castello. The settlement is divided in two: the medieval Castello above (11th century) and the Borgo or Mercatale below, extended in the 16th century around the old market. The 14th-century Ponte Lucchini connects the two. Enter the Castello by this bridge, then the chiesa e convento di San Francesco (mid-14th century), then the lanes toward the Torre Civica.

⏱ 60 min
2

Torre Civica and Palazzo del Podestà — Museo Civico — the Torre Civica (11th century, bell chamber added in the 18th century) and the Palazzo del Podestà (13th–14th century) dominate the Piazza Abstemio at the heart of the Castello. The civic museum occupies both buildings: eight rooms tracing the history of Pitinum from prehistory through the Roman period, with inscriptions, coins, bronze statuettes and everyday objects from the 1990–1997 excavations. The palaeontological section in the tower holds fossils collected by Arnaldo and Gino Rinaldi in the Montefeltro valleys. Six medieval tombs (12th–13th century) faithfully reconstructed in the museum rooms.

⏱ 60 min
3

Pieve di San Cassiano in Pitino — excavations of Pitinum — an 11th-century Romanesque three-nave pieve built directly over the ruins of the Roman municipium. Several fragments of early medieval carved relief with braids and scrollwork are visible on the façade. Inside, a 15th-century marble statue of San Sebastiano and early medieval architectural fragments. Beneath the pieve, archaeologists from the University of Urbino uncovered a paved Roman road in an east–west orientation (possibly the decumanus maximus) and a medieval necropolis — sarcophagi visible in a consolidated cross-section. The most significant areas are protected under glass and lit. Open access, 2 km along the SP6 toward Carpegna.

⏱ 60 min
4

Arco dei Pelasgi — a stone arch at the Castello entrance, commemorating the Pelasgian people who, according to local tradition, founded ancient Pitinum before the Romans. A symbolic marker of a historical depth stretching back, by legend, to a pre-Roman civilisation.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Visit the Museo Civico then the Pitinum excavations — start with the eight rooms in the Palazzo del Podestà, then climb the Torre Civica for the palaeontological section. Drive the 2 km along the SP6 to the Pieve di San Cassiano to see the excavations and the Roman road in situ. Allow half a day for the full experience.

⏱ 60 min

Pitinum Thermae — a thermal spa established in 1993, drawing on the natural sulphurous springs of Certalto and Apsa. Sulphurous-bicarbonate calcium-magnesium waters with anti-catarrhal and antioxidant properties. Inhalation treatments, mud baths, hydrotherapy pools. An ideal way to end the day after exploring the village.

Frontino

★★★★☆
1

Torre Civica covered in Virginia creeper — the symbol of Frontino: a 14th-century watchtower and campanile entirely covered in American Virginia creeper, turning from deep green in summer to blazing red in autumn. The smallest village in the province of Pesaro-Urbino (300 inhabitants), perched at 519 metres on a rocky spur above the Mutino torrent. The streets are paved with river stones from the Mutino — the same stones that built the houses. A Montefeltro border castle, always loyal against the Malatesta. In 1451, Malatesta soldiers scaled the walls at night during the winter truce. A woman leaving at dawn raised the alarm — the villagers, trapped inside, climbed onto the rooftops and hurled roof tiles and stones. Six soldiers were captured, five executed. Federico da Montefeltro exempted the village from taxes for ten years.

⏱ 60 min
2

Chiesa Parrocchiale dei Santi Pietro e Paolo — tucked under a small portico along the main vicolo. It holds a 1610 canvas by Antonio Cimatori, known as il Visaccio, a pupil of Federico Barocci — the most important painter of Urbino after Raphael — depicting the Madonna col Bambino e i Santi Ubaldo e Francesco.

⏱ 60 min
3

Fontana by Franco Assetto and Museum — Franco Assetto (1911–1993), a Turinese artist who lived in the United States, donated a large part of his work to the municipality. The monumental fountain of 1971 in the small western piazzetta is a 'scultura d'acqua' — a term Assetto himself coined. The museum displays works spanning Surrealism, ethnic art, matter-based Informel and Pop Art: mahogany and plastic wall sculptures, paper cut-out drawings, ceramics, jewellery.

⏱ 60 min
4

Convento di Montefiorentino — founded by Saint Francis in 1213, one of the largest convents in Le Marche, set among woodlands a few kilometres from the village. The cappella dei conti Oliva and the altarpiece by Giovanni Santi (Raphael's father) are centrepieces of Montefeltro Renaissance art. The great polyptych by Alvise Vivarini that once belonged to this church is now held at the Galleria Nazionale d'Arte di Urbino. Since 1981, the Premio Nazionale di Cultura Frontino-Montefeltro, founded by literary critic Carlo Bo, is held here each year (third Sunday of September).

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Walk through the village and discover the spaventapasseri — Frontino is known for its scarecrows (spaventapasseri) placed throughout every alley, each representing local characters and traditions. In July–August, the Festival Internazionale degli Spaventapasseri. The village takes about 30 minutes to explore — slowly, for the mountain air and views toward Monte Carpegna.

⏱ 60 min

Hiking in the Parco del Sasso Simone e Simoncello — Frontino sits at the heart of this nature park. Several marked trails lead to the two Sassi — massive rock formations rising dramatically from the hill landscape. Routes for all levels, with panoramic views across the Montefeltro.

⏱ 60 min

Sagra del Tartufo Nero (first weekend of August) — the summer black truffle of the Montefeltro is the area's signature product. The first August weekend festival is the village's main annual celebration, with tastings and local producer stands.

⏱ 60 min

Mercatello Sul Metauro

★★★☆☆
1

Piazza Garibaldi and the medieval heart — Mercatello sul Metauro was born in 1235 around the ancient pieve di San Pietro d'Ico — later the Collegiata dei Santi Pietro e Paolo — as a market village in the Metauro valley. 'Little market' is the literal meaning of its name. In the 13th century it became the common home of seven surrounding castles, transformed into a fortified commune. The generously proportioned Piazza Garibaldi, large for a village of this size, testifies to that past importance. It is lined by the Palazzo Gasparini (17th century, Tuscan style), the Palazzo Comunale — which preserves the Pergamena Stefani recording the founding on 21 March 1235 — and the Pieve Collegiata. The Monte di Pietà of 1516 retains its original period interior fittings.

⏱ 60 min
2

Pieve Collegiata dei Santi Pietro e Paolo — a Romanesque structure with origins in the 10th century, rebuilt in Gothic style in 1363 — both phases are legible on the outer walls. Façade from 1927. In the large nave consecrated in 1730, the Maria Immacolata painted in 1556 by Raffaellino del Colle, one of Raphael's last pupils. The centrepiece: the Madonna delle Grazie, a Byzantine icon from the mid-13th century (141 × 50 cm), preserved in a 17th-century carved and gilded wooden reliquary.

⏱ 60 min
3

Chiesa di San Francesco and Museo d'Arte Sacra — a gem of 13th–14th-century primitive Romanesque-Gothic architecture. In the choir hangs the Crocifisso of 1309 by Giovanni da Rimini, a pupil of Giotto — one of the ten most important Gothic crucifixes in Italy, where Giotto's lesson in human truth meets the decorative tradition of Ravenna. The museum also holds a Polyptych from the Rimini school, frescoes by Umbrian-Marchigian artists, panels by Pandolfi and Guerrieri, and a 13th-century Madonna col Bambino by Bonaventura di Michele.

⏱ 60 min
4

Via del Metauro, Porta Metauro and the 'porte del morto' — Via del Metauro is the village's medieval street, reached through Porta Metauro — the only one of the original four gates still standing. All along this 13th–14th-century street, look for the porte del morto: doorways walled up beside the main entrance, reopened only long enough to carry out a corpse, then bricked up again. A central Italian medieval tradition intended to prevent the soul of the dead from returning through the habitual door.

⏱ 60 min
5

Three-arch Roman bridge over the Metauro — from Porta Metauro, the Roman bridge appears immediately: three massive arches spanning the Metauro, the longest river in Le Marche. Nearby, the convent of the Clarissan Capuchins stands on the birthplace of Santa Veronica Giuliani (1660–1727), a mystic canonised in 1839.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Walk down to the Metauro and the Cascata del Sasso — cross Porta Metauro, walk over the Roman bridge and follow the Metauro riverbank downstream. A few kilometres toward Sant'Angelo in Vado, the Cascata del Sasso (also known as Balza del Metauro) is signposted from the main road: 12 metres high, 60 metres wide, considered one of the most beautiful waterfalls in Italy. Two viewpoints accessible on foot — one elevated for a bird's-eye view, one at the base for a head-on perspective.

⏱ 60 min

Visit the Museo d'Arte Sacra di San Francesco — an hour inside the church-museum to see Giovanni da Rimini's Crocifisso in its original architectural setting, then explore the full sacred art collection spanning the 13th to 17th centuries.

⏱ 60 min
