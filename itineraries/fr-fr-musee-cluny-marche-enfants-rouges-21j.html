Musee De Cluny

Where medieval tapestries whisper secrets in the shadow of Roman baths

★★★★☆
1

Start with the Musée de Cluny, nestled in the heart of the Latin Quarter within a Gothic mansion built in 1510 by Burgundian monks from Cluny Abbey—on the ruins of Gallo-Roman baths whose remnants you'll still see on the façade. The highlight of your visit: the six Lady and the Unicorn tapestries, woven around 1500 and rediscovered by chance by Prosper Mérimée and George Sand in 1841 in a château in Creuse. Five tapestries illustrate the five senses; the sixth, titled "À mon seul désir" (To my only desire), remains the most debated enigma in French medieval art. Allow 90 minutes for a leisurely visit—avoid Wednesday mornings, which are packed with school groups.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

After Cluny, wander through the narrow streets of the Latin Quarter around Place de la Sorbonne—the museum sits just steps from the main entrance of the university founded in 1257. The bouquinistes (used-book sellers) along Boulevard Saint-Michel and the nearby Fontaine Saint-Michel are worth a detour before heading to your next museum.

Musee D Orsay

A gilded train station reborn as a temple to the artists who painted light itself

★★★★★
1

The Musée d'Orsay has occupied the former train station built by Victor Laloux between 1898 and 1900 for the Universal Exposition since 1986—the painter Édouard Detaille said as early as 1900: "The station is superb and looks like a fine arts palace." It took 86 years for the prophecy to come true. Here you'll find the world's most important collection of Impressionist and Post-Impressionist paintings: more than 1,100 canvases, including 72 Monets, 64 Renoirs, plus Degas, Van Gogh, Cézanne, and Gauguin. You absolutely must climb to the 5th level for the Impressionist Gallery, which runs along the Seine quays—the light streaming through the great gilded clocks is incomparable. Book your ticket online: lines at the ticket office can exceed 45 minutes.

⏱ 150 min

Activities

Leaving Orsay, cross the Pont Royal to reach the Tuileries—you can grab coffee on a terrace facing the garden before continuing. If you have the energy, the Musée de l'Orangerie is a 10-minute walk from the Left Bank: the two can be combined in the same half-day.

Musee Du Louvre

Eight centuries of palace walls now shelter humanity's most treasured masterpieces

★★★★☆
1

The Louvre is the world's largest art museum: 72,735 m² of rooms and galleries, 35,000 works on display out of 460,000 in total—spanning from prehistory to 1848, from Europe to Iran via Egypt. The building itself is a 12th-century fortress transformed into a royal palace over 800 years before opening as a museum on August 10, 1793, with 537 paintings. I.M. Pei's glass pyramid, inaugurated in 1989, sparked as much scandal as the Eiffel Tower did in its day. Come with a strategy: in 2 hours, you'll see the Mona Lisa, Venus de Milo, and Winged Victory of Samothrace. In a full day, you'll traverse the Egyptian antiquities and the painting department. Arrive at opening time (9 a.m.) for the Mona Lisa—an average of 15,000 people visit the museum each day.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

After the Louvre, stroll through the Jardin des Tuileries, which Napoleon III had landscape architect Édouard André design—the garden connects the museum to Place de la Concorde over 1 km of perspective. Modern sculptures scattered throughout the pathways serve as an open-air museum.

Musee De L Orangerie

Step into Monet's infinite garden beneath the luminous Parisian sky

★★★★☆
1

The Orangerie is one of Paris's smallest museums—and one of its most mesmerizing. On the ground floor, two oval rooms built according to Monet's own plans house the eight Water Lilies panels, given to France in the aftermath of the November 11, 1918 armistice. Each panel measures 1.97 m tall; the total length of the canvases installed here reaches 91 meters. Natural zenithal light, filtered through a fabric veil according to the painter's instructions, makes the colors vibrate with the rhythm of the sky. André Masson called this ensemble the "Sistine Chapel of Impressionism." Come on an overcast day if possible—diffuse light works better than direct sun. In the basement, the Walter-Guillaume collection brings together 144 paintings by Renoir, Cézanne, Matisse, Picasso, and Modigliani.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Leaving the museum, cross the Jardin des Tuileries to Place de la Concorde—the 23-meter Luxor Obelisk, gifted by Egypt in 1830, marks the geometric center of the Parisian historical axis. From there, the Champs-Élysées climbs toward the Arc de Triomphe over 1.9 km.

Petit Palais

Belle Époque splendor meets five millennia of art, all without spending a euro

★★★★☆
1

The Petit Palais, built by Charles Girault for the 1900 Universal Exposition, houses the City of Paris's Museum of Fine Arts—and permanent admission is free. The architecture itself is worth the visit: a trapezoidal plan around an interior garden with colonnades, a central rotunda crowned by a dome, mosaics and Belle Époque murals. The collections span 5,000 years of history: Greek and Egyptian antiquities, medieval enamels, paintings by Courbet, Monet, and Cézanne, sculptures by Carpeaux. Allow 90 minutes at your own pace—the rooms are rarely crowded.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Petit Palais faces the Grand Palais—the two buildings connected by Avenue Winston Churchill form a cohesive architectural ensemble from the 1900 era. Stroll between the two to observe their façades: the allegorical sculptures, glass canopies, and friezes from the period testify to the decorative ambition of the Third Republic.
