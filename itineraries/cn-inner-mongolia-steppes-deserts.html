June, July, August, September

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 4 and 8 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Spring (April-May): Pleasant temperatures, blooming flowers, ideal weather, moderate crowds, excellent for most regions, comfortable travel.

Fall (September-October): Perfect weather, clear skies, comfortable temperatures, peak tourist season, higher prices, autumn colors beautiful.

Summer (June-August): Hot and humid, rainy season, crowded tourist sites, highest prices, but best for Tibet and western regions.

Winter (November-March): Cold temperatures, fewer crowds, lower prices, perfect for southern regions, harsh in north, unique winter experiences.

Regional Variations: Vast climate diversity, southern subtropical, northern continental, western plateau, plan according to regions visited.

🌍 What to discover in CN?

Ancient civilization meets modern superpower with Great Wall, forbidden cities, and cutting-edge technology. Experience diverse landscapes, incredible cuisine, and 5000-year cultural heritage. The country offers bustling megacities, pristine nature, and traditional arts. China provides overwhelming cultural richness and geographical diversity in rapidly modernizing nation.

📋 Important notes

The Badain Jaran Desert expedition must be done in a 4x4 convoy with a licensed local guide—any attempt to go independently is prohibited. The steppes are green only from June to September; in winter, the landscape turns gray-brown and temperatures drop to -30°C. The Inner Mongolia Naadam Festival is generally held in late July with no fixed date (unlike independent Mongolia's).

Stop 1

Hohhot

Where ancient lamaist temples rise from the capital of Inner Mongolia

📍 Dazhao Temple Hohhot📍 Wuta Si Hohhot📍 Inner Mongolia Museum Hohhot📍 Saishang Old Street Hohhot
1

You arrive in Hohhot, capital of Inner Mongolia, by high-speed train from Beijing (about 2.5 hours). Head first to Dazhao Temple, founded in 1579 by Tümed Mongol leader Altan Khan—it's the oldest and largest Tibetan Buddhist monastery in the city, and its original construction. Inside, the 2.55-meter silver Buddha consecrated by the 3rd Dalai Lama in 1586 has remained intact for over four centuries. The complex covers 30,000 square meters. Enter from the quieter pedestrian street on the south side.

⏱ 90 min
2

Five minutes on foot from Dazhao stands Xilitu Zhao Temple, built during the Longqing and Wanli reigns. More discreet, more alive—monks still chant here every morning. Push through the gate before 9 AM when the air is still cool and the courtyards empty. The murals in the main hall are superb, minimally restored, with their original pigments still vibrant.

⏱ 45 min
3

Built between 1727 and 1732 under Emperor Yongzheng, the Five Pagoda Temple (Wuta Si) is what remains of the grand Cideng Si complex, now vanished. The main structure, 16 meters tall, supports five stone pagodas covered with 1,563 small Buddha figures. Walk behind the pagoda: on the north wall you'll find the only known astronomical map carved in Mongolian script, cataloguing over 1,550 stars. Diameter 144.5 cm, unique in the world.

⏱ 60 min
4

The Inner Mongolia Museum houses over 100,000 pieces, including the famous dinosaur skeletons from the region—one of the most impressive Mamenchisaurus specimens on display in China. A good grounding before heading out to the steppe. Allow two hours; closed Mondays.

⏱ 120 min
5

Seven kilometers south of the center, the Tomb of Wang Zhaojun. Legend tells that this Han imperial concubine was sent in diplomatic marriage to the Xiongnu khan in 33 BC, sealing peace between the two peoples. The mound is immense, covered in spring green grass—hence its nickname, the Green Tomb. More than the tomb itself, it's the park and statues that warrant the visit.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

For dinner, head to Saishang Old Street or the Islamic quarter near the Great Mosque. Try the mutton shaomai (Hohhot's specialty), salty milk tea (suutei tsai), and nai pi flatbread. Locals eat fast, standing, in noisy canteens.

⏱ 90 min

Evening stroll through the Hui quarter around Hohhot's Great Mosque, built in the late 18th century in a Ming-style Sino-Islamic architecture unique in China. The minaret-pavilion crowned with a Chinese roof is an architectural curiosity. The neighborhood comes alive after dark with dried fruit merchants and Uyghur bread bakers.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 2

Xilamuren

Grassland horizons beneath some of China's purest starlit skies

📍 Xilamuren Grassland📍 Xilamuren Temple📍 Mongolian yurt camp Xilamuren
1

You leave Hohhot for the Xilamuren Grassland, about 90 km north. The road climbs gently onto the Mongolian plateau, and the landscape shifts abruptly: trees vanish, replaced by immense rolling grasses rippling in the wind. Stop at Xilamuren Temple (Puhui Si), founded in 1769 under Emperor Qianlong. A small monastery still active—the monks' chants carry far across the empty steppe.

⏱ 45 min
2

Check into a traditional yurt (ger) camp. The yurt is more than accommodation—it's the quintessential nomadic invention, assembled in two hours, transportable by camel. Ask your host to explain the interior arrangement: altar to the west, hearth at center, always facing south. Comfort is spartan, but the experience counts.

⏱ 60 min
3

Visit an ovoo, these stone cairns topped with blue prayer flags. They're the shamanic altars of the steppe, dedicated to mountain and sky spirits. Circle three times clockwise, add a stone—it's the custom. Many herders still stop here before long journeys.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

An afternoon devoted to the three manly sports of the Mongols (eriin gurvan naadam): archery, horse racing, and wrestling (bökh). Demonstrations at tourist camps are choreographed but authentic. If you ride, ask for a Mongolian pony—small, stocky, tireless, the same breed Genghis Khan rode 800 years ago.

⏱ 180 min

In the evening, a whole roasted lamb feast (khorkhog or Mongolian barbecue) around the fire, accompanied by Mongolian vodka and airag (fermented mare's milk, tangy and fizzy). Then stargazing: Xilamuren has one of the purest skies in eastern China, with no light pollution for 100 km in any direction. The Milky Way is visible to the naked eye.

⏱ 180 min
Stop 3

Xilamuren

Grassland horizons beneath some of China's purest starlit skies

📍 Xilamuren Grassland horse riding📍 Mongolian yurt camp Xilamuren
1

Second day on the steppe: wake at 5 AM for sunrise over the grassy hills. Raking light transforms the grass to pale gold, sheep flocks emerge to graze. Real herders (not camp staff) start their day at this hour; ask your host to take you to a neighboring family encampment.

⏱ 90 min
2

Meet a Mongolian herding family in their permanent yurt. They'll serve you salty milk tea, dried cheese (aaruul, hard as stone), and fresh butter. Conversation flows through your guide-translator. Ask about transhumance, winter life, children who've gone to school in the city. This is the most precious part of this stop.

⏱ 90 min
3

If your visit coincides with late July, the Inner Mongolia Naadam Festival is held here (dates vary, unlike the fixed calendar of independent Mongolia). Otherwise, amateur competitions are organized all summer at the big camps. Gripping spectacle: hundreds of riders, sumo-style wrestlers in leather costumes, mounted archers.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

A 2-3 hour horseback ride across the steppe with a local guide. Ask for a gallop on the real grasslands (the turf is soft, the horses are trained). It's physical—grip with your knees, not the reins; Mongolian horses don't steer like ours.

⏱ 180 min

Quick lesson in traditional Mongolian archery with a composite recurve bow and reed arrows. Genghis Khan's ancient warriors shot 200 meters at full gallop. You'll aim at a fixed target at 30 meters, which is already respectable. A local instructor corrects finger and shoulder position.

⏱ 90 min

Stop 4

Baotou

Steel city on the Yellow River's great northern bend through Ordos

📍 Baotou city center📍 Wudangzhao Monastery Baotou
1

Transitional stop in Baotou, a steel city on the northern bend of the Yellow River. Pause at Wudangzhao Monastery, 70 km northeast of the city. It's the largest Gelug branch Tibetan Buddhist monastery in Inner Mongolia, founded in 1749. Six grand halls, over 2,500 statues, still active. More authentic than Hohhot's temples, far less crowded.

⏱ 90 min
2

Quick stop on the banks of the Yellow River to see the water's color: ochre, laden with silt from the Loess Plateau. The river makes its great northward bend here, forming the "Ordos Loop" that gives the Kubuqi Desert its distinctive shape (named "bowstring" in Mongolian).

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 75 km · 1h20
Stop 5

Ordos

Where Genghis Khan's spirit guards the ghost towers of Kangbashi

📍 Mausoleum of Genghis Khan Ordos📍 Kangbashi District Ordos📍 Ordos Museum
1

You arrive at the Mausoleum of Genghis Khan, about 60 km southeast of Ordos in Ejin Horo Banner. Built between 1954 and 1956 by the Chinese government in traditional Mongolian style, the complex covers 5.5 hectares and comprises three grand yurt-shaped halls with yellow roofs. It's a cenotaph: the true tomb of Genghis Khan, who died in August 1227, has never been found. According to his wishes, he was buried without markers, somewhere in the Khentii Mountains.

⏱ 90 min
2

In the main hall, a 5-meter white marble statue of Genghis Khan stands beside a map of the largest contiguous empire ever assembled. The rear hall houses three yurts: at center, the Khan's silver coffin (empty, containing only caps and accessories), flanked by those of his wives and brothers. The guardians, the Darkhads, are descendants of the Khan's personal guard, passing the role from father to son for 800 years.

⏱ 60 min
3

Around the complex, the Donglian Scenic Area features the "Long Scroll of Mongolian History"—the world's longest painted scroll, tracing Mongolian history from Genghis Khan's birth to the fall of the Yuan Dynasty. Performance of "an Ordos wedding" and wrestling demonstrations. Touristy, but well executed.

⏱ 90 min
4

Thirty kilometers away, the ghost city of Kangbashi, Ordos's new district. Built in the early 2000s for one million residents, it now holds only 100,000—dubbed "China's most famous ghost town." Deserted avenues, empty tower blocks, the copper-colored meteorite-shaped Ordos Museum, a library like a giant book. A fascinating dystopian urban walk.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

If you arrive around 9 AM at the mausoleum, you'll witness the daily shamanic ceremony led by the Darkhads: milk libations, hada scarves, offerings at the cenotaph. Inscribed on China's intangible heritage list since 2006, the ceremony is purely religious, not a tourist show—stay back, don't photograph during chants.

⏱ 60 min

Traditional Mongolian meal in Kangbashi: boiled mutton carved with a knife, beef baozi, buttered milk tea. Restaurants around Genghis Khan Square are most popular with locals. Try the "Khan's vodka" if offered—short but formidable.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 90 km · 1h40
Stop 6

Xiangshawan

China's first desert resort where singing sands meet the Kubuqi dunes

📍 Xiangshawan Resort Kubuqi📍 Resonant Sand Bay📍 Lotus Hotel Kubuqi
1

Head to Xiangshawan, the "Resonant Sand Bay," at the eastern edge of the Kubuqi Desert. Kubuqi is China's 7th largest desert, covering 18,600 square kilometers. Its name means "bowstring" in Mongolian—the Yellow River draws the bow, the sand ribbon forms the string. You arrive at the reception station; a 1-km cable car drops you into the heart of the dunes.

⏱ 60 min
2

The crescent dunes reach 90 meters tall, with a 45-degree slope. It's the "singing sand" phenomenon that gave the place its name: in dry weather, slide down from a dune crest and you'll hear a deep rumble, compared variously to a jet engine or a bronze bell. The sound can reach 105 decibels and last over a minute.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Lotus Hotel, designed as a giant lotus flower resting on the dunes, is an architectural feat: no conventional foundation, but steel panels that float the 30,000-square-meter building on sand. Awarded the Iconic and A'Design Silver prizes, listed by CNN among China's most beautiful hotels. Even if you don't stay overnight, see the illuminated lobby at dusk.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Sandboarding descent from the summit of a 100-meter dune. Thrills guaranteed—the sand is fine as powdered sugar. Several packages in the park; choose the morning session (9-11 AM), before the sand burns bare feet. Allow 45 minutes to climb, 30 seconds to descend.

⏱ 60 min

Camel caravan through the dunes to reach Yuesha Island or Xiansha Island. The ride lasts 30-45 minutes, the camel's gait slow and rocking, viewing the desert from above. The camel drivers, Mongolian, speak little but smile often. For thrill-seekers, there are also buggies, archery, and a zipline over the dunes.

⏱ 60 min

In the evening, bonfire party with traditional Mongolian dances, an Ordos wedding reenactment, and fire spinning. Desert nights are cool even in summer—bring a fleece. If the sky is clear, climb a dune at 10 PM to watch the Milky Way; Kubuqi is far enough from urban light.

⏱ 120 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 360 km · 4h30
Stop 7

Yinchuan

Ancient Tangut capital crowned by UNESCO pyramid tombs

📍 Western Xia Tombs Yinchuan📍 Najiahu Mosque Yongning📍 Yinchuan Drum Tower
1

You cross into Ningxia and arrive in Yinchuan, former capital of the Tangut kingdom. Head first to the Western Xia Tombs, 30 km west at the foot of the Helan Mountains. Inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage list in July 2025, they're nicknamed the "Eastern Pyramids": nine imperial tombs and 271 subsidiary graves cover 50 square kilometers. The Tangut kingdom (1038-1227) was razed by Genghis Khan in 1227, its writing forgotten for eight centuries.

⏱ 120 min
2

Don't miss Tomb No. 3, attributed to founding emperor Li Yuanhao (1003-1048). The rammed-earth mound, 23 meters high, was originally a seven-story octagonal pagoda. Gobi winds stripped it bare over centuries, leaving only a brown earth cone. The modern museum at the entrance displays nearly 5,000 artifacts and attempts to reconstruct the Tangut language, of which 6,000 characters were created modeled on Chinese.

⏱ 90 min
3

South of Yinchuan, 25 km out, Najiahu Mosque (in Yongning) is one of the oldest Ming-style mosques in China, reputedly founded in the 15th century. Sino-Islamic architecture: pavilions with curved roofs, green and white colors, no dome or minaret. Still a very active Hui community.

⏱ 60 min
4

Stroll through Yinchuan's historic center: the Drum Tower and Bell Tower date from early Ming. The night market around them comes alive at 6 PM, with stalls of dried fruits, Yunnan mangoes, cumin lamb skewers. Yinchuan is also China's wine capital, the country's premier wine-growing region—try a local Cabernet.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Wine tasting from the Helan Shan vineyards at one of the many châteaux dotting the foothills. The region enjoys a semi-arid climate, loess soils, and 3,000 hours of sunshine per year—ideal conditions for Cabernet and Marselan. A guide takes you on a half-day tour of 2-3 estates.

⏱ 240 min

Halal dinner in the Hui quarter: lamb with hand-pulled noodles (lamian), Yellow River fish in vinegar, beef and cumin baozi. Hui-mei cuisine is dry and robust, heavily garlicked. Finish with "eight treasure tea": tea leaves, longans, jujubes, rock sugar crystals.

⏱ 90 min

Stop 8

Yinchuan

Ancient Tangut capital crowned by UNESCO pyramid tombs

📍 Helan Mountain Rock Art📍 Sand Lake Yinchuan📍 Ningxia Museum
1

Head north of Yinchuan into the Helan Mountains to see the Helankou petroglyphs. Over 6,000 rock carvings dating from 3,000 to 10,000 years ago, chiseled by successive nomadic peoples: stylized human faces, hunting scenes, animals. The most famous depict silhouettes of tigers and cervids. The canyon is dry, wild, the ground covered with black shards.

⏱ 120 min
2

Fifty-six kilometers northwest of Yinchuan, Sand Lake (Sha Hu)—a surprising combination of a 22-square-kilometer freshwater lake at the foot of the Tengger Desert. Reed beds shelter over 60 species of migratory birds (swans, herons, pelicans). A boat ride, then climb the neighboring dunes. Rare contrast of water and sand.

⏱ 120 min
3

The Ningxia Museum in Yinchuan complements the visit to the Xixia tombs. Halls dedicated to the Tanguts, Hui culture, calligraphy, and the region's prehistory. Well designed, free, air-conditioned—a welcome refuge in midsummer when it's 38°C outside.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Ningxia cooking class with a Hui family: learn to hand-pull noodles (the gesture, the rhythm), prepare cumin lamb stew, and shape baozi. Tasting and discussion about Ramadan, holidays on the Chinese Islamic calendar. About half a day.

⏱ 180 min

In the evening, climb to a rooftop bar terrace to see Yinchuan light up. The modern city is surprisingly polished, with its canals and parks. Cocktails made with local wines, fusion cuisine. Rest before the long desert stages ahead.

⏱ 90 min
Lavande Hotel Yinchuan Wanda International Convention and Exhibition Center10

Stop 9

Zhongwei

Ningxia oasis where the Tengger Desert plunges into the Yellow River

📍 Shapotou Tourist Area📍 Zhongwei Gao Temple📍 Tengger Desert Zhongwei
1

Head to Zhongwei, 220 km southwest of Yinchuan, to reach Shapotou—Ningxia's most spectacular site. The area, 17 km west of town, is the only place where the Tengger Desert plunges directly into the Yellow River. The dividing line is visible from the dunes: yellow sand on one side, ochre water and green oasis on the other.

⏱ 60 min
2

Before the experience, climb Shapotou's great sand slide—1,000 meters long, 100 meters high, slope at 60 degrees. It's China's largest natural slide, and one of the country's four "singing sand" fields. When you descend, the sand produces a deep rumble, likened to distant bells. An unexplained phenomenon that has fascinated travelers for centuries.

⏱ 60 min
3

Gao Temple, right in downtown Zhongwei, is a curiosity: one of the rare buildings in the world where three religions coexist—Buddhism, Taoism, and Confucianism. Built during the Ming era, restored under the Qing, the architecture blends the codes of all three traditions. Small but remarkable, see it before or after Shapotou.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Drift down the Yellow River on a sheepskin raft (yangpi fazi). The technique is 2,000 years old: assemble around twenty inflated sheepskins on a bamboo frame. Capacity of 1 ton, 3-4 passengers. Inscribed on Ningxia's intangible heritage list since 2009. The raft drifts 30-40 minutes with the current, the rower sings traditional songs.

⏱ 60 min

In the northern part of Shapotou, camel ride into the Tengger Desert. The caravan follows the ancient Silk Road route, which passed precisely here to reach the Hexi Corridor. Allow 1.5 hours for a loop. Bring a face scarf—sand flies when the wind picks up.

⏱ 90 min

At sunset, climb the main dune to watch the sky turn pink then mauve over the Yellow River. One of the most beautiful views in northwest China. Few people after 6 PM, the tour groups having already left. Bring a thermos of tea and a blanket.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 10

Alxa Left Banner

Gateway to the mega-dunes of Badain Jaran wilderness

📍 Bayanhot Alxa Left Banner📍 Alxa Museum
1

On the road between Zhongwei and Bayanhot, stop at Moon Lake (Yueliang Hu) in the heart of the Tengger Desert. Covering 3 square kilometers, this freshwater lake has a peculiarity: its shape resembles China seen from above, and it's divided in two by reed beds—half freshwater, half saltwater. Rated 5A site, ideal for breaking up a long 5.5-hour desert drive. Allow one hour on site.

⏱ 60 min
2

Transitional stop in Bayanhot (seat of Alxa Left Banner), the mandatory departure point for reaching Badain Jaran. Here you'll meet your 4x4 convoy and local guide—the desert expedition absolutely cannot be done independently. Take the opportunity to visit the Alxa Museum, which presents local Mongolian culture and the geology of the region's three great deserts.

⏱ 90 min
3

Last "civilized" night before the great desert. Bayanhot is a quiet little Mongolian town with a lamaist temple (Yanfu Monastery) and a few shopping streets where you can find necessary equipment. Buy water, high-protection sunscreen, a scarf, a headlamp.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Safety briefing with your guide for the Badain Jaran expedition: 3 days, 4 possible nights in yurts near interior lakes, no cell service, sand at 40°C by day and 5°C at night. Pack your bags: everything non-essential stays in Bayanhot.

⏱ 90 min

Stop 11

Badain Jaran

UNESCO desert of sky-high dunes cradling 144 jewel-colored lakes

📍 Badain Jaran Lake📍 Badain Jaran Temple📍 Bilutu Peak
1

The 4x4 convoy leaves Bayanhot early in the morning to plunge into the Badain Jaran Desert. Allow 5-6 hours of track to reach Badain Hu Lake, the heart of the desert. Inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage list in July 2024, Badain Jaran is China's 3rd largest desert (49,000 sq km) and home to the world's tallest stationary dunes. Bilutu Peak tops 1,611 meters in altitude, with a relative height of 460 meters—the "Everest of sands."

⏱ 120 min
2

On arrival, the contrast is striking: 144 lakes in incredible colors—turquoise, emerald, some pink or red—nestle between the mega-dunes. The name "Badain Jaran" means "mysterious lakes" in Mongolian. Many are saline (up to 330 g/L of salt), a few are freshwater. They're fed by underground springs from distant mountains.

⏱ 60 min
3

Visit the Badain Jaran Temple, built in 1755 under the Qing Dynasty on the shore of a lake. Nicknamed the "Forbidden City of the Desert," it's a Tibetan Buddhist monastery miraculously preserved: its total isolation saved it from the Cultural Revolution. Inside, original murals, prayer wheels, and a few monks who still maintain daily rituals. 273 square meters, two stories.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Check into the yurt camp by the lake, run by a Mongolian family. Sunset over the dunes—the light shifts in ten minutes from bright yellow to deep red to mauve. Silence is total, save for wind sculpting the sand. Simple Mongolian meal: boiled mutton, fermented milk, fire-baked bread.

⏱ 180 min

Stargazing from a neighboring dune. No light pollution for hundreds of kilometers. The Milky Way, constellations, sometimes Starlink satellites in single file. Lie down in the still-warm sand, stay a good hour for your eyes to adapt.

⏱ 120 min
Stop 12

Badain Jaran

UNESCO desert of sky-high dunes cradling 144 jewel-colored lakes

📍 Bilutu Peak Badain Jaran📍 Yinderitu Spring📍 Nuoritu Lake
1

Today, ascent of Bilutu Peak, the world's tallest stationary dune. The 4x4 drops you at the base; you climb on foot—2 hours of effort in soft sand, three steps forward for two steps back. At the summit, 360-degree panorama over the ocean of dunes and seven interior lakes. The crest is sharp as a razor blade.

⏱ 240 min
2

Discover the magical Yinderitu Spring, a tiny salt lake at whose heart gushes a 3-square-meter reef pierced by 108 freshwater springs. Unexplained hydrological mystery: the water is sweet, rich in trace elements. Mongolian princes of Alxa used to have it transported by camel to their palace. Even today, two Germans come to drink it every year.

⏱ 60 min
3

Nuoritu Lake, the largest and deepest in the desert (1.47 sq km, 28 meters deep). Reeds shelter surprising fauna: migrating swans, wild ducks, geese, mollusks, and even fish in moderately saline water. Picnic lunch in the shade of rare trees growing at the water's edge.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Slide on the singing sands of Baoritaolegai, nicknamed the "kingdom of singing dunes." When you rush down the slope, the sand produces a thunderous roar exceeding 105 decibels—the most powerful in the world. Phenomenon linked to electrostatic effects between grains, still poorly understood.

⏱ 60 min

Evening around the fire with Mongolian herders who tend the desert's camel flocks. Stories told by the guide, Mongolian vodka, camel cheese. Night falls at 9 PM in summer, followed by dry cold that quickly drops to 5°C. Slip into the yurt early.

⏱ 180 min
Stop 13

Badain Jaran

UNESCO desert of sky-high dunes cradling 144 jewel-colored lakes

📍 Badain Jaran 4x4 expedition📍 desert oasis Alxa
1

Final day in the desert. The 4x4 convoy takes you across the mega-dunes westward, toward the desert exit. Driving is extreme: 40-degree slopes, controlled sliding descents, stops to dig out vehicles stuck in sand. Hold your belongings tight—the 4x4 jolts hard.

⏱ 240 min
2

Discover the least-visited landscapes: wind-sculpted stone forests, hidden oases with desert poplars (huyang) that can live 1,000 years, sometimes exposed fossils—Badain Jaran was once a seabed 100 million years ago. Your guide easily spots tracks of gazelles and foxes.

⏱ 90 min
3

Exit the desert and arrive in Alxa Right Banner (Ehji-Ohlo), a small town marking the end of the Badain Jaran expedition. Allow about 6 hours of 4x4 from the desert heart. This is the time to thank your guide and the team—tips are expected, around 200 yuan per day per person for the lead guide.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Hot shower (finally!) at the Alxa Right Banner hotel, a luxury forgotten for three days. Hearty Chinese meal: vegetable noodles, dumplings, cold beer. Rest up—tomorrow is a big transit day to Jiayuguan.

⏱ 90 min

Short walk around town to see the main square, the local lamaist temple, and the bazaar. If you encounter Mongolian herders in deel (traditional coat), greet them with "sain bain uu"—hello in Mongolian. A smile does the rest.

⏱ 60 min

Stop 14

Jiayuguan

Ming fortress at the western gate where the Great Wall meets the Silk Road

★★★☆☆
📍 Jiayuguan Fort📍 Overhanging Great Wall Jiayuguan📍 First Beacon Tower Great Wall
1

Before leaving Alxa territory, make a 14-km detour southwest of Mandela town for the Mandela Mountain petroglyphs. Over 18 square kilometers, more than 6,000 rock carvings have been chiseled into basalt rocks by successive nomadic peoples over 5,000 years: hunting scenes, shamans, herds, war chariots. One of the world's largest rock art ensembles, and a perfect stop to break the 5-hour drive to Jiayuguan.

⏱ 90 min
2

You leave Inner Mongolia to enter Gansu and arrive in Jiayuguan, the western terminus of the Ming Great Wall. Jiayuguan Fortress, built in 1372, marks the western limit of historical Chinese empire: past this gate lay the "barbarian west," meaning the Silk Road. Three concentric enclosures, two gate-towers, 11-meter-high walls. The fortress is intact—one of the best-preserved along the entire Wall.

⏱ 180 min
3

Nine kilometers north, the "Overhanging Great Wall" (Xuanbi Changcheng) climbs steeply up the flank of the Heishan Mountains. Built in 1539, it closes the northern approach to Jiayuguan Pass. Ascent of 400 steps, immense panorama over desert and snow-capped mountains. The contrast between white peaks and yellow sands is striking.

⏱ 90 min
4

To the south, the "First Beacon Tower of the Great Wall" (Diyi Dun) plunges steeply into the Taolai River canyon. It's the very first structure of the Wall in the west, erected in 1539—symbolically, the wall begins here; over 6,000 km later it ends at the Bohai Sea. Transparent glass platform above the precipice, for the brave.

⏱ 90 min
5

Your journey ends here—Jiayuguan is also the classic starting point of the Chinese Silk Road toward Dunhuang, Turpan, and Kashgar. Direct trains to Lanzhou, Xi'an, or Urumqi, or flights from the local airport. Before you leave, have tea on the ramparts—the sunset over the fortress is worth every memory.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Final meal in a Muslim restaurant in Jiayuguan's Hui quarter: hand-pulled beef noodles, cumin lamb, wood-fired skewers. Gansu cuisine marks the transition to Uyghur flavors of the Silk Road. Try the desert apples, crisp and sweet, a local specialty.

⏱ 90 min

If time permits, visit the Great Wall Museum in Jiayuguan, tracing 2,000 years of Chinese fortification history. Models, plans, archaeological objects. Well done, not too long. Air-conditioned, which helps when it's 35°C outside.
