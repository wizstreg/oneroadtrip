Eisenach

★★★★☆
1

Wartburg Castle rises from a rocky spur 411 meters above Eisenach, deep in the Thuringian Forest. Founded around 1067 by Count Ludwig the Springer, its name springs from legend: upon first seeing the hill, the count allegedly shouted «Warte, Berg - du sollst mir eine Burg tragen!» («Wait, mountain - you shall bear my castle!»). The Romanesque palace (Palas), built between 1156 and 1162, is the oldest preserved residential building of its kind in Germany. The complex was extensively restored in the 19th century under architect Hugo von Ritgen at the behest of Grand Duke Karl Alexander - a restoration whose imagery directly inspired Ludwig II of Bavaria for Neuschwanstein. The castle has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1999. In 1817, student associations gathered here to demand a German constitution: the «Wartburgfest» is considered one of the founding acts of the German liberal-national movement.

⏱ 90 min
2

In the Lutherstube, a small vaulted chamber in the Vogtei, Martin Luther lived from May 1521 to March 1522 under the alias Junker Jörg - «Knight George». Declared an outlaw after refusing to recant at the Diet of Worms, he had been «kidnapped» by fake brigands dispatched by Elector Frederick the Wise to protect him. In ten to eleven weeks, he translated the New Testament from ancient Greek into German - a version that did as much to standardize the German language as to spread the Reformation. Luther called Wartburg his «Patmos», referring to the Greek island where the apostle John wrote the Book of Revelation. The museum also preserves works by Lucas Cranach the Elder and objects related to Saint Elizabeth of Hungary, who lived here from 1211 to 1228.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Panoramic view over the Thuringian Forest from Wartburg's ramparts. On clear days, the vista extends to the Rennsteig - the ridgeline that once separated Saxony from Franconia, and during the Cold War, the two Germanys.

⏱ 30 min

Hainich

★★★☆☆
1

Hainich National Park is Germany's largest contiguous deciduous forest with its 13,000 hectares. Established on December 31, 1997, it is Thuringia's only national park. Its uniqueness stems from its military past: vast sectors were restricted zones during the Cold War, leaving them to develop freely for decades. The forest has thus returned to an almost primeval state, with old-growth trees, abundant deadwood, and more than 1,600 identified fungus species. The 1,573-hectare core zone has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2011 as part of the «Ancient and Primeval Beech Forests of Europe». A 9.5 km heritage trail traverses the core zone.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Baumkronenpfad - canopy walkway - starts at the park center in Thiemsburg. Over 540 meters, the path gradually rises to 24 meters, at the level of the beech crowns, then climbs to an observation platform at 44 meters: Germany's highest platform of its kind, with unobstructed views across all of Hainich and the Thuringian Basin. The route is fully wheelchair-accessible thanks to an elevator. Panels explain at each level the life of the canopy and species diversity.

⏱ 75 min
3

The national park center features an interactive exhibition on Hainich's limestone geology, forest fauna, and conservation challenges. A reconstructed «root cave» illustrates subterranean life. The center also provides information on the wildcat - an emblematic species visible at the dedicated village of Hütscheroda, a few kilometers away.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Hike the heritage trail, a 9.5 km circuit through the primeval beech forest. The path follows deadwood zones, skirts forest pools, and crosses clearings where the black woodpecker - Europe's largest woodpecker - excavates its holes in old trunks.

⏱ 180 min

Bad Langensalza

★★★☆☆
1

Bad Langensalza owes its medieval prosperity to woad trade - a blue dye it shared with only four other Thuringian cities - enabling burghers to build grand churches and travertine ramparts. Later, the exploitation of mineral springs made it a spa town. Today, the city is known for its ten themed parks and gardens aligned along the spa promenade. The Japanese garden «Kofuku no Niwa» («garden of happiness») combines two classical pavilions, a koi carp pond, a dry garden, a bamboo grove, and cherry trees that blossom in spring.

⏱ 60 min
2

Bad Langensalza's rose garden is one of Thuringia's most significant. It showcases collections of antique and modern roses organized in themed beds, complemented by a small rose museum tracing this flower's cultural history.

⏱ 60 min
3

The arboretum gathers rare or spectacular trees and shrubs, native and exotic species, planted in thematic collections. Adjacent, the botanical garden displays medicinal and officinal plants recalling the town's spa tradition.

⏱ 60 min
4

Bad Langensalza's old town is one of Thuringia's best-preserved: an almost entirely conserved medieval wall, half-timbered houses from the 15th and 16th centuries restored since 1990, and several grand churches testifying to the past wealth of the woad trade.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Relaxation at the Friederiken Therme, a thermal spa fed by Bad Langensalza's sulphurous and saline springs. The complex features indoor and outdoor pools and several themed saunas.

⏱ 120 min

Erfurt

★★★★☆
1

St. Mary's Cathedral dominates Domplatz from a raised terrace, flanked by the collegiate Church of St. Severus - one of Central Europe's most impressive sacred ensembles. The current cathedral was begun around 1280 with a vast Gothic choir. Its glory is the Gloriosa: cast in 1497, it's the world's largest free-swinging medieval bell. It's rung only on grand occasions to protect the structure. The cathedral also houses the «Wolfram», a 12th-century bronze candelabra, and Gothic stained glass in the choir.

⏱ 75 min
2

Fischmarkt is Erfurt's central square, surrounded by Renaissance merchants' houses, some dating from the 16th century. The neo-Gothic town hall, built 1870-1874, overlooks the square with its murals of local legends. Erfurt miraculously survived World War II bombing, explaining the density of its historic fabric - the city is often called «the preserved medieval metropolis».

⏱ 45 min
3

The Krämerbrücke - «merchants' bridge» - is Central Europe's longest inhabited bridge: 120 meters long, with 32 half-timbered houses built directly on its structure. First mentioned in 1156, its current stone form dates from the 14th century. It recalls Florence's Ponte Vecchio or Venice's Rialto, except that its houses are inhabited and occupied by shops even today.

⏱ 45 min
4

Erfurt's Old Synagogue, built around 1094, is Central Europe's best-preserved medieval synagogue. It kept its walls intact because after the pogrom of March 21, 1349 - when about 900 of the city's Jews were massacred - it was converted into a warehouse and fell into oblivion, protecting it during Kristallnacht. The synagogue-mikveh-stone house ensemble has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2023. In the cellar, the Erfurt Treasure - discovered in 1998 in the wall of a neighboring cellar - has been permanently displayed since 2009: 3,141 silver coins, 14 ingots, and more than 700 Gothic goldsmith objects from the 14th century. The masterpiece is a gold ring adorned with a miniature temple bearing the Hebrew inscription «mazal tov». The treasure was exhibited at the Musée de Cluny in Paris and the Metropolitan Museum in New York before joining the permanent collection.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Visit the medieval Jewish treasure of Erfurt in the Old Synagogue's cellar. Examining the Gothic goldsmithery - gem-studded brooches, gold rings, ceremonial vessels - one grasps what Erfurt's Jewish community was at its apex, before its annihilation in 1349.

⏱ 60 min

Weimar

★★★★☆
1

Tiefurt Palace is a small summer residence on the banks of the Ilm east of Weimar. Originally a modest manor from the mid-16th century enlarged in 1765, it became Duchess Anna Amalia's favorite retreat from 1781 until her death in 1807. Despite its seven rooms per floor, it hosted literary and musical soirées bringing together Goethe, Wieland, and Corona Schröter, making Tiefurt a pastoral «Museion». The music room and the duchess's apartments are restored to their late 18th-century state with original furniture. Tiefurt is part of the UNESCO site «Classical Weimar».

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Stroll through Tiefurt's English landscape park along the Ilm River. Open meadows, century-old trees, and small monuments punctuate the main avenue: an artificial grotto, a temple of the muses, a Japanese-style wooden bridge. The park was designed in the spirit of 18th-century «natural» English gardens.

⏱ 60 min

Weimar

★★★★☆
1

The Bauhaus was founded in Weimar on April 12, 1919, by architect Walter Gropius, who merged the city's Academy of Fine Arts and School of Applied Arts. His manifesto proclaimed that «the ultimate goal of all creative activity is building». Among the recruited masters: Paul Klee, Wassily Kandinsky, László Moholy-Nagy, and Oskar Schlemmer. The school left Weimar in 1925 under pressure from a conservative regional government and moved to Dessau, before being closed by the Nazis in 1933. The Bauhaus Museum Weimar, opened in 2019 for the centenary, holds the world's oldest Bauhaus collection - started by Gropius himself - with 13,000 objects spread across three floors. The Bauhaus sites in Weimar have been UNESCO World Heritage Sites since 1996.

⏱ 90 min
2

Goethe's House at Frauenplan is the residence the poet occupied for 50 years, from 1782 to his death in 1832. It is preserved as he left it: his study with the mineral collection carefully arranged on shelves, his 5,000 volumes, the bed in which he died. It's the world's most important museum dedicated to Goethe. The house is part of the UNESCO site «Classical Weimar» inscribed in 1998.

⏱ 75 min
3

Schiller's House is the dwelling the poet occupied during his last three years of life, from 1802 to his death in 1805. It's here that «The Bride of Messina» and «William Tell» were written. The study is preserved with the inclined desk at which Schiller worked standing, his cane, the manuscripts. The modest rooms contrast with the grandeur of the works born there.

⏱ 60 min
4

The Duchess Anna Amalia Library is housed in the Renaissance «Green Palace» (1562-1565) converted into a library in 1761. Goethe was its director from 1797 to 1832 and doubled the holdings to reach 80,000 volumes. The three-story oval Rococo hall, in wood, is one of Germany's most photographed interiors. On September 2, 2004, a fire destroyed 50,000 volumes and damaged 118,000 - a national tragedy. After restoration, the library reopened in 2007. It preserves the world's largest collection of Goethe's Faust.

⏱ 60 min
5

The House of the Weimar Republic (Haus der Weimarer Republik), opened in 2019, traces the history of Germany's first democracy. In 1919, the National Constituent Assembly met in Weimar's German National Theater - Berlin was deemed too dangerous amid revolution - and adopted the Constitution of August 11, 1919, instituting universal suffrage and social rights pioneering for the era. The permanent exhibition illuminates the birth, crises, and fall of this Republic.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Bauhaus walking tour through Weimar's streets to discover buildings linked to the school: the Haus am Horn (1923), a Bauhaus housing prototype built for an exhibition, and Henry van de Velde's buildings where the school had its first workshops. Specialized guides offer commented tours.

Jena

★★★☆☆
1

Schiller's Garden House in Jena is the modest dwelling the poet occupied from 1797, when he was teaching at the university. It's in this pastoral setting that he began his creative collaboration with Goethe - who had brought him to Jena - and started «Wallenstein». The original building is preserved with some period furniture.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Zeiss Planetarium, inaugurated July 18, 1926, is the world's oldest continuously operating projection planetarium. It was designed by engineer Walther Bauersfeld of Carl Zeiss, who simultaneously developed the thin-shell concrete dome - an architectural invention that revolutionized 20th-century construction. The dome measures 25 meters in diameter. It's also Germany's largest planetarium. Current projections use a Zeiss Velvet full-dome laser system. The original idea dates to 1913, when the founder of the Deutsches Museum approached Zeiss for a «Himmelsschau» - a representation of the sky.

⏱ 75 min
3

The JenTower is a 160-meter cylindrical tower built during the GDR era to serve as a Zeiss research center - hence its local nickname «Keksrolle» (cookie roll). At 128 meters high, the panoramic platform offers views over the Saale's steep valley and the surrounding limestone hills.

⏱ 45 min
4

The Kunstsammlung Jena (Jena Art Collection) brings together more than 5,000 works ranging from the Middle Ages to the present, with a particular focus on German Expressionism. The museum is housed in several buildings in the historic city center.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Seitenroda

★★★☆☆
1

Leuchtenburg dominates the Saale Valley from 395 meters altitude, nicknamed «the Queen of the Saale Valley». Its first documented mention dates to 1221, when it was a military base of the Lords of Lobdeburg. It changed hands frequently and became in 1396 the administrative center of the Wettin villages. In 1553, an 80-meter-deep well was dug into the rock - Thuringia's second-deepest at the time. After its administration closed in 1705, it served variously as a prison, hospice, and asylum. Between 1920 and 1997, Thuringia's first youth hostel operated there. Purchased at auction in 2007 by the Leuchtenburg Foundation, the castle was restored and a new permanent exhibition opened in 2014.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Porzellanwelten Leuchtenburg traces 800 years of Thuringian porcelain history in the castle's halls. The exhibition explores the art, technique, and culture of this craft of which the town of Kahla, at the castle's foot, is a historic center. Modern installations deliberately contrast with the medieval walls.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Mythos Burg exhibition presents the castle's medieval history and legends linked to Leuchtenburg. The keep, accessible by a steep staircase, offers a 360-degree panorama over the Harz, the Thuringian Forest, and the Saale's steep valley.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Saalfeld

★★★☆☆
1

Saalfeld's Feengrotten - «fairy grottoes» - are the galleries of a former alum mine operated from 1530. After closing in 1850, the mine was rediscovered in 1910, and the third chamber - the «Fairy Realm» - was discovered in 1913. Opening to the public occurred on May 31, 1914. In 1993, the Guinness Book of World Records designated them «the most colorful grottoes in the world»: the mineral formations - stalactites, stalagmites, concretions - display hues ranging from blue to red through yellow, due to the oxidation of iron phosphate (diadochite), a rare mineral. Since 1914, more than 20 million visitors have traversed the three chambers connected by galleries.

⏱ 75 min
2

Since 1937, a gallery of Saalfeld's grottoes has housed Germany's first «Emanatorium» - a healing gallery by inhalation of underground air. The gallery's microclimate (constant temperature, mineral-charged air) was used to treat respiratory conditions. This therapeutic gallery is Germany's oldest.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Grottoneum, an interactive museum adjacent to the grottoes, presents the site's geological and mining history along with exhibitions on the region's minerals and crystals. Children's activities are offered.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Forest adventure trail near Saalfeld's Feengrotten, with family-friendly routes through the woods above the grottoes.

Uhlstadt Kirchhasel

★★★☆☆
1

Kochberg Castle is a moated castle (Wasserschloss) built around 1600 in Renaissance style, surrounded by a moat. It's known worldwide for its connection to Goethe and Charlotte von Stein. The von Stein family acquired the estate in 1733. Goethe was a regular guest of Charlotte von Stein from 1775 to 1788 - it was at Kochberg that he met Friedrich Schiller for the first time on September 7, 1788, before inviting him to Jena. He wrote his visit dates in ink on the castle's secretary desk, still visible today. More than 1,700 letters from Goethe to Charlotte von Stein testify to this thirteen-year relationship. The museum reconstructs the period's apartments with original furniture, portraits, and manuscript documents.

⏱ 75 min
2

The six-hectare landscape park was transformed by Carl von Stein (Charlotte's son) between 1800 and 1840, from a Baroque garden into a Romantic English park. Under imposing old trees, winding paths lead to a canvas hut, a tower ruin, a grotto with burial site, a bathing lake, and the flower garden with its floral staircase.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Liebhabertheater is a 75-seat private theater built around 1800 by Carl von Stein after the model of Weimar's court theater. It's a unique jewel worldwide: the classical columned portico, the hall with its hand-marbled wallpapers and floral friezes, the stage and pit are fully restored. It belongs to the «European Route of Historic Theatres» and hosts every weekend from May to October operas, chamber concerts, and theatrical performances.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Performance at Kochberg's Liebhabertheater: Baroque opera or chamber concert in this 75-seat theater, in the authentic atmosphere of the 18th century. Productions are conceived specifically for this space and employ historically informed performance practices.
