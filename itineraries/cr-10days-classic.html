Is it free?

When you book your hotels (even if not the ones we suggest), flights, cars etc. after clicking our links: you won't pay more, and we'll earn a few euros. That's what keeps us going. Either way, OneRoadTrip is 100% free. No false promises, no payment asked at the end.

Can you modify this itinerary?

Yes, you have three ways to do it:

Merge : lets you find nearby itineraries and integrate them into the map and visits.
Edit on map : lets you reorder stops, remove or add new ones.
Full editor : takes you to the next page where you can also edit visits/activities, add more, import your bookings, and print a travel journal.

Day 1

La Fortuna

1

The drive from San José to La Fortuna cuts through the Central Valley before hugging Lake Arenal's shoreline. Arenal's textbook cone emerges on approach: 1,670 meters of grey basalt commanding the plain below.

⏱ 30 min
2

Downtown La Fortuna is compact—a white-steepled church, a leafy plaza, and a lineup of tour operators. It's the perfect base camp for three days of volcano country exploration.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

First evening in La Fortuna: stroll the plaza, then settle in for dinner at one of the local restaurants with volcano views framed by the windows.

⏱ 90 min

Get to bed early—tomorrow starts with the Arenal 1968 Trail at dawn, before clouds roll in and swallow the summit.

⏱ 30 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 135 km · 3h30
Day 2

La Fortuna

1

The Arenal 1968 Trail takes its name from the July eruption that buried three villages under molten rock. Two routes to choose from: Sendero Bosque (4.7 km through the forest) or Sendero Colada (4 km, straight toward the lava fields). Black fractured terrain gives way to forest reclaiming its territory.

⏱ 120 min
2

Mirador El Silencio, accessed from the 1968 Trail, offers one of the most direct angles on Arenal's cone. On clear days, Lake Arenal spreads out below.

⏱ 30 min
3

Mistico Hanging Bridges features 3 km of trails punctuated by 16 bridges—six of them suspended high above the canopy. Guides armed with spotting scopes pick out sloths, toucans, and howler monkeys in the branches.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Late in the day, head to the natural hot springs at the volcano's base. Geothermal activity heats the water to between 25 and 38°C. A free thermal river flows near the Tabacon Hot Springs resort—accessible from the roadside.

⏱ 120 min
Day 3

La Fortuna

1

La Fortuna Waterfall plunges 70 meters into an emerald pool. The entrance fee gets you down some 500 steps to the base—and back up again. The climb is real in the midday heat.

⏱ 90 min
2

Cerro Chato is Arenal's dormant neighbor, accessed via the Observatory Lodge trailhead. Its crater cradles a blue lake where swimming is possible. The hike is demanding and should be avoided in rainy conditions.

⏱ 240 min

Activities

The Bogarin Trail is a private path on the edge of La Fortuna, known for sightings of two- and three-toed sloths. Local guides equipped with telescopes point out animals camouflaged in the branches.

⏱ 90 min

Lake Arenal, created in 1972, is Costa Rica's largest. The northern shore offers volcano views from the water. Local outfitters run kayak, stand-up paddle, and boat tours.

⏱ 120 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 35 km · 1h09
Day 4

Monteverde

1

The La Fortuna–Monteverde road follows Lake Arenal's northern shore. It's one of the country's most scenic drives: the volcano in your rearview, the Tilarán winds picking up, the road climbing steadily toward cloud forest.

⏱ 60 min
2

Santa Elena is Monteverde's base village. A handful of commercial streets gather restaurants, tour agencies, and lodging. The vibe is more laid-back than La Fortuna—a mountain town sitting at 1,400 meters.

⏱ 45 min
3

Santa Elena Reserve sees fewer visitors than its Monteverde neighbor yet sprawls across 310 hectares at 1,600 meters. On clear days, its mirador delivers views of Arenal and—conditions permitting—both coasts.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Evening in Santa Elena: wander the village, sample the local cheese—a specialty of the Quaker families who settled here in the 1950s. Artisan dairies sell directly to the public.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 115 km · 4h
Day 5

Monteverde

1

Monteverde Cloud Forest Reserve stretches across more than 10,500 hectares at 1,400 meters. Centuries-old trees vanish into mist, orchids—over 500 species—cling to their trunks. Nine marked trails thread through this unique ecosystem, including Sendero Bosque Nuboso, your best shot at spotting the resplendent quetzal.

⏱ 180 min
2

Curi-Cancha Reserve, 3 km from Santa Elena, draws smaller crowds than Monteverde and rivals it for biodiversity. Seven marked trails wind through 76 hectares of primary forest. Spider monkeys, capuchins, coatis, and quetzals appear regularly.

⏱ 120 min
3

Monteverde Butterfly Garden showcases around 490 species in screened enclosures. Chrysalises mid-emergence are particularly spectacular. Children and adults alike enjoy the life-cycle demonstrations.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Monteverde's canopy tour ranks among Costa Rica's most celebrated. Ziplines cross the canopy for several kilometers; some cables stretch beyond 700 meters. The circuit also includes suspension bridges and, for the bold, a Tarzan swing.

⏱ 180 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 165 km · 4h30
Day 6

Manuel Antonio

1

The descent from Monteverde toward the Central Pacific cuts across the Guanacaste agricultural plain before meeting the coast. Cloud-forest humidity gives way to coastal heat. Quepos, a small port town 5 km from the park, serves as the area's base.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Come evening, head to Playa Espadilla Norte, the public beach outside the park boundaries. This is the classic spot to watch the sun drop into the Pacific. Palms, gentle surf, golden light—Costa Rica's postcard image made real.

⏱ 60 min

Quepos comes alive at night. The town is known for fresh-catch restaurants and a relaxed vibe. Stroll the waterfront before dinner.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 180 km · 4h30
Day 7

Manuel Antonio

1

Manuel Antonio National Park is Costa Rica's smallest (683 hectares on land) yet one of its richest. The main trail, flat and well-maintained, follows an 18-hectare mangrove before reaching Playa Manuel Antonio—widely considered one of the country's finest beaches.

⏱ 180 min
2

Punta Catedral is a former island connected to the mainland by a sand tombolo, forming a whale-tail peninsula. The loop trail offers views of both beaches and the park's 12 offshore islands, which serve as seabird sanctuaries.

⏱ 60 min
3

The park's mangrove, visible from trails paralleling the beaches, covers 18 hectares of fresh and saltwater. Red and white mangroves form their own ecosystem, distinct from the adjacent rainforest.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Hire a certified naturalist guide at the park entrance. Armed with a spotting scope, they'll pick out two-toed sloths clinging to branches, white-headed capuchin monkeys, and Central American squirrel monkeys—endemic to Manuel Antonio. The difference between a solo visit and a guided one is night and day.

⏱ 150 min

After the hike, swim at Playa Manuel Antonio. The turquoise water is sheltered by overhanging forest. Snorkeling is possible at the beach's edges to spot morays, angelfish, and starfish.

⏱ 90 min
Day 8

Manuel Antonio

1

Biesanz Bay, a few minutes' walk from the hillside hotels, is a small cove protected from the surf. The water is clear, the bottom sandy—ideal for snorkeling. Rays, parrotfish, and sometimes sea turtles make appearances.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

The kayaking excursion through Isla Damas mangrove follows the Río Damas estuary for half a day. The network of channels shelters crocodiles, monkeys, shorebirds, and terns. Local outfitters provide gear and guides.

⏱ 180 min

Free afternoon at the beach. Quepos has an active marina: deep-sea sportfishing, sea-kayak rentals, and coastal tours depart from here.

⏱ 120 min
Day 9

Manuel Antonio

1

Playa Gemelas, a small cove inside the park, is Manuel Antonio's least-visited beach. Perfect for a quiet morning between forest and Pacific, after the big hiking days.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Board a catamaran for a cruise along the Pacific coast. The route follows the national park islands, with a snorkeling stop in waters rich with marine life. Coastal dolphins regularly accompany the boats; in season (July–November), humpback whales pass through the area.

⏱ 240 min

Final evening in Quepos: dinner on the waterfront with catch-of-the-day. The town is lively at night, with several restaurants along the water.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 89 km · 1h29
Day 10

San Jose

1

The National Theatre of San José, inaugurated in 1897, was financed by a coffee tax imposed on Central Valley growers. Its neoclassical facade opens onto an Italian-style auditorium adorned with imported marble and paintings commissioned from European artists. Visit by day or catch an evening performance.

⏱ 60 min
2

Beneath the Plaza de la Cultura, the Pre-Columbian Gold Museum gathers more than 1,600 pieces—animal figurines, ritual jewelry, frog-shaped pendants—created by the peoples who lived in Costa Rica before the Spanish arrived. The museum's subterranean architecture is remarkable in its own right.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Jade Museum, facing Plaza de la Democracia, houses the Americas' largest collection of pre-Columbian jade. Jadeite pendants, masks, and figurines date from 500 BC to AD 800. The five-story contemporary building also includes ceramics and stone objects in their ritual contexts.

⏱ 90 min
4

The Mercado Central, steps from the National Theatre, has been the city's covered market since 1880. Tropical fruit, herbs, pottery, and daily casado sit side by side with vendors of roasted coffee. The atmosphere is everyday tico life, far removed from tourism.

⏱ 45 min
5

Barrio Amón, north of downtown, lines up Victorian and Art Deco mansions from the early 20th century. A few galleries and cafés occupy the old homes of coffee-baron families. A fine end-of-trip stroll.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Wander Avenida Central, the pedestrian street connecting Plaza de la Cultura to Parque Central. Colorful buildings, live music, and sugarcane juice vendors compose the scene of a final Costa Rican afternoon.

⏱ 60 min

Parque Central is downtown San José's green lung. Families of green parrots nest in the trees and appear each afternoon—a distinctly tico urban quirk.

⏱ 45 min
