✨ Highlights
  • Aapravasi Ghat: UNESCO site where nearly 500,000 indentured laborers transited between 1834 and 1924
  • Black River Gorges National Park: 6,574 hectares of endemic forest with 3 to 5 hour hikes
  • Bois Chéri: Mauritius's first tea plantation, operating since 1892, with panoramic views over the south coast
  • Mauritius kestrel at Ferney Valley: raptor saved from extinction in the 1970s, from 4 individuals to over 400 today
📆 Best period

May, June, July, August, September, October, November

⏳ How many days

To make the most of it, plan between 3 and 7 days.

☀️ Weather

Dry Season (May-October): Perfect weather with minimal rainfall, comfortable temperatures (20-26°C), and trade winds. Peak season for outdoor activities, hiking, and beach experiences with clear skies.

Shoulder Season (April, November): Excellent weather with brief showers, moderate hotel rates, and fewer crowds. Ideal balance of good conditions and value pricing for most activities.

Wet Season (November-April): Hot, humid weather (25-32°C) with cyclone risk, but lush landscapes and excellent diving conditions. Lower accommodation rates but weather monitoring essential.

Cyclone Season (January-March): Highest rainfall and storm risk requiring flexible planning and comprehensive travel insurance. However, many cyclones pass without major impact.

Port Louis

Where centuries of migration converge in steaming market stalls and colonial harbors

★★★★★
1

Get to the Central Market by 7am – that's when the stalls overflow with spices, tropical fruits, and fresh street food. Try the dholl puri, a split-pea-stuffed flatbread that's been sold on Port Louis streets for generations. Mine frit, gâteau piment, bol renversé: the market delivers the best meal of the day, and the cheapest.

⏱ 90 min
2

Aapravasi Ghat has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2006. This wharf is where, starting in 1834, the first indentured laborers from India landed to replace enslaved workers after abolition. Between 1834 and 1924, nearly 500,000 people passed through. Plan for 45 minutes – the site is small but heavy with history.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Blue Penny Museum holds two of the world's rarest stamps: the One Penny and Two Pence from 1847, the first stamps issued in Mauritius. They're only displayed in dim light to prevent deterioration. The museum also traces the island's history from the Portuguese onward – allow an hour.

⏱ 60 min
4

Chinatown spreads around Rue Royale – a few blocks of pagodas, grocery stores, and Cantonese restaurants that recall Mauritius's waves of Chinese immigration since the 19th century. Not a polished tourist quarter: a genuine neighborhood enclave, with its smells and corner shops.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Caudan Waterfront occupies renovated colonial warehouses facing the harbor. It's the most open-air corner of Port Louis – a solid option for a shaded lunch before the afternoon heat.

⏱ 60 min

Climb up to Fort Adelaide – La Citadelle – late in the day. Built by the British between 1834 and 1840, it overlooks the city from 100 meters up. Late afternoon light on the harbor and Port Louis rooftops is the best you'll get all day. Bring water: the walk up from the center is steep.

⏱ 60 min

Curepipe

A misty highland town perched at 550 meters, gateway to the Tea Route

★★★☆☆
1

Eureka has 109 doors – yes, 109. This 1830 Creole house in Moka is the largest on the island, built in the typical colonial Mascarene style: verandas on three sides, camphor wood, period furniture. Wander through the rooms, look at the yellowed photographs, then head down to the garden – it follows a river where you can swim.

⏱ 90 min
2

Trou aux Cerfs is a volcanic crater 300 meters across and 85 meters deep right in the heart of Curepipe. A path circles it in less than a kilometer. On clear days, you get the central plateau mountain chain on one side – and sometimes both coasts of the island.

⏱ 45 min
3

Curepipe is Mauritius's wettest town, perched at 550 meters altitude. Misty in the morning, cool (18-22°C), it has the atmosphere of a colonial mountain town – wooden houses, covered market, slow rhythm. Pack a light sweater for the evening.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

If you're there on a Thursday or Sunday, detour to the Quatre Bornes market – the island's big textile market. Hundreds of stalls selling clothes, sheets, shoes at rock-bottom prices. Not touristy at all: this is where Mauritians do their shopping.

⏱ 60 min

Curepipe

A misty highland town perched at 550 meters, gateway to the Tea Route

★★★☆☆
1

Domaine des Aubineaux is a late-19th-century colonial house with Italian frescoes painted on the ceilings and intact period furniture. In the garden, a colophane tree is over 500 years old – one of the oldest trees on the island. Admission is often included in Tea Route combo tickets.

⏱ 45 min
2

Bois Chéri has been producing tea since 1892 – it's the island's first and oldest plantation. Tour the factory to understand the transformation process from leaf to cup. Then head up to the panoramic restaurant: the view over the south coast on clear days is worth the trip alone. Try the vanilla tea and coconut tea – they're the house signatures. ⚠️ The factory only runs tours certain days of the week: check before you go.

⏱ 90 min
3

Saint Aubin Estate: a sugar plantation since 1819, one of Mauritius's oldest. It's here that the island's first agricultural rum is produced. The Vanilla House walks you through the entire transformation chain – from the Vanilla planifolia orchid to the dried pod. Allow 1.5 hours to tour the estate.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

This is a day for the palate. Flavored tea tastings at Bois Chéri, aged rum and cane punch at Saint Aubin, fresh vanilla scraped on the spot. Leave space in your luggage – the teas and rums from these estates aren't as easy to find elsewhere.

⏱ 60 min

Chamarel

Where volcanic earth bleeds seven colors and rhum flows from mountainside stills

★★★★☆
1

Grand Bassin – Ganga Talao in Creole – is a crater lake at 550 meters altitude surrounded by multicolored Hindu temples. A 33-meter statue of Shiva greets you at the entrance. It's the most important pilgrimage site in Mauritius for the Hindu community. In February-March, during Maha Shivaratri, hundreds of thousands of pilgrims walk barefoot from across the island – sometimes 40 kilometers on foot to reach the lake.

⏱ 60 min
2

Black River Gorges National Park covers 6,574 hectares – it's the largest area of native forest still standing in Mauritius. The Macchabée trails cut through canopy forest with tree ferns and rare endemic fauna. Allow 3 to 5 hours depending on your pace. Pack water, closed-toe shoes, and sunscreen.

⏱ 240 min
3

Plaine Champagne is a 700-meter-high plateau with moorland landscapes unlike anything else in Mauritius. Alexandra Falls plunges into the gorges from this plateau – the viewpoint over the falls and the forest below is worth the 20-minute stop.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Physical day – arrive in Chamarel late afternoon and settle in. The village is quiet, accommodations often tucked deep in the greenery. Good evening to do nothing at all.

⏱ 60 min

Chamarel

Where volcanic earth bleeds seven colors and rhum flows from mountainside stills

★★★★☆
1

Arrive at the Seven Coloured Earth right at opening at 8:30am. After 10am, tour buses roll in and it's over. These volcanic earth dunes owe their colors to basalt decomposing into clays: iron oxides give the red and brown, aluminum oxides the blue and purple. The phenomenon is real – the colors never mix, even after torrential rain.

⏱ 45 min
2

Chamarel Falls drops nearly 100 meters – it's the island's highest waterfall. The viewpoint is accessible from the Seven Coloured Earth site. It's more impressive during the rainy season (December-March), but visible year-round.

⏱ 30 min
3

Chamarel Rum Distillery is the only arabica coffee operation in Mauritius, grown on volcanic soil at 300 meters altitude. It also produces rum – white rum, aged rum, spiced rum. Plan for the tasting: it's included in the tour and the bottles aren't available everywhere.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Ebony Forest is a private reserve of native ebony forest with 140 plant species. The trail climbs to Point Sublime – a plunging view over Le Morne and the entire west coast. Allow 1.5 hours round trip. It's one of the rare chances to see what Mauritius looked like before deforestation.

⏱ 90 min

Le Morne Brabant

A towering basalt sentinel that sheltered freedom seekers and now guards their memory

★★★★☆
1

Le Morne Brabant rises to 556 meters and has been a UNESCO site since 2008. This massive rock jutting into the sea served as refuge for maroon slaves in the 18th and 19th centuries. Legend says that when slavery was abolished in 1835, police climbed up to tell them – and seeing them approach, the slaves thought they were coming to capture them and threw themselves off the cliff. A guide is mandatory to reach the summit. Plan for 3 to 4 hours round trip.

⏱ 240 min
2

The Slave Route Monument stands at the foot of Le Morne, facing the sea. It recalls that this peninsula was one of the last refuges for escaped slaves in Mauritius. Stark, almost excessively so – a few minutes suffice, but they count.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Le Morne beach stretches at the foot of the rock, facing the lagoon. After the climb, this is where you land for the rest of the afternoon. The water is calm, the bottom white sand, and the view of Le Morne Brabant from the sea is the finest on the island.

⏱ 120 min

1

Flic en Flac is the big beach on the west coast – several kilometers of sand, clear water, sunset facing the sea. Less hidden than Le Morne, but much longer. Cathedral Dive Site, two kilometers offshore, features underwater arches at 30 meters depth – for certified divers only.

⏱ 120 min
2

Casela Nature Parks sits in the foothills of the Black River Range, between Le Morne and Flic en Flac. Zipline, quad, buggy safari, interaction with lions and giraffes: it's an animal and adventure park that works as well for families as for adults. Allow half a day.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

Depart at 6:30am for the dolphin outing in Tamarin Bay – 15 minutes from Le Morne. Spinner dolphins and Indo-Pacific humpback dolphins are resident in the bay and visible almost year-round at sunrise. Local operators run these trips on rigid inflatable boats. Get there early: the dolphins dive as soon as the heat rises.

3

1

Sir Seewoosagur Ramgoolam Botanical Garden in Pamplemousses was founded in 1770 by Pierre Poivre, a French administrator who acclimatized spices from around the world here – cinnamon, nutmeg, clove – to break the Dutch monopoly. ⏱ 90 min
3

Château de Labourdonnais is a 19th-century colonial manor with an orchard of 1,500 fruit trees and a rum distillery. The guided tour includes the restored house and tasting. It's more intimate than L'Aventure du Sucre – fewer visitors, quieter setting.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Late in the day, head to the northern beaches. Grand Baie is the liveliest town on this coast – restaurants, bars, shops. After 12 days of culture and nature, it's a welcome change of pace.
