Shoes On The Danube Bank

★★★★☆
1

Shoes on the Danube Bank memorial

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Don't walk past this memorial without stopping. Along roughly 45 metres of riverbank between the Chain Bridge and the Parliament, 60 pairs of cast-iron shoes are set into the stone, facing the water. This is where the fascist Arrow Cross Party executed hundreds of Jews between December 1944 and January 1945: before shooting them and throwing them into the Danube, the militiamen forced them to remove their shoes — footwear was a valuable commodity in wartime. Filmmaker Can Togay and sculptor Gyula Pauer conceived this memorial in 2005, inaugurated on 16 April, ahead of the 60th anniversary of the victory over fascism. The 60 pairs represent carefully chosen 1940s styles: men's boots, women's heels, children's small shoes. Nobody was spared. Three cast-iron plaques inscribed in Hungarian, English and Hebrew record the facts. Take tram 2 or 2A to get here. Allow yourself time: the stone bench behind the sculptures was designed for contemplation.

Great Market Hall

★★★★☆
1

Great Market Hall (Nagyvásárcsarnok)

⏱ 90 min

Activities

From the moment you step inside, you'll see why this building is sometimes mistaken for a railway station: the iron interior framework, the tall glass windows and the sheer dimensions — 10,000 m² across three floors — are truly spectacular. Architect Samu Pecz inaugurated the hall on 15 February 1897, and the roof was clad in the famous colourful ceramics from the Zsolnay manufactory in Pécs, the same tiles found on Matthias Church. The building was commissioned by Budapest's first mayor, Károly Kamermayer, to address the sanitary problems of food supply in a fast-growing metropolis. During the Second World War the hall was damaged, then declared derelict in 1991 before being restored and reopened in 1994. Arrive early in the morning — the produce is fresher and the tourists fewer. Ground floor: quality Hungarian paprika, charcuterie, cheese, honey and the essential unicum. Upper floor: crispy lángos (fried dough with sour cream and cheese) and souvenir stalls. Bring cash — many vendors don't accept cards.

Memory Garden

★★★☆☆
1

Memorial Garden (Raoul Wallenberg Park)

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Behind the Great Synagogue lies one of Budapest's most moving memorials. In this park — once part of the Budapest Jewish Ghetto where roughly 70,000 people were confined in autumn 1944 — stands the Tree of Life, a weeping willow in metal created by Hungarian sculptor Imre Varga and unveiled in 1991. Some also see the shape of an inverted menorah. On every steel leaf is engraved the name of a Jewish victim of the Holocaust; families still come to have the names of lost relatives added. The monument was partly funded by American actor Tony Curtis, whose parents were Hungarian. The park also pays tribute to Raoul Wallenberg, the Swedish diplomat who saved thousands of Hungarian Jews by issuing them diplomatic passports, as well as to other Righteous Among the Nations. More than 2,000 people who died in the ghetto were hastily buried in this garden during the winter of 1944–45, before Soviet forces liberated Budapest in February 1945.

Jewish Quarter

★★★★☆
1

Budapest's Jewish Quarter

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The 7th District, between Dohány Street and Kazinczy Street, is one of Budapest's most fascinating and contradictory neighbourhoods. From the second half of the 19th century, many Jews here Magyarised their names as a sign of integration. Today, crumbling inner courtyards sit alongside ruin bars that have become famous across Europe, and restored synagogues stand between former Art Nouveau tenement blocks. The quarter is defined by a 'triangle of synagogues' representing the three branches of modern Judaism: the Great Synagogue (Neolog), the Rumbach Street synagogue (Reformed) and the Kazinczy Street synagogue (Orthodox). Wander the lanes at random — some courtyards reveal frescoes, old pharmacies or remarkable wrought-iron staircases. In the evening, the quarter comes alive and ruin-bar terraces spill into courtyards that haven't been renovated since the 1940s.

Great Synagogue

★★★★☆
1

Great Synagogue on Dohány Street

⏱ 90 min

Activities

This is the largest synagogue in Europe and the second-largest in the world after Temple Emanu-El in New York: 75 metres long, 27 metres wide, two 44-metre towers, seating for 3,500. It was built between 1854 and 1859 by Viennese architect Ludwig Förster in a Moorish style inspired by the Alhambra, with much of its interior decoration by Hungarian architect Frigyes Feszl. On its inauguration day, Franz Liszt and Camille Saint-Saëns played the organ — it's one of the rare synagogues to have one. During the Second World War the building was used as a German radio base and then as a stable. Book tickets in advance during peak season — the queue can be long. The ticket includes the Hungarian Jewish Museum and Raoul Wallenberg Park. Allow at least an hour and a half to see everything. Men must cover their heads (kippas provided at the entrance). The synagogue is closed on Saturdays.

Hungarian State Opera House

★★★★☆
1

Hungarian State Opera House

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Architect Miklós Ybl spent nine years on this building, from 1875 to 1884, with a stated ambition: to surpass the Vienna Opera in elegance, if not in size (1,261 seats against Vienna's 1,709). Emperor Franz Joseph financed the construction, and according to legend, a ticket for the opening night on 27 September 1884 cost the equivalent of two horses. The neo-Renaissance façade is crowned by statues of Franz Liszt and Ferenc Erkel (composer of the Hungarian national anthem) at the main entrance, and sculpted portraits of Mozart, Beethoven, Verdi and other musical giants along the roofline. Inside, 7 kg of gold were used in the auditorium's decoration, and the acoustics rank among the finest in Europe — slightly ahead of the Palais Garnier, according to a Hungarian university study. Gustav Mahler served as music director here from 1888 to 1891. Book the guided tour in advance, especially in summer: it's one of the best in Budapest.

Vajdahunyad Castle

★★★★☆
1

Vajdahunyad Castle

⏱ 75 min
2

Városliget Park

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Here's a place that always surprises: this castle that looks straight out of the Middle Ages was actually built in 1896, from wood and cardboard, for the National Millennium Exhibition celebrating 1,000 years since the Magyar conquest of the Carpathian Basin. The Hungarian public fell so in love with it that they refused to see it demolished — so it was rebuilt in stone and brick between 1904 and 1908. Emperor Franz Joseph himself came to inaugurate it in 1907. Architect Ignác Alpár chose to blend Romanesque, Gothic, Renaissance and baroque in a single complex, representing the evolution of Hungarian architecture across ten centuries. The Gothic section is modelled directly on Vajdahunyad Castle in Transylvania (now in Romania), where legend places the imprisonment of Vlad Dracula. In the courtyard, don't miss the statue of the Anonymous, the masked chronicler at the court of King Béla III. A bust of Béla Lugosi (the Hungarian actor who became Count Dracula in Hollywood) has also been installed in a niche. In winter, the artificial lake becomes the largest open-air ice rink in Europe.

Szechenyi Baths

★★★★☆
1

Széchenyi Thermal Baths

⏱ 180 min

Activities

The big mustard-yellow building in Városliget park is the largest thermal complex in Europe. Designed by architect Győző Czigler in a neo-Baroque style, it was inaugurated on 16 June 1913 — more than 200,000 visitors came in the first year alone. The water rises from an artesian well drilled in 1879 to a depth of 1,246 metres, at a temperature of 76°C. It's rich in calcium, magnesium, sulphates and fluorides, and renowned for its effects on joint pain. The outdoor pools — three large basins between 27 and 38°C — have become one of Budapest's most iconic images: chess players facing off in the hot water even in the middle of winter. Indoors: 15 additional pools, saunas, steam rooms. Arrive early in the morning or on a weekday to avoid the crowds. Bring swimwear, a towel and flip-flops. If visiting in summer, book your ticket in advance.

Margaret Island (2.5 km long, 500 m wide, 100 hectares) takes its name from a true story: the daughter of King Béla IV, Margaret, lived in a Dominican convent here from childhood until her death in 1271, fulfilling her father's vow to dedicate her if he saved Hungary from the Mongol invasion of 1242. This building, erected in 1880 at 60 Andrássy Avenue, has known two dark lives: from 1944 to 1945 it was the headquarters of the fascist Arrow Cross Party; from 1945 to 1956 it became the base of the ÁVH, the communist secret police — Hungary's equivalent of the KGB. Take the audioguide (available in English and French) — without it, the explanations are very limited. Arrive at opening time to avoid school groups. The visit easily takes two to three hours.

Andrássy Avenue stretches exactly 2,310 metres, linking the centre of Pest to Városliget park. Construction began in 1871 at the initiative of Count Gyula Andrássy, and the avenue was officially inaugurated on 20 August 1876, though the last buildings were not completed until 1885. St Stephen S Basilica

★★★★☆
1

St Stephen's Basilica

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Construction of this basilica took 54 years — from 1851 to 1905 — and involved three successive architects, including Miklós Ybl (the same as for the Opera), who redesigned the dome after its catastrophic collapse on 22 January 1868. Its height of 96 metres is deliberately symbolic: it exactly matches that of the Hungarian Parliament, representing the balance between spiritual and secular power, and recalls the year 896, when the Magyars settled in the Carpathian Basin. Inside, more than 50 different types of marble adorn the walls. The Chapel of the Holy Right houses the country's most sacred relic: the mummified right hand of King Saint Stephen, Hungary's first Christian king (crowned in 1000, died in 1038, canonised in 1083). To see the relic up close, insert a coin into the lighting mechanism. Every 20 August (Hungarian National Day), it is carried in procession around the building. Don't miss the climb to the dome for a 360° panoramic view over Budapest — take the lift halfway, then finish on foot.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

This monument that looks like a medieval fortress was in fact never used for defence: it was designed from the outset as a panoramic terrace, built between 1895 and 1902 by architect Frigyes Schulek to commemorate the millennium of the arrival of the Magyar tribes in 895. The seven turrets with their conical roofs each represent one of the seven tribal chieftains who followed Prince Árpád into Hungary — the roof shapes deliberately echo the nomadic tents of the steppes. Its name comes from the fishing village that once stood at the foot of the hill in the Middle Ages, where the fishermen were charged with defending this stretch of the ramparts. The view of the Parliament from the bastion is the finest in Budapest — especially at sunset and dawn, when the golden light grazes the Parliament's white façade from across the Danube. The lower terraces are free; the upper turrets require a ticket during the day (free entry before 9 AM and after 9 PM in summer). In front of the building stands the equestrian statue of King Saint Stephen, by sculptor Alajos Stróbl.

Matthias Church

★★★★☆
1

Matthias Church

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Founded in 1255 by King Béla IV, converted into a mosque by the Ottomans in 1541, rebuilt in baroque style by the Habsburgs after 1686, then restored to its neo-Gothic appearance by architect Frigyes Schulek between 1873 and 1896: Matthias Church has survived every regime. It takes its popular name from King Matthias Corvinus (1458–1490), who celebrated two marriages here and added the Matthias Tower (60 metres), completed in 1470. On 8 June 1867, Emperor Franz Joseph I and his wife Elisabeth of Wittelsbach (Sisi) were crowned King and Queen of Hungary here — Franz Liszt conducted the premiere of his Coronation Mass for the occasion. Gellert Hill

★★★☆☆
1

Gellért Hill and Citadel

⏱ 90 min
2

Liberty Statue

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Gellért Hill rises to 235 metres, making it the highest vantage point within central Budapest. It takes its name from the Venetian Bishop Gerard (Gellért in Hungarian), invited to Hungary by Saint Stephen to evangelise the country — legend has it that he was hurled into the Danube from this hill in a nail-studded barrel during a peasant revolt in 1046. The Citadel was built in 1851 by the Habsburgs as a symbol of authority over a Hungary that had just lost its war of independence in 1848–49. In 1897 the Austrian army was permanently evacuated. The Liberty Statue, inaugurated in 1947 by the Soviets to commemorate the liberation of Budapest, depicts a 14-metre woman holding a palm above her head (40 metres including the pedestal). In 1990, with the fall of the communist regime, the Soviet soldiers that once flanked her were sent to the Memento Park. Walk up from Liberty Bridge or Elisabeth Bridge — allow 30 to 40 minutes. The 360° view over Budapest is the most complete in the city.

Hungarian Parliament Building

★★★★★
1

Hungarian Parliament (Országház)

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Hungarian Parliament is the third-largest parliamentary building in the world, behind those of Romania and Argentina. Its construction, which employed roughly 100,000 workers, 40 million bricks and 40 kg of gold for decoration, ran from 1885 to 1902 to the designs of Imre Steindl — who lost his sight before seeing his masterpiece completed. The building measures 268 metres long and 123 metres wide, contains 691 rooms, 29 staircases, 10 interior courtyards and a dome 96 metres high (a figure chosen in reference to the year 896, the founding of Hungary). This 96 is also the height of St Stephen's Basilica across the river — the two tallest buildings in Budapest are deliberately balanced. Beneath the dome, guarded day and night by soldiers in dress uniform, lies the Crown of Saint Stephen, the most precious treasure of the Hungarian nation. The changing of the guard is highly choreographed and worth catching. Visits are guided only and run in several languages. Book in advance: morning slots go first.

Buda Castle

★★★★☆
1

Buda Castle (Royal Palace)

⏱ 120 min
2

Hungarian National Gallery

⏱ 90 min

Activities

To reach the castle, take the Budavári Sikló funicular from Clark Ádám Square, directly opposite the Chain Bridge. In service since 1870, it's the oldest form of public transport in Budapest — it was destroyed during the war and faithfully rebuilt. The 95-metre ride climbs 50 metres in a few minutes and offers a striking view over the Danube banks. At the top, Buda Castle has commanded the city since the 13th century. A residence of Hungarian kings through to the end of Habsburg rule, heavily damaged during the Siege of Budapest (1944–1945), it was restored in the second half of the 20th century. The site has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1987. Today it houses the Hungarian National Gallery (Hungarian art from the 11th century to the present — a must for fans of the Nagybánya School and Expressionism), the Budapest History Museum and the Széchenyi National Library. The outdoor esplanade is freely accessible at all times — the gardens offer one of the finest views over Pest and the Danube.
