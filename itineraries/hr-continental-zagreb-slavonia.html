Zagreb

★★★★☆
1

Start at Ban Jelačić Square, Zagreb's centre of gravity. Beneath the equestrian statue of Ban Josip Jelačić — the national hero who abolished serfdom in 1848 — blue trams and hurried pedestrians converge at all hours. It's the ideal starting point: the Upper Town (Gornji Grad) climbs northward, while the grand avenues of the Lower Town (Donji Grad) stretch to the south.

⏱ 30 min
2

A two-minute walk north, the cathedral's neo-Gothic spires soar to 105 metres — visible from almost anywhere in the city. Hermann Bollé rebuilt it after the 1880 earthquake, but the interior walls still preserve Romanesque fragments dating back to the 11th century. Don't miss the tomb of Cardinal Alojzije Stepinac, who died under house arrest in 1960 and was beatified by John Paul II in 1998 — one of the most debated figures in modern Croatian history.

⏱ 45 min
3

Heading up toward Gradec, you'll pass through the Stone Gate (Kamenita Vrata), the only one of the four medieval gates still standing. A fire devastated the neighbourhood in 1731: everything burned except a small icon of the Virgin attached to the vault. Ever since, Zagrebians stop here to light a candle before an exam, surgery or journey. The gilded metal ex-votos on the walls tell centuries of quiet devotion.

⏱ 20 min
4

St Mark's Church is Zagreb's most photographed sight. Its roof of glazed tiles, laid in the 14th century in its current pattern, displays the coats of arms of the city of Zagreb and the historical Croatian-Slavonian-Dalmatian Kingdom. In front of it, the Parliament (Sabor) and the Ban's Palace (Banski Dvori) close off the square — this is where Croatian independence was proclaimed on 25 June 1991.

⏱ 30 min
5

To head back down, take the funicular (uspinjača): 66 metres long, inaugurated in 1890, it links the Lower Town to the Lotrščak Tower in 55 seconds. It's officially the shortest public funicular in the world still in service. At the top, the tower has fired a cannon at noon every day since 1877 — a tradition that began to synchronise the city's church bells. If you miss the noon blast, the view over the red rooftops is worth the trip at any hour.

⏱ 30 min
6

Away from the centre, Mirogoj Cemetery deserves a proper visit. Bollé designed its 79,000 m² with neo-Renaissance arcades, chapels and shaded alleys where poets, presidents and ordinary citizens rest side by side. Zagrebians come here for Sunday walks — it's as much a park as a cemetery. Allow at least 45 minutes to explore.

⏱ 60 min
7

The Museum of Broken Relationships was born from an idea by two local artists in 2006: displaying anonymous objects alongside the text of the breakup that made them useless. An axe, a vibrator, a pair of crutches — each piece is funny, heartbreaking, or both at once. The concept has since toured the world and opened a branch in Los Angeles. Allow an hour: you'll read everything.

⏱ 75 min
8

The Croatian National Theatre (HNK) is an imperial yellow building inaugurated by Emperor Franz Joseph I in person on 14 October 1895. Its neo-Baroque Viennese architecture stands out deliberately along the boulevard. In front of it, the Well of Life fountain sculpted by Ivan Meštrović depicts intertwined human figures. If you get the chance to catch an opera or ballet during the season, tickets remain very affordable compared to Western European capitals.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Get to Dolac Market before 9 AM to see it in full swing. Outside, under the red-and-white parasols, country women in headscarves sell fresh cheese, vegetables and flowers. Downstairs, the covered market offers meats, charcuterie and fish. After 10 AM, the best picks are gone. It's also the best place to buy kajmak (creamy fresh cheese) to take away.

⏱ 45 min

Tkalčićeva Street was once a river — the Medveščak — that was channelled and covered over in the 19th century. The waterway became an alley, the alley became Zagreb's liveliest strip of terraces. In the evening, every bar spills onto the pavement, and music carries from one end to the other. This is where all of Zagreb meets up from Thursday through Saturday.

⏱ 60 min

The Croatian Museum of Naïve Art in Zagreb is the only museum in the world dedicated to this genre: it showcases the work of 20th-century Croatian peasant artists, mainly from the Hlebine region. Ivan Generalić and his contemporaries painted village scenes on glass with a density of detail that feels equal parts dreamlike and documentary. American and Japanese collectors discovered these works in the 1960s — yet they remain surprisingly little known.

⏱ 60 min

Štrukli are Zagreb's signature dish: pastry parcels filled with fresh cheese, baked or boiled depending on the version. Look for a slastičarna (pastry shop) or traditional restaurant — some serve both versions. Later in the morning, finish with a cream-topped coffee at one of the grand Viennese-style cafés in the Donji Grad, such as Kavkaz or Café de l'Europe.

⏱ 60 min

Three kilometres east of the centre, Maksimir Park opens up its 316 hectares of forests, lakes and lawns. Inaugurated in 1794, it's the oldest public park in South-East Europe. Zagreb Zoo is also located here. Ideal in the late afternoon to recharge between days of sightseeing.

⏱ 60 min

Varazdin

★★★★☆
1

Stari Grad fortress is surrounded by moats and remarkably well preserved. Inside, the City Museum displays medieval armour, baroque furniture and mementoes from the era when Varaždin served as capital of the Croatian Kingdom — from 1756 until the fire of 1776 forced the government to relocate to Zagreb. Allow 45 minutes for the full tour.

⏱ 75 min
2

Varaždin's City Hall is the oldest still in use in Croatia — the earliest documents mentioning the town and its charter date from the 14th century. Its Renaissance façade faces the main square, and on Saturday mornings guards in period costume stand watch at the entrance. If you happen to be here that day, catch the changing of the guard: it's brief but charming.

⏱ 30 min
3

Sermage Palace houses the Gallery of Old Masters, assembled by the local aristocracy over the centuries. 17th-century Flemish paintings, Italian portraits and Austrian canvases hang together in rooms with stuccoed ceilings. A collection of this calibre in a town of 40,000 is a genuine surprise.

⏱ 60 min
4

St John the Baptist Church is Varaždin's finest baroque landmark: gilding, frescoes and 18th-century stuccowork cover every surface from floor to ceiling. The bell tower rises to around forty metres and offers a panorama over the red-tiled rooftops of the old town — ask whether the climb is open when you visit.

⏱ 45 min
5

Varaždin Cemetery is probably the most unusual in Europe. The caretaker Herman Haller, who held the post from 1905 to 1946, sculpted its cypress hedges into geometric shapes — cones, spirals, arches, living walls. This monumental topiary garden transforms a place of mourning into a work of art. Allow half an hour to wander through it.

⏱ 45 min
6

Stroll through the pedestrian streets of the historic centre. The pastel façades with baroque stuccowork, wrought-iron balconies and carriage gates are a legacy of the 18th century, when Croatian nobility turned Varaždin into their own 'Little Vienna.' The town is regarded as one of the best-preserved in the region.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

If you're here in late August, the Špancirfest turns the old town into an open-air stage for ten days. Street performers, concerts, shows and food stalls draw up to 300,000 visitors — it's the biggest street festival in Croatia. Book accommodation well in advance if you plan to attend.

⏱ 120 min

Try the klipič, a local brioche-style bread, paired with regional honey. The pastry shops in the centre also serve varaždinski kuglof, a ring-shaped spice cake traditionally baked for major family celebrations.

⏱ 30 min

Bike rental shops offer wheels to explore the surrounding countryside. The roads follow the Drava eastward, past vineyards and baroque castles in the Zagorje hills, nearly reaching the Hungarian border on flat, easy terrain.

⏱ 120 min

Hercer Palace houses an entomological collection of more than 10,000 specimens from around the world, displayed under the name World of Insects. Quirky and fascinating — and very well designed for children.

⏱ 45 min

Varazdin

★★★★☆
1

Forty kilometres northwest of Varaždin, Trakošćan Castle emerges from the forest like something out of a fairy tale. Its crenellated silhouette reflected in a man-made lake is one of the most iconic images of Continental Croatia. The medieval castle was entirely redesigned in the Romantic neo-Gothic style in the 19th century by Juraj Drašković, who was passionate about English architecture. Arrive early in the morning to have the lake to yourself.

⏱ 75 min
2

Inside, the rooms are furnished with period pieces: 16th-century suits of armour, family portraits, a Victorian library and neo-Gothic furniture assembled over five generations by the Drašković family. The private chapel holds the family's marble tombs. It's one of the rare Croatian aristocratic residences to have survived both World Wars with its furnishings intact.

⏱ 60 min
3

The English-style gardens slope down to the artificial lake, created in 1853 by diverting a stream. Populated by swans, lined with weeping willows and forest paths, it's an ideal setting for a stroll. Rowing boats are available for hire from the jetty during the warmer months.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The 63 hectares of forest surrounding the castle are laced with well-marked trails that are easy to follow. The full loop around the lake takes about an hour on foot and offers the finest views of the crenellated towers, especially from the southern shore. In autumn, the beech and oak trees put on a spectacular display of colour.

⏱ 75 min

Picnic by the lake or settle into the castle restaurant for traditional Croatian cooking with a view of the towers. The homemade cakes are worth the stop — a leisurely lunch here is a well-earned reward after the visit.

⏱ 60 min

Krapina

★★★☆☆
1

The Krapina Neanderthal Museum is an architectural surprise: a prize-winning contemporary building of sweeping concrete and glass curves, set into the wooded hillside. Inside, life-size reconstructions and interactive exhibits retrace the daily life of the Neanderthals discovered here in 1899 — remains of at least 80 individuals, making it one of the richest Neanderthal sites ever excavated. Allow an hour and a half.

⏱ 90 min
2

Climb Hušnjakovo Hill just behind the museum. This is where Dragutin Gorjanović-Kramberger conducted his excavations between 1899 and 1905, unearthing bones, flaked tools and hearth traces 130,000 years old. Life-size Neanderthal sculptures line the forest trail — some are strikingly realistic.

⏱ 45 min
3

Fifteen kilometres south of Krapina, the Basilica of Marija Bistrica is Croatia's main Marian sanctuary, drawing 800,000 pilgrims a year. The Black Madonna venerated here was hidden behind a wall during the Ottoman wars — for more than 200 years — before being rediscovered. The monumental Way of the Cross climbing the hill behind the church features fourteen bronze stations by sculptor Stjepan Skoko.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Take to the Zagorje back roads by car — this is some of the most bucolic driving in Croatia. Rolling green hills draped in vineyards, small baroque castles on every ridge, villages of painted wooden houses. Stop at a family cellar to taste Pušipel, a light, fruity local white wine, paired with smoked charcuterie and farmhouse cheese. Zagorje hospitality is the real thing.

⏱ 90 min

Make a detour to Veliki Tabor Castle, a 15th-century pentagonal fortress commanding the landscape from its hilltop. Its distinctive semi-circular bastions are unique in Croatia. Legend has it that Veronika of Desinić, sentenced to death by her father-in-law, was walled up inside and still haunts the towers. Concerts and festivals are held here in summer.

⏱ 60 min

End the day at the thermal baths of Krapinske Toplice, six kilometres from Krapina. The sulphurous hot springs (38–40°C) have been frequented since Roman times for their benefits on joint pain. The outdoor pools, open in summer, look straight out onto the forested hills.

⏱ 90 min

Samobor

★★★☆☆
1

Samobor's main square is one of the prettiest in Croatia: pastel Austro-Hungarian façades, shaded café terraces, a fountain and the town hall clock. It's a manageable place — 15,000 inhabitants — and that's precisely its charm. Grab a seat on a terrace with a coffee and watch the Samoborians go about their day.

⏱ 30 min
2

The old castle ruins crown a wooded spur above the town, reached by a steep 20-minute trail from the square. The medieval fortress has been crumbling since the earthquake of 1800, but the panorama from the top is spectacular on a clear day: you can make out the Slovenian Alps and the Zagorje hills. Sturdy shoes recommended.

⏱ 60 min
3

The City Museum traces Samobor's history from its 13th-century origins through the Biedermeier period, when the town became a favourite retreat for Zagreb's bourgeoisie. The collections include painted furniture, folk costumes and locally produced crystal glassware from as late as the 20th century.

⏱ 45 min
4

St Anastasia's is a 17th-century Franciscan foundation, recognisable by its cream-and-ochre baroque façade. Inside, stucco, paintings and gilded altars accumulate generously. The Franciscans still hold services here. If the church is closed when you arrive, it generally opens before and after the morning Mass.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Samobor's kremšnita is a custard-and-whipped-cream millefeuille — a speciality going back to the 19th century that every pastry shop on the square claims as the original. No need to pick a winner: try several. The Gradska Kavana on the main square is the oldest and best known.

⏱ 30 min

Žumberak Nature Park, a 15-minute drive away, is a forested massif shared between Croatia and Slovenia. Marked trails, waterfalls, mountain huts and viewpoints over the plain: local operators offer half-day guided hikes for all fitness levels.

⏱ 180 min

If you're here in February, Samobor Carnival is the oldest in Croatia — it has been held since 1827. Masked parades, brass bands in the lanes and the ritual burial of Princ Fašnik cap the festivities in a lively medieval atmosphere.

⏱ 120 min

Samobor muštarda is a local condiment made from mustard and candied fruit — Dijon mustard meets chutney, essentially. It goes perfectly with cold meats and cheese. The speciality shops on the square stock several varieties to take home.

⏱ 20 min

Five kilometres north, the village of Rude is home to former iron-crystal mines worked since the 18th century. The site has been converted into an underground geological attraction: guided tours descend into the galleries and explain the extraction methods. Original and uncrowded.

⏱ 60 min

Karlovac

★★★☆☆
1

Karlovac is a town best read from the sky: built in 1579 to the designs of military engineer Martin Gamboni, it forms a perfect six-pointed star, engineered to withstand Ottoman assaults. 2

3

4

Activities

In summer, the Foginovo river beach on the Korana is where the locals go: clear water, shaded lawns, casual eateries on the bank. Nothing formal — just a beautiful river and people making the most of it.

⏱ 90 min

Sisak

★★★☆☆
1

Sisak's triangular fortress is one of the most singular military monuments in Croatia: built between 1544 and 1550 with three bastions linked by curtain walls, it was the scene on 22 June 1593 of the decisive victory over the Ottomans — the first major defeat of the Ottoman army in Central Europe, which stopped its advance toward Vienna in its tracks. ⏱ 60 min
2

The City Museum holds exceptional pieces — in particular the Helmet of Sisak, a partially gilded bronze parade helmet pulled from the River Kupa.

⏱ 60 min
3

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Twenty kilometres southeast, Lonjsko Polje Nature Park protects the largest wetland in the Balkans, designated as a Ramsar site. The drive from Čigoč to Krapje is one of the most beautiful in Croatia in spring.

⏱ 180 min

The village of Čigoč is officially the 'European Capital of Storks' — the stork population regularly outnumbers the human one. An ethnographic museum occupies a traditional oak house. Come in May or June to see them nesting — by September they've left for Africa.

⏱ 60 min

Operators run boat trips from Sisak's river port to the confluence of the Kupa and Sava rivers. An hour on the water through a landscape of meadows and alluvial forest, utterly peaceful.

Slavonski Brod

★★★☆☆
1

Brod Fortress is the largest baroque defensive work in Croatia — and one of the best preserved in the Balkans. Its star-shaped bastions spread over 30 hectares along the Sava, built by the Habsburgs between 1715 and 1780 to hold the Ottoman frontier. The scale is impressive: walking the ramparts, you have Bosnia-Herzegovina directly opposite and the Sava flowing five metres below.

⏱ 75 min
2

The Korzo is the central pedestrian street, lined with elegant 19th-century Austro-Hungarian façades. In the late afternoon, it becomes a korzo in the true sense: locals stroll back and forth, stop at cafés, chat. A Mediterranean habit imported by the Habsburg administration and thoroughly adopted.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Regional Museum offers two collections worth a stop: archaeology spanning sites from prehistory to the Middle Ages discovered along the Sava, and traditional Slavonian embroidery. The latter has been inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list since 2017 under the name Brodsko kolo.

⏱ 45 min
4

If you're here in June, the Brodsko kolo is a spectacle on a staggering scale: up to 5,000 dancers in embroidered costumes from across Slavonia fill the streets for three days. ⏱ 120 min

⏱ 60 min

Kulen is the pride of Slavonian cuisine: a coarsely chopped pork sausage spiced with red paprika and cured over winter. Every butcher has a jealously guarded family recipe going back generations. Ask to taste before buying — the heat levels vary significantly.

⏱ 30 min

The Sava riverbank walk at sunset is a local ritual. The landscaped banks draw fishermen, cyclists and families. Views of the Bosnian hills across the water, low-angled light on the river — a fine moment to pause.

Osijek

★★★☆☆
1

The Tvrđa is a near-intact baroque citadel, built by the Habsburgs between 1712 and 1721 after retaking Osijek from the Ottomans. Its ochre-and-yellow barracks frame Holy Trinity Square in exceptional condition — no 20th-century war touched this quarter. Today, the former military buildings house the university, cafés and galleries. It's one of the best-preserved baroque ensembles in Central Europe.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Museum of Slavonia, founded in 1877 and therefore one of the oldest in Croatia, is housed in a baroque building within the Tvrđa. Its archaeological collections include Roman, medieval and Ottoman finds from across the region. The ethnographic displays on the ground floor showcase the full intricacy of Slavonian embroidered costumes.

⏱ 60 min
3

In the centre of Tvrđa Square, the Plague Column commemorates the 1729 epidemic that decimated the population. The Holy Trinity crowns the top, patron saints adorn the faces — Austrian baroque planted in the heart of what had been a war zone only years earlier. It's one of the most expressive monuments of Habsburg history in Slavonia.

⏱ 15 min
4

The Co-Cathedral of Saints Peter and Paul soars to 90 metres — the second-tallest spire in Croatia after Zagreb. Built between 1894 and 1900 in the neo-Gothic style, its interior holds stained glass and frescoes by Croatian painter Mirko Rački, covering an impressive surface area. Admission is free.

⏱ 45 min
5

Europska Avenija is a Belle Époque boulevard linking the Tvrđa to the modern city centre. Its turn-of-the-century Art Nouveau mansions — decorated façades, bay windows, ceramic detailing — reflect the prosperity of Osijek at a time when the city was one of the most dynamic in the Habsburg Empire.

⏱ 30 min
6

The pedestrian suspension bridge over the Drava offers a unique perspective on the fortress and the quays. Lit up at night, it draws evening strollers. Below, the landscaped banks welcome cyclists and fishermen.

⏱ 20 min

⏱ 150 min

The Baranja region, north of Osijek between the Drava and the Danube, produces Graševina white and Frankovka red wines. Operators organise tastings in the area's cellars, accompanied by local paprika-laced specialities. Ask for the Baranja Graševina first — it's the wine of this region.

⏱ 90 min

Don't leave Osijek without trying fiš paprikaš — a river-fish stew (carp, zander, catfish) cooked with paprika in a copper cauldron over an open fire. It's served in čarde, the rustic riverside inns. This is the signature dish of Slavonia, and it should be eaten here and nowhere else.

⏱ 90 min

Bike hire shops offer wheels for riding along the Drava levees. The cycle path runs between meadows and alluvial forest for kilometres — storks, herons and fishermen share this flat, restful landscape. Perfect in the late afternoon.

Akovo

★★☆☆☆
1

St Peter's Cathedral in Đakovo is visible from the plain for miles around. Its twin spires reach 84 metres, flanking a 90-metre-long nave — the largest church in Slavonia and one of the biggest neo-Romanesque buildings in Central Europe. Commissioned by Bishop Josip Juraj Strossmayer in 1866, it was completed in 1882 by Viennese architects Friedrich Schmidt and Alexander Seitz.

⏱ 60 min
2

Step inside and look up: the vaults, apses and side aisles are entirely covered with biblical frescoes painted between 1888 and 1896 by Maximilian and Alexander Seitz. This complete iconographic programme — from Genesis to Revelation — ranks among the greatest achievements of 19th-century religious painting in Europe. Take the time to sit down and absorb it all.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Đakovo Stud Farm has been breeding Lipizzaner horses since 1506 — one of the oldest studs in Europe, predating even Vienna's famous Spanish Riding School. These elegant white horses are the product of centuries of selective breeding. Guided tours of the stables are available, and equestrian demonstrations take place on Friday mornings in summer.

⏱ 75 min
4

The Diocesan Museum, accessible through the bishop's palace behind the cathedral, displays liturgical goldwork, embroidered vestments and manuscripts from this thousand-year-old episcopal seat. Strossmayer's correspondence with intellectuals such as Gladstone and Rački is particularly illuminating for understanding the bishop's political role in the Croatian national revival.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The Đakovački vezovi in early July is Slavonia's flagship folk festival: three days of flower-decked floats, Lipizzaner riders in historical dress and 3,000 dancers in embroidered costumes from across the region. If you're planning your trip for July, time your visit to coincide with it.

⏱ 120 min

Fifteen kilometres from Đakovo, the Kutjevo estate has been producing Graševina ever since Cistercian monks planted their first vineyard here in 1232. The medieval cellars are still in use. Operators arrange tastings by appointment in the vaulted underground galleries — it's one of the most authentic wine experiences in Croatia.

Vukovar

★★☆☆☆
1

Eltz Castle is an 18th-century baroque mansion restored after the 1991 war. The City Museum housed inside traces Vukovar's history from prehistory through the siege: 87 days of intensive shelling from summer to autumn 1991, which left this town among the most devastated in Europe since 1945. The exhibition pulls no punches — it's painful and essential.

⏱ 75 min
2

Walk along the Danube waterfront, rebuilt and lined with colourful façades. The river flows peacefully where fighting once raged — and it's precisely that dissonance that strikes you. Discreet plaques mark the buildings that were destroyed. Take time to sit on a terrace and simply watch the Danube.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Vukovar Hospital Memorial preserves the basement where doctors, nurses and patients survived 87 days of siege without running water or anaesthesia, operating by torchlight. It's one of the most powerful testimonies of the conflict. The guided tour lasts 45 minutes and is not recommended for children under 12.

⏱ 60 min
4

The Church of Saints Philip and James was 90% destroyed during the 1991 siege and rebuilt brick by brick following the original 18th-century baroque plans. It has become the symbol of Vukovar's rebirth — and one of the most meticulous reconstructions in recent European history.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

The Ovčara Memorial, five kilometres south of Vukovar, marks the spot where 264 wounded and hospital staff were executed on 20 November 1991 after the town fell. It's a place of sober, dignified remembrance — not a spectacular museum. Many visitors skip it, but many also regret not having gone.

⏱ 60 min

The memorial cemetery lines its rows of white crosses along the hills above the town. The national ceremony on 18 November draws thousands of people each year to commemorate the fall of Vukovar.

⏱ 30 min

Five kilometres north of Vukovar, the Vučedol Culture Museum occupies a prize-winning contemporary building constructed on the very site of the Copper Age civilisation that thrived here between 3000 and 2200 BC. Its curved architecture hugs the cliff above the Danube. Don't miss the Vučedol Dove, a 5,000-year-old ritual ceramic that has become the symbol of Vukovar and of peace — the original is here, not in the Zagreb Museum.

Ilok

★★★☆☆
1

Ilok is Croatia's easternmost town, at the tip of Slavonia where the country tapers to a point between the Danube and Serbia. The medieval castle crowns the promontory and commands a breathtaking view over the river and the Vojvodina hills beyond. The princely cellars carved into the ramparts are still in use — more on that later in the day.

⏱ 45 min
2

The Church of St John of Capistrano houses the sarcophagus of the Franciscan friar Giovanni da Capestrano, who preached a crusade against the Ottomans here in 1455 and died in Ilok in 1456. He was canonised in 1690 and his tomb remains a regular pilgrimage site. For the historical weight of the figure, it's one of Croatia's least-known monuments.

⏱ 30 min
3

The City Museum presents Ilok's multicultural history — Croatian, Serbian, German and Hungarian over the centuries. The collection of costumes and crafts from the successive communities that lived here is particularly rich.

⏱ 45 min
4

Ilok's medieval walls offer a panoramic walk above the Danube. Restored watchtowers and bastions testify to the town's strategic past — and the view across to Serbia from the battlements is striking.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

The Iločki Podrumi cellars extend for four kilometres of galleries dug into the limestone beneath the ramparts — one of the largest underground wine facilities in the Balkans. Wines have been ageing here since 1450. Guided tastings are held in the galleries: bring a sweater, as the temperature stays a constant 12°C year-round.

⏱ 90 min

Ilok's Traminac is a powerful, aromatic white wine — a Gewurztraminer varietal cultivated here for centuries. Its international reputation dates from the coronation of Queen Elizabeth II in 1953, for which several hundred bottles were ordered. It remains one of Croatia's finest bottles.

⏱ 30 min

The Danube riverbanks in Ilok are laid out for walking and cycling. You'll see cargo barges heading upstream toward Central Europe and the Serbian hills sloping down to the water on the far side. A border landscape, calm and wistful.

⏱ 45 min

Pozega

★★☆☆☆
1

St Theresa Square in Požega is one of the finest baroque ensembles in Slavonia: ochre-and-beige 18th-century façades, an ornate Plague Column in the centre, shaded arcades. This is a town sheltered from the plain, tucked behind hills — which earned it the nickname 'Little Florence of Slavonia.' Start with a coffee on a terrace to soak up the pace.

⏱ 30 min
2

The Church of St Teresa of Ávila is Požega's richest monument: the Carmelite order has been active here since 1701, and the lavish baroque interior — gilded altars with twisted columns, 18th-century frescoes, relief stuccowork — is in remarkable condition.

⏱ 30 min
3

The City Museum occupies a baroque palace and covers the archaeology, ethnography and winemaking tradition of the valley. Don't miss the hall devoted to Požega embroidery — different from Osijek's, more restrained and geometric.

⏱ 45 min
4

The 17th-century Jesuit college — now a secondary school — is a reminder that Požega was a significant intellectual centre under Habsburg rule, home to one of the region's earliest institutions of higher learning.

⏱ 15 min
5

Climb to St Roch's Chapel on the hilltop above the town. The walk takes 20 minutes from the centre. From the top, the panorama sweeps across the entire Požega Valley — vineyards, orchards, villages — with the Papuk massif as a backdrop.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

The Kutjevo estate is 15 kilometres east. The Cistercians planted their first vineyard here in 1232 and the medieval cellars are still operational. Kutjevo's Graševina regularly wins international awards. Tastings are arranged by appointment in the vaulted galleries — one of the most authentic wine experiences in Croatia.

⏱ 120 min

Papuk Nature Park is a UNESCO geopark of beech and oak forests, waterfalls and geological formations 350 million years old — some of the oldest rocks in Croatia. Operators offer guided hikes along marked trails to the ruined castle of Ružica, perched on a rocky spur at 459 metres.

⏱ 180 min

Farm tables in the hills around Požega serve homemade kulen, farmhouse cheese and bread baked over a wood fire. This is the best place to eat Slavonian — not in a restaurant, but in a farmyard with a glass of local wine.

Nasice

★★☆☆☆
1

Pejačević Castle in Našice is a handsome neoclassical mansion from the 19th century, set in a 30-hectare English-style park. The Pejačević family received Europe's cultural and political elite here until 1918 — Liszt is said to have stayed. The building is in excellent condition and the park, planted with century-old trees, is especially pleasant in summer.

⏱ 45 min
2

The museum inside the castle displays period furniture, family portraits and, above all, the work of Dora Pejačević (1885–1923), a Croatian composer who wrote symphonies, quartets and art songs in a post-Romantic style influenced by Mahler and Strauss. She was the first significant female composer in the region — and remains far too little known outside Croatia.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Church of St Anthony of Padua, founded and funded by the Pejačević family in the 18th century, holds the family tombs and a carefully maintained baroque interior of elegant proportions.

⏱ 20 min
4

The Franciscan Monastery in Našice is a 15th-century foundation, burned and rebuilt several times after the Ottoman wars. The adjoining Church of the Assumption is the oldest in town.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

A festival devoted to Dora Pejačević's music is held every autumn in her family castle. Chamber-music concerts are performed in the historic salons — a rare chance to hear her works in the very place where she composed them.

⏱ 120 min

The wooded hills around Našice — oak groves and vineyards — offer fine walks with few other visitors. The local nobility used to hunt here in the 19th century; today operators offer walking and cycling outings along the old paths.

Zagreb

★★★★☆
1

Start this final day at the Banski Dvori on St Mark's Square — the presidential palace that has housed the Croatian government since independence in 1991. Guards in full dress uniform perform the ceremonial changing of the guard at set times: ask at your hotel for the schedule so you don't miss it.

⏱ 20 min
2

The Museum of Contemporary Art (MSU), in Novi Zagreb, is the largest contemporary art museum in the Balkans. Igor Franić's building, inaugurated in 2009, is itself a work of art: 17,000 m² of white concrete and glass. The permanent collections cover Croatian and international art from 1950 onward — with particular attention to the Yugoslav New Tendency artists of the 1960s.

⏱ 90 min
3

Jarun Lakes, four kilometres from the centre, are Zagreb's urban beaches. Two large man-made lakes, cycle paths, floating bars and a fine-gravel beach. Locals come to swim, row and wakeboard. This is the city at its most laid-back.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

For your last shopping in Zagreb, look for Croatian crafts in the Upper Town galleries: the necktie (invented in Croatia in the 17th century — literally), silver filigree, figurines from the Hlebine naïve art tradition, or plum liqueurs. Arena Zagreb shopping centre (Novi Zagreb) offers more mainstream alternatives.

⏱ 90 min

Wrap up the trip with dinner in Zagreb's evolving food scene. Croatian chefs are reinventing Slavonian and Dalmatian cuisine with local produce. Book ahead for restaurants in the historic centre — the best tables fill up from Thursday evening.

⏱ 120 min

Before you leave, have one last coffee on Ban Jelačić Square. Watch the blue trams loop around and the Zagrebians criss-crossing in every direction — Zagreb remains a capital on a human scale, 800,000 people, and that's what makes it so likeable.
