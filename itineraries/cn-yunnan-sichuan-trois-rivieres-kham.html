This is the most politically complex route in the Yunnan-Tibet circuit: confirm all permits 30 days before departure.

Shangri La

★★★★☆
1

Touch down in Shangri-La (formerly Zhongdian) at 3,280 m altitude—this is your base camp for the journey ahead. If you're flying in from Kunming, dedicate the day to gentle acclimatization and preparing your 4x4 for the 13 days to come. Walk minimally, hydrate constantly, eat light—your body needs 24 hours to adjust.

⏱ 60 min
2

Head 5 km north to Songzanlin, nicknamed the "Little Potala" of Yunnan. Built in 1679 on the orders of the 5th Dalai Lama, it's Yunnan's largest Gelugpa monastery and one of the thirteen great monasteries of Kham. Today it houses over 600 monks. Arrive at opening (8 AM) to catch the tranquility and morning chants before tour buses flood the staircases.

⏱ 120 min
3

Wander into Dukezong Old Town, devastated by a 2014 fire that destroyed two-thirds of its wooden houses. It's since been rebuilt to match the original. Climb to the giant golden prayer wheel perched on Guishan Hill—21 m tall, 60 tons—it takes several people to turn, and it's the quintessential local experience.

⏱ 90 min
4

If you've got energy to spare, loop through Pudacuo National Park, 22 km east. China's first IUCN-standard national park (2007), it shelters two alpine lakes (Shudu and Bita) above 3,500 m, virgin forests, and a 13 km boardwalk circling the lakes. Feel free to skip if you arrive late from Kunming.

⏱ 180 min

Activities

Come evening, descend to Dukezong's main street for yak hot pot (Mongolian-style fondue with yak meat), spicy Tibetan potatoes (tudou si), and salted butter tea. One bowl will suffice if it's your first encounter—it's an acquired taste.

⏱ 90 min

Drink plenty of water and avoid alcohol tonight: altitude is beginning to bite, and poor sleep here will handicap the days ahead. If you're taking preventive medication like acetazolamide (Diamox), follow your doctor's dosage and timing—not a guidebook's.

⏱ 30 min

Benzilan

★★☆☆☆
1

Stop at the First Bend of the Yangtze viewpoint, one of China's most startling geographic marvels. The river, charging south, slams into bedrock and executes a 180° reversal northward. The official lookout sits 15 km north of Benzilan—it's the only spot with an unobstructed view of the entire loop; elsewhere you'll catch only half. Visit in spring or autumn for turquoise water; summer rains turn it muddy.

⏱ 45 min
2

Climb 23 km north of Benzilan to Dongzhulin Monastery, clinging to the slope at 3,000 m. Founded in 1667 under Emperor Kangxi and named "Gaden Döndrubling" by the 5th Dalai Lama in 1676, it ranks among the thirteen great Gelugpa monasteries of Kham. The current structure, rebuilt in 1985 after Cultural Revolution destruction, rises four storeys with 82 carved wooden pillars. On the third floor, you'll find two Maitreya and Sakyamuni Buddhas standing 7 and 10 m tall. Far less crowded than Songzanlin.

⏱ 75 min
3

Beyond Benzilan, you tackle the G214 toward Baima Pass (4,292 m), one of the highest crossings between Yunnan and Mount Meili. The vegetation shifts abruptly here: virgin spruce forests, rhododendrons, prayer flags. Pause at 4,000 m to watch for Himalayan griffons—this zone is one of the last refuges for red pandas and clouded leopards.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

In Benzilan village, try your hand at carving a Tibetan wooden bowl. This crucial Tea-Horse Road stopover was the last village before the snowy passes to Lhasa. Budget 1 to 2 hours, around 100 yuan—you leave with your own bowl.

Feilai Si

★★★★☆
1

Drop your bags at Feilai Si, perched at 3,470 m facing Mount Meili—one of Tibetan Buddhism's great pilgrimage sites. The temple was built in 1614 during the Ming dynasty, and legend says a meditating Buddha saw another Buddha fly from India and land here, hence the name (Flying Temple). It's tiny, but it's the antechamber to Kawagebo. Book a room facing the mountain—the view from bed is worth every yuan.

⏱ 60 min
2

Look up at Mount Meili (Kawa Karpo in Tibetan), unfurling 13 peaks above 6,000 m. The principal summit, Kawagebo, tops out at 6,740 m—Yunnan's highest peak and one of the eight most sacred mountains in Tibetan Buddhism. In 1991, a Sino-Japanese expedition lost all 17 members in an avalanche: since 2001, Chinese law has banned climbing to respect the mountain's sacred status. No one has ever stood on the summit.

⏱ 60 min
3

If clouds are blocking Feilai Si at sunrise, ask your driver to race 15 km south to the Wunongding viewing platform ("Foggy Peak"). The angle captures a wider panorama of all 13 peaks, and you'll see the 13 white Tibetan stupas aligned facing the massif. Far less crowded at dawn, and sometimes light breaks through here when it doesn't at Feilai Si.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Turn in early: tomorrow means a 5:30 AM wake-up for "Golden Sunlight on the Sacred Mountain" (Ri Zhao Jin Shan in Chinese), the phenomenon where the first golden ray strikes Kawagebo's summit and descends across all 13 peaks in a matter of minutes. It lasts only 5 to 10 minutes. The mountain reveals itself rarely: 30-50% visibility in dry season, 10-20% in summer.

⏱ 30 min

Pack down jacket, gloves, and beanie for dawn: at 3,470 m, it easily hits -5°C before sunrise, even in June. Several hotels rent oxygen bottles for 30 yuan—grab one if you're feeling headaches or nausea upon arrival.

⏱ 15 min

Feilai Si

★★★★☆
1

Follow the road 26 km south to Mingyong village, gateway to China's lowest-altitude glacier. The glacier descends from Kawagebo to 2,600 m—one of the rare oceanic monsoon glaciers in the world. Stretching 11.7 km, it crosses five vegetation zones, from tundra to deciduous forest. The access trail starts from the village (8 km, 4-hour round trip) and reaches Taizi Temple at 2,720 m. Don't step onto the ice—it's unstable.

⏱ 240 min
2

Stop repeatedly between Feilai Si and Mingyong to photograph the Lancang Jiang (Mekong) valley. The elevation drop between the road and the river reaches 4,700 m opposite Kawagebo—you're crossing one of the world's deepest gorges, part of the UNESCO "Three Parallel Rivers" site inscribed in 2003.

⏱ 30 min
3

At the glacier's foot, 2,720 m elevation, you'll encounter Taizi Temple marking the end of the observation trail. A small Tibetan Buddhist temple, stationed here to bless the sacred glacier. Pilgrims burn juniper as an offering to Kawagebo—you can buy a bundle at the temple for 5 yuan and join the ritual.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Take your time on the Mingyong trail: light shifts every hour, and you might cross paths with Tibetan pilgrims making the kora (ritual circumambulation) around Kawagebo. The full pilgrimage lasts 14 days, 240 km, crossing several passes at 4,800 m.

⏱ 60 min

Back in Feilai Si, grab butter tea at one of the many Tibetan guesthouses lining the main street. It's a chance to chat with yak drivers who haul supplies up to Yubeng—you're heading there tomorrow for the trek, so get their advice on trail conditions.

Yubeng

★★★★☆
1

Launch the big trek from Xidang (2,700 m), an hour's drive from Feilai Si. Depending on road conditions, you'll hike in on foot (5-7 hours, 18 km, 1,100 m elevation gain) or catch an official SUV shuttle to Lower Yubeng (45 minutes, 30 yuan). The trail passes through Nanzheng Pass at 3,729 m, the climb's high point. Factor in oxygen breaks—you're above 3,500 m for half the journey.

⏱ 60 min
2

Welcome to Yubeng, two Tibetan villages revealing themselves: Upper Yubeng (3,200 m) and Lower Yubeng (3,100 m), linked by a 30-minute trail. Roadless until recently, they've preserved traditional Tibetan wood-and-stone architecture. Around thirty families live here, most having opened their homes to trekkers. You're on the kora around Kawagebo, a major pilgrimage circuit.

⏱ 90 min
3

Detour to the small Yubeng Temple (Yubeng Si), which shelters the tooth-shaped rock (Sacred Tooth Rock) said to have given the village its name. According to Tibetan legend, it represents a divine molar that fell from a protective deity of Kawagebo. Pilgrims touch it before departing for the sacred waterfall the next day—do as they do.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Once settled, grab a ginger tea at a guesthouse café—"Hi I'm Joe," run by a Hongkonger and a local, even serves Hong Kong-style desserts. Rest early—tomorrow you're climbing to the glacial lake at 3,900 m.

⏱ 60 min

Eat light Tibetan fare tonight: tsampa (roasted barley flour), potato soup, momos. Avoid grease and alcohol—altitude doubles the effect.

Yubeng

★★★★☆
1

Tackle Yubeng's toughest hike, Glacier Lake (Bing Hu, 3,900 m): 12 km round trip, 700 m elevation gain, 6 to 8 hours with breaks. The trail departs Upper Yubeng, cuts through spruce forest, follows the glacial torrent, and ends in a high-altitude basin. The water is turquoise, the far shore a 200 m ice wall. Start at 7 AM, pack a warm lunch.

⏱ 360 min
2

Halfway up, you'll reach Xiaonong Base Camp (3,623 m), the former campsite of the 1991 Sino-Japanese expedition. A memorial commemorates the 17 climbers lost in the avalanche. It's also a good spot to catch your breath, eat, and watch marmots emerge into the sun.

⏱ 30 min
3

At the lake, listen: you'll hear water flowing under the ice. Walk carefully along the edges (never onto the central ice—hidden crevasses lurk). Above, a hanging glacier clings to Kawagebo: occasionally low rumbles echo through the basin—those are seracs collapsing.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Upon return to Upper Yubeng, schedule a hearty, early dinner (6-7 PM). Night falls fast at 3,100 m, and after 8 hours of walking, you'll be in bed by 9 PM without struggle.

⏱ 60 min

If you've got energy left, catch the sunset on Mainri (one of the 13 peaks) from the meadow above the village, a 5-minute walk. Yaks come home at this hour—it's cinematic.

Feilai Si

★★★★☆
1

Attack the Sacred Waterfall (Shen Pu, 3,700 m), easier than Glacier Lake: 12 km round trip, 600 m elevation gain, 4 to 6 hours. The trail departs Lower Yubeng, follows the river, runs through a valley strung with prayer flags. For Tibetan pilgrims, the water falling here is sacred—they circle three times beneath the cascade to purify themselves of sins. You can do the same, but the water's 4-5°C.

⏱ 240 min
2

Exit via the Ninong trail, more scenic than the Xidang route you took in: 18 km descent, 5 to 6 hours, hugging the Mekong through an impressive canyon. Once in Ninong, catch a shuttle back to your 4x4 at Feilai Si (1 hour). Your legs will hurt tomorrow—guaranteed.

⏱ 60 min
3

Between the waterfall and Lower Yubeng, you'll pass several small altars and chortens dotting the path. Pilgrims leave a stone, a flag, a ribbon—you can do the same if you wish, it's an accepted gesture.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Evening in Feilai Si: hot shower (ultimate luxury after three days in guesthouses), hot meal. Hotels offer hot-stone massages for 100-150 yuan—do it, your calves will thank you.

Cizhong

★★☆☆☆
1

Cizhong's Catholic church is an improbable historical object: built in 1867 by the Paris Foreign Missions, burned down in 1905 during the Tibetan Uprising (two French missionaries killed), then rebuilt in 1909 in the current village. Architecture half European, half Chinese—push open the door, the nave is Romanesque, the bell tower capped with a Chinese pavilion. Designated a historical monument in 1987. Sunday Mass in Tibetan still draws around a hundred faithful.

⏱ 60 min
2

Circle the church: surrounding it, you'll find vineyards planted by the missionaries in 1867. Grape variety: "Rose Honey," introduced from France via Burma, extinct in Europe after the phylloxera epidemic of the late 19th century. Cizhong is now the only place on earth where this variety is still cultivated. Two families produce wine: Wu Hongxing's (next to the church) and the church authority itself.

⏱ 60 min
3

Stroll the village lanes (roughly 200 families, 60% Catholic Tibetan/Naxi/Lisu, 40% Buddhist). The two communities live in harmony: Christmas and Tibetan New Year are celebrated together. Spot crosses on lintels beside prayer flags—you won't see that anywhere else in China.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Knock on Wu Hongxing's door, next to the church, for a tasting. Two cuvées await: young Rose Honey (light, fruity) and late-harvest Rose Honey (syrupy, bouquet of rose and honey—hence the name). Budget 50-100 yuan for tasting, option to buy a bottle to take home.

⏱ 60 min

If you land on a Saturday evening, attend Mass in Tibetan at 7 PM. Father Yao, a Han Chinese, has said Mass in Tibetan since 2008: liturgical chants in Tibetan minor mode, women in traditional chuba, candles in the nave. One hour, stay discreet, keep a scarf to cover your head (for women).

⏱ 60 min

Deqin

★★☆☆☆
1

Spend a night in Deqin before crossing the TAR border tomorrow. The town, at 3,400 m, isn't spectacular in itself, but its position makes it the logical stop between Cizhong and Yanjing: the road ahead, along the Mekong, offers no decent lodging before Yanjing. Yanjing

★★★☆☆
1

Yanjing ("salt wells," Tsakalo in Tibetan) is one of the most extraordinary sites in eastern Tibet, administratively within Markam County (TAR). More than 3,000 salt basins suspended on wooden scaffolds cascade down both banks of the Lancang Jiang (Mekong) at 2,300 m altitude. Ancestral salt production: at least 1,300 years documented according to records. You'll see Naxi women draw brine from natural wells (saline springs trapped in the plateau when the ocean uplifted) and carry it in baskets to the clay basins.

⏱ 90 min
2

Rise early to see the women at work from 7 AM onward—by Naxi tradition, men sell, women produce. About 25 families remain active across the 2,775 registered basins. White salt (food-grade) and red salt (livestock and tea) come from the same well but are harvested in different seasons. Annual production: 1.5 million kg, generating roughly 1.3 million yuan in revenue.

⏱ 60 min
3

Cross the bridge to Jiada, on the east bank—here's where you access the upper basins. The paths between basins are narrow and slippery; ask the Naxi women for permission before walking among their vats. Bring a few yuan to buy red salt in sachets (5-10 yuan)—it's the quintessential local souvenir.

⏱ 60 min
4

Push open the door of Tibet's only Catholic church, founded in 1855 by two French missionaries: 80% of Upper Yanjing's population is Catholic Tibetan. You'll see residents alternating crucifixes and prayer flags, just like in Cizhong. Small Mass every Sunday at 9 AM.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Sample Jiajia noodles, Yanjing's specialty. Small bowls of barley noodles, minced meat, local red salt—the goal being to down as many as possible. The count is kept with pebbles you add to your table with each bowl. The local record exceeds 100. For you, 7-8 will suffice amply.

⏱ 60 min

Your Tibet permit will be checked for the first time here: the border checkpoint between TAR and Yunnan sits at Yanjing's southern entrance. Without a valid permit, you'll be turned back toward Cizhong and Yunnan. With one, you continue toward Markam tomorrow.

⏱ 30 min

Markham

★★☆☆☆
1

Markham (Mangkang in Chinese) is the official crossroads between the G214 (Yunnan-Tibet) and G318 (Sichuan-Tibet) highways. At 4,371 m altitude, you'll find a modest town: local market, monastery, and registration of your permit to continue through the TAR. Without a permit, you'd have been stopped at Yanjing—here they simply confirm your passage.

⏱ 60 min
2

Stop at Hongla Pass, 4,376 m—here you cross from the Mekong valley to the Yangtze (Jinsha Jiang) valley. From there, the G318 plunges toward Batang over 110 km. Prayer flags, stupas, 360° mountain views. You'll understand then that you're truly on the roof of the world.

⏱ 30 min
3

Slip into Markham's Gelugpa monastery on the town outskirts—it hosts around twenty monks. Small, unassuming, but you'll attend morning chants at 6 AM without a single tourist. Ask your driver to drop you there the evening before.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Eat Tibetan at a Markham canteen: thukpa (yak noodle soup), steamed momos, butter tea. Budget 30-40 yuan per person. It's rustic but filling after the drive.

⏱ 60 min

Turn in early tonight: altitude is starting to wear you down (4,371 m), drink 3 liters of water throughout the day, absolutely avoid alcohol, and prep for a full driving day tomorrow to Batang (170 km, 4-5 hours on the G318).

Batang

★★★☆☆
1

Departing Markham, you cross the bridge over the Jinsha Jiang—the administrative border between TAR and Sichuan. From here on, no more Tibet permit required; you're officially in Sichuan, within the Tibetan autonomous prefecture of Garzê (Kham Tibet). Batang at 2,560 m is the lowest altitude since Shangri-La—your body will appreciate it.

⏱ 30 min
2

Visit Batang Chode Monastery (Ba Chode), a large Gelugpa complex founded in the 15th century at 2,600 m. Destroyed during the Cultural Revolution and rebuilt in the 1990s, it houses around a hundred monks, morning chants at 7 AM. Far less visited than famous sites—you'll likely be alone.

⏱ 90 min
3

Wander Batang valley, which produces Sichuan's finest apricots: in May-June, the blossoming trees are spectacular. The town retains authentic Tibetan character—cobbled alleys, painted rammed-earth houses, little concrete. You'll hear many residents speaking Kham Tibetan rather than Mandarin.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

On the road between Markham and Batang, ask your driver to stop at Maoya Grasslands: 4,200 m altitude, herds of yaks and sheep, nomad tents. The word maoya means in Tibetan "flat as a bronze mirror." The horizon is without relief, creating a unique sense of vastness.

⏱ 60 min

Dine early and light: in Batang you're recovering from altitude after several days above 3,500 m—take advantage of it to sleep well. Tomorrow you climb back to Litang at 4,014 m; the contrast will be brutal.

⏱ 45 min

Litang

★★★★☆
1

Litang, at 4,014 m, is one of the world's highest towns. The historical capital of central Kham, founded in the 17th century, it's the birthplace of the 7th Dalai Lama (Kelsang Gyatso, born 1708). Tibetans say: "If you haven't been to Litang, you've seen nothing of Kham." Visit Changqingchun Ke'er Gelugpa monastery, founded in 1580 and housing around 1,200 monks. Ancient library, frescoes, open-air philosophical debates.

⏱ 120 min
2

Pause at Haizi Mountain (4,500 m) on the road between Batang and Litang—it unfurls the largest field of glacial remains on the Tibetan plateau, 1,145 lakes of all sizes in a lunar landscape of granite and tundra. Sister Lake, one of the most accessible, is visible from the road. Spend 30 minutes at the viewpoint—the panorama is well worth the stop.

⏱ 45 min
3

Wander Litang town, which has stayed thoroughly Tibetan: no Chinese signage, chubas (long felt coats) on every man, Khampas recognizable by their black hair braided with red thread. If you're here in early August, don't miss the Horse Racing Festival (August 10-15 annually): 1,000 costumed riders, archery at full gallop, nomad tents across the prairie. It's Tibet's largest nomadic festival.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

At 4,014 m, sleep with a window slightly ajar (oxygen thins at night), drink 2 to 3 liters of water, eat light. Walking through the market will literally leave you breathless—that's normal. No more than 5 km on foot during the day.

⏱ 30 min

Take a spin through the yak meat market southeast of the monastery, one of Tibetan Sichuan's most authentic: air-dried meat, fermented yak cheese, rancid butter for tea. It's instructive and photogenic, but the smell lingers.

⏱ 45 min

Riwa Yading

★★★☆☆
1

You've reached your destination: Yading Nature Reserve, 110 km south of Daocheng, nicknamed "the Last Shangri-La." Three sacred peaks above 6,000 m surround you: Chenrezig (Xiannairi, 6,032 m, the Buddha of Compassion), Jambeyang (Yangmaiyong, 5,958 m, the Buddha of Wisdom), and Chanadorje (Xianuoduoji, 5,958 m, the Buddha of Power). The three form a perfect triangle according to Tibetan topography. American botanist Joseph Rock explored it in the 1920s-30s for National Geographic, and his accounts are often said to have inspired James Hilton's novel "Lost Horizon" (1933)—a connection frequently mentioned though Hilton never confirmed it.

⏱ 90 min
2

Settle at the foot of Chenrezig, before Pearl Lake (Zhuoma La Cuo, 4,080 m) which perfectly mirrors the snowy peak in clear weather. 30-minute trail from the eco-bus station. It's the first of three sacred lakes you'll see today, the most accessible.

⏱ 60 min
3

Higher up, you climb to Luorong Pasture (4,180 m), then Milk Lake (Niuna Hai, 4,600 m, 4-hour round trip on foot) and Five-Color Lake (Wuse Hai, 4,700 m). Depending on light, the water takes on green, blue, violet, and red reflections. It's the circuit's hardest hike—attempt it only if you're acclimatized to altitude.

⏱ 240 min
4

Come evening, return to Riwa to sleep before the flight back tomorrow. If you're departing early for Chengdu, note that Daocheng Yading Airport (DCY) sits 50 km from downtown, perched at 4,411 m altitude. Opened September 16, 2013, it's the world's highest civilian airport. The 5,000 m² terminal is shaped like a flying saucer landed on the plateau. The DCY → Chengdu Tianfu (TFU) or Shuangliu (CTU) flight lasts 1h15, roughly 1,200 yuan, book 2 months ahead.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Yading entrance requires a ticket (150 yuan + eco-bus 120 yuan, totaling 270 yuan) and mandatory shuttle use. Buy a 2-day pass if you can: morning light and late afternoon light are the two best, and one day isn't enough if weather turns against you.

⏱ 30 min

Sleep in Riwa (rebranded Shangri-La Town of Daocheng), 28 km downstream from the reserve entrance, at 2,900 m. You'll recover better than at Yading itself (3,700 m). Comfortable hotels, Chinese and Tibetan restaurants. One last Sichuan hot pot, one last glass of Rose Honey wine if you brought some from Cizhong, and your journey ends.

⏱ 90 min
