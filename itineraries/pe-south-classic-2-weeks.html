📆 Best period

May, June, July, August, September

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 8 and 12 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Dry Season (May-September): Peak tourist season with minimal rainfall, clear skies, and excellent conditions for Machu Picchu and highland trekking. Highest prices but best weather.

Shoulder Season (April, October): Good weather with moderate crowds and prices. Excellent balance of conditions and value, still dry enough for most activities.

Wet Season (November-March): Higher rainfall, especially in highlands, but lush landscapes and fewer tourists. Inca Trail closed in February, coastal areas remain accessible.

Regional Variations: Coast has minimal rainfall year-round, highlands have distinct wet/dry seasons, Amazon rainforest hot and humid with variable precipitation.

🌍 What to discover in PE?

Incan heritage with Machu Picchu, Amazon rainforest, and incredible cuisine. Experience ancient civilizations, traditional cultures, and natural beauty. The country offers cultural immersion with adventure activities and culinary excellence. Peru provides transformative experiences with historical significance and natural wonders.

📋 Important notes

Book a domestic flight Arequipa-Cusco (D8→D9). The Ollantaytambo-Aguas Calientes leg is exclusively by train. Reserve Huayna Picchu or Montaña Machu Picchu in advance (strict quotas). Account for altitude in Cusco (11,150 ft / 3,400 m): plan a half-day acclimatization upon arrival.

⚠️ SAFETY NOTE: Peru is generally safe in tourist areas with good infrastructure along main tourist routes. Exercise standard precautions regarding petty crime in cities, avoid displaying valuables, use official transportation, and stay in established tourist zones. Some remote areas and certain Lima neighborhoods should be avoided.

Lima

Capital founded in 1535 where colonial catacombs meet Pacific cliffs and Barranco street art

★★★★★
📍 Parque del Amor Miraflores Lima📍 Costa Verde Miraflores Lima📍 Malecón de Miraflores Lima📍 Puente de los Suspiros Barranco Lima📍 Bajada de Baños Barranco Lima
1

Drop your bags and head straight down to the Malecón: the cliffside promenade runs along Miraflores' edge 230 feet above the Pacific, and it's the perfect antidote to jet lag. Walk toward Parque del Amor—couples gather around Víctor Delfín's sculpture El Beso (The Kiss)—and watch surfers tackle the Costa Verde waves below. Lima is the only South American capital with direct ocean access, and from these cliffs you grasp it instantly. Continue north toward Barranco to stretch your legs.

⏱ 75 min
2

In the heart of Miraflores, Huaca Pucllana rises between modern high-rises: a 15-acre adobe pyramid built by the Lima culture between 200 and 700 CE. The night tours are particularly striking—the site is illuminated and the terraces stand out sharply against the city darkness. Opt for the guided visit, which includes access to the small on-site museum. Allow 1.5 hours.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

In the evening, head to Barranco, Lima's bohemian quarter, about fifteen minutes on foot or by taxi from Miraflores. Cross the Bridge of Sighs, stroll down Bajada de Baños—the pedestrian avenue that descends toward the beach—and admire the street art murals covering the walls of old colonial mansions. Barranco has the highest concentration of artistic graffiti in Lima. The bars come alive around 8 pm: perfect for your first pisco sour.

Lima

Capital founded in 1535 where colonial catacombs meet Pacific cliffs and Barranco street art

★★★★★
📍 Plaza Mayor Lima centro histórico📍 Catedral de Lima Plaza Mayor📍 Convento San Francisco Lima catacombes📍 Museo Larco Lima Pueblo Libre📍 Parque Kennedy Miraflores Lima
1

Arrive at Plaza Mayor around noon: the guards parade in front of the Government Palace to the sound of El Cóndor Pasa, pigeons scatter with every drum roll, and the ochre-yellow facades gleam under the Pacific light. This is where Pizarro founded Lima on January 18, 1535—the baroque cathedral across the square houses his tomb. The historic center was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1991. Walk up the pedestrian street Jirón de la Unión to reach Plaza San Martín, less touristy and more lively on weekdays.

⏱ 60 min
2

A stone's throw from Plaza Mayor, the San Francisco convent is one of the best-preserved baroque buildings in Latin America. The current structure was consecrated in 1672, after an earthquake destroyed its predecessor in 1655. Don't miss the underground catacombs: used as a cemetery until 1808, they hold the remains of approximately 25,000 people, with bones arranged in geometric patterns in pits. The guided tour lasts about an hour—photography is forbidden inside.

⏱ 75 min
3

The Larco Museum, in the Pueblo Libre district, occupies an 18th-century viceregal mansion built atop a 7th-century pre-Columbian pyramid. Founded in 1926 by archaeologist Rafael Larco Hoyle, it holds over 45,000 pieces: Moche ceramics, Paracas textiles, Chimú goldwork. The pre-Columbian erotic gallery—a collection of Moche pottery illustrating these civilizations' ritual practices—is among the world's most famous. The bougainvillea gardens provide a pleasant setting for a break. The museum stays open until 10 pm—convenient for avoiding morning crowds. Allow at least 2 hours.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Before or after the Larco Museum, stop by Parque Kennedy in the heart of Miraflores: the park's cats have become a local institution. Artisans set up on weekends. It's also the ideal spot to try picarones—Peruvian doughnuts made with sweet potato and served with molasses sauce—from street vendors.

Paracas

Where desert plunges into the Pacific and Humboldt penguins share rocky islands with sea lions

★★☆☆☆
📍 Puerto El Chaco Paracas bateaux Ballestas📍 Islas Ballestas Paracas Pérou📍 Reserva Nacional de Paracas📍 Playa Roja Paracas📍 Candelabro Paracas géoglyphe
1

En route to the Ballestas Islands, the boat skirts the peninsula and passes the Paracas Candelabra—a 590-foot-tall geoglyph etched into a dune, attributed to the Paracas people over 2,000 years ago and visible only from the sea. Book your excursion the day before and choose the first morning departure (around 8 am): the sea is calmer and the wildlife more active. On the islands' rocks, you'll spot colonies of South American sea lions, Humboldt penguins—the only penguin species in Peru—Peruvian pelicans, Guanay cormorants, and Peruvian boobies. You don't land on the islands: everything is viewed from the boat. The tour lasts about 2 hours.

⏱ 120 min
2

In the afternoon, head to Paracas National Reserve: it's one of the few places on Earth where desert meets Pacific Ocean head-on. Playa Roja owes its color to fragments of red volcanic rock—the contrast with the blue sea is striking. Note that the formation nicknamed "La Catedral," an emblematic rock arch of the reserve, collapsed in a 2007 earthquake. Catch the sunset from the cliffs.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Back at the small port of El Chaco, settle onto a terrace to sample ceviche made with freshly caught fish. Paracas Bay is one of the richest fishing grounds in the South Pacific, with over 180 documented fish species.

Stop 4

Huacachina

A palm-fringed lagoon lost amid towering dunes and sunset sandboarding runs

★★★☆☆
📍 Laguna de Huacachina Ica📍 Dunes Huacachina désert Ica📍 Oasis Huacachina palmiers lagune📍 Bodega pisco Ica vignobles
1

Huacachina is a fascinating geographical anomaly: a natural lagoon ringed by palm trees, lost amid dunes rising over 330 feet. The place is so iconic it appears on Peru's 50-sol banknote. Walk around the oasis on arrival, before the heat sets in—midday temperatures hover around 86°F (30°C). Swimming in the lagoon is not recommended.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The signature activity in Huacachina is dune buggy rides and sandboarding, ideally in late afternoon to catch the golden sunset light. Professional drivers take you hurtling across the dune crests in 4x4 buggies—buckle up tight. Stops let you race down the slopes on sandboards: standing for the athletic, lying on your stomach for everyone else (faster and just as fun). Book at any agency in the oasis for a departure around 4 pm.

⏱ 120 min

The Ica region is Peru's main wine and pisco zone. Bodegas offer tours and tastings a few miles from the oasis. It's a chance to understand how pisco—grape brandy fermented in clay jars—is produced using methods handed down since colonial times.

⏱ 60 min

Nazca

Ancient geoglyphs etched across the desert floor, visible only from the sky since 200 BCE

★★★★★
📍 Aéroport de Nazca survol lignes géoglyphes📍 Mirador Nazca géoglyphes observation sol📍 Cimetière de Chauchilla Nazca momies📍 Musée Antonini Nazca archéologie
1

The Nazca geoglyphs were etched into the desert between 200 BCE and 600 CE by the Nazca culture: they span 190 square miles and include over 800 straight lines, 300 geometric figures, and 70 depictions of animals and plants. Designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1994, they truly reveal themselves only from the air. The small-plane overflight lasts 30 to 35 minutes: the pilot banks the aircraft alternately to each side so every passenger sees the hummingbird, the spider, the monkey, the condor, and the astronaut. Arrive early in the morning—desert winds pick up by midday, and turbulence with them. If you're prone to motion sickness, take preventive medication. Ground alternative: the metal viewing tower along the Pan-American Highway offers glimpses of the tree and the hands from ground level.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Chauchilla Cemetery, 19 miles from Nazca, is one of Peru's most striking archaeological sites: mummies over 1,000 years old rest in open-air tombs in the desert. Thanks to the extremely dry climate, they're remarkably preserved—hair, teeth, and funerary textiles are still visible. The site was looted before archaeological protection; the mummies have since been returned to their original graves. The visit is not suitable for young children. Arrange an organized excursion from Nazca that includes transport and a guide.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Two options for reaching Arequipa. Option 1: overnight bus from Nazca (departs around 8–9 pm, arrives Arequipa around 6 am)—comfortable with major companies, and you save a hotel night. Option 2: day drive the next morning (~355 miles, ~9 hours on the Pan-American South). If you choose the night bus, book a cama seat (reclining sleeper) for a decent night's rest.

Arequipa

The White City carved from volcanic stone, where a monastery hid from the world for four centuries

★★★★☆
📍 Plaza de Armas Arequipa cathédrale📍 Quartier San Lázaro Arequipa ruelles sillar📍 Mirador Yanahuara Arequipa volcan Misti📍 Marché San Camilo Arequipa
1

Arriving in Arequipa from Nazca, something shifts in the air—altitude climbs to 7,660 feet (2,335 m), the desert's dry heat gives way to sharper, cooler light. Drop your bags and head straight to Plaza de Armas: everything is white. The buildings, the arcades, the cathedral—all carved from sillar, the volcanic stone quarried from Chachani's flanks, which earned the city its nickname "The White City" and its UNESCO listing in 2000. Look north from the square: El Misti (19,101 ft / 5,822 m) rises above the rooftops with its near-perfect cone. Settle onto a terrace and let the city come to you.

⏱ 60 min
2

Push on to the San Lázaro neighborhood, half a mile from the Plaza: it's Arequipa's oldest quarter, predating even the city's official founding in 1540. Step into Violín alley or Ripacha alley—passages so narrow shoulders touch the walls—and you'll stumble onto white sillar houses with wrought-iron grilles and flower-filled courtyards visible through half-open doorways. The quarter is nearly deserted on weekdays: wander without a map—it's the only way to truly see it.

⏱ 60 min
3

The Yanahuara viewpoint, 1.25 miles from downtown, offers Arequipa's most famous panoramic view. Ten sillar arches built in the 19th century, engraved with verses by Arequipeño poets, frame the silhouettes of El Misti (19,101 ft / 5,822 m) and Chachani (19,872 ft / 6,057 m). Go in late afternoon for sunset light over the white city. Admission is free.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Arequipa is considered Peru's gastronomic capital. In the evening, seek out a traditional picantería in the Yanahuara district to try rocoto relleno (stuffed spicy pepper with meat and eggs), chupe de camarones (river prawn chowder), or adobo (marinated pork stew). The San Camilo market, liveliest in the morning, lets you discover local products at any hour.

⏱ 90 min

Arequipa

The White City carved from volcanic stone, where a monastery hid from the world for four centuries

★★★★☆
📍 Monastère Santa Catalina Arequipa📍 Museo Santuarios Andinos momie Juanita Arequipa📍 Iglesia Compañia de Jesus Arequipa📍 Molino de Sabandia Arequipa campagne
1

Santa Catalina Monastery is Arequipa's most striking attraction: founded September 10, 1579, by Doña María de Guzmán, a wealthy widow who surrendered all her possessions to live there in seclusion, it remained hermetically sealed from the outside world for nearly 400 years. Its doors opened to the public only in 1970. At 215,000 square feet, it's a city within the city: streets named after Spanish towns (Córdoba, Sevilla, Burgos), cloisters with red and orange walls, nuns' cells with individual kitchens, communal laundry. Today, a few dozen nuns still live in the complex's northern wing. Allow at least 2.5 hours—a guide is highly recommended for the first section, then explore freely.

⏱ 150 min
2

The Museo Santuarios Andinos houses the mummy Juanita, discovered in September 1995 by anthropologist Johan Reinhard and Peruvian guide Miguel Zárate during an expedition on Nevado Ampato (20,702 ft / 6,310 m). This Inca girl aged 12 to 15 was sacrificed as part of the Capacocha ritual, likely to appease the gods after local volcanic eruptions around 1440–1450. Preserved by the cold at over 19,700 feet for 500 years, she's displayed in a refrigerated case at -13°F (-25°C). In 2023, a Polish-Peruvian team reconstructed her face in 3D. The visit lasts about 30 minutes and begins with an introductory film. Photography is forbidden inside.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

In late afternoon, walk up to the Church of the Company of Jesus, whose churrigueresque baroque facade is one of the most ornate in South America. It borders Plaza de Armas on the east side. Step inside to see the interior and its murals.

⏱ 45 min

Take a taxi to the Sabandía countryside, 5 miles southeast: the road descends into an irrigated valley where corn, alfalfa, and olive trees grow, with El Misti as a backdrop—the contrast with Nazca's desert two days ago is striking. At the end of the track, the colonial Sabandía mill dates from the 17th century; restored and still functioning, it grinds grain the old-fashioned way. Cows graze around it, Inca irrigation channels run between the terraces. If you like walking, the path along the Socabaya river offers an hour's peaceful stroll away from tour groups.

⏱ 90 min
2

Back in town, stop by the San Camilo market for final purchases of local products: Andean aromatic herbs, cheeses, sweet specialties including queso helado (not cheese but ice cream flavored with coconut, cinnamon, and cloves, despite its name).

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Late in the day, flight Arequipa → Cusco (~1 hour). Rodriguez Ballon Airport is 5 miles from downtown. Enjoy the volcano views from the window on takeoff—El Misti with its perfect cone is unmistakable.

Cusco

Where Inca stonework shoulders Spanish churches at 11,000 feet above the clouds

★★★★★
📍 Plaza de Armas Cusco cathédrale📍 Qorikancha Temple du Soleil Cusco📍 Quartier San Blas Cusco artisans📍 Mercado San Pedro Cusco
1

The moment you step off the plane, you feel it: the air is thinner at 11,150 feet (3,400 m). Climbing the airport stairs leaves you slightly breathless, your temples might start to throb. Don't worry—it's normal and passes in a few hours if you don't push yourself. Order a mate de coca as soon as you reach your hotel (coca leaf tea genuinely helps ease altitude sickness) and reserve this first afternoon for gentle strolling on Plaza de Armas. The Cathedral and the Church of the Company, built on Inca foundations, border the square—look at the lower stones: they're Inca, the upper ones, Spanish. Don't rush yet.

⏱ 60 min
2

Qorikancha—"golden enclosure" in Quechua—was the Inca Empire's most important temple, the spiritual center dedicated to Inti (the Sun). According to Spanish chroniclers, its walls were covered with 700 sheets of gold and its gardens held sculptures of animals and plants in precious metal. In 1533–1534, the conquistadors looted the gold to pay Atahualpa's ransom, then gave the site to the Dominicans who built Santo Domingo convent on its foundations. The original Inca walls withstood every earthquake—including the 1950 quake—while the colonial convent collapsed with each tremor. This contrast between the two architectures remains perfectly visible today.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

In the evening, wander the artisan quarter of San Blas, north of Plaza de Armas. Its cobbled lanes and craft workshops (silversmiths, potters, weavers) come alive in late afternoon. Finish at the San Pedro market to try fresh fruit juices and local cheeses.

Cusco

Where Inca stonework shoulders Spanish churches at 11,000 feet above the clouds

★★★★★
📍 Sacsayhuamán forteresse inca Cusco📍 Qenqo site inca Cusco📍 Tambomachay fontaines incas Cusco📍 Puca Pucara forteresse rouge Cusco
1

Sacsayhuamán lies 1.25 miles north of Plaza de Armas at 12,140 feet (3,700 m) altitude. This Inca complex, built at Pachacutec's initiative in the 15th century and completed under Huayna Capac, consists of three parallel zigzag ramparts stretching 1,970 feet. Its monolithic blocks weigh between 128 and 200 tons for the largest—assembled without mortar with precision that won't admit a sheet of paper. The Spanish dismantled stones to build Cusco's churches. You can reach the site on foot from the Plaza in 30 to 45 minutes, or by taxi. Arrive before 10 am to avoid tour groups.

⏱ 90 min
2

On the same circuit, a few miles from Sacsayhuamán, Qenqo is an Inca ceremonial center carved directly from a rock outcrop: its underground galleries and sculpted channels probably served for noble mummification rituals. The Quechua word means "labyrinth." Tambomachay, farther on, is a complex of ritual channels and fountains whose springs still flow today—the Incas possessed remarkable hydraulic mastery. Both sites are included in the Cusco tourist ticket (Boleto Turístico).

⏱ 90 min

Activities

In the afternoon, return to San Blas to explore the artisan workshops in more detail. The San Pedro market is ideal late in the day for shopping: alpaca textiles, ceramics, Andean medicinal herbs. Plan your dinner in the historic quarter—Cusco cuisine offers local dishes like cuy al horno (roast guinea pig) or lomo saltado.

Stop 11

Ollantaytambo

Peru's only living Inca village, where cobbled streets still follow 500-year-old canals

★★★★☆
📍 Ruines incas Pisac Vallée Sacrée📍 Terrasses Moray Maras agriculture inca📍 Salines Maras Pérou bassins sel📍 Forteresse Ollantaytambo terrasses temple soleil
1

En route to Ollantaytambo, first stop at Pisac: the Inca ruins perched on the heights overlook a valley of startling vastness. Take time to climb to the Temple of the Sun for the panoramic view. Below, Pisac's artisan market is one of the region's best-stocked—textiles, ceramics, silver jewelry.

⏱ 90 min
2

The circular terraces of Moray constitute one of Peru's most original archaeological curiosities: three concentric amphitheaters carved into the earth, up to 720 feet in diameter, reaching depths of 100 feet. The temperature difference between bottom and rim exceeds 27°F (15°C)—the Incas likely used them as an agricultural laboratory to acclimate different species to different altitudes. A few miles away, the Maras salt pans align over 3,000 white salt pools on a mountainside, exploited since Inca times. The visual contrast is spectacular.

⏱ 90 min
3

Ollantaytambo is doubly remarkable: its Inca fortress with monumental terraces and unfinished Temple of the Sun (the Spanish halted construction in 1536) ranks among the best-preserved military-religious complexes in the Andes. But the town itself is unique in Peru: its narrow streets and irrigation channels date from Inca times and are still inhabited today—the country's only living Inca village.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Book your train Ollantaytambo → Aguas Calientes in advance (several weeks in high season). The ride (~1.5 hours) follows the Urubamba River as it narrows progressively into a jungle gorge—the atmosphere changes completely. Dinner in Ollantaytambo at a restaurant facing the illuminated fortress.

Aguas Calientes

The jungle gateway to Machu Picchu, where thermal springs rise between cloud forest peaks

★★★★☆
📍 Gare de train Aguas Calientes Machu Picchu📍 Bains thermaux Aguas Calientes Pérou📍 Aguas Calientes village Machu Picchu Pueblo📍 Navettes bus Machu Picchu Aguas Calientes
1

The train deposits you in Aguas Calientes into another world: the jungle has taken over, slopes are cloaked in dense humid vegetation, and the Urubamba River roars below restaurant windows. Steam rises from the hot springs that gave the village its name. It's loud, damp, hemmed-in—and it's perfect, because it announces something. Collect your Machu Picchu entry ticket if you haven't already (kiosks are available at the ticket office opposite the station), and scout the shuttle queue for tomorrow: the first buses leave at 5:30 am.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The natural hot springs (Aguas Calientes owes its name to the thermal waters) are a 10-minute walk from the station. After long days of travel and sightseeing, it's ideal relaxation before the Machu Picchu stage. Turn in early: tomorrow's wake-up is before 5 am.

Cusco

Where Inca stonework shoulders Spanish churches at 11,000 feet above the clouds

★★★★★
📍 Citadelle Machu Picchu Intihuatana📍 Temple des Trois Fenêtres Machu Picchu📍 Huayna Picchu montagne Machu Picchu📍 Montaña Machu Picchu vue panoramique
1

Take the shuttle bus from 5:30 am onward from Aguas Calientes: the winding ascent takes 25 minutes to the citadel entrance. Machu Picchu was rediscovered by American explorer Hiram Bingham on July 24, 1911, after local farmers directed him to the site. Designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1983, it ranks among the New Seven Wonders of the World. The main circuit covers the Intihuatana (the Inca stone "sundial"), the Temple of the Three Windows, the Mortars Quarter, and the cascading agricultural terraces. A local guide is strongly recommended for understanding the site's organization. Allow 3 to 4 hours for a complete visit.

⏱ 240 min
2

For those who booked in advance: Huayna Picchu (quota of 400 people/day) is the vertiginous climb up the iconic peak that overlooks the citadel—the summit view is breathtaking but the ascent is steep. Less athletic but equally spectacular alternative: Montaña Machu Picchu (quota of 800 people/day), higher at 10,111 feet (3,082 m) but with a steadier slope, offers an even more panoramic overview of the citadel.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Return late afternoon: shuttle bus down to Aguas Calientes, then train to Ollantaytambo (departures between 4 pm and 7:30 pm depending on company), and finally minibus or taxi from Ollantaytambo to Cusco (~1.5 hours). Evening arrival in Cusco. Final dinner in the San Blas quarter.

Last morning in Cusco before the return flight. Use it to walk through San Blas once more: the artisan workshops open early. An hour twenty later, Lima and the Pacific Ocean. The circuit is complete: from coast to Andes and back to the coast, in two weeks and 14 days of a Peru unlike anywhere else.
