Bari

★★☆☆☆
1

Bari Vecchia takes patience: a labyrinth of tight alleys where laundry hangs across balconies and cats follow you to the next piazza. Four kilometers of packed lanes on a spur between two harbors. Look for Via Arco Basso, nicknamed "orecchiette street": the nonnas make the little ear-shaped pasta on trestles outside their doors, and yes, they sell them. Budget two hours to walk through without rushing, more if you get caught by a terrace. Avoid the 1-4pm slot: everything closes and the city naps.

⏱ 120 min
2

The Basilica of San Nicola doesn't look like much from outside—massive facade, almost austere—and that's for the best: the shock waits inside. Consecrated in 1197 to house the relics of Saint Nicholas, stolen from Myra (present-day Turkey) in 1087 by Barese sailors, it remains a pilgrimage site for Orthodox Christians worldwide. Go down to the crypt: that's where the saint rests, beneath a slab that weeps the "manna," an oily liquid the faithful collect once a year. Free entry, but covered shoulders required.

⏱ 45 min
3

Shorter lines, fewer crowds, more calm: the Cathedral of San Sabino plays in a different league than its neighbor San Nicola. Rebuilt in the late 12th century in pure Apulian Romanesque style, it houses a Byzantine icon of the Virgin Odigitria that the Barese have venerated since the Middle Ages. Ask to see the Trullo, a small annexed dome too often overlooked. The crypt preserves mosaic fragments from the 6th century. Twenty minutes suffice if you're pressed, a good half hour if you take your time.

⏱ 30 min
4

Founded by the Normans in 1131, rebuilt by Frederick II in 1233, Bari's Swabian Castle shows what makes Puglia unique: every empire passed through here, and each stacked its layer. Only part is visitable (a good chunk still serves as administrative offices), but the inner courtyard and the gallery of casts—plaster copies of the finest Romanesque portals in Puglia—are worth the 6-euro entry. Nice harbor view from the ramparts. Closed Wednesdays.

⏱ 45 min
5

Built under Mussolini in the 1920s, long, wide, monumental: the Nazario Sauro promenade borders the sea for nearly five kilometers with Art Deco palazzi on one side and the Adriatic on the other. This is where the Barese come to run, double-park to chat, and watch the sunset. At the Margherita, a former theater built on stilts above the water, grab gelato at Gentile: the specialty is pasta reale, made with almonds.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Try focaccia barese at Panificio Fiore in the historic center. Protected recipe: potato-based dough, cherry tomatoes poked into the dough, oregano, olive oil. Sold by weight, to be eaten in the street as tradition demands.

⏱ 30 min

Breakfast Bari-style at Molo San Nicola, in the corner known in dialect as N' dèrr'a la lanze ("at the feet of the boats"). Fishermen have unloaded their red and blue gozzi since dawn and sell the catch raw from trestles: octopus still twisted, sea urchins opened with a knife, mussels, oysters at one euro each on Sundays. Local tradition calls for washing it all down with an ice-cold Peroni at Bar El Chiringuito, open since 1950. Strange breakfast? The Barese have done it for three generations.

⏱ 45 min

Giovinazzo

★★★★☆
1

First stop leaving Bari: Giovinazzo, 20 km north. The historic center sits on a small promontory ringed by 15th-century ramparts, beautifully restored, nearly empty of tourists in May. Enter through the Arco di Traiano, circle around Piazza Vittorio Emanuele (trapezoid shape, Tritons fountain at center), and head toward the 12th-century Cathedral of Santa Maria Assunta, which overlooks the fishing harbor. An hour suffices. Did you know Domenico Modugno, who sang "Volare," was born here? The town dedicated a statue to him on the waterfront.

⏱ 75 min
2

The small fishing harbor, below the historic center, maintains real maritime life: gozzi painted red and blue, nets drying, old men repairing traps. A fifteen-minute loop along the quays, then climb back through the alleys toward the Torrione Aragonese, the last tower of the medieval ramparts. Fine view of the coast toward Molfetta.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Special caffè break at Piazza Vittorio Emanuele. Town specialty: espresso, lemon zest, sugar, and whipped cream. Served in several cafes downtown, ask for "caffè speciale" without specifying, they know.

⏱ 20 min

Molfetta

★★☆☆☆
1

Not the postcard charm of Giovinazzo, but something rarer: an Old Cathedral of San Corrado planted directly on the harbor, with its three aligned domes and two twin bell towers. Started around 1150, completed in the 13th century, the church is the purest example of Apulian Romanesque with domes: you can detect Byzantine, Romanesque, and Arab-Sicilian influences blended without forcing. No Baroque retouches, because in the 18th century it lost its cathedral title and was forgotten. Free entry, closed Sundays.

⏱ 45 min
2

Molfetta's harbor remains one of the busiest fishing ports in the lower Adriatic: fleet of trawlers, morning fish auction, shipyards still working. Walk to Banchina Seminario to see the Old Cathedral from the sea, as its builders intended. Coffee on the terrace at Piazza Municipio on the way back.

⏱ 30 min
3

A discreet but very real trace of the Templars, the Sala dei Templari occupies the former church of San Nicola, attested in Molfetta since 1148. The knights maintained an embarkation point here for the Holy Land, until the order's dissolution in 1312. Today the building serves as an exhibition and congress hall. Open only during events, check on site.

⏱ 20 min

Activities

Fish lunch at the harbor. Molfetta specialty: the local fish soup called "ciambotto," a mix of small fish cooked in spicy tomato sauce. Simple, reliable address: Osteria del Porto.

⏱ 75 min

Bisceglie

★★☆☆☆
1

A Bronze Age megalithic tomb dating from 2300 to 1750 BC, the Chianca dolmen, 5 km inland from Bisceglie, is the stop you don't expect to make in Puglia. Discovered by chance in 1909 by two archaeologists, Samarelli and Mosso. Corridor of 7.6 m, burial chamber covered by a slab weighing more than 10 tons, oriented due east toward the rising sun. Inside, six adult and child skeletons, stone knives, a bronze pendant. UNESCO recognized it in 2011 as "heritage testifying to a culture of peace." Free access, follow SP 138 from Bisceglie, last kilometer on dirt road.

⏱ 45 min
2

Return toward the coast through the historic center. The medieval town centers on the Cathedral of San Pietro (begun in 1073, facade redone in the 18th century) and the Castello Svevo, another fortress of Frederick II. Small streets descending toward a still-active fishing harbor. Budget an hour for the tour, more if you grab an aperitivo at Piazza Vittorio Emanuele. Bisceglie stays authentic: few souvenir shops, plenty of old men playing cards.

⏱ 60 min
3

Cala San Giacomo, a small cove 3 km south of downtown, offers a seaside break mid-day. Rocky bottom, transparent water, few people on weekdays. No beach bar, bring your towels and picnic.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Quick swim at Cala San Giacomo. Water shoes recommended, the pebbles are sharp. Good compromise to break the rhythm before tackling Trani.

⏱ 60 min

Trani

★★★☆☆
1

The Cathedral of San Nicola Pellegrino is, without detour, the most beautiful of Puglia's Romanesque cathedrals. Started in 1099 on a 4th-century paleo-Christian church, completed in the 13th century, it stands alone on its square, ten meters from the sea—a placement unique in Italy. The stone, called "Trani stone," is a pinkish-white limestone tuff almost unreal that changes color through the day: golden in the morning, pink at sunset. The 59-meter bell tower rests on a broken arch crossed by the street, a rare feat in Romanesque architecture. Bronze portal by Barisano (1180). Don't miss the triple crypt, one above the other, descending to the 4th-century vestiges.

⏱ 75 min
2

200 meters from the cathedral, the Castello Svevo completes Trani's medieval trilogy. Built between 1233 and 1249 by Frederick II—the emperor really left his mark everywhere in the region—it controlled the harbor. Square plan, four corner towers, moats on three sides, the fourth opening directly onto the sea. Restored, it now houses a museum and temporary exhibitions. The terrace offers the best view of the cathedral and harbor. 5-euro entry, closed Monday mornings.

⏱ 60 min
3

Trani's semicircular harbor, one of the most photographed in Puglia: blue and white fishing boats lined up, ochre facades, the pinkish-white stone cathedral reflecting in the water. The evening passeggiata starts around 7pm on Via Statuti Marittimi, and everyone ends their round in front of the illuminated cathedral. Several wine bars on the quay for an Aperol at sunset.

⏱ 60 min
4

The church of Ognissanti, called "church of the Templars," dates from the 12th century. Small Romanesque church with three naves and three apses, built by the Templars to bless crusaders before their embarkation toward the Holy Land from Trani's harbor. Sober atmosphere, off the main tourist circuit. Open by request, contact the parish.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Fish dinner at Trani's harbor. Recommended addresses: Corteinfiore (Michelin-starred, pricier) or Osteria Frangipane (simple, excellent value). Local specialty: troccoli ai frutti di mare, fresh long pasta with square cross-section.

⏱ 90 min

Walk along Molo Sant'Antuono, the jetty that closes the harbor on the east side. Panoramic view of the cathedral, a chance to understand why this town seduced all the Romantic painters of the 19th century. Very popular late in the day with locals.

⏱ 45 min

Trani

★★★☆☆
1

Behind the harbor, narrower, quieter alleys: you're entering the Giudecca. Trani was in the Middle Ages one of the largest Jewish centers in Southern Italy. In 1231, Frederick II granted the local community the monopoly on raw silk production, in exchange for a third of the profits. Four synagogues were built in the 13th century. The Scolanova, one of four medieval synagogues still standing in Italy, was confiscated by the Church in 1380 during a wave of antisemitism and converted into a church; it was returned to the Jewish community in 2006.

⏱ 45 min
2

Two steps from the Scolanova, the Sant'Anna Museum occupies another former medieval synagogue, also transformed into a church in the 14th century. It tells today a thousand years of Jewish presence in Trani: silk trade, persecutions, forced conversions, exile, and the recent return of a small community. Small museum, but dense. Budget an hour.

⏱ 60 min
3

A strange anomaly in the heart of Trani: Palazzo Caccetta, 15th-century Venetian Gothic facade, carved balconies, mullioned windows. One of the very rare examples of civil Gothic in Puglia, a sign that Trani then traded directly with Venice. Exterior visit only, but the facade is worth a photo stop.

⏱ 15 min
4

The historic center on the land side, less photographed than the harbor, deserves a morning. Small Romanesque churches (San Francesco, San Giacomo), degraded but inhabited noble palaces, morning market at Piazza della Repubblica. This is where you see the real Trani, not the postcard version.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Tasting of Moscato di Trani, a sweet DOC wine produced only in the municipality. Several wine bars downtown offer a selection from small producers. Reliable address: Enoteca Beverbar, Piazza Repubblica.

⏱ 60 min

Swim at Spiaggia di Colonna, just south of the cathedral. Small pebble and flat-rock beach, transparent water, cathedral in the background. Iconic Trani photo at sunset.

⏱ 90 min

Barletta

★★★☆☆
1

First morning stop: the Colossus of Barletta, planted in front of the Basilica of the Holy Sepulchre. This 5.11-meter bronze statue represents a late Roman emperor—probably Theodosius II or Valentinian I, the debate has lasted for centuries. Dated to the 5th century, it was probably cast in Constantinople. How did it get to Barletta? Several hypotheses: a Venetian shipwreck in 1204 returning from the sack of Constantinople, or a transfer ordered by Frederick II from Ravenna in 1231. In 1309, the local Dominicans melted down its legs and arms to make bells: what you see today is a restoration from 1491. It's the largest ancient bronze statue to survive intact. Locals call it Eraclio, or Aré in dialect.

⏱ 30 min
2

500 meters from the Colossus, Barletta's Castello Svevo: the most imposing Norman-Swabian castle on the coast. Founded by the Normans in the 11th century, profoundly remodeled by Frederick II in 1234, then fortified by Charles V in the 16th century with its four massive diamond-pointed bastions. The castle today houses the municipal library and an interesting lapidary museum. Fine harbor views from the ramparts. Budget an hour, more if you love medieval archaeology.

⏱ 60 min
3

January 15, 1503, during a banquet in this cantina in the historic center: the French knight Charles de la Motte, a prisoner of war, calls the Italians cowards after drinking too much Rosso Barletta. The insult doesn't pass. The Italians issue a challenge: 13 knights against 13 knights. The tournament takes place on February 13, 1503 on the plain between Corato and Andria. The Italians, led by Ettore Fieramosca, win. The cantina is now a small museum with weapons, costumes, and reenactments. Every February 13, Barletta replays the scene in period costume.

⏱ 30 min
4

Adjacent to the Colossus, the Basilica of the Holy Sepulchre dates from the 12th century. Built by the canons of the Holy Sepulchre of Jerusalem as a stop for departing crusaders, it blends Romanesque, French Gothic, and Oriental influences brought back from the Holy Land. Inside, a 13th-century Byzantine Christ and medieval frescoes being restored.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Quick lunch in Barletta: focaccia break at Panificio Cosma on the Corso, or a more substantial pranzo at Antica Cucina 1983, near the castle. The dish to try: cavatelli con cime di rapa, local pasta with wild turnip greens.

⏱ 75 min

Andria

★★☆☆☆
1

Head to Andria, 13 km inland. The town is less pretty than its coastal neighbors, but its Cathedral of Santa Maria Assunta, founded in the 11th century, holds something unique: the tombs of two of Frederick II's wives, Yolande de Brienne (died in childbirth in 1228 at 16) and Isabella of England (died in 1241 at 27). The crypt, partly carved into an earlier paleo-Christian church, is the most moving part. The cathedral also preserves a thorn from Christ's crown, offered in 1308 by Countess Beatrice, daughter of Charles II of Anjou.

⏱ 60 min
2

Porta Sant'Andrea, also called Arco di Federico, is the only one of Andria's five medieval gates to survive: the other four were demolished after Italian unification. The inscription carved on the facade, attributed to Frederick II in 1230 on his return from the sixth crusade—"Andria fidelis nostris affixa medullis" (Andria the faithful, engraved in our bowels)—explains why the emperor called the city his "fidelis." Small, sober, restored in the 16th century, but loaded with symbolism.

⏱ 20 min
3

Palazzo Ducale, on Piazza Catuma, was built by Frederick II on his return from the sixth crusade in 1230, originally as a Franciscan convent. Remodeled in the 16th century as a noble residence for the Carafa dukes, it's now city hall. Visit limited to public spaces, but the courtyard and facade are worth the detour. The square itself, called "Catuma" because of paleo-Christian catacombs underground, is the true heart of Andria.

⏱ 30 min
4

Andria's historic center explores through its narrow alleys between Porta Sant'Andrea and the cathedral: degraded but inhabited Renaissance palaces, small churches, local product shops. The Torre dell'Orologio, also erected by Frederick II, marks the medieval center. Budget an hour of relaxed wandering, the center is compact.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Burrata tasting straight from the source at Caseificio Olanda in Andria. Burrata is mozzarella enveloping a heart of stracciatella and cream: twelve hours freshness maximum after making. Lorenzo Bianchino invented it in Andria in 1956, to avoid losing his milk production during a winter blocked by snow. IGP since 2016. Workshop visit in the morning, tasting in the afternoon with a glass of Castel del Monte DOC.

⏱ 90 min

Dinner in a masseria around Andria. Several addresses between Andria and Castel del Monte offer regional cuisine: Lucanian lamb, fave e cicoria (fava bean puree with wild chicory), homemade pasta. Rustic atmosphere, reasonable prices, essential to book.

⏱ 120 min

Castel Del Monte Andria

★★★★☆
1

The highlight of the trip, saved for last: Castel del Monte, 18 km inland above Andria, at 540 meters altitude. Built around 1240 under Frederick II's orders, octagonal, with eight octagonal towers at the corners: everything revolves around the number eight. No moats, no drawbridge, no stables, no kitchens: neither fortress nor hunting lodge. A school of philosophy? An astronomical observatory? At the equinoxes, at precisely noon, the shadow of the walls reaches exactly the length of the inner courtyard. No written document mentions its function. This mystery inspired Umberto Eco for the library-labyrinth in "The Name of the Rose." UNESCO since 1996, Italian national monument, appears on the Italian 1-cent euro coin. The interior visit is austere (the marble was looted in the 17th century), but the exterior and panorama justify the detour.

⏱ 120 min
2

Around the castle, Alta Murgia National Park: austere limestone plateau, centuries-old olive trees, jazzi (dry-stone sheepfolds), walls and holm oaks. Almost Mongolian landscape, surprising for Puglia. Several marked hiking trails start from the castle parking lot; the shortest is 2 km around the hill and offers the best views of the octagon from outside.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Picnic on the esplanade below the castle. Several equipped areas with tables and shade, ideal after the visit. Buy the picnic basket in Andria before heading up: focaccia, burrata, tomatoes, local wine.

⏱ 60 min

Bari

★★☆☆☆
1

Return toward Bari to close the loop, about 1 hour by SS170 then SS16. The route crosses the lower Alta Murgia plateau before descending toward the Adriatic coast. If you have time before the flight, a detour through Ruvo di Puglia and its Jatta Archaeological Museum is worth it for the collection of 4th-century BC Apulian ceramics—one of Italy's finest.

⏱ 45 min
2

Final pass through Bari before the airport, located in Palese, 12 km northwest of downtown. If the flight is late afternoon, save time for a farewell lunch and a last focaccia. The rental agency is on the terminal ground floor: quick return, budget 15 minutes. Allow 2 hours before a Schengen flight, 2.5 hours for the rest.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Farewell lunch on the return route: Trattoria Antica Bari, in Palombaio (highway exit, 15 km before the airport), serves orecchiette with broccoli rabe and fave e cicoria like they made fifty years ago. Simple cooking, gentle prices, not a word of English. That's the real Puglia cuisine.

⏱ 75 min

If the flight is late, final swim at San Giorgio, public beach between Bari and the airport. Clear water, sand, easy access. Free parking. Showers on site, convenient before returning the car.

⏱ 90 min
