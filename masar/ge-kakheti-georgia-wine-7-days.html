Tbilisi

★★★★★
1

The Old Town sets the tone immediately: cobblestone alleys, carved wooden balconies jutting over the street, and the ruins of Narikala Fortress draped across the ridge above. The fortress dates from the 4th century, expanded by Arab emirs in the 8th century and by the Safavids after that - each conqueror added a layer, and the result is a patchwork of stone you can wander for an hour without retracing your steps. The cable car from the Bridge of Peace costs 6 GEL return and saves a sweaty climb. Go late afternoon for the light.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Sameba Cathedral - completed in 2004 after 10 years of construction - is the largest Orthodox cathedral in the Caucasus at 84 metres high, visible from most of the city. The interior is deliberately austere: dark stone, gold iconostasis, the low hum of monks chanting if you arrive at the right hour. Controversial when built (the site displaced an old Armenian cemetery), it's since become a genuine focal point of Georgian religious life. No admission fee; modest dress required.

⏱ 45 min
3

The Bridge of Peace - Michele de Lucchi's steel-and-glass pedestrian bridge, opened 2010 - cuts a deliberately jarring silhouette against the medieval skyline. Locals were divided at first; most have made peace with it. Walk it at dusk when the LED canopy starts to glow and the fortress reflects in the Mtkvari below. It costs nothing and takes five minutes, but the view both ways earns its place in the itinerary.

⏱ 20 min

أنشطة

First wine of the trip at 8000 Vintages on Erekle II Street: over 200 Georgian references by the glass and bottle, rotated by region and style. Ask for the flight comparing Rkatsiteli in qvevri versus stainless steel - the same grape, the same valley, two completely different wines. It's the clearest possible demonstration of why the vessel matters as much as the variety. The staff speak English well and genuinely enjoy the explaining.

⏱ 90 min

Dinner at Shavi Lomi ('The Black Lion') in the Baths quarter: Georgian cooking with a modern hand, served in the ground floor of an old townhouse. Order the khinkali (Georgian dumplings, meat-filled, eaten by hand - never cut them or the juice escapes), the Adjarian khachapuri (boat-shaped, with a runny egg and knob of butter in the centre), and a glass of Saperavi from qvevri to close. The whole meal with wine comes to 35-50 GEL per person. Tbilisi's quality-to-price ratio is one of the most remarkable in Europe.

⏱ 120 min

Tbilisi

★★★★★
1

The Georgian National Museum on Rustaveli Avenue holds the two things you came to see. First, the Gold Treasury: Scythian goldwork from the 3rd-2nd centuries BC, buried in royal kurgans in the Alazani valley - the craftsmanship is finer than most European goldsmiths could manage a thousand years later. Second, the archaeological hall with the Neolithic qvevris from Shulaveris Gora, dated to around 6000 BC. These are the jars that proved the 8,000-year claim - small, unadorned, with tartaric acid residue that changed the history of wine. They sit in a glass case with very little drama. They deserve more. Budget 2 hours; entry 7 GEL.

⏱ 120 min
2

Abanotubani - the Bath Quarter - is where Tbilisi began. The legend: King Vakhtang Gorgasali was hunting in 458 AD when a pheasant he shot fell into a hot spring and cooked. He founded a city around the source, naming it Tbili ('warm' in Georgian). The domed brick bathhouses visible today date from the 17th-18th centuries. Pouchkine wrote from here that he had 'never seen anything more luxurious than the baths of Tiflis.' The sulfur smell is real and immediate - part of the experience.

⏱ 30 min
3

The Mtatsminda funicular climbs to 727 metres above the city - the original cable was installed in 1905, rebuilt several times, and still runs. At the top: a Soviet-era amusement park sits alongside a panoramic restaurant and a small church. The view over Tbilisi at dusk is the best vantage point in the city, the entire bowl of the valley, the fortress, the two cathedrals, and the towers of the new financial district all in one frame. Funicular ticket: 3 GEL each way.

⏱ 60 min

أنشطة

An hour in the sulfur baths needs no justification. Chreli-Abano (the one with the tiled blue facade) and the Royal Orbeliani Baths both offer private rooms: hot sulfur water, a scrub-down from a bath attendant (the kisi massage), and complete silence except for water echoing off old stone. The basic soak runs 30 GEL/hour for a private room; add the massage for 60 GEL. Go midday - mornings fill with families, evenings with tourists.

⏱ 90 min

The Fabrika complex - a Soviet textile factory converted in 2017 into a cultural hub - is what happens when a city decides to remember its past without being nostalgic about it. The factory floor is now a courtyard with container bars, wine shops, and art studios. The Naturalist Wine Bar inside specialises in qvevri-only producers, many making a few thousand bottles a year. It's an evening place: grab a glass of Chinuri pétillant naturel and stay in the courtyard until you lose track of time.

⏱ 120 min

Sighnaghi

★★★☆☆
1

Sighnaghi sits on a ridge at 800 metres, with the snow-capped Greater Caucasus range behind and the Alazani Valley spread out below like a green carpet. Built in the 18th century by King Erekle II, it's the smallest town in Georgia - roughly 2,000 people - and one of the most complete: 4 kilometres of walls, 23 towers, all largely intact. The 'City of Love' nickname is recent and slightly absurd: since 2007, the civil registry has offered 24-hour wedding ceremonies without prior appointment, a tourism gimmick that somehow became a real tradition. Walk the walls; plan 45 minutes end to end.

⏱ 60 min
2

The Bodbe Monastery sits 2 km south of Sighnaghi, down a wooded path that was once a royal road. Founded in the 9th century, it holds the tomb of Saint Nino - the Cappadocian woman who converted Georgia to Christianity in 327 AD, arriving with a cross made of grapevines bound with her own hair. That detail matters here: in Georgia, wine and Christianity have been inseparable from the very beginning. A spring below the hill is considered miraculous; pilgrims queue to fill bottles.

⏱ 45 min

أنشطة

Pheasant's Tears is the winery that put Georgian natural wine on the international map. American painter John Wurdeman moved to Georgia in the 1990s and co-founded the estate in 2007 with winemaker Gela Patalishvili - working exclusively in qvevri, with near-biodynamic methods, and focusing on varieties that had almost disappeared during Soviet collectivisation (Tsolikouri, Chitistvala, Tavkveri, Aladasturi). The tasting covers six wines across 90 minutes, with the team explaining each grape's near-extinction and return. Rarely is a wine tasting also a conservation story. Roughly 25-40 USD; reservation essential.

⏱ 120 min

Dinner at Pheasant's Tears restaurant follows Kakheti's feasting logic - a succession of shared plates calibrated to the wines in glass. The pkhali (walnut-herb vegetable balls), the churchkhela hanging from the ceiling (walnut chains dipped in grape must - the original Georgian energy bar), and the mtsvadi grilled over vine cuttings: this is what the grape economy looks like on a plate. Budget 30-50 USD with wine. Worth every tetri.

⏱ 120 min

Telavi

★★★☆☆
1

Alaverdi Cathedral is 50 metres high and was completed between 1010 and 1029 - the tallest medieval church in Georgia. But the building is almost secondary to what happens in the basement: monks here have been making wine without interruption since 1011. During the Soviet era, when private winemaking was technically forbidden, the monastery continued in what the brothers describe with considerable understatement as 'a spirit of quiet persistence.' The marani below the cathedral today holds approximately 100 indigenous varieties, some resurrected from as few as five surviving plants. Twelve centuries of unbroken viticulture, under one roof.

⏱ 90 min
2

Telavi is Kakheti's administrative capital - 20,000 people, a weekly market that's been running for centuries, and a cluster of wine estates within 20 minutes in every direction. The old quarter around the 17th-century Batonis-tsikhe fortress is compact and walkable. The 900-year-old plane tree in the town centre has a circumference of 11 metres. It was already old when Alaverdi was new.

⏱ 45 min

أنشطة

Schuchmann Wines in Kisiskhevi (15 minutes from Telavi) represents the other face of Georgian viticulture: German investment, 165 working qvevris, a modern cellar, and a gastronomic restaurant. Burkhard Schuchmann arrived in 2008 and built what amounts to a Burgundy grand cru estate - with qvevris instead of barrels. The Vinoterra Rkatsiteli Qvevri is the benchmark amber wine for skeptics: structured, tannic, nothing like white wine as commonly understood, completely persuasive. The tasting covers both qvevri and classical method wines - a useful comparison for understanding what the vessel actually does to a wine.

⏱ 120 min

An evening supra - a Georgian feast - is not a dinner. It's a ritual with a tamada (the toast-master) who leads toasts at prescribed intervals: to the homeland, to the ancestors, to the women, to the children, to peace, to the guest. You don't drink between toasts; you wait. The toasts get longer and more elaborate as the evening progresses. If you can no longer drink, say so clearly in advance - refusing mid-toast is the only real faux pas. Ask your guesthouse to organise a family supra. The experience is irreplaceable and costs almost nothing.

⏱ 180 min

Telavi

★★★☆☆
1

The Khareba tunnel in Kvareli started as a Soviet military project in 1962 - 7.7 kilometres bored into the mountain to shelter command infrastructure in the event of nuclear war. It was never used for that. Converted into a wine cellar in 2003, it now holds over 25,000 bottles ageing at a constant 14°C, which the rock maintains automatically. You travel the tunnel by electric cart, stopping at intervals for tastings. It's unapologetically touristy, and the sheer scale of the thing - standing at the far end of a wine-filled military corridor, watching your breath fog in the cold - is genuinely strange and worth the trip.

⏱ 90 min
2

Tsinandali Estate was the property of Prince Alexandre Chavchavadze (1786-1846), the poet who first introduced European winemaking techniques to Georgia in the 19th century. The manor and its 18-hectare botanical garden have been beautifully restored. The Tsinandali white - a Rkatsiteli-Mtsvane blend, no qvevri, European-style ageing - was first produced in 1886, making it one of the oldest continuously produced wine labels in the Caucasus. Entry 5-8 GEL; worth an hour.

⏱ 60 min

أنشطة

The Kindzmarauli tasting at Kindzmarauli Marani is the most historically loaded pour of the trip. Kindzmarauli is a protected AOC semi-sweet red - Saperavi grapes grown only in five villages around Kvareli, fermentation stopped by cold to retain natural residual sugar. Stalin, born 150 km west in Gori, was devoted to it: he shipped cases to Roosevelt and Churchill during the Second World War, and it became the most exported Georgian wine of the Soviet era. The reserve aged in qvevri is the version to judge by; the export classic is a different wine entirely.

⏱ 90 min

Kvareli town itself is quieter and less groomed than Sighnaghi - more working village than destination. The morning market on the main street: dried churchkhela in long strings, walnuts by the kilo, jars of tkemali (sour plum sauce), and wine sold by locals from unlabelled plastic bottles that are sometimes the best thing you'll drink all week. Bring cash - the market operates entirely in GEL.

⏱ 60 min

Tbilisi

★★★★★
1

The Dry Bridge flea market spreads along the Mtkvari embankment daily, best on weekends: Soviet memorabilia, icons, old cameras, porcelain, military medals, handmade jewellery, and - if you know what you're looking at - antique hand-carved qvevri lids and old wine vessels. The sellers are mostly pensioners supplementing their income; haggling is expected but not aggressive. It's one of those places where you understand a city's 20th century through its cast-off objects.

⏱ 60 min
2

Rustaveli Avenue is Tbilisi's central spine - 1.5 kilometres of 19th-century neoclassical buildings housing the National Museum, Parliament, Opera, and Rustaveli Theatre. Built during the Russian imperial period to signal that Tbilisi was a proper European capital, it takes itself seriously in the right way. Walk it from Freedom Square toward Rustaveli metro; the architectural ambition reads best from the middle of the road, not the pavement.

⏱ 40 min

أنشطة

The farewell dinner belongs at Barbarestan. Chef David Lomadze built the menu around a recipe book written by Duchess Barbara Jorjadze in 1873 - every dish traces back to a specific historical recipe, updated for modern kitchens. The result is Georgian cuisine as it was before the Soviet period standardised everything: more herb-driven, more layered, less predictable. The walnut preparations alone span six dishes. Reserve a week ahead; 50-80 USD per person with wine. If you splurge once on this trip, this is the meal.

⏱ 150 min

Last wine stop: the Naturalist Wine Bar in the Fabrika courtyard. This is where Tbilisi's young wine crowd goes - producers you won't find in the tourist districts, pét-nats from tiny farms in Kartli, experimental qvevri-concrete hybrids from the new generation. Order something you can't pronounce and ask the bartender where it's from. That conversation, at the end of seven days, will land differently than it would have at the beginning.

⏱ 90 min

Tbilisi

★★★★★
1

Mtskheta is 30 minutes north of Tbilisi and several centuries removed in atmosphere. Georgia's ancient capital - seat of the Iberian kingdom from the 3rd century BC to the 5th century AD - sits at the confluence of the Mtkvari and Aragvi rivers. UNESCO listed it in 1994. The Svetitskhoveli Cathedral (completed 1029) is the spiritual centre of Georgian Orthodoxy; tradition holds that the robe of Christ is buried beneath the pillar at the crossing. Whether or not that's true, the pilgrimage energy in the nave is unmistakable - the cathedral is almost never empty.

⏱ 60 min
2

Jvari Monastery crowns the hill directly above Mtskheta - the view from its terrace of the two rivers meeting is one of those compositions that explains, without any commentary, why someone chose to build here in the 6th century. The monastery is small (one church, a courtyard, a handful of monks), but the location is exact: Jvari means 'cross' in Georgian, and Nino is said to have erected a wooden cross on this hill in 327 AD when she converted the kingdom. The current stone building dates from 586-605 AD.

⏱ 45 min

أنشطة

Lunch at Salobie in Mtskheta town: a sprawling traditional restaurant under vine trellises, best-known for its lobiani - flatbread stuffed with spiced kidney beans. Order it with the walnut-herb salad and a final glass of Saperavi. It's a simple meal in a simple setting. After seven days of winery tastings and monastery cellars, that simplicity is exactly right. Arrive by 12h30 before the Tbilisi tour groups fill every table.
