Charente Circuit: Cognac and Romanesque Heritage in 9 Days

Cognac ages in centuries-old cellars, Romanesque churches stand against the blue sky, the Charente flows at its own pace. A journey at the rhythm of eau-de-vie.

From the cognac cellars to Renaissance castles, from the comic book murals of Angoulême to the underground church of Aubeterre: the Charente marries the art of living with a thousand years of heritage. A leisurely circuit along the river, between tastings and character villages.

Key highlights (4)
  • Cognac: tour of the great houses, boat crossing, tasting
  • Angoulême: comic book capital, Romanesque cathedral, painted walls
  • La Rochefoucauld: Renaissance castle inhabited for 1,000 years
  • Aubeterre-sur-Dronne: church carved into the rock, Most Beautiful Village of France

The Cité internationale de la BD occupies a former warehouse on the banks of the Charente. Over 20,000 original comic plates, cutting-edge temporary exhibitions, and Europe's largest comic book library. Allow 2 hours to make the most of it.

The Cathédrale Saint-Pierre is Romanesque, not Gothic — and that changes everything. The sculpted 12th-century façade tells the story of the Ascension and the Last Judgement across 70 stone figures. The contemporary Treasury by Jean-Michel Othoniel is worth a detour.

The ramparts walk offers a full panorama over the Charente below. Angoulême sits on a rocky spur — you can see why it served as a stronghold. It's also the best vantage point for spotting the comic book murals.

Over 30 monumental murals across the streets: Titeuf on a gable end, Lucky Luke at a crossroads, Blake and Mortimer round a corner. Free map at the tourist office. Kids love it — adults too.

Under the Halles centrales, producers sell pineau, rillettes and cagouilles (local snails). Around them, cobbled lanes climb towards the town hall housed in the former comtal castle. Good bistros in the area.

→

Guided tour 'Comics in the City'. The guides know the stories behind every mural: who painted what, why there, the hidden details. Duration 1h30, departure from the tourist office.

→

Walk along the Charente riverbanks. The towpath runs from the Cité BD along the river. Canoe hire available at the watersports centre in season. Quieter option: traditional gabare boat with commentary.

→

Musée d'Angoulême in the former bishop's palace, behind the cathedral. Varied collections: Charente archaeology (the famous gold Agris helmet), African arts, regional paintings. Small but well done.

The Château de La Rochefoucauld has belonged to the same family since 1019 — a French record. The three-storey Renaissance galleries recall Italian palaces. Legend has it the spiral staircase was designed by Leonardo da Vinci.

The castle's four libraries hold 21,000 volumes. The guided tour (compulsory for this section) includes the Treasury room where the guide handles eight-century-old archives. Impressive.

The town at the foot of the castle is worth a stroll: half-timbered houses, cobbled lanes along the Tardoire. This is where the charentaise slipper was born — the shoe, not the resident. Specialist shop on Rue des Halles.

→

Full castle visit. Self-guided section (salons, kitchens, galleries) + guided tour of the libraries and archives. Allow 2 hours total. Children can dress up as knights or princesses — costumes provided.

💡 Children's costumes available free of charge
→

Chocolaterie d'Antan at the foot of the castle. Watch artisans at work from the shop. Local speciality: the pichotte, a chocolate-almond-icing sugar sweet. Open even on Sundays.

→

Hike along the Tardoire. Waymarked trails from the castle into the rolling countryside. Landscapes that hint at the neighbouring Périgord. Several loops from 5 to 15 km.

The Pont-Vieux in Confolens has spanned the Vienne since the 11th century (on ancient foundations). Postcard view of half-timbered houses reflected in the water. This is where the Goire meets the Vienne — hence the name 'confluent'.

The old town climbs the hill in winding lanes. Medieval half-timbered houses, craft shops, small shaded squares. The Saint-Barthélemy quarter keeps its old-world atmosphere.

The church of Saint-Maxime overlooks the upper town. Sculpted Romanesque doorway, square bell tower typical of Poitou. Inside, carved historiated capitals and contemporary stained glass. Free entry.

→

Walk along the Vienne. The towpath leads from the Old Bridge towards the countryside. Anglers, grey herons, abandoned locks. Peaceful atmosphere guaranteed.

→

Canoeing on the Vienne. The watersports centre offers routes from 2 hours to a full day. Calm water, no technical difficulty. Hire includes life jackets and dry bags.

→

Confolens Festival (mid-August). Since 1958, France's biggest folklore festival. Troupes from around the world, costumed parades, free concerts. If you're passing through at this time, it's unmissable.

The Château de Verteuil has overlooked the village from its rocky spur for centuries. Owned by the La Rochefoucauld family for 1,000 years (sold in 2020), it hosted Charles V who planted a yew still standing today. Visit the chapel and main courtyard.

The Moulin de Verteuil at the foot of the castle, with its waterwheel still turning. Now a restaurant with a terrace by the water. The castle + mill + Charente view = the perfect photo.

The church of Saint-Médard (12th century) hides a life-size Entombment in polychrome terracotta. Seven figures around Christ, attributed to the workshop of Germain Pilon. One of the Charente's hidden treasures.

The village lanes lead to the fairground shaded by lime trees, on the banks of the Charente. The Maison du Sénéchal (15th century) with its mullioned windows, remains of the old Protestant temple, wash houses. A Petite Cité de Caractère.

→

Castle visit. Access to the main courtyard and chapel (self-guided or guided depending on availability). Tickets from the wine merchant at the bottom of the drive.

→

Lunch at the Moulin de Verteuil. Terrace facing the waterwheel, local cuisine. Booking advised at weekends. Alternative: Jeux de Pots overlooks the Charente.

→

Hike around the castle. 6.6 km loop through the countryside and hamlets, returning along the Charente banks. Easy, 1h30–2h. Start from the castle car park.

The Hennessy house offers the most spectacular tour: solar-powered boat crossing of the Charente, ageing cellars where the 'angel's share' floats in the air, guided tasting. Booking essential.

Martell, the oldest of the great houses (1715), has renovated its historic site with its Art Deco tower. Tour of the centuries-old cellars and the founder's house. A more intimate atmosphere than Hennessy.

The Château de Cognac, birthplace of Francis I, houses Baron Otard cognacs. Renaissance vaulted cellars, rooftop view over the Charente. French history entwined with the story of cognac.

Old Cognac is best explored on foot: medieval houses with walls blackened by the cognac fungus (the famous Torula), merchants' townhouses, quays where gabares loaded barrels.

The Musée des Arts du Cognac traces the history of this spirit: stills, cooperage, vintage labels, retro advertisements. A good complement to cellar tours. Housed in two townhouses.

→

Tour of a major cognac house. Hennessy for the immersive experience, Martell for intimacy, Rémy Martin for connoisseurs (expert tours available). Allow 1h30 to 2h including tasting.

💡 Book 2–3 weeks ahead in summer
→

Cruise on the Charente. Traditional gabares with commentary (1h30) or licence-free electric boat hire. The river at the pace of the barrels of old.

→

Cycling along the Charente. The Flow Vélo (national route) passes through Cognac. Bike hire available in town. Flat route to Jarnac (15 km) or Saint-Brice (8 km).

The Courvoisier house stands in its château on the banks of the Charente. Tour of the cellars including the famous 'Paradis' where the oldest eaux-de-vie age. Tasting overlooking the river.

The church of Saint-Pierre blends eras: 11th-century Romanesque foundations, a 12th-century crypt where the former Counts of Jarnac rest, later alterations. Tombs of the local lords in the crypt.

The Musée Donation François-Mitterrand occupies a former warehouse. State gifts received during his terms: paintings, sculptures, ceramics from around the world. The former president was born here and rests here.

The Jarnac quays line up trading houses and bourgeois residences. The Charente flows peacefully, white façades reflected in the water. Starting point for river cruises.

→

Tour of the Courvoisier house. More low-key than the Cognac giants, with a family feel. The 'Paradis' cellar holds century-old eaux-de-vie. Tasting included.

→

River cruise Jarnac–Cognac. Licence-free barge for the day or guided gabare. The river winds between vineyards and villages. Manual locks, contemplative pace.

→

Visit to François Mitterrand's birthplace. The house where the former president was born in 1916. Guided tour by appointment, intimate atmosphere.

The medieval castle of Châteauneuf has vanished. Only the name and presumed site near the current fairground remain. However, the Logis de Barqueville (15th century) facing the church forms a handsome ensemble with the square. Pleasant walk along the shaded riverbanks.

The church of Saint-Pierre (12th century) boasts one of the finest façades in the Charente: three sculpted tiers, a horseman representing Constantine crushing paganism. Inside, carved Romanesque capitals and a little snail hidden in high relief.

The river port brings the town to life. A stop on the Charente for pleasure boaters and canoeists. Café terraces, shaded picnic area. Watersports centre in summer.

→

Canoeing on the Charente. The watersports centre hires kayaks and canoes with return shuttle. Routes from 2 hours to a full day. Calm water, swimming possible at designated stops.

→

Swimming at the Bain des Dames. The leisure area, labelled Station Sport Nature, has the only supervised beach on the Charente in the Cognac area. Lawn, toilets, snack bar. Free.

→

Fishing on the Charente. Quiet spots upstream and downstream of town. Zander, pike, carp. Daily fishing licence available at the tobacconist.

The underground church of Saint-Jean is the largest in Europe: 27 m long, 16 m wide, nearly 20 m high, carved into the cliff in the 12th century. Monumental nave, reliquary hewn from the rock, some 170 tombs in the floor. Stunning.

The church of Saint-Jacques at the top of the village: Romanesque façade with Hispano-Moorish carvings, a reminder of the pilgrims passing through on the Way to Compostela. Finely chiselled archivolt portal.

The village tiers like an amphitheatre above the Dronne. Cobbled lanes, white houses with tiled roofs, Spanish-style flower-decked balconies. Classified as one of the Most Beautiful Villages of France since 1993.

The Place Ludovic Trarieux, heart of the village, honours the founder of the League of Human Rights, born here. Lime-tree-shaded terraces, shops, artisans. The spot for a coffee.

→

Visit the monolithic church. Open year-round except 25 Dec and 1 Jan. Bring a layer — it's cool inside. The exceptional acoustics occasionally draw concerts.

💡 Bring a layer (cool inside)
→

Swimming in the Dronne. The managed beach below the village has sand, a shaded lawn, slides. Lifeguards in summer. Canoe hire on site.

→

Canoeing on the Dronne. Gentle routes from the village base. Clear water, wooded banks. Return by shuttle.

→

Artisan workshops. The village is home to potters, painters, sculptors. Open studio-shops in summer. One-off pieces, meet the creators.

The Château de Villebois-Lavalette crowns its hilltop with seven round towers visible for miles. Medieval fortress reworked during the Renaissance, two-storey Romanesque chapel. Guided or self-guided tour with booklet.

The 17th-century covered market hall stands against the castle walls. Chestnut timber frame, stone pillars. On Saturday mornings, the market comes alive: cheeses, poultry, garden vegetables.

The village retains some fine old houses, a restored Romanesque church, and an end-of-the-world feel. At the far edge of the Charente, the Périgord is only a few kilometres away.

→

Guided tour of the Château de Villebois-Lavalette. The association's guides bring history to life: two-storey Romanesque chapel (pilgrims below, lords above), medieval towers, 17th-century residence. Family escape games available.

→

Saturday morning market. Under the 17th-century hall, local producers and village atmosphere. Goat's cheese, free-range chickens, seasonal vegetables, honey. A real country market.

💡 Arrive before 10 am for the best choice
→

Hike around Villebois. Waymarked trails through wooded hills. Mixed-farming landscapes, isolated farms, views of the castle. Several loops from 5 to 12 km.

→

Drive back to Angoulême on back roads. Through southern Charente: vineyards, honey-stone villages, rolling hills. 40 km in about an hour at a gentle pace.
