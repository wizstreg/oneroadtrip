Lot-et-Garonne Road Trip: Medieval Bastides & Villages

Bastides with arcaded squares, plum orchards on hillsides, medieval towers commanding the countryside. Lot-et-Garonne—generous, authentic, timeless.

Discover the fortified bastides and agricultural landscapes of Lot-et-Garonne. Visit Agen with its traditional markets. Explore Nérac's fortified bastide. Wander through Monflanquin's medieval ramparts. Stroll along Villeneuve-sur-Lot's riverbanks. The countryside reveals living, authentic heritage at every turn.

  • Nérac: riverside bastide crown

Saint-Caprais Cathedral blends Romanesque and Gothic architecture in perfect harmony. This UNESCO World Heritage site houses 19th-century frescoes chronicling the story of Christianity in the Agen region.

The Musée des Beaux-Arts spans four Renaissance mansions, showcasing Goya's haunting self-portrait, the Venus du Mas, and Impressionist masterpieces by Sisley.

The Pont-Canal strides across the Garonne River on 23 stone arches spanning 539 meters—France's second-longest canal bridge after Briare.

Wander through Rue des Cornières with its medieval arcades and half-timbered houses now sheltering lively shops and sidewalk cafés.

Place des Laitiers marks where the ancient Saint-Étienne Cathedral once stood before the Revolution. Today it hosts the bustling local market.

→

Head to the Halle du Pin market to sample legendary Agen prunes, hazelnuts from the Lot valley, and silky foie gras.

→

Bike or stroll the canal towpath along the Garonne, where tree-lined banks offer peaceful riverside views.

→

Explore the market stalls at Place Durand to discover Agen's authentic local produce and regional specialties.

The Château-musée Henri IV preserves the north wing of this Renaissance palace. Its gallery with twisted columns evokes the golden age of the d'Albret court in the 16th century.

Follow the Allée des 3000 Pas through the Parc de la Garenne. This 2.5-kilometer promenade created by Queen Margot winds beneath ancient oaks along the Baïse River.

Discover the park's Renaissance fountains: the Fontaine Saint-Jean, Fleurette's grotto, and the Fontaine du Dauphin erected in 1601.

In the Petit Nérac quarter, stroll Rue Séderie lined with half-timbered houses. The Vieux Pont offers lovely views of the old port and quays.

At Place du Général Leclerc, Henri IV's statue commemorates the future king of France's years here with Marguerite de Valois between 1577 and 1582.

→

Follow in the footsteps of literary figures who frequented the court—Marguerite d'Angoulême welcomed Clément Marot and possibly Calvin himself.

→

Browse the market along the Allées d'Albret, where farmhouse cheeses, melons, and seasonal porcini mushrooms tempt food lovers.

→

The Place des Arcades forms the heart of this bastide founded in 1256. Its covered walkways supported by broad pillars frame six remarkably similar half-timbered houses.

The Maison du Prince Noir on the square displays beautiful mullioned windows. Edward of Woodstock stayed here during the Hundred Years' War.

The Gothic Église Saint-André was rebuilt in the 18th century. Its stone portal and 20th-century bell tower dominate the bastide's skyline.

Explore the carrerots—narrow pedestrian lanes topped by pontets (overhead passages) connecting upper floors. The medieval grid plan remains perfectly intact.

The Musée des Bastides explains the creation of these medieval new towns. Scale models and interactive projections bring their history vividly to life.

Climb to Cap del Pech for panoramic views from 181 meters elevation. The rolling countryside evokes Tuscany, with the Château de Biron visible in the distance.

→

Browse the Thursday morning market that has animated the Place des Arcades since 1256.

→

Join a theatrical guided tour with Janouille la Fripouille, a jester-guide who brings the bastide's story to life with humor and insight.

→

In August, the Journées Médiévales transform the village with troubadours, fire performances, and a grand period banquet.

The Pont Vieux (or Pont des Cieutat) dates from 1282. Its irregular arches have spanned the Lot River for over seven centuries, once defended by three fortified towers.

The Chapelle Notre-Dame-du-Bout-du-Pont in rosy brick welcomed river boatmen. This pilgrimage chapel stands sentinel at the medieval bridge entrance.

Place Lafayette forms the heart of this bastide founded in 1264. Its covered arcades shelter cafés and shops around the market hall.

The Porte de Paris and Porte de Pujols are twin square towers that guarded the bastide's entrance, complete with portcullises and machicolations.

The Romano-Byzantine Église Sainte-Catherine consecrated in 1937 preserves splendid 14th- and 15th-century stained glass from the earlier church.

Admire the half-timbered houses in the old center. Their overhanging upper floors gained space without increasing property taxes.

→

Visit the Place Lafayette market to sample Agen prunes, farmhouse cheeses, and seasonal vegetables.

→

Head to the waterfront activity center to rent a canoe, pedal boat, or electric boat on the Lot River.

→

Walk the Lot riverbanks along the towpath. These trails once served to haul barges loaded with wine and timber.

The 16th-century Église Sainte-Foy houses magnificent classified murals. Its Latin cross floor plan opens onto two side chapels.

The Flamboyant Gothic Église Saint-Nicolas is integrated into a town gate. Its bell tower formed part of the medieval defensive system.

Pass through the Porte des Anglais, a superbly preserved medieval entrance marking the northern access to the fortified village.

Wander the lanes between restored half-timbered houses. Rue de la Citadelle and Rue du Temple preserve their timeless charm.

The 19th-century market hall hosts the Sunday market. Its wooden beams shelter local producers and artisans.

The Basilique Notre-Dame de Peyragude crowns the hilltop. Its gleaming zinc dome visible for miles has dominated the Lot valley since 1948.

The Romano-Byzantine edifice contains 47 stained-glass windows depicting Mary's life. Behind the basilica, the primitive church has venerated the Virgin since the year 1000.

Pass through the Porte de Richard, the town's second fortified gate. These vestiges testify to this medieval stronghold's defensive past.

At Place Aliénor d'Aquitaine, admire the restored half-timbered houses. Place du Mercadiel once hosted the medieval market.

Climb the steep cobbled lanes between art galleries and artisan workshops. This classified village preserves its authentic character.

→

From the basilica summit, embrace breathtaking panoramas over the Lot valley and Boudouyssou. The sunset here is spectacular.

→

Visit the Sunday morning market to discover Agen region produce in a convivial atmosphere.

Descend to the Port de Penne on the Lot riverbanks. This Azilian site reveals thousands of years of human occupation.

The 13th-century Église Notre-Dame blends Gothic architecture with 19th-century restoration. Inside, admire the Baroque altarpiece and the Entombment sculpture.

The Renaissance Cloister built in 1540 preserves two elegant galleries. Its varied decoration showcases the refinement of the era.

The Jardin du Cloître landscaped in 1950 follows French formal garden principles. Boxwood parterres, sculpted yews, and a central basin create perfect harmony.

Stroll along Rue Labat lined with 16th-century half-timbered houses. These dwellings testify to Marmande's medieval prosperity.

The statue of Ferline with her pomme d'amour on Place Clemenceau reminds visitors that Marmande is the capital of heirloom tomatoes.

→

Browse the market to taste the famous Marmande tomatoes. Growers and market gardeners display their bountiful harvests.

The Château des Ducs de Duras commands the Dropt valley. This 12th-century fortress remodeled in the 18th century unfolds 35 rooms, with terraces offering exceptional panoramas.

Discover the Hall of the Three Marshals, the kitchen with its hundred faggots, and the bakery. The Durfort family—illustrious musketeers—held court here until the 19th century.

The Maison des Vins offers an educational journey through the vineyard. The vine garden designed by Cap Sciences explains local viticulture.

Explore the old town's cobbled lanes. Stone houses testify to the town's rich winemaking heritage.

→

Taste Côtes de Duras wines at the Maison des Vins. Reds, dry whites, rosés, and sweet wines reveal a 1,230-hectare terroir.

→

Visit surrounding wine cellars. Winemakers welcome visitors for guided tastings and cellar tours.

→

Browse the Monday morning market. Local producers and Duras wines animate the stalls.

Les Bains de Casteljaloux features indoor and outdoor thermal pools. Massage jets, jacuzzis, saunas, and hammams utilize healing thermal waters.

The 15th-century Église Notre-Dame-de-l'Assomption rebuilt in the 17th century displays lovely stained glass. This classified Historic Monument dominates the old center.

The 16th-century Maison du Roy houses the tourist office. Louis XIII and Louis XIV both stayed here, hence its prestigious name.

The municipal park and château hosted Jeanne d'Albret and Henri IV. Vestiges of 14th-century ramparts encircle old Casteljaloux.

Admire the 15th- and 16th-century half-timbered houses with overhanging upper floors. One is classified as a Historic Monument.

→

Head to Lac de Clarens for swimming on the sandy beach. The water sports center, treetop adventure course, and fitness trail animate this 130-hectare site.

→

Rent a canoe or pedal boat to explore the lake. The AquaFun Park offers giant inflatable structures on the water.

→

Treat yourself to a spa treatment or thermal cure. The chloro-sulfated waters relieve rheumatism and neuralgia.

→

Explore hiking trails at the edge of the Landes forest. Maritime pines offer striking panoramic views.
