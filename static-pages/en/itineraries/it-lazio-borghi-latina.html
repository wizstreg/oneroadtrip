Latina Province Circuit: Lepini Mountains and Coast

Where Roman temples grip mountainsides, Cistercian abbeys emerge from morning mist, Caetani fortresses overlook the Pontine Plain, and imperial caves face the Mediterranean—southern Lazio unfolds nine centuries of history between mountain and shore.

Discover Cori and its Roman temples clinging to the Lepini Mountains, then descend to Sermoneta where the Caetani castle watches over the Garden of Ninfa. Cross through Priverno and its Cistercian abbey of Fossanova, reach Fondi and its thousand-year-old Jewish quarter. Pass through Itri, land of Fra' Diavolo, before arriving at Campodimele, the village of centenarians. The coast opens up at Sperlonga, white as the Cyclades, and continues to Gaeta and its split mountain, then Minturno and the vestiges of the Roman city of Minturnae. Southern Lazio alternates sea, stone, and open horizons.

Key highlights (4)
  • Sermoneta: 13th-century Caetani castle commanding the Pontine Plain
  • Fossanova: Italy's oldest Cistercian Gothic abbey
  • Sperlonga: Tiberius' grotto and Hellenistic sculptures facing the sea
  • Gaeta: Split mountain and sanctuary carved into rock

Cori

9.4 City Vibes Corinth

The Temple of Hercules, built between 89 and 80 BC during Sulla's dictatorship, commands Cori's acropolis at 398 meters above sea level. Its Doric tetrastyle façade preserves eight intact columns with their entablature; on the door's architrave, the names of the two commissioning magistrates—Marcus Maglius and Lucius Turpilius—remain legible after more than 2,000 years. Originally covered in colored stucco, the structure so impressed Piranesi that he dedicated an entire series of 18th-century engravings to it. Raphael himself is said to have come to measure it when appointed architect of St. Peter's Basilica. The Church of San Pietro, built within the temple's cella, was destroyed by Allied bombing in 1944.

Of the Temple of the Dioscuri, constructed between the 4th and 2nd centuries BC and restored in Corinthian style around 100 BC, two columns of approximately 9 meters tall remain, still bearing a fragment of architrave. An inscription reveals the names of the magistrates who financed the restoration using the temple's treasury. The cella once housed colossal statues of Castor and Pollux in Parian marble, measuring 1.90 meters tall, depicted armed and holding the reins of 1.70-meter horses. The fragments are now displayed in the Museum of the City and Territory.

The Oratory of the Santissima Annunziata, a national monument built around 1400 commissioned by Spanish Cardinal Pedro Fernández de Frías, is nicknamed "the Scrovegni Chapel of southern Lazio." This small rectangle measuring 8 by 4 meters, vaulted with a pointed barrel vault, is entirely covered in late Gothic frescoes depicting scenes from the Old and New Testaments. On the façade, Cori's oldest coat of arms (circa 1440) sits beside the cardinal's inscription requesting a Pater Noster for his soul.

Step inside the Church of Sant'Oliva, built on the podium of a 3rd-1st century BC Roman temple, three columns of which are still visible in the nave. The complex blends Roman temple, 12th-century medieval basilica, 15th-century Augustinian convent, and the Cappella del Crocifisso (1467-1480) with 16th-century frescoes depicting stories from the Old and New Testaments. The Renaissance cloister, the work of Antonio da Como, features 27 different marble capitals. In the Middle Ages, this is where magistrates swore their oath of fidelity to the communal statutes.

The Museum of the City and Territory, inaugurated in 2000 in the former convent of Sant'Oliva, traces Cori's history from the Bronze Age to modern times. It houses a copy of the Capitoline Minerva from the Temple of the Dioscuri—the original is in Rome—gifted by the city of Rome in 1999, as well as a 1:75 scale model of the Temple of the Dioscuri reconstructing its original appearance.

→

Stroll between Cori Monte and Cori Valle, the two distinct quarters separated by an olive grove: the upper borough climbs in a triangle from the Temple of Hercules (397 m), while the lower settlement centers around Santa Maria della Pietà (270-300 m). The medieval lanes, often covered by vaults, preserve 14th and 15th-century houses and a fountain where, according to tradition, Frederick Barbarossa bathed.

→

Climb to the Sanctuary of Madonna del Soccorso, whose esplanade offers sweeping views of the Lepini Mountains and the Pontine Plain stretching to the sea. The sanctuary commemorates the Virgin's appearance in Cori in 1521, celebrated each year in late June with the Carosello Storico dei Rioni, a historical reenactment in early 16th-century costume.

→

Pass through Piazza Pozzo Dorico, an ancient underground Roman cistern transformed into a silent, secret little square nestled among the alleyways, then continue along Via delle Colonne where vestiges of the ancient forum emerge between the houses.

Sermoneta

Castello Caetani, perched at Sermoneta's highest point at 237 meters altitude, was founded in the 13th century by the Annibaldi family with a 42-meter-tall keep (Maschio) and a counter-tower called Maschietto. In 1297, Pietro Caetani, nephew of Pope Boniface VIII, purchased the castle for 140,000 gold florins. The Caetani family added the Hall of the Barons in the 15th century—22 meters long with four bays separated by pointed arches—and the Camere Pinte, frescoed by an artist from Pinturicchio's school. In 1499, Pope Alexander VI Borgia excommunicated the Caetani and entrusted the castle to Lucrezia Borgia; Antonio da Sangallo then reinforced the walls. Charles V was received here on April 2, 1536, with 1,000 horsemen and 4,000 infantrymen. The guided tour (approximately 1 hour) passes through the prisons where detainees' graffiti still survive.

The Cathedral of Santa Maria Assunta, built in the 13th century on the remains of a Roman temple dedicated to Cybele, preserves Quattrocento frescoes attributed to the school of Benozzo Gozzoli, remarkable for their vivid colors and meticulous detail. The Romanesque bell tower dominates the village's narrow streets.

The Loggia dei Mercanti, an arcade in the historic center, served as a filming location for "Non ci resta che piangere" with Roberto Benigni and Massimo Troisi. The nearby belvedere offers views across the Pontine Plain to the Circeo Promontory; a plaque commemorates Sermoneta's participation in the Battle of Lepanto in 1571, the victory over the Ottoman fleet still celebrated every second Sunday of October.

→

Visit the Garden of Ninfa, an 8-hectare English garden created starting in 1921 by Gelasio Caetani on the ruins of the medieval city of Ninfa, destroyed in 1381. The town then had 1,300 inhabitants, seven churches, two monasteries, and a double ring of walls. Pope Alexander III was crowned here in 1159 in the church of Santa Maria Maggiore, whose ruins still stand. Three generations of Caetani women—Ada, Marguerite Chapin, and Lelia—planted more than 1,300 species from every continent. The New York Times called it "the most beautiful garden in the world." Declared a Natural Monument in 2000.

→

Descend to Valvisciolo Abbey, founded in the 8th century by Greek Basilian monks and occupied by the Templars between the 12th and 14th centuries. In the stone cloister, you can spot a Templar cross carved near the central oculus of the rose window and the mysterious SATOR square, a Latin palindrome adopted by the Templars. According to legend, when Jacques de Molay, the last Grand Master, was burned in 1314, the architraves of Templar churches cracked—a fissure is still visible on the main portal.

→

Wander through Sermoneta's alleyways, passing artisan shops and the Church of San Giuseppe, built in 1525 by the Caetani family, Sermoneta's patron.

The Cathedral of Santa Maria Annunziata, reached by a broad staircase dominating the main square, is Priverno's mother church. It preserves elements of medieval architecture remodeled over the centuries. Several artists from Priverno worked here, including the painter Pietro Coleberti, active in the 15th century and author of frescoes throughout the region.

The Church of San Giovanni Evangelista preserves frescoes dating from the 13th to 15th centuries. The church testifies to Priverno's artistic activity during the Middle Ages, when the town supplied master builders to the region's major Cistercian construction projects.

The Archaeological Museum of Priverno, housed in the municipal palace, traces the territory's history from the first Volscian settlements to the Roman era through a collection of ceramics, inscriptions, and everyday objects from the excavations of Privernum.

The Archaeological Park of Privernum, a few kilometers from the village, lets you walk among the vestiges of the Roman city founded after the conquest of the Volscians. You can make out the mosaics of patrician domus, the bases of a theater, and the remains of a thermal complex, evidence of a prosperous city along the Via Appia.

→

Visit Fossanova Abbey, 5.5 km south of Priverno. Founded in 1135 when Pope Innocent II ceded a Benedictine monastery to the Cistercians of Saint Bernard of Clairvaux, the new church was consecrated in 1208 by Innocent III. Built entirely of travertine, it represents the earliest example of Cistercian Gothic art in Italy. The central nave, with seven bays covered by ribbed vaults, is stripped of all decoration according to the austere Cistercian rule. The cloister has three Romanesque sides and a fourth Gothic side added in the 13th century by Roman marmorari. It was in the abbot's house that Saint Thomas Aquinas died on March 7, 1274, struck down by fever en route to the Council of Lyon; his cell is now a visitable chapel.

→

Stroll through Priverno's alleyways, a village perched on a slope of the Lepini Mountains. The center preserves its medieval layout with narrow lanes, vaulted passages, and limestone palaces. The Svicolando festival brings the streets to life each summer with performances and art installations.

Castello Baronale Caetani, a rare example of a fortress built on flat ground, rises on Fondi's perimeter walls. Its base is a parallelepiped mastio in opus quadratum dating from the late 12th century, completed in the 15th century by the Caetani with an imposing cylindrical tower and machicolated crenellations. The grand rocca has three round bastions. A passetto—a covered walkway reconstructed in wood and steel on the original model—connects the castle to the neighboring Palazzo Caetani, offering a bird's-eye view of the town and countryside. The castle houses the Civic Museum with artifacts from protohistoric to medieval times.

The Cathedral of San Pietro, the historic center's cathedral, was erected on the site of an ancient temple dedicated to Jupiter. Fondi is linked to the Western Schism: in the 14th century, a group of dissident cardinals elected the antipope Clement VII here, dividing Christendom for decades.

The Museum of Medieval Judaism, housed in the former synagogue called "Casa degli Spiriti" in the La Giudea quarter, traces the Jewish presence in Fondi over nearly a thousand years, from the 5th to the 16th century. The community included dyers, fabric makers, merchants, and moneylenders active in Fondi's economy. After the expulsion decrees of 1541 under Charles V and the malaria epidemic of 1633, the inhabitants dispersed. The museum occupies five rooms on two levels in the restored premises of the former synagogue.

→

Walk through the La Giudea quarter, the former Jewish ghetto located in the northeast corner of the historic center. Its uniqueness lies in being integrated into the urban fabric rather than isolated like in most European cities. The low houses, on two floors, connect to one another: the ground floor served as shop or workshop, the upper floor as dwelling. The exterior staircases of the houses converge toward the square of the former synagogue.

→

Stroll around Lago di Fondi, the largest of the three lakes surrounding the town (along with Lago Lungo and Lago di San Puoto). The plain of Fondi, enclosed in a semicircle by the Ausoni Mountains, is one of the main market gardening centers of southern Lazio.

Monte San Biagio, a small village with labyrinthine alleyways climbing a hill between the Ausoni Mountains. The Church of San Giovanni Battista dominates the village. From Viale Littoria below, the panorama embraces the plain of Fondi, the three lakes, and the coast stretching to the Pontine Islands on clear days.

Castello di Itri, perched on Colle Sant'Angelo, has controlled the Via Appia since the 9th century. The first nucleus—a pentagonal tower—was erected in 882 by Duke Docibilis I of Gaeta. His grandson Marino I added around 950 a second tower, taller and wider. In the 13th century, the Caetani built the residential section and the cylindrical tower called the "Tower of the Crocodile": according to legend, prisoners condemned to death were thrown in as prey to a reptile kept in its waters. The walkway connects the towers and offers views to the coast.

The Church of San Michele Arcangelo, in Itri's upper section, is the village's oldest sacred building, dating back to the 11th century. In Arab-Norman style with three naves, it preserves 14th-century frescoes attributed to the Neapolitan Roberto Oderisio. Its square bell tower, decorated with colored majolica plates, is positioned centrally against the façade.

The Museum of Brigandage recounts the life of Michele Pezza, known as Fra' Diavolo, born in Itri on April 7, 1771. Initially wanted for murder, he became a colonel in the Neapolitan army against Napoleon's troops, using the castle's underground passages to ambush enemies on the Via Appia. Captured in 1806, he was hanged in Naples' Piazza del Mercato at age 35. Auber's opera "Fra Diavolo" (1830) and the Laurel and Hardy film (1933) made his name famous worldwide.

→

Climb to the Sanctuary of Madonna della Civita, at 673 meters altitude on Monte Fusco, 13 km from the village in the Aurunci Mountains Park. According to tradition, two Basilian monks fleeing iconoclasm hid an icon of the Virgin and Child from Constantinople; a deaf-mute shepherd found it on the mountain and recovered his hearing and speech. The first historical document of the sanctuary dates from 1174. The icon, called Madonna Nera, was crowned in 1777 and 1877. From the forecourt, spectacular views of the mountains and the Gulf of Gaeta.

→

Hike along the Via Appia Antica: in the Gola di Sant'Andrea, between Itri and Fondi, a 2-km section of the ancient route has been excavated and enhanced. The Roman basalt paving winds between opus quadratum constructions and polygonal walls. On the ruins of a 1st-century BC Roman villa stands a fortino used by Fra' Diavolo as a rear base against the French in 1798.

Campodimele, perched at 647 meters between the Ausoni and Aurunci Mountains, holds the European title of "Village of Eternal Youth." In 2000, it counted 14 centenarians among 660 inhabitants. In 1985, the World Health Organization sent researchers on site: they discovered a cholesterol rate half the Italian average and very low blood pressure. A 2012 study by La Sapienza University in Rome identified a mutation of the ANGPTL3 gene that protects against cholesterol and diabetes. The village is accessible only on foot via its cobblestone lanes that wind between stone houses, covered passages, and small gardens.

The Church of San Michele Arcangelo, at the village's highest point, dates from the 11th century and was built on a former pagan worship site. Damaged by an earthquake, it was faithfully rebuilt. Inside, a tabernacle, ancient paintings, and wooden statues merit attention.

The Monastery of Sant'Onofrio, founded in the 11th century, suffered several destructions over the centuries before being restored in the 1980s. Its position below the fortified enclosure offers a viewpoint over the fertile valley below.

→

Walk the medieval ramparts: the fortified enclosure, erected to protect inhabitants from Saracen raids, comprises twelve recently restored cylindrical towers. A path known to locals as the "Lovers' Walk" (Passeggiata dell'Amore) circles around it, offering panoramic views of the green valleys and Aurunci Mountains. On the Municipio square, an elm planted in 1789 allegedly commemorates the French Revolution.

→

Tasting of local products: the secret of longevity also lies in diet—little red meat, plenty of fruits, vegetables, and legumes from the garden, olive oil from the surrounding terraces. The village is part of "The Most Beautiful Villages in Italy" and has received the orange flag from the Italian Touring Club.

Sperlonga's historic center clings its whitewashed houses to a rocky promontory between two sandy beaches. The name derives from the Latin spelunca (cave), referring to the natural cavities dotting the coast. The alleyways, flowering with bougainvillea, spiral upward to the rock's summit where you can embrace the Gulf of Gaeta on one side and Cape Circeo on the other.

Torre Truglia, a 16th-century watchtower planted on a rocky spur between the two beaches, is Sperlonga's symbol. Destroyed and rebuilt several times after Barbary raids, it surveys the horizon 360 degrees.

The Villa of Tiberius and its National Archaeological Museum: discovered in 1957 during construction of the coastal road, this imperial residence extended 300 meters along the beach. Its natural grotto, converted into a banquet hall, housed five monumental marble sculptural groups in Hellenistic style (circa 180 BC) narrating Ulysses' adventures—the blinding of Polyphemus, the attack of Scylla, the theft of the Palladium. The fragments bear the signatures of sculptors Agesander, Athenodorus, and Polydorus of Rhodes, the same authors of the Laocoön in the Vatican Museums. In 26 AD, a collapse during a banquet killed three slaves; Sejanus shielded the emperor with his body, who then left Sperlonga for Capri. The museum, built in 1963, displays reconstructions of the groups from 15,000 fragments.

→

Swim on the white sandy beaches of the Riviera of Ulysses, within the eponymous Regional Park protecting 434 hectares including more than 100 of marine surface. The coves between the cliffs offer crystalline waters.

→

Boat excursion along the coast toward Gaeta, passing the cliffs and sea caves. The navigation allows you to see the Montagna Spaccata and Monte Orlando from the sea.

Medieval Gaeta unfolds at the foot of the Castello Angioino-Aragonese, an imposing fortress dominating the town and gulf. The medieval quarter preserves a tight network of alleyways, stairways, and vaulted passages descending toward the port.

Via Indipendenza, the main artery of the former comune of Elena (Gaeta's lower section), lines up palaces and shops. Adjacent alleyways plunge toward the fishing port where maritime tradition endures.

The Church of Santissima Annunziata, in the historic center, is a jewel of Gothic architecture whose gilded 15th-century façade is one of southern Lazio's most photographed. The interior houses a Chapel of the Immaculate Conception covered in gold, nicknamed "Grotta d'Oro" (golden cave) for its extraordinary Renaissance decoration.

The Sanctuary of Montagna Spaccata is built in a spectacular fissure of Monte Orlando. According to tradition, the mountain split at Christ's death. You descend 35 steps carved into the rock to the Grotta del Turco, a cavity open to the sea where Saracen ships found refuge in the time of the Duchy of Gaeta. On the wall, a "Hand of the Turk"—a natural imprint in the rock—fueled local legends for centuries.

→

Hike in the Parco di Monte Orlando, a nature reserve on Gaeta's promontory. Panoramic trails pass Bourbon fortifications (Polveriera Carolina), remains of Roman villas, and cliffs plunging into the sea where peregrine falcons nest.

→

Swim at Spiaggia di Serapo, a large fine-sand beach in the town center, one of the most famous on the southern Lazio coast. The sandy arc stretches in a crescent at the foot of Monte Orlando.

→

Detour to Castellone, Formia's medieval quarter, with its octagonal tower dominating the alleyways and the Cisternone Romano, a vast underground cistern from the 1st century BC that supplied water to the ancient city of Formiae.

The Archaeological Complex of Minturnae, on the Via Appia at km 156, preserves the vestiges of the Roman port-city founded in 295 BC on the ruins of an Auruncian town. The 1st-century AD Roman theater, built in opus reticulatum, accommodated more than 4,000 spectators; its restored cavea still serves as a setting for summer performances since 1960. Beneath the tiers, the Antiquarium displays statues, ex-votos, epigraphs, and coins recovered from the Garigliano River. An original section of the Via Appia, paved with basalt blocks, crosses the site. It was at Minturnae that General Gaius Marius, fleeing Sulla's assassins, hid in the marshes before being discovered—an episode recounted by Plutarch.

The Church of San Pietro Apostolo, the main church of Minturno's hilltop village, stands at the heart of the medieval historic center. Some of its marbles come directly from the ancient site of Minturnae, reused over the centuries in local constructions.

The Castello Baronale dominates the historic center from an elevated position. The village of Minturno was called Traetto until 1879, a name inherited from the Middle Ages when inhabitants of the ancient city, driven to the heights by the Lombards around 580 and by the swamping of the plain, rebuilt their village on the hill.

→

Walk through Minturno's historic center (formerly Traetto), a hilltop village with picturesque alleyways and views over the Garigliano valley. The Memory Trail passes commemorative plaques recalling World War II events; near the archaeological site stands the British Commonwealth War Cemetery, where 2,049 soldiers who fell in 1943-1944 rest.

→

Pass by the Ponte Real Ferdinando over the Garigliano, Italy's first iron suspension bridge, designed by engineer Luigi Giura and inaugurated in 1832 by King Ferdinand II of the Two Sicilies. The bridge connected the Papal States to the Kingdom of Naples. Destroyed during World War II, it was restored in 1998 and can be visited.
