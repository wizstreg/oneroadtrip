The Most Beautiful Villages of Grosseto Province

Ten villages from the Metalliferous Hills to the Tuscan archipelago: the deep Maremma reveals its tufa towns, medieval mines, and Spanish fortresses.

Grosseto province is the Tuscany few travelers truly dare: Massa Marittima with its Gothic cathedral on a stone square, Vetulonia the ancient Etruscan city forgotten on its hill, Santa Fiora with its Della Robbia treasures nestled at Monte Amiata's foot. Then the tufa country with Pitigliano the Little Jerusalem clinging to its cliff, Sorano the Tuscan Matera, Sovana and its Etruscan tombs in the forest. Further down, Magliano in Toscana and its witch's olive tree, Capalbio and the Tarot Garden. The coast with Porto Ercole and its Spanish fortresses, where Caravaggio died. And Giglio Island to finish, granite and scrub in the middle of the Tyrrhenian Sea.

Key highlights (6)
  • Massa Marittima: Gothic cathedral and scandalous 13th-century fresco
  • Pitigliano: Little Jerusalem suspended from tufa cliffs
  • Saturnia: natural thermal baths at 37°C with free access
  • Porto Ercole: Spanish fortresses where Caravaggio died
  • Sovana: Etruscan necropolis and rock-carved tombs
  • Giglio Castello: fortified medieval village in the heart of the Tuscan archipelago

Massa Marittima

Top hotels
9.8 Podere Vecchia Commenda
9.2 Agriturismo Pian del Vescovo
9.7 wine apartment le sedici
9.2 Tenuta Aronne

San Cerbone Cathedral sits diagonally across Piazza Garibaldi—a deliberate perspective trick: the architect spaced the facade pilasters unevenly so it appears perfectly symmetrical when viewed from the square's center. Built from the 11th century in Pisan Romanesque style, Giovanni Pisano redesigned the third register of the facade in 1287 and extended the apse in the Gothic forms we see today. It measures 58.72 meters long by 18 meters wide. Inside, the Ark of San Cerbone (1324) by Goro di Gregorio stands as one of the pinnacles of Italian Gothic sculpture. The Maestà attributed to Duccio di Buoninsegna (1316) hangs in the left chapel, while the travertine baptismal font by Giroldo da Arogno dates from 1267.

Piazza Garibaldi, formerly Piazza del Duomo, is the Maremma's most cohesive medieval square. It brings together the Palazzo del Podestà (early 13th century, its facade covered with coats of arms of successive podestas), the Palazzo del Comune, and the Loggia del Mercato. Massa Marittima was a free city in the 13th century before being conquered by Siena in 1335—precisely when the Sienese demolished the upper third of the Torre del Candeliere to punish the rebellious inhabitants.

The Palazzo del Podestà houses Massa Marittima's Archaeological Museum, with a prehistoric section featuring a reconstructed cave and an Etruscan section displaying artifacts from local sites. The town owes its medieval wealth to its argentiferous lead mines in the Metalliferous Hills—the Latin name Massa Metallorum bears witness.

The Museum of Sacred Art in San Pietro all'Orto, adjacent to the Palazzo del Podestà, has housed since 2005 the Maestà by Ambrogio Lorenzetti (c. 1335), a masterpiece of Sienese painting, along with the original Giovanni Pisano sculptures that once adorned the cathedral facade before their removal. The neighboring Augustinian church of Sant'Agostino (late 13th century) features a remarkable pentagonal apse and preserves relics of Saint Bernardino of Siena.

→

The Fonti dell'Abbondanza, a public fountain built in 1265 under Pisan podestà Ildebrandino, served as the walled city's only water source. During restoration in 1999, workers discovered beneath several layers of plaster a 5 x 6 meter fresco dating between 1265 and 1335: the Albero della Fecondità (Tree of Fertility), depicting a tree whose fruits are twenty-five phalluses that women are trying to pick. Interpreted as an apotropaic allegory of abundance, it's one of the rare examples of 13th-century secular painting to survive.

→

The Torre del Candeliere (Candlestick Tower), erected in the 13th century as a symbol of communal authority, takes its name from the custom of maintaining a permanent fire that made it resemble a candle. After the Sienese conquest of 1335, its top was demolished—today it stands a third shorter than originally. The arch connecting it to the Cassero Senese (Sienese fortress) was built by Siena to watch over the inhabitants. From the tower, on clear days, the view reaches as far as Elba Island.

→

The Museo della Miniera, installed in a reconstructed underground gallery beneath the town, traces the millennial history of ore extraction in the Metalliferous Hills. The guided tour descends into the galleries and presents the tools and techniques for extracting argentiferous lead, copper, and pyrite that made Massa's fortune from the 9th to 20th centuries.

Vetulonia

Top hotels
9.4 Il Pino
10 Orto de' Mandorli B&B
9.8 Casetta dell`Uliveto
9.6 Case di Vetulonia

Vetulonia—Vatluna in Etruscan—was one of the twelve cities of the Etruscan League, prospering from the 9th to 3rd centuries BC through metalwork and maritime trade via Lake Prile, now silted by alluvium. The city was rediscovered in 1880 by physician Isidoro Falchi, who identified the site beneath the medieval village of Colonna di Buriano. The archaeological zone of Poggiarello Renzetti, below the village, reveals the remains of the main street and Etruscan shops. The cyclopean walls of the Mura dell'Arce, which encircled a living space of about 16 hectares, are partially visible in the village center.

The necropolis spreads across the hills at the village's foot along the Via dei Sepolcri. The Tomba della Pietrera is the site's largest funerary monument: two tholoi stacked one atop the other, surrounded by a tumulus 60 meters in diameter, dating from the 7th century BC. One of the eight statues that once decorated it—a mourning figure—is now visible in Florence. The Tomba del Diavolino II, accessible through a long partially uncovered corridor, is another major tumulus from the same period. The panorama from the tombs embraces the Grosseto plain, the opposite hill of Roselle, and the sea in the distance.

The Civic Archaeological Museum Isidoro Falchi, dedicated to the archaeologist who rediscovered the city, houses a remarkable collection of objects from excavations: bronze banquet ware, jewelry, helmets, Greek and Phoenician imports testifying to Vetulonia's commercial openness across the entire Mediterranean.

→

Walk along the Mura dell'Arce walls and through the medieval village's narrow streets perched on its hill, with views over the Maremma plain and the Tyrrhenian Sea. The village retains a preserved atmosphere, far removed from the usual tourist circuits.

Santa Fiora

+3
Top hotels
9.2 Abbergà
9.6 Il Gaudente B&B
9.4 Casetta Belvedere - Santa Fiora
9.4 Casa di Nonno Gio' - Tuscan ToBe

The Pieve delle Sante Flora e Lucilla, a parish church built before the year 1000 and reconstructed in the 13th century, preserves one of the world's most important collections of glazed terracotta from Andrea della Robbia's workshop. The pieces, created between 1464 and 1490, include a Coronation of the Virgin, a Saint Francis Receiving the Stigmata, a Madonna della Cintola, and several polychrome altarpieces with the characteristic blue background. The reliquary of Saints Flora and Lucilla, jointly offered by the Sforza and Aldobrandeschi families, bears the arms of both houses and materializes the county's passage from one dynasty to the other in 1439.

The Palazzo Sforza Cesarini, erected in 1575 on the foundations of the Aldobrandeschi fortress whose two medieval towers still survive on Piazza Garibaldi, now serves as the town hall. Dante mentioned Santa Fiora in Purgatorio (VI, 111) when the Ghibelline county of the Aldobrandeschi was prey to pillaging—the plaque affixed to the square reproduces this verse. Since 2002, the palace has housed the Museum of Mercury Mines of Monte Amiata, with 22 panels documenting the technical and social history of mercury extraction, the dominant activity on the massif between the 19th and mid-20th centuries.

The church of Madonna della Neve, a small Renaissance building from the 15th century adjoining the Peschiera, contains a unique feature: its floor is a transparent glass slab beneath which flows the official source of the Fiora River. The river literally springs to life beneath visitors' feet. The interior frescoes, attributed to Francesco Nasini, and a glazed terracotta bas-relief by Della Robbia complete the ensemble.

→

La Peschiera, a medieval basin redesigned in the 16th century by the Sforza for trout farming in the Fiora's waters, was transformed into an English garden in the 19th century. Art critic Cesare Brandi wrote that it alone justified the journey. From its shaded banks, the village walls reflect in the water. It's one of the most photographed sites in Grosseto province, virtually unknown outside Italy.

→

Hiking in the beech forests of Monte Amiata, an extinct volcano reaching 1,733 meters, the highest mountain in southern Tuscany. The massif is geothermally active—steam columns from geothermal plants (including Bagnore 3 and 4) punctuate the landscape. Marked trails depart from Santa Fiora toward the state forest of Selva.

Saturnia

Top hotels
9.2 Saturnia Pian Di Cataverna
9.1 Terme di Saturnia Natural Spa & Golf Resort - The Leading Hotels of the World
9.2 Bio Agriturismo Poggio Aione
9.3 Bio Agriturismo Le Cascatelle

Saturnia rises on a travertine promontory at 294 meters elevation, near the remains of an Etruscan necropolis at Pian di Palma. The Porta Romana, dating from the 2nd century BC, is built over preexisting Etruscan defensive structures. In the village center, the Archaeological Museum of Saturnia preserves artifacts from local excavations spanning prehistory to the Roman era. According to mythology, the town's name derives from the god Saturn himself: angered at mankind, he struck the ground with his thunderbolt, causing the thermal spring to gush forth.

Montemerano, a hamlet of Manciano municipality 7 km from Saturnia, ranks among Italy's "Most Beautiful Villages." Its historic center, developed around the early 11th-century Aldobrandeschi fortress (of which a square tower survives), is a labyrinth of perfectly preserved paved alleys. The church of San Giorgio, built in the 13th century on Piazza del Castello, houses a polyptych by Sano di Pietro, a 15th-century Sienese painter, along with precious medieval frescoes.

→

The Cascate del Mulino (Mill Waterfalls) are a system of natural travertine pools created by Saturnia's thermal spring. The water, laden with sulfur, bicarbonate, and calcium, gushes at 37.5°C with a flow of 800 liters per second and has carved into the rock tiered pools with turquoise waters. Used since Etruscan and then Roman antiquity—a square Roman pool is still visible near the church of Maddalena—these cascades are freely accessible 24/7, year-round. Arrive early morning to enjoy the pools before the summer crowds.

→

Tasting of Morellino di Scansano DOCG wines in the cellars between Saturnia and Montemerano: this Sangiovese-based red produced in the Grosseto hinterland is one of the most renowned Tuscan reds outside the Chianti circuits.

Pitigliano

+5
Top hotels
9.4 La Casa all'Oliveto
9.5 Agriturismo Eco-Bio Villa Vacasio
9.6 Agriturismo Locanda Pantanello
10 Agriturismo Poggio delle Conche

Pitigliano bursts into view around the road's bend like a vision: houses grafted onto the edge of a volcanic tufa spur, the whole carried by the Medici aqueduct that springs toward the town. This aqueduct, commissioned by the Orsini and completed by the Medici between 1636 and 1639, forms 15 arches—two monumental and thirteen smaller—and constitutes the site's postcard image. It terminates at Piazza della Repubblica with the Fontana delle Sette Cannelle (16th century), whose seven spouts shaped as animal heads represented the seven historic quarters.

The Palazzo Orsini, a former Aldobrandeschi fortress transformed into a noble residence by the Orsini family between the 14th and 16th centuries, houses two museums: the Civic Archaeological Museum (with finds from the Poggio Buco necropolis) and the Palazzo Orsini Museum (21 rooms of frescoes, 15th-century paintings, goldsmith work, antique furniture, textiles donated by local parishes). The Orsini coat of arms above the portal depicts a bear—but the Maremman sculptor who executed it had never seen one; the animal rather resembles a dog.

The Cathedral of Saints Peter and Paul, whose origins trace back to the 13th century, presents an 18th-century Baroque facade and a 16th-century Renaissance portal. The interior blends several styles accumulated through restorations. The church of San Rocco, the town's oldest construction, and the sanctuary of Madonna delle Grazie outside the walls complete the village's religious circuit.

The Jewish ghetto and Little Jerusalem: first protected by the Orsini who refused to close its gates despite papal directives, Pitigliano's Jewish community settled in the 15th century, fleeing persecution from the Papal States. It prospered until Italian unification. The synagogue, built in 1598, was restored in 1995 and now serves as a museum of local Jewish culture. Underground, you can visit the unleavened bread oven (forno delle azzime), the kosher wine cellar, the butcher shop, and the women's ritual bath (mikvé), all carved into the tufa. The sfratto—a dry honey-and-walnut biscuit—is the local specialty of Jewish origin: in the 17th century, Medici soldiers would knock (sfrattare = to evict) on ghetto doors at sunset to force Jews to return home. The pastry takes the shape of a soldier's staff.

→

Walk through the Vie Cave around Pitigliano: a network of paths carved by hand into tufa rock by the Etruscans, with walls sometimes reaching 20 meters high over several kilometers. The Via Cava di San Giuseppe and Via Cava di Fratenuti, west of the village on either side of the river, are the most accessible. These passages served as communication routes between settlements and necropolises, probably also as a defensive system against cavalry attacks.

→

Tasting of Bianco di Pitigliano DOC, a Trebbiano Toscano-based white wine produced in natural caves carved into the tufa beneath the village. The underground cellars ensure stable temperature and humidity—ideal conditions for vinification.

Sorano

Top hotels
9.8 Palazzo Palloni Boutique Apartment
9.2 Hotel Della Fortezza
9.4 Appartamento TRE QUERCE di Guerrini Patrizia
9.4 Poderone Vecchio

Sorano, the "Matera of Tuscany," clings to a volcanic tufa cliff above the gorges of the Lente River. The Rocca degli Orsini, of medieval Aldobrandeschi origin and entirely renovated in 1552, is a multi-level fortress whose underground galleries partially date back to Etruscan times. It houses the Civic and Archaeological Museum. At the village's opposite end, the Masso Leopoldino—a fortress built by order of the Lorraine family in the 18th century—offers from its panoramic terrace a 360° view over the Tufa Valley, the Albegna and Fiora valleys, and neighboring villages. Its crenellated tower and clock make it the village's visual landmark.

The Cathedral of San Pietro in Sovana (a hamlet of 350 inhabitants 15 km from Sorano) is considered one of the most important Romanesque monuments in the Maremma. The building, whose origins trace back to the 7th century, exemplifies the transition from Romanesque to Gothic. Its 8th-century ciborium—a rare survivor from this era—ranks among the oldest in Tuscany. Sovana was also the birthplace of Pope Gregory VII (Ildebrando di Soana, c. 1020-1085—his exact birth date remains uncertain, historians place it between 1015 and 1025), whose Gregorian Reform transformed the medieval Church.

The Etruscan necropolis of Sovana, within the Città del Tufo Archaeological Park, is the region's most varied for the quality of its monumental tombs carved into tufa. The Tomba Ildebranda (3rd-2nd century BC) is the most spectacular: a temple with fluted columns of which only one of eight survives, all sculpted directly from the cliff. The Tomba della Sirena, whose entrance is adorned with a bas-relief depicting a siren holding two figures, and the Tomba del Tifone complete the circuit. All are accessible from the park's forest trails.

→

Hike along the Via Cava di San Rocco from Sorano: this Etruscan path carved into tufa descends toward the San Rocco necropolises with walls reaching 20 meters in places. It leads to a panoramic viewpoint over the Lente Valley and Sorano below. A trail then allows walking to Sovana on foot through the countryside.

→

Visit the site of Vitozza (5 km from Sorano, 2 km from San Quirico): a cave village abandoned in the 18th century, one of Italy's largest troglodyte sites with over 200 caves carved into a hill's tufa. Inhabited since at least the 11th century, and probably from Etruscan times, it preserves the remains of a fortress, several churches, and a medieval columbarium for pigeon breeding.

Magliano In Toscana

Top hotels

9.9 Agriturismo L'Aquilone
9.7 Casale Sterpeti

Magliano in Toscana is surrounded by one of Tuscany's best-preserved medieval walls, built in the 9th-10th centuries by the Aldobrandeschi then considerably reinforced by the Republic of Siena in the 15th century. The walkway runs the entire perimeter. Two monumental gates frame Corso Garibaldi: the Porta di San Martino to the north offers a remarkable view over the Maremman countryside; the Porta San Giovanni to the south guards access to the vineyards. The village occupies the site of ancient Roman Heba, itself heir to an Etruscan settlement whose several necropolises have been unearthed in the surroundings.

The church of San Giovanni Battista, on Piazza della Repubblica, has been documented since the 11th century. Its facade was restored in the Sienese style in 1471, and its interior preserves stucco statues and a Renaissance baptismal font from 1493. The church of Pieve di San Martino, older, presents characteristic Romanesque austerity. A few minutes' walk away, the small Civic Archaeological Museum houses the famous Magliano Disc—an Etruscan lead artifact bearing a long circular inscription in both directions, of still-debated interpretation—whose original is preserved at the Archaeological Museum in Florence.

→

The Olivo della Strega (Witch's Olive Tree), just outside Magliano's walls in an olive grove behind the Oratory of Santissima Annunziata, is a monumental tree whose circumference exceeds 8 meters. Medieval tradition held it to be the nocturnal meeting place of women accused of witchcraft. It's one of the oldest and most massive trees in Tuscany.

→

The ruins of San Bruzio Benedictine abbey, 5 km from Magliano in an olive grove, are accessible on foot from the village. Founded around the year 1000, this abbey retains an intact sacred aura despite its ruined state: its gray travertine nave, its arcades open to the sky, and its centuries-worn capitals make it one of the most melancholic and beautiful places in the Maremma.

→

Tasting of Morellino di Scansano DOCG at wineries in the Colli di Maremma zone: this Sangiovese-dominated red, produced on the hills around Magliano and Scansano, has enjoyed DOCG status since 2006. The Vinellando, a wine festival held every year in late August through Magliano's streets, offers the opportunity to discover all the producers in one evening.

Capalbio

Top hotels
9.2 Valle Del Buttero
9.2 Ghiaccio Bosco
9 Resort Capalbio
9.4 Agriturismo Monte Alzato

Capalbio is a medieval village whose urban layout has remained virtually intact since the 15th century. Its double-walled ramparts with walkway, built between the 11th and 15th centuries, can be walked entirely. The Porta Senese preserves its original massive wooden doors from the 15th century. Inside, the Rocca Aldobrandesca adjoins the Palazzo Collacchioni, a richly frescoed Renaissance noble residence. The village was given in 805 by Charlemagne to the abbey of Saints Anastasius and Vincent alle Tre Fontane in Rome—one of the oldest donation acts in the Tuscan territory.

The church of San Nicola, built in the Middle Ages atop the hill around which the village wraps, is an architectural palimpsest: Romanesque, Gothic, and Renaissance are layered one upon another. It preserves a fresco cycle from the Umbrian-Sienese school, and the Oratory of Providence outside the walls contains 18th-century frescoes attributed to Pinturicchio's workshop.

Talamone, 25 km north on the coastal road, is a tiny port dominated by the Rocca degli Aldobrandeschi, a medieval fortress in excellent condition perched on a rocky promontory plunging into the sea. In 1860, Garibaldi stopped here with his Thousand to resupply arms before sailing to Sicily—a plaque commemorates the event. The village retains the atmosphere of an authentic fishing port, with a few hundred permanent residents.

→

The Tarot Garden (Giardino dei Tarocchi), 2 km from Capalbio at the foot of the hamlet of Capalbiole, is a sculpture park created by Franco-American artist Niki de Saint Phalle starting in 1979, inspired by Gaudí's Park Güell in Barcelona. The 22 major arcana of the tarot are represented by monumental sculptures covered in ceramic mosaics, mirrors, and colored glass. Saint Phalle lived for several years inside the Judgement sculpture, transformed into an apartment. The garden opened to the public in 1998.

→

Walk along Capalbio's rampart walkway with panoramas over the Maremman countryside, the Tyrrhenian Sea, and on clear days Giglio Island and Monte Argentario. Local legend reports that Tiburzi, the Maremma's most famous brigand, spent much of his 19th-century flight here.

Porto Ercole

+2
Top hotels
9.1 La Roqqa
9.5 CASA SAN ROCCO
9.6 Isolotto Guest House

Porto Ercole is guarded by three Spanish fortresses built in the 16th century during the Hispanic domination of the State of Presidi. Forte Stella—whose six-pointed star shape allowed enemy targeting from each point—was begun in 1548 by Giovan Battista Belluzzi and completed by Giovanni Camerini under Cosimo I de' Medici; it's the most accessible and most photographed. The Rocca Spagnola, an irregular four-bastion star form, dominates the borgo antico. Forte Filippo, on the northern promontory, was built for Philip II of Spain according to a design by Giovanni Camerini.

The borgo antico of Porto Ercole, accessible only on foot from the Porta Pisana topped by a clock tower, is a labyrinth of alleys and staircases clinging to the hillside. Piazza Santa Barbara, above the bastion of the same name, offers the village's most iconic panorama over the bay and fortresses. The Palazzo dei Governatori, built in the early 16th century under Siena, served as residence of the Spanish commander and then the summer governor of the State of Presidi. The church of Sant'Erasmo (17th century), at the village's highest point, is the tallest building.

Caravaggio died in Porto Ercole on July 18, 1610, at age 39, in circumstances still debated. Fleeing a death sentence for murder, he had left Naples with three paintings intended for Cardinal Scipione Borghese, the price of an expected papal pardon. His death certificate, found in 2001 in the Sant'Erasmo parish register, indicates he died at the Hospital of Santa Maria Ausiliatrice, probably from malaria. A monument honors him in the historic center.

→

The Duna Feniglia Nature Reserve is a wooded dune 6 km long separating the open sea from the Orbetello lagoon. A dirt path traverses it under pines, inhabited by semi-wild fallow deer. According to local tradition, it was on this beach that Caravaggio was found dying in 1610—though his death certificate found in 2001 indicates he died in hospital, the exact circumstances remaining debated. Car access is prohibited: the dune is explored on foot or by bicycle.

→

Orbetello, a town perched on the narrow sand strip between the two tomboli of Argentario, is a discovery in its own right: 16th-century Spanish walls, compact old town, lagoon classified as a nature reserve (flamingos, herons, egrets). From the lagoon's edge, the view of Monte Argentario's green promontory rising from the sea is one of the most beautiful on the Tuscan coast.

→

Spiaggia Lunga beach, accessible from Porto Ercole by a steep path descending behind Forte Stella. Sand and pebble beach below the fortress, surrounded by Mediterranean scrub. Further along the same trail, the Acqua Dolce beach, accessible behind the Hotel Pellicano, is one of Argentario's most preserved coves.

Giglio Castello

9.5 Casa girasole

Giglio Castello, a fortified medieval village at 400 meters elevation in the island's center, is surrounded by 13th-century walls whose certain sections exceed 10 meters in height. Giglio Island, whose name derives not from the "lily" (giglio) but from the Greek aigulion ("goat"), was given by Charlemagne to a Roman abbey in 805 before passing to the Aldobrandeschi, the Pisans, the Orsini, the Piccolomini, then to Eleanor of Toledo, wife of Cosimo I, in 1558.

The Rocca Pisana (also called Rocca Aldobrandesca), a fortress built by the Pisans in the 12th century at the village's highest point, was designed to withstand long sieges: inside were a deep cistern, a vegetable garden, food stores, a poultry yard, and a dovecote, all ensuring self-sufficiency in case of attack. It communicated with the coast by fire and smoke signals.

The church of San Pietro Apostolo (15th century), in the alleys of Giglio Castello, houses the relics of San Mamiliano, patron saint of the island whose cult is one of the oldest and most vibrant in the Tuscan archipelago, as well as a gold chalice offered by Pope Innocent XIII. A Corinthian capital from the Roman villa of Giglio Porto is embedded in the walls.

Giglio Porto, the landing point for ferries from Porto Santo Stefano (crossing about 1 hour), retains the atmosphere of an authentic fishing port with its colorful houses and the medieval Torre del Saraceno. In the Cala del Saraceno behind the Medici tower, the Cetariae—fish-farming basins carved into rock by the Romans in the 1st century AD for the Domizi Enobarbi family—are visible in the port's clear waters.

→

Ferry crossing from Porto Santo Stefano to Isola del Giglio. The crossing takes about an hour and offers views of Monte Argentario, the Tuscan archipelago, and on clear days, Corsica and Montecristo Island.

→

Hiking trail Giglio Porto - Giglio Castello: a 45-minute climb on a stone-paved path traversing Mediterranean scrub (garrigue, mastic, strawberry trees). The view over the coast and archipelago widens as you ascend. The same trail continues to Giglio Campese (west coast) with its large sandy beach and the 18th-century Torre di Campese—last remnant of a pirate attack in 1799.

→

Campese beach, the largest beach in the Tuscan archipelago, in an open bay on the island's west side. Fine sand, clear waters, sunset facing the sea. In late afternoon, the same ferries return to Porto Santo Stefano.
