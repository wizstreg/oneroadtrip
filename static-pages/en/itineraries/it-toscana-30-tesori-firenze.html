Treasures Around Florence: A 15-Stop Loop

UNESCO Medici villas, Giambologna's stone giant hidden in forest, Pontormo guarded by a sleeping village, abbey woods and white truffles—the Florentine hills haven't given up all their secrets.

Just 8 kilometers from Florence, Fiesole conceals beneath its lanes a 1st-century Roman theater still in use and the first Renaissance villa, where Lorenzo the Magnificent gathered Plato and Ficino. From there, the loop opens northward through the wooded hills of Mugello—Pratolino and its 14-meter stone giant, Scarperia and its knives forged since 1306, Vicchio birthplace of Giotto and Fra Angelico—before heading west through Vinci and Leonardo's birthplace, Artimino with its villa of a hundred chimneys, Poggio a Caiano and its Pontormo frescoes. Prato and its Filippo Lippi, Carmignano and its Visitation, Impruneta and its terracotta, Greve and its Chianti Classico, Sammezzano and its Moorish halls locked in secret, Vallombrosa and its thousand-year firs, San Miniato and its white truffle: fifteen stops within a thirty-kilometer radius, a territory that shaped the Renaissance.

Fiesole

The archaeological area of Fiesole—Roman theater, thermal baths, Etruscan-Roman temple, and museum—spreads across three hectares in the heart of the hillside. Fiesole (ancient Faesulae) was founded by the Etruscans between the 9th and 8th centuries BC and only became a Roman colony in the 1st century BC, after the Romans had already settled in the valley below to establish Florence. The theater, carved into the hillside in the 1st century BC, still hosts performances each summer during the Estate Fiesolana festival. The layered Etruscan-Roman temple—first dedicated to Minerva (a bronze owl attests to this), then converted to a Roman temple in the 1st century BC—was destroyed by fire following the Roman conquest. Cyclopean Etruscan walls remain visible on the site's northern edge. The museum, built in 1914 as an Ionic temple, preserves a fragment of a bronze Capitoline wolf and the Costantini collection of Greek and Etruscan ceramics.

The Villa Medici in Fiesole, built between 1451 and 1457 by Michelozzo for Giovanni di Cosimo de' Medici—son of Cosimo the Elder—is the first pleasure villa of the Renaissance: it broke definitively with the medieval fortress model by making the loggia and panorama the pivots of its architecture. Lorenzo the Magnificent inherited it in 1469 and turned it into a meeting place for the Platonic Academy: Marsilio Ficino, Pico della Mirandola, and Poliziano philosophized here. The villa has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2013. A singular detail: this is where the Medici first introduced citrus cultivation to Tuscany, imported from southern Italy. An assassination attempt on Lorenzo the Magnificent by the Pazzi family took place during a gathering within these walls.

Villa Peyron al Bosco di Fontelucente, 2 km from central Fiesole, is a 20th-century Italian garden designed between 1934 and 1960 by Paolo Peyron—without an architect, following only his own instinct—across 40 hectares of wooded estate. Thirty terraced levels descend toward Florence, linked by double-hedged boxwood parterres, 29 fountains fed by a 16th-century spring, statues in Vicenza stone (replacing originals destroyed during the war), and an artificial lake. Bequeathed to the Bardini-Peyron Foundation in 1998, the complex is classified as an exemplary 20th-century garden by Florence's Monuments Authority.

→

Panoramic views of Florence from Piazza Mino da Fiesole or from the Villa Medici gardens, with vistas over Brunelleschi's dome, the bell towers of Santa Croce, and the Arno Valley extending to the Apennines. Evening light is particularly striking.

→

Hike the Sentiero dei Borghi trail between Fiesole and Settignano, through olive groves and historic villa gardens. The path follows the Etruscan walls for part of the route.

The Parco Mediceo di Pratolino (Villa Demidoff), a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2013, is the only major Medici park whose original villa was demolished—in 1821 by the Lorraine dynasty, who found the upkeep too expensive. The 30-hectare estate was created by Francesco I de' Medici beginning in 1568: he commissioned Bernardo Buontalenti to design a 'garden of marvels' with hydraulic surprise fountains, artificial grottoes, and water features that would spread the fashion for scherzi d'acqua (water tricks) throughout Europe. Montaigne visited Pratolino in 1580-1581 and left an enthusiastic description in his Travel Journal.

The Colossus of the Apennines, carved from rock between 1579 and 1583 by Jean de Boulogne (Giambologna), stands 14 meters tall. The statue embodies the Apennine mountain chain: its skin covered in simulated stalactites and algae shows it emerging from the mountain itself. The interior contains three levels of secret rooms: a lower grotto once adorned with frescoes and mother-of-pearl, two mid-level salons where musicians performed during Medici festivities, and a fireplace in the skull—when lit, smoke would pour from the giant's nostrils. Construction reportedly cost double that of the Uffizi. An old Italian rhyme laments that such a masterpiece is hidden in the woods rather than displayed in the city.

The Buontalenti Chapel, with its hexagonal plan surrounded by a loggia, is one of the few original buildings from the Medici estate still standing. The Cupid Grotto, also by Buontalenti (1577), once contained a pivoting bronze statue that would spray visitors who stepped on a hidden trigger plate. The Statue of the Mugnone, personifying the local river by Giambologna, was reconstructed around 1830.

→

Free walking in the park among centuries-old trees—oaks, cedars, horse chestnuts. The park is open free of charge on weekends and certain weekdays; deer, foxes, and hares roam semi-wild within its grounds.

The Palazzo dei Vicari has dominated Scarperia's main square since the town's foundation by the Florentine Republic on September 7, 1306. Conceived as a military outpost on the only road between Florence and Bologna, it became in 1415 the seat of the Florentine Vicariate administering all of Mugello. The facade bears the stone and Della Robbia majolica coats of arms of all the vicars who served here. Inside: a fresco from the school of Ghirlandaio (1501) depicting Saint Barnabas offering Scarperia to the Virgin surrounded by Florence's patron saints, and the ancient bell tower clock documented as the work of Brunelleschi—the only proof of his activity in clockmaking.

The Museum of Cutting Blades (Museo dei Ferri Taglienti), installed in the Palazzo's north wing restored in 1999, traces 700 years of knife-making in Scarperia. Since the town's founding in 1306, its strategic position on the trans-Apennine route ensured the spread of local knives throughout Europe. By the 15th century, Scarperia's reputation was international. In 1908, a law prohibiting knives exceeding the palm of the hand nearly ruined the craft; it survived and has experienced a revival since the 1990s. The museum presents the different phases of production—forging, tempering, sharpening, handle assembly.

→

Visit an artisan knife workshop in the historic center. Several independent cutleries offer demonstrations of forging and assembly using traditional Mugello techniques.

→

The Palio del Diotto, every September 8: the town's neighborhoods compete in Renaissance costumes on the main square, celebrating the Nativity of the Virgin. The evening before features Renaissance Days with processions, falconers, and fire spectacles.

Vicchio

Vicchio is the birthplace of two painting giants: Giotto di Bondone (born around 1267 in Vespignano) and Fra Angelico (born around 1395 in Vicchio). The Giotto House-Museum in Vespignano—a medieval farmhouse on the hill where, according to tradition, Cimabue discovered him drawing his sheep on stones—is now a cultural space open on weekends and during events. Vasari recounts this tradition and turned it into an emblematic story of artistic transmission. A bronze statue of Giotto with palette and brushes stands in Vicchio's main square.

The Museum of Sacred Art and Popular Religiosity Beato Angelico, inaugurated in 2000, was created in the 1960s to save artworks from rural churches abandoned during agricultural exodus. It preserves paintings and sculptures from the Middle Ages to the 18th century: a glazed terracotta Madonna and Child attributed to Andrea della Robbia, a Saint John the Baptist from the same workshop, and Gothic altarpieces from about fifteen Mugello churches. Of note: the former house of Benvenuto Cellini, who stayed in Vicchio between 1559 and 1571, now houses a goldsmithing school.

→

Painters' Trail: a marked hiking route linking Vespignano to the Cimabue Bridge, a late 13th-century medieval bridge over the Sieve River, symbolic site of the legendary meeting between Cimabue and the young Giotto. The route crosses the same landscapes of cultivated hills that inspired the painters of Mugello.

The Pieve di San Lorenzo, founded in 941, is the largest rural Romanesque building in the Florentine countryside. Its hexagonal bell tower with a semi-dodecagonal brick base, placed directly on the apse, is unique in Tuscany. The interior preserves a Madonna attributed to Giotto, a Crucifix from Giotto's school, a Virgin Enthroned attributed to Agnolo Gaddi, and a Virgin with Saints by Matteo Rosselli. The apse is entirely frescoed by Galileo Chini. On the right side facade, a polychrome terracotta tabernacle by Fornaci Chini (1926), created for the centenary of Saint Francis's death, is one of the finest street pieces of the Art Nouveau movement in Tuscany.

The Chini Museum in Villa Pecori Giraldi traces the history of the Fornaci San Lorenzo manufactory, founded in 1906 by Galileo and Chino Chini, two cousins born in Borgo San Lorenzo. Galileo Chini was one of the leading representatives of Italian Art Nouveau: his ceramics—first with floral motifs inspired by Botticelli, then with metallic-luster Deco designs—adorn the Salsomaggiore thermal baths, several Florentine palaces, and Borgo San Lorenzo's town hall. In 1911-1913, the King of Thailand invited him to Bangkok to decorate the Sathon Throne. The villa's rooms are frescoed by the Chini family: a high-quality Saint George cycle, medieval geometric motifs, and coats of arms of Florentine nobility.

→

Art Nouveau Trail of Mugello: a walking or driving route among Chini works scattered throughout the town—Misericordia facade, mayor's office decorated on floors and windows, Santissimo Crocifisso oratory. Borgo San Lorenzo was named 'Città della Ceramica' (City of Ceramics) by the Italian Ministry of Economic Development in 2017.

The Museo Leonardiano occupies two buildings: the Palazzina Uzielli and the 11th-century Castello dei Conti Guidi, given to the municipality in 1919 for the 400th anniversary of Leonardo's death and opened to the public in 1953. It presents Leonardo's machines reconstructed from his notebooks—war engines, hydraulic devices, flying mechanisms—as well as a section dedicated to the Battle of Anghiari, the unfinished mural work Leonardo designed for the Salone dei Cinquecento in Palazzo Vecchio in Florence, known only through contemporary copies. The Leonardian Library, founded in 1883, preserves manuscripts and facsimiles of the codices.

The birthplace in Anchiano, 3 km from central Vinci, is the rural farmhouse where Leonardo was born on April 15, 1452. The birth was noted by his paternal grandfather Antonio da Vinci on the last page of his family notebook: 'A grandson was born to me, son of my son Ser Piero, on April 15, a Saturday at three in the morning. He received the name Lionardo.' The house, documented since 1427, was donated to the municipality in 1952 by Count Giovanni Rasini. Since 2012, a life-size holographic installation of the aging Leonardo, speaking from Amboise while looking back toward his homeland, greets visitors. The landscape of vineyards and olive groves of Montalbano has remained very close to what Leonardo knew as a child.

→

Strada Verde (Green Route): a marked 3 km trail linking Vinci to Anchiano through the vineyards and olive groves of Montalbano, in the same landscape that inspired Leonardo. Details from the landscape of the Mona Lisa have been identified in this area.

Artimino

The Villa Medici 'La Ferdinanda,' built in just four years from 1596 to 1600 by Bernardo Buontalenti for Grand Duke Ferdinand I de' Medici, is the last great Medici villa and the masterwork of Buontalenti's old age—too old to travel, he directed the works from his house on Via Maggio in Florence by correspondence. Its nickname, 'Villa of the Hundred Chimneys,' comes from Ferdinand I's desire to equip each room with its own hearth for winter hunting seasons: the chimneys, all different, bristle from the roof like a crown. The interiors are frescoed by Domenico Cresti (Il Passignano) and Bernardino Poccetti. The villa has been a UNESCO site since 2013.

The Archaeological Museum of Artimino 'Francesco Nicosia,' reinstalled since 2011 in the former grand-ducal stables of the village, displays Etruscan finds from the region: tumuli of Montefortini and Boschetti at Comeana (7th century BC, Orientalizing period), necropolis of Prato Rosello in the Artimino hills, and a 4th-century BC Etruscan red-figure krater with its perfectly preserved bronze symposium set. The Pietramarina archaeological area completes the Carmignano Archaeological Park.

The Pieve di San Leonardo ad Artimino, at the village gates, is one of the most significant examples of Romano-Lombard architecture in Tuscany. Its foundation is attributed to Countess Matilda of Canossa. The sober, compact building contrasts with the Medici Villa facing it.

→

Tasting of Carmignano DOCG in a cellar in the Artimino hills. This red wine produced exclusively in the municipality of Carmignano is one of Italy's oldest appellation wines: Cosimo III de' Medici officially delimited its production zone in 1716. The dominant grape is Sangiovese, often blended with Cabernet Franc or Cabernet Sauvignon introduced to the region as early as the 17th century.

The Villa Medici at Poggio a Caiano, designed by Giuliano da Sangallo for Lorenzo the Magnificent beginning in 1485 and completed under Pope Leo X in 1519, is the prototype of the Renaissance villa: raised on an arcaded podium with an Ionic loggia on the facade, it established the relationship between building and landscape that would influence all European villas for two centuries, including Palladio's. A UNESCO site since 2013, it was the scene of three historic Medici marriages: Alessandro de' Medici and Margaret of Austria (1536), Cosimo I and Eleanor of Toledo (1539), Francesco I and Bianca Cappello (1579).

The Salone di Leone X, on the first floor, houses one of the most important fresco cycles of Florentine Mannerism. The painters were summoned under Leo X (son of Lorenzo the Magnificent) around 1520 according to a program by Paolo Giovio: Pontormo painted Vertumnus and Pomona (an allegory of the seasons and agriculture), Franciabigio the Triumph of Cicero, Andrea del Sarto the Tribute to Caesar (alluding to gifts received by Lorenzo from the Sultan of Egypt in 1487). Leo X died in 1521, the works stopped. They were only completed in 1578-1582 by Alessandro Allori under Francesco I. Since 2007, the second floor houses the Museum of Still Lifes: 200 paintings from the late 16th to mid-18th century from the Medici collections.

→

Walk in the villa park and visit the former stables renovated in 2000. The garden preserves rare plant species and terracotta statues. The complex includes the Cascine di Tavola—Medici agricultural estate—which defined the model of the 'villa-azienda' combining prestigious residence with productive rural exploitation.

Prato

The Cathedral of Santo Stefano in Prato is the only Italian cathedral with an outdoor pulpit—on the right corner of the facade, under a bronze canopy by Michelozzo, the 'Pergamo del Santo Cingolo'—from which the Holy Belt of the Virgin is solemnly displayed five times a year to the people of Prato. The relic, brought from Jerusalem in 1141 by merchant Michele Dagomari, triggered construction of the Chapel of the Belt (14th century), frescoed by Agnolo Gaddi. The main chapel houses Filippo Lippi's fresco cycle (1452-1466): Stories of Saint Stephen and Saint John the Baptist, which Vasari judged 'the most excellent of all his things.' During these years, Lippi painted Lucrezia Buti, a nun from the convent of Santa Margherita, as his model for the Virgin—and abducted her on the very day of the Belt procession in 1456. Cosimo de' Medici intervened with Pope Pius II to obtain their pardon.

The Imperial Castle of Frederick II is the only medieval building of this type preserved in Tuscany and the sole example of a Swabian fortress north of the Alps. Built between 1237 and 1248 for Holy Roman Emperor Frederick II of Hohenstaufen, it served to collect taxes and duties on the territory and to symbolize imperial power against papal authority. Square plan, four corner towers, no ornamentation: an architecture of power.

The Textile Museum traces 700 years of Prato's cloth-making history, which made Prato the European capital of textiles in the Middle Ages. The city owes its medieval wealth to merchant Francesco Datini (1335-1410)—whose house-museum is also open to the public—who built one of Europe's first multinational enterprises with branches in Barcelona, Valencia, Genoa, Paris, and London. His 150,000 business letters preserved in the Prato State Archives form the world's most important medieval commercial archive.

→

Luigi Pecci Center for Contemporary Art, on Prato's outskirts: one of the few Italian museums entirely dedicated to contemporary art since 1988. The ring-shaped 2016 extension by Dutch architect Maurice Nio doubles the exhibition space to 3,000 m².

Pontormo's Visitation (1528-1530) is preserved in the Church of Saints Michael and Francis in Carmignano—currently undergoing restoration for water infiltration. During the works, the painting is temporarily displayed at the Villa Medici of Poggio a Caiano. The painting (202 × 156 cm, oil on wood panel) depicts the meeting of Mary and Elizabeth in a city street, surrounded by two witnesses with fixed, vacant gazes. Acid colors—reds, greens, and iridescent grays—elongated bodies apparently lifted on tiptoe, monumental drapery: Florentine Mannerism at its most accomplished. American artist Bill Viola drew inspiration from it for his video The Greeting (1995).

The Tumuli of Montefortini and Boschetti at Comeana, in the hills below the village of Artimino, are monumental tombs from the Orientalizing period (7th century BC) among the best preserved in central Tuscany. The Montefortini tumulus, about 60 meters in diameter, houses two burial chambers accessible for visits.

→

Hiking or cycling through the vineyards of the Carmignano DOCG route, between the medieval village of Artimino and the wine estates of the Montalbano hills. In October the Palio dei Ciuchi takes place, a traditional race of the town's four neighborhoods on donkeys in medieval costumes.

The Basilica-Sanctuary of Santa Maria all'Impruneta was consecrated on January 3, 1060, on the site of an Etruscan place of worship. Its foundation rests on the legend of the discovery of an icon of the Virgin during construction: the oxen carrying the materials knelt at a precise point, indicating the site of the sanctuary. This Madonna—a 12th-century painted panel—has since been the principal Marian devotion of Tuscany. In 1711, she was proclaimed co-patron of Florence. The left chapel preserves a Madonna and Child by Giovanni Pisano (1317); the Treasury Museum houses a cross attributed to Ghiberti and illuminated choir books from the 14th-16th centuries.

Impruneta has been the world's principal center of terracotta production since at least 1098 (first documented mention). The local clay, refractory and extremely durable, was used by Brunelleschi for the tiles of the dome of Santa Maria del Fiore, and by the Della Robbia workshops for their glazed ceramics. The oil jars and garden vases of Impruneta adorn the gardens of Tuscany's greatest villas.

→

Visit an active terracotta workshop in Impruneta: throwing, drying, firing, and glazing according to traditional techniques. Production covers jars, tiles, sculptures, and garden pots—the same ones that adorned the Uffizi and Medici palaces.

→

The Fair of Saint Luke, October 18 in Impruneta, is one of Tuscany's oldest fairs, attested since the Middle Ages: originally a cattle fair linked to the Madonna procession, it now brings together markets, banquets, and horse races on Piazza Buondelmonti.

Greve In Chianti

Piazza Matteotti in Greve in Chianti has been the commercial center of the vineyard for five centuries. Its atypical triangular shape—surrounded by arcades under which merchants displayed wines and oils—is crossed by the Via Chiantigiana, the only road between Florence and Siena until the 18th century. At the center stands a statue of Giovanni da Verrazzano, born in 1485 at the nearby Castello di Verrazzano, who discovered New York Bay in 1524—his name is preserved in the Verrazano-Narrows Bridge between Brooklyn and Staten Island. In 1716, Cosimo III de' Medici officially delimited the Chianti production zone here, in the first European legal act delimiting a wine appellation.

The medieval village of Montefioralle, 2 km from Greve, is classified among Italy's Most Beautiful Villages. Still enclosed by its medieval walls, it preserves a compact plan of stone houses and streets virtually unchanged since the 13th century. The family of Amerigo Vespucci, the Florentine navigator who gave his name to America, originated from this village.

→

Tasting of Chianti Classico DOCG in cellars of hillside estates. In 1906, major landowners founded the Chianti Wine Union here, precursor to today's Consorzio. The Gallo Nero (Black Rooster) label, emblem of the town, certifies the strictly local origin of the wines. The Chianti Classico Expo, every second weekend of September, brings together about sixty producers in the main square.

→

The Abbey of Badia a Passignano, 8 km northwest of Greve, is a Vallombrosian abbey founded in the 11th century. The Antinori group vinifies Chianti Classico Riserva Badia a Passignano in its medieval cellars, and produces its Tignanello nearby—one of the first Super Tuscans. Estate visits and tastings can be reserved.

Figline Valdarno is the birthplace of Marsilio Ficino (1433-1499), the Neoplatonist philosopher who, under the patronage of Cosimo de' Medici then Lorenzo the Magnificent, translated into Latin the entire works of Plato (1482 edition), Plotinus's Enneads, and the Corpus Hermeticum—thus restoring to the West texts lost since Antiquity. He directed the Platonic Academy of Florence, founded by Cosimo de' Medici in 1459. The main square bears his name. The Collegiate Church of Santa Maria (founded 1257) houses the Madonna Enthroned by the Master of Figline (1317) and a terracotta Saint Joseph attributed to Andrea della Robbia.

The Castello di Sammezzano, 8 km north in Leccio (municipality of Reggello), is a Moorish castle transformed between 1853 and 1889 by Marquis Ferdinando Panciatichi Ximenes d'Aragona—a Florentine noble of Spanish origin. Inspired by the Alhambra in Granada, he had 365 rooms built decorated with arabesque ceilings, stalactite vaults, polychrome mosaics, and horseshoe arches, employing only local artisans. In 1878, King Umberto I was received here. The castle is currently closed to the public for restoration work (new ownership since April 2025, reopening envisioned at the earliest in 2028). The 65-hectare park—one of Tuscany's largest, with Italy's largest group of giant sequoias—remains accessible.

→

Hiking in Castello di Sammezzano park among giant sequoias, romantic alleys, and Moorish architectural elements of the garden (bridge, artificial grotto with statue of Venus, pools). Ideal in spring and autumn.

The Abbey of Vallombrosa (Santa Maria di Vallombrosa) was founded in 1036 by Giovanni Gualberto—a Florentine nobleman who had renounced his court life after granting forgiveness to his brother's murderer before the Crucifix of San Miniato al Monte, seeing Christ bow his head in approval. He retreated to these Apennine forests at 1,000 meters altitude to found the Vallombrosian Congregation, recognized by Pope Urban II in 1090. The abbey took its current form in the 15th century, with expansions in the 16th and 17th centuries. The church preserves Lorenzo Lippi's Trinity, a glazed terracotta altarpiece from Andrea della Robbia's workshop, and the reliquary of the arm of Saint John Gualberto by Florentine goldsmith Paolo Sogliano (1500). Perugino's Assumption (1498-1500), once on the altar, is now in Florence's Accademia Gallery. John Milton visited the abbey in 1638 and evoked its forests in Paradise Lost. Lamartine dedicated a poem to it in his Harmonies (1830).

→

Forest walk in Vallombrosa forest, a biogenetic nature reserve since 1973: centuries-old beeches, white firs, and Scots pines in a dense, silent undergrowth. The Sentiero del Calvario climbs from the abbey toward the Hermitage of Paradisino (11th century), where anchorite monks retreated to isolated cells—panoramic view over the Arno valley.

The Tower of Frederick II in San Miniato, rebuilt in 1958 after 1944 bombings, dominates from the highest point of the town. In the Middle Ages, San Miniato was called 'San Miniato al Tedesco': Otto I of Saxony made it the center of German imperial administration in central Italy in 962, and Frederick II of Swabia installed tax collection for all central Italy here in 1218. Bishop Sigeric of Canterbury stopped here in 990 during his pilgrimage to Rome on the Via Francigena. From the tower, the view embraces the Valdarno, the hills of Volterra, and, on clear days, the Apennines.

The Cathedral of Santa Maria Assunta and San Genesio, begun in the 12th century, has a sober facade adorned with 26 ceramic bowls mainly of Tunisian origin—the originals are in the Diocesan Museum. The baroque interior preserves altarpieces by Francesco d'Agnolo called Lo Spillo and Cosimo Gamberucci. On July 22, 1944, an American artillery shell killed 55 civilians sheltering in the church, gathered there by German soldiers—one of the darkest pages of Tuscany's liberation.

The archaeological area of San Genesio, a few kilometers from the center, is one of the historic sites of the Via Francigena mentioned by Bishop Sigeric around 990 AD. Recent excavations have uncovered the foundations of an important medieval town.

White truffle hunting in the forests around San Miniato with a guide and his dogs. The white truffle of San Miniato (Tuber Magnatum Pico) represents about one-fifth of national production. The National White Truffle Fair takes place the last three weekends of November since 1969; in 1954, a truffle weighing over 2 kg was presented to President Eisenhower.
