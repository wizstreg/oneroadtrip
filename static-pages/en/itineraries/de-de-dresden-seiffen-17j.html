Saxony Road Trip: Dresden, Leipzig & Saxon Switzerland

Augustus the Strong traded 600 Prussian soldiers for 151 porcelain vases. His palaces still stand. His collections remain. This itinerary follows his footsteps from Dresden to Pillnitz, then veers toward the sandstone cliffs, the Polish frontier, and the wooden toy workshops of the Ore Mountains.

Dresden

The Zwinger is Dresden's most iconic monument. Built from 1709 by Matthäus Daniel Pöppelmann for Augustus the Strong, Elector of Saxony and King of Poland, it began as an orangery and princely festival arena. Its official inauguration took place in 1719 for the marriage of Augustus's son to Archduchess Maria Josepha of Austria. Six pavilions linked by galleries enclose a vast interior courtyard with fountains and lawns—Augustus the Strong had himself depicted as Hercules above the Wallpavillon. The Kronentor, the gate crowned by a golden crown carried by four eagles, is the complex's most photographed image.

The Old Masters Picture Gallery, housed in the Semper Wing built between 1847 and 1855, contains over 700 paintings from the 15th to 18th centuries. Its absolute jewel is Raphael's Sistine Madonna, painted around 1512 and acquired by Augustus III in 1754 for the colossal sum of 25,000 ducats. Rubens, Vermeer, Rembrandt, Titian, Giorgione, and Botticelli are also represented. The paintings had been evacuated before the 1945 bombings—they were found hidden in underground mines and returned by the USSR in 1955 after ten years of detention in Moscow.

The Zwinger Porcelain Collection houses some 20,000 pieces of Chinese, Japanese, and Meissen porcelain. Augustus the Strong was so obsessed that in 1717 he traded a regiment of 600 Prussian soldiers for 151 Chinese porcelain vases—these pieces are still on display here. The collection also illustrates how the discovery of European porcelain at Meissen in 1708 arose from a royal commission for alchemy: Augustus wanted gold, his chemists invented porcelain.

The Semperoper, standing on Theaterplatz facing the Zwinger, is one of Europe's most renowned opera houses. The first building was designed by Gottfried Semper and inaugurated in 1841, then destroyed by fire in 1869. The second, also designed by Semper but completed by his son Manfred, reopened in 1878—Semper père was then banned from Saxony for participating in the 1849 insurrection. Razed by the February 13, 1945 bombings, it was rebuilt identically and reopened on February 13, 1985, exactly forty years after its destruction. Richard Strauss premiered several of his operas here, including Der Rosenkavalier in 1911.

The Residenzschloss, palace of the prince-electors and kings of Saxony, is one of Dresden's oldest buildings. Its construction dates to 1220; it was remodeled in Renaissance style between 1548 and 1556, then partially destroyed in 1945 and gradually rebuilt from 1986. It now houses several major museums. The Historic Green Vault (Historisches Grünes Gewölbe) presents 3,000 baroque goldsmith objects in their original 18th-century display cases—one of Europe's greatest treasure collections. On November 25, 2019, thieves broke a window at 5 a.m. and stole several diamond parures from the collection: the case caused a scandal throughout Germany.

The Fürstenzug stretches 102 meters along the outer wall of the Stallhof, the palace stable courtyard. This monumental mural depicts the procession of the 35 rulers of the House of Wettin, from Conrad I in 1127 to King George I in the early 20th century, accompanied by soldiers, artists, and scholars. Originally painted between 1871 and 1876 to celebrate 800 years of the dynasty, it was replaced between 1904 and 1907 by approximately 23,000 Meissen porcelain tiles to withstand the weather—it's the world's largest porcelain artwork. Despite the 1945 bombings, only 200 tiles had to be replaced during restoration.

The Frauenkirche, standing on the Neumarkt, is the symbol of Dresden's resurrection. Built between 1726 and 1743 by municipal architect Georg Bähr, its 96-meter stone dome—the largest north of the Alps—had withstood Prussian cannonballs in 1760 that bounced off it. It collapsed, however, on February 15, 1945, two days after the Allied bombings, from the accumulated heat in the sandstone. The ruins remained a memorial for 45 years under the GDR. Reconstruction, financed by donations from around the world, lasted from 1994 to 2005: 3,643 original stones blackened by fire were reintegrated into the new edifice, visible by their dark color among the light blocks. The golden cross at the summit was donated by British contributors, including the son of one of the pilots who had bombed the city.

→

The Neustadt, on the right bank of the Elbe, is Dresden's artistic quarter—it survived the 1945 bombings better than the Altstadt, and its Art Nouveau streets from the early 20th century have kept their character. The Kunsthofpassage, a series of five interconnected courtyards, is the best-known example of this creativity: each courtyard was entrusted to different artists. The most famous is the Court of Elements, whose blue facade is covered with gutters shaped like trumpets and funnels that transform rain into music.

The Brühlsche Terrasse, nicknamed "the balcony of Europe" by Goethe, is a 500-meter elevated promenade along the Elbe above the old ramparts. Developed in the 18th century on medieval fortifications by Count Brühl, it offers the classic view of the Altstadt silhouette with its domes and steeples. It's from this terrace that Romantic painters—notably Canaletto and Caspar David Friedrich—immortalized Dresden's panorama.

The Albertinum houses the Gallery of New Masters, covering painting and sculpture from the 19th century to the present. The collection includes works by Caspar David Friedrich, including Two Men Contemplating the Moon, as well as Impressionist and Expressionist paintings. The building itself, a 19th-century neo-Renaissance arsenal, was flooded during the 2002 Elbe floods—the works had to be urgently carried to upper floors to be saved.

Moritzburg Castle rises in the middle of a vast artificial pond, its silhouette with four round towers topped by copper roofs reflecting in the water—the image is striking, especially in misty weather. The first hunting lodge was built here between 1542 and 1546 by Duke Moritz of Saxony, whose name the castle bears. Augustus the Strong had it completely rebuilt in Baroque style from 1703, with the help of Pöppelmann, architect of the Zwinger. The interior of its more than 200 rooms is wallpapered with painted leather depicting hunting scenes—unique 18th-century pieces—and houses the world's largest collection of deer trophies. Napoleon stayed here in 1806, on the occasion of the coronation of his ally the King of Saxony. In winter, the castle hosts the Three Hazelnuts for Cinderella exhibition, named after the 1973 Czechoslovak fairy tale film that was shot here and remains cult throughout Central Europe.

The Fasanenschlösschen, the little pheasant palace, is located 2.5 km from the main castle at the end of a long perspective carved through the forest. This bright pink rococo pavilion, built between 1769 and 1782, was used as a secondary residence during hunting parties. Its miniature harbor with jetty and Saxony's only lighthouse—a pink lighthouse, too—served as scenery for the reconstructed naval battles that the princes organized on the ponds.

The Albrechtsburg dominates Meissen from a rocky promontory above the Elbe. This late Gothic castle, begun in 1471 by architect Arnold of Westphalia, is considered one of Germany's first residential palaces—designed as a princely residence rather than a fortress. In 1710, Augustus the Strong installed Europe's very first porcelain manufactory here, under the direction of alchemist Johann Friedrich Böttger, who had promised to make gold and discovered porcelain instead. The manufacturing secret was so well guarded that workers were not allowed to leave the castle. Production remained here until 1863, when it was transferred to new buildings below. The interior walls are covered with 19th-century frescoes illustrating Saxony's history.

The Cathedral of Saints John and Donatus, adjoining the Albrechtsburg on the same promontory, was built from 1250 on the ruins of a 10th-century Romanesque church. Its construction lasted until the end of the 15th century. It houses 14th-century Gothic sculptures depicting Emperor Otto I and his wife Adelaide of Burgundy, as well as paintings by Lucas Cranach the Elder. The Silbermann organ, installed in 1983, is one of Saxony's largest instruments.

The Meissen Porcelain Manufactory, in its current buildings at the foot of the hill, houses the most comprehensive museum on the history of European porcelain. The demonstration workshops allow you to see the different stages of production live—modeling, turning, glazing, hand painting—carried out according to methods unchanged since the 18th century. The crossed blue swords trademark, created in 1722, is one of the oldest commercial brands still in use in the world.

Radebeul

The Karl May Museum occupies two buildings: Villa Shatterhand, where the writer lived from 1895 until his death in 1912, and Villa Bärenfett, a log cabin in Far West style built next door. Karl May—who had never set foot in America when he wrote his novels about Winnetou—possessed here a collection of over 800 Native American ethnographic objects from the 18th and 19th centuries. His office, library, and Oriental salon are preserved identically in Villa Shatterhand. May is the most-read German novelist of all time, with 200 million copies sold worldwide.

The vineyards of Radebeul carpet the hillsides above the town, facing south on terraces supported by stone walls. This 507-hectare region is Germany's smallest and one of its northernmost—vines have been cultivated here since at least the 12th century. The dominant grape varieties are Riesling, Müller-Thurgau, and Pinot Blanc. Wackerbarth Castle, a state wine estate founded in 1728, is the valley's best known.

Pillnitz Castle is actually an ensemble of three palaces set in a 28-hectare park on the banks of the Elbe, 15 km southeast of Dresden. Augustus the Strong had received it from a disgraced former mistress—Countess of Cosel—and decided to make it a pleasure palace "in the Indian taste," meaning exotic. Architect Pöppelmann built between 1720 and 1724 the Wasserpalais and Bergpalais, two symmetrical buildings with characteristic pagoda roofs, framing a Baroque courtyard. A wide staircase descends from the Wasserpalais to the Elbe, where royal gondolas docked directly. In 1791, it was here that the Emperor of Austria and the King of Prussia signed the Declaration of Pillnitz, threatening military intervention in France if the French royal family were mistreated—which precipitated the declaration of war.

The New Palace, built between 1819 and 1826 after the 1818 fire that destroyed the Renaissance wing, closes the east side of the courtyard and now houses the Museum of Decorative Arts of the Dresden State Collections. Its classical dome is the only one of its kind in Dresden. The park also contains a Chinese pavilion from 1804 and a neoclassical English pavilion from 1780. The garden's camellia, imported from Japan around 1780 and planted in 1800, is Europe's oldest: it measures 8.9 meters tall and produces up to 35,000 carmine red flowers between February and April.

Pirna is the gateway to Saxon Switzerland—and a medieval town worth a stop in itself. Its lanes around the Marktplatz have remained almost intact since the 16th century, with Renaissance facades with Gothic porticoes and houses with Baroque elements in adjacent streets (Schmiedergasse, Schössergasse). The painter Bernardo Bellotto, known as Canaletto, worked there in the 1750s and left several paintings of the town that show how little it has changed—some are visible at the Residenzschloss in Dresden.

The Marienkirche, whose steeples dominate the Marktplatz, is a Gothic church built from the 14th to 16th centuries. Its interior, modified in the Baroque era, retains Gothic ribbed vaults and a Renaissance altarpiece.

Sonnenstein Castle dominates the town from a rocky spur. Built from the 13th century for the Margraves of Meissen, it became a psychiatric hospital in 1811—the first establishment of this type in Saxony. Between 1940 and 1941, as part of the Nazi euthanasia program Aktion T4, more than 13,700 people with mental and physical disabilities were gassed in its cellars. A memorial on the site documents these crimes and honors the victims. The castle now houses the district administrative headquarters.

The Bastei is Saxon Switzerland's iconic rock formation—sandstone columns sculpted by erosion over a million years, dominating the Elbe by 194 meters from a plateau at 305 meters altitude. The Bastei Bridge, built in sandstone in 1851 to replace a first wooden bridge from 1826, links several of these rocky pinnacles over 76.5 meters with its seven arches. Caspar David Friedrich painted the rocks as early as 1822. In season, crowds are dense from 10 a.m.—arrive early for the best views over the Elbe's meanders.

The ruins of Neurathen Castle, on the very site of the Bastei rocks, are the vestiges of Saxon Switzerland's largest rock fortress. Built by the Sorbs at the top of the ridge over 700 meters, it was conquered and burned in 1469. Metal walkways now allow circulation between the rock crevices and ruins.

→

Königstein Fortress occupies 9.5 hectares on a sandstone plateau that dominates the Elbe by 240 meters—it's one of Europe's largest mountain fortresses. Never taken by assault, it was nicknamed the "Bastille of Saxony": a state prison from 1591 to 1922, it notably housed Johann Friedrich Böttger, inventor of European porcelain, who was imprisoned there to try to recreate his secret formula. During World War II, it served as an oflag for Allied officers—General Henri Giraud escaped on April 17, 1942 by descending a rope made of knotted sheets, an action that put Hitler in a black rage. The well dug between 1563 and 1569 to supply the garrison measures 152.5 meters deep—the deepest in Saxony.

Bautzen perches on a meander of the Spree, surrounded by its medieval towers—eighteen are still standing. It's the cultural capital of the Sorbs, Germany's smallest ethnic minority: about 60,000 people still speak Upper Sorbian in this Upper Lusatia region. Their migrations from Eastern Europe brought them here in the 6th century. Bilingual German-Sorbian signs line the entire city. The Sorbian Museum traces their history, traditional costumes, and living culture.

St. Peter's Cathedral is Germany's oldest and largest simultaneous church—shared between Catholics and Protestants. Since the Reformation in 1524, the choir has belonged to Catholics and the nave to Protestants, separated by a rood screen grille. Each of the two denominations celebrates its services according to its own calendar. The Gothic part dates back to the 13th century; the Baroque dome of the tower was added in 1664 after the fire of 1634. The Baroque marble altar in Saxon marble (1723) is topped by a painting by Giovanni Antonio Pellegrini.

The Reichenturm, Bautzen's leaning tower, tilts 1.44 meters from the vertical—a foundation defect from the 14th century that was never corrected. It's visible from the market square and offers a beautiful view over the roofs from its summit.

→

Walk on the old town ramparts above the Spree. The path runs along several medieval towers and offers views over the river and gardens below.

Gorlitz

Top hotels

9.5 Ferienwohnung am Elisabethplatz

Görlitz is Germany's best-preserved city—more than 4,000 listed historic buildings in the center, covering five centuries of architecture from late Gothic to Art Nouveau, because the city emerged intact from World War II. Its prosperity in the 15th century rested on cloth manufacturing and trade in woad, a highly prized blue pigment. The Untermarkt, the lower square, is the heart of the old town: its colorful Renaissance and Baroque facades, wrought-iron balconies, and vaulted passages form one of Saxony's most beautiful ensembles. The city serves so often as a film location it's called "Görliwood"—Wes Anderson's The Grand Budapest Hotel, Inglourious Basterds, and The Reader were filmed here.

The Görlitzer Warenhaus, the former Art Nouveau department store from 1913 on Demianiplatz, is the building used as the hotel lobby in The Grand Budapest Hotel. Its large glass-roofed interior courtyard and multi-level shopping galleries are striking—the building was threatened with demolition before the filming saved it.

The Schönhof, dating from 1526, is one of Germany's oldest bourgeois Renaissance houses still preserved in their original state. It houses the Silesian Museum, which documents the history and art of the Silesia region shared between Germany, Poland, and the Czech Republic.

The Church of St. Peter and Paul stands directly on the banks of the Neisse, the border with Poland. Its Baroque "sun organ," built in 1703 by Eugenio Casparini, is one of Saxony's most remarkable instruments—its monumental case has remained intact since its creation.

→

Cross the Old Town Bridge (Altstadtbrücke) to Zgorzelec, the Polish side. The pedestrian bridge rebuilt in 2004 bears a European Union plaque at its center. You can stroll for a few minutes on the Zgorzelec side—the two municipal councils have jointly managed many facilities since the symbolic reunification of the two banks.

The Thomaskirche is Leipzig's musical heart. Johann Sebastian Bach was cantor here from 1723 until his death in 1750—he composed the St. Matthew Passion, the Christmas Oratorio, and more than 150 cantatas here. His tomb stands before the altar. The Thomanerchor, founded in 1212, is one of the world's oldest choral ensembles and still performs motets every Friday and Saturday. Martin Luther himself preached from this pulpit.

The Gewandhaus Orchestra, founded in 1743 by Leipzig's merchant bourgeoisie—a clean break from the tradition of princely or ecclesiastical orchestras—is one of the world's oldest symphony ensembles. Felix Mendelssohn was its director and it was under his direction that Bach's St. Matthew Passion was rediscovered in 1829, after a century of oblivion. Richard Wagner is a native of Leipzig, Robert Schumann lived and composed here. More than 500 composers have reportedly resided in the city.

The Nikolaikirche, a Gothic church remodeled in the 16th century with a green and white palm-themed neoclassical interior, is the church of the Peaceful Revolution of 1989. It was here that the Monday demonstrations (Montagsdemonstrationen) began, with prayers for peace organized every week since 1982. On October 9, 1989, 70,000 people marched through Leipzig's streets—it was the turning point that precipitated the fall of the Berlin Wall.

The Völkerschlachtdenkmal, inaugurated in 1913 for the centenary of the Battle of Nations, is Germany's largest monument: 91 meters high, an interior staircase of 500 steps, colossal sandstone statues of warriors in the Wilhelmine monumental tradition. The October 1813 battle, which pitted Napoleon against the Prussian, Austrian, Russian, and Swedish coalitions, left 90,000 dead in four days at Leipzig's gates—one of the Napoleonic era's deadliest.

The Spinnerei, in the Plagwitz district west of the city, is a former cotton mill built in 1884 that employed up to 3,500 workers. Since the 1990s, its 10 hectares of industrial buildings have been converted into artist studios, galleries, cafes, and cultural spaces—more than 100 artists work there. The "Neue Leipziger Schule," a figurative painting movement influential in 2000s global contemporary art, was born here.

The Mädlerpassage, one of Leipzig's historic covered passages, houses Auerbachs Keller in its cellars—a tavern whose origins date back to 1525. Goethe frequented it during his Leipzig student years and made it the setting for a scene in Faust: Mephistopheles makes wine gush from a wooden table. Two groups of statues at the entrance depict the scene.

Chemnitz was renamed Karl-Marx-Stadt in 1953 by the GDR and recovered its historic name in 1990 after reunification. The Karl Marx Monument, inaugurated in 1971, is a colossal 40-ton bust carved from Siberian granite, placed on a 13-meter pedestal. The locals call it the "Nischel," a local dialect term for "skull." In 2025, Chemnitz is European Capital of Culture under the slogan "C the Unseen"—150 projects and 1,000 events distributed throughout the city and 38 surrounding municipalities, in converted former industrial buildings.

The Kaßberg district is one of Germany's largest continuous Gründerzeit and Art Nouveau ensembles—a hundred blocks from the late 19th century with their sandstone facades and sculpted ornaments. It had largely escaped the 1945 bombings that devastated the center.

The Municipal Art Collections (Kunstsammlungen Chemnitz) present notably Germany's largest collection of works by Karl Schmidt-Rottluff—the Expressionist painter was a native of the city. The museum also includes an important collection of 19th-century paintings and contemporary art.

Seiffen is the iconic village of the Ore Mountains (Erzgebirge), nestled at 700 meters altitude on the Czech border. When the region's mines were exhausted in the 18th century, inhabitants converted to woodworking. In the 19th century, the village established itself as the world center for miniature toys and Christmas decorations. There are more than a hundred craftsmen and forty shops. The wood-turning technique called "Reifendrehen"—a log slice is turned then cut like a pie to obtain dozens of identical animal silhouettes—is unique to Seiffen and inscribed on UNESCO's intangible heritage list.

The Seiffen Toy Museum traces the village's history and its craftsmanship since the 18th century. Christmas pyramids—those wooden candle carousels that rotate by thermal convection—are exhibited in period versions. The nutcrackers in Saxon soldier form, whose first version was created in the region to ridicule authority figures, are the other local specialty. The tradition of the mining angel and the Schwibbogen, the luminous wooden arch placed in windows, also finds its explanation here: these objects recall the candles that miners placed at the mine entrance on Christmas Eve.

Gera

Gera is the birthplace of Otto Dix (1891-1969), one of the most important German painters of the 20th century. Born into a working-class family in the village of Untermhaus, he studied decorative arts in Dresden before leaving for war in 1914. His paintings of the Great War—The Trench, the War Cripples—are among the rawest and most disturbing visual testimonies of that conflict. In 1937, the Nazis confiscated 170 of his works as "degenerate art." The Otto Dix House and the Orangery of the Princes of Reuss, dedicated to him, house a selection of paintings and engravings including the famous Der Krieg (The War) series from 1924.

Gera's market square is one of Thuringia's most beautiful, with its Baroque town hall with mansard roof and entrance tower built after the 1686 fire. The neighboring Salvatorkirche is a Thuringian Baroque masterpiece with its frescoed ceiling and ornate galleries.

The Höhler are a network of vaulted cellars dug under the old town in the 17th and 18th centuries to store beer. Since the late 1980s, a section has been open as a museum. In October, the cellars host the Höhler Festival, one of Thuringia's largest folk festivals.

The Krämerbrücke is Europe's longest inhabited bridge—120 meters spanning the Gera River with 32 half-timbered houses built on both sides along the entire length of the deck. You don't realize you're on a bridge until you look from the banks. First mentioned in 1156, rebuilt in sandstone in 1325, it still has residents and artisan shops today. In June, the Krämerbrückenfest attracts hundreds of thousands for a medieval market.

St. Mary's Cathedral and St. Severus Church together form the Gothic ensemble dominating Domplatz from their twin hills. The cathedral, whose origins go back to the 8th century, is famous for the Gloriosa—the world's largest free-swinging medieval bell, weighing 11 tons, cast in 1497 by the Dutchman Geert van Wou. It rings only eight times a year and can be heard for several kilometers. It was in this cathedral that Martin Luther was ordained a priest in 1507.

The Augustinian Monastery, built in 1300, is where Luther pronounced his monastic vows in 1505 after surviving a violent storm. He spent five years here before being sent to Wittenberg. The cell where he studied theology is preserved. In 2023, the Old Synagogue and its 13th-century mikveh were inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List—the medieval treasure discovered in the walls in 1998 contained 3,141 gold and silver coins, Europe's largest medieval Jewish treasure.
