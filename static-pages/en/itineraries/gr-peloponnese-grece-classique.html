Classical Greece Road Trip: Athens To Olympia

Athens' Acropolis and Parthenon, Mycenaean citadels, the Theatre of Epidaurus, Byzantine Mystras, the stadiums of Olympia, the Oracle of Delphi — the foundations of the West in a single road trip.

From the Acropolis of Athens to the stadiums of Olympia, this road trip crosses the most foundational sites of Western civilization. The Cyclopean citadels of Mycenae, the theatre of Epidaurus with its unexplained acoustics, the ghostly Byzantine city of Mystras, the ruins of Olympia where the Games were celebrated for eleven centuries, and Delphi where Apollo's oracle advised kings and city-states — seven days of open air, marble and history.

Key highlights (4)
  • Athens: Pericles' Acropolis and the museum that brings it to life
  • Epidaurus: the theatre with unexplained acoustic properties
  • Mycenae: the city of Agamemnon and the Treasury of Atreus
  • Delphi: the navel of the world and the bronze Charioteer

Start at Syntagma Square, the modern heart of Athens. Facing Parliament — housed in the former royal palace — the Evzones guard change takes place every hour at the Tomb of the Unknown Soldier. These soldiers in pleated kilts and pom-pom shoes are equal parts kitsch and photogenic, and locals love watching tourists break into applause.

Head down to the Olympieion, or Temple of Olympian Zeus, whose construction began in the 6th century BC and wasn't completed until Emperor Hadrian finished the job in 131 AD — over six centuries of building work. Originally, 104 Corinthian columns standing 17 meters tall framed a chryselephantine statue of Zeus. Only 15 remain upright today: enough for the silhouette to stop you in your tracks from the street. Right next to it, walk through Hadrian's Arch, which once marked the boundary between classical Athens and the Roman city. The inscription carved on each side neatly sums up the ambition of the era.

Climb up to the Acropolis early in the morning — since 2023, a daily cap of 20,000 visitors is enforced, and tickets sell out fast in peak season. Book online the day before. The hilltop measures 170 meters long by 350 meters wide and rises to 156 meters above sea level. Once through the Propylaea, you emerge face-to-face with the Parthenon, built between 447 and 432 BC on the orders of Pericles by architects Ictinus and Callicrates, under the supervision of the sculptor Phidias. It measures 69.5 meters long by 30.88 meters wide, surrounded by 8 Doric columns on each façade and 17 along the sides — 22,000 tonnes of white marble quarried from Mount Pentelicus. The 12-meter-tall gold and ivory statue of Athena it once housed is long gone. Wear non-slip shoes: the paving stones are polished smooth and treacherously slippery.

On the way down, don't miss the Acropolis Museum, opened in 2009 at the foot of the hill. This is where the real Caryatids from the Erechtheion are kept (the ones outside are copies), along with the original Parthenon friezes and polychrome archaic pediments. The glass building is designed so that you can see the Parthenon in the background throughout the entire visit — a deliberate and strikingly effective touch. Allow at least 1.5 hours.

Spend some time at the Ancient Agora, at the foot of the hill: this is where merchants, philosophers and citizens gathered. Socrates held debates here, and Athens' earliest judges presided over trials on this ground. The Temple of Hephaestus, built around 450 BC, is one of the best-preserved classical Greek temples in the world — its roof is still intact. The museum housed in the Stoa of Attalos displays ostraka, the clay shards on which Athenians voted to banish citizens who had grown too powerful.

The Panathenaic Stadium — the Kallimarmaro, meaning "beautiful marble" in Greek — was first built in 330 BC and entirely refaced in white marble to host the first modern Olympic Games in 1896. It stretches 200 meters long and could seat 50,000 spectators in antiquity. In 2004, it served as the finish line for the Olympic marathon. You can run on the track yourself: the guards let you, as long as it's outside peak hours.

→

As evening sets in, lose yourself in the Plaka district, wedged between the Acropolis and Syntagma. It's the oldest continuously inhabited part of Athens, a tangle of narrow lanes, neoclassical houses and open-air tavernas. Skip the restaurants on the main street (tourist prices) and seek out the ones tucked into the side alleys — you'll find proper Greek cooking at half the cost. Climb up to Anafiotika, the tiny Cycladic-style neighborhood clinging to the rock face, built in the 19th century by workers from the island of Anafi who came to construct the royal palace.

→

The mezedopolia is the art of sharing a spread of small plates: taramosalata (cod roe dip), tirokafteri (spicy cheese spread), grilled octopus, dill-braised broad beans, lamb meatballs. In Athens, the ritual of drinks and meze on a terrace kicks off around 7 PM and can easily stretch past midnight. It's the best way to eat like a local.

First stop on the road: the Corinth Canal. Dug between 1882 and 1893 (plans date back to antiquity — Nero himself launched construction in 67 AD before giving up), it connects the Gulf of Corinth to the Aegean Sea over a length of 6.4 km. What really strikes you are the sheer vertical walls: 79 meters of limestone sliced clean. The width is only 21 meters — enough for small cruise ships, but not for supertankers. Park on the road bridge and look straight down: vertigo guaranteed.

The Theatre of Epidaurus is often described as the most perfect in antiquity, and for once, that's not an exaggeration. Built around 330 BC by the Argive architect Polykleitos the Younger, it originally had 34 rows of seats (6,200 capacity), with 21 additional rows added in Roman times to bring the total to 12,000 spectators. Climb to the very top row and ask someone at the bottom to whisper or crumple a sheet of paper: you'll hear it perfectly from 22 meters above. Researchers still debate the exact acoustic explanation — the limestone may filter out low frequencies while letting high ones through. Arrive early in the morning to beat the cruise ship coaches from Athens.

The Sanctuary of Asclepius, god of medicine, is considered the first major healing center of antiquity — the "Lourdes" of the ancient Greek world. The sick traveled from across Greece to practice incubation: they slept in the abaton (a 70-meter-long portico) hoping that Asclepius would appear in their dreams to reveal a cure. The complex also included a gymnasium, baths, a 180-meter stadium and a library — embodying the idea that body and soul heal together. A UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1988.

The small Archaeological Museum of Epidaurus, at the entrance to the site, displays ancient medical instruments, stelae carved with accounts of miraculous healings, and a remarkable statue of Hygieia dating from the 4th century BC. The inscriptions on the stelae are particularly gripping: they describe cures, visions and resurrections — a blend of real medicine and faith. Allow 30 to 45 minutes.

End the day in Nafplio, the first capital of independent Greece in 1829, nestled at the head of a sheltered gulf. Its Venetian center is one of the most beautiful in the Peloponnese: cobblestone lanes, ochre and burgundy façades, cafés lining the local Syntagma Square. If you still have energy, climb up to Palamidi Fortress — 999 steps according to some, 216 according to others. The view over the bay and the floating fort of Bourtzi is worth every step.

→

In summer, the Athens & Epidaurus Festival programs performances of ancient tragedies and comedies on Friday and Saturday evenings at the Theatre of Epidaurus. Aeschylus, Sophocles and Euripides performed in their original setting, under the open sky, with the Argolid mountains as a backdrop — an experience unlike anything else. Check the program before your trip and book well in advance.

→

The bay of Tolo, a small seaside resort a few kilometers from Nafplio, makes the ideal base for the night. The waterfront tavernas serve grilled fish straight off the morning boats. Order some psarosoupa (fish soup) and saganaki (fried cheese) with a glass of local white wine.

Tiryns is often overlooked in favor of Mycenae — unfairly so. This Mycenaean citadel from the 14th century BC boasts the best-preserved Cyclopean walls in the ancient world: up to 14 meters thick, with vaulted galleries carved into the walls themselves. The ancient Greeks believed only the Cyclopes could have assembled such massive blocks. Homer called it "Tiryns of the mighty walls." It's a UNESCO site and far less crowded than Mycenae: chances are you'll have the place to yourself.

Mycenae is the city of Agamemnon, the king who led the Greeks against Troy. Founded, according to legend, by Perseus, it reached its peak between 1400 and 1200 BC and influenced the entire eastern Mediterranean. Entering through the Lion Gate, erected around 1250 BC, you pass beneath the oldest monumental relief sculpture in Europe: two lionesses (their metal heads are lost) flanking a Minoan-style column. The Cyclopean rampart stones weigh several tonnes each and are fitted together without mortar. Arrive early — in peak season, the parking lot fills up by 9 AM.

Don't miss the Treasury of Atreus, 300 meters from the main gate — it's actually a royal tholos tomb dating from 1250 BC. You enter through a 36-meter open-air corridor (the dromos), flanked by walls 6 meters high. The circular chamber measures 14.50 meters in diameter and 13.20 meters in height: it was the largest dome in the Western world until the Pantheon in Rome was built five centuries later. The acoustics inside are eerie and mesmerizing — try clapping your hands.

Head back down to Nafplio to explore its Venetian old town. Palamidi Fortress, perched 216 meters above the harbor, was built by the Venetians between 1711 and 1714 in a record time of three years. From its upper bastion, the view sweeps across the entire Gulf of Argolis. If you're only going to climb one fortress at sunset on this trip, make it this one: the golden light on the gulf is stunning.

→

Nafplio deserves a proper on-foot exploration. Cross Syntagma Square, lined with Venetian and Ottoman houses, pass the Vourliotis Mosque (now a cinema), then walk along the harbor to the Bourtzi Fort, built on a tiny island in the middle of the bay. Water taxis shuttle you there in just a few minutes.

→

Argolid cuisine is generous: lamb kleftiko (slow-braised lamb), crispy spanakopita, local cheeses drizzled with Peloponnesian honey. The tavernas along Nafplio's harbor are among the best in the region — look for the ones without a laminated menu posted by the door.

Mystras is one of the great surprises of the Peloponnese. This ghostly Byzantine city, terraced along the slopes of Mount Taygetus at 620 meters altitude, is often called the "Wonder of the Morea." Founded in 1249 by the Frankish prince William II of Villehardouin, it passed to the Byzantines in 1262 and became the capital of the Despotate of the Morea in 1348 — a semi-autonomous province of the Byzantine Empire. At its height, 20,000 people lived here. In 1449, Constantine XI was crowned here as the last emperor of Byzantium before dying on the walls of Constantinople in 1453. A UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1989.

The Peribleptos Monastery is literally clinging to the rock face — the church is carved into the cliff itself. Inside, the 14th-century frescoes are among the finest examples of late Byzantine art: Christ Pantocrator in the dome, vivid narrative scenes from the life of Christ. The colors are still remarkably bright. Dress modestly (shoulders and knees covered) and wear sturdy shoes for the steep cobbled paths.

The Pantanassa Monastery is the only one in Mystras still inhabited: a small community of Orthodox nuns maintains the grounds. Its ornate stone façade and 15th-century frescoes, blazing with color, already betray the influence of the Italian Renaissance — Mystras and Florence were exchanging artists and intellectuals during this period. The flower-filled courtyard is an oasis of calm amid the ruins. The nuns sometimes sell aromatic herbs and homemade preserves.

For the brave, climb all the way up to the fortress at the hilltop. The ascent is steep (roughly 40 minutes from the lower town) but the reward matches the effort: a 360° panorama over the plain of Laconia, the Taygetus massif — sometimes capped with snow — and, on clear days, the Mediterranean Sea in the distance. This is where you truly grasp why the Byzantines chose this impregnable position.

→

Visiting Mystras works in three stages: the lower town (monasteries and churches), the upper town (Palace of the Despots) and the fortress (panorama). Allow at least 3 hours. In summer, start at opening time (8 AM) to avoid the midday heat on the exposed hillside. Bring water — there are no refreshment points anywhere on the site.

→

In the evening, modern Sparta is a functional town without much charm — but the handful of tavernas around the main square serve solid roast lamb and Kalamata olives (the best in Greece come from this very region). The Archaeological Museum of Sparta, a 10-minute walk away, is also worth a visit for its votive Laconian terracotta masks from the 7th century BC.

The road from Sparta crosses Kalamata, capital of Messenia and world-famous for its olives. If you pass through mid-morning, stop at the central covered market: spices, olive oils, marinated olives of every kind, local cheeses. It's the best spot in the Peloponnese for gourmet shopping before heading on toward the Messenian coast.

The Archaeological Museum of Olympia is one of the most important in Greece. Don't skip it in favor of the ruins — do both. Its star pieces include the Hermes of Praxiteles (one of the finest marble statues from antiquity, discovered in 1877, standing 2.10 meters tall), the winged Victory of Paionios, and the monumental pediments of the Temple of Zeus depicting the chariot race between Pelops and Oenomaus. The collection of ancient bronzes is the largest in the world. Allow at least 1.5 hours.

The ancient site of Olympia has been inhabited since the 3rd millennium BC, and the Games were held here without interruption from 776 BC to 393 AD — over 1,100 years. The Altis, Zeus' sacred grove, forms the heart of the sanctuary. The Temple of Zeus, built between 470 and 456 BC, once housed Phidias' chryselephantine statue of Zeus — 12 meters tall and one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World. Only toppled columns remain, brought down by earthquakes. The Olympic flame is still lit here every four years in front of the Temple of Hera during the official ceremony.

The ancient stadium of Olympia measures 212.54 meters long by 28.50 meters wide. Built in the 5th century BC, it could hold 40,000 to 45,000 spectators seated on the natural earthen banks. At the entrance, you pass through the same vaulted tunnel that athletes used in antiquity. Run the length of the track — it's the "stadion," the original Olympic event: 192 meters, one Greek stade. You won't be the first to try.

→

The modern village of Olympia, a few hundred meters from the site, lives entirely off tourism — but its tavernas are honest and convenient after a long day of sightseeing. Try the kokoretsi (spit-roasted lamb offal), a Peloponnesian specialty, and a glass of Mantinia wine, a fruity white Moschofilero produced in the Arcadia region.

→

If you arrive early in the afternoon, also explore the Museum of the History of the Ancient Olympic Games, housed in the former archaeological museum building. It traces the history of the events, the ceremonies and the sacred truce (the Ekecheiria) that suspended all wars during the Games. More accessible than the main museum, it's particularly well-suited for children.

The road from Olympia to Arachova crosses the Rio-Antirrio Bridge, inaugurated in 2004 for the Athens Olympic Games. Spanning 2.9 km, this cable-stayed bridge crosses the Gulf of Corinth at its narrowest point — it links the Peloponnese to mainland Greece and replaced a ferry crossing that took 15 minutes but could mean hours of waiting in season. Pull over at the rest area on the Antirrio side for a close-up view of the structure.

From Antirrio, the road climbs toward Parnassus through a landscape of limestone hills, pine forests and hilltop villages. Arachova appears suddenly at 960 meters altitude, its stone houses stacked along the cliff above a breathtaking drop. The village is nicknamed the "winter Mykonos" by Athenians who come to ski on the slopes of Parnassus, 20 km away. In summer, it's far more tranquil — and the late-afternoon light on the stone façades is extraordinary.

The Arachova clock tower, perched on a rocky spur overlooking the village, is the town's landmark. The small square below, ringed by open-air cafés, is where locals gather in the evening. From here you get the best view of the village tumbling down toward the Pleistos valley 600 meters below. The Church of Saint George, reached via 264 steps from the center, offers an even wider panorama of the region.

→

Arachova's main street is one long corridor of shops selling local specialties. Formaela is the region's PDO cheese: semi-hard, made from sheep's milk, with a sharp, slightly tangy flavor. It's eaten grilled over charcoal or pan-fried in olive oil and drizzled with local honey — order it as a starter at any taverna. Hand-woven rugs and textiles are the village's other artisan specialty.

→

After dinner, stroll through the cobbled lanes of Arachova after dark: the stone houses are softly lit, the tavernas hum with life, and the view of the lights in the valley below is striking. It's one of the most photogenic villages in central Greece, especially outside the peak tourist season.

The Sanctuary of Delphi, 10 km from Arachova, was considered by the ancient Greeks to be the navel of the world — the omphalos, an ogive-shaped sacred stone, was believed to mark its exact center. This pan-Hellenic sanctuary dedicated to Apollo was, from the 6th to the 4th century BC, the most influential in Greece: kings, tyrants and city-states came to consult the Pythia before every major decision — war, founding a colony, a dynastic marriage. The Sacred Way winds uphill from the entrance, lined with treasuries offered by the various cities (including the finely reconstructed Treasury of the Athenians) up to the Temple of Apollo, of which 6 columns still stand. The maxims of the Sages — including the famous "Know thyself" — were inscribed on the pediment. A UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1987.

Before entering the main sanctuary, make the detour to Marmaria, the Sanctuary of Athena Pronaia below the road. This is where you'll find the Tholos of Delphi, built around 380–360 BC by Theodoros of Phocaea: a circular structure of white marble and blue Eleusis limestone, with 20 Doric columns on the outside and 10 Corinthian columns within. Three columns have been re-erected, and it's the most photographed image of Delphi — deservedly so. Its function remains a mystery to archaeologists.

The Archaeological Museum of Delphi, next to the site, is essential to complete the visit. Its star piece is the Charioteer of Delphi, a bronze sculpture from 478 BC discovered in 1896 — 1.80 meters tall, with inlaid glass paste eyes still in place and drapery of breathtaking finesse. It is one of the very few classical Greek bronzes to have survived in its entirety. The museum also houses the original omphalos, the bronze bull from the island of Corcyra, and gold and silver votive offerings from every corner of the Greek world.

→

If you still have energy after Delphi, climb up to the theatre and the stadium above the sanctuary. The 4th-century BC theatre (4,500 seats) offers the best overall view of the site from its upper rows — with the columns of the Temple of Apollo in the foreground and the Pleistos valley stretching to infinity behind. The stadium, even higher up (7,000 seats, built for the Pythian Games held every four years), is often deserted, leaving you free to run the ancient track in peace.

→

The drive back to Athens via Livadia and Thiva (Thebes) crosses the Boeotian plains and the Parnassus passes. Allow 2.5 to 3 hours of driving. Arriving in Athens in the evening, head to the tavernas of Monastiraki or Exarcheia for a final Greek meal before departure — the souvlaki joints in Monastiraki stay open until 3 AM.
