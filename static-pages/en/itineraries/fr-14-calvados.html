Colleville Sur Mer

Perched on the cliff overlooking Omaha Beach, the American Cemetery at Colleville-sur-Mer spreads its 9,387 white crosses in perfect rows across an emerald lawn. The headstones face westward toward the homeland, while at the center, the bronze statue 'The Spirit of American Youth Rising from the Waves' watches over this haven of peace. The chapel, the Garden of the Missing and the Visitor Center movingly recount Operation Overlord and the sacrifice of an entire generation.

Walk down to Omaha Beach, the scene of 'Bloody Omaha' on June 6, 1944. The fine sand stretches peacefully today, but the remains of blockhouses and memorials recall the 2,500 American soldiers who fell in a single day. The calm sea contrasts with the memory of waves laden with men and equipment, creating a uniquely contemplative atmosphere.

At Pointe du Hoc, the 30-metre cliffs bear the indelible marks of bombardment. Moon-like craters, shattered bunkers and gaping casemates bear witness to the heroic assault by Colonel Rudder's 225 Rangers, who scaled these walls at dawn on D-Day. The site, managed by the American administration, offers a striking panorama over the English Channel and illustrates the ferocity of the fighting.

→

Walk the coastal path between Omaha Beach and Pointe du Hoc. Orientation tables line this historic route offering spectacular views of the cliffs and the sea.

→

Attend the flag-lowering ceremony at the American Cemetery. Every day at 4 pm, the American flag is retired to the sound of Taps in reverent silence.

The fishing port of Port-en-Bessin brings the small coastal village to life with its ballet of colourful trawlers. The quays echo with the cry of seagulls and the bustle of fishermen unloading their fresh catch. The authentic atmosphere of this working port contrasts with the neighbouring seaside resorts.

Stroll between the nearby D-Day beaches. Gold Beach lies just a stone's throw away, a reminder that Port-en-Bessin served as a fuel port during the Battle of Normandy thanks to the PLUTO pipeline.

→

Savour fresh seafood at one of the port's fish auctions. Scallops, oysters and fresh fish come straight from the boats to the restaurant stalls on the quayside.

→

Climb up to the Vauban tower overlooking the port. The panorama encompasses the Bessin coast and offers an unbeatable view of Gold Beach.

The remains of the Mulberry B artificial harbour emerge from the sea like sleeping giants. At low tide, the concrete Phoenix caissons reveal the scale of this technical feat that enabled the landing of 2.5 million men and 4 million tonnes of equipment. The floating pontoons and breakwaters bear witness to Allied daring.

Explore the fully renovated D-Day Museum, facing the very remains it documents. The large 28 m² animated model and video mapping precisely illustrate the construction and operation of the Mulberry harbour. Archive films and period artefacts complete this 90-minute immersive experience.

At the Arromanches 360° circular cinema, immerse yourself in the history of the Battle of Normandy. Images projected across nine screens envelop you in the story of the 100 days following the landings through to the liberation of Paris.

→

Climb to the museum's panoramic terrace. The viewpoint takes in the entire Bay of Arromanches and conveys the sheer scale of Operation Overlord.

→

Discover Norman gastronomy in the seafront restaurants. Moules-frites, sole meunière and farmhouse cider are enjoyed against the backdrop of the artificial harbour remains.

→

Visit the Battle of Normandy Museum in nearby Bayeux. This museum deepens your understanding of the Normandy campaign with its collections of vehicles and uniforms.

The Bayeux Tapestry unfurls its 70 metres of medieval embroidery recounting William the Conqueror's conquest of England. A masterpiece of 11th-century Romanesque art inscribed on UNESCO's Memory of the World Register, it captivates with its details of medieval life. An audioguide in ten languages accompanies the discovery of its 58 scenes embroidered in wool on linen canvas.

Admire Bayeux's Notre-Dame Cathedral, a jewel of medieval architecture. Consecrated in 1077 by Bishop Odo in the presence of William the Conqueror, it blends Romanesque and Gothic styles with remarkable elegance. Its vaults, stained glass and sculpted decoration make it one of the finest cathedrals in Normandy.

Explore Bayeux's beautifully preserved historic centre. Its cobbled lanes, half-timbered houses and grand townhouses bear witness to a thousand years of history. The town, miraculously spared from the bombardments of 1944, retains an exceptional medieval heritage.

→

Visit the Battle of Normandy Memorial Museum. Military vehicles, uniforms and dioramas trace the 77 days of fighting that liberated Normandy.

→

Stroll along the River Aure as it winds through the town. Watermills and old wash-houses line this bucolic walk between the two great abbeys.

→

Explore the Saint-Loup quarter and its local craft shops. Norman cheeses, farmhouse ciders and Bayeux lace perpetuate regional traditions.

The Abbaye aux Dames, founded around 1060 by Matilda of Flanders, houses the queen's tomb beneath a black marble slab in the Trinity Abbey Church. A masterpiece of Norman Romanesque art, its capitals carved with fantastical animals and its crypt with its forest of columns transport you to the Middle Ages. The 18th-century conventual buildings, now the seat of the Normandy Region, can be visited with enthusiastic guides.

The Caen Memorial, the leading museum on World War II, presents a gripping journey from the D-Day landings to the Cold War. Period films, reconstructions and testimonies plunge you into 20th-century history with immersive staging. Temporary exhibitions enrich this essential place of remembrance.

William the Conqueror's Castle, one of the largest medieval fortified enclosures in Europe, commands the city centre from its thousand-year-old ramparts. Inside, the Normandy Museum and the Museum of Fine Arts house remarkable collections. The ruins of the ducal palace evoke the power of the conqueror of England.

Explore the Abbaye aux Hommes, where William the Conqueror has rested since 1087. The Saint-Étienne abbey church masterfully combines Romanesque and Gothic architecture. The 18th-century cloister, now the town hall, can be discovered during daily guided tours.

→

Wander through the Vaugueux quarter, the medieval heart of Caen. Its narrow lanes lined with half-timbered houses are home to traditional restaurants and lively terraces.

→

Visit the marina right in the city centre. Accessible from Ouistreham via the Orne Canal, it offers an unexpected maritime atmosphere just steps from the castle.

→

Savour Caen's specialities in a traditional cider house. Tripes à la mode de Caen, andouille de Vire and teurgoule are enjoyed with a farmhouse cider from the Pays d'Auge.

Falaise Castle, birthplace of William the Conqueror born in 1027, raises its three imposing keeps above the town. The medieval fortress combines Caen stone and Norman architecture with majesty. The virtual tour recreating the castle in the time of the dukes offers a spectacular plunge into the 11th century.

Explore the historic centre of Falaise and its medieval remains. The ramparts, Saint-Gervais Church and half-timbered houses recount the history of this strategic ducal city.

→

Visit the Civilians in Wartime Memorial, the only one of its kind in France. Set in the castle's underground passages, it tells the story of everyday life under the Occupation and during the Liberation.

→

Discover the Automates Avenue museum, home to an astonishing collection of Belle Époque automata and antique toys. The magic of these mechanisms delights visitors of all ages.

Walk along the Planches de Cabourg, the iconic 4 km promenade stretching along the sandy beach. The Belle Époque atmosphere permeates this seaside resort with its unique fan-shaped layout. The villas with their bow windows, loggias and colourful ceramics showcase the architectural refinement of the 1900s.

The Grand Hôtel de Cabourg stretches its 300-metre façade facing the sea. Opened in 1907, this Belle Époque palace welcomed Marcel Proust, who stayed each summer in room 414 from 1907 to 1914. The writer composed passages of In Search of Lost Time here, renaming Cabourg 'Balbec'.

→

Explore the Villa du Temps retrouvé, a museum evoking the Belle Époque, housed in the former Villa Bon-Abri. Exhibitions, collections and recreations transport you to the art of living in 1900.

→

Follow the Marcel Proust promenade named after the writer. The Méridien de l'Amour, a giant sundial on the sand, marks this romantic spot cherished by couples.

→

Try your luck at the Cabourg casino, next to the Grand Hôtel. Rebuilt in 1911 in a neoclassical style, it carries on the tradition of elegant entertainment at the resort.

The Planches de Deauville, a legendary 643-metre boardwalk of azobé wood, line the beach with multicoloured parasols. The 450 bathing cabins bear the names of stars who attended the American Film Festival. The Art Deco Bains Pompéiens complex completes this postcard setting immortalised by Claude Lelouch.

Admire the Hôtel Normandy Barrière with its green half-timbered Anglo-Norman style. This five-star palace built in 1912 embodies the elegance of the Côte Fleurie. Just steps away, the Casino Barrière has been glowing with golden lights for over a century.

Visit Les Franciscaines, an innovative cultural hub set in a former convent. Museum, temporary exhibitions, media library and documentation spaces explore horses, cinema, photography and the Norman art of living.

→

Attend horse racing at the Deauville-La Touques racecourse. Inaugurated in 1864 by the Duc de Morny, this temple of racing hosts the most prestigious Group 1 events during the August meeting.

→

Enjoy the beach and its water sports. Land sailing, swimming and sunbathing set the rhythm of summer days on Deauville's fine sand.

→

Browse the luxury boutiques in the town centre. Deauville cultivates the art of elegant shopping, with fashion houses and designers side by side.

→

Unwind at the spa or thalassotherapy centre. Deauville's wellness establishments offer marine treatments and massages overlooking the sea.

Honfleur's Vieux Bassin sparkles with colourful façades reflected in the calm harbour waters. The tall shipowners' houses from the 16th to 18th centuries frame this picturesque port that inspired Boudin, Monet and the Impressionist painters. The Lieutenance, the former governor's residence, has guarded the harbour entrance since 1600.

The Church of Sainte-Catherine, the largest wooden church in France, unfolds its twin naves in the shape of an upturned ship's hull. Built in the 15th century by naval carpenters after the Hundred Years' War, it astonishes with its unique architecture. Its separate bell tower, an oak structure atop the bell-ringer's house, now houses an annex of the Eugène Boudin Museum.

Discover the Eugène Boudin Museum dedicated to Norman Impressionism. Works by Boudin, Monet, Courbet and Dubourg trace the artistic adventure born on these shores of light.

→

Stroll through the Sainte-Catherine quarter with its cobbled medieval lanes. Art galleries, designer boutiques and artists' studios enliven this maze of half-timbered houses.

→

Savour Honfleur's gastronomy in one of the restaurants overlooking the Vieux Bassin. Fresh fish, moules marinières and Norman andouille are enjoyed facing the dance of sailboats.

→

Cross the Pont de Normandie, a technical marvel linking Honfleur to Le Havre. The panorama from this 2,141-metre cable-stayed bridge encompasses the Seine estuary and the Côte Fleurie.
