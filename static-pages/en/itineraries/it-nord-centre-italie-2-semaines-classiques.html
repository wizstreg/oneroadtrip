The Duomo di Milano, begun in 1386 under Duke Gian Galeazzo Visconti's orders, mobilized hundreds of artisans from across Europe over five centuries—from Burgundy, the Rhineland, Bohemia—to erect one of the world's largest Gothic cathedrals: 158 meters long, 108.5 meters to the tip of the Madonnina, 3,400 statues distributed across 135 spires and pinnacles, entirely clad in pink Candoglia marble quarried from the shores of Lake Maggiore. Napoleon Bonaparte financed the completion of the façade in 1805 to be crowned King of Italy here. Climbing to the rooftop terraces brings you face-to-face with these sculptures and offers sweeping views across Milan's urban fabric.

The Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II, inaugurated on September 15, 1867 by King Victor Emmanuel II, connects Piazza del Duomo to Piazza della Scala across 196 meters. Its architect Giuseppe Mengoni died tragically in a fall from scaffolding the day before the triumphal arch was completed in December 1877. The central glass-and-iron dome soars to 47 meters—one of the first of its kind in Europe. On the floor, a mosaic depicts the bull from Turin's coat of arms: tradition holds that spinning your heel on the animal's genitals brings good luck, which explains why the motif requires regular restoration.

The Brera district grew up around its Academy of Fine Arts, founded in 1776, which drew painters and sculptors until this quarter became Milan's bohemian heart. Its cobbled lanes still shelter numerous art galleries, antique dealers, and artisan workshops. The Pinacoteca di Brera, housed in the baroque palazzo of the same name, holds one of Italy's most important collections of Italian painting.

→

Stroll along the Navigli at sunset—the old canal network dug from the 12th century onward to transport Candoglia marble to the Duomo construction site. At its peak the network stretched over 150 kilometers of navigable waterways. The remodeled embankments now host bars and restaurants that come alive during aperitivo hour.

The Castello Sforzesco originated from a fortress built in 1358 by Galeazzo II Visconti. After the fall of the Visconti in 1447 and a brief republican period that saw the castle demolished, Francesco Sforza had it entirely rebuilt from 1450, entrusting Florentine architect Filarete in 1452 with the construction of the 70-meter central tower. His successor Ludovico il Moro commissioned Leonardo da Vinci to decorate several halls, including the Sala delle Asse (1496–1497) with frescoes depicting intertwined tree branches. The castle now houses seven municipal museums, including one devoted to Michelangelo's final unfinished work, the Pietà Rondanini.

The church of Santa Maria delle Grazie was built from 1463 by architect Guiniforte Solari, then profoundly remodeled in the late 15th century by Bramante, who added a large apse with hemispherical dome. Ludovico il Moro had made it the Sforza family mausoleum after his wife Beatrice d'Este died in 1497. In the adjoining refectory, Leonardo da Vinci painted The Last Supper between 1495 and 1498 on commission from Ludovico: a tempera-on-plaster mural measuring 460 × 880 cm. He deliberately abandoned the traditional fresco technique in order to refine and rework his painting, which fragility set in by the 16th century. King Louis XII of France wanted to detach it from the wall to carry it off—an operation deemed impossible. Visits are limited to groups of 25 people maximum, advance booking mandatory.

→

Explore Milan's main neighborhoods on foot or by historic tram (Line 1) to grasp the contrasts between the neoclassical architecture downtown, the early-20th-century Art Nouveau buildings, and the contemporary towers of the Porta Nuova district.

Genoa's historic center covers 113 hectares and forms one of Europe's most extensive medieval ensembles. Its caruggi—narrow, often shadowy lanes that cut through the urban fabric like fissures—open onto unexpected squares and ornate façades. The entire area was designated a UNESCO World Heritage site in 2006. The cathedral of San Lorenzo, built between the 12th and 16th centuries, displays a façade with alternating bands of black-and-white marble characteristic of Ligurian Romanesque-Gothic style.

Via Garibaldi—formerly Strada Nuova, laid out in the 16th century—gathers the Palazzi dei Rolli, 42 Renaissance and Baroque palaces inscribed on the UNESCO list. In 1576, the Senate of the Republic of Genoa decreed a roster (the 'Rollo') designating aristocratic families obliged to host visiting dignitaries in their homes—a system unique in Europe, a kind of royal palace dispersed across private residences. Peter Paul Rubens, who stayed in Genoa between 1604 and 1606, was so impressed he published a compendium of plans and elevations of 31 of these palaces to spread the model in Flanders.

The Porto Antico, redesigned by architect Renzo Piano for the 1992 International Exposition, transformed old port warehouses into a cultural and leisure waterfront. The Bigo—a spidery metal structure with a panoramic cabin 40 meters high—evokes the historic port's cranes. The Genoa Aquarium, one of Europe's largest with over 12,000 marine animals, occupies a building designed by Renzo Piano.

→

Savor focaccia alla genovese and pesto alla genovese in a caruggi trattoria. Genoese pesto—Ligurian DOP basil, garlic, pine nuts, Parmesan, pecorino, and olive oil—has been protected by European geographical indication since 2023. Genoese focaccia, thin and drizzled with olive oil and salt, differs markedly from its regional variants.

Monterosso al Mare, the largest and westernmost of the five villages, splits into two parts via a tunnel cut through the cliff: to the east the medieval borgo antico with its caruggi and the church of San Giovanni Battista (13th century, white-and-green striped façade); to the west the Fegina quarter with the only sandy beach in the Cinque Terre. Monterosso is also the capital of salt-cured anchovies, prepared here using a salting technique inherited from centuries of Mediterranean fishing.

Vernazza is the only one of the five villages to possess a natural sheltered harbor. Its colorful tower-houses, built to serve as both dwellings and defensive ramparts, line up around a small square fronted by the church of Santa Margherita d'Antiochia (13th century) with its octagonal bell tower. The Castello Doria, built on the clifftop in the 11th century, offers views across the entire coast. Vernazza is accessible on foot from Monterosso via the Sentiero Azzurro (coastal trail marked in white and red), a hiking segment of about 3.5 km.

→

Climb to Corniglia at sunset. The only Cinque Terre village without direct sea access perches on a promontory 100 meters above the waves. You reach it from the train station via the Lardarina, a staircase of 382 steps, or by shuttle. The panoramic terrace of Santa Maria offers views of Manarola on one side, Vernazza on the other. Corniglia is the production center for Sciacchetrà, the rare sweet amber passito wine of the Cinque Terre.

Manarola, whose name traces back to a 1244 documentary mention (Manaiola), is a former wine-growers' village whose urban core developed in the 12th century around tower-houses clustered on the cliff. The view from the Punta Bonfiglio promontory, at the village's edge, ranks among the most photographed on the entire Riviera. In summer the rocky cove below allows for swimming. Each winter since 1997, the hillside has been illuminated with a nativity scene featuring more than 300 life-size figures.

Riomaggiore, the easternmost Cinque Terre village and closest to La Spezia, was likely founded in the 12th century by inhabitants from Val de Vara. Its name comes from the Rivus Major stream, now covered beneath the main street. The Via dell'Amore, a paved coastal path that linked Riomaggiore to Manarola across 2 km, was closed following landslides in 2012 and has partially reopened. The village harbor, with its colorful fishing boats hauled up on rails, is one of the most picturesque on the coast.

Portovenere, inscribed on the UNESCO list since 1997 along with the Cinque Terre, was acquired by the Genoese in 1113 to serve as a defensive stronghold against Pisa. Its tall, narrow tower-houses—dwellings and defensive rampart in one—line the waterfront. At the tip of the rocky promontory, the church of San Pietro was built in 1256 to thank the population for their contribution to battles against Pisa. It rises on the supposed remains of a Roman temple dedicated to Venus, whose name passed to the town. Beside it, the Grotta Byron—formerly Grotta Arpaia—was renamed in homage to English poet Lord Byron, who came here to swim and find inspiration in the 19th century.

→

Take a boat excursion from Portovenere to the islands of the archipelago: Palmaria (1.89 km², a nature reserve with trails and beaches), Tino, and Tinetto (both UNESCO-listed). Boats depart from Portovenere harbor and also allow you to observe the Cinque Terre coast from the sea.

Lucca's ramparts form one of the rare examples in Europe of a Renaissance fortification preserved intact around its entire perimeter—4.2 kilometers. Built between about 1513 and 1645 to adapt the city's defenses to artillery, they rest on a system of bastions made of compacted earth capable of absorbing cannonballs. History's paradox: these fortifications, among Italy's most advanced in their era, were never used in combat. Today transformed into a public promenade, their tree-lined summits offer a walking or cycling circuit above the old town's rooftops.

Piazza dell'Anfiteatro occupies the exact footprint of a 2nd-century AD Roman amphitheater, whose seating tiers served as foundations for the medieval houses built atop. Redesigned in the 19th century by architect Lorenzo Nottolini to reveal the original ellipse, the square retains its characteristic upturned-shell shape. The façades of the surrounding buildings still show, here and there, stones reused directly from the ancient arcades.

The Torre Guinigi, 45 meters tall, is Lucca's best-known medieval tower, formerly the property of the Guinigi family who dominated the city's political life in the early 15th century. It stands out for the holm oaks growing at its summit—a hanging garden 41 meters high, planted at the time of its construction in the habitable section that crowned the tower. Its 230 steps emerge onto a 360-degree view across the old town's brick rooftops and the Apuan Alps.

The cathedral of San Martino was profoundly remodeled starting in 1060–1070, but its most celebrated element is an early-15th-century sculpture: the tomb effigy of Ilaria Del Carretto, wife of Lucca's lord Paolo Guinigi, who died in 1405. Carved by Sienese sculptor Jacopo della Quercia, this white marble funeral monument depicts the young woman lying with a dog at her feet—symbol of fidelity—and such meticulous modeling of face and drapery that it ranks among the first sculptural works of the Italian Renaissance.

→

Lucca's old town built part of its medieval fortune on the silk trade: Lucchese merchants supplied European courts as early as the 13th century. Wandering the lanes around Via Fillungo, the historic shopping axis, conveys the scale of a city whose layout has barely changed since the Middle Ages. Lucca is also the birthplace of Giacomo Puccini (1858), whose house museum can be visited on Piazza della Cittadella.

Piazza del Duomo—nicknamed Piazza dei Miracoli, a name invented by writer Gabriele D'Annunzio in his 1910 novel—gathers four Romanesque buildings constructed between the 11th and 14th centuries on a single lawn carpet, inscribed together on the UNESCO World Heritage list since 1987. The cathedral of Santa Maria Assunta, begun in 1064 by architect Buscheto after the Pisan fleet's victory over the Saracens in Sicily, is the founding monument of the Pisan Romanesque style characterized by blind arcades and pale marble bands.

The Baptistery, begun in 1153 by architect Diotisalvi, is Italy's largest: 54.86 meters in diameter and 55 meters high. Its exceptional acoustics—a guide can produce a three-note chord simultaneously by singing in echo—has attracted visitors since the Middle Ages. Inside, the pulpit sculpted by Nicola Pisano in 1260 marks a decisive break with Byzantine art by returning to direct study of ancient Roman sarcophagi, opening the path to Renaissance sculpture.

The Leaning Tower, begun in 1173 and completed in 1372 after two long interruptions due to unstable alluvial terrain, measures 55.86 meters on the south side and 58.36 meters on the north—the visible asymmetry reflects successive corrections attempted during construction to compensate for subsidence. It weighs approximately 14,500 tons and had 294 steps until its closure in 1990. Consolidation work conducted through 2001, consisting of removing earth from beneath the higher side, reduced the tilt from 5.5° to 3.97°, stabilizing it for at least two hundred years. Galileo supposedly conducted his falling-bodies experiments from the summit, according to unverified tradition.

The Camposanto—literally 'holy cemetery'—is a monumental rectangular cloister begun in 1278. According to legend, its soil was brought back from the Holy Land by Archbishop Ubaldo Lanfranchi at the end of the Crusades so the dead could rest in sacred earth. Its galleries sheltered 14th-century frescoes including the famous Triumph of Death attributed to Buonamico Buffalmacco, severely damaged by fire during a 1944 bombing—the explosion melted the roof lead onto the paintings—then partially restored.

→

Pisa retains a little-visited historic center beyond the Piazza dei Miracoli: the Arno embankments, the lanes of the Borgo Stretto quarter, and the markets around Piazza delle Vettovaglie offer a glimpse of daily life in a Tuscan university town.

The cathedral of Santa Maria del Fiore—whose name refers to the lily, Florence's symbol—was begun in 1296 by architect Arnolfo di Cambio on the site of the old church of San Reparata. The design called for a dome above the transept, but no one knew how to build it: the problem remained unsolved for a century. In 1418, a competition was launched and won by goldsmith Filippo Brunelleschi, who proposed building without wooden scaffolding—no European tree was tall enough. His solution, a double shell of stone and bricks woven in herringbone pattern supporting each other mutually, was constructed between 1420 and 1436: outer diameter 55 meters, inner diameter 45.5 meters, 463 steps to the summit lantern. It remains the world's largest masonry vault. Pope Eugene IV consecrated the cathedral on March 24, 1436.

The Baptistery of St. John, whose construction in its present form dates to the 11th century, is the oldest monument on Piazza del Duomo. Its three bronze doors were the subject of a 1401 competition between sculptors, won by Lorenzo Ghiberti over Brunelleschi—a defeat that supposedly pushed the latter toward architecture. Ghiberti devoted forty years to the east door, completed in 1452, which Michelangelo according to tradition baptized the 'Gates of Paradise' for the quality of its ten relief panels depicting scenes from the Old Testament.

Giotto's Campanile, adjacent to the cathedral, was begun by Giotto di Bondone shortly before his death in 1337, then completed in 1359 by Francesco Talenti. Its height of 84.7 meters and its cladding in white Carrara marble, green Prato marble, and pink Sienese marble form a chromatic palette that would be repeated across the entire Piazza del Duomo complex. The 414 steps leading to the summit terrace offer a direct view of Brunelleschi's dome at equal height.

→

Wander the lanes between the Duomo and Palazzo Vecchio to understand Florence's medieval fabric: the city has no grand avenues, but a labyrinth of narrow streets opening onto unexpected squares. The covered Mercato Centrale (inaugurated in 1874 in an iron-and-glass structure) offers a panorama of Tuscan gastronomy—lampredotto, ribollita, Pienza pecorino.

The Uffizi Gallery occupies a palace built from 1560 by Giorgio Vasari for Cosimo I de' Medici, intended to group the offices (uffizi) of Florentine magistracies. Transformed into a public museum by the last Medici in the 18th century, it contains one of the world's greatest collections of Italian Renaissance painting, distributed across 45 rooms. Two works by Sandro Botticelli are its global icons: The Birth of Venus (circa 1484–1485) and Primavera (circa 1477–1478), both commissioned by the Medici. The museum also preserves works by Leonardo da Vinci, Raphael, Michelangelo, Titian, and Caravaggio.

Ponte Vecchio, whose current construction dates to 1345 after an Arno flood destroyed a previous bridge, is the only bridge in Florence to have survived World War II destruction—retreating Germans in August 1944 spared this bridge on direct order, according to tradition. Its jewelers' shops replaced the butchers who occupied it in the Middle Ages: the Medici, who used Vasari's corridor above the bridge to move between their palaces without crossing through the crowd, supposedly demanded this change for reasons of smell.

Piazza della Signoria has been Florence's civic square since the Middle Ages, dominated by the Palazzo Vecchio tower (94 meters) built between 1299 and 1314. The Loggia dei Lanzi, constructed between 1376 and 1382, shelters several sculptures including Benvenuto Cellini's Perseus (1554) and Giambologna's Rape of the Sabine Women (1583). Michelangelo's David originally stood at the entrance to Palazzo Vecchio—the marble block had been set aside for 25 years before the artistic commission chose Michelangelo in 1501.

→

The Arno's left bank (Oltrarno) offers a less touristy Florence: the San Frediano quarter with its artisan workshops, the Boboli Gardens behind Palazzo Pitti (Medici residence from 1550), and Piazzale Michelangelo with its panoramic view of the city at sunset.

The Galleria dell'Accademia owes its worldwide fame to a single work: Michelangelo's David, sculpted between 1501 and 1504 from a 5.17-meter block of Carrara marble that two previous sculptors had given up on. The figure represents young David before the battle with Goliath—an unusual choice, as tradition depicted the post-victory moment. Moved from Piazza della Signoria in 1873 to protect it from weather, it has been installed since 1882 in a room specially built for it, beneath a zenithal cupola.

The basilica of Santa Croce, built from 1294 by the Franciscan order, is the principal burial place of illustrious Florentines: Galileo Galilei (died in Arcetri in 1642, buried here only in 1737 after long Catholic Church opposition), Michelangelo Buonarroti (died in Rome in 1564, buried here at his express request), Niccolò Machiavelli, Lorenzo Ghiberti, and writer Vittorio Alfieri. The complex also houses Giotto's frescoes in the Bardi and Peruzzi chapels (circa 1320).

→

Florence is also a first-rate gastronomic city: bistecca alla fiorentina (Chianina beef T-bone grilled over embers, at least 5 centimeters thick, served rare), ribollita (vegetable and stale bread soup reboiled the next day, hence the name), crostini al fegatini (chicken liver on toasted bread). The Sant'Ambrogio market, less frequented by tourists than Mercato Centrale, is a good place to observe Florentines' daily shopping habits.

Piazza del Campo is Siena's main square, built on the site of a former marshy zone at the confluence of the three hills on which the city developed. Its shell-shaped curved form, sloping toward Palazzo Pubblico, is divided by nine lines of white travertine into nine sectors—direct reference to the Council of Nine, the Sienese Republic's government in the 14th century, which ruled the city during its period of greatest prosperity. The buildings surrounding it had to respect height and window regulations imposed by the commune; those that didn't conform were demolished. The Palio, a bareback horse race ridden by representatives of the city's seventeen neighborhoods (the contrade), is held here on July 2 and August 16—the square is then covered with earth.

The Torre del Mangia, built between 1325 and 1348 by brothers Francesco and Muccio di Rinaldo d'Arezzo, rises 87 meters (102 with lightning rod) alongside Palazzo Pubblico. It was meant to reach exactly the same height as Siena's Duomo—symbol of equality between civil and religious power. Its name comes from the nickname of its first bell-keeper, Giovanni di Duccio, called Mangiaguadagni ('earnings-eater') for his tendency to spend his income without saving. The Cappella di Piazza, a marble loggia at the tower's foot, was built in 1352 as an offering to the Virgin by the plague's survivors.

The Duomo di Siena, begun in the 13th century and consecrated in 1263, is one of the most accomplished examples of Italian Gothic architecture. Its cladding in horizontal bands of white and black marble—Siena's heraldic colors, drawn according to legend from the white and black horses of brothers Sénius and Aschio, mythical founders of the city—is repeated inside the nave. The floor is covered with 56 engraved and inlaid marble panels, created by 40 different artists between the 14th and 16th centuries. The Piccolomini Library, built in 1492 and decorated by Pinturicchio between 1502 and 1508, houses ten frescoes depicting the life of Pope Pius II.

→

Siena's contrade—the seventeen historic neighborhoods each with their own colors, flag, patron saint, and museum—still structure the city's social life. Wandering the lanes around the Duomo at sunset, when tourists have left the center, reveals a medieval city whose urban plan has scarcely changed since the 14th century. Palazzo Pubblico houses the Museo Civico with Ambrogio Lorenzetti's frescoes on the Effects of Good and Bad Government (1338–1339).

The Colosseum—Flavian Amphitheatre by its original name—is the largest amphitheater ever built in antiquity. Its construction began around 70–72 AD under Emperor Vespasian, on the site of Nero's Domus Aurea artificial lake, and was completed in 80 under his son Titus. Its dimensions: 189 meters long, 156 meters wide, 50 meters high, with an estimated capacity of 50,000 to 80,000 spectators. The outer walls combine three superimposed architectural orders: Doric, Ionic, Corinthian. Inside, a network of underground tunnels (the hypogeum) allowed gladiators and animals to rise directly into the arena through trapdoors. Used for gladiatorial combat, animal hunts, and executions until the 6th century, it subsequently served as a stone quarry—some of the stripped travertine blocks went into building St. Peter's Basilica.

The Roman Forum and Palatine Hill, included on the same ticket as the Colosseum, form the archaeological heart of ancient Rome. The Forum was the civic, religious, and commercial center of the Roman Republic and Empire for more than ten centuries, from the 7th century BC to the 7th century AD. The Palatine is the hill on which, according to tradition, Romulus founded Rome on April 21, 753 BC; it later became the residence of emperors, whose ruined palaces still occupy the entire hill. The Arch of Constantine, adjacent to the Colosseum, was erected in 315 to celebrate the emperor's victory over Maxentius at the Milvian Bridge in 312.

→

The Trastevere district, on the Tiber's right bank, is one of the rare sectors of Rome whose urban fabric has retained a medieval scale. Its cobbled lanes of sanpietrini (basalt paving stones) and lively evening squares make it a starting point for exploring popular Rome, far from the major archaeological sites. The basilica of Santa Maria in Trastevere, founded in the 4th century, is one of Rome's oldest churches and preserves 12th-century Byzantine mosaics.

The Pantheon is the best-preserved ancient building in Rome. A first temple was built on this site in 27 BC by Marcus Agrippa, general and son-in-law of Augustus—his name still appears on the entrance frieze (M.AGRIPPA.L.F.COS.TERTIVM.FECIT). Destroyed by fire, it was entirely rebuilt by Emperor Hadrian between 118 and 125 AD. Its unreinforced Roman concrete dome, 43.3 meters in diameter, remains the world's largest in that material. Its interior height equals its diameter. The central oculus, 8.92 meters in diameter, is the only light source; the floor, slightly domed at the center, directs rainwater toward a peripheral drainage system. In 609, Pope Boniface IV transformed the temple into a church, which ensured its preservation. It houses the tombs of Raphael (died 1520) and the first two kings of unified Italy.

Piazza Navona is built on the site of Domitian's stadium (1st century AD), whose elongated form is still perfectly legible in the square's plan—276 meters long, 54 meters wide. Its three Baroque fountains were commissioned under Pope Innocent X: the Fountain of the Four Rivers (1651), Bernini's masterpiece whose four allegorical figures represent the Nile, Ganges, Danube, and Rio de la Plata, occupies the square's center.

The Trevi Fountain marks the terminus of the Aqua Virgo aqueduct, built in 19 BC by Marcus Agrippa to supply his baths—this 22-kilometer aqueduct still functions today. The monumental fountain was commissioned in 1730 by Pope Clement XII from architect Nicola Salvi, financed through a Vatican lottery draw, and officially inaugurated in 1762 by Clement XIII—Salvi had died in 1751 without seeing it completed. It measures 20 meters wide and 26 meters high. The coin-toss tradition, popularized by William Wyler's film Roman Holiday (1953) and Federico Fellini's La Dolce Vita (1960), generates about one million euros annually, donated to Caritas.

→

Baroque Rome can be discovered on foot along an hour-long route linking the Pantheon to Piazza Navona then to Piazza Farnese and Campo de' Fiori—a lively morning market square with at its center the statue of philosopher Giordano Bruno, burned alive here in 1600 by the Inquisition. This part of Rome retains a density of Baroque palaces, fountains, and churches unmatched in Europe.

St. Peter's Basilica and Vatican Square constitute Catholic Christianity's largest architectural ensemble. The current basilica, built between 1506 and 1626 on the foundations of a 4th-century paleochristian basilica, mobilized the greatest Renaissance and Baroque architects: Bramante, Raphael, Sangallo, Michelangelo (who designed the dome, completed in 1590 by Giacomo della Porta after his death), Maderno, and Bernini, who also conceived the elliptical colonnade of the square (1656–1667). Michelangelo's dome, 42 meters in interior diameter, draws direct inspiration from Brunelleschi's in Florence. The Pietà, sculpted by Michelangelo between 1498 and 1499 when he was 23 years old, stands in the first chapel on the right.

The Vatican Museums gather one of the world's most important collections of art and antiquities, assembled over several centuries by popes. The Sistine Chapel, intended for papal ceremonies, owes its renown to the ceiling frescoes painted by Michelangelo between 1508 and 1512 at Pope Julius II's request—a four-year project during which the artist worked lying on scaffolding. The nine central panels depict Genesis, from back to entrance. The Last Judgment, painted on the back wall between 1536 and 1541 under Paul III, was created 24 years after the ceiling by a 61-year-old Michelangelo.

Castel Sant'Angelo, the former mausoleum of Emperor Hadrian built around 135 AD and transformed into a papal fortress in the Middle Ages, is connected to the Vatican by the Borgo's secret passage (the 'Passetto') that popes used in times of danger. From its summit it offers a 360-degree view of Rome and the Tiber. The Sant'Angelo Bridge leading to it, adorned with ten angels bearing the instruments of the Passion sculpted from Bernini's models around 1669, is one of Rome's most successful Baroque axes.

Villa Borghese, an 80-hectare park landscaped from 1606 for Cardinal Scipione Borghese, houses the Galleria Borghese, one of Rome's most concentrated museums. The cardinal had assembled his collection partly through legal seizures or pressure on artists—he forced Raphael to surrender the Deposition he'd just delivered to the original patron. The six ground-floor rooms gather Bernini sculptures including Apollo and Daphne (1622–1625) and Pluto and Proserpina (1621–1622), admirable for their technical virtuosity.

Piazza di Spagna owes its name to the Spanish Embassy to the Holy See that stood there since the 17th century. The monumental staircase linking the square to the Trinità dei Monti church—135 steps built between 1723 and 1726 thanks to a bequest from French diplomat Étienne Gueffier—is Rome's largest Baroque staircase. At the foot of the steps, the Barcaccia fountain (1627), attributed to Pietro Bernini, father of the famous Gian Lorenzo, represents a partially submerged boat referencing a Tiber flood.

The Via Appia Antica, the first major Roman road built in 312 BC to link Rome to Brindisi, is lined at its exit from Rome with ancient tombs, Christian catacombs, and pine groves. Renting a bike at Porta San Sebastiano and riding the first few kilometers on the original basalt paving stones—some dating from the Republican era—gives a sense of what these roads meant for the Roman Empire's cohesion.
