Grand Tour of Lazio: Etruscan Sites and Volcanic Lakes

Villa d'Este with 500 gravity-fed fountains, 120-hectare Villa Adriana, Italy's first printed book at Subiaco, 1,006-day conclave at Viterbo, Bomarzo's Sacro Bosco rediscovered by Dalí, 200 painted Etruscan tombs at UNESCO Tarquinia. The grand tour of Lazio beyond Rome.

Begin with Tivoli's three UNESCO villas - Villa d'Este's hydraulic gardens with 500 fountains, Villa Adriana's 120 hectares, and the wild gorges of Villa Gregoriana. Ascend the Aniene valley to Subiaco, where Italy's first book was printed in 1465. Cross the Sabine region to the volcanic Lake Bracciano, the hilltop village of Calcata, and Caprarola's pentagonal Palazzo Farnese. Immerse yourself in Bomarzo's Sacro Bosco and its 37 basalt monsters, before reaching Viterbo and its 1,006-day conclave. Civita di Bagnoregio floats above its calanchi, Lake Bolsena unfolds its 114 km² caldera. Then Etruscan Tuscia reveals its triptych: Tuscania's sarcophagi, Tarquinia's 200 painted tombs, and Cerveteri's city of the dead.

Villa d'Este - Inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2001, this villa was commissioned by Cardinal Ippolito II d'Este, appointed Governor of Tivoli in 1550. Frustrated at not being elected pope, he hired architect Pirro Ligorio to transform a former Benedictine convent into a prestigious residence. The 4.5-hectare terraced gardens house over 500 fountains, 300 water jets, and some sixty cascades - all powered solely by gravity, without a single pump, thanks to water channeled from the Aniene River through an underground conduit dug in 1564-1565. The Fontana dell'Organo (1568), a hydraulic organ built by Frenchmen Luc Leclerc and Claude Venard, still plays melodies every two hours. Franz Liszt lived here for twenty years and composed "Fountains of the Villa d'Este."

Villa Adriana - A UNESCO site since 1999, this imperial residence was built between 118 and 138 AD by Emperor Hadrian on a 120-hectare plateau (40 hectares visitable), 28 km from Rome. A great traveler and amateur architect, Hadrian recreated monuments that had impressed him across the Empire: the Pecile recalls Athens' Stoa, the Canopus evokes an Egyptian canal lined with statues, and the Teatro Marittimo is an artificial island encircled by a canal where the emperor withdrew to meditate. A network of underground carriage-accessible galleries allowed servants to move about without disturbing the imperial spaces. After centuries of pillaging (marble, mosaics, and sculptures scattered across European museums), the villa inspired the greatest Renaissance architects from Bramante to Palladio.

Villa Gregoriana - This nature park, landscaped in 1835 by Pope Gregory XVI, occupies the gorge of the Aniene River, where the river plunges in a Grande Cascata with a 120-meter drop. Steep paths wind through dense vegetation to reach caves (including Neptune's Cave and the Sirens' Cave) and the ruins of the Roman villa of Manlio Vopisco. On the ancient acropolis overlooking the gorge stand the Temple of Vesta (1st century BC), a tholos with Corinthian columns, and the more austere rectangular Temple of the Sibyl.

→

Stroll on the acropolis of ancient Tibur - From the esplanade of the temples of Vesta and the Sibyl, panoramic views embrace the entire valley of waterfalls and the wooded gorges of the Aniene. Below, the Ponte Gregoriano and ruins of Roman villas complete the scene.

Monastero di Santa Scolastica - The oldest Benedictine monastery in the world (founded by Saint Benedict in the 6th century, sole survivor of the twelve monasteries he established in the Aniene valley before departing for Monte Cassino). Its Romanesque cloister and Cosmatesque cloister frame a still-active community. The Biblioteca Statale holds approximately 150,000 volumes, 440 manuscript codices, and 213 incunabula. In 1465, two disciples of Gutenberg from Mainz, Konrad Sweynheym and Arnold Pannartz, installed Italy's first printing press here and produced the peninsula's first printed book: Cicero's De Oratore, followed by Saint Augustine's De Civitate Dei, each in 275 copies. The typographical style created here is called "stile Subiaco."

Monastero di San Benedetto (Sacro Speco) - Clinging to the cliff like an eagle's nest, this sanctuary is built around the cave where Saint Benedict lived as a hermit for three years (around 500 AD) before writing his Rule, foundation of Western monasticism. The walls of the cave and the superimposed chapels (upper church and lower church) are entirely covered with frescoes from the 11th to 14th centuries. Among them, the oldest known portrait of Saint Francis of Assisi - painted during his visit in 1223 - shows him without a halo and without stigmata, evidence it was made during his lifetime. The stairway carved into the rock leading to different levels offers vertiginous views over the valley.

Rocca Abbaziale - This fortress has dominated the town of Subiaco since the 11th century. Built for the abbots who controlled the valley, it was remodeled in the 16th century and now hosts temporary exhibitions. From its terraces, plunging views over the tufa rooftops, the medieval bridge of San Francesco, and the Aniene valley.

→

Stroll through the medieval town of Subiaco - The sloping alleys of this town, listed among Italy's most beautiful, descend from the Rocca toward the bridge of San Francesco, a Gothic arch spanning the Aniene. The path between the two monasteries (about 2 km) traverses a holm oak forest in the Regional Park of the Simbruini Mountains.

Riserva Naturale dei Laghi Lungo e Ripasottile - These two lakes are remnants of the ancient Lacus Velinus, a vast body of water the Romans began draining in the 3rd century BC by digging a canal toward the Marmore Falls (now in Umbria). The 3,000-hectare reserve protects reed beds, wet meadows, and riverside woods that shelter grey herons, egrets, Eurasian bitterns, and numerous migratory birds.

Castello di Rocca Sinibalda - Perched above the Turano valley, this medieval fortress was transformed in the 16th century into a Renaissance palace commissioned by the Cesarini family. Viewed from above, its plan resembles an eagle with outstretched wings. Inside, the halls are decorated with Mannerist frescoes inspired by Ovid's Metamorphoses.

Centro storico di Rieti - This Sabine town claims to be the Umbilicus Italiae, the exact geographical center of Italy (a stone disk in Piazza San Rufo marks the spot). The Cattedrale di Santa Maria Assunta (12th century), the arcaded Palazzo Vescovile, and the alleys of the medieval quarter preserve an atmosphere of a Roman provincial town untouched by mass tourism.

→

Birdwatching at Lakes Lungo and Ripasottile - Wooden observation hides dot the path circling the lakes. Migration months (March-May and August-October) are the richest. Binoculars recommended.

Lago di Bracciano - This nearly circular volcanic lake of 57 km² occupies an ancient crater of the Sabatini Mountains, northwest of Rome. Its deep waters (up to 165 m) supply part of the capital's drinking water network. Swimming and sailing are permitted, but combustion engines are forbidden, preserving the water's clarity. Three historic towns share its shores: Bracciano, Trevignano Romano, and Anguillara Sabazia.

Castello Orsini-Odescalchi di Bracciano - This 15th-century fortress, erected by the Orsini family, dominates the lake from a rocky promontory. Its rooms preserve frescoes, armor, and period furnishings. The castle has remained in the Odescalchi family and continues to host receptions and celebrations.

Trevignano Romano - This medieval town on the lake's northern shore preserves the ruins of the Rocca Orsini atop the hill (360° panorama over the lake and Sabatini Mountains) and the Chiesa di Santa Maria Assunta (16th century), whose interior houses frescoes attributed to Raphael's school. The Porta dell'Orologio (18th century) marks the entrance to the historic center.

→

Tasting of lake products - Lakeside restaurants offer grilled coregone (lake whitefish), eels, and in autumn, porcini mushrooms from the chestnut forests of the Sabatini Mountains. Sabina DOP olive oil accompanies all dishes.

Calcata Vecchia - This village perches on an isolated volcanic tufa spur, surrounded by wooded ravines carved by the Treja River. Abandoned in the 1930s after being declared in danger of collapse, it was repopulated from the 1960s by artists, artisans, and alternative communities who made it a unique bohemian village in Lazio. Narrow alleys, ceramic workshops, art galleries, and ivy-covered walls compose a setting that served cinema (scenes were filmed here for movies by Pasolini and Monicelli).

Parco Regionale Valle del Treja - The valley of the Treja, a tributary of the Tiber, forms a wooded canyon of oaks, hazels, and ferns. The main trail leads to the Monte Gelato waterfalls, a series of cascades over travertine slabs that served as natural backdrops for numerous Italian film productions. Remnants of a medieval mill and a Roman villa dot the route.

Parco Regionale di Veio - This nature reserve a few kilometers south protects the territory of the ancient Etruscan city of Veii, Rome's rival conquered in 396 BC after a ten-year siege. Trails connect the remains of Etruscan tombs (including the Tomb of the Ducks, the oldest painted tomb in Etruria), the sanctuary of Portonaccio, and the Cascata dell'Inferno on the Cremera stream.

→

Hike to Monte Gelato waterfalls - The trail descends from the parking area toward the waterfalls through riverside forest. Swimming possible in summer in natural pools. Easy route, approximately 3 km round trip.

Palazzo Farnese - An absolute masterpiece by Jacopo Barozzi da Vignola, this palace built between 1559 and 1575 for Cardinal Alessandro Farnese the Younger rests on the foundations of a pentagonal fortress begun in 1530 by Antonio da Sangallo the Younger. Vignola kept the five-sided shape but hollowed out the center to create an extraordinary circular courtyard with double superimposed porticoes. The Scala Regia, a helical staircase supported by 30 Doric columns, is so wide that - according to legend - the cardinal rode up it on horseback to the piano nobile. The halls are covered with 6,000 m² of Mannerist frescoes by brothers Taddeo and Federico Zuccari. The Sala del Mappamondo presents a map of the known world in 1574 painted by Giovanni Antonio Vanosino, surmounted on the ceiling by a representation of the zodiac. The Sala degli Angeli (or degli Echi) conceals an acoustic effect: two people whispering at opposite corners can hear each other as if standing side by side - a cardinal's stratagem for monitoring conversations.

Giardini Farnesiani - Behind the palace, two secret gardens (one summer, one winter) extend the architecture into the open air, with fountains, boxwood parterres, artificial grottos (Grotto of the Satyrs, Grotto of the Tartars), and statues. Higher up the hillside, the monumental park extends through woods to the Casina del Piacere, an isolated hunting lodge that served as summer residence for President Luigi Einaudi (1948-1955). The gardens are closed on Sundays and holidays.

Via Dritta and the town of Caprarola - Vignola wasn't content with just the palace: he had part of the medieval town razed to trace the Via Dritta, a 500-meter straight artery rising from the lower square to the palace piazzale, creating a theatrical perspective. The tufa and peperino houses of the side alleys preserve the atmosphere of a Tuscia Viterbese village.

→

Tasting of Cimini Mountain hazelnuts - Caprarola lies at the heart of the nocciola dei Cimini production zone, a round, fragrant hazelnut used in creams, cakes, and torroni. The town's bakeries offer them in every form.

Parco dei Mostri (Sacro Bosco) - The world's oldest park of modern sculpture. Created from 1547 by Prince Pier Francesco Orsini, known as Vicino, on a design by architect Pirro Ligorio (the very same who succeeded Michelangelo as director of the St. Peter's Basilica project in Rome). Across 3 hectares of forest, 37 monumental sculptures in local basalt - sphinxes, war elephant, dragon, giant tortoise, Hercules tearing apart Cacus, leaning house - were carved directly from rock blocks emerging from the ground. The Orco, a giant mouth whose interior is furnished as a room with table and benches, originally bore the inscription "Lasciate ogni pensiero voi ch'entrate," an inverted quotation from Dante's Inferno (now replaced by "Ogni pensiero vola"). Orsini dedicated the park to his wife Giulia Farnese with the motto "Sol per sfogare il core." Forgotten for centuries, the park was rediscovered by Salvador Dalí in 1938 then restored by the Bettini family in the 1950s.

Vitorchiano - 15 minutes from Bomarzo, this town is suspended on a peperino (grey volcanic tufa) spur above the Vezza valley. Medieval houses cling to the cliff and preserve their profferli, those external stone staircases typical of Tuscia. In the main square, a 4-meter moai sculpted in 1990 by master carvers from Easter Island - invited to demonstrate their expertise - faces the medieval façades.

→

Walk through the Sacro Bosco - The route winds through the forest between sculptures for about 1.5 hours. Each figure bears cryptic inscriptions in verse. The small tempietto dedicated to Giulia Farnese, attributed to Vignola, reproduces Florence's Santa Maria del Fiore dome at reduced scale.

Quartiere San Pellegrino - The best-preserved medieval quarter in central Italy: a maze of cobbled alleys, peperino arches, profferli (external staircases), towers, and fountains forming a virtually intact 13th-century urban ensemble. Grey stone façades alternate with pointed arches, in an atmosphere preserved from time.

Palazzo dei Papi - Gothic palace built between 1255 and 1266, papal residence when the popes left Rome for Viterbo from 1257 to 1281. Its loggia overlooking the valley is the site of the longest conclave in history: from September 1, 1268 to September 1, 1271 (1,006 days), the cardinals failed to elect a successor to Clement IV. The exasperated townspeople locked the cardinals in the conclave hall, tore off the roof to expose them to the elements, and put them on bread and water. Gregory X was finally elected and imposed the bull Ubi Periculum, which has since codified the rules of conclaves.

Duomo di San Lorenzo - Romanesque cathedral from the 12th century adjoining the Papal Palace, remodeled in the Renaissance. The two-tone striped bell tower and austere façade contrast with the interior decorated with 14th-century frescoes.

Piazza del Plebiscito and Palazzo dei Priori - Viterbo's civic square is bordered by the Palazzo dei Priori (15th-century town hall) whose Council chamber preserves 16th-century frescoes depicting the city's history, and by the Clock Tower. The central fountain has been a landmark since the Middle Ages.

Museo Nazionale Etrusco - Housed in the Rocca Albornoz (14th-century fortress), this museum presents archaeological collections from Etruscan sites across the province: ceramics, sarcophagi, ex-votos, and reconstructions of painted tombs.

→

Bathing at the free Terme del Carletti - These natural outdoor pools, carved into travertine on the outskirts of Viterbo, are fed by sulfurous springs emerging at 58°C. The water is rich in sulfur, fluoride, and calcium. Access is free, day and night, year-round.

Civita di Bagnoregio - Nicknamed "la città che muore" (the dying town), this village perches on a volcanic tufa spur surrounded by clay ravines (calanchi) in constant erosion. The progressive collapse of the cliffs - accelerated by rain and earthquakes - has isolated the village from the outside world: only a 300-meter pedestrian bridge suspended above the void connects it to the town of Bagnoregio. Fewer than a dozen permanent residents live here year-round. Founded 2,500 years ago by the Etruscans, Civita was the birthplace of Saint Bonaventure (1221). The Porta Santa Maria, flanked by two lions holding a human head in their claws (symbol of victory over tyrants), marks the entrance to a stone village frozen in time.

Chiesa di San Donato - On Civita's main square, this remodeled Romanesque church preserves a 15th-century wooden Crucifix from Donatello's school and Trecento frescoes in the side chapel. The terracotta floor and grey tufa walls give the building an intact medieval austerity.

→

Crossing the bridge and walking through Civita - The uphill pedestrian bridge (300 m, quite steep) offers a striking perspective on the isolated village and the calanchi surrounding it. Arrive early morning or late afternoon to avoid crowds and see the cliffs tinted by slanting light.

Lago di Bolsena - Europe's largest volcanic lake (114 km², maximum depth 151 m), formed in a caldera of the Volsini mountains approximately 300,000 years ago. Its clear, fish-rich waters contain two islands: Bisentina (former Farnese property, with Renaissance chapels) and Martana (where, according to tradition, Amalasuntha, Queen of the Ostrogoths, was strangled in 535). Swimming is permitted on several black volcanic sand beaches.

Basilica di Santa Cristina in Bolsena - This 11th-century basilica is linked to the Eucharistic Miracle of Bolsena (1263): a Bohemian priest on pilgrimage, doubting transubstantiation, saw the host bleed onto the corporal during Mass. Pope Urban IV, then in Orvieto, instituted the feast of Corpus Christi in response. The Cappella del Miracolo, in the catacombs beneath the church, preserves the altar of the episode. The 4th-century Christian catacombs, carved into tufa, are accessible by guided tour.

Castello Monaldeschi in Bolsena - 13th-century fortress dominating the lake from the medieval town. It houses the Museo Territoriale del Lago di Bolsena, devoted to the geological, archaeological (Etruscan and Roman remains from ancient Volsinii), and natural history of the lake.

Montefiascone - Perched on the crater's southern rim, this town offers circular panoramas over the lake and countryside. The Duomo di Santa Margherita has one of Italy's largest domes after St. Peter's in Rome, visible from afar. It's also the birthplace of Est! Est!! Est!!! di Montefiascone DOC wine, whose name comes from a medieval anecdote: a German bishop en route to Rome sent his cupbearer ahead to mark "Est" (there is some) on taverns with good wine; arriving at Montefiascone, the cupbearer inscribed the word three times, so pleased was he with the wine.

→

Tasting Est! Est!! Est!!! wine - Montefiascone's cellars offer tastings of this DOC dry white, accompanied by local products: acquacotta (Tuscia peasant soup), sbroscia (lake fish soup), and grilled eels.

→

Boat excursion to Isola Bisentina - Boats depart from the ports of Bolsena and Capodimonte to reach this wooded island housing seven Renaissance chapels, including one attributed to Vignola, and Italian gardens. Booking recommended.

Basilica di San Pietro - On the colle di San Pietro, probably the ancient Etruscan acropolis, this Romanesque-Lombard basilica (8th-13th century) stands among three watchtowers and the Palazzo dei Canonici, on a vast meadow dotted with Etruscan sarcophagi. The grey tufa façade is pierced by a Cosmatesque portal with mosaics and surmounted by a large white marble rose window with three concentric circles (symbol of the Trinity), flanked by sculptures of the four Evangelists and two dragons. Inside, the central nave preserves a rare Cosmatesque pavement with geometric motifs. The crypt, supported by 28 reused columns (Roman and early medieval), forms a stone forest beneath groin vaults. Orson Welles shot scenes of his Othello here, Monicelli filmed L'Armata Brancaleone, and Pasolini his Gospel According to St. Matthew.

Basilica di Santa Maria Maggiore - At the foot of the colle di San Pietro, this former cathedral of the Diocese of Tuscania (consecrated October 6, 1206) combines Romanesque and pre-Gothic styles. The façade associates a superb rose window, mosaics, and reused carved fragments. The interior houses a monumental 14th-century Last Judgment attributed to Tuscan painters Gregorio and Donato d'Arezzo, painted columns, and a very rare 13th-century immersion baptismal font. The massive, squat bell tower stands detached from the church body.

Museo Archeologico Nazionale - Installed in the former Franciscan convent of Santa Maria del Riposo, this free museum assembles dozens of sarcophagi in nenfro (local volcanic tufa) and terracotta from surrounding necropolises, notably those of the aristocratic Curunas and Vipinana families (4th-2nd century BC). The Amazons' Sarcophagus, decorated in relief with scenes of Amazonomachy on three sides, is one of the major pieces. In the early 19th century, the Campanari family exported sarcophagi to London (1837), arousing the enthusiasm of archaeologist George Dennis who baptized Tuscania "the city of Etruscan sarcophagi."

Medieval historic center - Tuscania's walls, erected by the Etruscans then expanded in the 13th century to a perimeter of 4,700 meters, encircle a town of plastered palaces and tufa houses. Etruscan sarcophagi are scattered everywhere: in streets, squares, embedded in church walls, used as street furniture. Piazza Basile lines semi-recumbent figures from sarcophagus lids along a low wall. The Parco Torre di Lavello, on the ancient walls, offers a panorama over the valley and the Colle di San Pietro.

→

Excursion to the Necropoli Madonna dell'Olivo - 2 km southwest of town, this Etruscan necropolis arranges its tombs on three rocky terraces in the Marta valley. On the upper level, the Grotta della Regina, a vast L-shaped hypogeum with a network of underground corridors on several levels, is one of the most important in central-northern Etruria. Visit by reservation.

Necropoli dei Monterozzi - Inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2004, this 130-hectare necropolis is the largest known in Etruria. It contains over 6,000 sepulchers carved into rock, of which approximately 200 are adorned with frescoes - the only major testimony of pre-Roman classical painting in the Mediterranean. Archaeologist Massimo Pallottino defined it as "the first chapter in the history of Italian painting." The oldest date back to the 7th century BC. Tombs are accessible through long dromoi (sloping corridors) and visible through climate-controlled glass doors that maintain constant temperature and humidity. Among the most famous: the Tomb of the Leopards (5th century BC, vividly colored banquet scene), the Tomb of Hunting and Fishing (hunting and diving scenes), the Tomb of the Augurs (funeral games scenes), the Tomb of the Lionesses (acrobatic dolphins, music and dance).

Museo Archeologico Nazionale - Palazzo Vitelleschi - Housed in this palace built between 1436 and 1439 for Cardinal Giovanni Vitelleschi, a blend of late Gothic and early Renaissance, this museum presents its collections on three levels. On the ground floor, stone sarcophagi from the Hellenistic period, including that of the Priest (Laris, head of the Partunu family). On the first floor, the famous Cavalli Alati (Winged Horses), a 4th-century BC terracotta high relief from the pediment of the Ara della Regina temple - one of the best-known works of Etruscan art. On the second floor, a panoramic loggiato and climate-controlled rooms reconstructing painted tombs whose frescoes have been detached for conservation: Tomb of the Triclinium, of the Chariots, of the Olympics, of the Ship.

Medieval historic center of Tarquinia - The perched medieval town overlooks the Tyrrhenian coast and the Maremma countryside. The Chiesa di Santa Maria in Castello (12th century), the most representative of the town's medieval architecture, stands near the cylindrical torrione known as "Matilde di Canossa's tower." The alleys contain Gothic palaces, Romanesque towers, and peperino façades.

→

In-depth visit to the painted tombs - About twenty tombs are open to the public in rotation along a marked route on the grassy hillside. QR codes along the path enable 3D virtual tours of the interiors, to appreciate fresco details invisible from the glass doors. For enthusiasts, it's possible to request extraordinary opening of normally closed tombs (Tomb of the Ogre, of the Shields, of Typhon) - by advance written request to the superintendency.

Necropoli della Banditaccia - Inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2004 (jointly with Tarquinia), this "city of the dead" extends over more than 20 hectares and constitutes the largest organized necropolis of the ancient Mediterranean. Unlike Tarquinia (frescoes), Banditaccia is distinguished by its urbanism: tombs are arranged along streets and squares, forming veritable funerary quarters. The oldest (9th century BC) are simple shafts; the monumental circular tumuli (7th-6th century) reach up to 30 meters in diameter and contain several burial chambers reproducing the interior of Etruscan houses with beds, pillars, doors, and windows carved into tufa. The Tomb of the Reliefs (4th century BC, Matuna family) preserves painted stuccos representing tools, weapons, domestic animals, and kitchen utensils - a 3D inventory of Etruscan daily life.

Museo Nazionale Cerite - Installed in Palazzo Ruspoli in the heart of Cerveteri, this small museum displays finds from excavations at Banditaccia and other necropolises of ancient Caere (one of the twelve members of the Etruscan Dodecapolis): painted ceramics, bucchero nero (typically Etruscan polished black pottery), gold and filigree jewelry, terracotta ex-votos. The most spectacular pieces - the Sarcophagi of the Spouses (6th century BC) - are kept at the Museo di Villa Giulia in Rome and the Louvre in Paris.

Historic center of Cerveteri - The medieval town, built on the ancient acropolis of Caere, dominates the coastal plain. Palazzo Ruspoli (16th century), the Rocca, and the sloping alleys preserve the atmosphere of a rural Lazio village, far from the bustle of Rome located just 45 km away.
