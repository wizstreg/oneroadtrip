Secret War Trail in Laos — 10-Day Plain of Jars and Pathet Lao Caves Journey

From Xiangkhouang's megalithic jars to Sam Neua's underground cities, this historical route reveals the secret Laos of bombing and resistance, where 20,000 people lived in caves for nine years.

Immerse yourself in Laos' hidden history through a journey unveiling the secrets of the Secret War, the most heavily bombed conflict in history. From the mysterious Plain of Jars scattered with craters to Vieng Xai's underground caves where the Pathet Lao resisted for years, this historical itinerary combines UNESCO sites, moving testimonies and spectacular landscapes. From Phonsavan to Luang Prabang via Sam Neua and Nong Khiaw, cross remote regions still bearing scars of a tragic past.

Key highlights (6)
  • Plain of Jars — Explore Sites 1, 2 and 3 with 250+ millennia-old megalithic jars surrounded by bomb craters, silent witnesses to history's most intense war
  • COPE Center Phonsavan — Discover the devastating impact of cluster bombs on Lao populations through moving testimonies and exhibits on unexploded ordnance
  • Vieng Xai Caves — Guided tour of Pathet Lao underground cities where 20,000 people lived from 1964 to 1973 with hospitals, schools and theater in caves
  • Old Phonsavan Airport — Walk among bombed hangars and crater-riddled runways maintained as Secret War memorial
  • Remote Sam Neua — Immerse in Hua Phan province, Pathet Lao stronghold, with authentic markets and pristine mountain landscapes far from tourist circuits
  • Dramatic Nong Khiaw — Regenerating rest between vertical karst cliffs on the Nam Ou, with climb to Pha Daeng viewpoint for breathtaking views

Phonsavan spreads across the heart of Xiangkhouang Province at 1,100 meters elevation, surrounded by green hills and pine forests. This new town was founded in the 1970s after the destruction of the old capital Xieng Khouang during the bombing.

The region bears the nickname hills of paradise for its rolling landscapes that contrast with the steep mountains of the north. The altitude moderates the climate and offers pleasant temperatures year-round.

Traces of war remain omnipresent in the region: bomb craters, metal scraps recycled into fences and planters, unexploded ordnance still dotting the fields.

The road from Luang Prabang crosses the mountains for eight hours on winding switchbacks. The landscapes change progressively as altitude increases.

Late arrival in Phonsavan reveals a modest town with straight streets lined by buildings of no particular charm. The interest lies in the surrounding sites rather than the town itself.

Site 1 of the Jars gathers over 250 stone jars arranged on a hill with panoramic views over the plain. The largest jars reach three meters high and several tons in weight.

The origin of these megalithic vessels remains mysterious despite theories about the Iron Age and funerary practices. Archaeologists continue debating their dating between 500 BC and 500 AD.

Bomb craters dot the immediate surroundings of jar sites, testifying to the intensity of bombing. Some jars bear traces of shrapnel and projectiles.

The MAG center organizes guided visits recounting ongoing clearance work. Experts still regularly evacuate unexploded ordnance from the 1960s and 70s.

Site 2 offers a wilder setting with jars scattered in the forest. Access requires a short walk on safely marked trails.

→

Visiting the COPE center in Phonsavan illustrates the impact of cluster bombs on local populations. Exhibits show prosthetics and survivor testimonies.

→

Exploring different jar sites requires strictly following marked paths. Unmarked areas may still contain unexploded ordnance.

→

Local guides tell legends about the jars including that of King Khun Cheung who supposedly had them made to ferment rice alcohol. Stories blend with history.

→

Returning to Phonsavan allows visiting craft workshops that recycle aluminum from war scrap. Spoons and bracelets made from aircraft metal testify to a form of resilience.

Site 3 concentrates the most imposing jars of the entire plain on two nearby hills. The climb offers spectacular views of surrounding landscapes.

Villages on the plain preserve traditional Lao architecture with houses on stilts. Inhabitants cultivate rice and vegetables on land cleared of ordnance.

The hot springs of Muang Khoun flow in a bucolic landscape about thirty kilometers from Phonsavan. Natural pools allow a relaxing swim.

Muang Khoun ruins testify to the past glory of the old provincial capital. The old That destroyed by bombing still stands amid the rubble.

A giant seated Buddha miraculously survived the bombing despite visible damage. The statue sits in the open surrounded by temple ruins.

Sam Neua

The road from Phonsavan to Sam Neua winds through remote mountains for about six hours of switchbacks. Landscapes evolve from plain hills to deep valleys of Hua Phan province.

Sam Neua stretches in a narrow valley dominated by forest-covered mountains. The town preserves a quiet atmosphere far from main tourist circuits.

Hua Phan province served as a Pathet Lao stronghold during the war. Nearby caves housed the communist movement's headquarters.

American bombing systematically pounded the region seeking to destroy communist bases. Populations took refuge in caves for years.

Arrival in Sam Neua offers an opportunity to discover a little-visited town where Lao life unfolds at tranquil rhythms.

Sam Neua

Vieng Xai caves sit thirty kilometers from Sam Neua in the limestone mountains. These caves served as an underground city for the Pathet Lao from 1964 to 1973.

Over 20,000 people lived in the caves during intensive bombing. Galleries housed offices, hospitals, schools and even a theater.

The mandatory guided visit leads through main caves where Pathet Lao leaders lived. Guides recount daily underground life.

Prince Souphanouvong's cave, Pathet Lao leader, preserves period furniture and documents. Spartan conditions contrast with his princely rank.

Vieng Xai artificial lake offers a serene landscape reflecting karst mountains. Locals come here to relax after work.

→

The cave circuit requires about three hours with audio guide in several languages. Explanations contextualize the secret war events.

→

Survivor testimonies collected in exhibits illustrate population resilience. Children born in caves testify to extreme conditions.

→

Walks around Vieng Xai lead to less frequented secondary caves. The karst landscape offers spectacular panoramas.

→

Returning to Sam Neua allows reflecting on the complexity of Lao history. Multiple perspectives enrich understanding of events.

A day in Nong Khiaw allows recovering after long journeys and discovering this picturesque village. Karst cliffs dominate the landscape spectacularly.

Pha Daeng viewpoint requires a steep hour climb through jungle. Views over the Nam Ou valley amply reward efforts.

Nam Ou river flows between mountains creating a spectacular natural corridor. River boats glide on rapid waters carrying passengers and goods.

Pha Tok caves are reached by boat on the Nam Ou. These caves also served as refuge during bombing.

The suspension bridge over the Nam Ou sways underfoot offering a unique perspective on the river. Locals cross it without hesitation with their motorbikes.

The road from Nong Khiaw to Luang Prabang unrolls three and a half hours of switchbacks through mountains. Karst landscapes progressively give way to gentler valleys.

Arrival in Luang Prabang marks return to civilization after days spent in remote regions. The UNESCO peninsula unfolds its golden temples and colonial villas.

Wat Xiengthong raises its cascading roofs on the Mekong's edge. This 16th-century Lao architecture masterpiece sparkles in afternoon sun.

Mount Phousi peaks at the peninsula's center and offers circular views over the two rivers' confluence. The 328 steps lead to Wat Chom Si's golden stupa.

The Royal Palace converted to museum displays the abolished monarchy's regalia and the Phra Bang. Rooms reconstruct court life before the 1975 revolution.
