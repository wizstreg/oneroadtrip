Gers Road Trip: Gascon Bastides, Armagnac & Musketeers

Armagnac ages in the cellars, white garlic dries beneath medieval halls, and somewhere on a hillside, d'Artagnan still dreams of Paris. The Gers lives to the rhythm of its bastides and seasons.

From the episcopal city of Condom to the jazzy bastide of Marciac, the Gers unfurls golden hillsides dotted with fortified villages, Cistercian abbeys and medieval bastides. Six of France's Most Beautiful Villages, unexpected museums—from Armagnac to d'Artagnan to Picasso—and a gastronomy that elevates duck and garlic to an art of living. A circuit through the heart of Gascony, between Compostela and Côtes de Gascogne.

Key highlights (9)
  • Condom: Gothic cathedral and Armagnac museum
  • Larressingle: France's smallest fortified city
  • Flaran Abbey: Cistercian jewel and Simonow collection
  • La Romieu: UNESCO collegiate church and village of cats
  • Lectoure: Pyrenean panorama and thermal baths in an 18th-century mansion
  • Saint-Clar: bastide with two arcaded squares, capital of white garlic
  • Auch: monumental cathedral and staircase facing the Pyrenees
  • Lupiac: d'Artagnan's birthplace and Tower of Termes
  • Marciac: jazz bastide and Saint-Mont vineyards

Condom's Saint-Pierre Cathedral has watched over the Baïse River since the 16th century. Built on the site of an 11th-century abbey church, it follows the classic plan of southern Gothic churches: a single wide nave flanked by lateral chapels squeezed between buttresses. Its 19th-century choir draws inspiration from Albi's Sainte-Cécile Cathedral. Bossuet briefly served as bishop here in 1669-1670.

The 16th-century cloister adjoining the cathedral unfolds vast galleries with elegant flamboyant Gothic arcades. It once led bishops to their private chapel. The eastern gallery showcases three pointed arches adorned with nine painted keystones bearing the coats of arms of the lord bishops. On the square outside, the Musketeers statue freezes the moment: Athos, Porthos, Aramis and d'Artagnan crossing swords.

The Armagnac Museum occupies the outbuildings of the former bishop's palace. Built around a monumental 19th-century beam press, the rooms trace the complete production cycle of Gascony's signature brandy: viticultural techniques, alembic distillation and barrel-making. France's oldest eau-de-vie certainly deserves its own museum.

Wander through the lanes of the old episcopal city. The 17th and 18th-century townhouses—Polignac, Cugnac—display their stone façades and wrought iron flourishes, witnesses to the wealth of armagnac merchants. The Baïse, once nicknamed the "silver river," carried barrels downstream to Bordeaux.

Head to the marina quays along the Baïse. Developed in the 18th century for river trade, the waterway remains navigable and offers a peaceful setting bordered by plane trees. Condom is listed among Occitanie's Grand Sites under the title "Armagnac, Abbey and Cities."

→

Guided tour of the episcopal city. The tourist office offers commentary-led circuits unveiling the secrets of the cathedral, cloister, townhouses and the Way of Saint James that passes through town.

→

Armagnac tasting in the town's cellars. Producers open their doors to explain the distillation process and offer guided tastings. Floc de Gascogne—a blend of grape must and armagnac—is served chilled as an aperitif.

💡 Several cellars open year-round
→

Stroll along the Armagnac greenway, built on a former railway line. This flat, shaded path is perfect for cycling or walking, connecting Condom to neighboring villages through vineyards and hillsides.

Larressingle, nicknamed the "little Carcassonne of Gers," is France's smallest fortified city. Its 13th-century ramparts stretch 900 feet and rise 20 to 26 feet high, enclosing a castle-keep, a Romanesque church dedicated to Saint Sigismond and a handful of golden stone houses. Cross the restored drawbridge and step back seven centuries.

Larressingle's Saint-Sigismond Church displays sober Romanesque architecture with a typically Gascon bell-wall. Curious detail: the saint's statue, commissioned from a Toulouse workshop, is actually a scaled-down version of Aimé Millet's Vercingétorix. A rather odd mustachioed Gaul watches over this medieval village.

Flaran Abbey, a Cistercian monastery founded in 1151 in the Baïse valley, ranks among the best-preserved in southwestern France. Romanesque church, cloister, chapter house, refectory and gardens compose a monastic ensemble of remarkable legibility. In 1913, the Gers archaeological society prevented its cloister from being dismantled and shipped to New York's Cloisters museum.

In the former monks' dormitory at Flaran, the Simonow Collection displays over 300 works of European art from the 16th to 20th centuries. Cézanne, Renoir, Picasso, Monet, Braque, Rodin—a small Gascon Louvre assembled by an Anglo-Saxon philanthropist who fell in love with the Gers. The department's most visited site.

Montréal-du-Gers, Gascony's first bastide, founded in 1255 by Alphonse de Poitiers, brother of Saint Louis. Perched on a rocky promontory, it preserves its grid layout, stone arcades, ogival fortified gate and half-timbered houses. Classified as one of France's Most Beautiful Villages.

A few miles from Montréal, the Gallo-Roman villa of Séviac reveals an immense 4th-century palace. Enfilade of rooms, marble colonnades, lavish baths and polychrome mosaic pavements—unique in Europe for their number and state of preservation. The motifs of flowers, vine scrolls and birds unfold like sumptuous carpets.

→

Medieval siege camp at the gates of Larressingle. Life-size catapults, trebuchets, stone-throwers and crossbows: operate medieval war machines. Firing demonstrations and activities for the whole family.

💡 Interactive activities for children
→

Hike from Larressingle to Condom on the GR 65, the Way of Saint James. Three miles through vineyards and hillsides, passing the UNESCO-listed Artigues bridge. A pilgrim's path accessible to everyone.

→

Montréal-du-Gers market on Friday mornings. Under the bastide's arcades, producers offer foie gras, shepherd's cheeses, seasonal vegetables and Côtes de Gascogne wines. Guaranteed Gascon atmosphere.

La Romieu's Saint-Pierre Collegiate Church is listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site as part of the Ways of Saint James. Built in just six years (1312-1318) by Cardinal Arnaud d'Aux, cousin of Pope Clement V, it displays southern Gothic architecture with a single nave, cloister and two towers. One contains one of Europe's oldest double-helix staircases. Climb the octagonal tower for panoramic views of the countryside and discover recently restored 14th-century murals in the sacristy.

On the arcaded square, dozens of stone cats keep watch from window ledges. They tell the legend of Angéline: during a terrible famine in 1342, this orphan hid her cats when villagers got rid of theirs. When rats threatened the following year's harvest, her twenty kittens saved La Romieu. The sculptures are the work of Maurice Serreau, who fell in love with this story.

The Gardens of Coursiana, classified as a Remarkable Garden, spread across fifteen acres facing the collegiate church. An arboretum with 700 species, an English garden, a family vegetable garden and 350 rose varieties compose a botanical journey created in 1974 by botanist Gilbert Cours-Darne.

→

Guided tour of the collegiate church and its secrets. The route leads from the church to the cloister, then into the towers with passage along the wall-walk and the sacristy paintings. Audio guides available.

→

Hike on the GR 65 starting from La Romieu. The village sits at the crossroads of the Le Puy-en-Velay route and the Rocamadour route. Marked trails wind through Gascon hills, between vineyards and sunflower fields.

→

Stone cat hunt through the village. Try to find all of Maurice Serreau's sculptures hidden on façades, windows and doorsteps. A bust of Angéline reigns over the square. Children love it.

Lectoure stands on a limestone promontory overlooking the Gers valley. Two miles of ramparts from the 13th to 17th centuries encircle the city, former capital of the Counts of Armagnac. On clear days, the Pyrenees chain cuts across the southern horizon. This part of Lomagne is nicknamed "French Tuscany."

Saint-Gervais-et-Saint-Protais Cathedral, built in the 12th century on the site of a Roman temple dedicated to Cybele, dominates the town with its 150-foot bell tower. The stone spire that once topped it, doubling the current height, was destroyed by a storm on the eve of the Revolution. The interior houses remarkable furnishings.

The Eugène Camoreyt Archaeological Museum, housed in the former bishop's palace—now the town hall—preserves a unique collection of twenty Gallo-Roman taurobolic altars dedicated to Cybele. The Hall of the Illustrious and the pharmacy room complete the visit to this building that also served as Marshal Lannes' residence.

Step through the doors of the Blue of Lectoure workshops. Woad, a blue pigment extracted from dyer's woad, made Occitanie's fortune in the 15th and 16th centuries. Here, the workshop perpetuates the tradition: dyeing scarves, dresses and cosmetics in that deep blue that once seduced kings and nobles.

The old castle of the Counts of Armagnac, now transformed into a hospital, closes off the western end of town. Opposite, the Gothic Fontaine de Diane supplied the city with water since the Middle Ages. The fountain trail leads from one to another, between stone stairways and hanging gardens.

→

Climb to the cathedral bell tower. The 150 feet offer stunning views of the town, the Gers valley and, on clear days, the Pyrenees chain from Pic du Midi to Canigou. Guided tours available through the tourist office.

→

Lectoure market on Friday mornings. In the town center lanes, producers sell Lectoure melons, foie gras, local saffron and Lomagne garlic. A must for Gascon flavors and temperaments.

→

Lectoure thermal spa. Housed in an 18th-century mansion, the thermal center draws its water from more than 3,000 feet deep, at 108°F. Pool at 90°F, jacuzzi, hammam and sauna—accessible for half-day sessions without medical treatment.

💡 Spa access available without medical prescription

Saint-Clar, a bastide founded in 1274 through a paréage between the King of England and the Bishop of Lectoure, possesses a rarity: two arcaded squares. On Place de la Garlande, the hall classified as a Historic Monument raises its 20 weathered wooden pillars supporting a 13th-century timber frame. The rear, rebuilt in stone in the 18th century, is topped with a neoclassical bell tower.

The second arcaded square, the Plaçote (Place de la République), preserves its "cornières"—covered galleries beneath golden stone houses. The old-fashioned storefronts hint at interior courtyards and a prosperous commercial past. Remarkably rare, these two squares lie just dozens of yards apart.

Head up to the Château-Vieux, Saint-Clar's original core. The old 12th-century Romanesque church, with its handsome bell tower, overlooks half-timbered houses packed into medieval lanes. The Garden of Four Seasons, created on the site of collapsed houses, pays tribute to Gascon poet Dastros (1594-1648).

Saint-Clar is the capital of Lomagne white garlic, cultivated here since the 13th century and protected by a PGI since 2009. The House of Garlic offers visits to understand the cultivation, drying and uses of this Gascon specialty. In summer, garlic braids adorn the market stalls.

→

Saint-Clar market on Thursday mornings. Under the wooden-pillared hall, tradition dates back to the Middle Ages. White garlic, fattened poultry, seasonal fruits and vegetables—a concentrate of Lomagne terroir. In summer, the garlic market runs from July through September.

💡 Estiv'Ail on the 1st Thursday of August
→

Guided tour of Saint-Clar bastide. The tourist office offers commentary-led circuits to understand the singular architecture of this two-square bastide. Medieval lanes, ramparts, wash houses and Gascon pigeon houses complete the picture.

→

Hike the hillsides around Saint-Clar. Marked trails offer views of the Arrats valley and Lomagne countryside—fields of sunflowers, garlic, wheat and vines compose a patchwork of colors depending on the season.

Sainte-Marie Cathedral in Auch, an imposing monument stretching 345 feet, was built starting in 1489 on a plan inspired by Notre-Dame de Paris. Its 18 stained-glass windows by Arnaud de Moles rank among the finest Renaissance stained glass in France. The oak choir features 113 carved stalls where more than 1,500 figures narrate the Old and New Testaments. Listed as UNESCO heritage as part of the Ways of Saint James.

Auch's monumental staircase plunges 115 feet between the upper and lower town. Built starting in 1863 on the model of Italian staircases, it counts 374 steps in total—though 233 suffice for the direct ascent. On one landing, Jaume Plensa's sculpture "l'Observatoire du temps" recounts the flood story in Latin.

Halfway up the staircase, d'Artagnan's statue faces the Gers valley. This bronze by Firmin Michelet (1931) represents the most famous Gascon, born Charles de Batz de Castelmore around 1613 in Lupiac, 35 miles away. Auch, historic capital of Gascony, proudly claims the musketeer.

The Tower of Armagnac, 130 feet tall, rises southeast of the cathedral on Place Salinis. Built in the 14th century as the archbishop's prison, it counts seven floors each containing a cell of 40-50 square feet. Today it houses the cathedral treasury museum.

The Jacobins Museum, housed in a former convent, contains France's second-largest collection of pre-Columbian and Latin American art, bequeathed by patron conservator Guillaume Pujos. Ceramics, textiles and metalwork from Latin American civilizations sit alongside collections of sacred art and Egyptian antiquities.

Get lost in the pousterles, those narrow, steep medieval lanes that tumble down the hillside between upper and lower town. Medieval houses, stone stairways, vaulted passages—a picturesque labyrinth that Auscitains have navigated for centuries.

→

Guided tour "Auch, capital of Gascony." The Pays d'art et d'histoire du Grand Auch offers commentary-led circuits: cathedral, cloister, Tower of Armagnac, pousterles and townhouses. Auch was the seat of an archbishopric whose authority stretched from the Pyrenees to the Atlantic.

→

Auch market on Saturday mornings. On Place de la Libération and adjacent streets, producers spread out foie gras, duck breast, confits, Gascon pastis and armagnac. The department's largest market, lively and delicious.

→

Stroll along the Gers River in the lower town. From the foot of the monumental staircase, follow the shaded riverbanks. Turn around to admire the silhouette of the cathedral and Tower of Armagnac perched on the hillside—the finest view of the town.

Lupiac, a castelnau perched at 800 feet on a Gascon hillside, is the birthplace of the most famous musketeer. Charles de Batz de Castelmore was born here around 1613 at the family castle, before setting off to conquer Paris around 1630. He became captain-lieutenant of King Louis XIV's Musketeers and died at the siege of Maastricht in 1673.

On the central square, the first equestrian statue of d'Artagnan rears skyward—an 11-foot monumental bronze inaugurated in 2015. Sword brandished, the rider seems ready to resume his journey to Paris. The village preserves Saint-Barthélemy Church, whose four bells date from the musketeer's era.

The d'Artagnan Museum, housed in the Saint-Jacques Chapel classified as a Historic Monument, traces the life of the real musketeer. Wax figures in Grand Siècle costume, period documents, editions of Dumas's Three Musketeers and movie posters. A twenty-minute film draws parallels between legend and reality.

Nine miles southwest, the Tower of Termes-d'Armagnac rises 120 feet above the Adour valley. Classified as a Historic Monument, it's the last vestige of the palace of the lords of Termes, vassals of the Counts of Armagnac (late 13th century). Its most famous resident, Thibault de Termes, accompanied Joan of Arc at the siege of Orléans in 1429 and remained faithful to her until the end. From the summit, spectacular views of the Pyrenees.

Inside the Termes keep, climb the 150 steps of the spiral staircase. Reconstructed armory, seigneurial chamber, reception hall—each floor plunges into the life of a 13th-century Gascon palace. Children can duel with foam swords in a mini-arena above the weapons room.

→

Visit the d'Artagnan Museum with activity booklet for children. Pencil in hand, young musketeers embark on an adventure through the rooms. Written guide available in six languages for adults.

→

Walk around Lake Lupiac, a few hundred yards from the village. Shaded trail, swimming possible in summer, fishing and picnic areas. The bucolic setting of Gascon hillsides lends itself to a peaceful break.

→

Medieval events at the Tower of Termes in summer: jousting tournaments, theatrical tours, medieval banquets. It's the only medieval site in Gers open year-round. In August, the "d'Artagnan chez d'Artagnan" festival transforms Lupiac into a 17th-century village.

💡 Tower open year-round except Tuesdays off-season

Marciac, a royal bastide founded in 1298 through a paréage between the Abbot of Case-Dieu and the Seneschal of Toulouse, has preserved its grid plan intact. Its impressively vast central square is bordered by elegant arcades with imposing wooden pillars. This is where jazzy Gascony's heart beats.

Notre-Dame Church raises its bell tower above the terracotta rooftops. Its painted portal exemplifies southern Gothic. Nearby, the spire of the former Augustinian convent chapel recalls that Marciac also lies on the Arles route leading to Santiago de Compostela.

Territoires du Jazz, a scenographic space unique in Europe, offers a journey into the heart of jazz history. Infrared headset on, explore twelve settings: from Africa's origins to New Orleans, from feverish blues to New York clubs, to Europe as the welcoming land for jazz musicians.

L'Astrada, a modern performance hall inaugurated to extend the festival spirit year-round. Dance, circus, theater, classical music and of course jazz take turns here. The Augustins cultural center, arranged in the former convent, houses the tourist office and exhibitions.

→

Stroll beneath the medieval arcades of the central square. Terraces, shops and art galleries animate this bastide that has vibrated to the rhythm of jazz since 1978. In summer, the free off-festival takes over the square with impromptu concerts on every corner.

→

Walk around Lake Marciac, half a mile from the bastide. Created in 1963, this body of water offers jogging, fishing, paddle boats and swimming in summer. The landscape setting, ringed by hills, invites a pause between visits.

→

Saint-Mont wine and armagnac tasting. The AOC Saint-Mont vineyard, a stone's throw from Marciac, holds a curiosity: the Sarragachies vine, dating from the early 19th century and classified as a Historic Monument—one of the oldest pre-phylloxera vines in France, with 21 grape varieties including 7 unknowns.

💡 Three wine routes: castles, bastides, Val d'Adour
→

Visit in summer for Jazz in Marciac (late July through mid-August). Created in 1978 by a handful of friends, the festival now attracts 250,000 spectators and the biggest names in world jazz. A 6,000-seat big top in the evenings, free concerts on the square during the day.
