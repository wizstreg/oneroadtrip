Sail in a loop from Paros, from the Byzantine hundred-door church to the granite formations of Kolymbithres, before diving into the unspoiled Lesser Cyclades of Iraklia, Schinoussa, and Koufonissia. Cross to Amorgos and its cliff monastery, contrast with Ios's energy, then head north to Milos and its pirate caves before closing the loop via Sifnos and Antiparos.

Parikia Paros

+2

9.2 Sunset View Hotel

Parikia's old harbor greets you on arrival with its whitewashed windmills standing sentinel on the breakwater—Cycladic postcard perfection in the flesh. Behind the ferries and bustling quays, the old town weaves through pebble-paved lanes around a Venetian kastro built in 1260 from marble blocks salvaged from a nearby Temple of Athena: look carefully and you'll still spot ancient columns embedded in the walls. The old market follows the main street to the kastro and brims with tavernas, bakeries, and little boutiques. Get lost in this maze—the late afternoon light washing over the white facades is worth more than any guidebook.

The Panagia Ekatontapiliani basilica—'the church of a hundred doors'—ranks as Greece's third-largest early Christian sanctuary. Legend traces its foundation to the 4th century by Saint Helena, mother of Constantine the Great; the present building dates from the 6th century and was restored under Justinian. The complex sits within a 252-meter perimeter wall and shelters a main church measuring 40 by 25 meters, flanked by the oldest baptistery in the Christian East. Tradition holds that the hundredth door remains invisible and will open only when Constantinople is reclaimed. Visit early in the morning—the filtered light in the nave, the Doric columns salvaged from a Roman gymnasium, and the 15th-century iconostasis are worth the stop. It's also a major pilgrimage site for the Assumption, rivaling even Tinos.

→

Swimming at Livadia Beach, the easiest beach to reach from the harbor—10 minutes on foot heading west. Fine sand, shallow water, perfect for a first plunge after the crossing.

→

Sheltered anchorage available at Krios Beach depending on conditions, just across from Parikia harbor—dinghy shuttle, good holding on sandy bottom.

Naoussa concentrates all of Paros's charm in one village. The fishing harbor is ringed by whitewashed lanes climbing toward the Kastro—a 15th-century Venetian fortification partially submerged, its round tower still standing guard at the harbor entrance. The old Venetian warehouses have transformed into elegant restaurants serving the morning catch. Naoussa actually boasts the largest fishing fleet in all the Cyclades: watch fishermen mending their nets early in the morning before the terraces fill. Come evening, the kastro lanes morph into an open-air lounge.

Kolymbithres, 4 km northwest of Naoussa, is Paros's most photographed spot. Giant granite boulders, polished by millennia of wind and sea spray, have carved out natural basins filled with translucent saltwater—somewhere between swimming pools and stone sculptures. The water often sits less than a meter deep and takes on milky-blue reflections against the pearl-gray stone. In July–August, the pools fill by 11 a.m.: arrive before 10 for first dibs or come late in the day. Caïques make the run from Naoussa harbor.

→

Anchorage at Naoussa marina or Ormos Plastira depending on conditions—good holding on sandy bottom, sheltered from the north.

→

Snorkeling in the coves of Kolymbithres: the granite formations create tunnels and underwater cavities accessible with just a mask. Colorful fish guaranteed.

Naxos Chora

+2

9.3 Kalergis Studios
9.6 Tesoro Elegant escapes- suites
9.5 Flèria Seaview Suites

The Portara greets you at the entrance to Naxos harbor—this monumental white marble gateway standing 6 meters tall is all that remains of a temple dedicated to Apollo, whose construction began around 530 BC under the tyrant Lygdamis. The building was never completed, and the Venetians carted off all the blocks to build the kastro, except these four 20-ton giants, too heavy to budge. The Portara faces toward Delos, Apollo's birthplace, and legend places Theseus's abandonment of Ariadne here. Sunset from the causeway is a popular classic—for crowd-free photos, go early in the morning.

The Kastro of Naxos Chora was erected in 1207 by Marco Sanudo, the Doge of Venice's nephew, after the Fourth Crusade. He moved the island's capital from the heights down to the coast. The inner precinct, accessible only through two gates—one still sporting its original 13th-century wooden door—shelters noble residences bearing carved Venetian coats of arms, the Catholic cathedral, the archaeological museum housed in the former Jesuit school (Nikos Kazantzákis studied here), and vaulted lanes nearly deserted. A time warp 15 minutes on foot from the harbor.

The Bourgos quarter, between the harbor and the Kastro, packs in artisan workshops, marble studios, tavernas, and galleries. Naxos has been a marble island since antiquity—the unfinished kouros statues abandoned in the quarries during the Archaic period still bear witness. Push open the workshop doors: sculptors still work the local white stone.

→

Sunset facing the Portara from anchorage or the islet causeway—the white marble turns amber, the sea turns gold. One of the Aegean's most photographed spectacles.

→

Waterfront stroll and provisioning at the harbor market: Naxos is the largest island in the Cyclades and its hinterland produces renowned cheeses, honey, citron, and potatoes. The market is well stocked.

Iraklia Livadi

Welcome to the Lesser Cyclades. Iraklia counts just 150 permanent residents across 18 km² and electricity only arrived in 1982. The port of Agios Georgios is endearing: the entire village descends to greet the ferry, collect deliveries, and chat under the tamarisks. Livadi beach, 1.5 km from the port, is the island's finest—a long crescent of blonde sand lapped by turquoise water where you'll find space even in high August. No umbrellas for rent on site, bring your own.

→

Snorkeling and swimming in Livadi bay: the crystalline waters let you spot fish, sea urchins, and if you're lucky, sea turtles that regularly visit the bay.

→

Hike to the village of Panagia (Chora), 4 km from the port via a marked trail. This inland hamlet is the island's traditional capital, with its Cycladic stone houses and whitewashed church. Those with energy left can continue to the Agios Ioannis cave, considered the largest in the Cyclades with 2 km of stalactite-adorned galleries—bring a flashlight and closed-toe shoes.

Schinoussa Tsigouri

Top hotels
9.8 Paralos Lodging
9.6 SOHOROS suites
9.1 Ailouros summer hotel

Schinoussa is even smaller than Iraklia—8 km², fewer than 300 residents. Its main village, Chora (or Panagia), perches 1.5 km from Mersini port: a single street you can walk down in 5 minutes, lined with family tavernas and geranium-draped houses. Here, everyone knows everyone and newcomers quickly blend in. Come evening, Chora comes alive on the central square: locals and the Greek vacationers who make up most of the island's clientele gather over a warm rakomelo. Push open the door of kafeneio Xara—an institution.

Tsigouri beach is Schinoussa's most accessible, 600 meters from Chora via a paved road. Golden sand, shallow water, and tamarisks for shade—it's the only spot on the island offering this comfort. A beach bar serves drinks and mezedes. For a wilder experience, Psili Ammos beach is worth the 45-minute walk from Chora: a pristine bay with water of surreal clarity, no infrastructure whatsoever. Arrive early in summer.

→

Swimming and lounging at Tsigouri—or swim out to the small islet 200 meters offshore for absolute solitude.

→

Short coastal hike to Livadi or Aligaria beach: Schinoussa's trails link the beaches one to another, all within 40 minutes on foot from Chora. No roads between them, just hillside paths.

Koufonisia Pori

Top hotels

Koufonissia—actually two islands, Ano and Kato Koufonissi—is the rising star of the Lesser Cyclades. Ano Koufonissi's Chora, the only inhabited village, sprawls around a colorful harbor dominated by the church of Agios Georgios. But what makes the island unique is its southeastern coast: a 45-minute coastal path from Chora links the beaches of Finikas, Fanos, and Italida—surreal turquoise waters on golden sand—before reaching Pori, the archipelago's finest beach. At Pori's northern tip, the Ksilobatis caves offer limestone arches bathed in emerald water. Budget 3 hours round trip for the full circuit from Chora, no shade along the way: leave early and pack water.

Gala Beach, between Pori and Italida, is a hidden cove whose Greek name means 'milk'—the cave's wave foam does indeed give the water a unique milky tint. Just beyond lies the Eye of the Devil, a natural rock pool with a diving platform. These two curiosities sit 5 minutes' walk from Pori.

→

Sea kayaking from Ammos beach toward the cliffs of Kato Koufonissi: paddling along the sheer walls reveals arches and coves invisible from land. Kayaks are for rent at the main beach. Check the wind (meltemi) before setting out.

→

Anchorage in Pori bay: sandy bottom, good holding depending on conditions. Water taxis from Chora make 4 runs per day—handy for alternating between swimming and exploring Chora.

Amorgos Katapola

9.3 Pension The Big Blue
9.5 Pension Titika

Katapola, one of Amorgos's two ports, opens onto spectacular mountain scenery. Amorgos Chora, at 320 meters elevation, is linked to the port by a winding road—take the municipal bus or hike 45 minutes up the switchback trail. At the top, white windmills keep watch over the village from a wind-lashed ridge, and Venetian coats of arms still adorn old doorways.

The Panagia Hozoviotissa monastery is one of the Aegean's marvels. Founded in the 11th century by Byzantine Emperor Alexios I Komnenos to shelter a miraculous icon from Palestine, it's built literally into a cliff face 300 meters above the sea. The building stands 40 meters tall yet only 5 meters wide in places—8 floors carved into rock, a labyrinth of small rooms and narrow staircases. You'll need to climb about 300 steps to reach it from the road. The monks (3 or 4 remain) welcome visitors with Amorgian raki, loukoumi, and Greek coffee. Modest dress required—garments are loaned at the entrance. Luc Besson filmed scenes from The Big Blue here in 1987.

Agia Anna beach lies below the monastery at the foot of the cliffs. Turquoise water, pebbly bottom, access via stairs from the road. This is where the monastery's icon supposedly washed ashore mysteriously on a boat from Palestine. Open sea, sometimes with swell.

→

Waterfront stroll at Katapola and provisioning: Amorgos is known for its honey, local wine, and sun-dried octopus. The harbor market is modest but authentic.

→

Easy hike between Chora and the windmills for panoramas over the archipelago—on clear days, the view reaches Naxos and Paros.

→

Swimming at Agia Anna (open sea, possible swell)—cliff diving is an island classic.

Ios Mylopotas

Ios has a dual identity you'll grasp on arrival: Chora perched on its hill is an authentic Cycladic maze of whitewashed lanes adorned with bougainvillea and 12 windmills—but come nightfall, the bars take over and the island has been one of the Aegean's party hotspots for over 40 years. If you're seeking tranquility, climb to Chora early and head down before noon. For history buffs, the prehistoric site of Skarkos, 10 minutes from Chora, is one of the best-preserved Minoan settlements in the Aegean. The hill said to hold Homer's tomb is accessible via trails north of the island.

Mylopotas is Ios's largest beach—a sand crescent over 1 km long facing due west, Blue Flag and crystalline waters. It's also the liveliest: beach clubs, wakeboarding, diving, evening concerts. The eastern part of the beach is calmer than the western end where nightlife concentrates. The bay offers well-held anchorage on sandy bottom, but very busy in July–August.

→

Swimming and paddleboarding in Mylopotas bay—SUP and canoe rental on the beach. Waters stay shallow for 5 to 10 meters from shore.

→

Anchorage in the bay (good holding on sand, high seasonal crowding). In season, better to drop anchor arriving early morning.

Santorini Vlychada

Santorini—the caldera and its perched villages seen from the sea offer one of the Mediterranean's most spectacular maritime entrances. Vlychada harbor on the south coast is calmer than the crowded caldera and ringed by lunar-white cliffs. It's the best overnight anchorage. The volcanic sand beach of Perissa and Perivolos stretches 7 km: black sand burning hot (bring sandals), crystalline water, and gently sloping sandy bottom. In high season, it's one of the island's liveliest.

The Akrotiri archaeological site is Santorini's equivalent of Pompeii—a Minoan city buried under meters of volcanic ash during the catastrophic eruption around 1500 BC. Excavations began in 1967 under Spyridon Marinatos. Beneath a massive protective dome, you wander the ancient city streets: two-story houses with sewer systems, murals depicting daily life, pottery, tools. No bodies were found, suggesting inhabitants fled before the eruption.

Red Beach sits 5 minutes from Akrotiri on foot: a blood-red cliff plunges vertically into the sea, creating a striking contrast with the blue water. The beach is narrow and crowded; access is sometimes restricted due to rockfall risk in summer. Go early or enjoy it from the sea.

→

Swimming on the volcanic beach of Perissa or Perivolos—the black sand absorbs heat, consider water shoes. Water is deep and clear.

→

Wine tasting in Santorini—the island's vineyards produce assyrtiko, a mineral white unique in the world, cultivated in basket-shaped (kouloura) vines on volcanic soil. Several estates are accessible from anchorage or by road.

Folegandros is an island of character—lashed by the meltemi, with 300-meter cliffs plunging into intense blue sea, and a Chora perched on a vertiginous ledge that long served as a place of political exile (until 1969). The main village is entirely pedestrian: three beautiful squares lined up and shaded by plane trees, stone-paved lanes, an inhabited Venetian kastro, and the persistent feeling that time passes differently here. From Chora, the switchback trail (20-30 min) leads to the Panagia church on its promontory—the view over the Aegean and neighboring islands at sunset ranks among the Cyclades' most spectacular.

Agkali (or Angali) beach on the south coast is accessible by car or bus from Chora. Pebble bottom sheltered by a cliff, deep blue water, two tavernas under the tamarisks. No natural shade except at the cliff base—bring an umbrella. From there, a 30-minute coastal trail leads to the wild cove of Agios Nikolaos, a favorite among island regulars.

→

Swimming and snorkeling at Agkali: the rocky seabed shelters varied marine life. The cove is sheltered enough to be pleasant even in moderate wind.

→

Sunset from anchorage or Panagia church—the golden light on Folegandros's white cliffs is a spectacle that regulars return for every summer.

Sikinos is the least visited of the inhabited Cyclades—300 residents, minimal infrastructure, no hotel chains. Alopronia port is a peaceful fishing village with a small, calm sandy beach. Then take the road (or better, the paved mule path) climbing 4 km to the Kastro and Chora, perched at 280 meters—two quarters facing each other, the first lively with cafés and the town hall, the second a labyrinth of white lanes bordered by bougainvillea. Three windmills stand watch over the ensemble from the cliff edge, facing the sea.

→

Climb to Zoodochou Pigi monastery, built in 1690 atop the Kastro: panoramic view over the archipelago and beyond. The island's most astonishing monument, however, is Episkopi—a 3rd-century Roman mausoleum converted to a Christian temple in the 17th century, accessible via trail from the Kastro. The place radiates a particular energy.

→

Swimming on Alopronia beach at the foot of the port—fine sand, calm water, and tamarisks. It's the island's most accessible beach, perfect for a pause before setting sail again.

Milos Adamas

+2

9.8 Iliana Olive Branch
9.6 Hotel Milos Sea Resort

Milos is the volcanic island par excellence—over 70 beaches, each different in sand color and cliff shape. Kleftiko, on the southwest coast, is the showstopper: ancient white limestone formations riddled with sea caves, accessible only by water. It was a Saracen pirate lair, and the name recalls that (kleftiko = bandits' place). Today, boat excursions depart each morning from Adamas. The caves are swim-accessible from the boat—filtered underwater light creates mesmerizing blue and green hues. Dolphins, turtles, and seals frequent the area.

Adamas, Milos's main port, sits on one of the Mediterranean's largest natural bays. The waterfront is lively and well supplied. From the port, boat excursions connect Kleftiko, Sarakiniko (the lunar scenery of the north), and the sirmata—these colorful rock-carved boat garages on the shore, Milos's symbol.

→

Snorkeling at Kleftiko: the sea caves are swim-accessible from boat excursions. Bring mask and fins. Don't land from your sailboat as access is regulated—daytime anchorage only.

Kimolos Rema

Top hotels
9.4 Alisea Resort
9.4 Hotel Kamari
9.7 Aerides Boutique Rooms
9.9 Tania Milos

Kimolos takes its name from the Greek 'kimolia'—chalk—and its houses are indeed whiter than white. The island covers just 36 km² and is easily explored on foot or by scooter from Psathi port. The village of Chorio, perched on a hill 2 km from the port, is dominated by a 14th-century Venetian kastro, some of whose houses are still inhabited. The metropolitan church of Panagia Odigitria (1592) is its religious heart. Wandering Chorio, look for the syrmata at tiny Goupa beach: these are former boat garages carved into rock, intact maritime heritage.

Prassa beach (also called Agios Georgios) is unanimously considered Kimolos's finest—some say of all the Cyclades. Almost surreal white sand, turquoise water of exceptional clarity. A beach bar, sun loungers, and sea caves within flipper range. Near Prassa, natural hot springs with reddish waters supposedly have healing properties according to locals. Spotting a monk seal in the neighboring waters isn't rare.

Skiadi rock is worth the 20-30 minute walk from the main road: erosion has sculpted a formation shaped like a giant mushroom, unique in the Mediterranean. Look for the marked path between Chorio and the west coast.

→

Swimming at Prassa and snorkeling around the rock formations: corals, sea urchins, and colorful reef fish in remarkably clear water. The nearby Vromolini sea caves will please freedivers.

Sifnos is the Cyclades' island of ceramics and gastronomy. Since at least the 9th century BC, the island has exported utilitarian pottery throughout the Mediterranean—Sifnos clay is rich in feldspars that speed vitrification. In the 18th and 19th centuries, about a hundred workshops ran at full tilt. Today a handful of potters perpetuate the tradition: at Platys Gialos and Vathy, you'll still see the old pottery buildings recognizable by their curved roof and elongated shape. Several workshops welcome visitors.

Platys Gialos is Sifnos's largest sandy beach—a long bay well exposed with Blue Flag, waterfront tavernas, and practical anchorage to the left of the bay. The White Tower sits 1.5 km northeast. A 2 km walk southeast leads to Chryssopigi beach, the island's most emblematic, with its little white monastery perched on a rocky spur—one of Sifnos's most photographed images.

→

Swimming and paddleboarding at Platys Gialos: wide sheltered bay, shallow water, fine sand. SUP rental on the beach.

Ormos Vathy is one of the Cyclades' finest anchorages—a perfectly sheltered horseshoe bay with a small fishing village at the back. On nearby Tsopos beach, the Atsonios pottery has operated for 4 generations: it's one of the last workshops set up the old way, right on the beach, with wood-fired kilns and drying tables. Watch the potter work before choosing from the utilitarian ceramics and artisan pieces on display.

Apollonia, Sifnos's capital, perches in the hills at the island's center. It's a maze of paved lanes where you'll happily get lost among artisan shops, traditional tavernas, and small flower-filled squares. It was founded in the 6th century BC and bears the god Apollo's name. Come evening, it's the island's liveliest spot—open terraces, Greek music, warm ambiance without Mykonos excess.

→

Walk in the island's south from Vathy to Faros—a fishing hamlet with its small sheltered beach in a former pottery port converted to a restaurant. The coastal trail skirts wild coves accessible on foot or by swim.

Serifos Megalo Livadi

Top hotels

9.9 Galazio Suites, Serifos
9.4 Klados Studios & Apartments

Megalo Livadi is a village out of time in southwest Serifos, 30 minutes of curves from the port. This is where the island's grand industrial history played out—until 1963, Serifos's iron mines (exploited since antiquity, reactivated at large scale from 1880 by a Franco-German company) loaded their ore from this quay onto cargo ships bound for Europe. The rusted metal loading bridge still stands, cumbersome and poetic. In 1916, a miners' strike led to a bloody confrontation with gendarmes—4 miners killed. It's considered the first bloody labor strike in modern Greek history. A memorial on the quay commemorates this episode.

→

Sunset from anchorage or the surrounding hills—Megalo Livadi bay is well sheltered and faces west. A small waterfront taverna serves good fresh fish.

Kolona is one of the Cyclades' great surprises. A slender sand tombolo—a natural tongue barely a few meters wide—connects the main island to the islet of Agios Loukas, creating two back-to-back beaches with different water temperatures: the right one is slightly warmer because hot springs surface nearby. It's rare to find such a formation in the Mediterranean. Access via dirt track (prefer the water taxi from Merichas, 15 min)—no shade on the beach, bring sun protection.

Dryopida, Kythnos's second village, deserves a 20-minute detour from Kolona. Its red-tiled lanes break from the usual Cycladic white—this is where the island's characteristic tiles have been made for centuries. Katafyki cave, beneath the village, sheltered residents during World War II. It's open in season.

→

Swimming between two seas: swim on one side then the other of the tombolo to feel the temperature difference. Snorkeling possible around the islet's rocks.

Despotiko is an uninhabited islet 700 meters southwest of Antiparos, and it's one of recent decades' archaeological revelations in the Cyclades. Systematic excavations led since 2001 by archaeologist Yannos Kouragios have unearthed a sanctuary of Apollo dating from the 8th century BC—20 buildings, over 70 fragments of archaic marble kouroi, inscribed pottery shards bearing Apollo's name. Like Delos, the island was a pilgrimage site attracting visitors from across the Aegean. The temple's Doric columns are under restoration. Access only by sea from Antiparos.

→

Swimming and snorkeling around Despotiko: the crystalline, lightly visited waters let you observe preserved marine life. Anchorage on sandy bottom in the small Livadi bay.
