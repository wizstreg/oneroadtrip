The Alcazaba of Almería towers over the city from its hilltop perch. Built in 955 by Caliph Abd al-Rahman III, this citadel—Spain's largest after the Alhambra—unfolds across three walled enclosures: the gardens of the first, the five-nave caliphal cistern of the second, and the Christian castle erected by the Catholic Monarchs in 1489.

Step inside the Cathedral of the Incarnation, a unique fortress-temple built after the 1522 earthquake. Its thick walls, battlements, and arrow slits bear witness to attacks by Barbary pirates. Inside, the neoclassical cloister and ambulatory chapels deserve close attention.

The Civil War Shelters burrow 9 meters beneath Paseo de Almería. These 4.5-kilometer underground galleries, designed by architect Guillermo Langle Rubio in 1937, could shelter 40,000 residents. The reconstructed operating room is particularly striking.

The Cable Inglés, an ore-loading pier built in 1904 in Eiffel-style architecture, juts into the sea from Almadrabillas beach. Declared a Cultural Heritage Site in 1998, it offers beautiful perspectives over the port at sunset.

Make your way to Plaza Vieja, the city's administrative heart. The 19th-century neoclassical town hall borders this shaded square. The Monument to the Martyrs of Liberty at its center commemorates victims of absolutist repression in 1824.

Paseo de Almería connects Puerta de Purchena to the port. This shopping avenue, lined with century-old ficus trees, cuts through downtown and passes above the underground shelters. Café terraces come alive as evening falls.

→

Walk the waterfront promenade of Parque Nicolás Salmerón to the marina. Palm trees line the path, with the illuminated Alcazaba glowing in the backdrop.

→

Head to Mercado Central for a tapas crawl. Almería upholds the tradition of free tapas served with every drink—the bars around the market compete in generosity.

→

Late afternoon swim at El Zapillo beach, Almería's urban beach, with views of the Cable Inglés and the Sierra de Gádor mountains.

Mónsul beach is the natural park's most famous stretch. Enormous black lava tongues frame its golden sand—this is where a scene from Indiana Jones and the Last Crusade was filmed. At the beach's center, a massive wind-sculpted rock serves as a landmark.

Want a kilometer of pristine sand? Los Genoveses beach stretches across a bay bordered by dunes, without a single building in sight. The shallow water slopes gently, perfect for swimming. In summer, vehicle access is limited to a few cars per day.

The Arrecife de las Sirenas (Mermaid Reef) at the cape's tip owes its name to the monk seals sailors once mistook for mermaids. Dark volcanic chimneys emerge from turquoise waters, right at the foot of the Cabo de Gata lighthouse built in 1863.

The village of San José serves as the park's main gateway. Its white houses and small fishing port live by the rhythm of the seasons. Terraces facing the sea serve fresh fish cuisine.

The Cabo de Gata salt flats extend across 400 hectares between the road and the sea. Over 80 bird species frequent these shallow basins, including flamingos visible from observation posts along the site.

Climb up to Mirador de La Amatista for a plunging view over the volcanic cliffs and Pico de los Frailes, the park's highest point. The violet-hued rocks give the viewpoint its name.

→

Coastal hike between Genoveses and Mónsul along the ridges. The trail follows the cliffs and crosses several deserted little coves with colorful geological formations—white, orange, gray.

→

Snorkeling in the clear waters around San José. Posidonia seagrass meadows shelter wrasses, white seabreams, and octopuses among the volcanic rocks.

→

Flamingo and black-winged stilt watching at the salt flats, ideally late in the day when the low light colors the basins pink and gold.

💡 Binoculars recommended

Cala San Pedro must be earned: an hour's walk from Las Negras along a coastal path overlooking the Mediterranean. The ruins of a 15th-century castle guard the entrance to this isolated cove, a former pirate hideout. A freshwater spring—the only one in the park—flows at the beach's edge.

Las Negras takes its name from Cerro Negro, the dark volcanic mass that dominates the village. About 350 people live here year-round, including many foreigners drawn by the tranquility. Fishing boats still beach on the black pebble shore each evening.

Take the road to Rodalquilar, a former mining village reinvented. The ruins of the gold mines, worked until 1966, can be freely visited. The El Albardinal Botanical Garden showcases the park's semi-arid flora.

The Playazo de Rodalquilar offers a striking contrast with the enclosed coves: 400 meters of golden sand bordered by the Playazo tower, an 18th-century fortification. The water here is calm and shallow.

From the trail above Cerro Negro, viewpoints offer spectacular vistas of black cliffs plunging into the sea and, in the distance, the silhouette of Isleta del Moro.

→

Hike Las Negras to Cala San Pedro via the coastal trail. The route crosses semi-arid landscape punctuated by red and yellow cliffs before diving toward the turquoise cove.

💡 No shade on the route, bring water and hat
→

Kayak along the volcanic cliffs from Las Negras. Rental shops offer guided outings that follow caves and rock formations inaccessible by land.

→

Snorkeling in the coves around Las Negras. The volcanic rocky seabeds create varied habitats for Mediterranean underwater fauna.

→

Evening drinks facing the sea on the Las Negras promenade. The village's bohemian vibe carries through to the small waterfront bars.

Mojácar Pueblo perches at 170 meters altitude at the end of the Sierra Cabrera. Its maze of whitewashed alleys preserves the village's Al-Andalus structure. On the facades, the Indalo—a Neolithic cave figure from the Cueva de los Letreros—is painted on doors to ward off the evil eye.

The Mirador del Castillo, built on the ruins of a 16th-century castle, offers 360-degree views: the sea, the Río Aguas valley, and the Sierra Cabrera mountains. Come at sunset for the best light.

The Fuente Mora is where, according to tradition, Mojácar's last Moorish governor negotiated peaceful surrender to the Catholic Monarchs in 1488. Its twelve water jets flow beneath a commemorative plaque.

The Church of Santa María, built around 1560 on the site of a former mosque, retains a fortress-like appearance. At its feet, the arcade-bordered Plaza del Parterre serves as a terrace with valley views.

Pass through the Puerta de la Ciudad, a 16th-century entrance gate adorned with Mojácar's emblem: a double-headed eagle. Just inside, the Casa del Torreón served as a customs house where visitors paid to enter the village.

Descend to Mojácar Playa for its 17 km of coastline. Among the beaches, Marina de la Torre and El Descargador fly the blue flag. Fine sand and calm waters make them family-friendly beaches.

→

Get lost in the flower-filled alleys of Mojácar Pueblo. Purple bougainvillea spills from white balconies, and every corner reveals a new sea view.

→

Olive oil and roasted almond tasting in the village's grocery shops. Almería province produces renowned extra virgin olive oil from Picual and Arbequina olives.

→

Grilled fish dinner on the Mojácar Playa waterfront. The espeto—sardines grilled on wood-fire skewers—is a local specialty.

The Tabernas Desert stretches across 280 km², Europe's only true desert with less than 250mm of annual rainfall. The ravines, canyons, and badlands in ochre hues recall American West landscapes—which attracted filmmakers from the 1950s onward.

Fort Bravo preserves Europe's finest western film sets, built in the 1960s for Sergio Leone's films. The wooden Far West town, Mexican village, and military fort still serve as active filming locations. Stunt and shootout shows are performed on weekends.

Oasys MiniHollywood, on the filming locations of A Fistful of Dollars and The Good, the Bad and the Ugly, has been transformed into a theme park. The reconstructed western town, 800-animal wildlife reserve, and cactus garden round out the visit.

The Castillo de Tabernas, an 11th-century Arab fortress, dominates the village from its hill. Though in ruins but undergoing restoration, it offers panoramic views over the desert and the Río Andarax valley.

The Plataforma Solar de Almería, a solar thermal energy research center, takes advantage of the desert's 3,000 hours of annual sunshine. Its giant parabolic mirrors are visible from the road.

→

Hike through the desert badlands on the GR-140 trail. From Oasys, a 9km route crosses plateaus, canyons, and ravines among eroded sandstone and clay formations.

💡 No shade, bring minimum 2L of water
→

Photography walk through the desert landscapes. The golden hours (sunrise and sunset) offer raking light that sculpts the relief and brings out the colors in geological strata.

→

Stargazing in the Tabernas Desert. Low light pollution and almost permanently clear skies make it one of Andalusia's best astronomical sites.

💡 Warm blanket recommended, desert nights are cool
