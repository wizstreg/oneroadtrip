Ciociaria Circuit: Abbeys and Medieval Hilltop Villages

Cyclopean walls raised before Rome, crypts ablaze with Byzantine frescoes, Cistercian abbeys, and waterfalls cascading through town centers: Ciociaria is an open-air museum that few travelers have yet discovered.

Step into Ciociaria through Anagni, the city of four popes, with its 540-square-meter frescoed medieval crypt. Climb next to the cyclopean acropolis of Alatri, built without mortar in the 7th century BC. Unwind at Fiuggi, whose springs cured Pope Boniface VIII of kidney stones. Explore the Charterhouse of Trisulti with its living museum of monastic pharmacy, then complete the circuit at Montecassino, where Saint Benedict laid the foundations of monastic Europe in 529.

Anagni

Anagni Cathedral was built between 1072 and 1104 by Bishop Pietro da Salerno and consecrated in 1105 by Pope Paschal II. This Romanesque-Gothic marvel stretches 80 meters and features a stunning Cosmatesque floor installed in 1231, along with a ciborium sculpted by Vassalletto in 1267. Anagni was the birthplace of four popes, including Boniface VIII (born c. 1235). Within these walls, Alexander III excommunicated Frederick Barbarossa on March 24, 1160, and Clare of Assisi was canonized in 1255.

Descend into the Crypt of San Magno, built simultaneously with the cathedral (1072-1104) and frescoed between 1237 and 1255 across 540 square meters—often called the Sistine Chapel of the Middle Ages. The transition from Byzantine to Romanesque style unfolds panel by panel. Lighting is switched off every half-hour to protect the pigments, with only twenty minutes of illumination per cycle: time your visit accordingly.

The Palace of Boniface VIII, built in the early 13th century by the Conti family in Gothic style, houses a museum with 13th-century frescoes. This palace was the stage for the infamous 1303 "Slap of Anagni," when Sciarra Colonna, acting on behalf of France's King Philip IV, struck Pope Boniface VIII to humiliate papal authority before royal power.

The Palazzo della Ragione, built around 1160 by Jacopo da Iseo, served as the seat of medieval civic assemblies. Its facade overlooking the main square stands as one of the best-preserved examples of Romanesque civil architecture in the region.

→

Wander through Anagni's historic center from the Porta degli Angeli to Piazza Innocenzo III: cobbled lanes reveal 12th and 13th-century cardinal palaces, medieval fountains, and chiseled limestone doorways that remind you at every turn of the era when this town rivaled Rome itself.

Piglio is the heartland of Cesanese del Piglio, Lazio's first and only red wine DOCG (awarded in 2008, after DOC status dating to 1973). The indigenous Cesanese d'Affile grape has been cultivated on the slopes of the Ernici Mountains since antiquity: Emperor Nerva built a palace at Piglio to secure his wine supply, and Popes Innocent III and Boniface VIII were documented enthusiasts. The vineyards cover approximately 15,300 hectares across Piglio, Serrone, Acuto, Anagni, and Paliano.

Serrone, perched on the slopes of Monte Scalambra just a few kilometers from Piglio, offers a sweeping panorama from its belvedere over the Sacco Valley and the ridges of the Ernici. The village preserves its tight medieval layout with lanes of local limestone and several 17th-century palaces.

→

Visit a DOCG-zone winery to discover the three expressions of Cesanese del Piglio: the base version (minimum 12°, red and black fruit aromas, silky tannins), the Superiore (minimum 13°), and the Riserva (minimum 14°, aged at least 20 months). This ruby-hued grape with violet reflections carries a slightly bitter finish—a distinctive character that local winemakers proudly claim as their signature.

→

Hike through the vineyards of the Ernici Mountains on marked trails connecting Piglio to Serrone via terraced vineyards: in autumn, harvest activity is still visible in some parcels, and cellars welcome drop-in visitors without appointments.

Fiuggi, renamed by royal decree in 1911 (previously called Anticoli di Campagna), is famous for two thermal springs: the Boniface VIII Spring and the Anticolana Spring. Pontifical archives preserve over 187 payment orders signed by Boniface VIII between 1299 and 1302 to have Fiuggi water transported to Rome—he suffered from kidney stones. Michelangelo, in a 1549 letter, wrote of this water: it dissolves stone. The current thermal zone covers 94,000 square meters, and the first facility opened in 1911.

The historic center of Fiuggi Città, perched at 747 meters elevation, preserves its medieval fabric with sloping lanes, stone arches, and Piazza Spaccasassi—whose name, meaning "stone-breaker," is a direct reference to the diuretic properties of the local water.

→

Stroll through the thermal park of the Boniface VIII Spring to drink the water directly from the source: very lightly mineralized (dry residue of 123 mg/l), it has been renowned since the Middle Ages for facilitating the elimination of urinary stones. The shaded Belle Époque walkways wind past marble fountains and early 20th-century spa pavilions.

→

Lake Canterno, about 6 km from Fiuggi, is the only natural lake in southern Lazio not fed by a river but by underground springs. Its wooded shores are traced by an easy 7-km trail through a protected wetland populated by grey herons and mallards.

The Castle of Fumone—officially Castello Longhi-De Paolis—was built in the 9th-10th centuries and served as a papal fortress for over five hundred years. Pope Celestine V, who abdicated on December 13, 1294 after just five months in office (the episode for which Dante cites him in his Inferno as "he who made the great refusal"), was imprisoned here by his successor Boniface VIII. He died on May 19, 1296 after ten months of captivity. Benedict XVI laid his pallium on Celestine's tomb in 2009 during a pilgrimage. In 1584, Sixtus V entrusted the castle to the Longhi family to preserve it as a memorial site.

The hanging garden, created by the Longhi family in the 17th century by uniting the walkways, moat, and four interior spaces of the towers, is presented as one of the highest private gardens in Europe. From its terraces at 800 meters altitude, the view sweeps across all of Ciociaria, from the Sacco Valley to the Lepini Mountains.

→

Explore Fumone's medieval borgo: the village houses have gradually merged with the castle walls, making it difficult to distinguish between town and fortress. Stepped lanes lead to viewpoints over the surrounding valleys and pass 17th-century votive niches embedded in the rock.

Alatri

Climb to the acropolis of Alatri, built in the 7th century BC by the Italic Hernici people. The outer wall, stretching over 2 km and nearly intact, is formed of polygonal blocks assembled without mortar, some exceeding three tons. It covers approximately 25 hectares and is considered by archaeologists as the largest pre-Roman acropolis in Italy after Athens. Two monumental gateways survive: the Porta Maggiore, whose lintel is a single monolithic block, and a lower pedestrian gate.

The Church of Santa Maria Maggiore was built in the 5th century on the ruins of a temple of Venus, then reconstructed with a 14th-century facade and bell tower dated 1394. Inside: a 13th-century carved wooden group depicting the "Virgin of Constantinople" and a triptych of the Redeemer attributed to Antonio da Alatri (14th-15th century).

Saint Paul's Cathedral, whose origins date to the 10th century, occupies the very esplanade of the acropolis. Its position is exceptional: it stands on the site of the ancient main temple of the Hernician city, within the cyclopean walls.

The Municipal Archaeological Museum, installed in the Palazzo Gottifredo (a Roman-era building), displays finds from excavations conducted on Alatri's territory since the 19th century: Hernician ceramics, coins, funerary stelae, and architectural fragments from the acropolis.

→

Walk the outer perimeter of the cyclopean walls following the trail that circles the hill: the view over the valley of the Ernici Mountains extends to the ridges of southern Lazio, and you can clearly perceive the defensive logic of the Hernician builders who selected their stones based on natural shape, without any cutting.

The Charterhouse of Trisulti, at 825 meters altitude in the forest of the Ernici Mountains, 6 km northeast of Collepardo, was first founded in 996 by Saint Dominic of Sora as a Benedictine abbey. The current construction dates to 1204: Pope Innocent III ordered its reconstruction and entrusted it to the Carthusians. The Church of San Bartolomeo was consecrated in 1211. A national monument since 1873, the monastery passed to the Cistercians of Casamari in 1947, due to insufficient Carthusian vocations.

The monastic pharmacy (spezieria) is the jewel of the site: furnished in the 18th century, it preserves its original cabinetry, labeled jars, alembics, and frescoes painted by Filippo Balbi in the 19th century. The adjoining herb garden supplied the medicinal plants used by the monks. Today it's a living museum that reveals the entire process of monastic remedy-making.

The monastery library preserves several medieval manuscripts and rare books. It's accessible during guided tours and illustrates the central role played by the Carthusians in copying and transmitting texts during the Middle Ages.

The Caves of Collepardo (Grotte dei Bambocci) are a karst network carved into the limestone of the Ernici about 1 km from the village. The main cavity measures 70 meters deep and 100 meters in diameter; stalactite formations have taken on shapes evoking human silhouettes, hence the popular nickname "bambocci" (dolls).

→

Hike through the Ernici state forest around Trisulti: marked trails traverse ancient beech and chestnut groves between 700 and 1000 meters altitude. Flora is particularly dense in spring (anemones, wild orchids), and views over the Ernici ridges open at clearings.

The Abbey of Casamari, 10 km east of Veroli, was founded in 1053 on the site of Cereatae Marianae, a Roman municipium dedicated to the goddess Ceres and birthplace of Caius Marius—the Roman general who was consul seven times. Originally Benedictine, it passed to the Cistercians in 1144 after Bernard of Clairvaux advocated for this affiliation during the schism of Anacletus II. Reconstruction in Burgundian Cistercian Gothic was carried out between 1203 and 1217. A national monument since 1874.

The cloister of Casamari is a square of galleries with pointed arches typical of Cistercian architecture: alabaster windows diffuse golden light, sober capitals without figurative decoration (Cistercian rule), and a central fountain in the courtyard. The abbey church with three naves reproduces identically the plan of Fossanova Abbey—the only other preserved example in Italy.

On May 13, 1799, French soldiers pillaged the abbey and killed six monks, known since their beatification on April 17, 2021 as the Martyrs of Casamari. Napoleon definitively closed the monastery in 1811; it only reopened in 1929 and today houses the headquarters of the Congregation of Casamari, which manages a dozen abbeys and, since 1930, a mission in Ethiopia.

Veroli's historic center preserves several witnesses to Roman antiquity: polygonal walls supporting the acropolis, remains of the ancient forum beneath the cathedral, and Latin inscriptions reused in medieval palace facades.

→

The monastic pharmacy of Casamari and its liqueur laboratory are open for visits. The monks have distilled several preparations from Ernici Mountain herbs for generations; products are sold at the abbey shop.

Castro dei Volsci—whose name derives from the Latin castrum (fortified camp) and the Volscian people who settled here between the 5th and 6th centuries BC—perches at 385 meters on a spur of the Ausoni Mountains. Its medieval historic center, certified as one of I Borghi più Belli d'Italia and awarded the Touring Club's Bandiera Arancione, is organized around the Via Civita: a ring road where the houses facing the valley themselves serve as ramparts. Four gates allow entry to the enclosure; the finest, the Porta della Valle, is a Romanesque pointed arch opening directly onto the Church of Santa Maria.

The Church of San Nicola was founded between 542 and 552 by monks from Montecassino around a simple Benedictine oratory. It preserves 12th-century Romanesque frescoes in distinctly Byzantine style, depicting scenes from the Old and New Testaments. The medieval village gradually developed around this church. Castro subsequently became a fief of the Colonna, Borgia, and then Carafa families.

Scattered across doorways, steps, and walls throughout the borgo, symbols carved in stone testify to the passage of the Knights Templar: the Golgotha (cross on three steps), the Triple Enclosure, and the Flower of Life. Their presence at Castro dei Volsci is explained by the village's strategic position on the route between Rome and the Kingdom of Naples, at the ancient border between the Papal State and the Bourbons.

The Monument to the Mamma Ciociara, inaugurated June 3, 1964 on the village's summit belvedere, is carved in white marble and commemorates the violence suffered by the region's women during World War II—episodes that Alberto Moravia fictionalized in La Ciociara (Two Women) and that Vittorio De Sica brought to the screen. On clear days, the panorama from this viewpoint embraces Montecassino, the Lepini Mountains, and the Ernici.

The Civic Archaeological Museum of Castro dei Volsci assembles finds from the archaeological zone of Santa Maria del Piano: remains of a 1st-century BC Roman villa, an imperial villa, an early Christian basilica, and a necropolis from the 6th-7th centuries. The centerpiece is a 3rd-century marble sarcophagus fragment.

→

Walk to the Pastena Caves, 11 km from Castro dei Volsci in the Regional Natural Park of the Ausoni Mountains: an underground network of chambers and galleries carved by the Fosso Mastro torrent, with stalagmites, crystalline lakes, and the spectacular Blue Lake Hall.

The Cascata Grande of Isola del Liri plunges 27 meters right in the town's historic center—a phenomenon unique in Italy. The waterfall is produced by the bifurcation of the Liri River, which splits into two arms around a natural island: the left arm forms the Cascata Grande, while the right feeds the Cascata del Valcatoio (or Gualcatojo), converted in the 19th century into hydroelectric power. A painting of the waterfall by Isabelle Bidault is now preserved in the Louvre; Philipp Hackert also depicted it.

The Boncompagni-Viscogliosi Castle is documented from the 12th century and has served as a noble residence since the year 1000. It sits precisely at the bifurcation of the Liri, atop both waterfalls. Its medieval tower and Renaissance halls are visible from the riverbanks.

The Museum of Paper and Telecommunications Civilization, housed in the former Béranger-Coste paper mill, traces the industrial history of Isola del Liri: in the 19th century, the town was one of Europe's largest paper manufacturing centers. In the early 20th century, it became the first city in the world to produce and distribute hydroelectric power for both public and private use.

→

Walk down the banks of the Liri on both sides of the island to see both waterfalls from water level: the Cascata Grande from the bridge on Via Argine, and the Cascata del Valcatoio from the trail on the right bank. Morning light on the main falls is particularly photogenic between 9 and 11 a.m.

The acropolis of Civitavecchia, at 627 meters altitude above Arpino, is the original core of the Volscian then Samnite city, dating to the 7th-6th centuries BC. Its mortarless polygonal walls extend approximately 3 km. It contains the only known pre-Roman pointed-arch gate in Europe: an ogival arch cut from cyclopean blocks that existed before this type of arch was theorized by medieval Gothic architects. The 1581 land registry still designated it as Civitas Ciceroniana.

Arpino was the birthplace of three great men of Roman antiquity: Cicero (born January 3, 106 BC), Caius Marius (born 156 BC, seven-time consul), and Marcus Vipsanius Agrippa (born 63 BC, architect of the Pantheon of Agrippa in Rome). Plaques and statues recall these illustrious natives throughout the historic center.

Arpino's historic center features cyclopean walls visible even in the lower town, integrated into the foundations of medieval houses. The lanes of the upper section (Civitavecchia) are partially deserted and preserve an atmosphere of partial abandonment since the 18th century.

→

Each year since 1980, the Certamen Ciceronianum Arpinas brings together high school students from around the world for an international Latin translation competition on texts by Cicero. If you're present in late April, the awards ceremony at the teatro comunale is open to the public.

Atina

9.6 Fontana Vecchia
9.5 Villa Visocchi Bed and Breakfast

Atina, holder of the Touring Club's Bandiera Arancione, was founded according to legend by the god Saturn—as were Anagni, Alatri, Arpino, and Ferentino. Cited by Virgil in the Aeneid among the cities allied with Turnus, it was first Volscian then Samnite (Pentri tribe) before being conquered by Rome during the Samnite Wars. Cicero wrote of it: "Atina, mother of many illustrious men, to such an extent that no city of Italy can be said to be richer."

Atina's polygonal walls form a total circuit of over 8,000 meters distributed in two distinct systems: the outer enclosure running along the heights of Monte Morrone, and the inner enclosure defending the hill of Santo Stefano. These works date to the 4th-3rd centuries BC and constitute one of the longest pre-Roman fortification perimeters in southern Lazio.

The Palazzo Cantelmo (ducal palace), built after the 1349 earthquake, features a two-tower facade and Gothic mullioned windows. It illustrates the architectural style of the Angevin lords who governed the region after the destruction caused by the earthquake.

The Giuseppe Visocchi Municipal Archaeological Museum assembles discoveries made on Atina's territory since the 19th century: a Roman funerary lion, elements of a pre-Roman necropolis discovered in 2002, a Roman mosaic uncovered in 1946, and scale models of war machines used by Roman and Samnite armies. Atina has been an episcopal seat since 800, attested by a diploma of Charlemagne.

→

Follow the trail along the best-preserved sections of Atina's polygonal enclosure: blocks of local limestone, some exceeding two cubic meters in volume, interlock without mortar to heights reaching 4 to 5 meters in places. The trail requires no special equipment and offers views over the Comino Valley.

Picinisco, certified as one of I Borghi più Belli d'Italia, occupies a ridge at 850 meters altitude in the Comino Valley, at the heart of the Abruzzo, Lazio and Molise National Park. Its name first appears in 1017 in a donation to the Abbot of Montecassino. The castle has four towers, the oldest—la Torretta—dating to the 11th century; a larger tower was built in the 15th century, incorporating it. The village is crossed by five historic gates: Porta Saracina, Porta Codarda, Porta Rione, Porta della Prece, and Porta della Piazza.

The Church of San Lorenzo was built in 1305 and expanded several times. Its bell tower, dated to the 11th century, originally served as a military watchtower before being integrated into the religious building.

The writer D.H. Lawrence stayed in Picinisco in 1919 and completed his novel The Lost Girl there. The house where he lodged is now an agriturismo. Additionally, some 60% of Italians in Scotland originate from Picinisco: a massive exodus at the end of the 19th century and after World War II populated Scottish cities with families from this single village.

→

Hike in the Abruzzo, Lazio and Molise National Park starting from Picinisco: marked trails climb toward the high-altitude pastures of Monte Meta (2241 m), where wolves and Apennine chamois are regularly observed. The park is one of Italy's oldest (founded in 1923) and one of the last refuges of the Apennine brown bear.

→

Taste Pecorino di Picinisco, a sheep's milk cheese recognized by local producers as a high-altitude specialty: the sheep graze on aromatic Apennine herbs between May and October, giving the cheese a slightly herbaceous flavor and a natural ocher-colored rind.

The Abbey of Montecassino was founded in 529 by Saint Benedict of Nursia on the hill overlooking ancient Casinum, on the site of a temple of Apollo. Here Benedict wrote his Regula Monachorum around 540, adopted by virtually all European monasteries from the 9th century onward and making Montecassino the cradle of the Benedictine order. The abbey was destroyed four times: by the Lombards in 577, by the Saracens in 844, by an earthquake in 1349, and by Allied bombing on February 15, 1944 (239 bombers, 453 tons of bombs, the building entirely razed, hundreds of civilian refugees killed).

Reconstruction, carried out under the program "Dove era, come era" (Where it was, as it was) by Abbot Ildefonso Rea with Italian state funding, took ten years. Paul VI reconsecrated the abbey in 1964. Among the rare pre-16th-century pieces that survived or were reconstituted: the 11th-century bronze portal. In December 1943, German Colonel Julius Schlegel organized the evacuation of archives and artworks to Rome, preserving them from the bombs.

The abbey library preserves more than 100,000 volumes and approximately 1,200 manuscripts, the oldest dating to the 6th century. It is one of the most important monastic libraries in Europe still in active operation.

→

The Polish military cemetery, below the abbey, holds the graves of soldiers from General Anders' II Polish Corps who fell during the Battle of Monte Cassino (January-May 1944). The capture of the hill by the Poles on May 18, 1944 was one of the deadliest operations of the Italian campaign. On each grave, the same inscription in Polish: "We Polish soldiers gave our souls to God, our bodies to Italian soil, our hearts to Poland."
