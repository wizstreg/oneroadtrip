Italy Road Trip from Nice: Riviera & Alps in 7 Days

A bridge painted by Monet, a casino opened in 1905, Nobel's last will, ski slopes with views to Corsica. Seven days between the Ligurian Riviera and Piedmont Alps.

Depart Nice for Dolceacqua, a medieval village immortalized by Monet in 1884, where underground alleys descend beneath the humpback bridge over the Nervia. Continue up the coast to Sanremo—its pine-cone-shaped fortified quarter, its Art Nouveau casino from 1905, the villa where Alfred Nobel drafted his will. Then Imperia doubled: Porto Maurizio on its promontory of oil and stone, Oneglia with its olive museum in a Liberty palazzo. Next Cuneo beneath its kilometers of neoclassical porticos, before finishing at Limone Piemonte, an Alpine resort where skiers see the sea from the summits.

Key highlights (5)
  • Dolceacqua: the Ponte Vecchio and medieval caruggi painted by Monet
  • Sanremo: the fortified Pigna, the Art Nouveau casino, and Villa Nobel
  • Imperia: the Parasio promontory and Oneglia's olive museum
  • Cuneo: neoclassical porticos and the Resistance quarter
  • Limone Piemonte: the Riserva Bianca and Mediterranean views from the peaks

Dolceacqua

Cross the Ponte Vecchio to enter the Terra quarter: this single-arch humpback bridge spanning 33 meters was built in the 15th century atop the ruins of an earlier structure. In 1884, Claude Monet was staying in Bordighera when he painted four canvases of it; in his letters, he described it as 'a jewel of lightness.' From the Ponte Nuovo a few meters downstream, the framing shows the bridge with the castle rising behind—exactly as Monet saw it.

Explore the Terra quarter through the caruggi—covered passages so narrow and dark that some require electric lighting even at midday. The neighborhood wraps in concentric rings around the castle, climbing six stories where space ran short. The Scasasse alley, connecting the bridge to the parish square, was once used by locals to move safely during conflicts. Emerge onto Piazza Mauro to see the Palazzo Doria, the noble residence where Napoleon Bonaparte stayed in 1794, and the small fountain dedicated to Rossese wine, where water flows red.

Climb up to the Castello dei Doria, built from 1151 by the Counts of Ventimiglia, then purchased in 1270 by Oberto Doria—the Genoese captain who had just crushed the Pisan fleet at the Battle of Meloria. The Doria family controlled the village for six centuries, until the 1887 earthquake severely damaged the castle and forced their departure. Inside, three museum rooms trace the history of the castle, the Doria genealogy, and the connection with Monet, including a reproduction of the painting held at the Musée Marmottan in Paris.

Visit the parish church of Sant'Antonio Abate, built in the 15th century by incorporating an angular tower from the old ramparts as the base of the bell tower. The Baroque interior houses a polyptych by Ludovico Brea (1515), leader of the Ligurian-Niçois painting school, depicting Saint Devota—patron saint of Monaco—with eight figures against a gold background. In the adjacent Oratory of San Sebastiano, a wooden statue of Saint Sebastian carved from fig wood by the Genoese Anton Maria Maragliano (1664-1739) is carried in procession every January 20th.

→

Photo walk through the Borgo, the neighborhood on the right bank of the Nervia, more open and luminous than the Terra: from its lanes, the view of the Ponte Vecchio with the castle rising behind is the most photographed scene in the village. On the facade of a Borgo house, look for the fresco by Barbadirame, a student of Picasso, depicting Admiral Andrea Doria—born of Caracosa, a woman from Dolceacqua, who became the greatest sailor in the Mediterranean in the 16th century.

→

Rossese di Dolceacqua DOC wine tasting: designated in 1972 as the first controlled designation of origin in Liguria, this light, fruity red wine is produced on the hillsides of olive groves and vineyards surrounding the village. Local producers offer tastings in the cellars along Via Roma that connects the square to the castle entrance.

Enter La Pigna through the Porta di Santo Stefano, a Gothic stone arch from the 14th century marking the boundary between the medieval town and the modern city. The quarter takes its name from its spiral form wrapping around the hillside, recalling the scales of a pine cone—a deliberately defensive design conceived after the 9th-century Saracen raids, ensuring that any invader entering the alleys would become lost without being able to advance. The Piazza della Cisterna, with its large underground medieval reservoir, shows how the quarter compensated for the absence of springs by collecting rainwater in a network of cisterns.

Climb to the Sanctuary of the Madonna della Costa, built in 1630 on the site of an earlier chapel, at the summit of the Pigna hill. The church was erected to celebrate Sanremo's liberation from the tutelage of Genoa's Doria family in 1361—an event the city still celebrates today. The Baroque interior houses wooden statues by Maragliano, paintings by Domenico Fiasella, and a 14th-century Madonna and Child. The facade is visible from the sea and has long served as a landmark for navigators.

Visit the Cathedral of San Siro, one of Sanremo's oldest churches, built in the 12th century in Romanesque style with round arches and an ashlar stone nave. It occupies the western edge of La Pigna, steps from the medieval quarter. In 1538, the Manara house at number 21 Via Palma hosted Pope Paul III, en route to Nice where he would sign a ten-year truce between Charles V and Francis I.

Spot the Saracen Tower, built between August and October 1550 in response to repeated attacks by Barbary corsairs. Cylindrical and slender in form, it was connected to the Pigna ramparts and its single vaulted interior room was occupied by guards during alerts. It now stands isolated from the rest of the quarter but remains visible from the base of the hill.

→

Stroll through the lanes of La Pigna from Piazza Santo Stefano to the summit: take the Rivolte San Sebastiano, a covered alley with high, uneven steps, then Via Palma—the former main street of La Pigna where the most illustrious families lived. Push open the door of the Oratory of San Sebastiano, built after the 1502 plague and restored: the 1642 inscription on the entrance colonnade and the 18th-century interior frescoes are well preserved.

→

Evening walk along the Corso Imperatrice, named in honor of Tsarina Maria Alexandrovna who gifted the city with palm trees during a stay on the Riviera. These palms, some of which are the Tsarina's originals, still line this marble promenade adorned with a Spring statue by sculptor Vincenzo Pasquali. Steps away, the Russian Orthodox Church of Saint Basil, inaugurated on December 23, 1913 in the presence of the Tsar's ambassador, served Sanremo's Russian community, which then numbered a thousand people.

Admire the exterior of the Casino di Sanremo, designed by French architect Eugène Ferret and inaugurated on January 12, 1905 under the name Kursaal. Mussolini's 1927 decree officially made it a casino. Sanremo is one of only four Italian cities authorized to have one, alongside Venice, Campione d'Italia, and Saint-Vincent. Its Art Nouveau halls have welcomed figures like King Farouk of Egypt (a regular in the 1950s after his exile), King Gustav of Sweden, and Princess Grace of Monaco. Poet Francesco Pastonchi created the tradition of Literary Mondays there.

Visit the park of Villa Ormond, on Corso Felice Cavallotti. The villa was built in 1889 by Swiss architect Émile Réverdin for Michel Louis Ormond, a wealthy Swiss entrepreneur, after the 1887 earthquake damaged the foundations of the original villa purchased in 1875. The municipality bought the villa and park in 1928. The garden is organized into themed zones: the palm grove, the orangery, the ficus area, and a Japanese garden in homage to Atami, Sanremo's sister city. Empress Elisabeth of Austria and the Princes of Prussia were among the Villa's guests.

Visit Villa Nobel, on the same Corso Felice Cavallotti, 200 meters from Villa Ormond. Alfred Nobel chose Sanremo in the late 19th century for health reasons and to set up a laboratory. The Moorish-style villa dates from 1870. It was here that he drafted the 1895 will in which he bequeathed his entire fortune to the foundation that would create the prize bearing his name. The salons, study, and bedroom are preserved with original furnishings; the exhibition traces the technological evolution of the 19th century and the role of his inventions.

Walk to the Fort of Santa Tecla, near the old port. Built by the Genoese in just eleven months and inaugurated on March 12, 1756, this star-shaped fort was erected to suppress Sanremo's independence aspirations vis-à-vis the Republic of Genoa. It now hosts cultural exhibitions and events.

→

Bike rental and ride on the Pista Ciclabile Riviera dei Fiori, built on the former Genoa-Ventimiglia railway line relocated inland from the coast starting in 1998. The path follows the sea for 32 kilometers between Ospedaletti and San Lorenzo al Mare, passing through converted former stations and coastal tunnels. The first Sanremo-San Lorenzo al Mare section was inaugurated in 2009; the extension to Imperia was completed in 2025. Bike rental points available at Sanremo's former station.

→

Stroll along Corso Matteotti, Sanremo's main shopping street: on the sidewalk slabs, brass plaques engraved with the name of each Italian Song Festival winner since its creation. The Teatro Ariston, which has hosted the festival every year since the 1970s, faces this same street.

Climb to the Parasio, the medieval promontory of Porto Maurizio, via the spiral road or the elevator from sea level. This quarter constitutes the original nucleus of Imperia: for centuries Porto Maurizio was an autonomous port and the Mediterranean's leading center for olive oil production and trade, under the protection of the Republic of Genoa. Only in 1923 did a government decree merge it with Oneglia—its Piedmontese rival separated by 3 kilometers—to form the municipality of Imperia. Inhabitants of the two towns still call each other 'ciantafourche' and 'cacelotti,' nicknames inherited from centuries of rivalry.

Enter the Basilica of San Maurizio, the largest church in Liguria, built between 1781 and 1838 on the site of an earlier chapel. Its neoclassical facade dominates the square at the Parasio's exit. Inside: frescoes, paintings, and 19th-century sculptures. On the adjacent Via Santa Caterina, visit the birthplace of San Leonardo da Porto Maurizio, born in 1676 in the Parasio and canonized in the 19th century: the house converted into a museum preserves his habit, his hair shirt, and the funeral cast of his face.

From the Logge di Santa Chiara, the panoramic loggias of the Convent of the Sisters of Santa Chiara atop the Parasio, the view embraces the Ligurian coast on both sides. Nearby, the Tower of Prarola rises directly from the sea west of the historic center: this cylindrical watchtower was built to monitor Saracen pirate raids, a constant threat on this coast until the 17th century.

→

Walk the Passeggiata degli Innamorati, the path that follows the cliffs at the base of the Parasio and connects Porto Maurizio to Borgo Marina. This promenade suspended between the medieval quarter and the sea offers the best views of the coast, particularly at sunset.

→

Sketching or photography session by Porto Maurizio's harbor, equipped with a long protective pier whose lighthouse provides the best viewpoint over the entire coast. Since 1986, Imperia has periodically hosted the Vele d'Epoca, a gathering of historic sailboats over 20 meters arriving from around the world.

Visit the Museo dell'Olivo Carlo Carli in Oneglia, housed in a Liberty-style palazzo and spread across eighteen rooms. The Carli brothers, olive oil industrialists, assembled a collection tracing olive cultivation techniques from Ancient Greece to the 20th century: Roman amphorae, parts of animal-drawn or hydraulic mills, documents on cosmetic and medicinal uses of oil. A reconstruction of a Roman ship's hold and a 17th-century press stand in the courtyard. The Taggiasca olive, cultivated in these hills since its Benedictine introduction in the 7th century, was the source of the entire region's historic wealth.

Visit the Basilica of San Giovanni Battista in Oneglia, consecrated in 1762 by architect Gaetano Amoretti and elevated to basilica status in 2017. This late Genoese Baroque building houses polychrome marbles, frescoes, and an 1874 three-keyboard organ. For centuries Oneglia was the main port of Piedmont under the Savoys, rival to Porto Maurizio which answered to Genoa—a rivalry so intense that in 1792, a French fleet bombarded Oneglia without touching neutral Porto Maurizio.

Stroll through Villa Grock, commissioned in the 1920s by Adrien Wettach—known as Grock—one of Europe's most famous clowns, born in Switzerland in 1880. The villa mixes Art Nouveau, references to the circus world, and elements recalling Dalí, Picasso, and Gaudí. It now serves as a museum of clowning and an architectural monument.

→

Wander beneath the arcades of Piazza Dante in Oneglia: this lively square is Oneglia's commercial and social heart, surrounded by cafes and boutiques. Wednesday mornings host the local producers' market—Taggiasca olives, olive oil, vegetables, and cured meats from the hinterland.

→

Bike or walk a section of the Riviera dei Fiori cycle path between Imperia and San Lorenzo al Mare: the Imperia section, inaugurated in 2025, connects the two parts of the city and links to the existing route toward Sanremo. This stretch passes converted former stations and follows the sea for nearly the entire route.

Begin at Piazza Galimberti, whose neoclassical design by Benedetto Brunati was approved in 1832 and implemented over fifty years. The square measures over 23,000 m² and is framed by ten identical arcaded palaces with round-arched vaults—a symmetrical arrangement inherited from the Napoleonic alignment plans of 1801, when the demolition of the ramparts finally permitted the city's southward expansion. Originally named Piazza Nizza, then Piazza Vittorio Emanuele II, it has borne since May 21, 1945 the name of Duccio Galimberti, a Cuneo lawyer and Resistance hero killed in 1944, who lived in Palazzo Osasco facing the square.

Walk the entire length of Via Roma: this medieval artery stretching over 800 meters, now pedestrianized, has always been Cuneo's commercial center. The porticos were gradually added to medieval facades to shelter activities under cover—in some buildings, you can still see the joint between the original masonry and the added arches. The 52-meter-high Civic Tower, built in 1317, and the Cathedral of Santa Maria del Bosco, whose dome and interior frescoes date from the 19th century, punctuate the route. From the tower's summit, accessible by elevator, guards would spot enemies and sound the alarm.

Explore Contrada Mondovì, Cuneo's former Jewish quarter: one of Piedmont's oldest ghettos since the 15th century, it now forms a picturesque pedestrian zone with several historic shops. Continue to the Church of San Francesco, built between the 13th and 15th centuries in Gothic style: its marble portal from 1418, crowned by a rosette pediment, is the city's most important medieval testimony. Its cloister houses the Civic Museum.

Visit the Museo Casa Galimberti, housed in the palace where Duccio Galimberti was born, an anti-fascist resistance fighter shot on December 3, 1944 at age 35. The museum includes a gallery of paintings by Piedmontese artists, the family library, and the hero's mementos. The building stands on Piazza Galimberti, at the very spot where the first call to armed resistance in Italy was pronounced on July 26, 1943.

→

Walk through the Parco Fluviale Gesso e Stura, along the banks of the two torrents that define the plateau on which Cuneo was founded in 1198. Cuneo—whose name comes from the Piedmontese 'cùneo,' meaning wedge or point—occupies precisely the point of this natural triangle formed by the confluence of the Gesso and Stura di Demonte, at 534 meters altitude at the foot of the Maritime Alps.

→

Aperitivo beneath the porticos of Piazza Galimberti: the arcades allow you to travel from end to end of the square and Via Roma without ever being exposed to the weather—making Cuneo one of the European cities with the longest continuous covered gallery route. Taste the Cuneesi al rhum, small chocolates filled with meringue and rum, a local pastry specialty present in every shop window.

Stroll through the historic center of Limone Piemonte, a village in the Vermenagna Valley at 1,000 meters altitude on one of the historic routes linking France, Piedmont, and the Ligurian coast—once traveled by convoys transporting salt from the Mediterranean inland. Inhabited since Roman times, Limone was one of the first villages in the Southern Alps to develop mountain tourism. Restaurants, shops, and refuges form a compact village center reachable from the train station within minutes on foot.

Visit the Museo dello Sci Agostino Bottero, opened in 2012, which traces the history of skiing in the Riserva Bianca from the first Alpine troops' training sessions in 1907 on the slopes of Monte Vecchio, to the 2006 Winter Olympics that modernized the resort. In 1937, 'lo slittone'—a large motorized sled—was the first mechanical lift installed in southern Piedmont, allowing riders to climb 700 meters of elevation.

→

Skiing or snowboarding session on the Riserva Bianca slopes (season December to early April): the ski area extends from 1,050 to 2,085 meters altitude over 80 kilometers of slopes divided into 41 runs served by 16 lifts. The difficulty distribution is 25% beginner, 55% intermediate, and 20% red and black runs. From the summits—notably Mont Alpetta at 2,070 meters—on clear days, the Mediterranean and Corsica are visible to the south, the Matterhorn and Monte Rosa to the north. Italian and French national teams have conducted training camps here.

→

Outside ski season, snowshoe or hiking trek on one of the 12 marked trails around Limone and Limonetto, some leading to 19th-century military forts built around the Colle di Tenda—Fort Alto, Fort Pernante, Fort Giaura, and Fort Margherita to the west, Fort Taburda and Fort Pepino to the east. These defensive works of the Kingdom of Sardinia then the Kingdom of Italy controlled the historic pass between Italy and France.

→

Excursion to the Cascate del Saut, waterfalls located in the valley near Limone Piemonte, particularly impressive in winter under snow and in early spring during the melt.
