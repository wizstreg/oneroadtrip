Northeast Brazil Road Trip: Lençóis Maranhenses, Parnaíba Delta, Serra da Ibiapaba & Ceará Coast

Northeast Brazil unveils its best-kept secrets: a sea of dunes studded with turquoise lagoons, a delta where scarlet ibis set the twilight ablaze, a misty serra with ancient caves, and infinite beaches where time stands still.

From the white dunes and crystalline lagoons of Lençóis Maranhenses to the mangroves of Parnaíba Delta, from remote fishing villages on Ceará's far western coast to the cave and cable car at Ubajara atop the Serra da Ibiapaba. This 13-day circuit crosses three Northeast Brazilian states between São Luís's colonial heritage, wild coastlines, and Fortaleza.

Key highlights (6)
  • São Luís: UNESCO historic center with 3,500 azulejo-clad facades
  • Lençóis Maranhenses: 155,000 hectares of white dunes and crystalline lagoons
  • Parnaíba Delta: the Americas' only open-sea delta, 80 mangrove islands
  • Ubajara: limestone cave, cable car, and waterfalls in Serra da Ibiapaba
  • Viçosa do Ceará: 1695 Jesuit church, Ceará's oldest
  • Moitas/Icaraizinho: Túnel do Amor, Lençóis Cearenses, and wild coast

Sao Luis

São Luís's Centro Histórico preserves over 3,500 buildings from the 17th, 18th, and 19th centuries—the largest collection of Portuguese colonial architecture in Latin America. More than 300 different azulejo tile patterns dress the facades, imported from Portugal starting in the 1840s to protect walls from tropical humidity. The district has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1997.

Walk up Rua Portugal, where you'll find the city's highest concentration of azulejo-clad facades. Stone pavement leads to Beco Catarina Mina, a narrow alley flanked by sobrados with wrought-iron balconies. A museum dedicated to azulejo tiles occupies the ground floor of a restored townhouse.

The Palácio dos Leões, seat of the Maranhão state government, has overlooked the Bay of São Marcos since the 17th century. Behind its neoclassical facade lie reception rooms furnished with period pieces and paintings. The neighboring Igreja da Sé cathedral preserves gilded altars and a restored pipe organ.

The Teatro Arthur Azevedo, inaugurated in the 19th century, is one of Brazil's oldest theaters still in operation. Its Italian-style hall seats 800 beneath a painted ceiling. Guided tours reveal the original stage machinery still in working condition.

→

Stroll across the Praça Grande esplanade at sunset: the pastel facades turn golden while viewpoints offer sweeping vistas over the Bay of São Marcos and the historic center's terracotta rooftops.

→

São Luís is Brazil's reggae capital. Come evening, bars in the historic quarter pulse with reggae danced in pairs—a unique local tradition. Bumba-meu-boi, a folkloric spectacle mixing dance, music, and embroidered costumes, brings the squares to life during festival season.

→

Sample Maranhão cuisine in the alleyways of Reviver: cuxá (a sauce made with local herbs served over rice), grilled fish with tucupi, and juçara juice—the Amazonian cousin of açaí—sold by street vendors.

Board a fast lancha in Barreirinhas for a downriver journey along the Rio Preguiças. The river winds between sandy banks and buriti palm groves before reaching the Pequenos Lençóis of Vassouras, the first dunes accessible by water.

The Farol de Mandacaru lighthouse rises 35 meters high on a sandy promontory. Climb its 160 steps: from the top, your gaze sweeps across the Rio Preguiças delta, the Atlantic Ocean, and the white expanse of Lençóis Maranhenses stretching to the horizon.

Caburé occupies a spectacular tongue of sand between the Preguiças River and the ocean. On one side, the calm, warm river waters; on the other, Atlantic breakers. Hammocks strung between palm trees invite a post-lunch siesta.

Atins, a village of 2,000 nestled among dunes, river, and ocean, retains its sandy lanes and low fishermen's houses. The atmosphere recalls what Jericoacoara was like twenty years ago: no paved roads, no crowds—just wind and silence.

→

In late afternoon, head to the banks of the Rio Preguiças to watch guarás (scarlet ibis) flying over the river at twilight, crossing from shore to shore in crimson formations above the mangroves.

→

Try the famous grilled shrimp with molho secreto at Canto de Atins, the signature dish of the Lençóis Maranhenses region. The two restaurants at the point serve fresh catch of the day and carne de sol on terraces facing the dunes.

Lençóis Maranhenses National Park unfolds across 155,000 hectares of white sand dunes as far as the eye can see. During the rainy season (January-June), valleys between dunes fill with turquoise freshwater lagoons, trapped by impermeable rock below. The Ponta do Mangue circuit leads to Lagoa Tropical and Lagoa Esmeralda, among the park's most crystalline pools.

The Canto de Atins circuit crosses an almost lunar landscape: wind-sculpted sand crests, crystal-clear pools where small fish swim, and not a tree in sight. The barefoot walk on warm sand between lagoons takes about two hours.

Atins beach stretches out facing the constant trade winds that make it a renowned kitesurfing spot. Sandbars sculpted by the Rio Preguiças form a natural lagoon sheltered from ocean swells—ideal for beginners.

→

Climb the highest dune accessible from Atins for sunset. The white sand turns pink, then orange, while the lagoons below reflect the sky like mirrors.

💡 Bring water—no shade on the dunes
→

Guided hike through the dunes and lagoons from Atins. A local guide knows the least crowded lagoons and adapts the route to the season's water level.

→

After the hike, relax in a hammock by the beach. Sand bars serve cajá caipirinhas and fresh coconut water facing the Atlantic.

From Caburé, the route follows the beach to Tutóia, the Maranhão gateway to the Parnaíba Delta. This is the only delta in the Americas that opens directly into the open sea: the Parnaíba River splits into five channels forming over 80 islands across 2,700 km².

Navigate the labyrinth of the Delta: narrow igarapés (channels) wind through mangroves whose aerial roots plunge into brackish water. Capuchin monkeys leap from branch to branch while iguanas bask on the banks.

Ilha das Canárias, the delta's largest island, hosts a fishing community and a traditional restaurant where you'll lunch on grilled fish and crab. Nearby dunes, nicknamed Pequenos Lençóis Piauienses, form freshwater lagoons between mounds of sand.

Ilha do Caju, 50 km from Parnaíba, brings together six distinct ecosystems across 100 km²: 11 miles of virgin beaches, four types of mangroves, flooded fields, lagoons, dunes, and forests.

→

At twilight, the revoada dos guarás spectacle is unforgettable. Hundreds of scarlet ibis (Eudocimus ruber) converge on the same mangrove islets for the night. Their crimson plumage—derived from the crustaceans they feed on—literally sets the trees ablaze in the setting sun.

💡 Guides maintain a respectful distance to avoid disturbing the birds
→

Watch crab fishermen at work in the mangrove mud: they locate burrows by touch and extract crustaceans bare-handed, an ancestral technique passed from father to son.

→

Optional nighttime safari in the delta by motorboat: lamps reveal the red eyes of caimans, arboreal snakes, and owls hunting just above the water.

The route from Tutóia crosses the Piauí-Ceará border, passing through Chaval and Barroquinha. The landscape evolves from coastal plains to low cliffs and coconut groves of Ceará's far west.

Praia das Curimãs opens onto a vast bay bordered by dunes and mangroves. Morning brings calm seas; afternoon trade winds whip up powerful waves. Fishermen's colorful jangadas strain at their anchors along the strand.

Curimãs is an authentic fishing community with virtually no tourist infrastructure. Low houses line sandy lanes, nets dry on the beach, and children play football at low tide.

Head to neighboring Praia de Bitupitá, accessible by beach or track: a wide band of fine sand, vegetated dunes, and a few artisanal fishing boats.

→

Sail out with a local fisherman on a jangada for a one- to two-hour sea voyage. These traditional flat-bottomed sailboats glide across turquoise waters, cruising the coast from dune to dune.

💡 Inquire in the village in the morning
→

Explore by 4x4 or on foot along the coastline between Curimãs and Bitupitá: isolated coves among mangroves, lagoons formed at low tide, and ocean panoramas from the dunes.

→

Go tidepooling at low tide on the exposed sandbars. Locals gather shellfish, crabs, and small fish trapped in pools among rocks and mangrove roots.

Spend a morning moving to the village rhythm: Curimãs fishermen depart at dawn and return between 9 and 10 a.m. On the beach, fresh fish is traded right on the sand in a lively, good-natured atmosphere.

Explore the freshwater lagoons that form behind Curimãs dunes at low tide. These natural pools, ringed by restinga vegetation, offer calm swimming sheltered from the wind.

Excursion by 4x4 or buggy to Camocim along the beach (about 40 min). En route, the coast alternates between low red clay cliffs, deserted beaches, and mangrove estuaries where fishing canoes are moored.

In Camocim, Barra dos Remédios forms a tongue of sand between river and sea, reminiscent of Caburé. Jangadas are moored along the bank, and Ilha do Amor, accessible on foot at low tide, offers an isolated sandbar in the middle of the estuary.

→

Kitesurfing session or beginner lessons on Curimãs beach. Consistent afternoon trade winds (15-25 knots) and the flat water between sandbars make this a favorite spot for practitioners.

💡 Schools available in Curimãs and Bitupitá
→

Watch fishermen return and prepare grilled fish on the beach in late afternoon. Lobsters, mackerel, and red mullet are cooked over coconut shell coals, served with farofa and lime.

→

Sunset from the Curimãs dunes: the sky shifts from gold to fiery red as jangada silhouettes cut across the horizon.

The long drive from Curimãs gradually climbs toward the Serra da Ibiapaba. The landscape transitions from arid coastal plains to verdant hills cloaked in high-altitude Atlantic forest. Temperatures drop several degrees as you reach the plateau at around 850 meters elevation.

Ubajara, 324 km from Fortaleza, perches atop the serra. Praça do Senhor da Canoa, the heart of the small town, is shaded by century-old trees and bordered by the Casa da Cultura, which exhibits local lace and straw crafts.

Ubajara's Igreja Matriz, founded in 1934, dominates the main square. Its white facade contrasts with the serra's intense green. From the church steps, views plunge down to the sertão valley below.

Mirante da Ladeira offers a spectacular panorama of the Ibiapaba mountain chain and surrounding forests. On clear days, views stretch to the plains of Piauí to the west.

→

Take your first acclimatization hike on the trails at the entrance to Ubajara National Park. The path crosses dense, humid forest where 100-foot trees grow beneath a canopy of morning mist.

→

Sample serra specialties: rapadura (compacted cane sugar), artisanal cachaça, alfenim (white confection), regional honey, and high-altitude tropical fruits like pupunha, which arrived in the region with Amazonian migrations in the 1970s.

→

In the evening, the serra's coolness (63-72°F) invites a stroll through Ubajara's lanes. Bars serve regional coffee and homemade fruit liqueurs.

The cable car at Ubajara National Park descends 1,800 feet along the cliff face, offering dizzying panoramic views of the serra and sertão. Created in 1959, this is Brazil's smallest national park but holds Ceará's most important speleological heritage.

The Gruta de Ubajara, a limestone cave formed over millennia, extends for 3,670 feet—1,378 of which are open to visitors via illuminated trails. Nine chambers follow one after another: the Pedra do Sino resonates with dripping water, the Sala da Rosa displays flower-shaped formations, and the Sala dos Retratos lines up silhouettes carved by erosion.

The Gameleira viewpoint overlooks the park, offering a plunging view into the canopy. Below, Cachoeira do Cafundó cascades more than 230 feet down, forming a natural pool perfect for swimming.

Rio das Minas, a section of the Ubajara River near the cave, forms a series of natural pools among mossy boulders. The cool, crystal-clear water contrasts with the heat of the sertão visible below.

→

Hike the Ubajara-Araticum trail, the park's classic route connecting the visitor center to the cave entrance through Atlantic forest. The path descends in switchbacks beneath a canopy of foliage, among tree ferns and wild orchids.

→

Swim in the natural pool at the base of Cachoeira do Cafundó. The water stays cool even in the dry season, fed by serra springs.

💡 Wear hiking shoes for the slippery access trail
→

Visit the park's visitor center: permanent exhibition on the cave's geological formation, local fauna (monkeys, toucans, armadillos), and the conservation history since 1959.

The Igreja de Nossa Senhora da Assunção, built by Jesuits starting in 1695, is Ceará's oldest church, designated cultural heritage by IPHAN. Its main altar is original, and 17th-century paintings adorn the walls. Underground tunnels are said to connect the building to old colonial houses in town.

Perched on a rocky promontory, the Igrejinha do Céu (Little Church of Heaven) offers 360° panoramas of the serra and valleys. The climb up a steep path rewards you with one of the finest views in all of Serra da Ibiapaba.

The Solar of the Pinho Pessoa family, a monumental colonial mansion with 165 doors and windows, testifies to the town's 19th-century prosperity. Nearby, the birthplace of the jurist who drafted Brazil's first civil code has been transformed into a memorial and is open for visits.

Viçosa's historic center, the serra's first colonial settlement founded in 1700 as a Jesuit mission, preserves its pastel-fronted sobrados, the Sobrado da Marcela and the Casarão dos Pinhos—witnesses to three centuries of history.

→

The Casa dos Licores is a Viçosa institution: here you'll taste dozens of artisanal liqueurs made from tropical fruits (cajá, graviola, caju, jenipapo), serra herbs, and honey. Production of rapadura (cane sugar loaf) continues in the town's workshops.

💡 Request a guided tasting of seasonal liqueurs
→

Hike to Cachoeira da Pirapora, on the border with Piauí. The trail crosses sugarcane plots and vegetable farms before reaching a waterfall set deep in a forest canyon.

→

Catch the sunset from a mirante on the serra ridge. Your gaze sweeps across the plains of Piauí's sertão stretching infinitely westward, drowned in golden haze.

The descent from Serra da Ibiapaba to the coast takes about four hours. The landscape transforms: high-altitude forest gives way to the dry plains of the sertão, then to coconut groves and wind farms along Ceará's western coast.

Icaraí de Amontada (Icaraizinho), a village of 5,000, occupies a 3.7-mile bay between dunes and a river mouth. Sandy and cobbled streets, low houses without fences, and jangadas pulled up on the beach give it an end-of-the-world feel.

Praia de Moitas, 5 miles from Icaraizinho, is an isolated beach with pale sand and morning calm seas. At low tide, sandbars emerge to form natural pools. The atmosphere is that of a preserved fishing village, virtually without tourist infrastructure.

The Nossa Senhora dos Navegantes chapel, at the heart of Icaraizinho, serves as the village landmark. Around it, a handful of shops and restaurants form a peaceful centrinho, far from the bustle of Fortaleza or Jericoacoara.

→

Take an exploratory walk on Icaraizinho beach at sunset. Constant trade winds fill the sails of kitesurfers while fishermen sort the day's catch on the sand. Wind turbine towers stand in silhouette against the reddening sky.

→

Dine on grilled fish or fresh seafood at one of the centrinho restaurants. Dishes are prepared from the morning catch: grouper, shrimp, octopus, served with farofa and lime.

→

Take your first swim in the natural lagoon that forms at low tide in front of Icaraizinho: warm, shallow water without waves—an ideal stretch to unwind after the long drive.

The Rio Aracatiaçu winds through dunes and mangroves before meeting the ocean at Moitas. From Barra das Moitas, board a small boat heading upriver: the water shifts from ochre to emerald green as you penetrate the mangrove.

The Túnel do Amor is the journey's highlight: mangroves form a canopy over the river. At high tide, the water takes on a translucent turquoise hue and the canoe glides silently through a corridor of green. Crabs scuttle across aerial roots.

Ilha das Ostras, a sandbar in the middle of the river, is an unmissable stop. Mangrove oysters are harvested and shucked on the spot. You'll eat them with your feet in the water, facing the dunes.

The tall dunes of Moitas, sculpted by trade winds, offer panoramas over the river mouth, ocean, and neighboring wind farm turbine towers. Fine sand is dotted with coconut palms whose roots have been exposed by erosion.

→

Buggy excursion from Icaraizinho to Moitas along the beach. The route passes giant wind turbine towers, crosses stretches of deserted beach, and stops at a swing suspended over the Rio Aracatiaçu.

💡 Buggies hold 4 people; book the day before
→

Navigate the Túnel do Amor by boat at high tide. The contrast between the cool shade of the mangrove and the turquoise glow of the water is striking.

→

Sunset from the Duna do Pôr do Sol at Moitas: the sun plunges into the Atlantic while kite wings punctuate the sky with color.

Excursion to Caetanos, a village even more isolated than Icaraizinho, 12 miles to the east. The buggy route crosses a wind farm and passes a century-old cemetery buried by dunes—a surreal image of white crosses amid golden sand.

The Lençóis Cearenses, a miniature version of Lençóis Maranhenses, hide behind the dunes of Caetanos. From May to September, freshwater lagoons fill between mounds of white sand, forming warm pools bordered by low vegetation.

Praia do Jiqui surprises with its coconut palms whose roots have been exposed by coastal erosion and its natural freshwater spring (bica) gushing from the sandy cliff. The contrast between the cool spring water and warm sea is striking.

Lagoa de Flexeiras, 4 miles from Icaraizinho, is a translucent freshwater lake bordered by coconut palms and dunes. Barely three feet deep, the warm, still water makes it an ideal swimming spot for the whole family.

→

Hike or buggy through the Lençóis Cearenses. Between lagoons, the barefoot walk on warm sand recalls the Lençóis Maranhenses experience, with more intimate landscapes and without the crowds.

→

Swim in Lagoa da Cobra, one of the area's largest lagoons, surrounded by wind-sculpted dunes. The clear freshwater is warmed by the sun.

→

Kayak or stand-up paddle on the Rio Aracatiaçu, from the mouth heading upstream. The route crosses zones of silent mangrove, sandbars, and stretches bordered by coconut palms leaning over the water.

The Centro Dragão do Mar Arte e Cultura, in the Praia Iracema district, houses a planetarium, Ceará Museum of Contemporary Art, and Museum of Ceará Culture (featuring jangadas and forró). Open-air performance stages. Fortaleza's vibrant cultural heart.

The Teatro José de Alencar, inaugurated in 1910, is the Northeast's architectural jewel. Art Nouveau facade in wrought iron imported from Scotland, stained glass windows, gardens by Roberto Burle Marx. Guided tours are free.

The Ponte dos Ingleses (1921) extends into the sea from Praia Iracema. Its partially ruined metal structure has become a romantic viewpoint over the ocean, spectacular at sunset.

The Mercado Central houses 600 vendors across 4 floors: labyrinth-point lace, ceramics, hammocks, cashew nuts, nordestino crafts. One of Ceará's largest artisan markets.

→

Praia do Futuro, 4 miles from downtown, lines up large barracas with lounge chairs, crab restaurants, pools, and live music. On Thursdays, the signature dish is crab in molho sauce.

💡 Taxi or bus from downtown
→

The Feirinha Beira Mar sets up every evening at 6 pm along the coastal avenue: 800 stalls of jewelry, lace, clothing, and specialties. Musicians and forró dancers animate the aisles.

→

At dawn, Mucuripe's fish market comes alive by 5 am: jangadas—traditional sailboats—return from night fishing and unload tuna, lobsters, and tropical fish. Stalls grill fish on the spot.

❓ Frequently asked questions

What weather should you expect?

Summer (December-March): Peak tourist season, hottest weather, Carnival season, highest prices, crowded beaches, vibrant atmosphere.

Fall (April-June): Excellent weather, fewer crowds, good prices, ideal for most regions, comfortable temperatures.

Winter (July-September): Dry season in most regions, perfect for Pantanal wildlife, cooler temperatures, great for cultural activities.

Spring (October-November): Good weather returning, moderate crowds, reasonable prices, excellent for outdoor activities.

Regional Variations: Amazon best May-September (dry season), Northeast beaches year-round, South cooler June-August.

How many days should I plan?

We believe you will enjoy this itinerary best by dedicating between 11 and 17 days. The next step will allow you to adjust the duration of your stay.

What to discover in Brazil?

Carnival energy, Amazon rainforest, and pristine beaches create the ultimate South American adventure. Experience vibrant cities, incredible biodiversity, and passionate culture from Rio's beaches to Pantanal wetlands. Samba music, capoeira martial arts, and incredible football culture showcase national spirit. Brazil offers unparalleled natural wonders with infectious cultural energy.
