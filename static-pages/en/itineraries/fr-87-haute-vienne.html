Haute-Vienne Circuit: Limoges, Porcelain & Historic Villages

Kiln fires glow red-hot in porcelain workshops. Stone villages crown rolling hills. Bell towers pierce the sky. Haute-Vienne guards its heritage — fully customizable to your pace.

Limoges and its porcelain heritage invite you on a journey through centuries. Saint-Léonard-de-Noblat keeps watch with its towers and ramparts. Oradour bears the weight of memory. Rochechouart rises from ancient stones. Craftsmanship and remembrance intertwine throughout the region.

Key highlights (5)
  • Limoges: world capital of hand-painted porcelain and champlevé enamels
  • Saint-Léonard: UNESCO-listed collegiate church on the pilgrimage route
  • Oradour: preserved ruins bearing witness to war's darkest hour
  • Rochechouart: France's only asteroid impact crater, crowned by a château-museum
  • Saint-Junien: luxury glove-making tradition spanning nine centuries

The Musée national Adrien Dubouché showcases 5,000 ceramic pieces from a reserve collection of 18,000. The circular route spans antiquity to contemporary porcelain, centered around the world's richest Limoges collection.

Step through the doors of Gare des Bénédictins, a classified Historic Monument. Built between 1924 and 1929, it blends Art Nouveau, Art Deco, and Neoclassical styles. Its campanile rises 61 meters high, while the copper dome crowns a hall bathed in light from Francis Chigot's stained glass depicting Limousin chestnut trees and foliage.

The Four des Casseaux, a classified Historic Monument, is a former porcelain kiln built in 1900. It bears witness to the titanic work of porcelain workers during 19th-century firings and hosts temporary exhibitions of contemporary ceramics.

Walk down to Cathédrale Saint-Étienne, the only monument in Limousin built in pure Gothic style. Construction stretched from 1273 to 1888. Admire the Flamboyant Gothic Saint-Jean portal, the Renaissance rood screen commissioned in 1533, and three sculpted tombs in the ambulatory.

The Jardins de l'Évêché cascade in terraces below the cathedral, offering sweeping views over the Vienne River. The botanical garden assembles medicinal plants and toxic species. The Musée des Beaux-Arts, housed in the former 18th-century episcopal palace, holds a remarkable collection of medieval enamels.

Cross the Pont Saint-Étienne, built around 1210 over the Vienne. Its medieval arches link the Cité quarter to the left bank and offer one of the finest views of the cathedral.

→

Wander through the Quartier de la Boucherie, Limoges' most picturesque neighborhood: half-timbered houses, narrow lanes, and the 15th-century Chapelle Saint-Aurélien, owned by the butchers' guild. The Maison traditionnelle de la Boucherie traces the techniques and tools of this corporation active since the Middle Ages.

💡 On Friday mornings, the neighborhood comes alive around the nearby market halls
→

Beneath Place de la République, the Crypte Saint-Martial preserves vestiges of the Benedictine abbey founded in the 9th century. This medieval scriptorium spread the art of illumination and polyphonic music — the word 'troubadour' was born here, from the 'tropes' composed by monks.

💡 Check opening hours; the crypt periodically closes for archaeological work
→

Head up to Place de la Motte in late afternoon to enjoy the terraces and illuminated half-timbered facades. The atmosphere buzzes with life in the evenings.

Visit a porcelain manufacture for an immersive guided tour. From raw material to finished product, you'll follow every manufacturing stage: modeling, slip casting, glazing, and high-fire kiln firing. Artisans work before your eyes.

The Église Saint-Michel-des-Lions, built between the 14th and 16th centuries, takes its name from two granite lions flanking its portal. It preserves the relics of Saint Martial and Saint Valérie, displayed during the septennial Ostensions.

The Église Saint-Pierre-du-Queyroix features a typically Limousin bell tower and beautiful stained glass. Its late Gothic atmosphere contrasts with the dark granite facade.

Discover the Cour du Temple, an elegant 17th-century Renaissance courtyard tucked between Rue du Temple and Rue du Consulat. Its arcades and terraces form an unexpected jewel in the heart of downtown.

The Pavillon du Verdurier, an octagonal Art Deco building designed by the railway station's architect, is adorned with porcelain and enamel tiles. It regularly hosts exhibitions.

→

The Halles centrales de Limoges are a food lover's rendezvous. Fine-grained Limousin beef, farmhouse cheeses, chestnuts, and clafoutis: taste the spirit of Limousin among the market stalls.

→

Try your hand at modeling or painting on porcelain in a workshop. Beginners welcome — you'll leave with your own creation.

→

Stroll along the Vienne riverbanks in late afternoon for a peaceful walk between medieval bridges. Limoges boasts 52 m² of green space per inhabitant: nature is never far away.

The Collégiale de Saint-Léonard, inscribed on UNESCO's World Heritage List as part of the Routes of Santiago de Compostela, dominates the town from its seven-story bell tower rising 52 meters. Pass through the Limousin portal to discover the barrel-vaulted nave, the octagonal lantern tower, and the vast choir with ambulatory encircled by seven radiating chapels.

At the heart of the collegiate church, Saint Léonard's tomb is covered with chains of 'freed prisoners.' Tradition holds that women touch the saint's bolt to have their prayers answered. Small booties are still placed on the tomb in thanks.

The baptistery rotunda, vaulted with a cupola resting on eight columns, was erected around 1100, during the time of the First Crusade. It illustrates Limousin Romanesque architectural mastery at its finest.

The Moulin du Got, Limousin's last working paper mill, brings to life handmade papermaking and printing techniques from the 15th to 20th centuries. Take home a souvenir made from paper crafted on-site.

The Musée Gay-Lussac, labeled Maison des Illustres, honors the chemist born here in 1778. Personal clothing, laboratory instruments, and publications trace the life and work of this scientist who gave his name to several fundamental laws of gas physics.

→

Roam the lanes of the medieval town between the round tower and square tower. Gothic facades, secret passages, and remnants of fortified gates mark the route. Saint-Léonard is classified among France's 'Plus Beaux Détours.'

→

Walk down to the old 13th-century stone bridge over the Vienne. The small 4 km loop offers views of the viaduct and surrounding countryside — ideal for a post-meal stroll.

→

Taste the massepain de Saint-Léonard, a biscuit made from ground almonds, sugar, and egg white. Legend says this Mediterranean-flavored delicacy was brought back by pilgrims returning from Compostela.

The Centre de la Mémoire d'Oradour, inaugurated in 1999, is the sole entrance to the martyred village ruins. Its permanent exhibition traces, through archival documents and filmed testimonies, the context of World War II and the events of June 10, 1944.

The martyred village preserves intact the ruins of the Limousin town destroyed on June 10, 1944, by the SS Das Reich division. 643 victims — men, women, and children. The cleared streets, reinforced walls, and bullet marks left untouched allow you to measure the massacre's full extent. Classified as a Historic Monument since 1946, the site receives 300,000 visitors annually.

The martyred village church is the most emotionally charged place: here 450 women and children were locked inside and killed. Fire and impact traces are carefully preserved during each restoration.

The memorial crypt, designed in the 1950s, houses in its display cases personal objects of victims found in the rubble — stopped watches, toys, keys — relics of shattered daily life.

→

Take time to walk the esplanade linking the Centre de la Mémoire to the new town, rebuilt after the war. The new Oradour church faces the ruins — two eras, one village.

→

At the communal cemetery, the Tombeau des martyrs gathers the victims' ashes. Ex-votos placed over decades bear witness to enduring remembrance.

The Collégiale de Saint-Junien, one of the largest and oldest Romanesque churches in Limousin, was built from the 11th to 13th centuries in local granite. Its tomb of Saint Junien, carved around 1100 in La Rochefoucauld limestone, bears on three faces the twenty-four elders of the Apocalypse and Christ in a mandorla.

In the nave, look up: the fresco of the twenty-four elders of the Apocalypse, from the 12th century, echoes the tomb's iconography. The Saint-Martial chapel preserves Romanesque paintings showing the display of relics followed by pilgrims seeking healing.

The Chapelle Notre-Dame-du-Pont, in Flamboyant Gothic style, has guarded the Vienne bridge entrance since 1451. Enlarged thanks to the generosity of King Louis XI, who came on pilgrimage in 1463, it remains a place of popular devotion.

The Cité du Cuir, opened in late 2025 on the Vienne riverbank at a former tannery site, traces the complete luxury glove manufacturing cycle. The immersive museum route runs from raw hide to finished product, passing through reconstructed workshops.

The Maison des Consuls, built in the 14th century, testifies to the medieval town's prosperity. A few steps away, the Bourse du Travail on Place Lénine recalls Saint-Junien's working-class social history, a glove-making town since the 11th century.

→

Every seven years, Saint-Junien celebrates the Ostensions septennales, inscribed on UNESCO's list: 1,500 costumed participants parade through town in procession around the relics. Four Swiss guards watch over the tomb year-round — a unique feature in Limousin.

→

Follow the Glane riverbanks to the Corot site, classified as a Natural Area of Ecological Interest. The 2 km trail crosses one of the Vienne's largest islands, where painter Corot once set up his easel.

→

Visit a glove-making workshop to observe artisan craftsmanship, from cutting leather to assembling gloves. Saint-Junien has supplied the greatest fashion houses since the 19th century.

The Château de Rochechouart rises on a rocky spur formed by an asteroid impact 210 million years ago. The gatehouse and keep date from the 13th century, the main building from the 15th century. The Renaissance gallery with twisted columns opens onto the Graine valley. The contemporary art museum housed within its walls preserves more than 1,300 works.

On the château's first floor, the Galerie d'Hercule reveals rare late-15th-century murals. In the hunting hall, venery scenes alternate between colored paintings and grisailles. A space opened in 2017 offers 3D reconstructions of the Rochechouart lords' genealogy.

The Espace Météorite Paul Pellas, housed in the Maison de la Réserve, devotes 150 m² to the asteroid impact. 210 million years ago, a celestial body 1.5 km in diameter struck the ground at 72,000 km/h. The Rochechouart-Chassenon astrobleme is France's only recognized impact crater.

Push open the door of Église Saint-Sauveur, consecrated around 1060, a former priory of Charroux Abbey. Its walls are entirely built of impactites, rocks transformed by the meteoritic shock. Its helical spire, erected between 1767 and 1774, rotates 45° on its octagonal base and rises 20 meters high.

Inside the church, 15th-century frescoes stand alongside Nicolaï Greschny's Byzantine paintings, created in 1969 in the nave. The sacred art museum, housed in the gallery, gathers liturgical objects from Limousin.

→

Follow the Meteorite Circuit, a 2 km interpretive loop through town. Panels point out impactites used in house facades, château walls, and church foundations. The rock reveals geological history beneath your feet.

→

The National Nature Reserve of the Astroblème, created in 2008, protects the crater's geological outcrops. The International Center for Research on Impacts (CIRIR), established since 2016, hosts geologists from around the world. Field trips are sometimes open to the public.

💡 Inquire at the Espace Météorite for geological outing dates
→

Walk down to the Graine valley for a peaceful stroll. Wooded banks contrast with the rocky cliffs of the château spur. Remarkable viewpoint from the old bridge below.

The Église Sainte-Croix, built in the 13th century, owes its name to a legend: a pilgrim returning from Jerusalem reportedly rested here with a fragment of the True Cross. Its Limousin portal and three naves shelter a remarkable treasure of 21 reliquaries, including two champlevé enamel shrines from the 13th century.

The Tour Jeanne d'Albret, a classified Historic Monument, is the last vestige of the fortress built on the rocky spur overlooking the confluence of the Aixette and Vienne. From the summit, views embrace both valleys.

The Pont de Malassert, called the 'Roman bridge,' spans the Aixette with a single stone arch dating from the 14th century. The sole remnant of a former wooden plank bridge, it reveals itself along a shaded path.

Reach the Sanctuaire d'Arliquet, a neo-Gothic brick chapel unique in Limousin, built between 1865 and 1868. Its contemporary stained glass by Francis Chigot replaced 19th-century windows. Four large shrines hold martyrs' relics brought from Rome in 1870.

The sanctuary park invites a quiet moment. A Way of the Cross with 14 stations winds through trees to the Grotte de la Passion, set in a landscape of woods and rocks.

→

Aixe sits at the crossroads of GR 4 (Royan-Grasse) and GR 654 (Vézelay-Compostela). Follow a section of the trail along the Vienne for a 5 km hike between riverbanks and meadows.

→

Stroll through the old town's lanes, among remnants of three medieval gates — Lavaud, Auroux, and Guingand — which protected the town since the Middle Ages. Aixe was a strategic point on the via Lemovicensis to Compostela.

→

Settle in the medieval garden laid out along the Vienne for a break. The riverbanks offer a peaceful setting for a picnic facing the river.

The Abbatiale Saint-Pierre-Saint-Paul de Solignac is a unique case in Limousin: its three-bay nave is covered with hemispherical domes on pendentives, in the manner of Périgord churches. The Benedictine abbey, founded between 632 and 638 by Saint Éloi, goldsmith and advisor to King Dagobert, was Limousin's most powerful from the 10th to 13th centuries.

Look up at the choir capitals: griffins seized by men, figures battling serpents, and stacked palmettes stand out in limestone. The nave capitals, more archaic, show checkered patterns and animal heads.

The oak choir stalls, carved between 1457 and 1484, rank among Limousin's most remarkable. Flip the misericords: profane scenes appear — grimacing faces, mocking characters, and licentious figures hidden beneath the monks' seats.

In the right transept arm, a 15th-century fresco depicts Saint Christopher carrying the Christ Child, inspired by the Golden Legend. Donors' coats of arms and a detailed ship complete the composition.

The Château de la Borie, a 17th-century Renaissance residence, houses the Fondation La Borie dedicated to sound and music arts. Its garden of sounds, set in a 7-hectare park, blends sound installations and outdoor sculptures.

→

Cross the 12th-century Romanesque bridge, built by monks for Compostela pilgrims. Its three ogival arches and two cutwater piers carved to break the Briance current span the river with timeless grace.

→

Wander the medieval village lanes, among half-timbered houses and stone foundations. The abbey's turbulent history — Saracen and Norman raids, Wars of Religion, Revolution — left scars that Benedictine monks, returned in 2021, work to heal.

→

Take a section of GR 654 toward the Pont-Rompu, a 13th-century vestige on the old Roman road. The 6 km loop follows the Briance through preserved bocage landscape.

Bellac

The Église Notre-Dame de l'Assomption de Bellac, former chapel of the Counts of La Marche, presents two parallel naves: the 12th-century Romanesque once opened onto the vanished castle keep, while the 14th-century Gothic bears a 25-meter granite spire. The ensemble is classified as a Historic Monument.

Inside the church, the enameled shrine dated around 1130 is Limousin's oldest. This sarcophagus-shaped reliquary in gilt copper bears 14 medallions in raised champlevé enamel: Christ, evangelists, Virgin, and Lamb. Its colors — dark blue, green, turquoise, whitish pink — and cabochon gemstones testify to Limoges' first enamel workshops.

The Chapelle du Cardinal, adjoining the church, opens through a 15th-century Flamboyant Gothic window. Its limestone carving is remarkably delicate for the region.

Walk down to the Pont de la Pierre, a 13th-century medieval granite bridge over the Vincou. It's one of France's oldest preserved bridges. Below, the former 15th-century hide-drying shed evokes the tannery tradition that sustained the town for centuries.

Writer Jean Giraudoux's birthplace, at 4 Rue Jean-Jaurès, has been transformed into a museum with interactive and immersive installations. An escape game called 'Secrets d'État(s)' invites you to investigate the circumstances of his mysterious death.

→

Explore old Bellac on foot around the church: half-timbered houses, Renaissance windows, winding lanes, and former merchants' shops. The town, a commercial crossroads between Limousin, Marche, Berry, and Poitou, preserves traces of its medieval prosperity.

→

The Festival national de Bellac, founded in 1953, programs theater, dance, and music annually in late June through early July. Performances take over the old town's squares and courtyards.

→

Crave megaliths? The Monts de Blond, a granite massif rising to 514 meters, lie 15 minutes away. The megalith circuit from Ceinturat (11 km) leads to the Pierre Branlante de Boscartus — a 120-ton boulder used in the Middle Ages as an ordeal stone — then to the Roche aux Fées, a Neolithic shelter and necropolis.

→

The village of Mortemart, 11 km from Bellac, is among France's 'Plus Beaux Villages.' Its Augustinian convent, wooden market halls, and Carmelite convent transformed into artists' workshops merit the detour.
