Maine-et-Loire Road Trip: Anjou and Loire Valley Châteaux

Where vine-covered slopes descend to the Loire, where morning mist unveils medieval châteaux, where thousand-year abbeys slumber in golden stone. Anjou—gentle as its wines, enduring as its history.

Discover Anjou where vineyards cascade to royal rivers, medieval fortresses command limestone cliffs, and abbeys preserve centuries of devotion. Visit Angers and its imposing fortress guarding the world's largest medieval tapestry. Explore Saumur, where cavalry tradition meets sparkling wine cellars carved into tuffeau stone. Wander through Fontevraud's royal abbey, final resting place of Plantagenet monarchs. Meander through Montreuil-Bellay at the heart of wine country. At every turn, history flows as richly as the wine.

  • Saumur: where cavalry meets sparkling cellars
  • Fontevraud: Europe's grandest royal abbey

The château commands its promontory above the Maine River with 17 towers of black schist and sandstone. Inside these walls hangs the Apocalypse Tapestry—103 meters of UNESCO-listed medieval textile that tells the Book of Revelation in wool and silk.

The château gardens weave together Renaissance kitchen gardens and grapevines, offering sweeping panoramas over the city and the Maine valley below.

Saint-Maurice Cathedral showcases the distinctive domed vaults of Angevin Gothic architecture. Its stained glass windows have filtered light since the 12th century.

The 13th-century Toussaint Abbey houses the David d'Angers Gallery. A contemporary glass roof floods the space with light, illuminating the monumental sculptures of this local artist. His original plaster casts served as models for works now displayed in the Panthéon and Père-Lachaise Cemetery.

Step through the doorway of Maison d'Adam, a 15th-century half-timbered house adorned with intricate wood carvings that have survived five centuries.

The Museum of Fine Arts occupies the 15th-century Logis Barrault, presenting collections spanning from the Middle Ages to contemporary art.

→

Terra Botanica is Europe's first theme park dedicated to plants. Its 5,000 species from five continents sprawl across 22 hectares. The tethered balloon lifts you 150 meters above the gardens for a bird's-eye view of this botanical universe.

💡 Allow 4 to 6 hours to fully experience the park
→

Stroll along the Maine riverbanks and cross into the Doutre quarter via the bridges. The quays offer striking perspectives of the château rising above the water.

→

The Saumur region's wine cellars produce crémants and white wines. Several estates offer tastings in their troglodyte caves carved into the hillsides.

The château-palace of the Dukes of Anjou has watched over the Loire since the 14th century. Its Gothic silhouette was immortalized in the Très Riches Heures du Duc de Berry, one of medieval Europe's most celebrated illuminated manuscripts.

The Cavalry School has perpetuated French equestrian tradition since 1763. Its 18th-century buildings, classified as historic monuments, represent one of Europe's finest examples of military equestrian architecture.

The Tank Museum assembles over 200 military vehicles—one of the world's most significant collections of armored machinery.

The 12th-century Church of Saint-Pierre preserves rich liturgical furnishings and medieval sculptures that speak to centuries of devotion.

→

Saumur's sparkling wine cellars extend for kilometers through the tuffeau limestone. Maison Bouvet-Ladubay, founded in 1851, offers tours through its subterranean galleries where méthode traditionnelle wines age in darkness before their fine bubbles see daylight.

→

The Loire à Vélo cycling route passes through Saumur. Landscaped quays follow the royal river to neighboring châteaux through vine-covered countryside.

→

The Saumur-Champigny vineyards cascade down limestone slopes. Cabernet Franc, Chenin Blanc, and Chardonnay grapes produce the renowned red, white, and sparkling wines that have made this region famous.

The Royal Abbey of Fontevraud, founded in 1101, ranks among Europe's largest monastic cities, sprawling across 13 hectares. UNESCO-listed and remarkable for its history, it was governed for 700 years by 36 abbesses—an extraordinary matriarchy in medieval Christendom.

In the abbey church lie the polychrome effigies of Eleanor of Aquitaine, Henry II Plantagenet, Richard the Lionheart, and Isabelle of Angoulême. This royal necropolis bears witness to the Plantagenet empire that once stretched from Scotland to the Pyrenees.

The 12th-century Romanesque kitchens are architecturally unique. Twenty stone chimneys rise beneath an octagonal structure crowned with slate scales—a medieval engineering marvel.

The Grand-Moûtier cloister invites contemplation. Its 16th-century galleries frame a garden where silence reigns, much as it has for centuries of monastic life.

The chapter house served as the nuns' daily meeting place. Converted into a prison in the 19th century, it has been restored to reveal its Gothic beauty once more.

→

The abbey welcomes artists in residence, contemporary art exhibitions, and concerts within its history-laden spaces where ancient stones meet modern creativity.

→

The village of Fontevraud-l'Abbaye, classified among France's Most Beautiful Villages, reveals itself on foot. White tuffeau stone houses line flower-filled lanes that seem untouched by time.

→

Saumur vineyards surround the abbey. Several estates within a few kilometers offer tastings where you can sample the terroir that has been cultivated here for centuries.

The Château de Montreuil-Bellay raises 15 towers and 600 meters of ramparts above the Thouet valley. Founded in 1025 by Foulques Nerra, the fortress blends medieval military architecture with Renaissance elegance.

The 15th-century medieval kitchen with its central hearth is exceptionally well-preserved. Dry moats and vaulted underground passages testify to the fortress's defensive past when it guarded this strategic river crossing.

The collegiate church and canons' lodge, built in the 15th century, showcase the ornate beauty of Flamboyant Gothic architecture at its finest.

The château's 15th-century vaulted cellars house its wine estate. Sixteen hectares of organic vineyards produce Saumur reds, whites, and sparkling wines beneath these ancient stones.

The Duchess of Longueville's chamber preserves its period furnishings. This cousin of Louis XIV was exiled here after the Fronde rebellion—royal punishment in gilded surroundings.

→

The village of Montreuil-Bellay, a medieval walled town, unfolds as you wander on foot. Cobbled lanes climb toward the château, passing between white tuffeau stone houses that glow in the afternoon sun.

→

The Saumur vineyards embrace Montreuil-Bellay. Clay and flint slopes produce powerful Cabernet Francs and elegant Chenins that express the unique character of this terroir.

→

The Thouet cycling route follows the river through pastoral landscapes. This cycle path connects Montreuil-Bellay to neighboring châteaux via bucolic trails.

The Château de Brissac is France's tallest, soaring 7 stories and 204 rooms high—earning it the nickname 'Giant of the Loire Valley.' At 50 meters, it dominates the Aubance valley with unmistakable vertical drama.

Two medieval towers stand alongside the Renaissance façade—evidence of an unfinished transformation. Charles II de Cossé commissioned a new palace in 1606, but his death in 1621 permanently halted construction, freezing this architectural paradox in time.

The Belle Époque theater was built in 1890 by Jeanne Say, Marquise de Brissac and renowned opera singer. This intimate opera house welcomed the artistic elite of Paris to performances in the Loire countryside.

Gold-leaf ceilings, precious tapestries, and period furnishings create sumptuous interiors. The Louis XIII chamber preserves the memory of the 1620 reconciliation between the king and Marie de Médicis—a moment when royal family drama played out in these very rooms.

The 16th-century chapel features frescoes painted by Coppin Delf. Sixteen musical angels glorify the Virgin Mary and Christ in celestial harmony.

A 250-meter underground channel diverts the Aubance beneath the park. Its schist vault reflects in the water, creating an illuminated subterranean spectacle where stone and water mirror each other in darkness.

→

The 70-hectare romantic park invites leisurely exploration. Century-old cedars, the mausoleum avenue, Jeanne Say's cascade, and vineyards compose a bucolic landscape that changes with each season.

→

The village of Brissac-Quincé nestles within vineyards bordered by the Aubance. The 16th-century church and wine-growers' houses testify to generations of local viticultural tradition.

→

The Montgolfiade hot air balloon festival gathers thousands of visitors each late August to the château park, where dozens of balloons rise into the sunset sky in a magical display.

The island of Béhuard rises from the Loire, entirely floodable except for the church that crowns its rock. Classified as a Petite Cité de Caractère, this remarkable island sanctuary welcomes 150,000 visitors annually.

Notre-Dame Church has been built on this rock since the 5th century, when Saint Maurille placed a statue of the Virgin here. Louis XI rebuilt the current structure in 1469 after being saved from drowning in the Charente—a vow fulfilled in stone.

The sanctuary features two naves arranged in an L-shape. The rock itself breaks through the church floor beneath the statue of the Virgin and Child—nature and faith literally intertwined.

The 15th-century choir stalls and stained glass windows spanning the 15th to 19th centuries adorn the building. Louis XI made fifteen pilgrimages here between 1470 and 1479, seeking divine favor.

The picturesque village unfolds on foot through an island landscape frozen in time. Cobbled lanes and ancient houses create an atmosphere of bucolic serenity where the river dictates the rhythm of life.

→

The Loire à Vélo cycling route crosses Béhuard, connecting the island to Anjou's châteaux via landscaped paths that follow the river's curves through valley landscapes.

→

The Loire riverbanks invite wandering exploration. Guinguettes and terraces open when fair weather arrives, offering refreshment with views of the flowing river.

→

The heritage trail begins at the church square, revealing the island's treasures and its thousand-year history through interpretive panels and markers.

The Church of Sainte-Madeleine dominates Segré with its 30-meter-high neo-Byzantine dome. Classified stained glass windows flood the interior with colored light.

From the church esplanade, views sweep across the town and the confluence of the Oudon and Verzée rivers. These two waterways flow through this settlement founded in ancient Roman times.

The Château de la Lorie, built during Louis XIII's reign, stands as the northernmost of the Loire châteaux. Its grand salon, entirely composed of marble, evokes princely palaces with its elegant opulence.

The historic center preserves ancient houses and classified churches. Rue Pasteur features a half-timbered dwelling listed as a historic monument—a rare survival of medieval domestic architecture.

→

The Mine Bleue in Noyant-la-Gravoyère descends 126 meters underground into an authentic slate quarry. Guided tours reveal the harsh working conditions of miners and the extraction techniques for the prized blue slate that roofed countless buildings.

💡 Wear comfortable shoes and bring warm clothing
→

The Oudon riverbanks offer shaded walks through rolling landscapes. The trail follows the peaceful river as it meanders through verdant countryside.

→

The local market showcases producers from the Angevin terroir. Artisan workshops and gastronomic events regularly animate the town with regional flavors and crafts.

The Museum of Art and History devotes its history gallery to the Choletais region's defining moments from Gallo-Roman times forward. Extensive focus on the Vendée Wars that profoundly marked the city in blood and memory.

The Textile and Fashion Museum occupies the former linen bleaching factory built in 1881. Demonstrations on century-old looms reveal the manufacturing stages that made Cholet's textile industry famous.

The museum workshop still weaves Cholet's famous red handkerchief. Red for the blood spilled during the Vendée Wars, white for the monarchy—this local symbol has been sung about since 1900.

Notre-Dame Church rises in the city center. Its architecture testifies to Cholet's reconstruction after the Revolutionary Wars' devastation.

The Oriental Park of Maulévrier, just kilometers from Cholet, is Europe's largest Japanese garden at 29 hectares—a landscape of contemplation where Eastern philosophy meets French countryside.

→

Puy du Fou lies 25 km from Cholet. Voted Best Theme Park in the World, it presents grandiose historical spectacles and enchanting settings. The nighttime Cinéscénie gathers 2,400 volunteer actors in an unforgettable epic performance.

💡 Arrive at opening and stay through nightfall
→

Cholet's Saturday market ranks among Anjou's most important. Local terroir products and regional specialties fill the stalls with authentic flavors.

→

Parc de Moine and Lac de Ribou offer natural spaces for restoration and recreation. Hiking trails and water activities provide outdoor escape.
