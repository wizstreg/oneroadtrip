Experience the French Pyrenees at their most spectacular: dramatic cirques, medieval heritage, thermal wellness, and mountain adventures in one unforgettable journey.

Journey through the spectacular Hautes-Pyrénées region, where towering peaks meet medieval villages and thermal valleys. This 9-day circuit takes you from the historic city of Tarbes through the pilgrimage site of Lourdes, up to the UNESCO-listed Cirque de Gavarnie—one of Europe's most breathtaking natural amphitheaters. Experience mountain hiking, thermal relaxation, Romanesque architecture, and authentic Pyrenean culture as you traverse valleys shaped by glaciers and history.

Key highlights (6)
  • Marvel at the 423-meter Grande Cascade in the UNESCO-listed Cirque de Gavarnie
  • Explore Lourdes' sanctuaries and ascend Pic du Jer for panoramic mountain views
  • Hike the Chemin des Cascades through six waterfalls to the turquoise Lac de Gaube
  • Discover Saint-Bertrand-de-Comminges' magnificent cathedral and Roman ruins
  • Relax in Cauterets' thermal baths surrounded by snow-capped peaks
  • Wander through medieval Arreau and taste authentic mountain cheeses

The Jardin Massey, classified as a "Remarkable Garden," spans eleven hectares in the city center. Designed in the 19th century by botanist Placide Massey, former director of the Trianon nurseries in Versailles, it features centuries-old trees, an orangery, free-roaming peacocks, and the remains of the cloister from the Saint-Sever-de-Rustan abbey, whose 15th-century capitals are classified as a Historic Monument.

The Musée Massey occupies the former residence of the botanist, recognizable by its Moorish tower offering a panorama over the Pyrenees mountain chain. On the first level, the International Hussars Collection brings together more than 15,000 objects tracing four centuries of history of this elite cavalry, from the 16th to the 20th century, across thirty countries.

The second level of the museum presents a fine arts collection assembled in the 19th century by deputy Achille Jubinal. It features works from the Italian, Flemish, Dutch, and French schools from the 15th to the 20th century, with paintings by Girolamo del Pacchia, Frans II Francken, Baron Gérard, and Jean-Léon Gérôme.

The Birthplace of Marshal Foch, at 2 rue de la Victoire, is an 18th-century Bigourdan house classified as a Historic Monument. Its exterior gallery with balusters, marble-framed windows, and carved wooden staircase exemplify local architecture. Ferdinand Foch spent his first twelve years here before becoming Marshal of France, Great Britain, and Poland.

The Cathedral of Notre-Dame de la Sède is the oldest monument in Tarbes. Largely remaining Romanesque, it blends Romanesque and Gothic styles. Inside, the Baroque high altar combines local green and red marble, modeled after that of Saint Peter's in Rome. You can also discover Louis XVI's will engraved on a black marble plaque.

The Haras de Tarbes, created by decree of Napoleon I in 1806, occupies eight hectares in the heart of the city. Its Empire-style stables, collection of horse-drawn carriages, ceremonial saddlery, and the Maison du Cheval trace the history of breeding the Anglo-Arab horse, the emblematic breed of the Hautes-Pyrénées born here from the crossbreeding of local mares and oriental stallions.

The Grotte de Massabielle, on the banks of the Gave de Pau, is the site where Bernadette Soubirous reported eighteen apparitions of the Virgin Mary in 1858. This rock cavity (3.80 m high, 9.50 m deep) houses a Carrara marble statue sculpted by Fabisch, placed in the niche of the apparitions. The spring discovered by Bernadette still flows, captured and channeled to fountains accessible to visitors. The site is protected as a Historic Monument.

The Basilica of the Immaculate Conception, built from 1866 to 1871 atop the Massabielle rock, is in neo-Gothic style. Its single nave of five bays is adorned with ex-votos, and the arches bear the words that the Virgin addressed to Bernadette. The bell tower rises to 70 meters.

The Basilica of Our Lady of the Rosary, constructed between 1883 and 1889 below the previous one, adopts a Greek cross plan and Romano-Byzantine style. Its interior walls are covered with mosaics over more than 2,000 m² depicting the mysteries of the Rosary. It can accommodate 1,500 worshippers.

The Underground Basilica of Saint Pius X, inaugurated in 1958 for the centenary of the apparitions, is one of the world's largest churches with a capacity of 25,000 people. Its gemmail stained-glass windows diffuse a particular light in this elliptical concrete space buried beneath the esplanade.

The Château Fort, erected in the 11th century on a rocky outcrop, dominates the town and surrounding valleys. Classified as a Historic Monument, it houses the Pyrenean Museum (labeled Museum of France) dedicated to Pyrenean cultures, traditions, and crafts from the 18th to the 20th century. A botanical garden enhances the visit.

The Pic du Jer, accessible by a funicular inaugurated in 1900, rises to 1,000 meters altitude. At the summit, a 360° panorama embraces the Pyrenees chain, the Tarbes plain, and the town of Lourdes below. Developed caves can be visited on site.

The Pont d'Espagne, at 1,500 meters altitude and seven kilometers south of Cauterets, is a stone bridge from 1886 spanning the confluence of the Gave de Gaube and the Gave du Marcadau. A former pastoral route linking France to Spain via mountain passes, it marks the entrance to the heart of the Pyrenees National Park. The waterfall beside it is accessible in a few minutes' walk from the parking lot.

Lac de Gaube, nestled at 1,725 meters altitude, offers its turquoise waters facing the north wall of Vignemale, the highest peak of the French Pyrenees at 3,298 meters. Accessible on foot in 1h15 from Pont d'Espagne via the GR10, or by chairlift then twenty minutes' walk, it is one of the most photographed sites in the Hautes-Pyrénées.

The Chemin des Cascades connects La Raillère to Pont d'Espagne over approximately four kilometers through the forest. Six waterfalls succeed one another along the Gave de Jéret: the Lutour waterfall, the Mauhourat waterfall above the old sulfur thermal baths, the Escane-Gat waterfall (literally "cat strangler" in Gascon), the Ceriset waterfall with its enormous balanced rock, the Pouey Bacou waterfall which can be approached closely, and the Boussès waterfall.

The thermal village of Cauterets preserves 19th-century spa architecture, from the era when aristocracy came to take the waters. Victor Hugo, George Sand, and Chateaubriand stayed here. The thermal center offers treatments and a balneotherapy space.

The Marcadau Valley opens beyond Pont d'Espagne toward the Clot plateau and the Wallon refuge. This sector of the National Park shelters numerous high-altitude lakes - Aratille, Nère, Pourtet, Cambalès - in a preserved high-mountain setting where chamois and marmots are regularly encountered.

The Lutour valley, south of Cauterets, leads toward La Fruitière and Lac d'Estom in a wild setting of waterfalls, forests, and high-altitude meadows. The shaded route from Cauterets takes about four hours round trip.

The Church of Saint-André, called "of the Templars," is actually the work of the Hospitallers of Saint John of Jerusalem, who fortified it in the 14th century to protect pilgrims on the Way of Saint James from miquelets, Aragonese bandits. Its battlements, arrow slits, and defense towers give it a fortress-like appearance. Classified as a Historic Monument in 1840 - one of the very first Pyrenean monuments to receive this distinction - it contains a painted 16th-century vault depicting Christ surrounded by the four evangelists in tetramorphic form, and an 18th-century Baroque high altar.

The Pont Napoléon spans the Gave de Gavarnie at 63 meters height with an arch of 68 meters span. Constructed between 1859 and 1861 under Napoleon III, it was erected in gratitude to the valley inhabitants after Empress Eugénie stayed at the Saint-Sauveur thermal baths. The site has become a bungee jumping spot.

The Solférino Chapel, built in 1859 at Napoleon III's request to commemorate the victory at the Battle of Solferino in Italy, stands on a promontory overlooking the valley. Its Byzantine-inspired tower and surrounding benches make it a peaceful viewpoint over Luz and the surrounding mountains.

The Gorges de Luz carve a narrow passage through rock walls downstream from the village. The road that crosses them offers a striking view of the erosive power of the gave, with geological strata visible on the cliff faces.

The Château Sainte-Marie, in ruins on the heights at 630 meters altitude, preserves two medieval defense towers. The remains offer a viewpoint over the confluence of the Barèges and Gavarnie valleys.

Luz-Saint-Sauveur itself comprises two united towns: Luz, a commercial center animated by its Monday morning market at the foot of the fortified church, and Saint-Sauveur, a thermal spa where Second Empire elites stayed. Victor Hugo described the light of this valley by comparing its name - Luz - to the word "light."

The Cirque de Gavarnie, inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List since 1997 as part of the cross-border ensemble "Pyrénées-Mont Perdu," is a natural amphitheater 6.5 km in circumference whose limestone walls rise to 1,700 meters high. Sculpted by glacial erosion approximately 50 million years ago, its concentric tiers of striking symmetry are surrounded by peaks exceeding 3,000 meters. Victor Hugo called it an "impossible and extraordinary object."

The Grande Cascade de Gavarnie, with its 423-meter drop, is the highest waterfall in mainland France. Fed by snow and the Marboré glacier, it unfolds in long white veils on the cirque wall. Its flow varies considerably by season, with the spring melt months offering the most powerful spectacle.

The Brèche de Roland, at 2,804 meters altitude, is a natural notch 40 meters wide and 100 meters high in the rock wall separating France from Spain. According to legend, the knight Roland, Charlemagne's nephew, opened it with a blow of his sword Durandal to destroy it so it wouldn't fall into enemy hands.

The village of Gavarnie, starting point toward the cirque, preserves traditional Pyrenean architecture of stone houses and slate roofs. A historic stop on the paths to Santiago de Compostela, it also constitutes the birthplace of Pyreneanism, that current of Pyrenean exploration and ascent born in the 18th century.

The Refuge des Sarradets, at 2,587 meters altitude below the Brèche de Roland, dates from 1955-1956. Its terrace offers one of the most striking panoramas of the Pyrenees, with the Cirque de Gavarnie and the Grande Cascade as a backdrop. It serves as a stop for hikers climbing toward the Brèche.

The Cirque de Gavarnie is part of a set of three glacial cirques with Estaubé and Troumouse, forming the "Pyrénées-Mont Perdu" site recognized by UNESCO. This sector shelters remarkable fauna: chamois on the slopes, marmots in high-altitude meadows, bearded vulture (Europe's largest raptor with 2.80 m wingspan) and golden eagle in the updrafts.

→

From the village, walk about 1h30 on the main trail to the foot of the cirque to see the Grande Cascade up close. The path, without technical difficulty, crosses meadows and follows the gave - it can also be done on horseback with village outfitters.

→

For seasoned hikers, reach the Brèche de Roland from the Col des Tentes (2,208 m) via the Sarradets refuge. Allow 5 to 6 hours round trip for this demanding hike (approximately 1,000 m elevation gain, possible passages on snowfields even in summer).

→

Tour the medieval village of Gavarnie observing the stone architecture of Pyrenean houses and barns, and stop at one of the small restaurants to taste garbure or valley sheep cheese.

→

Stay until evening if you can: the sunset light on the cirque walls transforms the rock into gradients of ochre and pink, a spectacle that photography struggles to reproduce.

Saint-Lary-Soulan was born from the 1963 merger of the villages of Saint-Lary and Soulan to enable the ski resort's expansion. Located in the Aure valley and classified as a Land of Art and History, the village is a historic stop on the Way of Saint James. The main street bears the name of Vincent Mir, founder of the resort. The Pyrenean architecture of its stone houses and enhanced facades, the Pic Lumière cable car and the Vignec gondola linking the village to the slopes compose an authentic mountain setting.

The Maison du Patrimoine, located at 41 rue Vincent Mir on the Tourist Office square, houses several museum spaces dedicated to Pyrenean heritage: traditions, fauna, architecture, old-time schools and livestock farming. Upstairs, an interactive 3D map allows immersion in the valley's trails, passes, and lakes. Temporary exhibitions of painting, photography, and sculpture are presented throughout the year. Guided village tours are organized by the Aure-Louron Land of Art and History association.

The Church of Saint-Bertrand, built between 1950 and 1952 and consecrated in October 1952, replaced the old Saint-Hilaire church. Nearby, the Sainte-Marie Chapel and the Romanesque chapels from the 11th and 12th centuries in surrounding villages testify to the valley's rich religious heritage. The restored Debat Mill can be visited, as can the Moulin de la Mousquère in Soulan village, renovated and dating from the Middle Ages.

Soulan, a preserved and sunny pastoral village, dominates Saint-Lary from the heights toward the ski resort. Three kilometers away, Vielle-Aure offers a typical village with cobbled streets, a 12th-century Romanesque church, and the Château de Moulor with its keep and chapel, also from the 12th century. Further on, Ancizan (six kilometers), a former village of clothiers and weavers, preserves the Saint-Blaise-et-Saint-Martin church. Gouaux and Grailhen offer a remarkable exhibition and breathtaking view of the peaks, with the Gouaux goat farm to visit.

The Rioumajou Valley, accessible from the Frédancon parking lot at 1,380 meters altitude (twelve kilometers from Saint-Lary), is an easy two-hour round-trip family hike on a forest track following the Neste du Rioumajou. The pastoral valley unfolds with vast meadows crossed by a stream winding to the Hospice du Rioumajou, a refuge-restaurant at 1,560 meters altitude with 27 sleeping spaces. Labeled "Esprit Parc national," it is open from June to September. For more experienced walkers, the Balcon de l'Hospice (1,969 m) offers a viewpoint overlooking the site, accessible in two and a half hours round trip. Vehicle access is regulated from July to August between 12 pm and 4 pm.

The Néouvielle Nature Reserve, created in 1936 and one of France's first nature reserves, covers 2,313 hectares between 1,800 and 3,091 meters altitude. It shelters seventy lakes (Aubert, Aumar, Orédon, Cap-de-Long, l'Oule), record-breaking black pines for longevity and altitude, 1,250 vascular plants, 370 animal species, and 570 algae species. Access is via the lakes road from Saint-Lary, with shuttles between Orédon and Aubert in season. Bivouacking is authorized from 7 pm to 9 am in specific zones, dogs and swimming are prohibited.

Arreau, at 700 meters altitude, occupies an exceptional position at the confluence of the Aure and Louron valleys, where the Neste d'Aure meets the Neste du Louron. Former capital of the Four Valleys and seat of the judicature until the 16th century, then of the lieutenancy until 1789, the town was an important economic center thanks to its fairs, markets, and cloth-making craft. Classified as a Great Occitanie Site and Land of Art and History, it is protected by a Heritage Protection Zone created in 1995 covering the entire municipal territory.

The Maison des Lys, classified as a Historic Monument, is a 16th-century Gothic building whose half-timbered facade is adorned with fleurs-de-lis in homage to Louis XI. Its base is in gray Pyrenean marble and its two cantilevered floors give it a slender silhouette characteristic of the valley's medieval civil architecture.

The Chapel of Saint-Exupère, classified as a Historic Monument, is distinguished by its 16th-century Gothic bell tower that dominates the village. Its Romanesque entrance consists of six columns topped by a tympanum adorned with a chrism. Inside, the stained glass, colored ceiling, and statues trace devotion to Saint-Exupère, 5th-century Bishop of Toulouse and supposed native of Arreau - the 16th-century school adjoining the chapel would be his birthplace.

The Church of Notre-Dame, listed as a Historic Monument since 1989, features a square tower bell tower and houses a remarkable 16th-century Pietà. Nearby, the chapels of Saint-Michel de Garian (12th century) and Notre-Dame-du-Bon-Rencontre, the latter called "pilgrims' chapel," complete the town's religious heritage.

The Château des Nestes, on the banks of the Neste du Louron, is a former 17th-century sawmill symbolizing the union of the two rivers. It now houses the Pyrénées2vallées Tourist Office and the Cagot Museum, dedicated to this marginalized community from the Middle Ages to the Ancien Régime. The museum is open daily. Two hundred meters away via rue Saint-Exupère, the Château de Ségure preserves a 16th-century square tower built directly on rock, replacing a keep four hundred years older. It is now the headquarters of the Aure Louron Community of Communes.

Arreau's remarkable houses line the streets: the Maison Foche (or Maison Pic-Molié), Maison Feraud, Maison Laguens, Maison Ducuing-Forgue and others testify to rich civil architecture from the Middle Ages to the 19th century. The Halle-Mairie, built in 1932 replacing a 16th-century wooden hall, hosts a traditional market every Thursday under its remarkable framework.

Saint-Bertrand-de-Comminges, labeled "Most Beautiful Villages of France," "Great Occitanie Site," and "Remarkable Heritage Site," occupies a promontory at 515 meters altitude overlooking the Comminges plain. The medieval city unfolds in winding, steep streets around the cathedral, with a fortified enclosure called "scepte" that gathered the episcopal palace, canonical houses, cemetery, and cloister. The city walls incorporate numerous stones and sculptures reused from ancient Lugdunum Convenarum.

The Cathedral of Sainte-Marie, classified as a Historic Monument in 1840 and inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List in 1998 as part of the Ways of Saint James, is a Romano-Gothic edifice whose construction dates to around 1100. Bishop Bertrand de l'Isle (1083-1123), canonized in 1218, had the Romanesque cathedral and first cloister built. In 1307, Bertrand de Got, who became Pope Clement V, launched reconstruction of the Gothic apse and choir, and had Saint-Bertrand's relics transported in 1309.

The cathedral interior holds exceptional treasures. The carved Renaissance choir stalls, commissioned by Bishop Jean de Mauléon (1523-1551), are among France's finest. The organ, built in the 16th century at an angle on five Corinthian oak columns, rises to sixteen meters high and has three keyboards, a thirty-note pedal board, forty stops, and 2,621 pipes - it is considered one of Europe's most beautiful classical organs. The treasury preserves embroidered copes offered by Clement V, including the Cope of the Passion depicting scenes from Christ's passion, and Saint-Bertrand's pastoral staff, carved from a narwhal tusk.

The Romano-Gothic cloister from the 12th to 14th centuries, adjoining the cathedral, consists of four galleries from distinct periods: the western Romanesque gallery is the oldest, the southern and eastern galleries date from the first half of the 13th century, and the northern gallery, the only one vaulted, dates from the 14th century. Its openings onto nature, pierced in the early 20th century, offer an unexpected panorama over the Pyrenees. A stuffed crocodile, an ex-voto brought from the Holy Land in the 14th century, hangs in one of the galleries.

The remains of Lugdunum Convenarum, a Gallo-Roman city among the most important in Roman Aquitaine, extend at the hill's foot. Founded in the Augustan era, the city had up to ten thousand inhabitants at its peak before being destroyed in 585 during Frankish succession wars. Excavations, conducted from 1913 to 1970 then resumed from 1985 to 2006, have uncovered a theater of five thousand spectators from the 1st century, a temple of imperial cult in Pyrenean marble, a forum, baths, and a Christian basilica. The Augustan trophy, in white Saint-Béat marble, is one of the most complete found in France. The upper town ramparts, from the 5th century, are listed as a Historic Monument.

The Basilica of Saint-Just de Valcabrère, classified as a Historic Monument and inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List like the cathedral, is an isolated Romanesque edifice in the middle of fields, a few hundred meters from Saint-Bertrand. Built on the site of a Roman necropolis whose stones it reuses, it is mentioned as early as Bishop Bertrand's time (1083-1123). Remains of a cloister and annex buildings testify to a former regular canonical house.

Bagnères-de-Bigorre, a thermal spa nestled at the entrance to the upper Adour valley and dominated by Pic du Midi, was known since Roman times as "Vicus Aquensis." The town has forty registered springs. A terrible earthquake in 1660 destroyed all dwellings, rebuilt in the same style. In 1823, construction of the Grand Thermes was launched, inaugurated in 1828, and the railway's arrival in 1862 accelerated resort development. In 1993, a "facades" operation restored the town's colors.

The Tour des Jacobins, classified as a Historic Monument in 1932, is the vestige of the former church and Dominican convent built around 1373. This thirty-five-meter-high belfry in Flamboyant Gothic features a square base over two levels then an octagonal section. Its first-floor bays offer 15th-century molding with round arches topped by accolades and finials. Partially destroyed by lightning, it was rebuilt in the 16th century.

The Church of Saint-Vincent, classified as a Historic Monument in 1990, constitutes an edifice of primary importance for High Pyrenean architecture after the 14th century. Built outside the walls in the 13th century on the site of older buildings, it was profoundly altered between the late 14th and early 16th century. Its bell-gable, portal, vaults mixing stone and wood, and frameworks are remarkable.

The Musée Salies, listed as a Historic Monument in 2014, occupies an Art Deco building designed by architect Léon Jaussely and completed in 1931, on Place des Thermes. Its facade is inspired by the 1925 Paris International Exhibition of Decorative Arts, with allegorical figures and bas-reliefs of female nudes. Collections include paintings from major 19th-century Paris salons, obtained thanks to deputy Achille Jubinal and his friendship with Princess Mathilde, as well as the Blanche Odin collection (1865-1957), watercolorist nicknamed "the Fairy of roses and flowers," and Pyrenean folk art objects bequeathed by Jeanne Sayous.

The Musée du Marbre, installed in the former Thermes du Salut whose main building dates from 1781, presents the Jacques Dubarry de Lassale collection: 320 European marble origins, including about a hundred Pyrenean quarries from the Spanish Basque Country to Languedoc. The historical gallery preserves original marble cabins and bathtubs, paving, and a stairway testifying to Pyrenean marble-working expertise. It is the only collection of this type displayed in France. The museum also houses a space dedicated to bats, with infrared camera observation of colonies nesting in the attics in summer.

The Médous Caves, located between Bagnères and Campan in Asté commune, were discovered by speleologist Norbert Casteret. The approximately forty-five minute to one-hour visit takes place at 13°C and crosses the Gallery of Wonders, where concretions, eccentrics, petrified waterfalls, gours, and draperies succeed one another, including a column twenty meters in circumference. The visit ends with a boat ride on the underground river in the cave's largest chamber.
