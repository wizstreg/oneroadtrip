Tarbes

Jardin Massey unfolds across 27 acres of lawns, pebble canals, and centuries-old trees right in the city center. Classified as a "Remarkable Garden," it shelters a 15th-century cloister brought stone by stone from Saint-Sever-de-Rustan Abbey, its sculpted capitals illustrating scenes from the Old and New Testaments.

Step inside the Musée Massey, an oriental-style building crowned with an observation tower overlooking the Pyrenees. Its international Hussars collection brings together 17,000 objects tracing 400 years of light cavalry history across 30 countries. One hundred and thirty period-uniformed mannequins guide you through the chronological journey.

Upstairs, the Fine Arts gallery showcases major works from the Italian, Flemish, and Dutch schools of the 16th-17th centuries, alongside French paintings from the 18th-19th centuries. Rotating exhibitions reveal pieces normally kept in storage.

Head to Marshal Foch's birthplace at 2 rue de la Victoire. This 18th-century Bigourdan house, a classified Historic Monument, preserves portraits, documents, and gifts from Allied nations honoring the marshal's role in World War I. Notice the exterior gallery with its balusters and the carved wooden staircase.

Notre-Dame-de-la-Sède Cathedral, one of Tarbes' oldest buildings, blends Romanesque and Gothic styles dating from the 12th century. Inside, a baroque marble baldachin draws inspiration from St. Peter's in Rome. On a black marble plaque, Louis XVI's testament is engraved in full.

The Haras de Tarbes occupies 20 acres in the heart of the city. Founded in 1806 under Napoleon, this stallion depot features Empire-style stables housing a collection of horse-drawn carriages, an ornate saddlery, and the House of the Horse. Its façades and roofs are listed as Historic Monuments.

→

Follow the heritage trail marked by studs in the pavement. From Place de Verdun to Rue Maréchal-Foch, then Rue Abbé-Torné, the façades tell the city's story: timber-framed houses, the imperial lycée, and the former prefecture.

→

Circle the garden by horse-drawn carriage—a brief ride that lets you discover rare tree species and free-roaming peacocks without breaking a sweat.

→

The covered Halles Marcadieu market brings together local producers and Pyrenean specialties. Sheep's milk cheeses, gâteau à la broche (spit cake), black pork of Bigorre, and Tarbais beans compose an authentic gourmet tableau.

Lourdes

The Grotto of Massabielle, site of the 18 apparitions of 1858, carves into the rock 12 feet high and 31 feet deep. The Carrara marble statue occupies the apparition niche 6 feet above ground. Pilgrims touch the wall polished smooth by millions of hands. Access is free around the clock.

Above the grotto, the Basilica of the Immaculate Conception (1866-1871) raises its tall spire. Its stained glass windows narrate the events of Lourdes. Below, the Rosary Basilica (1883-1889) unfolds 21,500 square feet of polychrome Byzantine-Romanesque mosaics beneath a dome of gold and indigo.

Descend beneath the esplanade: the underground St. Pius X Basilica (1958), stretching 627 feet long, can welcome over 20,000 people. Its architecture evokes an overturned ship's hull. Gemmail stained-glass windows create a multicolored pathway of light.

Climb up to the fortress perched on a rocky outcrop since the 11th century. Keep, drawbridge, and arrow slits tell a thousand years of military history. The Pyrenean Museum, founded in 1921 and labeled "Museum of France," presents costumes, furniture, and pastoral traditions from both French and Spanish Pyrenees.

From the castle ramparts, the panorama sweeps across the town, the sanctuaries, and the Pyrenean range. The botanical garden offers a quiet moment among miniature models of traditional mountain dwellings and sculptures.

The monumental Way of the Cross winds through the wooded hillside above the sanctuaries. Its 15 life-size stations in gilded cast iron mark a path offering plunging views of the Gave de Pau river and the mountains.

→

Attend the torchlight procession, one of Lourdes' most moving moments. Thousands of candles ripple through the night from the esplanade to the basilica, accompanied by the singing of Ave Maria.

→

Take the century-old Pic du Jer funicular, inaugurated in 1900. In 10 minutes it climbs 1,550 feet to the summit at 3,110 feet. Enjoy a 360° panorama over Lourdes, Tarbes, Pau, and the Pyrenean peaks. A large illuminated cross crowns the summit.

💡 First ascent at 10 AM, last at 5 PM
→

Nine miles from Lourdes, the Bétharram Caves offer a 1.7-mile underground journey across five levels: stalactites, stalagmites, and limestone draperies follow one another. You descend 250 steps to the underground river, travel by boat, then a little train brings you back to the surface. Constant temperature of 57°F—bring a warm layer.

→

Lake Lourdes, nestled in greenery five minutes from the center, invites an easy stroll along its shoreline path. Picnic tables and birdwatching complete the peaceful stop.

Cauterets, a thermal resort at 3,117 feet elevation, preserves remarkable Belle Époque architecture. Grand 19th-century hotels, monumental façades, and ornate balconies testify to the golden age of spa culture. Wander the lanes to soak in the atmosphere of this village labeled "Land of Mountaineering."

The Pont d'Espagne, at 4,908 feet and 4 miles from the village, spans the thundering confluence of the Cauterets and Marcadau mountain streams. Once a mule trail to Spain, it opens the gateway to Pyrenees National Park. Five minutes from the parking lot, waterfalls stage a permanent spectacle.

Lake Gaube rests at 5,659 feet, dominated by Vignemale (10,820 feet), the highest summit of the French Pyrenees. Its turquoise waters mirror the eternal snows. Reach it in 1 hour 15 minutes on foot from Pont d'Espagne or by cable car then chairlift.

The Griffons Thermal Baths at La Raillère, upstream from the village, have exploited naturally sulfurous waters since the 16th century. The buildings are listed as Historic Monuments. Across the way, the Lutour waterfall cascades down from Lake Estom.

→

Follow the Waterfalls Trail between La Raillère and Pont d'Espagne. Six cascades succeed one another through the forest: Mauhourat, Escane-Gat, Ceriset, Pouey, Bousses, Cerisey. A shaded path ideal for families.

→

Relax at Bains du Rocher, a thermal spa in the heart of the village. Indoor and outdoor pools heated with natural sulfur water, hammams, saunas, and tropical showers—no reservation needed.

💡 Open daily, walk-ins welcome
→

From Pont d'Espagne, hike into the Marcadau valley: the Cayan plateau offers exceptional viewpoints. The most motivated can continue to the Wallon refuge and high-altitude lakes (Aratille, Nère, Pourtet, Cambalès).

→

On Fridays, Cauterets market gathers local producers and artisans in the village center. Berlingots—traditional candies born in the 16th century to mask the sulfurous taste of thermal waters—are the signature specialty.

The Church of St. Andrew, called the "Templars' Church," was actually built by the Knights Hospitaller of St. John of Jerusalem who fortified it in the 14th century. Its 14-sided crenellated enclosure, arrow slits, and two towers (Arsenal and clock keep) make it a unique fortress. It was classified as a Historic Monument as early as 1840.

Inside, look up at the 16th-century painted vault: Christ surrounded by the four evangelists forms a colorful tetramorph. The 18th-century baroque main altar in gilded wood and a 17th-century polychrome Pietà complete the décor. The Chapel of Our Lady of Pity (1650), attached to the ramparts, was erected to ward off a plague epidemic.

The Pont Napoléon spans the mountain stream from 207 feet up on a single stone arch 138 feet in diameter. Built between 1859 and 1861 under Napoleon III by engineer Bruniquel, it links the districts of Luz and Saint-Sauveur. Vertigo is guaranteed at the center of the 223-foot deck.

On a hilltop overlooking the town, the Solférino Chapel raises its Byzantine tower. Rebuilt in 1859 on Napoleon III's orders, it occupies the site of the ancient Chapel of St. Peter. Traditionally, its priest blessed flocks departing for high mountain pastures.

The thermal quarter of Saint-Sauveur stretches along a promontory above the stream. Its baths, exploited since the 16th century and listed as Historic Monuments, welcomed Empress Eugénie and Napoleon III. The wellness center continues the tradition of water therapy.

Wander the lanes of old Luz: stone wash-houses, marble window frames, and wrought-iron balconies sketch a preserved mountain heritage. The Maison Gradet (current town hall), built in 1828, hosted a cabinet meeting of Napoleon III.

→

The Napoleon-Eugénie Walk forms an easy 1.9-mile loop between the Town Hall and the Thermal Baths, passing by the Pont Napoléon and Solférino Chapel. Interpretive panels and valley landscapes punctuate this 2-hour historical stroll.

→

From the Pont Napoléon, a via ferrata is installed on the rock of the left pillar. Vertical thrills guaranteed 207 feet above the stream, supervised by mountain professionals.

→

Luz-Saint-Sauveur is the starting point for the ascent of Col du Tourmalet, a legendary Tour de France climb. Bike rental shops offer equipment suitable for tackling the switchbacks.

→

The Bastan Trail follows the torrent along the valley floor. An easy, peaceful path accessible to all, offering direct contact with the natural landscape of Pays Toy.

The Cirque de Gavarnie, inscribed on UNESCO's World Heritage List ("Pyrénées-Mont Perdu" ensemble, 1997), unfolds its 5,577-foot-high wall stretching 8.7 miles around. Its concentric limestone tiers, sculpted by glacial erosion over 50 million years, are crowned with summits exceeding 10,000 feet: Marboré (10,656 feet), Taillon (10,315 feet), Astazou.

The Grande Cascade de Gavarnie plunges 1,388 feet—the highest waterfall in metropolitan France. Fed by the Marboré glaciers, it's visible from the hotel at the foot of the cirque. In spring, snowmelt gives it its most spectacular flow.

The village of Gavarnie, home to fewer than 200 souls, is a historic stop on the Way of St. James. Its parish church is among the properties inscribed on UNESCO's World Heritage List. Stone houses huddle along narrow lanes at the foot of the natural amphitheatre.

The Brèche de Roland, a natural notch 131 feet wide and 230 feet high at 9,209 feet elevation, opens the border between France and Spain at the foot of the Casque de Marboré. Legend has it that Roland, Charlemagne's nephew, cleaved it with a stroke of his sword Durandal after the Battle of Roncevaux.

From the Bellevue viewpoint or the Saugué plateau (above Gèdre), the panorama embraces the entire cirque and three neighboring cirques: Estaubé, more pastoral and wild, and Troumouse, the vastest with its 6.8-mile circumference and alpine meadows accessible by road.

→

Hike from the village to the foot of the cirque along the path following the Gave de Gavarnie. Verdant terrain, autumn crocuses, and streams accompany this easy 1.5-hour walk. At arrival, the immensity of the vertical walls is dizzying.

→

Reach the Brèche de Roland from the Col des Tentes (7,244 feet, accessible by road). The trail passes through the Port de Boucharo (7,451 feet), crosses the Taillon stream below the glacier, then climbs to the Sarradets refuge (8,488 feet) before the final ascent to the breach. Cross-border panorama over the Gavarnie cirque and Spain's Ordesa canyon.

💡 Optimal period: early August to mid-September. Crampons may be needed off-season.
→

A horseback ride to the foot of the cirque lets you experience the landscape differently. Local outfitters offer mounts suited to all skill levels.

→

The Cirque de Troumouse, accessible via a scenic road from Gèdre, offers an amphitheatre stretching 6.8 miles around—even vaster than Gavarnie. Alpine meadows, marmots, and griffon vultures populate this grandiose setting that sees far fewer crowds.

The Pyrenees National Park Visitor Center occupies the Tour Hachan, a typical building in the village heart. Renovated in 2014, the three-story museum space presents the forests of the Aure valley: riparian woodland along the Neste, beech-fir forest, and the hooked pine forest of the Néouvielle Nature Reserve—Europe's highest forest. Touch-and-feel displays, soundscapes, and scent stations complete the sensory discovery.

The Heritage Center, an interpretation facility opened in July 2017, presents the cultural riches of the Aure valley in a blend of tradition, authenticity, and modernity. Located in the village center near the tourist office, it complements your exploration of local Pyrenean heritage.

The town hall occupies the Maison Fornier, a manor dating from 1629. The last owner conditioned the sale on housing the town hall there, which happened in 1974. In front of the building, a bear fountain recalls the historical presence of the plantigrade in the Pyrenees. The village maintains an architectural unity of wood-and-schist buildings, even recent ones.

The Néouvielle Nature Reserve, created in 1936 among France's first, protects a unique granite massif between 5,905 and 10,141 feet elevation. It counts over 70 glacial lakes, 570 species of algae, and hooked pines setting records for longevity and altitude. Accessible via the lakes road from Orédon, the site shelters marmots, griffon vultures, bearded vultures, and the extremely rare Pyrenean desman.

Lakes Aubert (7,047 feet) and Aumar, separated by a tongue of land, offer sumptuous panoramas in the heart of the reserve. The Laquettes trail from Orédon (1h45 round trip, 755 feet elevation gain, easy) threads through a chaplet of small natural lakes sheltering rich fauna. The Col d'Aumar and Hourquette d'Aubert offer viewpoints over the massif for more seasoned hikers.

The Saint-Lary ski area spans three sectors: Pla d'Adet (5,577 feet), Espiaube (6,234 feet), and Vallon du Portet, totaling 62 miles of slopes and 59 runs between 5,413 and 8,251 feet elevation. In summer, the Pla d'Adet cable car provides access to numerous hiking trails and panoramas over the Aure valley.

→

Relax in the thermal waters of Sensoria: indoor and outdoor pools, jacuzzis, saunas, and hammams with mountain views. The naturally warm waters, diverted from the old Tramezaygues spa, soothe rheumatism and muscle tension after hiking.

→

Hike in the Rioumajou valley, one of the wildest in the Pyrenees, or climb toward the lakes of the Néouvielle reserve on a hut-to-hut trek (5 staffed refuges: Orédon, Bastan, La Glère, Campana de Cloutou, Aygues-Cluses, totaling 226 beds in summer).

→

Cycling over legendary Tour de France cols: Col du Portet and Col d'Azet starting from Saint-Lary, regularly tackled by the Grande Boucle. Mountain biking possible on a vast network extending into Spain via Zona Zero.

→

Discover the villages of the Aure valley: Vielle-Aure and its stone houses, Guchen and its traditions, Sailhan and its typical architecture. Together they form the Art and History Region of the Aure and Louron valleys.

The Chapel of St. Exuperus, a classified Historic Monument, takes its name from the Arreau peasant who became the sixth bishop of Toulouse in the 5th century. The Romanesque porch (10th-12th century) shelters a tympanum adorned with a chi-rho. The octagonal bell tower and 16th-century Gothic nave are of exceptional structure. The chapel's grandeur stems from its political role: it served as a meeting place for representatives of the Aure valley, and the archives of the Four Valleys were kept in the bell tower chambers.

The Maison des Lys, a classified Historic Monument, is a magnificent late-16th-century Gothic residence. Its finely sculpted gray marble façade supports two corbelled half-timbered floors adorned with wooden fleurs-de-lis, honoring King Louis XI after the Four Valleys' allegiance to the French Crown in 1475.

The Château des Nestes (or Château Camou), a listed Historic Monument, recalls ten centuries of Aure valley history. A former military and hospitaller commandery built on the enclosure of the Neste du Louron quarter, it protected the sanctuary of St. Exuperus. Its central tower topped with a wooden bell turret served as a family astronomical observatory in the 19th century.

The market hall-town hall, built in 1932 on the site of the 16th-century wooden hall, houses the Thursday market on the ground floor. Its exterior walls are decorated with faux timber framing echoing the style of Aure valley houses. A three-faced clock turret crowns the building. The ensemble testifies to Arreau's role as economic capital of the Four Valleys since the Middle Ages.

The Church of Notre-Dame d'Arreau preserves a treasury of Pyrenean religious art, including a remarkable 16th-century Pietà. The village also harbors the Chapel of Notre-Dame-du-Bon-Rencontre (1761, pilgrims' chapel), the Romanesque-origin Chapel of St. Michael, and a small museum of the Cagots tracing the history of this marginalized Pyrenean population.

The marked historical circuit starting from the Château des Nestes lets you discover Arreau's remarkable residences: Maison Féraud with exceptional framework decoration, Maison Foch (which belonged to the marshal's grandfather), Maison Laguens with pink marble piers, Maison Ducuing-Forgue. The entire municipal territory has been protected by a Heritage Protection Zone since 1995.

→

Stroll along the flower-lined banks of the Neste d'Aure and Neste du Louron, the two rivers that give Arreau its unique charm. The village, at 2,306 feet elevation, is classified as a Grand Site of Occitanie and Art and History Region, a crossroads between the Aspin and Peyresourde passes.

→

Excursion to the Col d'Aspin (4,885 feet), a legendary Tour de France stage. Exceptional panorama over the central Pyrenees. In summer, you can hike toward the Signal de Bassia or Lake Payolle (3,051 feet), a verdant jewel at the foot of Pic du Midi.

→

Discover Pyrenean gastronomy in Arreau's restaurants: garbure, Barèges-Gavarnie lamb (PDO), sheep's milk cheese, black pork of Bigorre. The Thursday market under the halls perpetuates a centuries-old commercial tradition.

→

River fishing in the Neste, renowned for its brown trout. Arreau is an ideal starting point for whitewater fishing outings in a preserved mountain setting.

The Cathedral of Sainte-Marie, a classified Historic Monument since 1840 and inscribed on UNESCO's World Heritage List as part of the Ways of St. James of Compostela, has dominated the village since the 11th century. Bertrand de l'Isle erected the Romanesque cathedral around 1100; it was enlarged in the 14th century by Bertrand de Got, future Pope Clement V, who imposed the Gothic style. The bell tower gives the edifice the air of an impregnable fortress.

The Renaissance choir houses 66 oak choir stalls carved in the 16th century under Bishop Jean de Mauléon, considered among the finest in France. The monumental organ, also commissioned by Mauléon, rises 52 feet on five Corinthian columns; it has 3 manuals, 40 stops, and 2,621 pipes. The treasury preserves copes embroidered in opus anglicanum offered by Clement V, and St. Bertrand's pastoral staff made from narwhal tusk.

The cloister, built in the 13th century and remodeled under Clement V, has the distinctive feature of galleries open to the surrounding nature. It served as a prayer space and burial ground for canons. The western Romanesque gallery is the oldest, while the 14th-century north gallery is the only vaulted one. At the center, a stuffed crocodile recalls the legend of St. Bertrand slaying a monster.

The Gallo-Roman archaeological site of Lugdunum Convenarum spreads across the plain at the foot of the cathedral. Capital of the Convenae founded in the 1st century BC, the city included a forum, theater, market, and baths that made it a little Pyrenean Rome. A trophy of Augustus, an exceptional sculpted composition, was erected around 16 BC after victories over the Aquitanians.

The Basilica of Saint-Just de Valcabrère, a few hundred yards away, is one of the oldest Christian churches in southern Gaul. Built largely with stones from the Roman site on an ancient necropolis, it's inscribed on UNESCO's World Heritage List. Its Romanesque architecture and countryside setting reminiscent of Tuscany make it a jewel of serenity.

The medieval Upper Town reveals itself on foot through picturesque lanes leading to the cathedral. Les Olivétains, a former monastery of monks from Monte Oliveto in Italy, now houses the tourist information office, a bookshop, a local products boutique, and contemporary art exhibition halls.

→

Four historical walks are offered (30 minutes to 2 hours): the town and its suburb, the archaeological promenade, the three sanctuaries, and the medieval city. The brochure is available at the cathedral reception.

→

The Festival du Comminges brings the cathedral and basilica to life each summer with classical music and organ concerts in an exceptional acoustic setting. The Renaissance organ, restored thanks to the festival, recovers a sound aesthetic close to that of the 17th century.

→

Hikes in the Pyrenean foothills with views of Pic de Cagire, Pic du Gar, and the Garonne basin. Saint-Bertrand-de-Comminges, at 1,690 feet elevation, offers panoramas over the surrounding mountains and marks a stop on the Ways to Compostela.

The Salies Museum, a listed Historic Monument since 2014, presents an Art Deco façade completed in 1931 by architect Jaussely, inspired by Paris's 1925 Exposition des Arts Décoratifs. Founded in 1852 by deputy Achille Jubinal, it brings together canvases from the great 19th-century Paris salons, Barbizon School landscapists (Daubigny, Dupré, Jongkind), an Orientalist room, and 48 watercolors by Blanche Odin, nicknamed the Fairy of Roses.

The Jacobins Tower, sole remnant of the 14th-century Dominican convent, stands in the heart of Bagnères. Its massive, austere architecture, typical of the mendicant order, testifies to the town's medieval past.

The Church of Saint-Vincent, a classified Historic Monument since 1990, constitutes the principal religious edifice of Bagnères-de-Bigorre. The Saint-Jean Cloister completes the downtown religious heritage.

The Marble Museum, installed in the former Salut thermal baths, displays France's only collection of over 400 varieties of European marbles, including about a hundred from Pyrenean quarries. The Espiadet marble from Payolle was used for Versailles, the Louvre, and the Paris Opera.

The Médous Caves, discovered in 1948 in Asté, rank among France's most beautiful underground wonders. Over a 0.6-mile course, stalactites, stalagmites, helictites, and calcite flowstones evoke cascades and draperies. The visit concludes with 820 feet by boat on the underground Adour—a moment of rare intensity. Constant temperature of 55°F.

The Pic du Midi de Bigorre (9,439 feet), a Grand Site of Occitanie, shelters an astronomical observatory founded in the 1870s. Accessible in 15 minutes by cable car from La Mongie, it offers 8,000 square feet of 360° panoramic terraces spanning 186 miles of Pyrenean chain. The Pont dans le Ciel, a 39-foot glass walkway jutting over the void, and Europe's highest planetarium complete the experience.

→

Wellness cure at the Grand Thermes de Bagnères-de-Bigorre, a thermal spa since Roman times (Vicus Aquensis). The naturally hot waters (122°F) are renowned for their therapeutic virtues. The Aquensis spa space offers treatments and relaxation.

→

Stroll through the Vallon du Salut, a classified site since 2007 and gathering place for Bagnères residents since the 18th century. The path winds beneath the canopy of centuries-old trees, carried by the murmur of warm thermal springs.

→

Stargazing evening at Pic du Midi: observation from the world's largest International Dark Sky Reserve, certified since December 2013. Instant Science facilitators guide the discovery of constellations and celestial phenomena.

→

Excursion to Col du Tourmalet (6,939 feet), the highest paved pass in the Pyrenees and a legendary Tour de France stage since 1910. En route, stop at Lake Payolle (3,051 feet), a verdant jewel at the foot of Pic du Midi, ideal for picnicking and family walks.
