Hidden Villages Around Rome and the Castelli Romani

Where Renaissance fortresses meet volcanic lakes, ghost villages harbor artists, and Rome's ancient oracular sanctuaries still stand guard over the countryside.

The route opens on Lake Bracciano and the Castello Orsini-Odescalchi, a Renaissance fortress built by the same masons working on the Sistine Chapel. Calcata, a village declared uninhabitable in 1935 and reclaimed in the 1960s by artists from around the world, offers a journey outside time in the Treja gorges. Palestrina reveals Republican Rome's largest oracular sanctuary and its 5.85 m Nile Mosaic, a Hellenistic masterpiece. The Castelli Romani unfold through Nemi with Caligula's ships burned in 1944, Castel Gandolfo with its 55 hectares of papal gardens, and Grottaferrata with its Byzantine abbey founded in 1004. The circuit concludes on the Cyclopean walls of Norba and the heights of the Monti Lepini.

Castello Orsini-Odescalchi — A Renaissance fortress begun in 1470 under Napoleone Orsini's commission, likely built by the very masons working simultaneously on the Sistine Chapel. Napoleone's brother, Cardinal Latino Orsini, served as chamberlain to Pope Sixtus IV. Construction was completed in 1495 under his son Gentil Virginio's supervision. The complex incorporates five towers on five corners and three concentric defensive walls. In 1558, Duke Paolo Giordano Orsini married Isabella de' Medici and converted the fortress into a ducal palace, bringing in the Zuccari brothers to fresco several rooms. When the Orsini line died out in 1696, the castle passed to the Odescalchi family, nephews of Pope Innocent XI, who remain the owners today. The museum route covers twenty rooms displaying weapons collections, period furniture, and paintings spanning the 15th to 19th centuries.

Anguillara Sabazia — A lakeside village perched on a promontory of the Sabatini Mountains overlooking Lake Bracciano, with the 16th-century Collegiata dell'Assunzione and medieval lanes clinging to the hillside. From the belvedere in the central piazza, you look straight across the lake to the wooded shores of Parco Regionale di Bracciano-Martignano, designated a protected natural area in 1999.

Trevignano Romano — A small fishing village on the lake's northern shore, with a parish church built on the ruins of a medieval castrum. The main piazza, perched above the water, offers one of the most sweeping panoramas of Lake Bracciano's 56.5 km² expanse. These waters have supplied Rome since the 17th century via the Acquedotto Paolo.

→

Swimming on the shores of Lake Bracciano — A volcanic crater lake 160 meters deep, ranked among the cleanest lakes in central Italy. The best access points are at Trevignano and Anguillara, where you'll find pebble beaches and picnic areas within the natural park.

Monterano Antica — A ghost town abandoned after a malaria epidemic in the late 18th century. The ruins include a partially standing baroque church and an ornate fountain featuring a lion, attributed to Bernini's circle. The site sits within the Riserva Naturale Monterano, where collapsed houses disappear beneath advancing vegetation. The gradual abandonment accelerated after 1799 when French troops ravaged the area.

Calcata Vecchia — A medieval village perched on a red tuff spur in the Treja valley, declared uninhabitable by fascist decree in 1935 due to the risk of the cliff collapsing. Its residents were relocated to Calcata Nuova, built 2 km away. In the 1960s-70s, artists, intellectuals, and hippies from across Europe and America recolonized the abandoned houses, transforming the village into a creative community that thrives to this day. The 15th-century Palazzo Baronale degli Anguillara now houses the visitor center for Parco Valle del Treja. The small Chiesa del Santissimo Nome di Gesù, built in the 14th century and restored in 1793, preserves a reliquary in a 15th-century tabernacle. Local legend claims that the Holy Prepuce, stolen during the 1527 Sack of Rome, was kept here until the 1980s.

→

Hiking in the Parco Regionale Valle del Treja — The Treja River, a tributary of the Tiber, has carved gorges through volcanic tuff for several kilometers. Trail 015 leads to the ruins of the ancient Faliscan city of Narce on the rocky spur opposite Calcata, with a necropolis dating to the 8th century BC. The Cascate di Monte Gelato, a series of small waterfalls and pools formed by the river, lie 5 km away.

→

Visiting artists' workshops in Calcata — The caves and cellars carved into the tuff house workshops for ceramics, sculpture, jewelry-making, and furniture restoration. Several artists welcome visitors into their working spaces, continuing the tradition that repopulated the village since the 1960s.

Castello Orsini di Nerola — A medieval fortress overlooking the village from a hill in the Monti Lucretili, held by the powerful Orsini family from the 13th century. The castle witnessed the 1483 birth of Felice della Rovere, illegitimate daughter of Cardinal Giuliano della Rovere, the future Pope Julius II. Felice became a remarkable historical figure in her own right, serving as governor of the State of Bracciano and emerging as one of the most influential women in early 16th-century Rome.

Centro storico di Nerola — A compact village with medieval lanes, the Chiesa di San Nicola di Bari featuring a 13th-century Romanesque portal and bell tower, and several 16th-17th century noble palaces. The village preserves its medieval radial plan, with houses clustered tightly around the castle.

→

Views over the Monti Lucretili from Nerola's heights — The vista stretches across the wooded ridges of Sabina, the Aniene valley, and on clear days, all the way to Rome. The Parco Naturale Regionale dei Monti Lucretili extends immediately to the north, its slopes cloaked in oak and hornbeam forests.

Castello Massimo di Arsoli — A medieval fortress with origins tracing back to the 11th century, transformed into a noble residence in the 16th-17th centuries by the Massimo family, one of Rome's most ancient patrician houses, who claim descent from Fabius Maximus. The castle retains terraced gardens with centuries-old trees and a Renaissance loggia overlooking the Aniene valley. Arsoli sits at 473 m altitude on the eastern foothills of the Monti Simbruini.

Centro storico di Arsoli — A medieval village with cobbled lanes, the Chiesa di Santa Croce, and vestiges of fortifications. The village occupies a strategic position on the ancient Via Valeria, the Roman road linking Rome to the Adriatic via the Abruzzo.

→

Hiking in the Monti Simbruini — The Parco Naturale Regionale dei Monti Simbruini is Lazio's largest natural park, covering 30,000 hectares of forests, high-altitude pastures, and springs. The Aniene River rises in this massif. Marked trails depart from Arsoli toward the wooded plateaus.

Guadagnolo — Lazio's highest village at 1,218 m altitude in the Monti Prenestini, reached via a winding road from Capranica Prenestina. On clear days, the panoramic belvedere sweeps across a 180° arc covering Rome, the Tyrrhenian Sea, and the Apennines. The village's permanent population barely exceeds twenty residents.

Castel San Pietro Romano — A tiny village at 763 m overlooking Palestrina from the summit of Monte Ginestro, listed among the Borghi più Belli d'Italia. Medieval houses step along the rocky ridge, incorporating vestiges of ancient Praeneste's acropolis, with polygonal walls in opus cyclopeum identical to those of the sanctuary below.

Santuario della Fortuna Primigenia — The largest religious complex in Republican Roman Italy, built in the late 2nd century BC across seven cascading terraces on Monte Ginestro's flank. The sanctuary dedicated to Fortuna Primigenia—'Jupiter's firstborn' and oracular goddess—was consulted throughout the Empire. The oracle functioned through the drawing of wooden lots (sortes), and Cicero reported that Romans considered no site more suited to divination. The structure employed Roman concrete made with pozzolana on a grand scale for the first time, its durability proven by its preservation two millennia later. In 1944, Mussolini demolished several hundred medieval houses obstructing the complex, revealing the terraces in their current state.

Museo Archeologico Nazionale Prenestino and the Nile Mosaic — The museum, installed in Palazzo Barberini built directly atop the ancient structures, preserves on its third floor the Nile Mosaic, a Hellenistic masterpiece measuring 5.85 m × 4.31 m. Created in the 1st century BC by Alexandrian craftsmen, the mosaic depicts the Nile's course from the Ethiopian sources to the delta, populated with hippos, crocodiles, pygmy hunters, and a walled city flanked by four colossal statues. Discovered in 1507 still in place within the sanctuary, it was cut into 27 pieces in 1625 by Palestrina's bishop, transported to Rome, then returned to Palestrina by Cardinal Francesco Barberini in 1640 after several restorations. It has been displayed here since 1956.

→

Walking through Palestrina's historic center — The medieval town developed directly atop the sanctuary's ramps and terraces, such that some cellars and basements of houses still reveal Roman masonry in opus incertum. The Cattedrale di Sant'Agapito, dedicated to the 2nd-century local martyr, preserves a paleo-Christian crypt.

Palazzo Rospigliosi di Zagarolo — A 16th-century noble palace built by the Colonna family on the remains of a medieval citadel, acquired in 1629 by Camillo Rospigliosi, whose nephew became Pope Clement IX (1667-1669). The complex includes a palatine church and terraced gardens. The Porta di San Martino, a medieval tuff arch with crenellations, marks the entrance to the historic center.

Centro storico di Zagarolo — A medieval village on wooded hills with sloping lanes, several 16th-17th century noble palaces, and the 17th-century Collegiata di San Lorenzo e Santa Maria Maddalena. Zagarolo was a stop on the ancient Via Praenestina, the Roman road departing from Porta Praenestina in Rome.

→

Tasting Zagarolo DOC wines — The Zagarolo wine appellation, one of Lazio's smallest, produces whites based on Malvasia and Trebbiano cultivated on the volcanic soils of the hills. Local cellars sell directly from the production.

Lanuvio — A hilltop village at 324 m in the southern Colli Albani, with a 17th-century Collegiata dell'Assunzione and the vestiges of an ancient sanctuary dedicated to Juno Sospita. This cult was so important that newly elected Roman consuls traveled to Lanuvio to perform a sacrifice before taking office. 20th-century excavations unearthed ex-votos and coins spanning several centuries of devotion.

Nemi — A picturesque village at 521 m overlooking the crater lake from a rocky ridge, famous since antiquity for the sanctuary of Diana Nemorensis, whose priests obtained their position by killing their predecessor in single combat—a ritual documented by Strabo and Suetonius that inspired James Frazer's comparative anthropology of religions. The historic center preserves medieval lanes and the 15th-century Chiesa di Santa Maria del Popolo.

Museo delle Navi Romane di Nemi — A museum built between 1933 and 1939 specifically to house two ceremonial galleys commissioned by Caligula (37-41 AD) for Lake Nemi, measuring respectively 71.30 × 20 m and 73 × 24 m—extraordinary dimensions for lake vessels. Extracted from the lakebed in 1929-1931 after partial drainage of the lake, both original ships were entirely destroyed by fire on the night of May 31-June 1, 1944, under circumstances still debated (retreating German troops or Allied shells). The museum now displays 1:5 scale models, recovered fragments, anchors, lead pipes bearing Caligula's initials, and a partial reconstruction of the stern.

→

Walking around Lake Nemi — A small volcanic lake of 1.67 km² and 33 m depth, surrounded by oak and chestnut forests. The ring road passes the old wild strawberry farms (fragoline di Nemi), cultivated since the 15th century and celebrated annually at the Sagra delle Fragole in early June.

Ariccia — Piazza di Corte and Palazzo Chigi — A baroque ensemble designed by Gian Lorenzo Bernini for the Chigi family after they acquired the town in 1661, comprising Palazzo Chigi with its two curved wings framing the piazza, and the Chiesa di Santa Maria dell'Assunzione capped by a dome inspired by the Pantheon. Palazzo Chigi preserves a collection of 17th-18th century paintings and period-furnished apartments. To reach Ariccia's center, you cross the Ponte Monumentale, a viaduct 58 m high and 301 m long built in 1847-1854 over the Vallericcia gorge.

Frascati — Ville Tuscolane and Villa Aldobrandini — Frascati developed on the ruins of ancient Tusculum, destroyed by Roman armies in 1191. From the 16th century onward, cardinals and noble families built about a dozen country villas on its hills—the Ville Tuscolane—of which Villa Aldobrandini (1598-1603), designed by Giacomo della Porta and Carlo Maderno, is the most imposing. Its baroque-style terraced gardens with nymphaeum and cascades are open to the public. Cicero already owned a villa at Tusculum where he wrote the Tusculanae Disputationes.

Abbazia Greca di San Nilo at Grottaferrata — A monastery founded in 1004 by Saint Nilus of Rossano, a Basilian monk of Calabrian origin, fifty years before the Great Schism. It remains the last Byzantine-Greek rite monastery in Italy, still in communion with Rome. The abbey church, consecrated in 1024 by Pope John XIX, preserves a 13th-century mosaic on the triumphal arch depicting the Pentecost on a gold ground, attributed to the same workshops as Monreale Cathedral. The crypta ferrata—a Republican-era Roman burial chamber closed by iron grilles—gave Grottaferrata its name.

Palazzo Apostolico di Castel Gandolfo — The papal summer residence since Urban VIII first stayed here in spring 1626, after entrusting the conversion work to Carlo Maderno, architect of St. Peter's facade. The Ville Pontificali complex covers 55 extraterritorial hectares—a status granted by the 1929 Lateran Pacts—including 35 hectares of gardens and 20 hectares of farmland. In 2016, Pope Francis transformed the palace into a museum, ending four centuries of papal summer residence. The gardens include remains of Emperor Domitian's villa (81-96 AD).

→

Walking along Lake Albano's shores from Castel Gandolfo — The largest lake in the Colli Albani at 6 km² surface area and 170 m depth, connected to the sea since antiquity via a 4th-century BC Roman emissary still in operation. The ring road follows the shore for 10 km with views of the tuff cliffs.

→

Tasting porchetta d'Ariccia IGP in the local fraschette — Ariccia porchetta has held IGP status since 2011. It's prepared with a whole deboned pig, stuffed with rosemary, garlic, and black pepper, rolled and slow-roasted in a wood oven. The fraschette—traditional osterias where you eat elbow-to-elbow—perpetuate a simple dining tradition documented since the 18th century in the Castelli Romani.

Monte Porzio Catone — A Castelli Romani village at 451 m with an astronomical observatory inaugurated in 1938, operated by INAF (National Institute for Astrophysics). The Cantina Sociale, a wine cooperative founded in 1934, is one of the most important producers of Frascati DOC, an appellation created in 1966.

Monte Compatri — A village at 576 m among the highest in the Colli Albani, with the 17th-century Chiesa di Santa Maria Assunta and a 360° belvedere covering Rome, Lake Albano, and the Apennines. The historic center, sheltered from winds by a natural spur, preserves quiet medieval streets.

Rocca di Papa — A village at the foot of Monte Cavo, the 949 m summit of the Colli Albani and the ancient mons Albanus—sacred site of the Latins where the Feriae Latinae were held, annual festivals of the Latin federation presided over by Rome since the 6th century BC. A Roman-paved via sacra still climbs Monte Cavo's flank from Rocca di Papa. The historic center rises in an amphitheater on the slope with 17th-18th century tuff houses.

→

Hiking up Monte Cavo on foot from Rocca di Papa — A 5 km round-trip trail on the Roman paved via sacra, passing through the Parco dei Castelli Romani's oak forest to the 949 m summit where a radio antenna occupies the site of the ancient Temple of Jupiter. Panoramic views over Rome, both volcanic lakes, and the sea.

Norma and Parco Archeologico di Norba — Norma sits on the 'Rave,' a limestone spur at 433 m plunging over 400 m sheer above the Pontine Plain. One kilometer from the modern village lie the ruins of ancient Norba, founded in the 5th century BC by the Volsci as a fortress controlling the plain. The 2.5 km circuit of opus polygonale walls—massive limestone blocks dry-fitted without mortar—culminates in two acropolises. Norba sided with Marius against Sulla and was destroyed in reprisal around 82 BC; abandoned, it was never rebuilt. From the site, on clear days the panorama stretches to Nettuno, Terracina, and the Pontine Islands.

Segni — A village at 668 m in the Monti Lepini with Cyclopean walls from the Republican Roman era among the best preserved in Lazio. The Porta Saracena, a monumental arch in 3-meter-high polygonal blocks dating to the 4th-3rd century BC, was frequently reproduced by Grand Tour artists. The Cattedrale di San Pietro e Paolo preserves a 12th-century Romanesque apse.

Carpineto Romano — A village at 745 m in the heart of the Monti Lepini, a feudal holding of the Colonna from the 14th to 17th centuries, with a preserved medieval historic center of sloping lanes and several noble palaces. The 16th-century Collegiata di Santa Maria Assunta preserves an altarpiece from the Roman school.

→

Hiking the trails of the Monti Lepini around Carpineto — The limestone massif offers routes between 700 and 1,500 m altitude through beech and oak forests. The villages of Segni, Norma, Bassiano, and Sermoneta are all accessible via marked trails of the Comunità Montana dei Monti Lepini.
