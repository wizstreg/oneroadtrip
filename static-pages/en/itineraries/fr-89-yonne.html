Yonne Circuit: Northern Burgundy & Chablis Wine Country

Vines climb the terraces. Cathedral vaults echo with bells. Stone sculptures whisper prayers. The soul of Burgundy reveals itself, entirely customizable.

Auxerre commands attention with its cathedral and medieval architecture. Chablis reveals an iconic vineyard and celebrated winemaking craft. Noyers-sur-Serein enchants as a perched riverside village. Vézelay marks the history of great pilgrimages. Between wine and spirituality, the Yonne blends heritage with character.

Key highlights (5)

  • Chablis: terraced vineyards of crisp elegance
  • Noyers: riverside stone village frozen in time
  • Vézelay: basilica crowning the pilgrimage route
  • Tonnerre: river town wrapped in ramparts

Saint-Étienne Cathedral has towered over Auxerre since the 13th century. Its unique 68-meter tower can be spotted from miles away. Inside the Romanesque crypt lies the only known depiction of Christ on horseback.

Descend into the Carolingian crypt of Saint-Germain Abbey, built between 841 and 859. Its 9th-century frescoes—including the stoning of Saint Stephen—rank among France's oldest surviving wall paintings.

The Clock Tower, erected in the 15th century, marks the beating heart of the old town. Its two Gothic dials display both the hours and lunar phases—a proud symbol of Auxerre's communal independence.

Saint-Eusèbe Church, built in the 12th century, preserves a Romanesque bell tower and remarkable stained glass. It once housed Saint Germain's relics before their transfer to the abbey.

Head to Place Charles Surugue, the former pillory square. A fountain crowned by a statue of Cadet Roussel—a local bailiff turned folk-song hero—stands at its center, surrounded by colorful half-timbered houses.

Step into the Museum of Art and History, housed in the former monks' dormitories of Saint-Germain Abbey. The collections span from prehistory through contemporary times.

→

Stroll along the Yonne riverbanks in the Marina quarter. Moored barges and evening views of the illuminated cathedral and abbey create Auxerre's most iconic panorama.

💡 The view from Pont Paul Bert at sunset is the most photographed
→

Follow the 'In the Footsteps of Cadet Roussel' trail: 67 curiosities dot the old town, winding through cobbled lanes, half-timbered houses, and Renaissance façades.

💡 Brochure available at the tourist office
→

Cycle to the Caves de Bailly-Lapierre, birthplace of Crémant de Bourgogne, set in a former underground quarry spanning 4 hectares, 60 meters beneath the earth.

The Collegiate Church of Saint-Martin, built in the 12th and 13th centuries, displays a south portal adorned with 111 horseshoes nailed there by passing pilgrims. Gothic vaults shelter finely carved capitals.

Push open the door to the Obédiencerie, a 9th-century monastery where Chablis wines were born. The 12th-century cellars house an intact 13th-century lever press.

Porte Noël, flanked by two round towers, is the last remnant of the 15th-century ramparts. Chablis once counted 29 square towers and 3 gates protecting the 'lower town.'

The Cité des Climats et des Vins de Bourgogne offers an immersive journey through the history of Chablis vineyards. The visit concludes in the former monks' cellar from Pontigny with a tasting introduction.

Along the Serein River, the Tour Mirau d'Aval recalls the old eastern defenses of Chablis. Just opposite, a nine-arched washhouse reflects in the calm waters.

The Vinéa Passion museum presents a collection of antique winemaking tools gathered over twenty years of flea market finds. The space traces the evolution of Chablis viticultural practices.

→

Climb to the Aire des Clos viewing table. The panorama embraces the entire vineyard: the seven Grands Crus on the right bank of the Serein, the Premiers Crus on the opposite hillsides.

💡 Explanatory panels on appellation classifications
→

Take the 'Seven Grands Crus' hiking trail winding through Chablis vineyards. The short route (4 km, ~45 min) borders the most prestigious plots: Blanchot, Bougros, Les Clos, Grenouilles, Preuses, Valmur, and Vaudésir.

→

Visit Chablis's Sunday market. Stalls overflow with Burgundian terroir products: Chablis andouillettes, Duché biscuits, and local cheeses.

→

End the day with a tasting in a village cellar. The four appellations—Petit-Chablis, Chablis, Premier Cru, and Grand Cru—each reveal a distinct mineral character from limestone soil 150 million years old.

Noyers-sur-Serein, classified among France's Most Beautiful Villages since 1982, nestles in a meander of the Serein River. The village counts 78 buildings listed as Historic Monuments, most dating from the 15th century.

Look up at Place du Marché-au-Blé and Place de la Petite-Étape-aux-Vins. The half-timbered façades bear carved pinnacles, small columns, and stone decorations testifying to medieval commercial prosperity.

Notre-Dame Church, built between 1491 and 1515 in flamboyant Gothic style, encloses a luminous nave. It crowns the village from the castle hill.

The Museum of Naïve and Popular Arts occupies the former 17th-century college. It presents donations of naïve art, popular art works from Southern Europe, Asia, and Latin America, plus Hokusai albums.

Walk the ramparts and their 19 defensive towers, still partially visible. The Venoise Tower, called 'cave au loup,' preserves its intact vaulted room and feeds a local legend.

The ruins of the medieval castle, destroyed in the 14th century, offer beautiful viewpoints over the village below and the Serein's loop. The climb follows a short path from the center.

→

Follow the Serein riverbanks along the path starting beneath the ramparts. Reflections of half-timbered houses in the calm water create one of the village's loveliest scenes.

→

The village hosts artists' workshops and artisan studios open to the public. Galleries, potters, and sculptors occupy shops along the cobbled lanes.

→

In autumn, attend the Burgundy truffle market. Tastings and activities celebrate the 'black diamond' in a convivial atmosphere.

Fosse Dionne has intrigued visitors since antiquity. This vauclusian spring forms a turquoise pool whose known gallery plunges 61 meters deep over 360 meters. A circular half-rotunda washhouse, listed as a Historic Monument, has surrounded it since 1758.

The Hôtel-Dieu, founded in 1293 by Marguerite de Bourgogne, is Europe's longest medieval hospital. Its patients' hall stretches 90 meters beneath original timberwork. The 15th-century Entombment sculpture is preserved here.

Climb to Saint-Pierre Church, perched on the hill overlooking Tonnerre. Its architecture blends flamboyant Gothic and Renaissance. The interior houses an antique organ, an 18th-century pulpit, and polychrome stained glass.

Notre-Dame Church, in the town center, presents a mix of Romanesque and Gothic styles. Its stones bear witness to Tonnerre's different construction eras—a town destroyed and rebuilt multiple times.

The medieval-inspired gardens, adjoining the Hôtel-Dieu, feature boxwood hedges, hornbeams, and yews. Medicinal plants are identified according to their period uses.

Ten kilometers away, Château de Tanlay rises from its moat amid a vast park. This Burgundian Renaissance residence houses a trompe-l'oeil room and the League Tower adorned with allegorical frescoes.

→

The Canal de Bourgogne cuts through Tonnerre's heart. Take the towpath on foot or by bike for a waterside stroll past locks and barges.

→

Tonnerrois winemakers open their cellars for tastings. The AOC Bourgogne Tonnerre and Bourgogne Épineuil reveal a discreet but quality terroir.

→

On Wednesdays and Saturdays, Tonnerre's market offers snails, gougères, Chaourçois cheese, and Armançonnettes—local sweet specialties.

Sens Cathedral of Saint-Étienne is the world's first Gothic cathedral. Construction began in 1135, predating Notre-Dame de Paris. Its nave was the first to employ ribbed vaulting on a grand scale.

The transept's stained glass, created between 1500 and 1530, forms an exceptional ensemble. Two immense rose windows and 12th-century glass panels rival those of Chartres or Bourges in quality.

The Cathedral Treasury, one of Europe's richest, displays 15th-century Flemish tapestries, Thomas Becket's chasuble, and the Holy Shrine—a Byzantine ivory reliquary casket.

The 13th-century Palais Synodal and the former Archbishops' Palace form a complex of 4 listed buildings. The Museums of Sens present collections spanning Paleolithic to contemporary eras.

The covered market of Sens, in the town center, shelters terroir product stalls beneath a Baltard structure. The neighboring Pays d'Othe region supplies cider and apples.

Half-timbered houses and Gallo-Roman vestiges dot the old town. Sens, formerly Agedincum, was the capital of the Senones Gallic tribe—those who took Rome in 390 BC.

→

Climb the 330 steps of the cathedral's south tower. At the summit, 66 meters high, the view plunges over the glazed roofs of the Palais Synodal, the square, and the Yonne valley.

💡 Guided tour required in summer
→

The Moulin à Tan, a former tannery mill, houses a botanical park with rose garden, arboretum, and tropical greenhouses. The 'winter garden' recreates an equatorial forest atmosphere.

💡 Free entry, greenhouses open year-round
→

In summer, a free sound and light show is projected onto the cathedral façade on Friday and Saturday evenings. The portal sculptures come alive under colored lights.

Saint-Thibault Church, built between 1490 and 1529, blends flamboyant Gothic with early Renaissance influences. Its choir leans curiously to the left. Inside, admire the hanging vault keystones and Renaissance pulpit.

Château des Gondi, a Renaissance edifice, overlooks Joigny from the heights of the Saint-Jean quarter. The panorama embraces the entire town and Yonne valley.

At Place du Pilori, the Tree of Jesse House displays half-timbered carvings of Christ's genealogical tree, enhanced with polychrome ceramics. It's one of Burgundy's finest half-timbered houses.

The Bailli House, at 36 rue Montant-du-Palais, is a 16th-century tower house. Its façades bear Latin and Greek inscriptions dated 1573; its half-timbers are carved with scales and lozenges.

Saint-Jean Church, a castle chapel turned parish church in the 11th century, contains the tomb of Countess Aélis de Joigny (early 13th century) and a Renaissance Entombment of great refinement.

Cross the Pont Saint-Nicolas, long the only passage on the Paris-Lyon route. From the Baltard market halls, the view of half-timbered houses tiered in an amphitheater is striking.

→

Climb into the vineyards of Côte Saint-Jacques, an AOC Bourgogne appellation. The trail offers an open view over the Yonne valley, between forests and grain fields.

→

Wander through Joigny's three historic quarters—Saint-Thibault, Saint-Jean, and Saint-André—linked by steep lanes, stairs, and narrow passages. Each quarter has its own personality and church.

→

The river port of Les Maillotins, at the foot of the old town, welcomes boaters between the Seine, the Canal de Bourgogne, and the Yonne. Landscaped banks invite strolling.

→

The Museum of the Resistance traces the action of Groupe Bayard, created in Joigny in 1940. Documents, photos, and period objects testify to the fight against the occupier.

Château de Saint-Fargeau, a 'giant of pink brick,' unfolds its five corner towers crowned with bell turrets around a pentagonal courtyard. Its origins date to 980, when the Bishop of Auxerre built a fortified hunting lodge here.

Tour the furnished apartments of La Grande Mademoiselle, cousin of Louis XIV, exiled here in 1652. The wooden library, trophy room, and reconstruction of her bedroom transport you to the Grand Siècle.

The château's timberwork, open to the public, forms a spectacular labyrinth of beams. An attic full of old-fashioned toys is arranged under the rafters—children can handle the toys from yesteryear.

The château chapel preserves wall paintings and a grand stone staircase. The 120-hectare English-style park extends behind the ramparts.

Twenty-five kilometers away, the Guédelon construction site builds a fortified castle using only 13th-century techniques. Stonemasons, carpenters, and blacksmiths have worked before visitors' eyes since the late 1990s.

→

In summer, the château's historical pageant brings together 600 volunteers and 50 horsemen to retrace 1,000 years of history in 90 minutes. On Friday and Saturday evenings in July-August, the château blazes at nightfall.

💡 Doors open at 7 PM, show starts at 10:30 PM in July
→

Lake Bourdon, the Yonne's largest lake, spreads at the village's foot. Supervised swimming in summer, hiking trails, and fishing enliven its wooded shores.

💡 Supervised swimming in July-August
→

On Thursday summer evenings, candlelit night tours of the château reveal private apartments. Costumed characters guide visitors between legend and reality.

Colette's birthplace, in the village heart, has been restored according to the writer's own descriptions. Wallpaper, parquet, woodwork, and vegetable garden recreate the setting of her childhood in Puisaye.

The Musée Colette occupies the château rebuilt in the 17th century. It offers a sensory discovery of the writer's life: manuscripts, photographs, personal objects, and immersive artistic installations.

The Tour Sarrasine, an ovoid 11th–12th century keep, is the last vestige of the old medieval fortress. Colette evokes it in 'Claudine at School': 'the Saracen tower, thick, low, sheathed entirely in ivy, crumbling at the top.'

The village church, recognizable by its absent bell tower, stands on the main square. Dating from the 13th century, it's part of the 'Following Gabrielle's Footsteps' trail through the village.

On the road to Moutiers, the Poterie de la Bâtisse perpetuates ten generations of know-how. The old 18th-century reclining kiln and 1930 workshop are still in operation. Colette's pencil pot is among the iconic creations.

→

Take the 'Following Gabrielle's Footsteps' walk through the village. The one-hour route passes places evoked in Colette's work, from the Petit Saint-Jean washhouse to woods that 'ripple to infinity.'

→

Puisaye has been the land of potters since the Middle Ages. Visit the open workshops of Saint-Amand-en-Puisaye and discover the region's characteristic blue stoneware.

💡 Saint-Amand-en-Puisaye, 12 km away
→

The Puisaye countryside—a bocage of ponds and forests—lends itself to peaceful hikes. Sunken lanes bordered by hedges lead from village to village through a landscape Colette described as 'the most beautiful country in the world.'

💡 Several marked circuits from the village

The Collegiate Church of Saint-Lazare, founded in the 12th century, preserves a Romanesque portal of exceptional richness. Spiral columns, archivolts carved with a zodiac and labors of the months, scrollwork and Greek key patterns rival Vézelay and Autun.

The Clock Tower, erected in 1456, rises 49 meters high. A former municipal belfry, it marks the entrance to the old town. Its clock mechanism still functions.

The medieval ramparts, flanked by six circular towers, bastions, and sentry boxes, encircle the rocky promontory. The Gaujard Tower (1438) and Escharguet Tower (1522) are among the best preserved.

The Jean Després Museum of Avallonnais presents archaeological collections from Arcy-sur-Cure caves, Art Deco goldsmith works, and an ensemble of Yao people costumes.

The House of the Lords of Domecy, on the collegiate square, dates from the 15th century. Its corbelled turret houses a spiral staircase. The neighboring 1490 salt granary now hosts temporary exhibitions.

Avallon's terraced gardens, cultivated since the 10th century, descend in tiers beneath the ramparts. These green spaces offer multiple viewpoints over the Cousin valley below.

→

Descend into the Cousin valley via the Petite Porte promenade. The 70-meter elevation drop under linden trees opens a panorama over the first Morvan hills and converted old mills.

→

Follow the 'Frog Circuit,' a 3-km urban route marked by bronze studs in the ground. It passes all of Avallon's major monuments: Clock Tower, ramparts, collegiate church, and historic squares.

→

On Saturday morning, market day, the upper town comes alive. Morvan producers offer cheeses, charcuterie, and local specialties under the covered market and on the square.

The Basilica of Sainte-Marie-Madeleine, a UNESCO World Heritage site, crowns the hill of Vézelay. Its narthex tympanum, the 'Pentecost,' is a masterpiece of 12th-century Romanesque sculpture.

The basilica's Romanesque nave displays exceptional historiated capitals: over one hundred biblical and allegorical scenes carved on the columns. Summer solstice sunlight traces a path along the nave floor.

The crypt houses relics attributed to Mary Magdalene, which made Vézelay a major pilgrimage site from the 11th century. Bernard of Clairvaux preached the Second Crusade here in 1146 before King Louis VII.

Climb rue Saint-Étienne from Porte Neuve at the village base. Golden stone houses, art galleries, and artisan workshops succeed one another up to the basilica square.

Vézelay's ramparts, still visible at several points, testify to the site's strategic importance. Porte Neuve and Porte Sainte-Croix frame the hilltop village.

→

From the terrace behind the basilica, a 360° panorama opens over the Morvan, the Cure valley, and wooded hills. On clear days, the view extends for dozens of kilometers.

💡 At sunset, the light on the Morvan hills is exceptional
→

Vézelay is the starting point of the Via Lemovicensis, one of four French routes to Santiago de Compostela. Walk the first kilometers of the GR 654 through Burgundian countryside.

→

Vézelay's artisan workshops—potters, painters, sculptors—enliven the village year-round. Push open gallery doors scattered through the lanes to discover local creations.
