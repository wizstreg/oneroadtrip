Tuscany & Cinque Terre Road Trip: Renaissance Art, Chianti Hills & Coastal Villages

Where vineyard-covered hills cascade toward turquoise seas, medieval towers pierce the clouds, and villages cling to cliffs above the Mediterranean—Tuscany sings of heritage, the Cinque Terre whisper coastal poetry.

From the Leaning Tower of Pisa to the Art Nouveau ramparts of Marina di Pisa, from the wine-growing hills of Chianti to the medieval alleys of Siena, this 15-day road trip crosses Tuscany in all its depth before pivoting to Liguria. Etruscan Volterra, San Gimignano and its towers, the wild Etruscan coast between Vada and Populonia, then the five suspended villages of the Cinque Terre and the Riviera from Portofino to Camogli.

Key highlights (6)
  • Florence: Renaissance galleries and Brunelleschi's dome
  • Chianti: medieval villages and Classico DOCG vineyards
  • Siena: Palio, Torre del Mangia, and Gothic Duomo
  • Volterra: perched Etruscan city and Guarnacci Museum
  • Cinque Terre: five UNESCO villages clinging to the cliffs
  • Portofino and Camogli: Ligurian Riviera and San Fruttuoso Abbey

Bocca d'Arno - Where the Arno River meets the Tyrrhenian Sea, this engineered river mouth owes its existence to Ferdinand I de' Medici, who ordered it relocated roughly 1,500 meters north in 1606 to protect Pisa from flooding. Along the riverbank, traditional fishing nets called 'retoni' hang like portals above the water—relics of an activity that has shaped local life for centuries. On clear days, Corsica, Elba, and the island of Gorgona materialize on the horizon.

Piazzale delle Baleari and the Art Nouveau Waterfront - Marina di Pisa emerged as a seaside resort in the late 19th century when Gaetano Ceccherini commissioned the first wave of villas here after 1869. The seafront is lined with some of the finest Liberty-style buildings on the Tuscan coast, including Villa Bondi, built in 1909 on Piazza Baleari for Angelo Bondi, a Florentine terracotta industrialist. Poet Gabriele D'Annunzio stayed here and drew inspiration from the nearby pine forest to compose 'La pioggia nel pineto' (Rain in the Pine Grove), one of the most celebrated Italian poems of the 20th century.

Piazza dei Miracoli - The Leaning Tower of Pisa, begun on August 9, 1173 and completed in 1372 after two centuries of construction, stands 56 meters tall on its lowest side and weighs approximately 14,500 tons. It began tilting during construction of the third floor around 1178 because the soft alluvial soil couldn't support its weight. Stabilization work between 1990 and 2001 reduced the tilt by 44 centimeters, bringing it to roughly 4 degrees. The Cathedral of Santa Maria Assunta rises to 77 meters and towers over the campanile. The entire Piazza dei Miracoli has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1987.

Baptistery of San Giovanni in Pisa - Started in 1152 by architect Diotisalvi, who also designed the tower, the Baptistery of Pisa is Italy's largest at 54 meters high and 107 meters in circumference. Its construction spanned two centuries, which explains the coexistence of Romanesque and Gothic styles in its galleries. Its acoustic properties are remarkable: the double-shell dome creates a reverberation lasting several seconds, allowing a single voice to form a complete chord.

Lungarno of Pisa - The ochre and terracotta facades lining the Arno in Pisa form one of the most photographed urban panoramas in Tuscany. Legend has it that Galileo, born in Pisa in 1564, observed the oscillations of a chandelier in the nearby cathedral—an experience traditionally presented as the starting point of his pendulum research.

Orto Botanico di Pisa - Founded in 1544 by Cosimo I de' Medici on the proposal of botanist Luca Ghini, the Botanical Garden of Pisa is one of the world's oldest scientific gardens still in operation. It covers about 3 hectares in the historic center and houses a collection of over 3,000 plant species, including a magnolia planted in the 18th century that stands as one of Europe's oldest specimens.

→

Bike ride Marina di Pisa → Pisa via the Ciclopista del Trammino - This 13 km bike path follows the Arno along the former route of the steam tram inaugurated in 1892, which connected Pisa to the sea and ceased operation in 1932. The route passes through the pine forest of San Rossore Regional Park before reaching downtown Pisa.

Lucca - morning stop en route to Florence (Lucca sits on the A11 highway, halfway between Pisa and Florence). City Walls - Passeggiata sulle Mura: Lucca's ramparts form a 4.2-kilometer Renaissance enclosure built between 1504 and 1645. Unlike most Italian fortifications, they were never breached and remain virtually intact. Their flat crown, 30 meters wide and shaded by centuries-old plane trees, was converted into a public promenade in the 19th century. Eleven star-shaped bastions punctuate the circuit, designed to withstand artillery using the military engineering techniques of the era.

Torre Guinigi - Built in the second half of the 14th century by Paolo Guinigi, Lord of Lucca from 1400 to 1430, the Guinigi Tower rises 45 meters in red Romano-Gothic brick. Its absolute singularity is the hanging garden at its summit: seven holm oaks (Quercus ilex), symbols of rebirth in the Guinigi family tradition, grow in a soil-filled chamber 45 meters above ground. In the early 15th century, Lucca counted nearly 250 towers; the Guinigi is one of the few still standing. Local legend claims that the tallest tree was planted by Paolo Guinigi himself, and that it lost all its leaves when the lord was imprisoned by Francesco Sforza. The 230 steps lead to a panorama over the entire medieval center.

Torre delle Ore - Lucca's medieval public clock tower dates from the 14th century and was one of Tuscany's first mechanical public clocks. The climb is optional but offers a different view over the rooftops and the nearby Torre Guinigi.

San Michele in Foro - Built on the site of the Roman forum, the church of San Michele in Foro is one of the finest achievements of Pisan Romanesque architecture in Lucca. Its four-tiered facade, adorned with twisted columns, marble lozenges, and polychrome inlays, was erected between the 11th and 14th centuries. The facade is deliberately higher than the nave to impress merchants in the square.

Piazza dell'Anfiteatro - This elliptical square, unique in the world, is built on the foundations of a 1st-century AD Roman amphitheater that could hold up to 10,000 spectators. The tiers were gradually integrated into medieval houses, whose facades still follow the exact curve of the ancient arena. Four vaulted passages, heirs to the Roman entrances, are the only access points to the enclosed square.

Cathedral of San Martino - The Cathedral of Lucca preserves one of the most venerated relics of the Middle Ages: the Volto Santo, a cedar wood crucifix representing the face of Christ, which tradition attributes to Nicodemus, a disciple of Jesus. The tomb of Ilaria del Carretto (1406), a masterpiece by Jacopo della Quercia, is one of the most moving examples of Italian Gothic sculpture.

→

Bike ride on Lucca's ramparts - The complete circuit of the ramparts is 4.2 km and takes 30 to 45 minutes by bike. Rental shops are located at the main city gates.

Ponte Vecchio and Lungarno - The Ponte Vecchio owes its existence to an earlier Roman bridge built around 120 AD on the Via Cassia. Destroyed by a flood in 1333, it was rebuilt in stone in 1345 by Taddeo Gaddi and Neri di Fioravante with three arches, the largest measuring 30 meters. In 1593, Ferdinand I de' Medici, bothered by the smells of butchers and fishmongers when crossing the Vasari Corridor above, ordered that only jewelers and goldsmiths could keep shops there—a regulation still in effect today. The Vasari Corridor, built in 1565, runs above the bridge for nearly a kilometer.

Piazza del Duomo - Cathedral of Santa Maria del Fiore, Brunelleschi's Dome, Giotto's Campanile - The Cathedral of Florence, whose construction began in 1296 under Arnolfo di Cambio, is crowned by Brunelleschi's dome completed in 1436: with its 45-meter diameter and 91-meter height, it remains the world's largest brick dome. Brunelleschi had to invent new construction techniques because no contemporary knew how to close such a cupola. Giotto's Campanile, begun in 1334, reaches 84 meters and offers from its 414 steps a direct view of the dome. The Baptistery of San Giovanni, whose 'Gates of Paradise' were sculpted by Ghiberti between 1425 and 1452, is one of the city's oldest buildings.

Mercato Centrale (San Lorenzo) - The halls of the Mercato Centrale, built in iron and glass between 1870 and 1874 by architect Giuseppe Mengoni (also architect of Milan's Galleria Vittorio Emanuele), constitute one of Tuscany's liveliest covered food markets.

Piazza della Signoria - Loggia dei Lanzi - The Palazzo Vecchio, whose construction began in 1299 under Arnolfo di Cambio, was the seat of the Florentine Republic and later of the Grand Duchy of Tuscany. Its 94-meter tower is offset from the facade because it incorporates a pre-existing medieval tower. In the square, a circular plaque marks the spot where Savonarola was hanged and burned in 1498. The Loggia dei Lanzi houses Benvenuto Cellini's Perseus (1554) and Giambologna's Rape of the Sabine Women (1583).

Palazzo Pitti and Boboli Gardens - The Palazzo Pitti, whose construction began in 1458 for banker Luca Pitti on a design attributed to Brunelleschi, was acquired by the Medici in 1560. It's the largest palace in Florence with a 205-meter facade. The Boboli Gardens, laid out from 1549, constitute one of the first examples of Italian-style gardens with their geometric paths, fountains, and statues.

Oltrarno Quarter - The neighborhood situated beyond the Arno retains a more authentic atmosphere. The Basilica of Santo Spirito, designed by Brunelleschi in 1436, and the alleys around Piazza Santo Spirito concentrate artisan workshops, antique dealers, and trattorias frequented by Florentines.

→

Walking loop through Florence's historic center (Duomo → Signoria → Ponte Vecchio → Oltrarno) - The entire historic center of Florence has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1982. The loop covers approximately 3 km.

Greve in Chianti - Piazza Matteotti - Considered the gateway to Chianti from Florence, Greve has been inhabited since Etruscan times. Its triangular square, bordered by asymmetrical arcades that testify to different owners over the centuries, served as the central market for the entire region since the Middle Ages. The Castello di Verrazzano, visible in the surroundings, is linked to the memory of Giovanni da Verrazzano, navigator born here around 1485 who explored the east coast of North America in 1524 and discovered the bay now occupied by New York.

Castellina in Chianti - Via delle Volte - Perched on its hill at the historic border between Florence and Siena, Castellina possesses a 15th-century medieval fortress. Its Via delle Volte, a vaulted medieval corridor carved into the ramparts for about 150 meters, served as a defensive passage and interior route. The archaeological museum of Chianti preserves Etruscan objects from the 7th century BC found in the tumulus of Montecalvario.

Radda in Chianti - Former capital of the Chianti League - Radda in Chianti was the capital of the Chianti League, a military organization founded in the 12th century to defend the region against incursions from Siena. The League's emblem, the Gallo Nero (black rooster), is today the official label of Chianti Classico DOCG. The Palazzo del Podestà, whose facade is covered with coats of arms of successive governors, dominates the main square.

Gaiole in Chianti - Birthplace of Chianti Classico - For centuries, Gaiole was the supply market for the surrounding castles. The Castello di Brolio, property of the Ricasoli family since the 12th century, is Italy's oldest winery still in operation. It was Bettino Ricasoli, future Prime Minister of Italy, who codified the recipe for Chianti Classico in 1872.

→

Panoramic drive on SR222 Chiantigiana - The Strada Chiantigiana (SR222) connects Florence to Siena over 69 kilometers through the Chianti hills. Winding between Sangiovese vineyards, cypress trees, and sleeping castles, it traverses a territory shaped by the centuries-old rivalry between Florence and Siena.

→

Chianti Classico DOCG tasting at a winery - The cellars around Gaiole (Castello di Brolio, Castello di Ama, Badia a Coltibuono) offer tours and tastings. Chianti Classico is a DOCG requiring a minimum of 80% Sangiovese.

Piazza del Campo - Torre del Mangia - Palazzo Pubblico - The Piazza del Campo, shaped like a scallop shell sloping toward the Palazzo Pubblico, was laid out from 1293 under the government of the Nine Lords. Its red brick floor laid in a herringbone pattern is divided into nine sectors by white stone ribs. The Torre del Mangia, built between 1325 and 1348, measures 87 meters (102 with the lightning rod) in cotto (red Tuscan brick) except for the travertine crowning. Its name comes from the nickname of its first bell-ringer, Giovanni di Duccio, called 'Mangiaguadagni' because he immediately spent what he earned. The Cappella di Piazza was erected in 1352 by Sienese survivors of the Black Death. The Palio is run twice a year (July 2 and August 16) on this square covered with earth.

Duomo di Siena - Built between 1136 and 1382, the Cathedral of Our Lady of the Assumption of Siena is characterized by alternating bands of white and dark green marble. Its floor entirely covered with figurative marble marquetry—56 panels created between the 14th and 16th centuries—is one of the most ambitious achievements of the Renaissance. The Piccolomini Library (1492-1502) is accessible from the nave. In 1339, Siena had decided to enlarge its cathedral to make it the largest in the Christian world: the project was abandoned in 1348 after the Black Death.

Baptistery of San Giovanni in Siena - Built between 1316 and 1325 to support the enlarged choir of the cathedral above, the Baptistery of Siena houses the hexagonal baptismal font, the most important of the early Italian Renaissance. Its gilt bronze decoration (1417-1431) was created by Donatello, Lorenzo Ghiberti, and Jacopo della Quercia.

Basilica of San Domenico and panorama over Siena - Erected between 1226 and 1465, the Basilica of San Domenico preserves the relics of Saint Catherine of Siena (1347-1380), patron saint of Italy and Doctor of the Church. Her mummified head is displayed in a side chapel. Set on a promontory, the bare brick facade offers one of the most striking views of the Duomo and medieval rooftops.

→

Wandering through Siena's contrade - Siena is divided into 17 contrade (historic neighborhoods), each with its own animal emblem, church, museum, and colors. The oratories and fountains of the contrade, the emblems painted on walls, and the flags testify to an identity transmitted from generation to generation.

Siena - Palazzo Pubblico and Museo Civico - The Palazzo Pubblico, built between 1288 and 1342, is one of Italy's finest Gothic communal palaces. Its Museo Civico preserves Ambrogio Lorenzetti's Allegory of Good and Bad Government (1338-1339), considered the first secular representation of a city and countryside in Western painting. The Sala del Mappamondo houses Simone Martini's Maestà (1315).

Monteriggioni - Ramparts, 14 towers, and Piazza Roma - Monteriggioni was founded between 1213 and 1219 by the Republic of Siena to watch over the Florentine border. Its ramparts of approximately 570 meters perimeter, pierced by two gates, are flanked by 14 square towers still standing. The walls reach 10 meters high. Dante Alighieri evoked these towers in the Inferno (canto XXXI) to describe the giants guarding the entrance to the last circle. In 1554, Captain Giovanni Zeti surrendered the fortress to the Florentines, sealing the end of the Republic of Siena. The village counts barely 50 houses within the ramparts.

Monteriggioni in Arme - This museum offers a collection of reproductions of medieval and Renaissance armor and weapons, providing insight into siege and defense techniques. The ticket includes access to both sections of the walkway.

→

Walk on Monteriggioni's ramparts - The complete circuit of the walkway is about 500 meters and offers a panoramic view over the Tuscan countryside. Monteriggioni is also a major stop on the Via Francigena, the pilgrimage route connecting Canterbury to Rome.

San Gimignano - Piazza della Cisterna and Piazza del Duomo - In the 14th century, San Gimignano counted about 72 tower-houses erected by noble families who competed in height as symbols of wealth. Fourteen remain today, including the Torre Grossa (54 m), built in 1311. The historic center has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1990. The Piazza della Cisterna takes its name from the octagonal public cistern of 1287 that still occupies its center. Dante Alighieri stopped in this town on May 18, 1300 to plead in favor of the Tuscan Guelph league.

San Gimignano - Collegiata Santa Maria Assunta - The collegiate church, a 12th-century Romanesque building, houses fresco cycles from the 14th and 15th centuries, including the cycle of Saint Fina by Domenico Ghirlandaio (1477-1478), considered among the most graceful frescoes of the Tuscan Renaissance.

Volterra - Piazza dei Priori and Palazzo dei Priori - Perched at 550 meters altitude, Volterra was founded by the Etruscans under the name Velathri in the 7th century BC. The Palazzo dei Priori, begun in 1208 and completed in 1257, is the oldest town hall in Tuscany—it's believed to have served as a model for Florence's Palazzo Vecchio.

Porta all'Arco - An Etruscan gate from the 4th-3rd century BC, the Porta all'Arco is one of the best preserved in Italy. Three heads sculpted in basalt, probably protective deities of the city, adorn its arch. During World War II, residents formed a human chain in front of retreating German soldiers to prevent its destruction.

Roman Theater of Volterra - Built in the late 1st century BC and rediscovered during excavations in the 1950s, this Roman theater is one of the best preserved in central Italy. The adjacent 2nd-century AD Roman baths complete the complex.

Museo Etrusco Guarnacci - Founded in 1761 by Mario Guarnacci, who donated his private collection to the city, this museum is one of Europe's oldest public museums. It preserves over 600 Etruscan funerary urns. The most famous piece is the Ombra della Sera (Shadow of the Evening), a bronze votive statuette with extreme elongation close to Giacometti sculptures, as well as the Urna degli Sposi representing an elderly married couple.

→

Scenic drive Monteriggioni → San Gimignano → Volterra - This route crosses the hills between the Val d'Elsa and the heights of Volterra. San Gimignano stands out on the horizon from several kilometers away, its medieval towers emerging from the Tuscan scrubland.

Castagneto Carducci - Medieval village - Perched on a hill overlooking the Etruscan Coast, Castagneto Carducci bears the name of poet Giosuè Carducci (1835-1907), Italy's first Nobel Prize winner in Literature in 1906, who spent his childhood here from 1838 to 1848. The medieval village preserves its ramparts and stone alleyways. The municipality is surrounded by vineyards producing Bolgheri and Bolgheri Sassicaia DOC wines.

Bolgheri - Viale dei Cipressi and medieval borgo - The avenue of cypress trees leading to Bolgheri from San Guido, planted in 1831, was immortalized by Giosuè Carducci in his poem 'Davanti San Guido' (1874). The cypresses, now centuries old, form a vegetal corridor nearly 5 kilometers long. Inside the medieval village, small shops sell Bolgheri DOC wines including Sassicaia, created in 1948 and which became the first Super Tuscan classified in an individual appellation in 1994.

Vada and beaches of the Etruscan Coast - Vada was the ancient port of Volterra from the 4th century BC and was mentioned in Cicero's writings. Amphorae discovered on the seabed testify to the intense commercial activity of this port. The beach of Vada, stretching 5 kilometers of fine sand, has regularly received the Blue Flag certification.

→

Walk on the Viale dei Cipressi of Bolgheri - The 4.7 km cypress avenue between San Guido and Bolgheri can be walked or cycled in both directions. The road is lightly trafficked and shaded.

→

Swimming at Vada - The beaches of Vada offer accessible swimming areas. The northern zones (Secche di Vada) are rockier and appreciated by divers; the southern zones are sandy.

Populonia - Medieval village and Etruscan acropolis - Populonia is the only one of the twelve great Etruscan cities built directly on the sea, on the Piombino promontory overlooking the Gulf of Baratti. Founded around the 6th century BC, it became the wealthiest mining city in the ancient Mediterranean thanks to iron processing extracted from Elba Island. The medieval village built on the Etruscan acropolis offers a 180° view over the gulf and the islands of the Tuscan Archipelago.

Archaeological Park of Baratti and Populonia - This 80-hectare park preserves Etruscan and Roman remains, accessible via developed trails. The necropolis of San Cerbone is the only Etruscan necropolis built by the sea. The 7th-century BC Tumulo dei Carri with its bronze chariot is particularly well preserved. Iron slag accumulated during centuries of metallurgical activity still forms black dunes on the beach.

Gulf of Baratti - The beach of the Gulf of Baratti, nestled between wooded hills and the sea, is one of the most preserved beach sites on the Etruscan coast. Submerged remains from the Roman era have been discovered offshore.

→

Castiglioncello - Baia del Quercetano and Castello Pasquini - Originally an Etruscan village (4th-3rd century BC), Castiglioncello owes its modern fame to patron Diego Martelli, who established his residence here (Castello Pasquini, 1889) and hosted the Macchiaioli painters (Giovanni Fattori, Odoardo Borrani, Telemaco Signorini), Italian precursors of French Impressionism. These artists depicted the coves, pine forest, and red cliffs of Castiglioncello in hundreds of paintings.

Coastal path Punta Righini and coves - The coastal path follows the characteristic red limestone cliffs, overlooking turquoise, shallow coves. Some coves are accessible only on foot or by swimming.

Archaeological Museum of Castiglioncello - Installed in the Palazzo Bombardieri of Rosignano Marittimo since 1996, this museum preserves collections from a necropolis of over 350 Etruscan and Roman tombs discovered in Castiglioncello, testifying to connections with the city of Volterra in the 3rd-2nd centuries BC.

Technical Naval Museum of the Italian Navy - Considered one of the world's oldest naval museums, the Museo Tecnico Navale is housed in the military naval arsenal built in 1869. Across more than 3,000 square meters, it preserves ship models, navigation instruments, 28 figureheads (the oldest dating to 1738), and a section dedicated to Guglielmo Marconi, who conducted his first long-distance wireless communication experiments in the Gulf of La Spezia in 1897.

Castello San Giorgio and Archaeological Museum - Documented from the second half of the 14th century, the Castello San Giorgio houses the Civic Archaeological Museum with collections from Lunigiana from Prehistory to the Middle Ages, notably the famous anthropomorphic statue-menhirs from the Copper and Bronze Ages. The ramparts offer a panorama over the Gulf of La Spezia.

Via del Prione and historic center - The main pedestrian artery of La Spezia's historic center is lined with arcades and 19th-century palaces. The Museo Civico Amedeo Lia, installed in a 17th-century Franciscan convent, preserves a collection spanning the 13th to 18th centuries including works by Tintoretto and Titian.

Harbor promenade and Passeggiata Morin - From the Molo Italia, a panorama extends over the Cinque Terre. The Gulf of La Spezia, nicknamed the 'Gulf of Poets' since Dante, Shelley, and Byron stayed here, is one of the largest protected natural gulfs on the Ligurian coast.

→

Logistics reconnaissance for the Cinque Terre - La Spezia Centrale station is the main departure point for the Cinque Terre. The regional train serves all five villages in a maximum of 20 minutes. The Cinque Terre Card (train + trail access) is available at the station.

Riomaggiore - Marina and stairway alleys - The southernmost village of the Cinque Terre was reportedly founded in the 8th century by Greek refugees. Its five- to six-story houses, painted in ochre, yellow, and terracotta, are built directly on rock—an ancient technique that allowed sailors to recognize their home from the sea. The Via dell'Amore, a ledge carved into the cliff during the construction of railway tunnels in the 1920s, is partially reopened.

Manarola - Belvedere and postcard view - Manarola is renowned for its production of Sciacchetrà, a sweet, amber-colored white wine obtained by drying the grapes. Its belvedere on the rocky promontory is the most famous photographic viewpoint of the Cinque Terre. The church of San Lorenzo, built in 1338, preserves a medieval rose window and bell. The giant Christmas nativity scene installed on the cliffs every year since 1961 is visible from several kilometers at sea.

Corniglia - Village perched 100 meters above the sea - Corniglia is the only one of the five villages without direct access to the sea. Perched at 100 meters altitude, it is preceded by the Lardarina, a staircase of 382 steps from the station. The 14th-century church of San Pietro is adorned with a rose window in white Carrara marble. Less crowded than its neighbors, Corniglia offers the calmest atmosphere of the Cinque Terre.

→

Cinque Terre Card train between villages (south) - The regional train connects La Spezia to Riomaggiore, Manarola, and Corniglia in less than 15 minutes. The Cinque Terre Card allows unlimited boarding.

→

Hike Manarola → Corniglia (trail 586/506) - The route takes the heights from Manarola, crossing terraced vineyards, holm oak forest, and a pass at 360 meters before descending to Corniglia. Allow 2h30-3h.

Vernazza - Port, Piazza Marconi, and Castello Doria - Vernazza is regularly cited among Italy's most beautiful villages. Its natural port, protected by a medieval breakwater, is the only true mooring harbor of the Cinque Terre. The Castello Doria, built in the 11th century on the rocky ledge, is one of the oldest fortifications of the Cinque Terre and offers the most iconic view of the village and harbor.

Monterosso al Mare - Medieval borgo and Fegina beach - Monterosso is the largest of the five villages, divided into two parts: the medieval borgo (Colle di San Cristoforo) and the beach resort of Fegina, which has the only true sandy beach of the Cinque Terre. The statue called Il Gigante, a 14-meter reinforced concrete sculpture representing Neptune, was erected in 1910. The 14th-century church of San Giovanni Battista in Ligurian Gothic style striped with white and green marble is the largest in the Cinque Terre. The hinterland of Monterosso produces the Monterosso lemon, a local variety used for limoncino.

Santa Margherita Ligure - Port and waterfront - Santa Margherita Ligure developed its tourist vocation from the 19th century when aristocrats from across Europe discovered its Gulf of Tigullio. The town was long known as Pescino before being renamed in 1863. Its marina, Belle Époque villas, and tree-lined promenade form the backdrop of a resort that maintains its elegance without Portofino's ostentation. The town is included in the Portofino Regional Natural Park, created in 1935.

Portofino - Piazzetta and harbor - Portofino, founded in the 10th century as a simple fishing port, became in the 19th century one of the most sought-after destinations on the Italian Riviera. Its Piazzetta, surrounded by pastel houses overlooking the horseshoe-shaped harbor, concentrates cafés, boutiques, and prestige yachts. Lord Byron swam in the bay. The Castello Brown, a medieval fortress renovated in the 16th century and purchased by British consul Montague Yeats Brown in the 19th century, offers the most complete panorama over the village.

Paraggi - Protected bay and beach - Nestled between Santa Margherita and Portofino, the bay of Paraggi is one of the rare sandy beaches on this Ligurian coast. Its clear, shallow waters, protected by the Portofino promontory, make it one of the most frequented swimming spots on the Riviera.

→

Coastal trail Santa Margherita → Portofino via Paraggi - This 5.3 km trail follows the coast overlooking the sea, passing through the bay of Paraggi before descending to Portofino. Elevation gain of only 47 meters: one of the most accessible coastal routes on the Riviera. Local boats provide the return service.

Camogli - Port and waterfront - Camogli is one of the most preserved fishing villages on the Ligurian Riviera. Its name reportedly comes from 'Casa dei Mogli' (house of wives). Its five- to six-story houses, painted in bright colors to be visible to sailors, overlook a lively harbor. The Basilica of Santa Maria Assunta, built directly at the water's edge on rocks, was rebuilt several times after storms. Camogli is known for the Sagra del Pesce, fish festival celebrated on the second Sunday of May since 1952, when thousands of sardines are fried in a giant 4-meter diameter pan.

Abbey of San Fruttuoso - Accessible only by boat or on foot, the abbey occupies a secret cove between Camogli and Portofino. Its foundation dates to the 7th century, when Greek monks fleeing the Muslim conquest of Hispania brought the relics of Saint Fructuosus of Tarragona. The current structure dates largely from the 11th century, built at the request of Adelaide of Burgundy, widow of Emperor Otto I. In the 13th century, the Doria family added the Gothic cloister and family tombs in black and white marble. A defensive tower was erected in 1562 to repel pirates. In 1954, the Christ of the Abyss statue was submerged at 17 meters depth—a 2.5-meter bronze created by Guido Galletti in memory of diver Dario Gonzatti who died here in 1947. The FAI has managed the site since 1983.
