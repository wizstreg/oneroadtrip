The Château de Fontainebleau commands 130 hectares of parks and gardens. Eight centuries of monarchs lived here continuously, from the 12th to the 19th century. With 1,500 rooms, it's the most furnished royal palace in France.

Walk through the François I Gallery, 60 meters long. The frescoes and stuccowork by Rosso Fiorentino depict mythological scenes commissioned by the king to transform Fontainebleau into a "new Rome".

The Napoleon I Museum occupies the Louis XV wing. Uniforms, weapons, imperial jewels, and personal items trace the emperor's life—he called the château "the true home of kings, the house of centuries".

Step into Empress Eugénie's Chinese Museum. Cloisonné vases, jade, and porcelain form an Asian cabinet of curiosities installed during the Second Empire.

The Imperial Theatre, commissioned by Napoleon III and inaugurated in 1857, was rediscovered after 150 years of neglect. Its refinement echoes that of the Versailles opera house. Accessible by guided tour only.

Descend to the Court of Honor, known as the "Farewell Court". The horseshoe staircase marks the exact spot where Napoleon bid farewell to the Imperial Guard on April 20, 1814, before his exile to Elba.

→

Stroll through the English Garden, landscaped under Napoleon I, then follow the Grand Canal dug under Henri IV. Rowboats let you glide across the water facing the château.

💡 Free access to gardens
→

The Fontainebleau Forest stretches across 22,000 hectares. Its sandstone boulders are the world reference for bouldering, drawing climbers from across the globe. Over 30,000 problems across 270 sectors are mapped, from beginner to expert.

💡 Bring a crashpad or hire a local guide
→

Follow the marked forest trails: the GR 1, 2, 11, and 13 cross the massif. Landscapes alternate between sandstone chaos, oak groves, and sandy plateaus.

The Château-Musée de Nemours rises above the Loing River. Built in the late 12th century by Gauthier I de Villebéon, it's one of the rare medieval urban castles still standing in Île-de-France. Its rectangular keep is flanked by four corner turrets.

Climb the turret to discover the oratory, a Gothic architectural gem on two levels. The château houses 25,000 objects, from 19th-century paintings to antique prints.

The Saint-Jean-Baptiste Church, founded in 1170, was rebuilt after the 1358 fire during the Hundred Years' War. The choir dates from the 15th century. The stained glass windows and high altar were added in the 19th century.

The Prehistory Museum of Île-de-France occupies a building designed by Roland Simounet, classified as 20th-century heritage. The collections span 500,000 years of human history, from early hunters to Gallic metalworkers. A 9th-century Carolingian boat completes the tour.

Head to the banks of the Loing from the château. The picturesque streets of Nemours preserve the charm of an ancient town, with stone bridges and medieval facades.

→

Extend your museum visit with the Nemours communal forest loop. This marked trail crosses a sea of sand, old quarries, and strangely shaped boulders.

→

Canoe down the Loing from Grez-sur-Loing to Moret-sur-Loing. The river winds between willows, meadows, and artists' villages.

💡 Must know how to swim, ages 7 and up
→

The nearby village of Larchant deserves a detour for its spectacular Saint-Mathurin church ruins and the Dame Jouanne rock, one of the highest bouldering spots in the massif.

Pass through the Porte de Bourgogne, remnant of the 1,400 meters of fortifications erected by Philippe Auguste in the 12th century. Its defensive systems—portcullis, murder hole, and machicolations—remain visible.

The bridge over the Loing, immortalized by the Impressionist painter who lived here his last twenty years, offers one of the most photographed views in town. Enameled lava plaques mark the exact spots where he set up his easel.

Notre-Dame Church, Gothic from the 13th to 15th centuries, was painted fourteen times by the same artist. Its two-tower facade frames a richly sculpted portal.

The keep, remnant of the royal castle, has dominated the town since the 12th century. The ramparts still preserve several turrets and the Porte de Samois, the town's second fortified entrance.

Step into the Maison du Sucre d'Orge (Barley Sugar House). This confection, created in 1638 by Benedictine nuns, has been made here for nearly four centuries using a closely guarded secret recipe.

The so-called François I Gallery, dating from 1527, features a Renaissance facade adorned with detailed sculptures. Ancient facades line the town's cobbled streets.

→

Follow the "In Sisley's Footsteps" trail: a route marked by enameled plaques at the locations where the artist set up his easel. Reproductions of his paintings are visible on-site.

→

Cycle along the banks of the Loing. A bike path connects Moret-sur-Loing to Souppes-sur-Loing following the Loing Canal towpath.

→

Visit the Halle de Moret-sur-Loing on market days to taste local Brie specialties: valley cheeses, forest honey, and barley sugar drops.

The Auberge Ganne, heart of the Barbizon Painters Museum, welcomed generations of artists between the 1820s and 1860s. On the ground floor, the dining rooms and grocery store recreate the atmosphere with period furniture and panels decorated by the artists themselves.

Climb to the inn's first floor: bedroom walls preserve drawings, paintings, and humorous graffiti applied directly by visiting artists. The permanent collection brings together a hundred works.

Théodore Rousseau's house-studio, where the landscape painter lived his last twenty years until his death in 1867, hosts temporary exhibitions alternating with displays related to the painter's work.

Wander down Barbizon's Grande Rue, a former woodcutters' hamlet that became an artists' colony from 1830. Art galleries and shops occupy the old stone houses.

At the forest edge, painters found their subjects among centuries-old oaks and sandstone chaos. The same intact landscape still inspires artists who come to set up their easels today.

→

Join an open-air sketching workshop, regularly organized by the museum. Charcoal or watercolor in hand, set up facing the same landscapes that inspired the Barbizon School.

→

Take forest trails from Barbizon to reach the Apremont boulders, one of the most accessible climbing sites in the massif. Color-coded circuits indicate difficulty levels.

→

Explore Barbizon's contemporary art galleries. The village maintains an active artistic life, with open studios and outdoor exhibitions.

The Château de Vaux-le-Vicomte was born from the collaboration of three artists in service to Louis XIV's finance superintendent: architect Le Vau, landscape architect Le Nôtre, and painter Le Brun. This 17th-century masterpiece directly inspired the Palace of Versailles.

On the ground floor, the reception salons, library, and king's chamber reveal grandiose decor: exceptional tapestries, period furniture, and ceilings painted by Le Brun.

Climb to the château's dome for a panoramic view over the 33 hectares of formal gardens. The perspective drawn by Le Nôtre extends over three kilometers to the Hercules statue.

In the basement, the period kitchens and cellars remain intact. A permanent exhibition traces Le Nôtre's genius using a large audiovisual model of the garden.

The Carriage Museum assembles coaches, carriages, and harnesses from the 17th to 20th centuries. The collection illustrates the art of travel and aristocratic transport.

→

Tour the formal gardens by electric cart. Flower beds, boxwood embroidery, pools, and fountains unfold across 33 hectares enclosed by a 13-km perimeter wall.

💡 Rental on-site
→

On summer Saturday evenings, 2,000 candles illuminate the gardens for candlelit evenings. A gold and silver fireworks display concludes this magical nocturnal promenade.

💡 Booking recommended, bring a picnic
→

Walk to the Hercules statue at the top of the perspective. The viewpoint embraces the garden's different terraced "plateaus" down to the château: the perfect picnic spot with a view.

The Tour César, a 12th-century keep, is unique in France: an octagon set on a square base, raised on an artificial mound. From the top, the panorama embraces the medieval town and the Brie plain.

The upper town's ramparts, 1,200 meters long with 22 varied towers, were erected from 1226 to 1314 to protect the wealth of the Champagne Fairs. A walkway runs along their length.

Descend into Provins' underground passages. These galleries carved in limestone served as merchant cellars during the fairs. The guided tour crosses vaulted rooms with sculpted capitals, in groups of 25. Constant temperature: 13°C.

The Grange aux Dîmes, a 13th-century merchant house once rented to Toulouse traders, recreates the atmosphere of the Champagne Fairs. An audio-guided exhibition presents the trades and merchants of the era.

The Saint-Quiriace Collegiate Church, begun in the 12th century, was never completed. Its Gothic choir and 17th-century dome create a striking contrast with the unfinished nave. The Place du Châtel, at the church's foot, was the heart of comital power.

The Saint-Ayoul Priory, whose forecourt hosted the first fairs born from pilgrimages, preserves a thousand-year-old architectural ensemble with its crypt and Romanesque sculpted capitals.

→

Watch "Les Aigles des Remparts" (Eagles of the Ramparts): equestrian falconry at the foot of the fortifications. Eagles, falcons, and horses recreate medieval life in 50 minutes.

→

"The Legend of the Knights," a chivalry show in the ramparts' moats, features sword fights, jousting, and equestrian stunts.

→

The Provins Rose Garden spreads across three hectares at the foot of the upper town. The Provins rose, reputedly brought from Damascus by a Count of Champagne, appears in jam, crystallized petals, and rosewater.

→

Wander through the upper town's streets to the half-timbered houses. Artisans offer medieval specialties: honey, hypocras, and Provins rose products.

The Commanderie des Templiers de Coulommiers, built between the 12th and 15th centuries, is the best preserved north of the Loire. The rectangular courtyard serves the Tithe Barn, dovecote, commander's lodging, and Sainte-Anne Chapel.

Enter the Sainte-Anne Chapel: its 13th-century wall paintings are among the rare preserved medieval decorations in the region. A permanent exhibition traces the history of the Knights Templar.

The Parc des Capucins, considered one of the most beautiful parks in Île-de-France, occupies the site of the former château of the Duchess of Longueville, who inspired "La Princesse de Clèves". The château remnants and Notre-Dame-des-Anges Church are classified Historic Monuments.

The Capucins Municipal Museum, housed in the former convent chapel, preserves the last vestiges of the tannery industry that made Coulommiers prosper for 800 years.

The Halle aux Fromages (Cheese Market Hall), labeled Heritage of Regional Interest, was built in 1887 in cast iron modeled after Paris's Les Halles. It was the trading and sales place for Brie cheeses.

→

Walk along the Grand Morin's brassets, small canals created in the Middle Ages by Champagne tanners. Thirty-six bridges and footbridges span these waterways that earn Coulommiers the nickname "Little Venice".

→

The Commanderie's medieval garden recreates a vegetable patch, medicinal plants, and cut flowers cultivated in the Middle Ages. A 4,000-square-meter space to explore at your own pace.

→

Taste Coulommiers cheese at one of the local fromageries. Smaller and creamier than Brie de Meaux, this soft cheese is best enjoyed when aged on-site.

💡 The Cheese and Wine Fair takes place each spring over four days

The Saint-Étienne Cathedral of Meaux, begun around 1175, displays four centuries of Gothic styles layered: classical, rayonnant, and flamboyant. Five richly sculpted portals frame a central tympanum depicting the Last Judgment. The vaults reach 33 meters.

Inside, look for the 14th-century Crucifixion stained glass window above the choir, the great 15th-century rose window, and Bossuet's tomb—the famous 17th-century bishop and orator nicknamed "the Eagle of Meaux".

The Bossuet Museum occupies the former episcopal palace, built from the 12th to 17th centuries. Its vaulted rooms house a collection of 17th and 18th-century paintings. Wood paneling, paved ramp, and garden views make the site remarkable.

The Bossuet Garden, attributed to Le Nôtre, extends over 8,500 square meters in the shape of an episcopal mitre. Boxwood parterres, a pool, centuries-old linden trees, and views of the Gallo-Roman ramparts make it one of the finest formal gardens in Île-de-France.

The Museum of the Great War of the Pays de Meaux, inaugurated in 2011, is one of the largest museums dedicated to World War I in Europe. It stands on the very spot where the German advance was halted in September 1914.

The Gallo-Roman ramparts, erected in the late 3rd century, line the Boulevard Jean Rose for 250 meters. Their brick chains alternating with small stone courses are characteristic of late Roman architecture.

→

Visit the Maison du Brie de Meaux for a discovery tour: from milk collection to a recreated 19th-century aging cellar. The visit concludes with a tasting of the "king of cheeses".

→

Walk along the Marne and Ourcq Canal. The Parc naturel du Pâtis, one of the largest urban parks in Île-de-France, offers hiking trails, waterways, and birdwatching.

→

In summer, the Grand Spectacle historique de Meaux brings together over 500 costumed actors to retrace the town's history outdoors.

The Château de Champs-sur-Marne, an 18th-century gem, illustrates aristocratic living under Louis XV. Its furnished and decorated interiors—painted paneling, chinoiserie, and refined furniture—are among the best preserved in Île-de-France.

The formal garden, attributed to Claude Desgots, nephew of Le Nôtre, unfolds its embroidered parterres facing the château. Geometric perspectives frame pools and sculpted topiaries.

The English-style landscape park, laid out in the 19th century, contrasts with the formal garden's rigor. Large centuries-old trees border rolling lawns and winding paths.

The château has served as a backdrop for numerous historical films and series. Its salons, staircases, and gardens recreate the Enlightenment atmosphere.

→

Picnic in the château park, beside the Marne. The shaded lawns offer a peaceful setting to conclude your Seine-et-Marne circuit.
