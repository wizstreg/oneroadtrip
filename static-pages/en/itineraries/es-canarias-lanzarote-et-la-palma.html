Lanzarote & La Palma: Where Fire Shaped Art and Nature

The ground opened, spat fire, burned for six years, and swallowed entire villages. Three centuries later, vines grow from ash and an artist transforms caves into galleries. This is Lanzarote. Forty minutes by air, La Palma awaits with ancient pine forests and smoking volcanoes.

The Charco de San Ginés defines Arrecife: a natural seawater lagoon in the heart of the capital, lined with whitewashed fishermen's houses. This natural harbor has existed for over six centuries—the city was born here after the Franco-Norman conquest in the 15th century. César Manrique redesigned it as a serene promenade. Walk its perimeter in early morning when the light is soft and the terraces still quiet.

Castillo de San Gabriel deserves a full hour. Built in the 16th century on a small island to defend against Berber pirates—it was first burned by the corsair Morato in 1586 before being rebuilt in stone—it now houses the Museum of the History of Lanzarote with over 3,000 artifacts. You reach it via the Puente de las Bolas, a drawbridge crowned by two stone cannonballs: this is Arrecife's postcard image. The castle was declared a national historic monument in 1972.

The Castillo de San José, perched on the bay of Los Mármoles port, houses the International Museum of Contemporary Art, founded in 1975 on César Manrique's initiative. The building itself dates to the 18th century, and its construction traces back to a legend: in 1630, a French merchant named Francisco García Santaella supposedly founded a hermitage here. The interior blends Baroque, Mudéjar, and Neoclassical styles—a typical Canarian architectural fusion. The castle's panoramic restaurant offers harbor views worth the visit alone.

End with Calle Real—officially called León y Castillo—Arrecife's main artery for centuries. You'll find the Plazuela (Constitution Square) and San Ginés Church, whose white dome dominates the square where an artisan market sets up every Saturday. The church became a parish in 1798 and was dedicated to San Ginés, the city's patron saint. The complete walk takes 2 to 3 hours.

→

Lunch around Charco de San Ginés: the area brims with restaurants serving Canarian classics—papas arrugadas with mojo verde or mojo rojo, fresh fish from the port, and malvasía wines from the island. It's also the best spot to watch fishing boats maneuver through the lagoon.

→

Swimming and relaxation at Playa del Reducto, Arrecife's main beach. It stretches over half a kilometer with golden sand and calm waters, perfect for a mid-afternoon break. The promenade bordering it is the island's busiest.

→

Late afternoon, return to stroll around Charco de San Ginés at sunset. Locals gather here for terrace drinks. If you're there on a Wednesday or Thursday, a popular market sets up around the lagoon—a chance to pick up local crafts.

Day

Yaiza

Timanfaya National Park is Lanzarote's volcanic heart—and one of Europe's most surreal landscapes. Between September 1, 1730 and April 16, 1736, dozens of volcanoes erupted successively for six years, burying 167 km² of the island, destroying 420 houses, and forcing half the population to emigrate. The 5,107-hectare park was created in 1974 to protect this intact lunar landscape. The visit begins with the Route of the Volcanoes: a 14 km bus circuit through craters, lava tunnels, and red ridges, with multilingual commentary. Arrive early—parking fills fast and the last Route of the Volcanoes entry is at 5 PM.

At Islote de Hilario, don't miss the geothermal demonstrations: staff thrust brushwood into a simple hole in the ground—it ignites within seconds. Then a bucket of water poured into a pipe erupts in a geyser of boiling steam, because the earth's crust is only two meters thick here and temperatures exceed 400°C below. At 27 meters depth, near the main crater, the thermometer reads 700°C. Since 2013, NASA astronauts have trained in this park—the basaltic soil geologically resembles Mars.

Restaurant El Diablo, designed by César Manrique above a natural volcanic chimney, is the world's only restaurant that cooks meat and fish using geothermal heat—at about 400°C, via a grill positioned over a five-meter pit. The volcanic stone and glass architecture blends into the landscape so seamlessly it's almost invisible from the road. Note that this restaurant doesn't take reservations.

→

Dromedary ride at Echadero de Camellos, just outside the park gates. Camels were long essential to Lanzarote's agriculture, and a small museum on-site traces their history. The 20-minute ride takes you up the volcano flanks—an unexpected perspective on the sea of lava.

→

Detour to Los Hervideros in late afternoon: a stretch of volcanic coastline where Atlantic waves surge into caves and chimneys formed by the 1730-1736 lava flows. The effect is especially spectacular when the sea is rough. Just nearby, El Golfo's green lagoon—an emerald lake nestled in a former crater open to the sea—offers one of the island's strangest landscapes.

→

End the day in Yaiza, considered Lanzarote's prettiest village. Its flower-filled white houses and manicured streets contrast with the lava fields surrounding it. The Iglesia Nuestra Señora de los Remedios dates to the 17th century. It's also the best sunset viewpoint over the Montañas del Fuego—the red light on black lava is stunning.

Costa Teguise

Jameos del Agua is César Manrique's first major work in Lanzarote, opened to the public in 1966. The site sits inside a lava tunnel formed by the Corona volcano's eruption about 4,000 years ago—a 6 km tube, 1.5 km of which continues under the ocean (the Atlantis Tunnel, the world's longest underwater cave). You descend through Jameo Chico to an underground saltwater lake, fed by the Atlantic, where blind albino crabs (Munidopsis polymorpha) live—1 cm long and endemic to this place alone. Don't throw coins in the water—rust kills them. The visit continues to Jameo Grande, an open-air cave with a turquoise pool and an auditorium seating 600. Book your ticket online to avoid the line.

Cueva de los Verdes lies minutes from Jameos—it's the same Corona volcanic tube, but here the roof remained intact for nearly a kilometer. The guided tour (mandatory) takes you through geological formations lit by a system designed by Manrique that enhances colors without altering the rock. The cave was used in the 17th century as a refuge by locals fleeing pirates. The visit also hides a visual surprise: prepare for a memorable optical illusion that guides never reveal in advance.

Jardín de Cactus in Guatiza is César Manrique's final major work, inaugurated in 1990—he built it in a former abandoned quarry as a vegetable amphitheater. The garden houses about 4,500 cactus specimens from 500 species originating from five continents, planted on black volcanic stone terraces. The area was historically dedicated to prickly pear cultivation for cochineal dye. At the entrance, an 8-meter cactus sculpture signed by Manrique welcomes you. Allow 45 minutes for the visit.

→

Detour to the César Manrique Foundation in Tahíche, 20 minutes from Costa Teguise. The artist built his house directly on the 1730 lava flows, integrating five natural volcanic bubbles into the architecture. Since his death in 1992, it's been a museum exhibiting his works and those of international contemporaries. This is where you best understand his philosophy: art and nature must coexist without one crushing the other.

→

Volcanic malvasía tasting at one of La Geria's vineyards, en route to Costa Teguise. This protected designation of origin produces white wine from grape varieties planted in pits dug into volcanic lapilli, surrounded by semicircular stone walls (zocos) that protect from wind. The landscape is so singular that a photo of La Geria was exhibited in 1964 at New York's MoMA in the "Architecture Without Architects" exhibition.

→

Late afternoon swim at one of Costa Teguise's beaches, Lanzarote's most family-friendly northern resort. Or head up to Haría and the Valley of a Thousand Palms—a green oasis in the northern hills, accessible in 20 minutes from Costa Teguise.

Santa Cruz de La Palma was founded on May 3, 1493 by Alonso Fernández de Lugo. In the 16th century it became Europe's third port after Antwerp and Seville—riches from the American colonies passed through here. This prosperity left a historic center classified as a Historic-Artistic Ensemble, with 17th and 18th-century palaces and mansions, colorful facades, and carved wooden balconies facing the Atlantic. Start with Avenida Marítima: the 17th-century wooden balconies, flowering and each unique, form one of the Canaries' most photogenic promenades. Note that these enclosed balconies (ajimeces) drew inspiration from Arab architecture and served to cool houses through sea breezes.

Walk up Calle Real—also called Calle O'Daly, named after an Irish banana merchant who lived there. This long cobbled pedestrian street concentrates palaces, craft shops, and small squares. Plaza de España, in the city's heart, is one of the Canaries' finest Renaissance ensembles: the arcaded town hall, public fountain, and El Salvador Church (whose origins date to 1500) form a setting seemingly preserved for five centuries. Casa Salazar, a bit further, is the city's most remarkable Baroque palace—built in the second half of the 17th century, its patio surrounded by wood-balconied galleries is open to the public through temporary exhibitions.

On the port esplanade, don't miss the Barco de La Virgen—a full-scale replica of Christopher Columbus's Santa María, housing the city's naval museum. Just beside it, Castillo de Santa Catalina, a 16th-century fortification, defended the port against corsair attacks. From the esplanade, the view of the Avenida balconies and the castle facade is Santa Cruz's iconic photo.

→

Lunch in the historic quarter around Calle Real. La Palma's gastronomy revolves around palmero cheese (PDO), palm honey, and bienmesabe—an almond cream you'll find in every dessert. Pair it with a glass of malvasía wine, produced in the island's vineyards.

→

The city is small and entirely walkable in 2 to 3 hours. Late afternoon, climb to Mirador de la Concepción for a panoramic view over the city, colorful balconies, and port. It's the best time of day: the low light ignites the yellow and ochre facades.

→

Swim in a natural pool on the coast near Santa Cruz, depending on sea conditions. Volcanic pools are numerous on La Palma's east coast—check with your hotel about conditions, as Atlantic swells can be strong.

Caldera de Taburiente is La Palma's most spectacular national park—protected since 1954, one of Spain's first. This gigantic erosion crater, 8 km in diameter and 1,500 meters deep, is covered with Canarian pine forests and cut by ravines. If you don't wish to hike, Mirador de La Cumbrecita (1,287 m), accessible by car with a permit between 8:30 AM and 4 PM, offers a vertiginous view into the caldera's interior. From up there, the walls rising to over 2,000 m give you the feeling of standing at the bottom of a natural amphitheater.

For those wanting to enter the caldera on foot, trail PR-LP 13 from Los Brecitos is the most popular (16 km, about 6 hours, -700 m elevation). It descends through Canarian pine forests to the riverbed and passes the Cascada de Colores—a 6-meter waterfall whose rocks are naturally tinted yellow, orange, and red by iron dissolved in the ferrous water of Barranco de las Angustias. The green comes from algae and moss. The spectacle changes with season and flow. Bring plenty of water—there are no services inside.

Roque Idafe, a basaltic monolith rising in the caldera's heart, was venerated by the Benahoaritas—La Palma's prehistoric inhabitants—who attributed supernatural powers to it. It stands at the confluence of the Almendro Amargo and Limonero ravines, just before the Cascada de Colores. If you hike here, take a break at this spot: the view of the caldera walls is breathtaking.

→

Guided hike into the caldera from Los Brecitos: several operators offer accompanied descents in small groups, with a 4x4 bus to reach the starting point from Los Llanos de Aridane. It's the recommended option if you don't know the trail, as ravines can be slippery and weather changes fast inside.

→

Stargazing from La Palma's heights. The island obtained UNESCO's first Starlight Reserve status worldwide in 2011—no light pollution, perfectly stable skies. Roque de los Muchachos (2,426 m), the island's summit, houses one of the world's most important astronomical observatories. Outside observatory visiting hours, the mountain roads are freely accessible for night observation.

→

Picnic at the Playa de Taburiente camping area, inside the park. This sandy riverbed with small natural pools is one of the Canaries' only permanent streams. The water is cold and clear—a welcome break after the descent.

Los Llanos De Aridane

Los Llanos de Aridane is La Palma's most populous town, set in the green Aridane valley on the western slope—a microclimate warmer and sunnier than the capital's. The historic center houses the Museum of the Street, a permanent outdoor contemporary art exhibition unique in Europe: since 1999, murals painted directly on the city's blank facades by recognized artists cover up to 140 m² of walls. Wander the alleys to find them.

The Benahoarita Archaeological Museum traces the life of La Palma's prehistoric inhabitants, the Benahoaritas—a people of Berber origin who arrived on the island before the Spanish conquest. Reconstructions of their habitat, beliefs, and crafts help understand how they lived in this volcanic environment before being expelled in the 15th century.

Tajogaite volcano—also called Cumbre Vieja—erupted on September 19, 2021 and flowed for 85 days until December. The flow covered 1,218 hectares, destroyed 1,345 houses and 73 km of roads, and expanded the island by 47 hectares into the sea. From Los Llanos, Mirador de Tajuya offers the best view of the entire lava field and the new volcanic cone. Guided 5 km trails allow you to approach the crater's base from Llano del Jable.

→

Hike the Route of the Volcanoes (Cumbre Vieja) from the El Pilar refuge. This GR-131 trail follows the volcanic ridge where all the island's historic eruptions occurred—1949 (Hoyo Negro), 1971 (Teneguía), and 2021 (Tajogaite). The panorama from the ridges embraces the entire Aridane valley on one side and the Atlantic on the other. Start early: wind can be fierce and heat intense on this shadeless terrain.

→

Relaxation at Puerto de Tazacorte, a colorful fishing port 10 minutes from Los Llanos. Waterfront restaurants serve the day's catch—grouper, vieja (parrotfish), and local prawns. The sunset from the terrace facing the Atlantic is one of La Palma's finest. Boat trips to observe dolphins and whales depart from this port.

→

Tasting at Casa Museo del Vino Las Manchas, which traces La Palma's winemaking history and offers local wine tastings. The island produces malvasía, listán negro, and several Canarian grape varieties that exist nowhere else.

Los Llanos De Aridane

Fuencaliente, at La Palma's southern tip, is the territory of the island's most recent volcanoes. San Antonio volcano (430 m) erupted in 1677 and its crater is freely accessible with an interpretation center tracing Canarian volcanic history. Just beside it, Teneguía spewed lava as recently as October 1971—less than 55 years ago. You can walk on its still-warm flanks and observe vegetation slowly recolonizing the lava. Don't miss Fuencaliente lighthouse at the cape's end: from the point, you have a panorama of both island coasts simultaneously.

Fuencaliente's vineyards merit a stop. As in Lanzarote, vines grow here in volcanic soils on terraces—but the landscapes are even more dramatic because Teneguía's lava fields descend right to the vineyards. The La Palma appellation produces volcanic malvasía and red wines from listán negro whose reputation far exceeds the island.

If weather permits, climb to Roque de los Muchachos (2,426 m), the island's highest point. The road winds through Canarian pine forests and alpine zones to the summit, where domes of the astrophysical observatory stand—one of the world's most advanced observation centers, benefiting from the island's low light pollution. On clear days, you can spot Tenerife's Teide from the summit.

→

Swimming in the natural pools of La Fajana or Charco Verde, on La Palma's west coast. These seawater basins carved into black volcanic rock are the island's most photogenic swimming spots—the contrast between lava black, water blue, and foam white is striking. Check weather before departing, as Atlantic swells can close certain pools.

→

Market or end-of-trip stroll in Los Llanos de Aridane. The town is also the finish line of the Transvulcania, one of the world's best mountain races. The lively pedestrian center and cafe terraces offer a final immersion in local life before heading to the airport.

→

Closing lunch with La Palma specialties: potaje de berros (watercress soup), pork in mojo sauce, and bienmesabe for dessert. The island is renowned for exceptional agricultural products—avocados, bananas, and palmero PDO cheese that you can bring home vacuum-sealed.
