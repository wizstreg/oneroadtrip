A canal dug by prisoners of war, the castle where forty kings rest, ships sunk to protect the city: Denmark tells its story through stone and fjords.

Two days in Copenhagen for Nyhavn, Rosenborg, and Tivoli, then Hamlet's castle in Helsingør, the baroque gardens of Frederiksborg, and the five Viking ships of Roskilde. A compact loop that concentrates the best of Denmark in five days—with an Andersen and LEGO extension for families.

  • Helsingør: Hamlet's UNESCO-listed castle
  • Hillerød: baroque gardens and national museum
  • Roskilde: Viking ships and royal cathedral

Nyhavn, the canal dug between 1671 and 1673 by order of King Christian V, using Swedish prisoners of war as labor. The colorful houses on the north side belonged to merchants who made their fortune from maritime trade. The oldest, at number 9, dates back to 1681. Hans Christian Andersen lived successively at numbers 20, 67, and 18—it was at number 20 that he wrote his first fairy tales. By the 1960s, the neighborhood was crumbling and some wanted to demolish it entirely; local residents saved it by restoring the facades and bringing historic ships back to the quay.

Rosenborg Castle, built between 1606 and 1633 by King Christian IV as a summer residence. This red-brick palace in Dutch Renaissance style became the king's favorite dwelling—he requested to be brought here to die in 1648, transported by sleigh from Frederiksborg. Since 1838, it has housed the Danish Crown Jewels. In the vaulted cellars, four parures of diamonds, rubies, pearls, and emeralds are displayed. The Knights' Hall contains the ivory throne and three life-size silver lions. A curious detail: beneath the Winter Chamber ran acoustic conduits that allowed musicians hidden in the basement to send their music up into the room above—guests believed the music appeared out of nowhere.

Christiansborg Palace, home of the Danish Parliament on the island of Slotsholmen, has burned down twice—in 1794 during a court ball when candles set fire to the decorations, and again in 1884 during renovation work. The current building, in gray granite, was completed in 1928. Climb to the top of the tower (106 meters) for a 360° view of the city—it's the only vantage point from which you can see the harbor, copper roofs, and modern districts all at once.

→

Bike ride through central Copenhagen—the city has over 390 km of marked cycle paths. Bike rental shops are plentiful near the main stations.

→

Canal tour departing from Nyhavn—about 1 hour of navigation past the Opera House, Christianshavn island, and along the Royal Library nicknamed the 'Black Diamond.'

Tivoli Gardens, opened on August 15, 1843 by Georg Carstensen after convincing King Christian VIII by saying: 'When people amuse themselves, they don't think about politics.' Tivoli is the second-oldest amusement park in the world—the first, Dyrehavsbakken, is located 12 km north of Copenhagen. Walt Disney drew direct inspiration from it when creating Disneyland. The wooden roller coaster Rutschebanen, built in 1914, still operates with a brakeman on board for each descent—one of only seven of its kind remaining in the world. In summer, rock concerts, jazz, and pantomime shows are performed in the park.

The Little Mermaid (Den Lille Havfrue) on the Langelinie promenade, a 1.25-meter bronze sculpture perched on a rock since 1913. She has been beheaded twice, in 1964 and 1998, and had her right arm torn off in 1984. Continue along the Øresund to Amalienborg Palace to watch the changing of the guard (noon).

Freetown Christiania, an autonomous neighborhood founded in 1971 by squatters in a former military barracks. Around 900 people live here according to their own rules. The main street, Pusher Street, is the only zone in the neighborhood where photography is prohibited. The colorful buildings, artisan workshops, and communal spaces make it a place unlike any other.

→

Torvehallerne market, the large covered hall with twin pavilions near Nørreport, for lunch on-site or gourmet shopping—smoked fish, Danish cheeses, smørrebrød.

Frederiksborg Castle in Hillerød, built between 1602 and 1620 by Christian IV, is the largest Renaissance castle in Scandinavia—set on three islets in the middle of a lake. The king demolished his father's manor to build this palace with towers and spires in red brick. A fire in 1859 destroyed much of the interiors, but brewer J.C. Jacobsen (founder of Carlsberg) financed the restoration. Since 1878, the castle has housed the Museum of National History: 500 years of Danish history through royal portraits, tapestries, and period furniture. The chapel from 1617 survived the fire and remains the coronation site for Danish monarchs.

The Baroque Garden at Frederiksborg, created in the 1720s and restored to its original design in 1996, descends in four terraces toward the lake with a central cascade. The precisely trimmed hedges form the royal monograms of four sovereigns in boxwood. The baroque architects used an optical illusion: the paths narrow as they descend to make the garden appear larger than it actually is.

Kronborg Castle in Helsingør (Elsinore), listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2000. It's here, at the narrowest point of the Øresund (4 km separates Denmark from Sweden), that Danish kings collected passage tolls—the 'Sound Dues'—from all ships entering the Baltic Sea starting in the 15th century. The original fortress dates back to 1420. Frederick II transformed it into a Renaissance castle between 1574 and 1585. A fire in 1629 destroyed everything except the chapel; Christian IV rebuilt it. In 1658, the King of Sweden occupied it and plundered its treasures. Shakespeare probably never visited Kronborg, but accounts of the extravagant festivities held there spread across Europe—they inspired him to set Hamlet here. In the underground casemates, a stone statue depicts Holger the Dane, a legendary knight said to awaken if Denmark is threatened.

→

Walk along the ramparts of Kronborg with views over the Øresund strait and the Swedish coast. In summer, international troupes including the Royal Shakespeare Company perform Hamlet outdoors in front of the castle.

Roskilde Cathedral, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1995. Its brick construction began around 1170, making it the first Gothic brick cathedral in Scandinavia—a considerable technical challenge at the time. With its vaults rising 25 meters high, it served as a model for numerous churches around the Baltic Sea. Forty Danish kings and queens are buried here, including Margaret I, the queen who unified the three Scandinavian kingdoms in the 14th century. The tomb of Queen Margrethe II (who passed away in 2025) is already in place.

The Viking Ship Museum, built in 1969 facing the Roskilde Fjord to house the five Skuldelev Viking ships, excavated in 1962. These vessels had been intentionally sunk around 1070 to block the fjord channel and protect Roskilde from enemy attack by sea. The five wrecks represent five different ship types: two warships, a large merchant vessel, a fishing boat, and a small cargo boat. In 1997, excavations to expand the museum's shipyard uncovered nine additional medieval ships—including Roskilde 6, the longest Viking ship ever discovered, measuring 37 meters.

→

Rowing or sailing excursion on a reconstructed Viking ship from the museum harbor (from May to late September, weather permitting). Tickets are sold on-site in the morning.

→

Stroll along Roskilde harbor, with views of the fjord and the reconstructed Viking ships moored at the museum quay.

The National Museum of Denmark (Nationalmuseet), housed in the former Crown Prince's Palace. The collection spans 14,000 years of Danish history, from prehistoric humans to the 1980s. Don't miss: the Trundholm Sun Chariot (circa 1400 BC), a bronze wheel bearing a gold disc representing the sun—one of the most fascinating objects from the European Bronze Age—and the bronze Viking horns, the lurs.

The Round Tower (Rundetårn), built by Christian IV between 1637 and 1642. At 34.8 meters tall, it's the oldest functioning astronomical observatory in Europe. You ascend via a 209-meter spiral ramp—no stairs—designed to allow a horse to pull a cart to the top. Peter the Great of Russia rode up on horseback in 1716.

→

Exploration of the Nørrebro and Vesterbro neighborhoods—the real living quarters of Copenhageners, far from typical tourist circuits. Independent cafés, street markets, local designer boutiques.

Hans Christian Andersen's birthplace in Odense, in the small yellow house on Hans Jensens Stræde where he was born in 1805. The new immersive museum opened in 2021, designed by Japanese firm Kengo Kuma, recreates the universe of the fairy tales through light installations and labyrinthine spaces—a journey for children and adults through The Little Mermaid, The Snow Queen, The Ugly Duckling.

St. Canute's Cathedral in Odense (Sankt Knuds Kirke), built between the 13th and 15th centuries in late Gothic style. The crypt holds the remains of King Canute IV, canonized in 1101 after being assassinated in St. Alban's Church in Odense in 1086 by peasants revolting against his taxes. The gilded wooden altarpiece from 1521 is considered one of the finest examples of late Gothic sculpture in Scandinavia.

Den Fynske Landsby, the open-air museum on the edge of the city, recreates an 18th-19th century Funen village with about twenty authentic buildings relocated from the region. Farms, windmill, forge, worker's cottage. In summer, traditional craft demonstrations bring the site to life.

→

Walking tour through Odense's pedestrian city center, following the footprints drawn on the cobblestones that lead to addresses connected to Andersen's life.

LEGO House in Billund, opened in 2017 facing the brand's headquarters. The building shaped like a giant stack of bricks covers 12,000 m². Inside, five colored zones offer building experiences around creativity, logic, emotions, and storytelling. The centerpiece: a fountain made of 25 million LEGO bricks constructed for the opening. It was in Billund that Ole Kirk Christiansen created the first wooden LEGO brick in 1932—the name comes from the Danish expression 'leg godt' (play well).

→

LEGOLAND Billund, the original park opened in 1968, right next to the LEGO factory. Miniland recreates world monuments in bricks at 1:20 scale. Plan a full day with children.

❓ Frequently asked questions

What weather should you expect?

Summer (June-August): Warmest weather, longest daylight hours, peak tourist season, highest prices, perfect for cycling and outdoor activities.

Spring (April-May): Pleasant temperatures, blooming flowers, fewer crowds, moderate prices, excellent for sightseeing and cultural activities.

Fall (September-October): Comfortable weather, autumn colors, fewer tourists, good value, cozy indoor attractions, harvest season.

Winter (November-March): Cold and dark, Christmas markets, hygge season, lowest prices, indoor cultural experiences, northern lights possible.

How many days should I plan?

We believe you will enjoy this itinerary best by dedicating between 6 and 8 days. The next step will allow you to adjust the duration of your stay.

What to discover in Denmark?

Hygge lifestyle with stunning coastlines, Viking heritage, and modern design excellence. Experience Copenhagen's cultural scene, fairy-tale castles, and sustainable living practices. The country offers excellent quality of life with beautiful architecture and progressive values. Denmark provides Scandinavian charm with cultural sophistication and environmental leadership.
