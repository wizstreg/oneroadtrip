Malaysian Borneo Circuit: 12 Days Through Sarawak & Sabah

The only itinerary that combines in a single circuit the proboscis monkeys of Bako, Mulu's 3 million bats, the wild orangutans of Kinabatangan, and Asia's rarest pygmy elephants.

Kuching and its riverside proboscis monkeys, the world's largest caves at Mulu, the orangutans of Sepilok and Semenggoh, the Kinabatangan River where pygmy elephants and crocodiles coexist—twelve days on the Malaysian side of Borneo island.

Key highlights (5)
  • Bako NP: proboscis monkeys found nowhere else on earth
  • Semenggoh: semi-wild orangutans 24 km from Kuching
  • Mulu UNESCO: world's largest cave passage and Bat Exodus
  • Sepilok: world's first orangutan rehabilitation center (1964)
  • Kinabatangan: the only place to see orangutans, pygmy elephants, and proboscis monkeys together

Start with Kuching's Waterfront in late afternoon—the promenade runs along the Sarawak River facing Fort Margherita, built in 1879 by White Rajah James Brooke to defend the city against pirates. Kuching means "cat" in Malay, and feline sculptures have lined the streets since colonial times. Behind the quay, the old town preserves its 19th-century Chinese shophouses in pristine condition.

Head up to the Sarawak Museum—one of Southeast Asia's finest, according to Alfred Russel Wallace who stayed here in 1854. The Dayak, Iban, and Penan sections tell the story of Borneo's forest peoples better than any book could.

Evening calls for street food along Carpenter Street and Main Bazaar. Kuching has its own specialties: kolo mee, dry noodles with char siu pork, and umai, a Dayak ceviche of raw fish marinated in lime juice with onions and chilies.

→

Sunset river cruise on the Sarawak. Boats depart from the Waterfront and pass Fort Margherita lit up against the evening sky. It's the simplest way to see both banks of Kuching in an hour.

Leave early for Bako National Park—40 minutes by road to Kampung Bako, then a 30-minute boat ride along the coast to park headquarters. Bako is Sarawak's oldest park, gazetted in 1957, and the best place on earth to see proboscis monkeys on foot. Around 300 individuals live here in 27 km² of forest protected for 68 years—they're so habituated to humans they sometimes wander down to the cafeteria.

Take the Telok Paku or Telok Delima trail to find the proboscis—the dominant male sports a pendulous ten-centimeter nose and a belly that can weigh up to 20 kg. The gut isn't fat: it's a multi-chambered stomach that digests toxic mangrove leaves other primates can't handle. Early morning or around 5pm, they descend to the river for the night. The macaques around HQ will steal anything they can grab—keep your bag closed.

After the hike, stay for sunset on the sculpted coastline—red sandstone cliffs carved by erosion, sea arches, isolated beaches. The park contains seven complete ecosystems within 27 km², from peat swamp forest to cliff vegetation. Warning: saltwater crocodiles have been spotted in the bays in recent years—swimming is not recommended.

Take a Grab to Semenggoh Wildlife Centre, 24 km south of Kuching. Founded in 1975 as a sanctuary for orphaned or confiscated orangutans, the center now houses around thirty semi-wild individuals who roam freely across the 653-hectare reserve and return when the forest runs short of fruit. Arrive before 9am for the morning session—rangers call each orangutan by name and distribute bananas, papayas, and hard-boiled eggs on the feeding platform.

After the feeding, stay in the reserve for a hike around the center. If you visit between April and October, chances of seeing multiple individuals are excellent—during the fruiting season from November to January, the forest is their buffet and they don't bother showing up. It's a sign of successful rehabilitation, but you'll go home empty-handed.

→

Back to Kuching for one last evening in Chinatown. The evening market around Carpenter Street is quieter than the night before—take advantage of the temples open in the evening and the last Iban and Penan craft shops before you leave.

4
Day

Mulu

Mulu is accessible only by air—MASWings operates flights from Kuching, Miri, and Kota Kinabalu in 64-seat ATRs. There's no road. Since 1974, the Sarawak government has chosen not to build one to preserve the park's ecological integrity. Check into the park lodge, then take the 3 km boardwalk from HQ to Deer Cave in the afternoon.

Enter Deer Cave—the world's largest cave passage at 120 to 150 meters in diameter and 197 meters high. It held the world record until the discovery of Hang Son Doong in Vietnam in 2009. The floor is covered in guano accumulated by the 3 million bats living overhead. Follow up with Lang Cave next door, discovered in 1977 by a Berawan local named Lang Belarek whose name the cave bears—stalactites lit to perfection, total silence.

→

Stay at the observation platform outside Deer Cave from 5:30pm for the Bat Exodus. Every evening, 3 million bats emerge in spiraling columns—a giant helix that swirls for kilometers above the forest for 45 minutes. No two evenings are identical depending on weather and predators.

5
Day

Mulu

Take the longboat up the Melinau River in the morning to reach Clearwater Cave and Wind Cave—accessible only by boat or via a 4 km forest trail. Clearwater is Asia's longest cave network with 227 km of mapped passages. Wind Cave owes its name to the air currents coursing through its corridors—the King's Room inside is a chamber whose floor and ceiling bristle with stone columns joined together.

In the afternoon, walk the park's canopy walkway—one of the longest in the world, suspended several dozen meters above the forest floor. Mulu has been a UNESCO site since 2000 for its 52,864 hectares covering 17 vegetation zones. The Sarawak Chamber, closed to the public, is the world's largest natural underground chamber—600 meters long by 415 wide, or 40 Boeing 747s end to end.

6
Day

Sandakan

Arrive in Sandakan and head straight to Sepilok Orangutan Rehabilitation Centre, 25 km west of town. Founded in 1964, it's the world's first orangutan rehabilitation center—Guinness World Record for the longest-running program still in operation. Since its creation, more than 300 orphaned or trafficked individuals have been released into the wild. Arrive before 10am for the first feeding session from the forest platform.

Continue to the outdoor nursery—young orangutans in the final phase of rehabilitation learn to climb and swing before being released into the 43 km² forest reserve. You'll see adolescents play while rangers monitor from a distance. The difference from Semenggoh: there's also a nursery for the youngest ones, closed to visitors to prevent transmission of human diseases.

Follow up with the Bornean Sun Bear Conservation Centre next door—the Malayan sun bear is the world's smallest bear and the least studied of all. Rescued from the pet trade, they learn here to regain wild behaviors in forest enclosures before being released.

7
Day

Kinabatangan

Check into a lodge in Sukau on the Kinabatangan River—the only way to properly see wildlife here is from a boat, and cruises are included in all lodge packages. The river runs 560 km from the Crocker Range to the Sulu Sea, and its protected forest corridor is the only place on earth where you can encounter wild orangutans, Bornean pygmy elephants, and proboscis monkeys in the same location.

→

Sunset cruise between 4pm and 6pm—the time when proboscis monkeys descend from the trees to settle along the riverbank for the night. Harems with their dominant males gather on tributaries like the Menanggul River. Saltwater crocodiles are regularly visible from the boat.

→

Night cruise after dinner with powerful flashlights. The light makes animals' eyes glow in the darkness—civets, slow lorises, owls. Crocodiles whose eyes you see just above the waterline are particularly impressive at night.

8
Day

Kinabatangan

→

Dawn cruise from 6am—the best odds of encountering pygmy elephants are early morning when they come to drink along the riverbank. Only 1,500 remain on all of Borneo, classified as critically endangered. Endemic to Borneo, they're Asia's smallest elephants. Also look up into the trees above the water: wild orangutans sleep in nests built each evening and are visible at daybreak.

→

Forest walk with a local guide in the afternoon. Oil palm plantations encircle the river on both sides—the protected forest corridor is only a few hundred meters wide in places. The guide shows what tourist signs don't reveal.

9
Day

Kinabatangan

→

Final morning cruise on the oxbow lakes off the main river—these cutoff arms are the quietest spots for birdwatching. Eight hornbill species live along the Kinabatangan, along with several kingfisher species. Some guides combine this outing with a detour to the Gomantong Caves, Sabah's most important source of edible bird's nests.

10
Day

Kota Kinabalu

Arrive in Kota Kinabalu—Sabah's capital, nicknamed KK. The city was virtually leveled by 1945 bombing raids and almost entirely rebuilt after the war. Head up to Signal Hill Observatory for an overview of the bay and the islands of Tunku Abdul Rahman Marine Park.

Descend to the Filipino Market along the waterfront for lunch—Sabah's largest fresh seafood market. Choose your fish, crab, or prawns at the counter, and one of the stalls across the way cooks it to order. No menu, no card.

Continue to the City Mosque on the heights—nicknamed the floating mosque because it's built on an artificial lagoon that makes it appear suspended on water at sunset. Open to non-Muslims outside prayer times.

→

Sunset from the waterfront—KK is one of the few Southeast Asian cities where you can watch the sun plunge directly into the sea. Bar and restaurant terraces along the Waterfront fill up from 5:30pm.

11
Day

Kota Kinabalu

→

Take the ferry from KK port to the islands of Tunku Abdul Rahman Marine Park—15 minutes across to Sapi or Manukan. The coral reefs around Sapi shelter green turtles, clownfish, and moray eels. The marine park covers five islands less than 10 km from downtown.

🚗 90 km · 2h30
12
Day

Kota Kinabalu

Day trip to Kinabalu Park—a UNESCO site for its exceptional biodiversity. Without summiting the peak, the park is worth visiting for its carnivorous plant gardens, its giant Rafflesia—the world's largest flower, reaching up to a meter in diameter—and its montane forest trails. Mount Kinabalu at 4,095 meters is Southeast Asia's highest mountain.

Descend afterward to Poring Hot Springs, 40 km from the park. Natural pools at 40°C in the middle of tropical rainforest—the contrast after a morning at altitude is striking.

❓ Frequently asked questions

What weather should you expect?

Dry Season (March-September): Best weather for most regions with minimal rainfall, comfortable temperatures (24-32°C), and ideal conditions for outdoor activities and island visits.

Shoulder Season (February, October): Good weather with occasional showers, moderate hotel rates, and fewer crowds. Excellent balance of conditions and value for most destinations.

Monsoon Season (October-February): Regional variations with east coast affected by northeast monsoon, while west coast remains largely dry. Lower prices but weather-dependent planning required.

Year-Round Destination: Tropical climate means travel possible anytime, though regional monsoon patterns affect specific areas differently throughout the year.

How many days should I plan?

We believe you will enjoy this itinerary best by dedicating between 10 and 14 days. The next step will allow you to adjust the duration of your stay.

What to discover in Malaysia?

Multicultural paradise with incredible diversity, pristine rainforests, and vibrant cities. Experience cultural fusion, delicious cuisine, and natural beauty.
