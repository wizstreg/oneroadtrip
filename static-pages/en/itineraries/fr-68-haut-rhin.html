Haut-Rhin Road Trip: Colmar and the Southern Wine Route

Half-timbered houses mirrored in canal water, vines climbing impossible slopes. Colmar's painted facades, cheese-making traditions, village church bells ringing across the plain. Southern Alsace whispers its story in every stone—and you can shape it to fit your journey.

Drift through Colmar's Little Venice canals, explore Eguisheim—France's favorite village—taste Munster cheese in its mountain valley, and follow the Wine Route through Thann and Guebwiller. The Haut-Rhin: vineyards tumbling down to meet the Vosges.

Key highlights (4)
  • Colmar: Renaissance beauty on canal-threaded streets
  • Eguisheim: spiral village at the heart of wine country
  • Munster: mountain valley where cheese and alpine tradition meet
  • Thann: medieval ramparts guarding the gateway to wine

Little Venice follows the Lauch River where half-timbered houses in ochre, pink, and sky blue lean toward the water. Flat-bottomed boats glide beneath ancient bridges, their reflections rippling across the surface.

The Unterlinden Museum occupies a 13th-century Dominican convent. Matthias Grünewald's Isenheim Altarpiece—painted between 1512 and 1516—commands the former chapel with its haunting Crucifixion scene.

Saint Martin's Collegiate Church raises its Gothic spires above the old town. Gargoyles peer down from the 13th-century carved portal, presiding over the square below.

Look up at the Pfister House, built in 1537 by a wealthy merchant. Its octagonal turret, Renaissance frescoes, and carved wooden gallery showcase the opulence of Colmar's trading heyday.

The Tanners' Quarter lines narrow half-timbered houses along the Fishmonger's Quay. Their upper floors once served for drying animal hides, stretched between the timber beams.

→

Step into a flat-bottomed boat for a gentle glide through Little Venice's canals. Painted facades dance in the water as you drift past flower boxes spilling geraniums.

→

The covered market at Place du Marché-aux-Fruits brims with local treasures. Munster cheese, golden mirabelle plums, bottles of Gewurztraminer chilling in ice.

→

Wander the cobbled lanes of old Colmar where every corner reveals another half-timbered house painted in jewel tones. Carved wooden signs swing above doorways, and window boxes overflow with blooms.

The Munster Valley unfolds at the foot of the Vosges Ballons. High mountain pastures feed Vosgienne cows whose milk produces the pungent, orange-rinded Munster AOP cheese.

The Cheese House traces every step of farmhouse Munster production. Watch the molding demonstration in the barn, then settle in for a commented tasting paired with local wine.

Grand Ballon rises to 1,424 meters—the highest peak in the Vosges massif. From the summit, views sweep across the Alsatian plain, the Black Forest, and on clear days, the Alps gleaming white on the horizon.

The Blue Devils Monument honors the alpine chasseurs who fell during the Great War. A white meteorological radar sphere adds a futuristic note to this somber memorial.

Eguisheim spirals in concentric circles around its octagonal 8th-century castle. Named France's Favorite Village in 2013 and classified among the Most Beautiful Villages in France.

→

Mountain farm inns on Petit Ballon serve the traditional marcaire meal. Roïgebrageldi potatoes, fleischnacka meat rolls, warm Munster cheese melting on its plate, blueberry tart to finish.

Eguisheim's octagonal castle stands at the heart of Place Saint-Léon. Founded in 720 by Count Eberhard, it witnessed the birth of the future Pope Leo IX in 1002.

The octagonal fountain in Place Saint-Léon is Alsace's largest in this shape. Pope Leo IX's statue rises above the Renaissance basin, presiding over the village center.

Saint Peter and Saint Paul Church blends Romanesque transept with Gothic nave. Its square bell tower in yellow sandstone marks the village entrance.

Follow Rue du Rempart Sud as it traces the medieval moat. A hexagonal stone dovecote marks where the fortification circuit begins its loop around the village.

Half-timbered winegrowers' houses spiral outward in concentric circles. Their ochre, rose, and butter-yellow facades jut forward in overhangs above the cobblestones.

→

The Eichberg Grand Cru wine trail climbs through south-facing limestone slopes. Riesling and Gewurztraminer vines have thrived on these renowned hillsides since Roman times.

→

The Stork Park at the village edge shelters several nests. Every spring, storks return to nest on rooftops and specially prepared platforms.

→

Push open a winemaker's cellar door to taste all seven Alsatian grape varieties. More than 30 winegrowers carry on Eguisheim's centuries-old viticultural tradition.

Neuf-Brisach draws a perfect eight-pointed star across the Alsatian plain. Built from scratch in 1698 by Vauban after the loss of Alt-Breisach, it embodies his third defensive system.

Place d'Armes occupies the geometric center of the octagon. Louis XIV personally selected this design from three proposals Vauban submitted. The fountain served as a fire reserve.

The Vauban Museum installed in the Belfort Gate traces the fortified town's construction. Scale models, original plans, and period accounts explain the military engineer's genius.

The Royal Church of Saint Louis, built in 1731, expresses the sober classical style of 18th-century France. Its clean lines survived American bombing in 1945.

The fortifications extend 2.4 kilometers of ramparts accessible from four gates. Ravelins, counter-guards, and bastions succeed one another in the moats.

→

Walk the sentry path that makes a complete circuit of the enceinte. Contemporary art installations punctuate the route between military structures.

→

A period-costumed guide recounts Neuf-Brisach's history. Vauban, his wife, and other characters resurrect Louis XIV's grand ambitions for this border fortress.

→

Soar above the citadel in an ultralight to grasp the geometric perfection of the octagonal plan. Forty-eight blocks organize around the central square like a spider's web.

Notre-Dame of the Assumption Church in yellow sandstone marries the Romanesque transept from the 11th century with a 13th-century Gothic nave. Its rose window with twenty lancets illuminates the western facade.

The Smile of Rouffach carved in stone frames the former sacristy door. Two delicate faces—a young girl and boy—have gazed at the faithful from the choir since medieval times.

The Witches' Tower raises its walls on the old medieval ramparts. Topped with a stork's nest, it recalls the witchcraft trials that once darkened this town.

The Recollets Church built in the 13th century by Franciscan friars expresses the austerity of mendicant orders. An exterior carved pulpit allowed preachers to address crowds gathered in the square.

Isenbourg Castle overlooks Rouffach from its vineyard-covered spur. Built by the Merovingians in the 5th century, it served as a residence for Dagobert II.

→

The Vorbourg Grand Cru wine trail winds between vine rows on limestone hillsides. Gewurztraminer and Riesling flourish on this sun-drenched terroir.

→

Stroll through Place de la République surrounded by Renaissance houses with stepped gables. The Town Hall with its twin pediments and the former Grain Hall testify to past prosperity.

→

The Notre-Dame de Schauenberg sanctuary watches over the valley from the forest. The path climbs through vineyards before plunging beneath beech trees.

Saint Leger Church raises its twin Romanesque towers from the 12th century. Its carved portal and Gothic choir exemplify Rhineland art.

The Dominican Convent transformed into a cultural center preserves its Gothic cloister and 13th-century vaults. Carved capitals narrate medieval monastic life.

Notre-Dame Church built in the 18th century expresses neoclassical restraint. Its Callinet organ resonates beneath the central dome.

The Théodore Deck Museum traces the work of Guebwiller's celebrated ceramicist who became director of the Sèvres Manufactory. Enameled faience and Art Nouveau creations gleam in the cases.

The Florival Valley opens toward Grand Ballon. The Lauch River descends from the Vosges through meadows and beech forests.

→

Guebwiller's vineyards cling to vertiginous terraced slopes. The only commune in Alsace producing four Grands Crus: Kessler, Kitterlé, Saering, and Spiegel.

The Cité de l'Automobile houses the Schlumpf collection. Over 450 cars including six of the world's existing Bugatti Royales. Two are here.

The Schlumpf brothers—textile industrialists—secretly amassed these treasures in their Malmerspach factory for years. The gleaming chrome hall displays the dream cars of the 1930s.

The Cité du Train spreads across 60,000 square meters—Europe's largest railway museum. From 19th-century steam locomotives to TGVs, the epic story of French rail unfolds here.

The spectacle circuit recreates the golden age of rail travel. Animated figures, period music, and special effects bring 1900s mountain stations and platforms back to life.

Saint Stephen Temple raises the tallest Protestant spire in France at 97 meters. Its 19th-century stained glass windows illuminate the neo-Gothic nave.

→

Place de la Réunion concentrates Renaissance facades and the Town Hall with its trompe-l'oeil frescoes. The Reformed Temple dominates the cobbled square.

→

The Électropolis Museum traces the history of electricity. The 170-ton Sulzer-BBC machine powers up each day—it once supplied the DMC textile mill.

→

The Zoological and Botanical Park shelters 1,200 animals in 25 hectares of greenery. Madagascar lemurs, polar bears, and arctic wolves roam naturalistic habitats.

Saint Thiébaut Collegiate Church unites all three forms of Rhenish Gothic. Built from the 13th to 16th centuries, it rivals Strasbourg Cathedral in sculptural richness.

The west portal's carvings number over 500 figures across three tympana. The Life of the Virgin, the Nativity of the Magi Kings, and the Crucifixion unfold in stone.

The stone lace spire soars to 78 meters. Locals claim it's the most beautiful in Alsace. Inside the choir, 15th-century choir stalls carve 169 characters.

Fifteenth-century stained glass filters light through eight 15-meter-high windows. The choir preserves the most extensive ensemble of medieval stained glass in Alsace.

The Witch's Eye watches over Thann from the ruins of Engelbourg Castle. In 1673, Louis XIV ordered its destruction. The main tower, split open, forms a cyclopean eye at 432 meters elevation.

→

The Rangen vineyard climbs volcanic slopes facing due south. This entirely classified Grand Cru produces Rieslings and Gewurztraminers with pronounced mineral character.

Hartmannswillerkopf—nicknamed Vieil Armand—dominates the Alsatian plain from 956 meters. This strategic spur contested throughout the Great War claimed 30,000 French and German soldiers.

The National Monument carved into bedrock shelters a crypt where 12,000 unknown soldiers rest. Two winged bronze Victories sculpted by Antoine Bourdelle guard the entrance.

The Altar of the Fatherland overlooks the Silberloch necropolis. Inspired by the 1790 Federation altar, it bears the coats of arms of twelve donor cities. President Albert Lebrun inaugurated it in 1932.

The Franco-German Historial inaugurated in 2017 by Presidents Macron and Steinmeier traces the battles and the building of Franco-German friendship. Modern scenography and war archives tell the story.

The battlefield preserves kilometers of trenches carved into sandstone. Underground shelters, barbed wire networks, and shell craters testify to the 1915 fighting.

→

Walk the historical trail through French and German trenches. Interpretive panels explain strategic positions and the murderous offensives that swept back and forth.

→

From Rocher panorama, climb to the Aussichtsfelsen viewpoint. Clear views extend across the southern Alsatian plain, Mulhouse, Basel, and in good weather the Bernese Alps.

→

Pay respects in the necropolis that gathers 1,640 bodies including 384 unknowns interred in six ossuaries. Each cross bears a name, a regiment, a date.
