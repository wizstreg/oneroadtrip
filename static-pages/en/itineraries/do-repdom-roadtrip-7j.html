From white-sand beaches where your footprints wash away to waterfalls tumbling through jungle, from whales singing in the bay to crocodiles guarding a lake below sea level: the Dominican Republic is an island that never stops surprising.

Santo Domingo

The Ciudad Colonial (Colonial Zone) is the historic heart of Santo Domingo—and the oldest European-origin settlement in the Americas, a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1990. Begin at the Catedral Primada de América: overlooking Parque Colón since 1541, it's the first cathedral built in the New World. The cornerstone was laid in 1514; construction was relaunched in 1519 by Bishop Alejandro Geraldini, and the cathedral was consecrated in 1541. Allow 45 minutes to explore its Gothic vaults and colonial altars.

From Parque Colón, walk down Calle Las Damas—the oldest paved street in the Americas, laid out in 1502. At its end stands the Alcázar de Colón, the palace Diego Columbus, son of Christopher Columbus, built between 1510 and 1514 from coral stone without a single nail. Conquistadors Cortés, Velázquez, and Balboa passed through these 22 rooms to plan the colonization of the Americas. Allow at least 90 minutes—the museum houses one of the island's richest collections of 15th-16th century furniture and tapestries.

A short walk from the Alcázar, don't miss the Fortaleza Ozama, built between 1502 and 1508 by Governor Nicolás de Ovando: it's the oldest military fortification of European origin in the Americas. Its Torre del Homenaje, 60 feet high with walls 6 feet thick, served variously as a prison for Christopher Columbus himself (arrested for tyranny in 1500), then for political opponents until the 1960s. Climb the ramparts for panoramic views over the mouth of the Ozama River and the city rooftops.

As evening falls, head to the Malecón, George Washington Avenue that runs along the Caribbean Sea for several miles. This is where the city comes alive at night: locals gather for evening strolls, Haitian artists display their naïve paintings along the waterfront, and the atmosphere feels like a tropical version of Miami. Grab a bench facing the sunset and watch the city change colors.

→

At lunchtime, find a local restaurant in the Zona Colonial to taste Dominican classics: mangú (mashed plantain), bandera dominicana (rice, beans, and meat), and tostones (fried plantain rounds). The area around Plaza de España offers several cozy spots with views of the renovated colonial shipyards.

En route from Santo Domingo, stop in Bayahibe, a charming fishing village on the edge of Parque Nacional del Este. It's the main departure point for Saona Island—about an hour by boat from the dock. If you arrive in the morning, this is where the best island excursions depart: aim for early departures to avoid the crowds.

Upon arrival in Punta Cana, Cap Cana Marina is the perfect spot for an evening stroll: sailboats, terrace restaurants, and boutiques by Dominican designers. It's also the departure point for catamaran excursions along the Bávaro reefs.

→

Playa Bávaro is one of the longest white-sand beaches in the Caribbean. The waters are calm and shallow—ideal for a first swim after the drive. For serious snorkeling, the beach itself is fairly ordinary; instead, book a catamaran excursion to the Cabeza de Toro reefs or save yourself for tomorrow's grand Saona Island trip.

→

Playa Macao, about 12 miles north of Bávaro, is a wild beach with no hotels or all-inclusive resorts. The waves are stronger here, beloved by local surfers. If you've rented a car, this detour makes all the difference: golden sand, tropical fruit vendors, and the feeling of having the beach to yourself.

Full day dedicated to Saona Island, the country's most iconic excursion. The island is part of Parque Nacional del Este and accessible only by boat from Bayahibe (~1 hour crossing). Its white-sand beaches fringed with coconut palms, turquoise waters over sandy bottoms, and natural pool where starfish live create a postcard-perfect scene. Book in advance—organized groups fill boats quickly, especially November through April.

→

On the way to Saona, the boat stops at the Blue Lagoon natural pool: a sandbar in the middle of the sea, knee-deep water, starfish within arm's reach. It's one of the country's most photographed moments—enjoy it early, before other boats arrive. On Saona itself, snorkeling is possible from wild beaches like Canto de la Playa, with moray eels, trumpetfish, and rays.

The drive from Punta Cana to Samaná passes through Miches, a small town on a large, still-undeveloped wild bay. Stop along the panoramic road overlooking the northeast coast: the landscapes of green hills plunging into turquoise sea contrast sharply with the manicured beaches you just left. From January to March, Samaná Bay is the Caribbean's premier humpback whale sanctuary: females travel over 1,500 miles from the North Atlantic to give birth here. Official whale-watching excursions are permitted January 15 through March 30 only.

Cayo Levantado, nicknamed Bacardi Island, is an islet in Samaná Bay accessible in 15 minutes by boat from the Santa Bárbara dock. Its white-sand beaches and palm trees leaning over the water make it one of the region's most beautiful coves. Ideally combine with the whale excursion during high season.

→

If you're not here during whale season, spend the afternoon around the town of Santa Bárbara de Samaná: stroll the dock, watch frigatebirds and pelicans, and dine on fresh fish at one of the waterfront restaurants. The town is small and lacks major architectural interest, but the atmosphere is authentic and the welcome warm.

Playa Bonita, 3 miles west of Las Terrenas village, is considered by many the peninsula's most beautiful beach. Its white sand bordered by coconut palms, its calm waters on one side and surf waves on the other make it a versatile spot. November through April it's a good place to surf. Bring a picnic—facilities are limited.

Playa Cosón stretches west of Playa Bonita—one of the peninsula's longest beaches, still little developed. Ideal for a long barefoot walk in the sand, far from crowds. Seafood lovers will find a few small beachfront eateries run by locals.

Las Terrenas itself is worth an evening stroll: a former fishing village turned cosmopolitan small town, with a large community of French, Italian, and North American expatriates. Wander through the Pueblo de los Pescadores, the fishermen's quarter converted into a row of bars and terrace restaurants, for sunset drinks with your toes in the sand.

→

Excursion to El Limón waterfall: set out on horseback (or on foot for the more athletic) from El Limón village through tropical forest to reach this 165-foot waterfall cascading into a turquoise natural pool. The horseback ride is short (about 30 minutes) but the path gets muddy in wet weather—wear closed-toe shoes. Swimming beneath the falls is possible but stay out of the direct current.

→

Boat excursion in Los Haitises National Park: from Sánchez (30 minutes from Las Terrenas), boats venture into a labyrinth of karst cliffs, mangrove forests, and caves covered with Taíno petroglyphs. The park covers 620 square miles and shelters hawks, herons, and pelicans. Plan for the full day—it's one of the peninsula's most spectacular excursions.

Puerto Plata is dominated by Loma Isabel de Torres, a 2,600-foot mountain topped by a replica of Christ the Redeemer. Christopher Columbus named it "Monte de Plata" during his first voyage in 1493 before it took the name of Isabella the Catholic. The cable car (in service subject to maintenance), operating since 1975, ascends in 10 minutes—it's the only cable car in the Caribbean. The botanical park at the summit harbors endemic species, caves, and trails through cloud forest. If the cable car is closed, an access road allows you to drive up. Arrive in the morning: clouds engulf the summit by afternoon.

Fortaleza San Felipe, perched on the western point of Puerto Plata's waterfront, is one of the country's best-preserved colonial forts. Built from 1564 to defend the town from English and French pirate attacks, it commands the entrance to the bay. The cannons along the ramparts face the Atlantic—the view from the bastions is among the finest on the north coast.

Cabarete, 15 miles east of Puerto Plata, is the Dominican capital of kitesurfing and windsurfing. Trade winds blow reliably December through July, attracting competitors from around the world. Even if you don't practice, Cabarete's lively beach with its waterfront bars and international atmosphere is worth the late-afternoon drive.

→

The Damajagua waterfalls (27 cascades) are located about 20 miles east of Puerto Plata, near Imbert village. The excursion involves progressing cascade to cascade by swimming, jumping, and sliding through natural pools carved in volcanic rock. One of the country's most popular outdoor activities—bring water shoes and expect to get soaked head to toe.

Jarabacoa is nicknamed "the city of eternal spring": at 1,740 feet elevation in the Cordillera Central, average temperature hovers around 72°F year-round, a striking contrast to the coastal heat. The drive from Puerto Plata crosses mountain landscapes that recall the Andes more than the Caribbean. Upon arrival, push on to Salto de Jimenoa Uno: this 245-foot waterfall bursts from a spectacular rock fracture in a setting of moss-covered cliffs. The trail from the parking area is short (15 minutes round-trip, easy) but rocks are slippery—wear closed-toe shoes.

Salto Baiguate, about 80 feet high, is more accessible and more intimate than Jimenoa. Nestled in a forested canyon, it forms an emerald natural pool perfect for swimming. You can reach it on foot in 10-15 minutes from the parking area, or on horseback via a ride through the countryside around Jarabacoa.

→

Rafting on the Río Yaque del Norte: at 190 miles long, it's the longest river in the Caribbean, and the only whitewater rafting course in the entire archipelago. Rapids vary in difficulty by season—March through May water levels are ideal for beginners, June through August the rapids are more intense. Count on 2.5 to 3 hours on the water, with local outfitters based along the riverbank.

→

In the evening, stroll along the Malecón de Jarabacoa by the Yaque del Norte: locals gather here at sunset, street vendors offer local coffee and dulce de leche. The atmosphere is warm and far from the bustle of beach resorts—this is where you'll feel the pulse of mountain Dominican life.

The drive toward Barahona takes you across the Sierra de Bahoruco, a national park that's part of a UNESCO-listed transboundary biosphere reserve—one of only 21 transboundary reserves in the world. Stop at Lake Enriquillo: it's the largest lake in the Caribbean, sitting about 130 feet below sea level—the lowest point of all the islands in the Americas. Its name honors Taíno chief Enriquillo, the first insurgent to resist Spanish conquistadors for 14 years. A boat excursion to Isla Cabritos allows observation of American crocodiles, endemic rhinoceros iguanas, and flamingos in their natural habitat.

On the coastal road south of Barahona, Playa San Rafael is a beach of smooth, colorful pebbles where a freshwater river descends directly from the mountains to pour into the sea. The sensation is strange and delightful: you can bathe in the cool fresh river water and the warm Caribbean sea by taking two steps. One of the country's most unusual spots.

Bahía de las Águilas is a 2-hour drive southwest of Barahona, in Jaragua National Park. It's a 5-mile-long beach, completely wild, with no hotels, no shops—one of the most pristine in the Caribbean. Accessible by 4x4 via red laterite tracks from La Cueva, or more comfortably by boat from Cabo Rojo. The seabed is among the least polluted in the country: corals, seagrass, lobsters, and hawksbill turtles. Note: this day combines Lake Enriquillo, Playa San Rafael, and Bahía de las Águilas—an ambitious program. If time is short, choose two of three; Bahía de las Águilas is priority if you're not coming back.

→

The coastal road from Barahona between the city and Pedernales is considered one of the most beautiful in the Dominican Republic: mountains plunge literally into the Caribbean Sea, the road snakes between cliffs and turquoise water. Drive slowly, make photo stops—the journey is worth as much as the destination itself.

Return to Santo Domingo (about 3 hours' drive). If you have time before departure, the Plaza de la Cultura houses the Museo del Hombre Dominicano, an immersion into the island's Taíno, colonial, and African history—the Taíno collections are particularly remarkable. The Gazcue neighborhood around the Plaza is also a chance to see Santo Domingo's other face: elegant early 20th-century villas, shaded gardens, and quiet streets, far from the Colonial Zone's bustle.

Final stroll on Calle El Conde, the pedestrian street connecting Parque Colón to Puerta del Conde—the gate through which General Sánchez proclaimed Dominican independence in 1844. It's also the Colonial Zone's commercial artery: souvenirs, Dominican amber (one of the world's largest reserves), larimar (the blue stone unique to the island), and cigars. Take time to browse a few craft shops.

→

Final Dominican meal before catching your flight: sancocho is the quintessential national dish—a hearty seven-meat stew, served with white rice and avocados. In restaurants around the Zona Colonial, you'll easily find a decent version to close out the circuit in style.
