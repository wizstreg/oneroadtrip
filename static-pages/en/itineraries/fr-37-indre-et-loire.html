Indre-et-Loire Road Trip: Loire Valley Châteaux, Secret Valleys & Touraine Vineyards

Where white tufa captures the light, cabernet franc ripens on sun-soaked slopes, and Leonardo da Vinci still sketches in his Clos Lucé workshop—Touraine has been perfecting the art of living well for five centuries.

From Langeais to Chenonceau, Indre-et-Loire concentrates the Loire Valley's finest châteaux in a compact area. Medieval fortresses at Chinon and Loches, Renaissance jewels at Azay-le-Rideau and Villandry, royal residences at Amboise and Chenonceau—every valley bend reveals a new castle poised between river and vines. This circuit follows the Loire westward, plunges into the Vienne and Indre valleys, then ascends the Cher to the most famous château of all.

  • Loches: royal citadel, Foulques Nerra's keep and Agnès Sorel's tomb

  • Vouvray: troglodyte caves and chenin blanc wines

Langeais

The Château de Langeais, built in 1465 under Louis XI's orders, rises dramatically from a promontory overlooking the Loire. Facing the town, it displays all the attributes of a medieval fortress: massive towers, a still-functioning drawbridge—one of the last in France—and a machicolated walkway. On the garden side, the façade opens up with large windows heralding the early Renaissance.

The richly furnished rooms recreate the atmosphere of a 15th-century noble residence: chests, millefleur tapestries, monumental carved fireplaces. The highlight is the recreation of the secret marriage of Charles VIII and Anne of Brittany, celebrated here at dawn on December 6, 1491—an event that sealed Brittany's union with France.

In the grounds, the remains of Foulques Nerra's keep (late 10th century) stand among the oldest stone donjons in France. A reconstructed medieval scaffold with period lifting equipment demonstrates the building techniques of the year 1000. Climb into the tree house perched in a bicentennial cedar, then head to the belvedere for sweeping views over the Loire.

Cross the Langeais bridge to admire the château's silhouette dominating the river from the left bank. Here, the UNESCO-listed Loire unfurls its sandbanks and ever-changing light. The village retains an old market hall and houses built from the region's signature creamy tufa stone.

→

Drawbridge demonstration. Each summer, the château's drawbridge is manually operated in front of visitors—a rare sight in France. The counterweight system requires two people to maneuver.

→

Cycling on La Loire à Vélo. This cycling route passes below the château, connecting Tours to Saumur along the river. Langeais is 18 miles from Tours on flat terrain.

→

Wine tasting of AOC Bourgueil and Saint-Nicolas-de-Bourgueil in the troglodyte caves of the right bank. Cabernet franc, the Loire's signature red grape, produces fruity, structured wines here.

Azay Le Rideau

+2

9.2 Les INSOLITES DE LA TOUCHE

The Château de Villandry, last of the great Renaissance châteaux built in the Loire Valley (1532). Inside, beautiful 18th-century decor and a collection of Spanish paintings await. But Villandry's true glory lies in its gardens: seven spaces arranged on terraces, redesigned in the early 20th century by Joachim Carvallo in the Renaissance spirit.

Villandry's ornamental kitchen garden is the masterpiece: nine geometric squares where cabbages, chard, eggplants and celery create a polychrome tapestry. Two plantings per year refresh the colors. From the terrace of the medieval keep, the patterns reveal themselves as if seen from above. Love gardens, water garden, labyrinth and sun garden complete the ensemble.

The Château d'Azay-le-Rideau, "a faceted diamond set in the Indre," as Balzac called it. Built from 1518 to 1524 on a river island for Gilles Berthelot, treasurer to François I, it blends French Gothic tradition with Italian influences. Its straight-flight staircase was one of the first in France. The white tufa façades reflect in two ornamental pools created in the 19th century.

The Azay-le-Rideau landscape park, twenty acres of English-style garden shaped by the Marquis de Biencourt in the 19th century. Giant sequoias, Lebanese cedars, and Virginia tulip trees line winding paths. The trails lead to ornamental pools where the château appears to float—the finest view is from the south bank.

→

Guided tour of Villandry's gardens with a gardener. Discover the organic techniques used since 2009, crop rotations, and the secrets behind the colorful compositions. The vegetable garden peaks in September.

💡 Best time: September for the vegetable garden, June for roses
→

Walk along the Indre from Azay-le-Rideau. The river winds between willows and poplars through the valley beloved by Balzac. Two miles upstream, Château de l'Islette—Azay's "little brother"—sheltered the romance between Rodin and Camille Claudel.

→

Wine tasting of AOC Azay-le-Rideau. This small vineyard produces elegant dry whites and rosés from chenin blanc and grolleau grapes. The troglodyte caves carved into the hillside offer an authentic setting.

Chinon

The royal fortress of Chinon, perched on its rocky spur, unfurls its ramparts for nearly a third of a mile. Three enclosures—Fort Saint-Georges, Château du Milieu, Fort du Coudray—chronicle ten centuries of history. Henry II Plantagenet stored his treasury here in the 12th century. It was here, on February 25, 1429, that Joan of Arc recognized Charles VII among his courtiers and convinced him to march to Reims for his coronation.

The royal lodgings, restored after 200 years as ruins, recreate the apartments of Charles VII and Marie d'Anjou. The Histopad tablet offers 360° augmented reality reconstructions: Henry II Plantagenet's Christmas court, the Recognition scene with Joan of Arc, medieval war machines.

The old town of Chinon, at the fortress's foot, lines up half-timbered houses and tufa mansions in a maze of cobbled lanes. Rue Voltaire, the main artery, leads to Place Jeanne-d'Arc. François Rabelais, native son, set several episodes of Gargantua here. The collegiate church of Saint-Mexme preserves a 10th-century nave.

Stroll the quays of the Vienne for panoramic views of the fortress crowning the town. The river winds between vineyards—Chinon's 5,700-acre vineyard, on both banks, produces some of the Loire's most renowned cabernet franc reds.

→

Visit to troglodyte wine caves and AOC Chinon tasting. Ancient tufa quarries, carved into the hillside, house naturally cool cellars. Chinon's cabernet franc reds are the perfect match for Tours rillettes and fouées.

💡 Caves open year-round, clustered around Rue Diderot
→

Chinon market on Thursday morning, Place Jeanne-d'Arc. Goat cheeses, rillettes, fouées, garden vegetables and Chinon wines. Authentic Touraine market atmosphere.

→

Cruise or canoe on the Vienne. The calm river invites lazy paddles with views of the fortress and vineyard slopes. Several outfitters offer routes from one hour to half a day.

Sainte Maure De Touraine

L'Île-Bouchard, an unusual village composed of two settlements separated by an island in the Vienne. The Priory of Saint-Léonard, founded in 1067, preserves only its Romanesque choir and ambulatory with three apsidal chapels. Its capitals, carved around 1130-1140 depicting scenes from sacred history, form one of Touraine's most complete ensembles of Romanesque sculpture.

The church of Saint-Gilles in L'Île-Bouchard, remodeled in the 15th then 19th centuries, dominates the Vienne's right bank. The Franciscan convent, with 12th- and 17th-century remains classified as Historic Monuments, completes this discreet heritage. Joan of Arc, en route to Chinon in 1429, supposedly paused here before Saint-Gilles' Romanesque portal.

Sainte-Maure-de-Touraine, the small town that gave its name to the famous AOP goat cheese. The truncated cone-shaped log pierced by a labeled rye straw has been produced here since Carolingian times. Legend says it was Moorish women, who remained after the 732 defeat, who transmitted their cheesemaking knowledge to the people of Touraine.

The market hall of Sainte-Maure, built in the 17th century, houses a lively market. Nearby, a dolmen and remains of a Roman camp testify to ancient occupation of the plateau. The 11th-century church and 16th-century houses complete this Touraine market town's heritage.

→

Visit to a goat farm and Sainte-Maure-de-Touraine AOP tasting. Farm producers open their doors to explain the raising of Alpine and Poitevine goats, lactic coagulation, ladle molding and aging. The rye straw, laser-engraved, guarantees the cheese's authenticity.

💡 Best tasting from April to August
→

Walk in the Vienne valley around L'Île-Bouchard. The Loire-Anjou-Touraine Regional Natural Park offers marked trails between vineyard slopes, riverbanks and tufa villages. Bucolic landscapes and absolute peace.

→

Sainte-Maure-de-Touraine market. Under the 17th-century market hall, producers display goat cheeses, rillettes, honey and seasonal fruit. Sainte-Maure AOP pairs perfectly with a red Chinon or dry Vouvray.

The royal citadel of Loches, one of France's finest medieval fortresses. Built on a rocky spur overlooking the Indre valley, it unfurls over a mile of ramparts protecting both keep and royal lodgings. Five centuries of construction, from the 11th to 16th century, can be read in its stones.

The keep of Loches, 120 feet high, built by Foulques Nerra starting in 1013. A masterpiece of Romanesque military architecture, it's one of the best-preserved in Europe. Transformed into a state prison by Louis XI, it still bears graffiti and frescoes painted by prisoners—including Ludovico Sforza, Duke of Milan.

The royal lodgings welcomed Joan of Arc on May 23, 1429, after the victory at Orléans, when she convinced Charles VII to proceed to Reims for his coronation. The king installed his mistress Agnès Sorel here in the 1440s. Anne of Brittany's oratory, in Flamboyant Gothic style, is the jewel of the wing added by Charles VIII and Louis XII.

The collegiate church of Saint-Ours, between keep and lodgings, presents a profile unique in France: its nave is topped by two octagonal pyramids called "dubes." The polychrome portal is one of the finest Romanesque testimonies in the Loire Valley. Inside, the alabaster tomb of Agnès Sorel watches over the "lady of beauty" for eternity.

Descend into Loches' lower town through winding medieval lanes. Half-timbered houses, Renaissance mansions, and hanging gardens compose a historic town scenery. The Cordeliers Gate and Saint-Antoine Tower punctuate the promenade.

→

Interactive scenographic tour of the royal citadel. Tactile model, projections, video animations and sound markers immerse you in the monument's great moments. At the keep, the Histopad offers an augmented reality visit.

→

Stroll in the royal citadel's medieval gardens. At the base of the thousand-year-old keep, a "hortus deliciarum" inspired by medieval illuminations offers a peaceful haven with aromatic, medicinal and heirloom plants.

→

Walk along the Indre from Loches. The verdant, peaceful valley offers waterside trails with views of the perched citadel's silhouette. The abbey church of Beaulieu-lès-Loches, visible from the lodgings terrace, merits a detour.

The Cathedral of Saint-Gatien in Tours, a Gothic masterpiece begun in the 13th century and completed in the 16th. Its two façade towers, crowned with Renaissance domes, dominate the city. Inside, 13th-century stained glass illuminates the choir. The adjacent Psalette cloister preserves a scriptorium and 15th-century vaulted library.

Place Plumereau, the beating heart of old Tours. Surrounded by half-timbered houses and Renaissance mansions in white tufa, it's one of France's most beautiful medieval squares. Café terraces, pedestrian lanes and student energy—Tours is the Loire Valley's most populous city.

The Museum of Fine Arts of Tours, housed in the former Archbishop's Palace beside the cathedral. Collections ranging from Mantegna and Rembrandt to Delacroix and Degas. In the garden, an 18th-century Lebanese cedar and Fritz the elephant, taxidermied, surprise visitors.

The Basilica of Saint-Martin, erected in the late 19th century above the tomb of Saint Martin, evangelizer of Gaul and patron saint of Tours. The old sanctuary, an immense medieval abbey church, was destroyed during the Revolution. The Charlemagne Tower and Clock Tower, vestiges of the Romanesque building, frame Rue des Halles.

Wander the lanes of old Tours between Place Plumereau and the Loire. Rue Colbert, Rue du Commerce, Passage du Coeur-Navré—the names tell the history. Half-timbered façades stand beside 18th-century classical townhouses. The Pont Wilson (1778) offers panoramic views over the quays.

→

Guided tour "Tours, City of Art and History." The tourist office offers themed circuits: medieval old Tours, the cathedral quarter, the Loire riverbanks. Tours served as the kingdom's capital under Louis XI.

→

Gourmet market at Les Halles de Tours. Under the great metal hall, producers and artisans offer rillettes, rillons, fouées, goat cheeses, Vouvray wines and Tours nougat. One of the region's finest covered markets.

→

Apéritif on the terrace at Place Plumereau. When the sun sets, the medieval façades glow and the terraces overflow. A sparkling Vouvray or red Chinon accompanies rillettes on fouées—the quintessential Touraine ritual.

Stop in Vouvray, six miles east of Tours en route to Amboise. The AOC Vouvray vineyard, nearly 5,000 acres on the right bank slopes of the Loire, produces chenin blanc wines—dry, semi-dry, sweet or sparkling—aged in troglodyte caves carved from tufa. These underground galleries, stretching for miles, maintain a constant temperature of 54°F.

The royal Château d'Amboise, perched on its promontory above the Loire. Favorite residence of Charles VIII, who died here in 1498 after striking a door lintel, then of François I who invited Leonardo da Vinci. The Saint-Hubert Chapel, a jewel of Flamboyant Gothic, houses Leonardo's presumed tomb. From the rampart balcony, sweeping views over the river and town.

The Clos Lucé, Leonardo da Vinci's final home, a third of a mile from the château. The Tuscan genius lived here from 1516 until his death in 1519, invited by François I who gave him this red-brick manor. The workshops recreate his study. In the seventeen-acre park, forty giant models of his inventions—swing bridge, armored vehicle, aerial screw—line the path.

The old town of Amboise stretches at the château's foot between Loire and hillside. Medieval lanes, Renaissance mansions and a covered market compose an authentic setting. The Clock Tower, former city gate, dates from the 15th century.

Amboise conceals remarkable troglodyte heritage. The granaries of Caesar, carved into the limestone hillside, served as grain silos since antiquity. Further on, troglodyte dwellings inhabited until the 20th century show the ingenuity of these houses—cool in summer, warm in winter—carved from soft tufa rock.

→

Tasting in Vouvray's troglodyte caves. Winemakers welcome visitors into their underground galleries to discover the range of chenin blanc: mineral dry, fruity semi-dry, golden sweet or lively sparkling. Traditional method sparkling Vouvray is the local pride.

💡 Caves open year-round, free at most wineries
→

Leonardo's inventions trail at the Clos Lucé. The Leonardo da Vinci park displays full-scale models in a verdant setting. Children operate drawbridges, gears and propellers. Underground, digital painting galleries immerse you in Leonardo's work.

→

Walk on the Île d'Or, between the Loire's two channels facing the château. This shaded park offers the finest view of the château's north façade and gardens. Picnicking possible by the river.

→

Excursion to the Pagoda of Chanteloup, two miles from Amboise. This 144-foot folly, inspired by 18th-century chinoiserie taste, is the sole vestige of the Duke of Choiseul's château. The climb to its summit offers panoramas over forest and valley.

The Château de Chenonceau, nicknamed the "Ladies' Château," spans the Cher on elegant arches. Diane de Poitiers built the bridge over the river, Catherine de Medici added the two-story gallery—200 feet long above the water. It's France's most-visited privately owned château and the second-most visited monument after Versailles.

Chenonceau's interiors unfold like a museum: Diane de Poitiers' bedroom with its fireplace by Jean Goujon, Catherine de Medici's bedroom with coffered ceiling, vaulted kitchen in the bridge piers, gallery transformed into a military hospital during World War I. The collection includes works by Rubens, Primaticcio and Correggio.

The gardens compete in symmetry. Diane de Poitiers' garden, terraced by the Cher, aligns its flower beds protected by moats. Catherine de Medici's garden, more intimate, surrounds a round pool. The flower vegetable garden, labyrinth and caryatid statues complete the 170-acre estate.

The Gallery of the Domes, former orangery, houses a collection of wax figures and historical souvenirs. Don't miss the 16th-century wine cellar or the floral workshop where the château's vase arrangements are created each morning with flowers from the estate.

→

Visit to Chenonceau's château and gardens. Allow 2 hours minimum for the complete experience. In summer, the "Nocturnal Promenades" illuminate the château and gardens with a thousand lights set to music—a magical spectacle.

💡 Arrive at opening time to avoid crowds
→

Walk along the Cher at Chenonceau. The banks offer unique perspectives on the château's arches reflecting in the river. In summer, boats are available to approach the monument from the water.

→

Tasting of Touraine wines in Chenonceau's estate cellars. The château produces its own vintages from 135 acres of vines—a sauvignon blanc and an elegant rosé bear the château's name.
