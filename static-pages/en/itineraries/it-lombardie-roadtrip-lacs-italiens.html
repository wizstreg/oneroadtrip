Italian Lakes Road Trip: Como, Maggiore, Orta and Garda

Where neoclassical villas perch on lake shores, baroque islands transformed by a dynasty of patrons, and a Roman peninsula plunges into Garda's waters—the Italian Lakes distill five centuries of refined living between Alps and plains.

From Milan, head to Lake Como and its neoclassical villas where Stendhal and Liszt once stayed. Ferry across to Bellagio and Varenna, villages clinging to the shores of the Lario. Reach Lake Maggiore and the Borromean Islands—a baroque palace on a fishermen's rock, transformed in 1632. Stop at Lake Orta and San Giulio island, a monastic haven founded, legend says, by a saint sailing on his cloak. Finish at Sirmione, a Lake Garda peninsula where Roman ruins overlook turquoise waters. Eight days, four lakes, one circuit.

Key highlights (5)
  • Milan: Gothic Duomo from 1386, 3,400 marble statues
  • Lake Como: Bellagio, Varenna, and the silk villas
  • Isola Bella: Baroque Borromeo Palace and ten-terrace gardens
  • Orta San Giulio: UNESCO Sacro Monte and monastic island
  • Sirmione: Scaliger Castle and Roman ruins on Lake Garda

Santa Maria delle Grazie and The Last Supper - This Renaissance church, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, houses Leonardo da Vinci's Last Supper (Cenacolo) in its refectory, painted between 1494 and 1498. The 460 x 880 cm fresco captures the moment Jesus announces that one of the apostles will betray him. Visits are strictly regulated: groups of 25 maximum, 15 minutes only—book weeks or even months in advance.

Castello Sforzesco - Built in the 15th century by Francesco Sforza, Duke of Milan, on the ruins of the Visconti castle. Its museums house Michelangelo's final unfinished sculpture, the Rondanini Pietà. The Sala delle Asse preserves Leonardo da Vinci's murals depicting intertwined branches. Behind the castle, Sempione Park stretches toward the Arc of Peace (Arco della Pace).

Pinacoteca di Brera - One of Italy's greatest art galleries, housed in a 17th-century palace. The collection spans from Raphael's Marriage of the Virgin to Caravaggio's Supper at Emmaus, including Mantegna's famous foreshortened Dead Christ and works by Bellini. The Brera quarter around the museum, with its art galleries and cafés, is Milan's bohemian heart.

Teatro alla Scala - Inaugurated in 1778, this 2,000-seat opera house ranks among the world's most celebrated. Verdi premiered several of his operas here. The small adjacent museum displays period scores, costumes, and instruments. You can often glimpse the auditorium from inside the museum.

→

Aperitivo on the Navigli - Milan's historic canals (designed in part by Leonardo da Vinci for irrigation) now form the city's nightlife district. The banks of Naviglio Grande and Naviglio Pavese come alive in the evening with bar and restaurant terraces.

→

Naviglio Grande antiques market - Every last Sunday of the month, more than 400 vendors set up along 2 km of canal. Furniture, antique books, vintage jewelry, and decorative art pieces.

Duomo di Milano - Begun in 1386 under Gian Galeazzo Visconti, this Gothic cathedral required five and a half centuries to complete (finished in 1965). At 157 meters long and 45 meters high under the central nave, it can hold 40,000 people. Over 3,400 statues adorn the interior and exterior, 135 spires pierce the sky, and 96 gargoyles drain rainwater. At the summit, the golden Madonnina (1774) watches over Milan from 108.5 meters. Beneath the cathedral, a 4th-century paleochristian baptistery preserves the font where Saint Ambrose is said to have baptized Saint Augustine in 387. Climb to the rooftop terraces: among the pinnacles of Candoglia marble, on clear days the view reaches all the way to the Alps.

Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II - Inaugurated in 1877, this shopping arcade designed by Giuseppe Mengoni links Piazza del Duomo to Teatro alla Scala beneath a glass and iron vault 47 meters high. The mosaic floor depicts the coats of arms of Milan, Turin, Florence, and Rome. Local tradition: spinning three times on your heel on the Turin bull's attributes supposedly brings good luck.

Quadrilatero della Moda - The four streets forming the heart of Milanese fashion (Via Montenapoleone, Via della Spiga, Via Manzoni, Corso Venezia) occupy the quarter of 18th-century aristocratic palaces. Even without shopping, the neoclassical façades and interior courtyards merit a stroll.

→

Milan → Como drive - About 80 km, allow 1 hour via the motorway. Arrive in late afternoon for an initial walk along Como's lakefront promenade (Lungolago).

Duomo di Como - The last Gothic cathedral built in Lombardy, begun in 1396. Construction lasted nearly four centuries. The façade blends flamboyant Gothic with Renaissance elements. Inside, 16th-century Flemish tapestries and paintings by Bernardino Luini and Gaudenzio Ferrari.

Broletto and communal tower - Right beside the Duomo, this 13th-century communal palace in polychrome marble bands (white, gray, and pink) served as the seat of city government in the Middle Ages. The adjacent tower punctuates Como's urban landscape.

Tempio Voltiano - This neoclassical temple erected in 1927 on the lake shore celebrates the centenary of Alessandro Volta's death. Born in Como in 1745, the inventor of the electric battery is honored here through period scientific instruments and models of his experiments.

Bellagio, the 'Pearl of the Lario' - Take the ferry from Como (about 40 minutes by hydrofoil). Bellagio occupies the central promontory where Lake Como divides into its two branches. Climb the Salita Serbelloni, a stepped shopping street lined with silk boutiques—Como has historically been Italy's silk capital.

Villa Melzi and its gardens - Built between 1808 and 1810 by architect Giocondo Albertolli for Francesco Melzi d'Eril, vice president of the Italian Republic under Napoleon. The English-style park descends to the lake among Japanese cedars, Chilean palms, giant rhododendrons, and a Japanese pond. Franz Liszt stayed here in 1837 with the Countess d'Agoult; Stendhal found inspiration for his writings. The neoclassical chapel houses the duke's tomb. Don't miss Comolli's 1810 sculpture of Dante and Beatrice, commissioned by Melzi himself.

Basilica San Giacomo - Fine example of Lombard Romanesque style on Bellagio's main square. Its mid-19th-century organ, restored in 2013, resonates during summer concerts.

→

Ferry Como → Bellagio → Como - The boat network (Navigazione Laghi) connects all lake villages. The hydrofoil makes Como-Bellagio in about 40 minutes. The classic ferry takes longer but allows you to admire the villas from the water.

Passeggiata degli Innamorati in Varenna - This cantilevered walkway, anchored into the rock above the lake, connects the landing pier to the village center over a few hundred meters. Its red railing—the color of love—and metal heart have become Varenna's symbol. Arrive by ferry from Bellagio (15 minutes) for an approach from the water.

Villa Monastero - A former 13th-century Cistercian convent transformed into a noble residence in the 17th century, then into a holiday villa at the turn of the 19th and 20th centuries. Its 2 km lakeside botanical garden shelters tropical and Mediterranean species that thrive in the microclimate. The Casa Museo preserves 14 furnished rooms with period furniture.

Villa Cipressi - Adjacent to Villa Monastero, this villa has become a hotel, but its terraced gardens descending toward the lake remain open to the public. Centuries-old cypresses, sculpted hedges, and fountains cascade down multiple levels with lake views.

Castello di Vezio - Perched on the promontory above Varenna, this medieval castle was reputedly founded by Lombard Queen Theodelinda. The climb takes 30 minutes along a shaded mule track. From the tower, the view embraces all three branches of the lake. The garden harbors strange white plaster sculptures—'ghosts'—remnants of art installations.

Piazza San Giorgio and church - Varenna's main square opens onto the 14th-century church in Lombard Gothic style, adorned with medieval frescoes. The small port of Riva Grande with its colorful boats lies just below.

→

Triangle ferry route Como → Varenna → Bellagio → Como - The ferry service connects the three key lake villages. Combine your Varenna day with a return stop in Bellagio for dinner.

Stresa's Belle Époque lakefront - The Lungolago promenade is lined with grand late-19th-century hotels and palm gardens. Stresa became an international resort from the 1860s onward, attracting aristocrats, writers (Hemingway set part of 'A Farewell to Arms' here), and crowned heads.

Isola Bella and Palazzo Borromeo - Until 1630, this was merely a fishermen's rock. In 1632, Count Vitaliano Borromeo launched its transformation into a baroque palace, completed around 1670. The island was named for his wife Isabella. The four-story palace houses a domed hall of honor, neoclassical ballroom, 16th-century Flemish tapestries in silk and gold, and grottos decorated with pebbles and shells. Napoleon slept here in August 1797. The Italian gardens descend in ten pyramidal terraces (37 m high) planted with orange trees and camellias, crowned by the Teatro Massimo and its statues by Carlo Simonetta. White peacocks roam freely.

Isola dei Pescatori - The smallest of the Borromean Islands (350 x 100 m) never belonged to the Borromeo family. About twenty fishing families inhabit it year-round. The narrow lanes, wooden balconies, and Romanesque church of San Vittore form a village frozen in time. Have lunch here: grilled lavarello (whitefish) is the local specialty.

Villa Pallavicino - On Stresa's southern outskirts, this neoclassical villa is surrounded by an 18-hectare English park that serves as a refuge for about forty animal species in semi-freedom: flamingos, llamas, Tibetan goats, macaques.

→

Boat shuttle Stresa → Isola Bella → Isola Pescatori → Stresa - Boats depart approximately every 30 minutes from Stresa's landing pier. Allow half a day for the two main islands.

Hermitage of Santa Caterina del Sasso - Clinging to a cliff on Lake Maggiore's eastern shore, this monastery was founded in the 12th century by Alberto Besozzi, a merchant from Leggiuno who survived a shipwreck. The complex comprises three chapels built at different periods: the Cappella della Vergine (1270), the Cappella di Santa Caterina, and the Cappella di San Nicola with a 14th-century Crucifixion. Access is by 268 steps (or an elevator installed in 2009), or by boat from Stresa in 20 minutes. The courtyard's wine press dates from 1759.

Botanical Gardens of Villa Taranto in Verbania - In 1931, a Scottish captain acquired this villa with the aim of creating a dream garden on the shores of Lake Maggiore. The result: 16 hectares, 7 km of paths, and over 20,000 plant varieties, including Victoria Amazonica (giant Amazon water lily), Antarctic Dicksonia (tree ferns), and dahlias in spectacular bloom from August to October. From spring to autumn, species succeed each other in a symphony of colors.

Pallanza quarter in Verbania - The old center of Pallanza, with its narrow streets and waterside squares, faces Stresa across the Borromean Gulf. The Museo del Paesaggio presents collections of art and sculpture related to the lake landscape.

→

Ferry crossing Stresa → Santa Caterina del Sasso → Verbania - From Stresa, the boat reaches the hermitage in 20 minutes (check schedules, limited frequency). From the hermitage, reach Verbania by boat or by car via the eastern shore.

Sacro Monte d'Orta - Inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2003, this sacred mount rises 400 m above sea level on a wooded promontory overlooking the lake. Created between 1590 and 1760, it differs from other Sacri Monti in being dedicated to Saint Francis of Assisi (rather than the life of Jesus). Twenty chapels unfold along a spiral path, each adorned with frescoes and life-sized polychrome terracotta statues. The styles range from late Renaissance to Rococo. The church of San Nicolao at the summit offers a plunging view of San Giulio island.

Piazza Motta and medieval borgo - Orta San Giulio's main square opens directly onto the lake and island. The Palazzo della Comunità (1582) with its external staircase dominates the square. From there, medieval lanes climb among Renaissance palaces and baroque oratories. The church of Santa Maria Assunta (14th century) stands out for its columned portico.

Isola San Giulio - Water taxis depart from Piazza Motta every 15 minutes. Legend says that around 390, Saint Julius of Orta crossed the lake on his cloak to drive out dragons (symbols of paganism) and found a church. The current basilica, 12th-century Romanesque with three naves, preserves a 12th-century ambo in green serpentine (green marble) carved with scenes of good versus evil. In the crypt, a silver and crystal shrine displays the saint's relics. The Benedictine monastery remains active—the path circling the island is called 'Via del Silenzio e della Meditazione' (Way of Silence and Meditation).

→

Tasting on Piazza Motta - The waterfront terraces serve Piedmontese specialties: risotto al Gorgonzola, agnolotti del plin, and tapulone (donkey stew, a traditional dish of the Novara area). Pair with a Nebbiolo from the Novara Hills.

Castello Scaligero - Built in the second half of the 13th century by the della Scala family who ruled Verona, this water-surrounded castle guards the entrance to the Sirmione peninsula. You enter via a drawbridge. The keep, ramparts, and fortified inner harbor—rare for a lakeside castle—are perfectly preserved. From the tower top, the panorama embraces all of southern Lake Garda. Under Napoleon, the castle served as an armory and barracks.

Grotte di Catullo - At the peninsula's northern tip, the ruins of this early 1st-century AD Roman villa extend across nearly two hectares—the largest Roman villa in northern Italy. The name 'grottoes' dates from the Renaissance, when the overgrown ruins resembled caverns. The villa likely belonged to the senatorial Valerii Catulli family. The poet Catullus sang of Sirmione in his verses but died in 54 BC, before this villa was built. A small museum at the entrance displays mosaics and artifacts found on site.

Church of San Pietro in Mavino - This small 12th-century Romanesque church, perched amid the peninsula's olive groves, shelters medieval frescoes depicting scenes from Christ's life and a Last Judgment. One of Sirmione's few quiet spots, away from the crowds in the center.

Historic pedestrian center - You enter the old town through the gate adjoining the castle. Narrow lanes are lined with shops, gelaterias, and small restaurants. The route naturally leads from the entrance toward the peninsula's tip, passing the church of Santa Maria Maggiore.

→

Swimming at Jamaica Beach - At the peninsula's tip, below the Grotte di Catullo, this free public beach offers turquoise waters against a backdrop of Roman ruins. In certain spots, underwater thermal springs warm the water to 36°C.

→

Sirmione thermal springs - The Romans exploited these waters from the 1st century BC. In 1889, a Venetian diver named Procopio dove to 20 m near the Grotte di Catullo and managed to channel the Boiola spring (sulfurous water gushing at 70°C from the rock). Two thermal establishments operate on the peninsula.
