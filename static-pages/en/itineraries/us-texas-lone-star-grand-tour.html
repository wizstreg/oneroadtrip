Explore the Dallas Museum of Art and the adjacent Nasher Sculpture Center, showcasing world-class collections spanning 5,000 years of human creativity. The city boasts the largest urban arts district in the United States, offering exceptional cultural experiences for art enthusiasts and casual visitors alike. These neighboring institutions create a comprehensive artistic journey through both traditional and contemporary works.

Visit the Sixth Floor Museum at Dealey Plaza, housed in the former Texas School Book Depository from which President John F. Kennedy was assassinated in 1963. The museum provides a detailed and moving account of the president's life, death, and legacy through historical exhibits, photographs, and artifacts. This landmark site offers profound insights into one of America's most significant historical events.

→

Discover the Perot Museum of Nature and Science, a striking architectural landmark featuring interactive exhibits on everything from dinosaurs to DNA and space exploration. Families and science enthusiasts will find hours of engaging educational entertainment across multiple floors of cutting-edge displays. The museum's dynamic approach makes complex scientific concepts accessible and exciting for all ages.

→

Stroll through the Dallas Arboretum and Botanical Garden, a 66-acre oasis on the shores of White Rock Lake featuring seasonal flower displays and themed garden areas. The Swiss Avenue Historic District nearby showcases magnificent 19th-century Victorian and neoclassical architecture. The vibrant Deep Ellum neighborhood offers excellent nightlife, live music venues, and eclectic street art.

Palo Duro Canyon

Marvel at Palo Duro Canyon State Park, known as the Grand Canyon of Texas, featuring dramatic landscapes of colorful rock formations, steep mesa walls, and extensive hiking trails stretching over 120 miles long and up to 20 miles wide. The canyon's geological formations display a vivid palette of red, orange, and white hues that become especially stunning at sunrise and sunset. As the nation's second-largest canyon, this natural wonder offers breathtaking vistas at every turn.

Hike the Lighthouse Trail to reach the park's iconic Lighthouse Peak, a towering hoodoo formation that has become the symbol of Palo Duro Canyon. The moderate trail winds through varied terrain showcasing millions of years of geological history carved by the Prairie Dog Town Fork of the Red River. The panoramic views from this landmark reward hikers with some of the most spectacular scenery in all of Texas.

→

Explore the CCC Trail, which provides insights into the remarkable work of the Civilian Conservation Corps during the 1930s, who built much of the park's infrastructure during the Great Depression. Wildlife enthusiasts can observe the Texas state bison herd that has been reintroduced to the park, along with diverse bird species and other native animals. The Pioneer Amphitheater within the park hosts concerts, theatrical performances, and special events throughout the season.

Conquer Guadalupe Peak, the highest point in Texas at 8,751 feet, where breathtaking panoramic views reward determined hikers who complete the challenging summit trail. This ancient fossilized reef mountain, dating back over 260 million years, rises dramatically from the Chihuahuan Desert floor. Standing atop Texas provides an unparalleled sense of accomplishment and connection to the state's geological heritage.

Discover McKittrick Canyon, renowned for producing the most spectacular fall foliage display in West Texas when maples, oaks, and other deciduous trees transform the desert canyon into a blaze of autumn colors. The canyon's unique microclimate supports a surprisingly diverse ecosystem within the arid surrounding landscape. Crystal-clear streams and towering limestone walls create a hidden oasis that contrasts dramatically with the desert terrain.

→

Hike the El Capitan trail for closer views of the park's iconic rocky outcrop, a massive limestone cliff that served as a landmark for travelers, including Butterfield Overland Mail stagecoaches, crossing the desert for centuries. The park offers exceptional stargazing opportunities due to minimal light pollution from surrounding areas. Over 300 species of birds have been documented here, making it a premier destination for wildlife observation.

Experience Big Bend National Park, an expansive wilderness covering over 800,000 acres where the Rio Grande carves dramatic canyons through ancient mountains. The park's vast, open spaces encompass stunning desert landscapes, diverse ecosystems ranging from Chihuahuan Desert to forested highlands, and some of the darkest skies in North America for exceptional stargazing. This remote sanctuary offers solitude and natural beauty on a scale difficult to find elsewhere in the continental United States.

Explore Santa Elena Canyon, where sheer limestone cliffs rise 1,500 feet above the Rio Grande, creating one of the most photographed and awe-inspiring landscapes in Texas. The canyon trail leads visitors through a dramatic corridor where Mexico rises on one side and Texas on the other, separated only by the flowing river below. Morning light illuminating the towering rock faces creates particularly stunning photographic opportunities.

→

Venture into the Chisos Basin, the only mountain range in the United States entirely contained within a national park, offering spectacular views and notably cooler temperatures ideal for summer visits. The Window Trail provides a moderate hike to a natural rock window framing stunning sunset views over the desert below. For more challenging adventures, the South Rim trail rewards experienced hikers with 360-degree panoramas across the vast Big Bend landscape.

→

Take advantage of Big Bend's designation as a Dark Sky Park, offering some of the best astronomical viewing opportunities in North America thanks to its remote location far from urban light pollution. The park's rich cultural heritage includes evidence of Native American habitation, Spanish explorer routes, and pioneer settlements. Fossils dating back millions of years dot the landscape, making this region a geological wonderland for those interested in Earth's ancient history.

San Antonio

Top hotels

Walk the legendary San Antonio River Walk, a picturesque network of pathways winding along the San Antonio River below street level, lined with restaurants, shops, and stunning landscaping. This unique urban waterway has become one of the most visited attractions in Texas, offering boat cruises, seasonal festivals, and enchanting nighttime ambiance when thousands of lights reflect on the water. The River Walk perfectly blends historic charm with modern entertainment in the heart of downtown.

Stand before the Alamo, the iconic 18th-century Spanish mission where the famous 1836 battle became a defining moment in Texas independence and American history. The preserved church and grounds now serve as a shrine to the defenders who fell during the siege, attracting millions of visitors annually to learn about this pivotal chapter in Texas heritage. Museum exhibits detail the complex history of the mission and its transformation into a symbol of courage and sacrifice.

→

Explore the San Antonio Missions National Historical Park, a UNESCO World Heritage Site encompassing five historic Spanish colonial missions including the Alamo, representing the largest concentration of Spanish colonial missions in North America. Each mission tells a unique story of religious conversion, cultural exchange, and frontier life in the 18th century. The park provides an extraordinary window into the Spanish colonial period that shaped the region's culture.

→

Discover the Pearl District, a transformed historic brewery complex now hosting a vibrant farmers market, culinary destinations, and artisan shops that have made it a center of San Antonio's food renaissance. Market Square, the largest Mexican market outside Mexico, offers authentic crafts, traditional foods, and cultural experiences celebrating the city's Hispanic heritage. The annual 11-day Fiesta San Antonio celebrates the city's diverse cultures with parades, music, and festivities drawing hundreds of thousands of visitors.

Austin

Tour the Texas State Capitol, a stunning pink granite building that stands taller than the United States Capitol in Washington, D.C., and serves as the seat of Texas government since 1888. Free guided tours explain the building's history, architecture, and the colorful characters who have shaped Texas politics within its walls. The surrounding grounds feature monuments, gardens, and excellent views of Austin's evolving skyline.

Enjoy Zilker Park, a 351-acre urban oasis featuring the spring-fed Barton Springs Pool, where the constant 68-degree waters have provided refreshing swimming for over a century. The park offers hiking and biking trails, botanical gardens, and spectacular views of the downtown Austin skyline. Paddle boarding and kayaking on adjacent Lady Bird Lake provide peaceful ways to experience the city from the water.

→

Experience Austin's legendary live music scene, which has earned the city its designation as the Live Music Capital of the World, with venues hosting performances every night across genres from country to indie rock. Major festivals including South by Southwest and Austin City Limits attract hundreds of thousands of music fans annually. The city's murals and street art installations provide perfect photo opportunities throughout the downtown and East Austin neighborhoods.

→

Witness the spectacular evening emergence of the Congress Avenue Bridge bat colony, the largest urban bat colony in North America, when up to 1.5 million Mexican free-tailed bats take flight at sunset from March through November. The city's commitment to sustainability has made it a leader in green initiatives with extensive trail systems and farm-to-table dining options. Explore the burgeoning food truck scene that has made Austin a culinary destination rivaling much larger cities.

Houston

Visit Space Center Houston, the official visitor center of NASA's Johnson Space Center, featuring authentic spacecraft, interactive exhibits, and behind-the-scenes tram tours of actual NASA facilities including historic Mission Control. This world-class museum allows visitors to touch a real moon rock, see the massive Saturn V rocket, and learn about current and future space exploration missions. The center provides an unparalleled look at America's space program from Mercury through the International Space Station.

Explore Houston's renowned Museum District, home to 19 museums within walking distance including the Museum of Fine Arts and the Houston Museum of Natural Science. The diverse collections span art, science, history, and culture, with several museums offering free admission on designated days. The district represents one of the largest cultural concentrations in the United States, rivaling collections in much larger cities.

→

Wander through the Houston Zoo and adjacent Hermann Park, where the Japanese Garden provides a peaceful retreat amid the bustling fourth-largest city in America. The Museum of Natural Science features exceptional dinosaur exhibits, a planetarium, and a butterfly center housing thousands of live butterflies. Houston's multicultural dining scene reflects its diverse population, offering exceptional cuisines from around the world.

→

Experience the Texas Medical Center, the largest medical complex in the world treating millions of patients annually, representing Houston's role as a global leader in healthcare and medical research. The city serves as the world energy capital with over 500 oil and energy firms headquartered in the metropolitan area. Save on major attractions including the Space Center and Zoo with a Houston CityPASS for convenient and affordable exploration.

Padre Island

Discover Padre Island National Seashore, the longest undeveloped barrier island in the world, stretching over 70 miles along the Gulf of Mexico with pristine beaches untouched by commercial development. The protected coastline provides critical nesting habitat for endangered Kemp's ridley sea turtles, with public releases of hatchlings creating unforgettable wildlife encounters during nesting season. The island's wild, windswept beauty offers a serene escape into coastal wilderness.

Explore the diverse ecosystems where Gulf waters meet coastal dunes, grasslands, and tidal flats supporting remarkable biodiversity. The Malaquite Visitor Center provides orientation to the park's natural and cultural history, including the shipwrecks that dot the treacherous coastline. Designated sections allow four-wheel-drive vehicle access to remote beach areas far from crowds.

→

Experience world-class bird watching opportunities, as Padre Island lies along the Central Flyway migration route, attracting hundreds of species throughout the year including rare shorebirds and raptors. Fishing enthusiasts can cast into the surf for redfish, speckled trout, and other Gulf species along miles of accessible shoreline. Swimming, beachcombing, and camping under starlit skies provide classic coastal recreation in an unspoiled natural setting.
