Milan to Chianti Circuit: Design, Art, and Wine

UNESCO Sabbioneta, Cremona's Torrazzo the world's tallest brick bell tower, Bologna's porticoes, Brunelleschi's dome, Chianti Classico since 1716, Monteriggioni cited by Dante: Northern Italy from Milan to Siena.

The circuit begins in Milan with Michelangelo's unfinished Pietà Rondanini and the Brera collections assembled by Napoleon. Piacenza reveals Mochi's Baroque equestrian statues (1625) and the Klimt recovered in 2019 from a walled niche. Cremona displays the 112-meter Torrazzo and the workshops of Stradivari's luthiers, before Sabbioneta, a UNESCO ideal city built from nothing by a Gonzaga prince in 1554. Parma unfolds Correggio's trompe-l'oeil frescoes, Bologna its 62 kilometers of UNESCO porticoes and 1655 meridian. Brisighella and its 15th-century Via degli Asini precede Florence, Brunelleschi's dome, and the Uffizi. The circuit concludes in the Chianti Classico vineyards delimited in 1716, at Monteriggioni and its 14 towers cited by Dante, then in Siena.

Duomo di Milano - Gothic cathedral whose construction spanned from 1386 to 1965, making it one of the longest building projects in Western architectural history. With 135 spires and 3,400 exterior statues, it ranks as the world's third-largest cathedral by floor area after St. Peter's in Rome and Seville Cathedral. Napoleon Bonaparte personally financed the façade during his coronation as King of Italy in 1805. The terraces, accessible by stairs or elevator, allow visitors to walk among the spires at 108 meters above street level.

Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II - covered shopping arcade stretching 196 meters, inaugurated in 1877 and designed by architect Giuseppe Mengoni, who tragically fell from scaffolding two days before the official opening. The central glass and iron dome soars 47 meters high. The mosaic floor depicts emblems of Italy's four capitals of the era - Milan, Rome, Florence, and Turin. Local tradition holds that spinning three times on your right heel over the bull's testicles brings good luck.

Castello Sforzesco - fortress built in 1368 by the Visconti family, destroyed and then rebuilt from 1450 by Francesco Sforza. The 180,000-square-meter complex houses eleven civic museums, including Michelangelo's Pietà Rondanini, a sculpture he worked on just six days before his death in 1564. The central tower, Torre del Filarete, was reconstructed in 1905 based on 15th-century original plans.

Pinacoteca di Brera - national gallery housed since 1809 in a 17th-century palazzo in the Brera district, with a collection assembled by Napoleon from works seized from Lombardy and Veneto convents and churches. The 38 rooms preserve Raphael's Marriage of the Virgin (1504), Mantegna's Dead Christ (circa 1480), and Caravaggio's Supper at Emmaus (1606).

Pirelli HangarBicocca - former Pirelli locomotive factory spanning 15,000 square meters, converted into a contemporary art space in 2004, with ceiling heights reaching 30 meters. The permanent installation I Sette Palazzi Celesti by Anselm Kiefer (2004-2015) occupies 4,500 square meters with seven concrete and lead towers ranging from 14 to 18 meters high, evoking Babylonian cosmology.

→

Stroll through the Brera district - cobblestone alleys, interior courtyards, and Saturday antiques market along Via Fiori Chiari, in Milan's neighborhood of galleries and artists' studios.

→

Evening walk through the Quadrilatero della Moda - the perimeter formed by Via Montenapoleone, Via della Spiga, Via Sant'Andrea, and Via Manzoni, where 18th and 19th-century palazzos house the fashion houses that have called Milan home since the 1950s.

Piazza Cavalli di Piacenza - main square of Piacenza dominated by two bronze equestrian statues cast by Francesco Mochi between 1620 and 1625, representing Alessandro Farnese (died 1592) and his son Ranuccio I, the third and fourth Dukes of Parma and Piacenza. These rank among the first Baroque equestrian representations depicting horses at full gallop with two hooves airborne, a bronze-casting technique considered a major technical challenge of the era. The 13th-century Palazzo del Comune (Gotico), in red brick and white marble, has bordered the square since 1281.

Palazzo Farnese and Galleria Ricci Oddi - the Palazzo Farnese, begun in 1558 and never completed, houses seven museum spaces including the Ricci Oddi Modern Art Gallery founded in 1931. This gallery holds Gustav Klimt's Portrait of a Lady, stolen in 1997 and recovered in 2019 from a niche walled up behind a panel on the museum's own exterior wall - one of the strangest art theft cases in recent history. The painting has been on display again since 2020.

Duomo di Piacenza - Romanesque cathedral built between 1122 and the 13th century, with a dome decorated by Guercino frescoes (1626-1628). The façade alternates bands of white marble and pink sandstone in Lombard Romanesque style. The octagonal baptistery in pink Verona marble stands adjacent to the cathedral.

→

Tasting of coppa piacentina DOP and salami piacentino DOP in historic center delicatessens - Piacenza boasts three distinctive DOP charcuterie designations (coppa, salami, and pancetta), separate from Parma's, produced in the hills south of the city.

Cattedrale di Cremona and Torrazzo - Romanesque-Gothic cathedral built between 1107 and the 14th century, with the Torrazzo rising 112.7 meters - the world's tallest brick bell tower and Italy's third-tallest campanile. The Torrazzo was constructed between 1250 and 1309 and connected to the cathedral in 1490 by a soaring Gothic tribune. The cathedral façade integrates 12th-century Romanesque elements with 13th-14th century Gothic additions.

Piazza del Comune di Cremona - medieval square surrounded by the Palazzo del Comune (1206), Palazzo dei Giureconsulti (13th century), Romanesque Battistero (1167), and Loggia dei Militi (1292). The ensemble forms one of the best-preserved examples of Lombard medieval civic architecture, with five distinct monuments comprising a single integrated complex.

Museo del Violino - museum housed in the Palazzo dell'Arte dedicated to Cremonese violin-making, whose history traces back to Andrea Amati (circa 1505-1577), the first documented violin maker. Antonio Stradivari (1644-1737) crafted over 1,100 instruments in Cremona, of which approximately 650 are still accounted for worldwide. The museum preserves eleven Stradivari violins, including the Cremonese from 1715, which are regularly played during concerts in the museum's integrated performance hall.

→

Visit to a violin-making workshop in the luthiers' quarter around Piazza del Comune - Cremona still counts about one hundred active luthiers, concentrated in the alleys surrounding the cathedral. Several workshops accept visitors by appointment.

→

Tasting of Cremona's torrone - nougat made from honey, egg whites, and almonds or hazelnuts, crafted in Cremona since the 15th century according to archival documents dating to 1441, during the wedding banquet of Francesco Sforza and Bianca Maria Visconti.

Sabbioneta - Renaissance ideal city built from nothing between 1554 and 1591 by Vespasiano Gonzaga (1531-1591), inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2008 alongside Mantua. Vespasiano, a prince and condottiere in the service of Philip II of Spain, conceived his city according to the humanist theories of Alberti and Vitruvius - orthogonal plan, hexagonal fortifications spanning 2.5 kilometers, monumental public buildings designed for a city of 10,000 inhabitants that today numbers only 4,000.

Palazzo Ducale - palace of Vespasiano Gonzaga built between 1568 and 1578, featuring the Hall of Rivers decorated with allegorical frescoes and a cycle of full-length portraits of the Gonzaga sovereigns. The gilded hall, with its golden coffered ceiling, served as the throne room.

Teatro Olimpico di Sabbioneta - one of Europe's first permanent covered theaters, built by Vincenzo Scamozzi in 1590 on commission from Vespasiano, with a capacity of 250 spectators. The antique-style interior decoration - colonnaded loggias, painted friezes - ranks among the best-preserved from the late Renaissance. Not to be confused with Palladio's Teatro Olimpico in Vicenza (1580).

Galleria degli Antichi - 97-meter-long gallery built in 1584 by Vespasiano to house his collection of antiquities and sculptures, one of the first museum galleries in European history. The 97-meter length was calculated to allow cavalry exercises indoors during inclement weather.

Chiesa dell'Incoronata - octagonal church built between 1586 and 1588 to house the mausoleum of Vespasiano Gonzaga, with a bronze equestrian statue of the founder (1588) and frescoes by Bernardino Campi. The octagonal form, symbolizing resurrection, draws direct inspiration from Florence's baptistery.

→

Walk along Sabbioneta's ramparts - the 2.5-kilometer hexagonal fortifications with five bastions and two gates (Porta Vittoria and Porta Imperiale) feature an accessible walkway offering views across the Po plain.

Cattedrale di Santa Maria Assunta - Romanesque cathedral built between 1059 and 1106, with a 13th-century Gothic bell tower. The central dome was decorated between 1526 and 1530 by Correggio, who depicted the Assumption of the Virgin with 150 figures in trompe-l'oeil creating the illusion of an opening to heaven. Titian, upon seeing this fresco, reportedly declared it worth its weight in gold - at the time, roughly 1,000 ducats.

Battistero di Parma - octagonal baptistery in pink Verona marble built between 1196 and 1307 by Benedetto Antelami, with five sculpted portals and 16 façade loggias representing the months, seasons, and zodiac signs. The 14-meter-diameter interior dome is covered with 13th-century Byzantine-style frescoes on a gold background. It stands as one of the rare medieval Italian baptisteries preserved with its complete iconographic program.

Camera di San Paolo - room in the San Paolo convent decorated in 1519 by Correggio for Abbess Giovanna da Piacenza. The frescoes depict putti in garlands of vine leaves and mythological figures, in a humanist program unusual for a monastic space. The room remained closed to the public for two centuries after the convent's dissolution.

Palazzo della Pilotta and Teatro Farnese - unfinished Farnese palace built between 1583 and 1622, whose interior Teatro Farnese (1618) ranks among Europe's first permanent Baroque theaters with a 40-meter-wide stage and wooden cavea seating 4,500 spectators. Entirely destroyed by 1944 bombing, the theater was reconstructed identically between 1956 and 1962 using original fragments.

→

Tasting of Prosciutto di Parma DOP and Parmigiano Reggiano DOP in historic center delicatessens - prosciutto aged minimum 12 months in the hills south of Parma, Parmigiano produced exclusively in the provinces of Parma, Reggio Emilia, Modena, Bologna, and Mantua.

→

Visit to a Parmigiano Reggiano cheese dairy in the countryside around Parma - local producers offer production and aging facility tours, with tastings of cheeses at different stages (12, 24, and 36 months).

Duomo di Modena - Romanesque cathedral built from 1099 by architect Lanfranco over the relics of San Geminiano, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1997 along with Piazza Grande and Torre Ghirlandina. The façade and portals are adorned with bas-reliefs by Wiligelmo (circa 1106-1120), considered among the first great works of Romanesque sculpture in Italy.

Torre Ghirlandina - Romanesque-Gothic bell tower rising 89 meters, built between the 12th and 14th centuries, whose name derives from the crown of Gothic balustrades encircling the spire. The tower houses the Secchia Rapita, a wooden bucket stolen from Bologna during the Battle of Zappolino in 1325 - an event that inspired Alessandro Tassoni's mock-heroic poem of the same name in 1622.

Piazza Maggiore di Bologna - central square measuring 115 by 60 meters, surrounded by public buildings constructed between the 13th and 16th centuries: the Palazzo dei Notai (1381), Palazzo d'Accursio (city hall seat since 1336), Palazzo dei Banchi (Vignola façade, 1568), and the Basilica di San Petronio whose unfinished façade still reveals the alternating brick-and-marble pattern.

Due Torri di Bologna - the Torre degli Asinelli (97.2 meters, built between 1109 and 1119) and Torre Garisenda (48 meters, with a 3.2-meter tilt). In the 12th century, Bologna counted over 180 towers built by noble families as symbols of power - only 22 remain today. Dante mentioned Torre Garisenda in the Inferno (Canto XXXI).

Archiginnasio di Bologna - first unified seat of the University of Bologna, built in 1563 by Antonio Morandi. The walls and ceilings are covered with 6,000 stemmi (coats of arms) of students and professors from 1563 to 1737. The Teatro Anatomico (1637), entirely in fir wood, served for public dissections in the presence of the bishop - a cleric blessed each cadaver before each session.

→

Tasting of aceto balsamico tradizionale di Modena DOP in an acetaia - traditional balsamic vinegar is aged minimum 12 years (Affinato) or 25 years (Extravecchio) in batteries of 5 to 7 barrels of different woods (oak, chestnut, cherry, ash, mulberry).

→

Walk beneath Bologna's porticoes - 62-kilometer network of covered arcades inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2021, connecting the city's principal monuments. The porticoes were mandated by municipal decree as early as 1288.

Basilica di San Petronio - Gothic basilica whose construction began in 1390 and was never completed - the lower façade in white and red marble contrasts with the upper section in exposed brick. At 132 meters long and 66 meters wide, it ranks as the world's fifth-largest church. A 66.8-meter astronomical meridian line traced on the floor in 1655 by astronomer Gian Domenico Cassini was used to reform the Gregorian calendar.

Fontana del Nettuno - monumental bronze fountain created by Giambologna (Jean de Boulogne) between 1563 and 1566, commissioned to celebrate papal authority over Bologna. Neptune stands 3.2 meters tall and dominates a 6-meter-high composition of sirens, putti, and dolphins. Authorities of the time required certain anatomical parts to be reduced for reasons of decency.

Santuario della Madonna di San Luca - Baroque sanctuary built between 1723 and 1765 on San Luca hill at 291 meters elevation, connected to Bologna's center by the world's longest portico: 3.8 kilometers and 666 arches constructed between 1674 and 1793. The preserved icon of the Virgin is traditionally attributed to Saint Luke, though historians date it to the 12th century.

Cineteca di Bologna - archive and cinematheque founded in 1963, one of Europe's most important for silent film preservation and digital restoration. The L'Immagine Ritrovata laboratory has restored over 5,000 films since 1992, including Fritz Lang's Metropolis and works by Chaplin.

→

Bolognese pasta all'uovo making class - the Bolognese repertoire includes tagliatelle (8mm width according to regulations filed with the Chamber of Commerce in 1972), tortellini, tortelloni, and maltagliati. Several cooking schools in the center offer classes.

→

Ascent to Santuario di San Luca via the 666-arch portico - 3.8 kilometers from Porta Saragozza with progressive views over Bologna and the Po plain.

Brisighella - medieval borgo of 7,500 inhabitants in the Faenza Apennines, built upon three gypsum outcrops rising from the Lamone valley. The three heights respectively bear the Rocca (fortress), Torre dell'Orologio, and Santuario del Monticino - three silhouettes defining the village's distinctive profile.

Rocca di Brisighella - fortress built by the Manfredi in the 14th century on the highest outcrop (approximately 200 meters), enlarged by the Venetians in 1503 and by Cesare Borgia during his Romagna campaign in 1500-1501. The view from the ramparts encompasses the entire Lamone valley and surrounding Apennines.

Via degli Asini (or Via del Muraglione) - 15th-century covered street built mid-height on the second outcrop, with brick arcades that allowed donkeys carrying gypsum extracted from the hills to travel sheltered from weather without crossing pedestrians in the lower alleys. Stretching 150 meters, it now ranks among Romagna's most characteristic promenades.

Torre dell'Orologio - clock tower built in the 14th century on the second outcrop, connected to Via degli Asini by a stairway carved into rock. The current tower dates from a 17th-century reconstruction and houses a still-functioning mechanical clock.

→

Tasting of Brisighella DOP extra virgin olive oil - one of Emilia-Romagna's rare DOP olive oils, produced since the Middle Ages in olive groves on hills around the village thanks to an exceptional microclimate for such a northern location.

→

Hike on the three-outcrop trail connecting the Rocca, Clock Tower, and Santuario del Monticino - 4-kilometer loop with views over the Lamone valley.

Cattedrale di Santa Maria del Fiore - Gothic cathedral whose construction began in 1296. The dome designed by Filippo Brunelleschi between 1420 and 1436 measures 43.7 meters in interior diameter and remained for over a century the largest dome in the Western world. For its construction, Brunelleschi invented lifting machines and a double-shell system using bricks laid in a herringbone pattern that required no centering.

Battistero di San Giovanni - Romanesque baptistery built between the 4th and 11th centuries, where Dante was baptized according to Florentine tradition. The three bronze doors constitute a sculptural program spanning two centuries: south door (Andrea Pisano, 1336), north door (Lorenzo Ghiberti, 1403-1424), and east door called the Gates of Paradise (Ghiberti, 1425-1452), which Michelangelo reportedly said deserved to be the gates of Paradise itself.

Piazza della Signoria and Palazzo Vecchio - Florence's central square since the 13th century, with Palazzo Vecchio (1299-1314) whose 94-meter tower dominates the city. The Loggia dei Lanzi (1382) shelters in open air Cellini's Perseus (1554) and Giambologna's Rape of the Sabine Women (1583). A copy of Michelangelo's David occupies the exact location of the original from 1504 to 1873.

Galleria degli Uffizi - museum housed since 1769 in offices built by Giorgio Vasari for Cosimo I de' Medici between 1560 and 1580. The 45 rooms preserve Botticelli's Primavera (circa 1482) and Birth of Venus (circa 1485), Leonardo da Vinci's Annunciation (1472-1475), and Raphael's Madonna of the Goldfinch (1506).

Ponte Vecchio and Oltrarno - bridge rebuilt in 1345 after a devastating flood, the only Florentine bridge to survive 1944 destruction because Hitler personally ordered it spared. Goldsmith shops replaced butchers in 1593 on order of Cosimo I, who found the meat odor unbearable from the Corridoio Vasariano overlooking the bridge.

→

Walk through the Oltrarno district - between Santo Spirito, restoration workshops of Via Maggio, and Boboli Gardens, the Oltrarno preserves a neighborhood life less touristy than the right bank.

→

Climb to Brunelleschi's dome (463 steps, 91 meters) or Giotto's campanile (414 steps, 84 meters) for views over Florence's rooftops.

Cappelle Medicee - Medici funeral chapels attached to the Basilica di San Lorenzo, with the Sagrestia Nuova designed by Michelangelo between 1520 and 1534 to house the tombs of Lorenzo II and Giuliano II de' Medici. The allegorical figures of Day, Night, Dawn, and Dusk (1524-1534), sculpted in white Carrara marble, were left unfinished at Michelangelo's flight from Florence in 1534.

Greve in Chianti - borgo of 14,000 inhabitants on the SR222 Chiantigiana, whose triangular arcaded square from the 19th century is surrounded by wine and olive oil producers. The Chianti Classico DOCG zone was delimited by grand ducal decree as early as 1716 by Cosimo III de' Medici - one of the first controlled wine appellations in history.

Panzano in Chianti - hilltop borgo perched at 478 meters elevation between Greve and Castellina, with 360-degree views over the Chianti hills from the central piazza and medieval castle ruins. The village overlooks one of the most renowned Chianti Classico production zones.

Radda in Chianti - medieval borgo of 1,600 inhabitants at 530 meters elevation, former capital of the Lega del Chianti since 1384. The 15th-century Palazzo del Podestà, whose façade is covered with 51 stemmi in pietra serena of successive podestas, dominates the central piazza.

→

Scenic Chianti drive on SR222 Chiantigiana between Florence and Radda - 60 kilometers through vineyards, olive groves, and cypress alleys between Greve, Panzano, Castellina, and Radda.

→

Chianti Classico DOCG tasting at a wine estate around Radda - the Gallo Nero (black rooster), symbol of the appellation since the 13th century, appears on the label of all producers belonging to the consortium founded in 1924.

Monteriggioni - fortified village of 570 inhabitants within medieval walls built by the Republic of Siena between 1213 and 1219 to guard the northern frontier with Florence. The circular 570-meter perimeter wall is flanked by 14 square towers 15 meters tall. Dante Alighieri compared these towers to giants standing in the pit of Malebolge in the Inferno (Canto XXXI, verses 40-45), confirming the walls were already complete before 1321.

Monteriggioni walkway - the 570-meter interior walkway is open to the public and allows a complete circuit of the ramparts with views over the Chianti hills to the north and Crete Senesi to the south. Of the 14 original towers, 11 still stand at their initial height.

Chiesa di Santa Maria Assunta - 13th-century Romanesque church at the center of Piazza Roma, built simultaneously with the walls. The austere interior preserves a 14th-century altarpiece and a Virgin and Child in painted terracotta attributed to Donatello's workshop.

→

Complete circuit of Monteriggioni's ramparts at sunset - the illuminated walls in evening and the village of 570 inhabitants create a timeless atmosphere, very different from daytime crowds.

→

Piazza del Campo - shell-shaped medieval square with a 333-meter perimeter, built from 1293 by gently sloping the ground toward Palazzo Pubblico. The nine radiating sectors recall the Government of Nine (1287-1355), Siena's period of greatest prosperity. The Palio dei Cavalli has been run here each year on July 2 and August 16 since the 17th century, with ten jockeys representing ten of the seventeen contrade.

Torre del Mangia - Palazzo Pubblico tower rising 102 meters, built between 1338 and 1348. Its summit exactly matches the height of the cathedral nave, signifying the balance between civil and religious power desired by the Government of Nine. The tower owes its name to its first bell-ringer, Giovanni di Duccio, nicknamed Mangiaguadagni for his prodigality.

Duomo di Siena - Gothic cathedral built between 1196 and 1348, with alternating white and green marble cladding. The interior floor of 56 inlaid marble panels (14th-16th centuries) is fully visible only during a few weeks in August-September. The Piccolomini Library (1502) preserves illuminations by Attavante degli Attavanti of exceptional precision.

Museo dell'Opera del Duomo - museum installed in the unfinished bay of the cathedral expansion, halted by the Black Death of 1348 which killed two-thirds of Siena's population. Duccio di Buoninsegna's Maestà (1308-1311), a monumental painting measuring 4.5 by 4.9 meters, was carried in procession from the artist's workshop to the cathedral by the entire population in 1311.

Contrade di Siena - Siena's seventeen historic neighborhoods, each with its own museum, church, baptismal font, and colors. Rivalry between contrade dates to the 12th century and still structures the city's social life today.

→

Visit to a contrada museum - each contrada has a museum recounting its Palio victories, with costumes, flags, and heads of winning horses.

→

Walk along Siena's medieval ramparts - the 7 kilometers of 14th-century walls offer views over the Crete Senesi hills to the south and Chianti to the north.
