Lucca Province Circuit: Medieval Villages, Garfagnana and Versilia Coast

Intact Renaissance ramparts, a Roman amphitheatre turned village square, the Devil's Bridge over the Serchio, a village submerged beneath a reservoir lake, marble workshops frequented by Michelangelo. Lucca province reveals a Tuscany the crowds don't know.

From Lucca and its Renaissance ramparts, the circuit climbs to the wine hills of Montecarlo, descends toward the Devil's Bridge at Borgo a Mozzano and the thermal baths of Bagni di Lucca before plunging into medieval Garfagnana: Castelnuovo where Ariosto served as governor, Barga the town of Giovanni Pascoli, Coreglia Antelminelli and its figurinai exporting plaster art. The descent to the sea passes through Camaiore and its Benedictine abbeys, Pietrasanta the Little Athens of marble where Michelangelo, Botero, and Mitoraj worked, Forte dei Marmi and its pier from which Carrara blocks departed. Mountain and coastline, stone and marble, poetry and sculpture.

Key highlights (4)
  • Lucca: Renaissance city encircled by ramparts intact since 1513
  • Barga: medieval village classified among Italy's most beautiful, homeland of Pascoli
  • Pietrasanta: world capital of marble sculpture since Michelangelo
  • Castelnuovo di Garfagnana: medieval fortress residence of Ariosto

Piazza dell'Anfiteatro: Lucca's oval plaza is no reconstructed set piece—it traces the exact perimeter of a 2nd-century AD Roman amphitheatre that once held 10,000 spectators. Medieval residents built their houses directly against the ancient stone tiers, erasing the arena but preserving its elliptical form. The 19th-century restoration by architect Lorenzo Nottolini cleared the square and opened the four arched passages that still produce the element of surprise described by Grand Tour travelers. Enter through one of the vaulted passages to understand how an entire city grew from the bones of a Roman monument.

San Martino Cathedral: Founded in the 6th century by Bishop Fridian, rebuilt from the 11th century onward, and completed in the 16th–17th centuries, the cathedral displays a distinctive asymmetrical façade—the architect had to narrow the final arcade to fit the pre-existing bell tower, creating an elegant dissonance. Inside, the main treasure is the Volto Santo, a 13th-century wooden crucifix traditionally attributed to Nicodemus and carried in a candlelit procession every September 13th since the Middle Ages. In the sacristy, the tomb of Ilaria del Carretto (1406) sculpted by Jacopo della Quercia shows the young wife of Paolo Guinigi lying with a dog at her feet—a symbol of marital fidelity—in a style that would influence Donatello.

Torre Guinigi: The Guinigi family's red brick tower, erected in the late 14th century, reaches 44.25 meters. Its unique feature is a garden of holm oaks planted at the summit—a symbol of rebirth chosen by the Guinigi, then lords of Lucca. Paolo Guinigi, who ruled the city from 1400 to 1430, made this tower the emblem of his mercantile and banking power. Climb the 230 steps for sweeping views of tiled rooftops, ramparts, and, on clear days, the Apuan Alps.

San Michele in Foro Church: Built on the site of the ancient Roman forum, San Michele displays a 12th-century Pisan-Lucchese façade with four tiers of sculpted columns, each different from the next. Throughout the Middle Ages, the building also served as the courthouse for the Republic of Lucca. Inside: a 14th-century frescoed Virgin and Child and a glazed ceramic Madonna by Luca della Robbia.

Puccini's Birthplace (Museo Puccini): Giacomo Puccini was born on December 22, 1858, in this apartment on Corte San Lorenzo, where the Puccini family had served for five generations as organists and chapel masters at the cathedral. The museum preserves the Steinway & Sons piano purchased in 1901 on which he composed Turandot, opera manuscripts, costumes, and stage photographs. Puccini bought back the family apartment after his sister sold it—proof of the composer's attachment to his native city.

→

Bike ride on the ramparts: Built between 1513 and 1650 along a 4.2 km circuit, Lucca's Renaissance walls constitute one of the best-preserved examples of modern fortifications in Europe. Thirty meters wide at the base and 12 meters high, they never saw combat—Lucca maintained its independence through diplomacy until 1799. Today they're transformed into a tree-lined promenade bordered by plane trees and oaks, accessible on foot or by bike. Rentals are available at the main gates.

→

Colline Lucchesi DOC wine tasting: The Colline Lucchesi appellation covers the hills immediately north and east of Lucca. It produces Trebbiano-based whites and Sangiovese-based reds cultivated on these slopes since Roman times. Several estates in the province offer tastings.

Palazzo Mansi - National Museum: Built in the 17th century for a wealthy family of Lucchese merchants, this palace preserves its state apartments almost intact—music room with allegorical frescoes, hall of mirrors, painting collections from the Medici and Grand Duchy holdings. Lucca built its fortune between the 13th and 15th centuries on silk trade, exporting fabrics as far as England and Flanders. This palace is the direct architectural expression of that wealth.

Palazzo Pfanner and Gardens: Built in 1660 for the Moricani family, then purchased in the mid-19th century by the Austrian-origin Pfanner family—who still own it. It opens onto one of the rare Italian-style gardens still preserved within the walls, with a central fountain surrounded by statues of deities and seasons. Jane Campion's film Portrait of a Lady was partly shot here.

Via Fillungo and Historic Center: The main artery of the pedestrian center concentrates historic cafés, artisan shops, and fragments of medieval architecture. The Torre delle Ore, whose first clock dates to 1390, marks its northern end. Puccini frequented Caffè Di Simo, an establishment opened in 1875 whose Art Nouveau storefront is still visible.

Villa Guinigi - National Museum: Former country residence of Paolo Guinigi, lord of Lucca from 1400 to 1430, built outside the medieval walls in the style of Venetian Palladian villas with an arcaded loggia. The museum houses collections ranging from 8th-century BC Etruscan discoveries to medieval sculptures from churches suppressed during the Napoleonic era.

→

Antiques market: Every third Sunday of the month, the antiques market spreads across the main squares of the historic center—Piazza San Martino, Piazza San Giovanni, Piazza Bernardini—with several hundred vendors. It's one of the most frequented antiques markets in Tuscany.

Rocca di Cerruglio: The village of Montecarlo owes its name to Charles IV of Luxembourg—Mons Charoli in Latin—who contributed in 1333 to liberating Lucca from Pisan occupation. The Republic of Lucca then had the destroyed village rebuilt on Cerruglio hill and erected this three-tower fortress: the semi-circular keep, the Tower of the Apparition, and the Tower of Saint Barbara, the latter named for a legend that the Virgin appeared to defend the village during a 15th-century Pisan siege. The fortress is open to visitors with views over the plain of Lucca and the Valdinievole.

Collegiata di Sant'Andrea: In the heart of the village, this collegiate church founded in the 14th century preserves its original Gothic façade and portal, remodeled in Baroque style in the 18th century. Inside, a fresco of the 15th-century Florentine school depicts the Madonna del Soccorso snatching a child from the devil's claws. Two plaques commemorate visits by poet Giuseppe Ungaretti in the 1960s and composer Giacomo Puccini.

Teatro dei Rassicurati: Built in the early 18th century, this tiny Italian-style theater ranks among the five smallest in Italy. It takes its name from the Academy of the Rassicurati, a local learned society founded in 1690. Puccini visited several times. The auditorium, with its lacquered wooden boxes and ceiling frescoes, has remained almost unchanged since construction.

Pieve di San Piero in Campo: At the foot of the hill, this 12th-century Romanesque parish church with three naves is one of the oldest Christian buildings in Lucca province. Its sober limestone façade and austere interior contrast with the Baroque exuberance of the collegiate church perched atop the village.

→

Montecarlo DOC tasting at family estates: Montecarlo's winemaking tradition dates to Roman times, but it was in 1870 that a local landowner imported French grape varieties from Bordeaux (Sauvignon, Sémillon, Cabernet, Merlot) and Burgundy (Pinot Blanc, Pinot Gris) to blend with Tuscan Trebbiano. Montecarlo Bianco DOC—recognized in 1969—has been celebrated since the 15th century: Pope Gregory XII praised it in the early 1400s. Village estates welcome visitors year-round.

Ponte della Maddalena (Devil's Bridge): This humpback bridge spans the Serchio with a main arch rising 18.50 meters flanked by four smaller asymmetric arches—a silhouette that has intrigued travelers since the 16th century, notably Michel de Montaigne who described it in his 1581 Travel Journal. The structure probably dates to the 11th century, but its current appearance is due to Castruccio Castracani, lord of Lucca, who had it rebuilt between 1324 and 1327. On the Via Francigena pilgrimage route from Canterbury to Rome, the bridge held during World War II: retreating German troops had mined it but it was never destroyed, which proved crucial for the Allies. Legend tells that the mason, despairing of floods from the Serchio, made a pact with the devil to finish the work in one night—in exchange for the first soul to cross the bridge. The village priest sent a dog across, outwitting the devil. They say this white dog still walks the bridge on October evenings.

Bagni Di Lucca

Historic Thermal Center and Terme Bagni di Lucca: Known since Roman times, Bagni di Lucca's hot springs took off in the 11th century thanks to Countess Matilda of Canossa, before becoming in the 19th century one of Europe's most fashionable spa resorts. Elisa Baciocchi, Napoleon's sister and Princess of Lucca, had the Bernabò establishment built here in 1810, where waters emerge naturally at 39.2°C. The thermal complex has welcomed Byron, Shelley, Montale, Henry James, and Puccini. The English called the place "the Switzerland of Tuscany."

Real Casino of Ponte a Serraglio: Inaugurated in 1839, this casino was one of the very first in Europe—some sources credit Bagni di Lucca with inventing roulette around 1837. The Art Nouveau building, restored and reopened in 2009 after years of closure, overlooks the banks of the Lima and constitutes one of the best-preserved testimonies of 19th-century aristocratic tourism.

Ponte delle Catene (Chain Bridge): This iron suspension bridge designed by Lorenzo Nottolini—the same architect who redesigned Lucca's Piazza dell'Anfiteatro—is one of the oldest metal bridges still standing in Italy. It spans the Lima between the hamlets of Ponte a Serraglio and Villa Fiori.

Italy's first Anglican church and English cemetery: The British community established in Bagni di Lucca from the early 19th century built the country's first Anglican church here. The adjoining cemetery holds the graves of English, American, and other nationalities who chose to end their days in this valley.

→

Hiking in Orrido di Botri: A few kilometers north, this canyon carved by the Pelago torrent through the Apennines offers vertical limestone gorges and waterfalls. The ascent follows the riverbed for several kilometers; a protective helmet is recommended. Access is regulated and best done with a local guide.

→

Thermal treatments: The Bagni Caldi thermal establishments offer pools, natural steam grottos, and treatments using sulfurous waters whose natural temperature approaches 39°C.

Rocca Ariostesca: This trapezoidal fortress has dominated Piazza Umberto I since the 10th century, but its 14th-century reconstruction by Castruccio Castracani gave it its current form. It takes its name from poet Ludovico Ariosto—author of Orlando Furioso—who resided here from 1522 to 1525 as Este governor for Alfonso I d'Este, Duke of Ferrara. Ariosto, who called Garfagnana "land of wolves and brigands," nevertheless wrote several of his Satires here. The central tower, equipped with a civic clock, and the terrace added in 1675 overlooking the square make it one of the most complete examples of communal fortification in the region. Every Thursday, the weekly market is held at the foot of the rocca—a tradition so ancient that a ducal decree once forbade arresting criminals on that day.

Cathedral of Santi Pietro e Paolo: Built in the 15th century in Renaissance style on an 11th-century Romanesque church, then largely remodeled in the Baroque era. The austere gray stone façade contrasts with the richness of the interior: apse redone in the 19th century, Baroque altars, and series of 15th–16th century paintings. The church is a waypoint on the Via Matildica del Volto Santo linking Mantua to Lucca.

Medieval Historic Center and ramparts: The fortified heart of Castelnuovo, at the confluence of the Serchio and Turrite Secca, preserves its medieval alleys, arched gates (Porta dei Borselli), and Clock Tower. The town is the administrative capital of Garfagnana and the meeting point of two historic pilgrimage routes.

→

Sentiero dell'Ariosto to Fortezza di Mont'Alfonso: A 6 km hiking trail dedicated to the poet, starting from Rocca Ariostesca, crossing the bridge over the Turrite Secca and climbing toward the village of Torrite then the bastioned fortress of Mont'Alfonso, built by Alfonso II d'Este between 1579 and 1586, with panoramas over the Apuan Alps and Apennines.

→

Thursday weekly market: Castelnuovo's market is the largest in Garfagnana, with cheeses, charcuterie (biroldo della Garfagnana, Colonnata lard), and mountain products.

Orecchiella Nature Reserve: Created in 1980, this 500-hectare reserve in the Tuscan-Emilian Apennines shelters chamois, deer, foxes, and vegetation of beech forests and alpine meadows. The Corfino visitor center offers exhibitions on local fauna.

Lago di Vagli and the sunken village of Fabbriche di Careggine: The artificial lake created in 1953 for a hydroelectric plant submerged the village of Fabbriche di Careggine. During the decennial draining of the dam, the ruins reappear—a phenomenon that attracts thousands of visitors each time. The site offers panoramas over the Apuan Alps.

→

Hiking in the Apuan Alps (Parco Regionale): The regional park protects the white marble peaks dominating Versilia. Trails of varying difficulty lead toward Pania della Croce (1858 m). CAI (Italian Alpine Club) refuges allow outings of 4 to 8 hours.

Duomo di San Cristoforo: Perched at the village's highest point (410 m), this Romanesque cathedral was built in stages from the 11th to 16th centuries in blocks of alberese, a local gray-cream limestone. Its 13th-century marble pulpit, the work of Guido Bigarelli, is supported by four columns, two resting on recumbent lions. Inside: a ciborium of the Holy Oils by Andrea della Robbia, a 14th-century Madonna del Molino, and a medieval polychrome wooden statue of San Cristoforo. From the churchyard, views extend over the entire historic center and, on clear days, to the Apuan Alps.

Palazzo Pretorio and Museo Civico del Territorio: Residence of Florentine commissioners from 1341 to 1859—when Barga chose to submit to Florence rather than Lucca, obtaining exemption from numerous taxes. The palace houses the municipal museum with archaeological collections (prehistory, Ligurians, Romans) and artworks from the Middle Ages and Renaissance. The old medieval prisons are still visitable.

Loggia dei Mercanti and Piazza Salvo Salvi: This loggia with slender columns was built in 1546 on orders from Cosimo I de' Medici to shelter Barga's market, whose main products were silk and salt. The stone lion (Marzocco) on the façade symbolizes Florence's political guardianship. Palazzo Pancrazi, seat of the town hall, displays a diamond-bossed façade characteristic of 16th-century Florentine architecture.

Teatro dei Differenti: Inaugurated in 1689 and rebuilt in 1795, this Italian-style theater is one of the best preserved in Tuscany. It was here that Giovanni Pascoli delivered on November 26, 1911, his famous speech La grande proletaria si è mossa in favor of the Italo-Turkish War in Libya.

→

Barga Jazz Festival (August): Since 1986, this internationally renowned jazz festival has animated the squares and alleys of the medieval historic center each summer. Musicians from around the world perform in this medieval setting for a week in August.

Casa Museo Giovanni Pascoli in Castelvecchio (4 km from Barga): Giovanni Pascoli, major poet of Italian Symbolism, purchased this 18th-century country house in 1895 and lived here with his sister Mariù until his death in 1912. He composed here his most celebrated collections, including Myricae and Canti di Castelvecchio, directly inspired by the surrounding landscapes. The museum preserves books, manuscripts, and everyday objects in the state the family left them. Pascoli is buried in the garden chapel, beneath cypresses and wisteria.

Medieval Historic Center of Barga: Classified among "Borghi più belli d'Italia" and labeled Cittaslow (slow cities network for quality of life), Barga has kept its 12th–14th century communal urban fabric almost intact. Enter through Porta Reale—so renamed after Grand Duke Leopold of Lorraine's visit—and climb the cobbled alleys to the Duomo. The presence of a significant Scottish community, heirs of hundreds of Barghigiani who left to settle in Glasgow between the 19th and 20th centuries as ice cream vendors and fish and chips shop managers, has earned Barga the nickname "most Scottish town in Italy."

→

Via dei Poeti and Castelvecchio surroundings: Marked trail linking the main sites associated with Pascoli in the countryside around Castelvecchio, with views of the Apuan Alps.

Museum of the Plaster Figurine and Emigration (Palazzo Vanni): From the 16th century until the 20th, the figurinai of Coreglia Antelminelli crafted plaster statuettes—religious subjects, mythological characters, celebrity busts—and left to sell them in the great cities of Europe and beyond. This migratory phenomenon, unique to this village, led to the founding in 1975 of the museum that today houses 1,300 pieces divided into two sections: one dedicated to productions from the 17th–20th centuries, the other to artisan emigration with period passports, letters, and documents. Among the most unusual pieces: 18th-century cats colored with candle smoke.

San Michele Arcangelo Church and Tower of Castruccio Castracani: Built in the 12th century on the site of an old Antelminelli fortress, San Michele church preserves an Annunciation from the Pisan school (1352), a 14th-century painting of the Archangel Saint Michael, and a pre-Romanesque ambo depicting a cow. The imposing adjoining bell tower—former watchtower of Castruccio Castracani, erected in the 11th century and approximately 25 meters high—dominates the village from the ridge at 595 meters altitude.

Historic Center and panorama over the Serchio valley: The medieval village, classified among "Borghi più belli d'Italia," preserves its vaulted streets, arched gates, and 16th-century palaces (Palazzo Antonimi, Palazzo Vincenti, 1572 town hall). From the square outside the walls, views extend over the entire Serchio valley and the silhouette of the Apuan Alps to the west.

→

Medieval Day on August 15th: Every August 15th, Coreglia Antelminelli organizes a historical reenactment with a procession of halberdiers, artisan market, tastings of local products, and street performances in the medieval village lit by torches.

Collegiata Santa Maria Assunta: Founded in the 13th century and profoundly remodeled in the Baroque era, this collegiate church is Camaiore's central monument. Its Romanesque façade contrasts with the decorative richness of the Baroque interior. The adjoining sacred art museum collections preserve furnishings, objects, and paintings from the 14th–16th centuries, including several noteworthy works of local provenance.

San Pietro Abbey: Founded by Benedictine monks between the 8th and 13th centuries, this three-nave Romanesque abbey with bell tower constitutes one of the oldest testimonies of Christianity in Versilia. Accessible on foot from the center via an olive-lined path, it's approached through a 14th-century gate preserving part of the original convent walls. The sober architecture in local limestone reflects Benedictine austerity.

Pieve dei Santi Giovanni e Stefano: Erected in the 12th century at the junction of the Via Francigena (of which Camaiore is the 26th stage), this Romanesque parish church has a bell tower with twin windows on three levels. Inside, the baptismal font was carved from a 3rd-century BC Roman sarcophagus—a characteristic reuse of medieval religious architecture.

Archaeological Museum of Palazzo Tori Massoni: Installed in a Renaissance palace, this museum preserves discoveries covering prehistoric, Ligurian, Etruscan, Roman, and medieval periods. The Camaiore region (from the Roman Campus Major, the great plain) was inhabited since the Neolithic, as attested by finds from the woods of Lucese.

→

Hiking in the hills of Versilia around Camaiore: The hills rising toward the Apuan Alps offer routes through vineyards, olive groves, and perched villages like Monteggiori, Casoli, or Gombitelli, with views of the sea and countryside. Camaiore is an ideal starting point for hikes toward Monte Prana.

Piazza del Duomo and Duomo San Martino: Pietrasanta's rectangular square is the artistic heart of the town. San Martino Cathedral, whose construction dates to the 14th century, presents a façade clad in local marble and an interior enriched with 19th-century frescoes and artworks from preceding centuries. The brick bell tower, commissioned around 1520 from Donato Benti—a collaborator of Michelangelo—conceals an extraordinary self-supporting helical staircase in brick, long overlooked. It was during the period when Michelangelo stayed in Pietrasanta to extract marble destined for the façade of San Lorenzo in Florence that this staircase was designed, according to most historians.

Museo dei Bozzetti (Museum of Sketches) - Chiostro di Sant'Agostino: Created in 1984 in the former convent of Sant'Agostino (14th century), this museum unique in the world documents the creative process of more than 350 Italian and international sculptors who have worked in Pietrasanta. It preserves over 700 maquettes and plaster models that preceded marble or bronze works now dispersed in museums and public spaces worldwide. Botero, Mitoraj, Folon, Cascella, Pomodoro, Miró, and Henry Moore are represented.

Marble and bronze workshops - Parco Internazionale della Scultura Contemporanea: Pietrasanta houses some fifty marble sculpture workshops and artistic bronze foundries. The town, nicknamed "Little Athens," has welcomed since the 15th century—when Michelangelo stayed here to work marble from nearby quarries—sculptors from around the world. Henry Moore was among the first modern foreigners to discover Pietrasanta in the 1950s; he was followed by Noguchi, Miró, Mitoraj, and Botero. The International Park of Contemporary Sculpture presents over 70 monumental works donated to the town, visible in the streets and squares.

Museo Archeologico Bruno Antonucci (Palazzo Moroni, 16th century): Located on Piazza del Duomo, this museum presents collections ranging from prehistory to the medieval era, with Etruscan and Roman pieces discovered in the Versilia territory.

→

Stroll among art galleries and sculptors' workshops: Pietrasanta counts more than one gallery per 1,200 inhabitants—a record density even in Tuscany. Several workshops open their doors to the public, allowing observation of artisans working marble or bronze.

→

Thursday weekly market and antiques market (1st Sunday of month): The Thursday market covers Piazza del Duomo and adjacent streets with food, crafts, and local products. The first Sunday of the month, antique dealers set up in the same squares.

Fortino (Fort Lorenz): At the center of Piazza Garibaldi, this quadrangular fort was built in the late 16th century on orders of Grand Duke Pietro Leopoldo of Tuscany, initially to protect the coastline and store marble from the Apuan Alps before loading it onto ships. The town takes its name from this fort—Forte dei Marmi literally means "fort of the marbles." Until April 26, 1914, Forte dei Marmi was attached to Pietrasanta; it became an independent municipality on that date. The fort today houses the Museum of Satire and Caricature, founded on the collections of the Political Satire Prize created in 1973.

Pontile (historic pier): Built between 1867 and 1877 to load marble from the Apuan Alps directly onto ships, this wharf equipped with rails and a steam crane was destroyed during World War II and rebuilt in 1955. The walk on the jetty, with the Apuan Alps behind and the Tyrrhenian Sea ahead, is the emblematic panorama of Forte dei Marmi.

Parco della Versiliana: On the border between Forte dei Marmi and Marina di Pietrasanta, this park of umbrella pines spanning several dozen hectares was frequented by literary and artistic figures of the 19th and 20th centuries. It now hosts a renowned summer cultural festival (theater, concerts, exhibitions) throughout Italy.

→

Beach and swimming at Versilia: The beach establishments of Forte dei Marmi, with their white-striped tents and wooden cabanas, are set on a fine sand beach of about 5 km. The sandy seabed slopes gently into the Tyrrhenian Sea.

→

Excursion to the marble quarries of Carrara: About 20 km north, the quarries of the Apuan Alps have been exploited since Roman times. Michelangelo came to personally select his blocks. Guided tours by all-terrain vehicle allow approach to active quarry faces.

Pontile di Lido di Camaiore: The wooden pier of Lido di Camaiore, symbol of the seaside resort, extends into the Tyrrhenian Sea facing the Apuan Alps. Less frequented than those of Forte dei Marmi or Viareggio, this promenade on stilts offers a peaceful panorama over the Versilia coastline.

Beach and waterfront: Lido di Camaiore stretches for several kilometers of fine sand between the Tyrrhenian Sea and the pine forest. Less fashionable than Forte dei Marmi, the resort offers family beach establishments with all the infrastructure of traditional Italian beach culture. The coastal bike path links Lido di Camaiore to Forte dei Marmi in the north and Viareggio in the south.

❓ Frequently asked questions

What weather should you expect?

Spring (April-June): Perfect weather, blooming landscapes, comfortable temperatures, fewer crowds, excellent value, Easter celebrations, ideal touring conditions.

Summer (July-August): Warmest weather, peak tourist season, highest prices, crowded attractions, vibrant atmosphere, beach season, festival time.

Fall (September-October): Excellent weather, harvest season, wine tourism, comfortable temperatures, fewer crowds, beautiful colors, culinary focus.

Winter (November-March): Mild southern weather, Christmas markets, lowest prices, minimal crowds, cultural focus, cozy atmosphere, indoor attractions.

We believe you will enjoy this itinerary best by dedicating between 8 and 12 days. The next step will allow you to adjust the duration of your stay.
