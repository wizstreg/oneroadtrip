Eure Road Trip: From Giverny to Normandy's Finest Medieval Villages

Journey through Eure, land of Impressionism and medieval heritage. From Monet's gardens in Giverny to Richard the Lionheart's Château Gaillard, from the Most Beautiful Villages of Lyons-la-Forêt and Le Bec-Hellouin to Pont-Audemer's Norman Venice, this route reveals secret Normandy between the Seine and ancient forests.

Claude Monet's House and Gardens in Giverny. Normandy's second-most visited site, this Impressionist sanctuary was the master's refuge for 43 years. The Clos Normand unfolds its flower beds beneath rose-covered arches, while the water garden reveals the iconic Japanese bridge and water lilies that inspired the monumental canvases. The pink stucco house preserves the reconstructed studio, the yellow dining room with its Japanese print collection, and the kitchen with its blue Rouen tiles.

Museum of Impressionisms Giverny. Steps from the Monet Foundation, this contemporary museum presents rotating exhibitions dedicated to Impressionism and its global influence. The building harmonizes beautifully with landscaped gardens that extend the spirit of the place.

Vernon's Old Mill. Perched on the pillars of a 12th-century medieval bridge, this 16th-century half-timbered house seems to defy the laws of balance. A historic monument since 2025, this suspended waterwheel mill immortalized by Monet is one of France's last inhabited bridges. Facing it, the Château des Tourelles once guarded the fortified town's entrance.

Notre-Dame Collegiate Church of Vernon. This church of mixed styles bears witness to eight centuries of religious architecture. The Romanesque façade neighbors Gothic vaults and Renaissance additions. Inside, admire the ancient stained glass windows and the flamboyant rose window.

Old Vernon and Rue Potard. Spared by bombings, this medieval street has preserved its 15th and 16th-century half-timbered houses. The Archives Tower, the castle's former keep, dominates Place de l'Ange. A heritage trail punctuated by interpretive markers retraces this royal city's history.

→

Bike ride along La Seine à Vélo. The cycling route connects Giverny to Vernon following the river. From Vernon station, shuttles and bike rentals allow car-free access to Giverny. The journey crosses landscapes that inspired the Impressionists.

→

Château de Bizy in Vernon. Nicknamed Normandy's little Versailles, this 18th-century château hosted Louis XV and the Bonaparte family. The monumental stables and park with fountains by Pierre Contant d'Ivry are worth the detour.

Château Gaillard. Richard the Lionheart's proud fortress has dominated one of the Seine's most beautiful loops for 800 years. Built in just two years (1196–1198), this feat of military architecture prompted the King of England to exclaim: 'How beautiful she is, my one-year-old daughter!' The keep offers breathtaking panoramas over the valley and Petit-Andely below. Guided tours available April through November.

Petit-Andely. This medieval village born in the castle's shadow retains its picturesque charm. The 12th-century Saint-Sauveur church and narrow streets lined with old houses invite wandering. Facing the Seine, the quays offer the finest view of the perched fortress.

Notre-Dame Collegiate Church of Grand-Andely. Founded in the 6th century by Queen Clotilde, wife of Clovis, this church blends styles from the 13th to 17th centuries. Its 16th-century Renaissance stained glass windows rank among Normandy's finest. The Sainte-Clotilde fountain, a miraculous spring that appeared during construction, still draws pilgrims.

Nicolas Poussin Museum. Housed in a 17th-century mansion, this museum honors the classical painter born in Les Andelys in 1594. Local history collections, paintings, and art objects trace this strategic city's past.

→

Richard the Lionheart Loop hike. This 8 km walking trail starts from the Seine riverbanks, crosses Petit-Andely, and climbs to the castle via limestone hillsides. The viewpoints over the river's meanders are spectacular, especially at sunset.

→

Seine river cruise. Cruise ships dock at Les Andelys to admire Château Gaillard from the river. Boat tours reveal this majestic loop from a unique perspective.

Village of Lyons-la-Forêt. Ranked among France's Most Beautiful Villages and a Green Resort, this Norman jewel served as the backdrop for both adaptations of Madame Bovary (Jean Renoir 1933, Claude Chabrol 1991). Its 17th and 18th-century half-timbered houses, flower-filled streets, and 18th-century wooden market hall classified as a Historic Monument compose a portrait of preserved authenticity.

Lyons-la-Forêt Market Hall. Beneath their oak framework, these 18th-century halls have punctuated village life for three centuries. They still host the market (Thursday, Saturday, and Sunday mornings) and cultural events.

Mortemer Abbey. Normandy's first Cistercian abbey, founded in 1134 by Henry Beauclerc, William the Conqueror's son. Nicknamed 'France's most haunted abbey,' it houses the Museum of Legends and Ghosts in its 12th-century cellars. The White Lady of Matilda the Empress, Saint Catherine's fountain for singles, and the 18th-century dovecote fuel fantastic tales.

Saint-Denis Church of Lyons-la-Forêt. This 12th-century parish church houses an eagle-shaped lectern from Mortemer Abbey. Its peaceful cemetery invites meditation.

→

Hiking in Lyons Forest. At 10,695 hectares, this beech cathedral is Normandy's largest forest and one of Europe's most beautiful. Century-old beeches soar up to 45 meters high. Over 200 km of marked trails traverse this massif dotted with clearings and valleys.

→

Les Bordins Arboretum. In the forest's heart, this woodland garden presents a hundred tree species from around the world. A playful orienteering course allows families to discover botanical diversity while having fun.

→

Saint Catherine's Fountain. This Fouillebroc spring, nestled in the forest, is linked to Mortemer Abbey. Young women came here to pray for a husband within the year. A place steeped in mystery and romance.

Notre-Dame Cathedral of Évreux. A jewel of Gothic art, this vast sanctuary brings together styles from the 11th to 16th centuries. Its 70 stained glass windows constitute a masterpiece of glass art, including the famous 'Évreux Yellow'—a 14th-century silver-salt color whose golden brilliance has never been equaled. The flamboyant north portal by Jehan Cossart and the lantern tower with its 'silver steeple' complete this treasure. The contemporary organ by Pascal Quoirin sports a futuristic case designed by Bruno Decaris.

Évreux Belfry (Clock Tower). The sole vestige of medieval fortifications, this 44-meter flamboyant Gothic belfry built between 1490 and 1497 houses the 'Louyse' bell cast in 1406. Unique in Normandy, it symbolizes the communal freedoms of the Middle Ages and offers a panorama over the city.

Saint-Taurin Abbey Church. Founded in the 10th century by Richard the Fearless, this former Benedictine abbey preserves a priceless treasure: the shrine of Saint Taurin, a masterpiece of 13th-century medieval goldsmithing, considered one of France's most precious.

Museum of Art, History, and Archaeology. Housed in the former 15th-century episcopal palace flanking the cathedral, this museum traces Évreux's history from Gallo-Roman times (when it was called Mediolanum Aulercorum) to modern Reconstruction.

Iton riverbank promenade. This developed path along the river crosses Évreux's historic heart. Old washhouses, rampart remains, stone bridges, and a reflecting pool offer a romantic perspective on the cathedral and episcopal palace.

→

Gisacum Archaeological Site. A few kilometers from Évreux, this Gallo-Roman site reveals the vestiges of an exceptional 2,000-year-old city. The interpretation center offers guided tours, children's workshops, and themed events.

→

Évreux–Le Bec-Hellouin Greenway. This 42 km pastoral route follows an old railway line through woods, bocage, and villages. Accessible on foot, by bike, or on rollerblades, it passes near Château du Champ de Bataille.

Village of Le Bec-Hellouin. Eure's second village labeled among France's Most Beautiful Villages, this Norman gem enchants with its colorful half-timbered houses, flower-filled cobbled streets, and preserved atmosphere. Nestled in the Bec valley hollow, it offers a condensed essence of authentic charm that echoes its neighbor Lyons-la-Forêt.

Notre-Dame Abbey of Le Bec. Founded in 1034 by knight Herluin, this Benedictine abbey became one of Europe's greatest spiritual and intellectual centers. Two of its abbots, Lanfranc and Anselm, became Archbishops of Canterbury. The 15th-century Saint-Nicolas tower, the site's symbol, dominates the cloister and Maurist buildings with their elegant facades. Monks perpetuate monastic life and sell their delicate handcrafted ceramics in the shop.

Saint-André Church of Le Bec-Hellouin. Erected in 1039 and dependent on the abbey until the Revolution, this neo-Gothic parish church houses the tomb of Blessed Herluin, the abbey's founder.

Sainte-Françoise Romaine Monastery. Two kilometers from the abbey, 26 Benedictine nuns live according to the Rule of Saint Benedict in buildings constructed since 1950. You can buy handcrafted candles there and enjoy absolute calm for a spiritual retreat.

→

Le Bec-Hellouin Organic Farm. This permaculture farm, located 500 meters from the abbey, is a global reference in agroecology. Training sessions and tours reveal this productive agriculture without tractors or pesticides, where ecosystems interact harmoniously.

→

Château d'Harcourt and arboretum. Eight kilometers from Le Bec-Hellouin, this 12th-century medieval castle is surrounded by one of France's oldest arboretums (1802). Nearly 500 species of century-old trees compose this remarkable park.

→

Château du Champ de Bataille. Twelve kilometers away, this 17th-century château restored by decorator Jacques Garcia presents sumptuous interiors and exceptional French formal gardens. A journey into the French art de vivre.

Pont-Audemer, the Norman Venice. Crisscrossed by Risle canals, this former tanners' city has retained its medieval charm. Classified among France's Most Beautiful Detours and labeled a City of Art and History, it reveals its treasures along the water: 15th and 16th-century half-timbered houses, old skin-drying sheds, private mansions, and small stone bridges.

Saint-Ouen Church of Pont-Audemer. This imposing church blending Romanesque, Gothic, and Renaissance styles impresses with its 15th and 16th-century stained glass windows and remarkable organ case. The building dominates the town from the main street.

Ruisseau des Pâtissiers and Ruisseau des Carmes. These canals crisscrossing the old town bear names evocative of local history. Views from the small bridges strangely recall Venice, with reflections of colorful facades in the water.

Alfred Canel Museum. Housed in the former home of the historian and politician, this cabinet of curiosities presents local history collections, antique library holdings, and temporary exhibitions.

→

Kayaking on Pont-Audemer's canals. Trade the gondola for a kayak to glide along Risle branches through medieval quarters. The route passes under picturesque stone bridges and along the tanners' old wharves.

→

Mirliton tasting. This local specialty created in 1340 by Guillaume Tirel, known as Taillevent, cook to the kings of France, this rolled cookie filled with praline cream and sealed with chocolate is the pride of Pont-Audemer pastry makers. This is where Thierry Hermès lived before founding his famous Parisian house.

→

Marais Vernier. A few kilometers away, this largest peat marsh in France (4,500 hectares) is a paradise for migratory birds. Ramsar-labeled, it offers 44 hiking trails and wild basketry workshops in this preserved setting.

Notre-Dame Abbey Church of Bernay. Normandy's oldest Romanesque church still standing, it was built between 1013 and 1050 at the initiative of Judith of Brittany, William the Conqueror's grandmother, under the direction of builder monk Guillaume de Volpiano. Its three levels, exceptional for the era, and sculpted capitals (human masks, leafy heads, monsters) bear witness to the blossoming of Norman Romanesque art.

Bernay, City of Art and History. Eure's only town bearing this label, Bernay has preserved its historic center with 15th and 16th-century half-timbered houses. The 'Water, Stone, and Wood' trail guides visitors through winding alleys, old doorways, and manor houses via 62 pictograms scattered throughout the town.

Notre-Dame de la Couture Basilica. Becoming a pilgrimage site after the miraculous discovery of a Virgin statue, this Gothic church begun in 1340 houses funeral slabs and apostle statues from Le Bec-Hellouin Abbey, including a remarkable 15th-century Entombment.

Bernay Museum of Fine Arts. Housed in the former abbatial lodging, this museum presents collections ranging from archaeology to decorative arts. Contemporary art temporary exhibitions in the abbey's exceptional setting.

Cordeliers Convent. Two hundred meters from the abbey church, this 13th-century Franciscan convent preserves some cloister remnants in a private courtyard accessible from Rue de la Comédie.

→

Walk along the Charentonne. This river flows through Bernay and gives the town its particular charm. Developed riverbanks invite strolling between stone bridges and old mills.

→

Bernay Market. Every Saturday from 8am to 1pm, the market animates Rue Thiers and Place Sainte-Croix. Norman regional products, cider, Camembert, and teurgoule (oven-baked rice dessert) delight food lovers.

Eure Valley and its villages. Heading back toward Vernon, pass through the charming Eure valley villages: Chambray, La Croix-Saint-Leufroy, Houlbec-Cocherel. These preserved hamlets bear witness to rural, authentic Normandy.

Pacy-sur-Eure and the Eure Valley Railway. This tourist steam or diesel train covers 22 km of bucolic landscapes between Pacy and Breuilpont. The period station and historic rolling stock transport travelers into the railway atmosphere of the early 20th century.

→

Final stroll through Giverny. Before leaving Eure, return to wander the Impressionist village. Visit Claude Monet's grave in the small cemetery near the church, where the father of Impressionism has rested since 1926.

→

Local produce shopping. Farmhouse cider, Calvados, Camembert, Livarot, and Pont-l'Évêque: Eure's cheese shops and cider houses offer the authentic flavors of Normandy.
