Greece: Athens, Delphi and the Meteora

From the Acropolis to the sandstone pillars of Meteora, ten days to traverse three millennia of Greek civilization.

Begin in Athens at the foot of the Acropolis, then set out on the roads of mainland Greece: the oracle of Delphi, the suspended monasteries of Meteora, and the lakeside fortress of Ioannina. Ten days between ancient marble, Byzantine mosaics and dizzying cliffs.

Key highlights (4)
  • Athens: Acropolis, Agora and museums at the heart of ancient democracy
  • Delphi: sanctuary of Apollo and the Pythia's oracle
  • Meteora: monasteries built atop sandstone pinnacles at 400 meters
  • Ioannina: lakeside fortress with a thousand years of Byzantine and Ottoman history

The Parthenon is why you came. Built between 447 and 432 BC by architects Ictinus and Callicrates under the direction of sculptor Phidias, it took 22,000 tons of marble quarried from Mount Pentelicus, 17 km away, to raise it. Notice that the marble contains traces of iron that have oxidized over time—at sunrise or sunset, it turns honey-gold. Arrive as early as possible, right when it opens: crowds thicken after 9:30 AM.

The Erechtheion, built between 421 and 406 BC in Ionic style, houses the famous Porch of the Caryatids. These six statues of young women serving as columns are copies—the originals are in the Acropolis Museum. Take time to walk around the building: the north façade, the most ornate, is the best preserved.

The Propylaea form the monumental gateway to the Acropolis, designed by architect Mnesicles starting in 437 BC, in Pentelic marble. This five-opening portico was so imposing that ancient travelers described it as rivaling the Parthenon itself. Look at the ceiling of the central hall—the coffers painted in blue and gold are still partially visible.

The Temple of Athena Nike, perched to the right of the entrance, was built between 427 and 424 BC to celebrate victory over the Persians. It's the oldest Ionic temple on the Acropolis, and also the smallest: only 8 meters long. It was completely dismantled by the Ottomans in 1686 to build a gun battery, then reconstructed stone by stone in the 19th century.

The Theatre of Dionysus, carved into the southern slope of the Acropolis in the 6th century BC, is considered the birthplace of Western theater. This is where Aeschylus, Sophocles and Aristophanes first presented their plays, before 17,000 spectators. The marble seats you see date from the Roman renovation, but the original wooden stage goes back to the Great Dionysia festivals.

The Acropolis Museum, opened in 2009 and designed by Swiss architect Bernard Tschumi, houses 4,000 pieces exclusively from the sacred hill. Don't miss the third floor, oriented exactly like the Parthenon: you'll see 160 meters of friezes presented according to their original arrangement. The empty spaces, marked "BM," remind you that two-thirds of the marbles are still in the British Museum. Book your ticket in advance during high season.

→

Head up to the Acropolis right when it opens (8 AM in summer). The raking morning light gilds the Parthenon's marble and tourists are still scarce—it's the best window of the day to photograph the site without crowds.

→

After the Acropolis, descend toward Plaka, the historic neighborhood that stretches at the foot of the rock. Its paved stepped alleys, neoclassical houses and bougainvillea make it one of the most pleasant places in Athens to stroll in the shade. Avoid restaurants directly on the tourist thoroughfares—push into the side streets.

→

Moussaka is the dish to try in Athens: melting eggplant, spiced ground meat, and golden béchamel baked in the oven. The best versions are found in the tavernas of Plaka or Monastiraki, not in terrace restaurants with laminated photo menus.

→

In the evening, head back up toward the Acropolis rock at sunset from the Hill of the Muses or from Filopappou—free views, no crowds, of the Parthenon turning orange. It's the day's spectacle.

If the Acropolis is Athens' symbol, the Ancient Agora is its brain. Here, on these 25 hectares northwest of the sacred rock, democracy took shape: the Bouleuterion housed the Council of 500 members, the Tholos the standing committee. Socrates paced these very porticoes. Allow 1.5 to 2 hours to tour it completely with the museum—don't skip the latter, it preserves history's first voting ballots.

Take time to climb up to the Temple of Hephaestus, perched at the northwest corner of the Agora: it's the best-preserved Doric temple in Greece, built between 460 and 415 BC. Its survival owes to happy chance—converted into a Christian church in the 7th century, its walls and roof were preserved. The north and south friezes recount the exploits of Theseus and Heracles. Athenians themselves often mistakenly call it the "Theseion."

Entering the Stoa of Attalos, you're walking into history's first shopping mall. Built around 150 BC by Attalos II, king of Pergamon, in gratitude to the city that had welcomed him during his studies, this 116-meter portico on two levels now houses the Agora Museum. Reconstructed identically between 1953 and 1956 by the American School of Archaeology, it retains Doric columns on the ground floor and Ionic upstairs. Look for the ostraka—those pottery shards on which Athenians inscribed the name of the citizen to be banished from the city.

Right next to the Roman Agora, don't miss Hadrian's Library: built in 132 AD by the Roman emperor, it was much more than a library—a vast complex of 122 by 82 meters with reading rooms, gardens and a central pool. Today only the façade with Corinthian columns is clearly visible from the street, but the site merits a brief stop. Entry is included in the combined ticket for Athens' seven archaeological sites.

The National Archaeological Museum, founded in 1829, is Greece's largest museum. If you only have time for one room, go directly to the Mycenaean collection: the gold mask called "Agamemnon's," discovered by Schliemann in 1876 at Mycenae, is one of the most striking pieces of Greek antiquity. Reserve at least 2 hours if you want to cover the Cycladic collection and the bronzes.

→

Monastiraki, right next to the Agora, is the flea market district, shops of all kinds and local buzz. On Fridays and Sundays, the flea market spills into the surrounding alleys—you can find anything, from Byzantine icons to old Greek coins.

→

Push into the Psyrri neighborhood, just north of Monastiraki. It's the haunt of Athenian artists and night owls: walls covered in graffiti, used bookstores, cafés where people play cards until 3 AM. The neighborhood really only comes alive after 8 PM.

→

Spanakopita—spinach and feta pastry—is the ideal snack to hold you between visits. You'll find it in all the market bakeries, still warm in the morning. Pastitsio is the big dish of the day: baked pasta, ground meat, tomato sauce and gratinéed béchamel—moussaka pasta-style.

→

In the evening, try ouzo with a few mezze on a Psyrri terrace: cephalopods, olives, taramosalata. Ouzo is drunk diluted with cold water—it turns cloudy and the anise flavor softens. It's been the Athenians' aperitif ritual for generations.

The Panathenaic Stadium—Kallimarmaro in Greek, "of beautiful marble"—is the world's only stadium built entirely of white marble. Founded in 330 BC by statesman Lycurgus for the Great Panathenaea, it was completely rebuilt in Pentelic marble under Herodes Atticus in the 2nd century AD (capacity: 70,000 spectators). Fallen into ruin then pillaged for its stones in the Middle Ages, it was restored to host the first modern Olympic Games in 1896. You can run on the track: it's included in the ticket. Caution: in summer, the marble heats up—visit in the morning or late afternoon.

Lycabettus Hill rises to 277 meters, Athens' highest point. From the summit, you have 360° views of the Acropolis, Piraeus and, on clear days, as far as the island of Aegina. Climb on foot from Kolonaki (30 minutes) or by funicular from Aristippou Street. In the evening, it's the city's best spot to watch Athens light up.

Athens' National Garden extends over 15 hectares in the city's heart, planted by Queen Amalia in the 19th century. It's the Athenians' green oasis—ducks, turtles, shaded pergolas—a welcome break between visits. At the southern exit stands the Zappeion, a neoclassical palace from 1888 designed by Theophil Hansen, used for official ceremonies and exhibitions.

The Academy of Athens, the University and the National Library form the Neoclassical Trilogy of Panepistimiou Street, all three designed by Theophil Hansen between 1859 and 1902. The Academy is the most impressive: Ionic columns, colored friezes, statues of Apollo and Athena on their pedestals. It's the iconic photo of neoclassical Athens—and often ignored by tourists hurrying off to the Acropolis.

If you're feeling a bit weary of ancient sites, the Benaki Museum is an excellent antidote. Housed in a beautiful neoclassical mansion in Kolonaki, it traces Greek history from prehistory to the present day—Mycenaean, Byzantine, Ottoman, modern—in one fluid journey. It's also one of Athens' best-curated museums. Go up to the rooftop café terrace: the view over the gardens and city is worth the trip.

→

Kolonaki is Athens' upscale neighborhood, at the foot of Lycabettus. Its cafés in Kolonaki Square are perfect for a midday break to watch well-heeled Athenians—it's a local sport. Greek designer boutiques cluster in the streets around the square.

→

Plan your Lycabettus ascent for late afternoon, so you reach the summit an hour before sunset. When the light tips over the Acropolis and the city begins to glow beneath your feet, it's one of the strongest moments of an Athens stay. The little café at the summit stays open until nightfall.

→

Dolmades—vine leaves stuffed with rice, fresh herbs and lemon juice—are often served as mezze in the tavernas of Kolonaki or Plaka. Greek salad is always better here than elsewhere: thick tomatoes, cucumbers, Kalamata olives, a block of feta and dried oregano on top.

→

If you like Greek wines, look for an Assyrtiko from Santorini in evening restaurants: dry white, very mineral, with cutting acidity that comes from the island's volcanic soils. It's Greece's most internationally famous white wine, and one of the best to pair with seafood.

After three days of ancient Greece, pause at the Byzantine and Christian Museum—you'll discover another Greece, just as rich. Icons, mosaics, frescoes, liturgical embroideries: the collection covers fourteen centuries, from the 4th to the 15th. Some gold and tempera icons are stunningly refined. Allow about 1.5 hours to enjoy it unhurried.

Don't pass by Kerameikos: it's one of Athens' most moving sites, and one of the least crowded. This former potters' quarter was also the city's main necropolis, used from the 12th century BC through the Roman era. You'll find sculpted funerary steles, marble lions, and the Sacred Way that ran from here to Eleusis. The on-site museum houses the finest steles. On weekdays, you'll often have it to yourself.

Kapnikarea Church, nestled right in the middle of Ermou Street since the 11th century, is one of Athens' oldest Byzantine churches. Its central brick dome and gilded interior mosaics contrast radically with the street's commercial clamor. Push the door—it's often open in the morning.

Syntagma Square is the heart of modern Athens, dominated by the Parliament housed in the former Royal Palace of 1843. In front, the Tomb of the Unknown Soldier is permanently guarded by the Evzones, presidential guards in traditional costume—white pleated fustanella with 400 pleats, white stockings and shoes with black pompoms. The changing of the guard happens every hour, and on Sundays at 11 AM in full dress with music.

The Museum of Cycladic Art houses one of the world's most important collections of Cycladic art, dating from 3200 to 2000 BC. These white marble figurines with refined forms—barely sketched silhouettes, smooth faces—fascinated Picasso and Modigliani in the 20th century. The effect is striking: they could be contemporary sculptures.

→

For breakfast or a midday break, look for a café in Syntagma Square or the adjacent alleys for bougatsa—warm pastry filled with custard cream or fresh cheese, dusted with cinnamon and powdered sugar. It's the traditional Thessaloniki breakfast that's become popular throughout Greece.

→

Ermou Street, pedestrian between Syntagma and Monastiraki, is Athens' main shopping artery. This is where you'll encounter locals doing their shopping—not just tourists. Kapnikarea Church cuts it in two, a symbol of two overlapping millennia.

→

If you have the energy for an evening out, Exarcheia is Athens' most alternative neighborhood—bookstores, politicized bars, walls entirely covered in political graffiti, cafés open until the early hours. It's a very different Athens from Kolonaki or Plaka, and much more authentic.

→

For dinner, try keftedes—meatballs flavored with mint and cumin, fried and served as mezze. Mavrodaphne from Patras, a sweet red Greek wine, is the ideal companion to finish the meal with honey-based desserts.

The Temple of Poseidon raises its 15 surviving Doric columns on a promontory 60 meters high, at the southern tip of Attica. Built between 444 and 440 BC—at the same time as the Parthenon, part of Pericles' program—it served both as a sanctuary to the god of the seas and as a landmark for sailors returning to Piraeus from the Mediterranean. On clear days, you'll make out the islands of Kea, Kythnos and, in fine weather, as far as Serifos in the distance. Plan your arrival for sunset: the orange light on the white marble is spectacular.

On one of the temple's columns, look for the carved name of Lord Byron, who visited the site in 1810. The English poet was so fascinated by Sounion that he captured his reaction in verses from Don Juan (1819)—an ode to this wave-battered marble promontory he describes as the ideal place to listen to the sea in silence. This kind of graffiti would be a crime today—at the time, Byron was simply an enthusiastic Romantic tourist.

Take time to explore the site beyond the main temple: the cape also encompasses vestiges of ancient fortifications and, on the adjacent hill, the foundations of a temple of Athena Sounion destroyed in the 1st century AD. Less documented, this second sanctuary confirms that Sounion was a major cult site—not just a navigation point for sailors.

As you walk the promontory, keep in mind you're standing on one of Greece's most mythologically charged sites: according to legend, this is where King Aegeus threw himself into the sea, believing his son Theseus dead after the expedition against the Minotaur. Theseus had forgotten to change his black sails to white to signal his victory. The sea has borne the king's name ever since. Homer himself mentions Sounion in the Odyssey as "the sacred promontory of Athens."

→

The coastal road from Athens follows the Athenian Riviera for 70 km—a succession of beaches, coves and seaside resorts. Plan to leave in the morning to enjoy the water before summer crowds arrive. Vouliagmeni and Varkiza beaches are the most frequented by Athenians.

→

At Vouliagmeni, stop at the thermal lake of the same name: a turquoise body of water fed by underground hot springs, nestled in a partially open cave. The water hovers between 22 and 29°C year-round. Entry is charged but the swim is worth it, especially if you've spent several days at archaeological sites.

→

Arrive at Cape Sounion about an hour before sunset. The site stays open until sunset—check the exact time depending on the season. Caution: there's no shade on site, bring a hat and water. The terrain is rocky, avoid flimsy sandals.

→

After sunset, tavernas along the Sounion coast serve ultra-fresh locally caught fish: grilled octopus with oregano and lemon, Mediterranean fish soup, calamari and scampi. An ouzo or a Greek Alfa beer watching the last light fade over the Aegean: hard to beat as a day's ending.

The Sanctuary of Apollo clings at 600 meters altitude to the flank of Mount Parnassus—a site that functioned as an oracle for over a thousand years, from the late 7th century BC until the 4th century AD. According to tradition, this is where the omphalos stood, the stone Zeus designated as the center of the world. Don't expect a peaceful museum: in summer, tour buses arrive early. Plan your ascent at opening time (8 AM) to enjoy the site in calm.

The Temple of Apollo you see dates from the 4th century BC—it's actually the sixth built here, after the 373 BC earthquake ravaged the previous one. A Doric structure 60 meters long by 24 wide, with six columns on the façade and fifteen on the sides, it housed the Pythia seated on her tripod in its cella. Carved here was the maxim Socrates would make famous: "Know thyself." Three columns still stand—they give an idea of the original building's scale.

The theatre, built in the 4th century BC and restored by the Romans in the 2nd century, seated 5,000 spectators during the Pythian Games, held every four years in honor of Apollo. Climb to the upper tiers: the view over the Pleistos valley, the olive trees and the Gulf of Corinth in the distance is one of the site's finest. Look at the proscenium's bas-reliefs depicting the labors of Heracles before heading back.

Don't rush past this small building in Parian marble: the Treasury of the Athenians, built between 510 and 480 BC to celebrate the victory at Marathon, is one of the few monuments at Delphi entirely reconstructed—French archaeologists reassembled it stone by stone between 1903 and 1906. Its friezes depict Theseus, Heracles and an Amazonomachy. And on the exterior walls, carved in marble, you can still see two hymns to Apollo with their musical notation—among the only sound documents to have survived from Antiquity.

The stadium, at the very top of the site, is Delphi's least visited monument—yet one of the best preserved in Greece. Built in the 5th century BC then remodeled in the 2nd century AD by patron Herodes Atticus who replaced the earth tiers with stone, it measures 178 meters long for about 6,500 to 7,000 seats. The runners' starting line is still visible in the ground. Allow 15 minutes to climb from the theatre.

→

Take time to walk slowly along the Sacred Way from the sanctuary entrance to the temple. On each side once stood dozens of statues and treasuries offered by Greek cities in rivalry for prestige. The empty bases you see today once bore bronzes long since melted. This gradual ascent with the oracle at the end creates an atmosphere that even crowds can't quite erase.

→

The Delphi Archaeological Museum deserves half a day on its own. Its masterpiece is the Charioteer (Hêniokhos): a 1.82-meter bronze discovered in 1896 beneath the Sacred Way, buried during the 373 BC earthquake. Cast around 470-466 BC to commemorate the tyrant of Gela's victory at the Pythian Games, it's one of only five large classical bronzes preserved worldwide. Look closely at the eyes: iris in brown chalcedony, pupils in obsidian, tear ducts in pink coral—a stunning level of detail for a 2,500-year-old work.

→

In late afternoon, from the site's heights, the panorama over Parnassus (2,457 meters according to official sources) and the valley of olive trees descending toward the Gulf of Corinth is at its best. The oblique light brings out the nuances of the temple's blond stone. If you're sleeping in Delphi or Arachova, you can walk back down the path along the ravine—allow 30 minutes and good shoes.

→

Formaela from Arachova, a PDO sheep's cheese, is sold in the neighboring village shops. Grill it and drizzle with a thread of local honey—a combination you won't find everywhere in Greece. Arachova is 10 km from Delphi by a road offering some beautiful viewpoints over the valley.

→

Lamb kleftiko—slow-baked in a paper envelope with herbs and garlic—is the region's mountain specialty. You'll find it in most tavernas around Delphi and Arachova. The name comes from the "klephtes," brigands who cooked meat underground so the smoke wouldn't betray them.

→

Agiorgitiko, a red wine from Nemea (Peloponnese), pairs perfectly with the region's mountain cuisine. Full-bodied and low in tannins, it drinks young. You'll find it in local tavernas by the carafe.

The Castalian Spring lies in the ravine between the two Phaedriades rocks, just before entering the sanctuary. Every pilgrim had to purify themselves here before consulting the oracle—wash their hands, or their entire head for first-timers. Today it's a basin carved in the rock, with water still flowing. The setting, between cliffs over 200 meters high, has a beautiful austerity.

Below the main sanctuary, the Sanctuary of Athena Pronaia is often less visited—wrongly so. Its most famous monument is the tholos, a circular 4th-century BC edifice whose exact function remains unknown. Three exterior Doric columns and three interior Corinthian columns have been re-erected—they give the site an almost photogenic allure. Allow 20 to 30 minutes to descend from the main site entrance.

The Delphi gymnasium, midway between the tholos and the main sanctuary, was the training ground for athletes participating in the Pythian Games. You can still make out the running track (xystos) and the cold bath basins. A calm and often deserted spot you may have entirely to yourself.

Arachova (960 meters altitude) merits at least an hour's stop. Its sloping alleys open onto craft shops—hand-woven rugs, cheeses, local liqueurs—and viewpoints over the valley. In winter it's a lively small ski resort; in summer the village is considerably calmer. Look for the viewpoint above the main church for the best view of the surroundings.

Detour to Hosios Loukas, 37 km from Delphi—it's an hour's drive well worth the trip. This monastery founded in 945 by hermit Saint Luke the Stiriote has been UNESCO-listed since 1990 and preserves 11th-century gold-ground mosaics among the best preserved in the entire Byzantine world. The great katholikon, built in 1011, introduced an architectural plan—octagonal cross-in-square—that would become influential throughout Byzantine Greece. Inside, look up at the dome: Christ Pantocrator gazes down at you. The site opens in the morning; check hours beforehand as mid-afternoon closings often surprise visitors.

→

The walk through the Pleistos gorges, the river flowing below Delphi, is accessible from the village and takes 1.5 to 2 hours round trip. The path skirts thousand-year-old olive groves—some trees reportedly over a thousand years old. The calm contrasts with the archaeological site's bustle. Walking shoes recommended, the path is sometimes slippery.

→

In Arachova village, push open the doors of craft shops lining the main street. Hand-woven rugs and kilims have been the village specialty for several generations—red and black geometric patterns you'll only see here. Local cheeses (formaela, fresh goat cheese) are sold directly from producers.

→

En route to Hosios Loukas from Delphi, stop at the village of Distomo—known for the 1944 tragedy, when the SS massacred 218 inhabitants in reprisal. A sober memorial marks the village center. This context gives another dimension to the monastery visit that follows, a place of continuity and cultural resistance for a thousand years.

→

Hilopites—square dried pasta typical of the region—are eaten in soup or gratinéed with cheese. A simple, hearty dish you'll find in mountain tavernas around Arachova.

→

Feta saganaki, pan-fried in olive oil and served with honey and sesame seeds, is a classic starter in regional tavernas. The sweet-salty balance works particularly well with a glass of dry white wine.

→

Tsipouro from Thessaly, grape marc brandy sometimes lightly aniseed, is enjoyed chilled with mezze—olives, cheese, mountain charcuterie. It's the aperitif drink throughout central Greece. Don't confuse it with ouzo: tsipouro is more concentrated and without added sugar.

When you arrive in Kalambaka and see these sandstone pillars surging from the plain for the first time, you understand why monks chose this place in the 11th century. These rocks formed 60 million years ago from river sediments, uplifted then sculpted by erosion into pinnacles over 400 meters high. At their peak in the 15th century, 24 monasteries occupied the summits—built without roads, with materials hoisted in nets by pulley. Six remain active. A 17-km circuit from town lets you tour them by car with stops; allow a full day.

Start with the Great Meteoron—the largest, the oldest, the most visited, and for good reason. Founded in the 14th century by Saint Athanasius the Meteorite, its main church was rebuilt between 1545 and 1582 on the Mount Athos model, with frescoes of the Cretan school covering the walls floor to ceiling. Plan good shoes: you must climb 300 steps carved in rock from the parking lot. Arrive at opening (9 AM in season) or late afternoon—between 11 AM and 2 PM buses disgorge hundreds simultaneously. Don't leave without glimpsing the museum of manuscripts and icons from the 14th to 20th centuries.

Varlaam Monastery, perched at 373 meters on its pinnacle, is Meteora's second largest. Founded in the 16th century by brothers Theophanes and Nectarios Apsaradas, its katholikon preserves frescoes painted in 1548 by Frangos Katelanos, one of the great masters of post-Byzantine painting. Don't miss the old pulley system and net—still on display—used to hoist people and provisions before the stairs were built. And look at the immense 16th-century wooden barrel: 12,000-liter capacity.

Roussanou is a nunnery founded in the 16th century, built on a narrow rock and accessed by bridge. Its Cretan-school frescoes, still vivid in color, are among Meteora's finest. The garden extending to the cliff edge is tended and peaceful. It's often the least crowded monastery on the loop—go last, when the buses have left.

Agios Nikolaos Anapafsas is the first monastery on the road from Kastraki, the smallest and often least visited. Founded in the late 14th century, it's famous for its frescoes executed in 1527 by Theophanes the Cretan, considered the founder of the Cretan school of post-Byzantine painting. The interior space is tiny but the paintings are of exceptional quality. The terrace offers one of the best viewpoints over the rock pillars.

→

The Meteora scenic road runs 17 km and links all the monasteries with spectacular viewpoints between each stop. Some overlooks let you see several monasteries simultaneously stand out against the sky. In late afternoon, the golden light on the sandstone rocks is photographically remarkable. Kastraki village, at the foot of the rocks, is calmer and more photogenic than Kalambaka for overnight stays.

→

Hiking trails link some monasteries via ancient paths—those monks traveled before roads were built. One of the most interesting descends from the Great Meteoron to Kastraki (about 1 hour). Caution: some passages run along cliffs without guardrails. Maps available at Kalambaka tourist office.

→

In Kalambaka and Kastraki, artisans offer workshops on icons painted according to traditional Byzantine technique—gold ground, natural pigments, egg binder (tempera). These workshops often work on commission but sometimes accept visitors. Ask at your hotel or the tourist office.

→

Kontosouvli, pork skewer spit-roasted over charcoal, is Thessaly's meat specialty. You'll find it in Kalambaka tavernas, often served with country bread and Greek salad. A simple dish but particularly successful in establishments that make their own fire.

→

Boureki is a savory pie with zucchini, potatoes and feta, enclosed in crispy phyllo pastry. It varies from house to house according to cheese proportions and herbs used. In Thessaly, it's often made thicker than in Crete, where it's also popular.

→

Xinomavro from Naoussa, a red wine from northern Greece, is tannic and full-bodied with aromas of black cherry and spices. It pairs well with roasted meats and aged cheeses. Producers from the Naoussa region often age their cuvées several years in oak barrels.

Agios Stefanos is Meteora's most accessible monastery—no steps, just a bridge spanning the gap between the parking lot and entrance. Founded in the 14th century, it was transformed into a women's convent in 1961 after heavy damage during World War II. The nuns have carefully restored the spaces. The view from the terrace over the Thessaly plain is open and impressive.

Agia Triada (Holy Trinity) is the most difficult monastery to access: 147 steps carved in rock to reach a narrow pinnacle where gardens advance to the void's edge. This is where the 1981 James Bond film "For Your Eyes Only" was shot—filming equipment had to be helicopter-lifted. The view from the gardens is dizzying. If you only do one difficult monastery, make it this one.

The Cathedral of the Dormition of the Virgin in Kalambaka is often ignored by visitors heading straight for the monasteries. Built on an ancient temple's foundations, it preserves 12th-century Byzantine frescoes and a marble pulpit placed at the nave's center—a unique arrangement in Greece. Allow 30 minutes and it's a fine introduction to the region's religious art.

Kalambaka's Natural History Museum presents Meteora's geology and explains how these sandstone pillars formed over 60 million years from river deposits then were sculpted by erosion. A clear, well-illustrated exhibition, particularly useful if you have children. Allow 30 to 45 minutes.

The Pineios gorges, east of Kalambaka, are accessible by car and offer preserved nature very different from Meteora's mineral landscape. The river flows between wooded walls, with several old Ottoman stone bridges along the route. Traditional villages hide in perpendicular valleys. A half-day excursion to get off the classic circuit.

→

Sunrise at Meteora is an experience apart. Several viewpoints below the monasteries (notably near Kastraki) let you watch the rocks emerge from valley mist with golden, raking light. Plan to be on site before 7 AM—in summer the sun rises early. It's the ideal moment for photography and total silence.

→

Kalambaka market (Saturday mornings) offers products from the monasteries and neighboring farms: mountain honey, dried Pindus herbs, olive oil, green walnut jam. Shops around the main square also sell icons and craft items.

→

If you have a car, leave early morning to explore the 50-km loop from Kastraki toward the mountain villages of Agios Dimitrios, Gavros and Vlachava at 1,000 meters altitude. The ridge road offers a panorama over Meteora from above—a completely different perspective from below. En route: beekeepers, goat herds, troglodyte chapels and Liakou estate vineyards.

→

Galaktoboureko is central Greece's emblematic pastry: vanilla-scented semolina custard, enclosed in phyllo pastry and drizzled with lemon syrup. You'll find it in all Kalambaka pastry shops, often still warm late morning.

→

Stifado, beef stew with pearl onions, red wine, cinnamon and cloves, simmers for hours and is served in an earthenware pot. It's a seasonal dish that Kalambaka tavernas often cook in the evening. Order ahead if you want to be sure to find it.

→

Retsina Malamatina is a resinated white wine produced in Attica according to a tradition dating to Antiquity—originally pine resin was added to amphorae to preserve them. Very distinctive, it rarely pleases on first tasting but mellows with fatty Greek cuisine. Regional tavernas always serve it by the carafe.

Take the boat from the dock facing the fortress—the crossing takes five minutes and departures are regular. Nisi Island, in the middle of Lake Pamvotis, is smaller than it appears but concentrates several Byzantine monasteries and a gripping history: it was in Agios Panteleimon monastery that Ali Pasha took refuge in 1822, pursued by Ottoman troops who had withdrawn the sultan's trust. He was executed there at 82, and his decapitated body buried within the walls. His head was sent on a silver platter to Constantinople. The island circuit on foot takes 20 minutes.

Ioannina fortress occupies a rocky promontory jutting into Lake Pamvotis—three of four sides surrounded by water, making it a naturally defensible position. Justinian laid its first stones in the 6th century; Ali Pasha almost entirely rebuilt it in the early 19th century, completing work in 1815. He made it what you see today. Stroll the old town's alleys within the walls: at the entrance of the plane tree where Turks tortured rebels, cafés and silversmiths' shops coexist with vestiges of Ali Pasha's palace. One of the rare still-inhabited Ottoman town centers in Greece.

In the fortress's northeast citadel, don't miss the Aslan Pasha mosque—built in 1618 on a Byzantine church's site after the Christian population's expulsion. It now houses the municipal ethnographic museum, documenting the coexistence of Ioannina's three communities: Christian, Muslim and Jewish. What few know is that the city was long one of the great Sephardic Jewish communities of the Balkans. The costumes, silver jewelry and weapons on display give a concrete image of what the city was before the 20th century.

Five km from the center, Perama Cave merits the detour. Discovered by chance in 1940 by villagers seeking shelter during the war, it extends over a kilometer of galleries covering 14,800 m2. Archaeologists found the skeleton of a cave bear there—the species is extinct, no worries. Visits are guided group tours (Greek and English), lasting about 45 minutes. Bring a sweater: even midsummer, 15°C inside surprises. Last entry around 5:30 PM.

Descend to the old bazaar's alleys, around the fortress, and push open a silversmith's workshop door if you find one open. Ioannina was nicknamed "the silversmiths' city"—an Epirote tradition dating at least to the Ottoman era and whose techniques a few artisans still perpetuate old-style. The silverwork museum, inside the citadel, displays worked silver pieces from the Ottoman period giving an idea of the region's craftsmanship. The bazaar street is also the best place to buy homemade pita to go.

→

Late in the day, settle on a lakeside terrace by Lake Pamvotis and order local fish: smoked eel, carp, trout—fishermen directly supply the lakefront tavernas. Ioannina is also known for its frogs, exported as far as France. Stay until sunset: the lake reflects the mountains turning violet, and the fortress silhouette stands out against the water.

→

The walk on the fortress ramparts lets you follow the walls lakeside with spectacular views over Pamvotis and the mountains behind. In evening, the city lights' reflection on the water is striking. The café installed in the Its Kale acropolis, up high, is a good place to stop.

→

Bakaliaros skordalia, fried cod served with garlic-potato purée, is the emblematic fritter of popular Greek cuisine. You'll find it in traditional tavernas around the fortress, often on Fridays—an Orthodox calendar legacy.

→

Ioannina pita differs from industrial flaky pitas: a thick pie of homemade phyllo dough, filled with spinach and local cheeses, baked in a wood-fired oven. Each taverna has its recipe. It's THE specialty you eat standing or sitting lakeside, wrapped in paper.

→

Epirus tsipouro differs from Thessaly's by the total absence of anise: it's dry, almost neutral, and gives full place to the mezze. In kafeneion (traditional cafés) around the fortress, you'll be served a small carafe accompanied by a few slices of cheese and mountain charcuterie—a local institution.
