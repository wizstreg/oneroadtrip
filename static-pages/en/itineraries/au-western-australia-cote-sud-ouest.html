Southwest Coast of Australia: Vineyards, Ancient Forests & Wild Capes

Award-winning vineyards, crystalline caves, 400-year-old forest giants, and cliffs that tremble beneath the Southern Ocean's assault: southwest Australia reveals its treasures one by one.

From Margaret River to Mount Barker, Western Australia's southwest coast unveils its greatest treasures over 8 days: Cape Leeuwin where two oceans collide, Pemberton's towering karri forests, the turquoise pools of Greens Pool, Torndirrup's thundering cliffs, and the solitary summit of Bluff Knoll.

Key highlights (4)
  • Cape Leeuwin: the continent's most southwestern cape, where two oceans meet
  • Valley of the Giants: 40-metre-high walkway through the canopy of ancient tingle forests
  • Gloucester Tree: 61-metre giant karri, former forest fire lookout tower
  • Greens Pool: turquoise granite lagoon sheltered from the Southern Ocean

Cape Naturaliste Lighthouse, commissioned in 1904, stands sentinel at the northern tip of the Leeuwin-Naturaliste Peninsula, perched 100 metres above the Indian Ocean. Its 59 steps lead to a 360° viewing platform overlooking Geographe Bay and the jagged cliffs of the national park. It's one of the few operational lighthouses still standing in Australia. Between September and December, humpback and southern right whales cruise past the headland—the lighthouse serves as one of the region's premier whale-watching vantage points.

Canal Rocks is a spectacular granite formation where millennia of Indian Ocean erosion have carved natural channels through massive boulders. The sea surges through these rocky passages in seething torrents. A steel footbridge spans the formation, allowing visitors to venture right into the heart of the rocks without risk. The site is particularly dramatic during heavy swells, when spray explodes between the granite walls.

Injidup Natural Spa is a granite rock pool carved by nature itself, just minutes from Yallingup in Leeuwin-Naturaliste National Park. Indian Ocean swells funnel through a fissure in the rock and fill the basin with swirling, churning water—a natural jacuzzi courtesy of the sea. Sacred to the Wadandi people, the saltwater custodians who have inhabited these lands for millennia. Swimming is not recommended during heavy swells; low tide on calm days offers the safest conditions.

Sugarloaf Rock, visible from the Cape Naturaliste road, is an isolated granite monolith encircled by white foam. Ranked among the most photographed coastal formations in southwest Australia, it sits below the trails of the Cape-to-Cape Track. The view is most striking at sunrise, when the pink rock catches the first light.

→

Walk the Cape Naturaliste Circuit Loop (3.8 km, 1.5 hours return) from the lighthouse car park. The trail follows wooden boardwalks clinging to cliff edges, passes through Indian Ocean lookouts, and joins the Sugarloaf Rock Trail before looping back to the lighthouse. Breathtaking views of the two oceans colliding offshore.

→

Head down to Meelup Bay beach at dusk. Sheltered by the Cape Naturaliste headland, its intensely turquoise waters are among the calmest on the coast—perfect for a late-afternoon swim before heading into Margaret River town.

Jewel Cave is Western Australia's largest show cave, discovered in 1957 by a local timber worker named Ron Sinkinson. Its three enormous chambers—Jewel, Reflection, and Empress—are adorned with stalactites, stalagmites, helictites, and shawls sculpted over millennia from aeolian limestone. The showpiece is one of the world's longest straw stalactites, measuring 5.4 metres. Thylacine (Tasmanian tiger) fossils were found deep in the cave, trapped after a fatal fall.

Cape Leeuwin Lighthouse, commissioned on 10 December 1896, is mainland Australia's tallest lighthouse, its 39-metre tower built from local limestone. It marks the continent's most southwestern point—where the Indian and Southern Oceans collide in a turbulent embrace. Along with the Cape of Good Hope and Cape Horn, it's one of the three great capes that mariners must round during a circumnavigation. The lighthouse remains operational today. It's named after a Dutch ship, the Leeuwin ('Lioness'), which charted this coast in 1622.

Gnarabup Beach, 3 km from Margaret River town, is a long stretch of white sand protected by offshore reefs—far gentler than the neighbouring surf breaks. The rocks bordering the bay to the south form a chaotic jumble of erosion-sculpted formations. It's the locals' favourite spot for family swimming.

→

Guided climb to the top of Cape Leeuwin Lighthouse (176 steps) to hear keepers' tales and gaze out at both oceans from the lantern room 39 metres high. The Lightkeepers' Museum, housed in one of the original cottages, traces the lives of the families who've tended this isolated cape since 1896.

💡 Book online - tours limited to 10 people
→

In the afternoon, tour the cellar doors of the Margaret River Wine Region along Caves Road. The region is renowned for exceptional Cabernet Sauvignon and Chardonnay—it represents just 3% of Australian wine production but 20% of premium award-winning wines. Most estates offer free or paid tastings on weekdays; some open their cellars by appointment.

💡 Caves Road and Boodjidup Road have the most accessible estates from Margaret River town
→

End the day at Gnarabup Beach for sunset. The golden evening light on the reefs and white sand is one of the coast's most photogenic scenes. Dolphins are often spotted in the bay late in the day.

The Gloucester Tree is a giant karri (Eucalyptus diversicolor) standing 61 metres tall, 2 kilometres from Pemberton in Gloucester National Park. Selected in 1947 as a fire lookout tower, it's the second-tallest lookout tree in the world. Its 153 steel spikes, hammered in a spiral pattern, once allowed forest rangers to climb without harnesses to a platform 53 metres above ground to watch for smoke. The tree is currently closed for climbing due to platform renovation works; reopening is expected soon.

The karri forests encircling Pemberton are the world's third-tallest hardwood forests—karris can exceed 90 metres. Their smooth trunks shift colour with age, from pale pink to orange and silvery white, creating light-dappled cathedrals beneath the canopy. The Karri Forest Explorer Drive is an 86 km signposted circuit through five national parks.

Beedelup Falls, in Greater Beedelup National Park 20 minutes from Pemberton, cascade over granite slabs before joining Beedelup Brook. The 25-metre suspension bridge spanning the base of the falls is an attraction in itself—it sways underfoot. Further along the trail, a hollow karri allows walkers to pass right through its trunk.

→

Walk to the Gloucester Tree (6 km return from Pemberton town) following the blue trail markers through karri forest. Even closed to climbing, the imposing trunk and spiral of steel spikes make an irresistible photographic subject.

→

Afternoon wine tasting in the Pemberton Wine Region. The area's vineyards produce highly regarded Pinot Noir and Chardonnay thanks to the cool microclimate created by the surrounding forests. Estates offer tastings and some provide guided tours of their timber cellars.

→

Evening stroll at Big Brook Dam. The reservoir encircled by karri forest is the locals' favourite swimming spot. The cool water and silent canopy at dusk offer perfect serenity after a day's hiking.

The Valley of the Giants Tree Top Walk is a 600-metre steel walkway suspended up to 40 metres above the ground in the canopy of tingle trees—giant eucalypts endemic to the Walpole area. These trees can reach 75 metres in height, with some base circumferences of 15 metres. The light walkway, built on steel pylons with no ground foundations, sways gently in the wind just like the trees themselves. The botanical origins of this forest stretch back 65 million years to the supercontinent Gondwana.

The Ancient Empire Walk (650 metres, free entry) plunges to ground level among the monumental trunks of red tingle trees, some 400 years old. The wooden boardwalk passes between—and sometimes through—trunks whose time-hollowed bases form natural doorways. The star of this trail is 'Grandma Tingle', whose hollow trunk is one of the forest's most impressive.

Nornalup Inlet is a dark, calm-water estuary encircled by tingle and karri forests—a striking contrast to the roar of the nearby Southern Ocean. Accessible from Nornalup village, the inlet attracts black swans, wading birds, and in winter, dolphins that venture upstream from the coast.

Conspicuous Cliff is a sheer granite promontory above the Southern Ocean, a few kilometres from Walpole. From the lookout, the panorama sweeps across kilometres of wild coastline without a single house or boat in sight—one of the most solitary views on the Australian coast. The cliff is reached by a short signposted walk from the car park.

→

Combine the Tree Top Walk and Ancient Empire Walk in a single visit. Perfect timing: ascend into the canopy at opening (9 am) before the crowds arrive, then descend for the ground-level trail. Allow about 1.5 hours for both circuits.

💡 Tree Top Walk is wheelchair accessible. Ancient Empire is free.
→

Kayak or canoe on Nornalup Inlet at sunrise. The tranquil, tannin-stained waters bordered by dense forest and granite cliffs offer an extraordinary setting for silent paddling among the wildlife. Rentals available in Walpole.

Greens Pool, in William Bay National Park 14 km from Denmark, is one of Australia's most iconic beaches. Massive granite boulders form a natural reef that shelters a bay of near-still turquoise water—a lagoon carved from the Southern Ocean itself. Snorkellers spot Port Jackson sharks, stingrays, and schools of colourful fish in the crystal clarity. In summer, arrive before 9 am to secure parking.

A few hundred metres from Greens Pool, Elephant Rocks is a formation of rounded granite boulders that uncannily resemble a herd of elephants bathing in the water. These rocks, shaped by millions of years of erosion, overlook Elephant Cove and gaze out at the Southern Ocean. The site is believed to have been a sacred birthing place for Aboriginal peoples for centuries.

Ocean Beach is Denmark's big surf beach—facing due south, it receives Antarctic swells and draws surfers from up and down the coast. From nearby Lions Lookout, a 180° panorama embraces the entire sweep of Denmark Bay, Wilson Inlet, the forested hills, and on clear days, the Stirling Ranges to the north. It's one of the finest viewpoints on the Rainbow Coast.

Wilson Inlet is Western Australia's largest freshwater lagoon, separated from the Southern Ocean by a sandbar. Its calm shores attract 145 bird species—pelicans, ibis, royal spoonbills, and black swans in abundance—as well as dolphins that sometimes venture several kilometres upstream into the estuary.

→

Snorkelling at Greens Pool in the sheltered lagoon waters. The submerged rocks harbour dense marine life: multicoloured fish, stingrays, and rock lobsters hiding in granite crevices. The water is clear and shallow near shore, ideal for beginners.

💡 Bring your own mask and snorkel - no rental facilities on-site
→

Coastal trail from Greens Pool to Elephant Rocks (1 km return) via the granite path linking the two sites along the headland. The track climbs slightly over the rocks—plunging views over both bays before descending to Elephant Cove.

→

Sunset from Lions Lookout above Ocean Beach. The panorama over Wilson Inlet, the sandbar, and the Southern Ocean is stunning in the golden hour. Denmark town is a 5-minute drive for dinner at one of its restaurants.

The National ANZAC Centre, opened in 2014 on Mount Clarence in Albany Heritage Park, is Australia's most significant museum dedicated to the Anzac legend. It was from Albany Harbour that the first two ANZAC convoys departed—41,265 Australian and New Zealand soldiers on 38 ships, most of whom would never see their homeland again. Visitors assume the identity of one of 32 documented soldiers or nurses, following their journey from enlistment through to the final fate revealed at the visit's end.

Albany's historic centre, the first settlement established in Western Australia in 1827, clusters around Stirling Terrace and its limestone colonial buildings. Albany is WA's oldest town—its natural harbour at King George Sound, one of the largest and most protected on the continent, gave it decisive strategic importance. The Residency Museum, the replica brig Amity, and the fortifications of Princess Royal Fortress on Mount Adelaide complete the historical tour.

Middleton Beach is Albany's town beach—a long ribbon of sand sheltered by the Flinders Peninsula headland, with calmer waters than the southern coast. The waterfront promenade links the beach to Ellen Cove and Emu Point jetty. At dusk, the sun sets over the Stirling Ranges to the north.

→

Climb to the summit of Mount Clarence via the Convoy Walk to discover the Desert Mounted Corps Memorial and Padre White Lookout. The 360° panorama encompasses King George Sound, Albany town, and the Torndirrup cliffs to the south—the same waters contemplated by ANZAC soldiers departing for Gallipoli.

→

Stroll through the historic Stirling Terrace precinct to see 19th-century limestone buildings. The local market, art galleries, and independent boutiques make this one of the best-preserved town centres on the south coast.

The Gap and Natural Bridge are Torndirrup National Park's two most spectacular formations, 20 km from Albany. The Gap is a 25-metre-deep channel carved through granite by the Southern Ocean—a stainless steel platform overhangs the abyss 40 metres above the roaring sea. A few dozen metres away, the Natural Bridge is a granite arch sculpted by millions of years of erosion.

The historic Frenchman Bay whaling station (Cheynes Beach Whaling Station) operated until 1978—the last of its kind in Australia. Now transformed into a museum, it houses the whaler Cheynes IV (decommissioned in 1979) and interactive installations tracing the history of whaling in the Great Southern. Whale skeletons, harpoons, and flensing boilers provide a striking glimpse of an industry now vanished.

Two Peoples Bay Nature Reserve, 35 km east of Albany, is one of Western Australia's most precious ornithological sanctuaries. It's home to the noisy scrub-bird, a species thought extinct until its rediscovery in 1961. Little Beach, at the heart of the reserve, is a pink granite cove with impossibly turquoise water—accessible after a short 1.5 km walk from the car park.

→

Explore the Torndirrup Blowholes (1.6 km return, 78 steps): cracks in the granite plateau connect with the sea below and eject water and air with each wave. The effect is spectacular during winter's heavy swells.

💡 Trail climbs entirely on the return - 78 steps up
→

Swim at Little Beach in Two Peoples Bay Reserve. After the approach walk (1.5 km return), the reward is a pink granite beach framed by cliffs, whose calm, crystalline waters invite swimming. Arrive early morning to avoid crowds.

💡 No food to take away - protected nature reserve
→

Hike the Stony Hill Walk Trail (500 m accessible loop), a flat circuit offering 360° views over Torndirrup Park, Peak Head, and the Southern Ocean. Short but panoramic—one of the area's best viewpoints accessible without effort.

The Castle Rock Granite Skywalk in Porongurup National Park (15 minutes from Mount Barker) is a steel-mesh structure bolted directly into granite, perched atop a chaos of rocks 1.2 billion years old—Australia's oldest mountain range. The Porongurup Range stretches 12 km and harbours one of the highest concentrations of endemic plants in southwest Australia. On clear days, the panorama from the Skywalk embraces the Stirling Ranges to the north and the distant Southern Ocean.

Bluff Knoll (Pualaar Miial—'great many-faced hill'), the 1,095-metre summit in Stirling Range National Park, is the highest point in the southern half of Western Australia. Its sheer cliffs, winter snow gullies, and 1,500 plant species (many found nowhere else) make it one of the continent's most singular peaks. The Noongar name for the range, Koi Kyenunu-ruff, means 'mist rolling around the mountains'—a phenomenon visible almost daily from the plains.

→

Ascent to Castle Rock Granite Skywalk (4.4 km return, 2h). The trail passes through jarrah and karri forest before climbing over granite boulders via a series of steel ladders and walkways anchored into the rock. The Skywalk itself—a mesh-floored platform over a sheer drop—offers a unique 360° panorama of the Porongurup Range.

→

Hike Bluff Knoll (6.3 km return, 637 m elevation gain). The well-marked stepped trail climbs through schist and quartzite cliffs to the 1,095 m summit—the 360° views over the plains, the Porongurup, and on exceptional days, the coast 100 km away, are worth the effort. Bring a warm jacket even in summer: the summit experiences WA's only alpine microclimate.

💡 Early start recommended - clouds often form by early afternoon
→

Wine tasting in the Mount Barker Wine Region late in the day. This cool-climate wine zone produces excellent Riesling, Pinot Noir, and Cabernet Sauvignon. Estates are scattered in the hills around town—some offer cheese and charcuterie boards paired with wine flights.

What weather should you expect?

Peak Season (December-February): Summer holidays, warmest weather, perfect for beaches, highest prices, most crowded attractions, school holidays.

Shoulder Seasons (March-May, September-November): Excellent weather, fewer crowds, moderate prices, ideal for most regions and outdoor activities.

Winter (June-August): Perfect for tropical north (dry season), ski season in Snowy Mountains, cooler in south, lower prices except ski areas.

Regional Variations: Northern Australia best in dry season (May-October), southern regions pleasant year-round, consider vast distances and climate zones.
