Complete Calabria Loop: Tyrrhenian and Ionian Coasts

Where Greek bronze warriors sleep in museums, Norman cathedrals rise on ancient columns, and a single temple column still guards the Ionian shore after 2,600 years.

This complete Calabria circuit follows the Tyrrhenian coast from Pizzo to Tropea and Scilla, then plunges south to Reggio Calabria and the Riace Bronzes. The ascent along the Ionian coast reveals Byzantine Calabria: Gerace with its Norman cathedral resting on 26 ancient columns, La Cattolica di Stilo with its five 10th-century domes, Capo Colonna and its solitary column from the Temple of Hera—monuments that mark the layers of millennia.

Key highlights (5)
  • Tropea: cliffside village on 60-meter limestone heights, Costa degli Dei, and IGP sweet red onions
  • Reggio Calabria: Riace Bronzes from the 5th century BCE, lungomare facing Mount Etna
  • Gerace: Norman cathedral from 1045 standing on 26 ancient Greek columns
  • Capo Colonna: Temple of Hera from the 6th century BCE, solitary column visited by Hannibal
  • La Cattolica di Stilo: 10th-century Byzantine church with five domes on ancient columns

Nicastro—the historic heart of Lamezia Terme and former capital of the Norman County of Calabria in the 11th century. The Castello Normanno-Svevo, built by Roger I around 1060 on a 320-meter spur, served as Frederick II of Hohenstaufen's residence during his Calabrian visits in the 13th century. The devastating 1638 earthquake—one of Italy's deadliest with 10,000 deaths in the Savuto Valley alone—reduced the castle to ruins that still brood over the valley today.

Swimming at Gizzeria Lido beach, 10 km from the airport—a sweep of fine sand along the Tyrrhenian coast, perfect for unwinding after arrival before heading south to Pizzo the next day.

Castello Aragonese di Pizzo—an Aragonese fortress built in 1486 by Ferdinand I on a rocky promontory commanding the Gulf of Sant'Eufemia. In this castle's courtyard, Joachim Murat, King of Naples and Napoleon's brother-in-law, was arrested on October 8, 1815, after attempting to reclaim his throne with 250 men landed from Corsica. Five days later, on October 13, he faced a firing squad at age 48. His detention cell remains preserved with personal artifacts.

Chiesa di Piedigrotta—a cave church carved into the volcanic tuff cliffs at sea level, founded in the 17th century according to legend by Neapolitan sailors shipwrecked here, who sculpted a Virgin into the rock in gratitude. Inside, dozens of tuff sculptures depict religious scenes and contemporary figures, including a 17th-century Pietà and 20th-century additions that blur sacred and secular.

Centro storico di Pizzo—a medieval town perched on 35-meter cliffs, with Piazza della Repubblica surrounded by 18th-century palazzi. The town's fame rests on centuries of bluefin tuna fishing in the Gulf of Sant'Eufemia, with traditional mattanze (collective tuna hunts) documented since the 16th century.

→

Tasting the tartufo di Pizzo—an ice cream creation born in Pizzo in the 1950s: a chocolate-hazelnut gelato sphere with a molten chocolate core, dusted in bitter cocoa. The gelaterias around Piazza della Repubblica still guard the original recipe.

→

Sunset walk along Pizzo's lungomare with views across the Gulf of Sant'Eufemia to the Aeolian Islands hovering on the horizon.

Tropea—a town suspended on 60-meter limestone cliffs above the turquoise Tyrrhenian Sea in the Gulf of Gioia Tauro. Legend claims Hercules founded it on his return from Iberia; history records it as Greek, Roman, Byzantine, Norman, and Aragonese in succession. The medieval core preserves noble palaces from the 16th-18th centuries built in local volcanic tuff, their balconies flowering over narrow cobbled lanes.

Santa Maria dell'Isola—a sanctuary built on an isolated rock linked to the mainland by a footbridge, first a 6th-century Basilian monastery, then a Benedictine church from the 11th century onward. The neo-Gothic façade dates from a 20th-century restoration after World War II damage. The rock offers 360-degree views of the Costa degli Dei and the Aeolian Islands floating offshore.

Cattedrale di Tropea—a Norman cathedral founded in the 12th century, remodeled in the 17th-18th centuries. Two unexploded American bombs from 1943 hang at the entrance as ex-votos—locals attribute their failure to detonate during Allied bombardments to the intercession of Tropea's Madonna, a miracle commemorated in bronze.

→

Swimming on the beaches of the Costa degli Dei between Tropea and Capo Vaticano—some of the clearest waters in the Mediterranean, with seagrass meadows between 2 and 15 meters depth creating underwater gardens of startling transparency.

→

Boat excursion from Tropea's harbor along the Costa degli Dei to Capo Vaticano and its coves unreachable by land—white cliffs, sea caves, and waters that shift from cobalt to jade.

→

Tasting cipolla rossa di Tropea IGP—the sweet red onion grown in the sandy coastal soils of the Tyrrhenian shore, awarded IGP status in 2020, eaten raw in salads or transformed into jammy preserves with a haunting sweetness.

Palmi—a town of 19,000 perched on a 240-meter promontory above the Tyrrhenian Sea, with sweeping views of the Aeolian Islands and the Strait of Messina. The Museo Calabrese di Etnografia e Folklore, founded in 1965 in the Casa della Cultura Leonida Repaci, houses one of Calabria's most important ethnographic collections: traditional costumes, musical instruments, rural life objects, and a section dedicated to the Varia di Palmi—an 18-meter processional machine inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Heritage list in 2013.

Stop at Palmi's belvedere for the view across the Strait of Messina to Mount Etna before descending toward Scilla.

Chianalea di Scilla—a fishermen's quarter built on rock at the castle's foot, with houses whose back doors open directly onto the sea. Homer placed the six-headed monster Scylla in this strait, devouring sailors (Odyssey, Book XII), opposite Charybdis on the Sicilian side. The quarter remains one of the rare preserved examples of southern Italian Tyrrhenian fishing architecture.

Castello Ruffo di Scilla—a fortress on a 72-meter rock commanding the Strait of Messina, inhabited since Greek times. The current structure is Norman (11th century), remodeled by the Ruffo family in the 15th century. In 1809, during the Napoleonic Wars, British troops resisted Marshal Murat's French army here for several weeks. The castle now houses an active lighthouse.

→

Tasting pesce spada in Scilla—the Strait of Messina is one of the last places in the Mediterranean where swordfish are still hunted with harpoons from traditional feluccas fitted with 30-meter walkways, a millennia-old technique still practiced in summer.

→

Swimming at Spiaggia di Marina Grande below the castle—a pebble beach with crystalline waters between the castle rock and the harbor breakwater.

Museo Nazionale della Magna Grecia—a museum founded in 1882 housing the Riace Bronzes, two 5th-century BCE Greek bronze statues discovered in 1972 at 8 meters depth in the sea off Riace by an amateur diver. The two warriors (statue A: 1.98 m, statue B: 1.97 m) are considered the best-preserved examples of Classical Greek bronze sculpture. After restoration in Florence from 1975 to 1981, they're displayed in a climate-controlled room with regulated pressure and humidity.

Lungomare Falcomatà—a 3-km seafront promenade rebuilt after the devastating earthquake of December 28, 1908 (magnitude 7.1, 80,000-120,000 deaths) that destroyed 90% of the city. Gabriele D'Annunzio called it 'the most beautiful kilometer in Italy' for the simultaneous view of Mount Etna, Sicily, and the strait.

Castello Aragonese di Reggio Calabria—a fortress built by the Aragonese in the 15th century on the foundations of a Norman fortification, itself built on a Greek and Roman defensive site. Damaged by the 1908 earthquake and restored in the 20th century, it hosts temporary exhibitions and offers views over the Strait of Messina.

→

Tasting bergamot from Reggio Calabria—a citrus fruit grown exclusively along 35 km of coast around Reggio, whose essence enters the composition of 85% of the world's luxury perfumes. Calabria produces 80% of global bergamot production.

→

Ferry crossing to Messina to see the strait from the water—the 20-minute crossing offers simultaneous views of both shores with Mount Etna looming in the background.

Gerace—a Byzantine village perched at 480 meters on a rocky spur inaccessible on three sides, founded in the 9th century by inhabitants of Locri fleeing Saracen raids. The nearly intact medieval core preserves 23 documented churches, noble palaces, and stone lanes for a current population of 2,700.

Cattedrale di Gerace—the largest Norman cathedral in Calabria, built by Roger I between 1045 and 1060 on the foundations of an earlier Greek temple, then enlarged in the 13th century. At 63 meters long, it rests on 26 ancient columns of granite and cipolin marble from Locri Epizefiri. The 11th-century three-nave crypt is one of the best preserved in southern Italy.

Locri Epizefiri—ruins of the Greek colony founded around 680 BCE on the Ionian coast, the first Greek city to adopt a written code of laws with the Laws of Zaleucus (around 650 BCE). The 230-hectare archaeological site includes temples, a 4th-century theater, a necropolis, and a museum displaying ceramics, coins, and votive offerings.

→

Wandering through Gerace's medieval lanes between half-restored noble palaces and gardens suspended on the rocky spur.

La Cattolica di Stilo—a 10th-century Byzantine church, one of the most important monuments of Byzantine architecture in southern Italy. The centrally-planned building with five cylindrical domes on drums rests on ancient marble columns with mismatched capitals (some inverted—a sign of hasty reuse). Stilo is also the birthplace of Tommaso Campanella (1568-1639), philosopher and author of The City of the Sun, imprisoned for 27 years by the Spanish Inquisition.

Walking through Stilo's historic center to see the medieval stone houses and the view over the Ionian plain before continuing toward Catanzaro.

Castello Le Castella—a 16th-century Aragonese fortress built on an islet linked to the mainland by a narrow causeway in the Capo Rizzuto marine protected area. The current structure was enlarged by the Aragonese on a 14th-century Angevin tower itself built on Greek foundations. The surrounding sea is part of the Área Marina Protetta Capo Rizzuto, Italy's largest marine protected area at 14,700 hectares.

Capo Colonna—an Ionian promontory bearing the remains of the Temple of Hera Lacinia, a Panhellenic sanctuary founded in the 6th century BCE and frequented by all the Greek colonies of Magna Graecia. A single 8.16-meter Doric column still stands from the original 48—the others were dismantled in the 16th century to build Crotone's cathedral. Hannibal stopped here in 203 BCE during his withdrawal from Italy.

→

Diving or snorkeling in the Área Marina Protetta Capo Rizzuto—the protected zone's seabed preserves Greek and Roman amphorae in situ, with visibility reaching 30 meters in summer.

→

Swimming on the beaches of Catanzaro Lido at day's end after the visits.

Amantea—a medieval town of 13,000 on a 177-meter promontory above the Tyrrhenian Sea, 30 km from Lamezia Terme airport. The historic center preserves the 15th-century Torre Aragonese and the 13th-century church of San Francesco d'Assisi with a Gothic portal. The town was an important silk production center until the 19th century, exporting to Naples and Genoa.
