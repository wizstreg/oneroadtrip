Family Road Trip Through Austria: Mountains, Lakes & Imperial Palaces

Where valleys burst into wildflower bloom, castles crown the Danube's bends, and villages nestle into alpine stone—Austria's heart reveals its most closely guarded treasures.

Austria is perfect for families: Innsbruck with its imperial Golden Roof, the flower-covered alpine meadows of Zillertal and Alpbach, the vertiginous Krimml Falls in Hohe Tauern, Carinthia's turquoise lakes, Graz's UNESCO-listed baroque Old Town, Vienna with its Habsburg palaces, Melk Abbey dominating the Danube, and Salzburg to finish in Mozart's footsteps. An enchanting journey at every stop, between wide-open spaces and imperial splendor.

Key highlights (4)
  • Innsbruck: Alpine capital with the imperial Golden Roof
  • Zillertal & Alpbach: Flower-decked alpine valleys and centuries-old villages
  • Lake Wörthersee: Turquoise Carinthian lake among the warmest in the Alps
  • Melk & the Wachau: Baroque Danube abbey and UNESCO-listed vineyards

Innsbruck

At number 9 Herzog-Friedrich-Strasse, look up: 2,657 gilded copper shingles shimmer in the sunlight. The Golden Roof (Goldenes Dachl) was commissioned by Emperor Maximilian I between 1497 and 1500 to celebrate his marriage to Bianca Maria Sforza. From this flamboyant Gothic loggia, the Emperor watched tournaments and festivities on the square below. The sandstone reliefs, masterpieces bridging late Gothic and Renaissance, illustrate the eight territories of his crown.

The Goldenes Dachl Museum, housed in the building itself, presents Maximilian I's history through multimedia exhibits and original 15th-century reliefs. An interactive station for children offers a quiz-hunt through the Emperor's halls.

Walk through the Old Town (Altstadt) to the Imperial Palace (Kaiserliche Hofburg), former Habsburg residence restored to its 18th-century state. The state apartments, with their rococo gilding and dynastic portraits, paint a vivid picture of Habsburg power from Innsbruck.

The Hofkirche houses Maximilian I's cenotaph, surrounded by 28 life-size bronze statues representing his ancestors and allies—including Clovis, Arthur of England, and Charlemagne. The tomb itself is empty: the Emperor was buried in Wiener Neustadt, but this funerary monument is considered one of the most important of the Germanic Renaissance.

Head to Ambras Castle (Schloss Ambras), 4 km southeast of the center. A secret love gift from Archduke Ferdinand II to Philippine Welser, this Renaissance castle houses the world's oldest museum still preserved on its original site. The Spanish Hall, 43 meters long, ranks among the finest Renaissance halls. The Chamber of Art and Curiosities displays Ottoman chainmail, corals, exotic ivories, and portraits of extraordinary characters collected in the 16th century.

Lake Achensee, nicknamed the "Tyroleans' sea," is Tyrol's largest lake—6 km long, 133 meters deep—nestled between the Karwendel and Rofan massifs. Its turquoise waters, with visibility up to 10 meters, make it one of the clearest mountain lakes in the Alps. The villages of Achenkirch, Maurach, and Pertisau offer free-access beaches right on the water.

→

Ride the Nordkettenbahn cable car from downtown Innsbruck up to 2,256 meters. The plunging view over the city, framed by the Karwendel range and the Inn valley, ranks among the most striking in the Austrian Alps.

💡 Bring warm clothes even in summer—it's noticeably cooler at altitude
→

Swimming and family fun on the beaches of Lake Achensee at Maurach. The flat southern shore at Buchau is perfect for children: shallow water, clean sand, playgrounds. Maurach's SEE-Bad features an adventure play boat, slides, climbing walls, and beach volleyball courts.

💡 Water reaches 20–22°C in summer—refreshing but swimmable

The Zillertal valley opens east of Innsbruck, framed by peaks exceeding 3,500 meters. Its villages—Zell am Ziller, Mayrhofen, Finkenberg—preserve their large white-façade houses and pointed bell towers. In Zell am Ziller, don't miss the Erlebnis-Sennerei Zillertal cheese dairy: an interactive exhibit explains how alpine cheeses have been made for centuries.

Alpbach, a neighboring village in the Alpbachtal valley, has been voted Austria's most beautiful village several times. A local law forbids any building material other than wood: the result is rare architectural harmony. Geranium-covered balconies, stone fountains, and barns attached to houses compose a postcard scene that the years have not dimmed.

Rattenberg, at the valley entrance, is Austria's smallest town with only 400 inhabitants. Renowned since the Middle Ages for glass craftsmanship, its medieval lanes and baroque Augustinian church merit a stop. Artisan blown-glass workshops and boutiques line the main street.

→

Family hike in the Alpbachtal on marked trails departing from Alpbach village. To gain altitude quickly without effort, the Wiedersbergerhornbahn gondolas take families up to panoramas over the Zillertal Alps and Lake Achensee. From the upper station, an hour's walk reaches the 2,127-meter summit.

💡 The gondola operates in summer—perfect for families with young children
→

Board the Zillertalbahn, a historic steam train linking Jenbach to Mayrhofen over 32 km, crossing 35 bridges above the Ziller River. At a maximum 35 km/h, the wooden carriages open onto meadows and valley villages. Children love this journey in a vintage locomotive.

💡 Departs from Jenbach—check seasonal schedules

The Krimml Falls (Krimmler Wasserfälle) plunge in three successive cascades of 140, 100, and 140 meters—380 meters total—from a hanging valley at 1,470 meters altitude. They're Europe's highest waterfalls and the world's fifth tallest. The 4-km hiking trail, well-maintained with viewing platforms, reaches the lower cascade in 15 minutes, the upper one in about an hour. The constant mist from water crashing on rock creates a unique atmosphere, and the vegetation of mosses and giant ferns lining the banks reinforces the feeling of entering another world.

Hohe Tauern National Park, with its 1,856 km² of alpine wilderness, shelters the Grossglockner (3,798 m), Austria's highest peak, glaciers, ibex, marmots, and golden eagles. The Mittersill visitor center features interactive exhibits on protected flora and fauna. Rangers organize guided outings to discover the alpine ecosystem.

Lake Wörthersee, a stone's throw from Villach, is one of Europe's warmest alpine lakes—its waters reach 28°C in summer thanks to sub-lacustrine thermal springs. Its shores are equipped with promenades and beaches, and the villages of Pörtschach and Velden offer a Belle Époque setting with their century-old villas and hotels.

→

Family swimming and paddleboarding on Lake Wörthersee. Rental operators offer pedal boats, kayaks, and stand-up paddleboards on the beaches at Pörtschach and Maria Wörth. For children, supervised swimming areas with shallow bottoms are safe.

💡 Water at 26–28°C in peak summer—ideal for families
→

⚠️ Logistics note: Krimml Falls are 120 km from Villach. Day 3 is very packed: it's recommended to leave early for the falls, pack a picnic on-site, and reach Villach and its lakes late afternoon. Consider spending the night in the Salzburgerland (Zell am See or Mittersill) for more balanced logistics.

Graz's Old Town (Grazer Altstadt), a UNESCO World Heritage site, unfolds its baroque and Renaissance façades around Hauptplatz—the central main square. Hidden interior courtyards, sloping cobblestone lanes, and discreet passages form a medieval labyrinth renewed at every turn. Head to Herrengasse for its arcaded houses and sculpted façades.

The Schlossberg is the wooded hill dominating Graz from its center, once crowned by a medieval fortress dismantled by Napoleon in 1809. Only the Clock Tower (Uhrturm) remains, the city's emblem, whose clock has displayed time backwards since 1712: the big hand shows hours. Ascend by funicular or 260 steps—the view over Graz's red roofs and bell towers is panoramic.

The Kunsthaus Graz, affectionately nicknamed the "Friendly Alien," is a bluish bubble with a spaceship look perched on the Mur riverbanks. Designed by Peter Cook and Colin Fournier in 2003 for Graz's bid as European Capital of Culture, it presents contemporary art exhibitions with spectacular installations. Even children remain fascinated by its organic shapes and "nozzles"—those porthole-antennas oriented toward the city.

The Styrian Armoury (Landeszeughaus) preserves over 32,000 suits of armor, swords, pikes, and cannonballs—Austria's largest collection of historic armor. Entire halls of 16th-century ceremonial armor, still arranged as if their owners might return, create an impressive spectacle. Perfect for captivating children with tales of chivalry.

→

Climb to the Schlossberg summit by funicular, and once at the top, spot the giant underground slide carved through rock that takes visitors back down to the Old Town. An unusual attraction loved by children, tunneled through the hill's rocky core.

💡 The slide (Schlossberg-Rutsche) is included in the funicular ticket
→

Stroll through the markets at Kaiser-Josef-Platz and Hauptplatz, where Styrian producers display fruits, vegetables, cheeses, and the essential Kürbiskernöl—green pumpkin seed oil, a regional specialty with a roasted hazelnut flavor, drizzled over salads and cheeses.

Schönbrunn Palace, nicknamed the "Viennese Versailles," was the Habsburg summer residence for centuries. Its 1,441 rooms aren't all open to visitors, but Franz Joseph and Elisabeth's (Sissi's) apartments, Maria Theresa's state rooms, and rococo chinoiserie décor constitute a complete immersion into imperial court life. The Children's Museum lets little ones dress as princes and princesses and learn the games and customs of imperial children.

Schönbrunn Gardens, open free from 6:30 a.m., stretch 1.2 km from east to west. The Gloriette, a triumphal colonnade atop the hill, offers a panoramic view over Vienna. The topiary maze and classified fountains are perfect for a family stroll.

The Sisi Museum (Hofburg) plunges into Empress Elisabeth's intimate life through her original dresses, letters, and portraits. The Sisi Museum, located in the Hofburg, reveals the complex and often melancholic personality behind the legend: her strict diets, incessant travels, and difficult relationship with court etiquette.

The Haus der Musik (House of Music) is an interactive museum housed in a downtown palace. Each floor is dedicated to a Viennese composer—Haydn, Mozart, Beethoven, Schubert, Strauss, Mahler. Children can compose their own waltz, conduct the virtual Vienna Philharmonic Orchestra, or hear music through their own heartbeat.

St. Stephen's Cathedral (Stephansdom), built from 1160 in regional limestone, is Vienna's symbol. Its multicolored tile roof forms spectacular geometric patterns visible from the Old Town rooftops. The catacombs beneath the cathedral hold the bones of 11,000 Viennese victims of 17th-century epidemics.

→

Picnic and stroll in the Prater, Vienna's vast popular park on the Danube banks. The Wurstelprater, an amusement park opened in 1766, houses the Giant Ferris Wheel (Riesenrad) built in 1897, rising 65 meters: a panoramic view over Vienna from its steel cabins. The 250 attractions—carousels, haunted houses, roller coasters—delight families.

→

Schönbrunn Zoo (Tiergarten), founded in 1752, is the world's oldest zoo still in operation. Over 700 animal species, including giant pandas, African elephants, and white rhinos, occupy enclosures redesigned as natural habitats. Daily programs let children observe animals up close during feeding times.

💡 Included with the Schönbrunn combination ticket

Perched on a rocky spur plunging directly into the Danube, Melk's Benedictine Abbey is one of European baroque's masterpieces. Built between 1702 and 1736 by Jakob Prandtauer, its ochre façade and gilded domes are visible for several kilometers. Since 1089—over 930 years—Benedictine monks have lived here continuously under St. Benedict's rule. The library with its 100,000 volumes, featuring a ceiling painted by Paul Troger, ranks among the world's most beautiful baroque libraries. The abbey church, imperial apartments, and gardens with three-hundred-year-old lindens complete a ninety-minute to two-hour visit.

Dürnstein, a small village of 870 inhabitants on the Danube shore, is famous for two reasons: its cobalt-blue baroque bell tower—nicknamed "God's finger"—that has signaled boats since the 18th century, and the medieval castle ruins perched above the village where Richard the Lionheart was imprisoned by Leopold V of Austria in 1192–1193 after the crusade. The climb to the ruins (30 to 45 minutes) rewards with an exceptional view over the Danube loop and Wachau vineyards.

The Wachau Valley, a UNESCO World Heritage site since 2000, is a 30-km stretch between Krems and Melk where the Danube winds through terraced vineyards and medieval villages. Wachau's Grüner Veltliner and Riesling rank among Austria's most renowned white wines. Each village hides its own interior courtyards, cobbled lanes, and fortification ruins.

→

Short Danube river cruise through the Wachau. Boats navigate between wine-growing villages, pass beneath Dürnstein's ruins, and glide along terraces of apricot trees and orchards that define the region's gastronomic reputation. The Wachau apricot, protected by a European controlled designation of origin, is transformed into schnapps, jams, and traditional dumplings.

💡 Wachau cruises available from Melk to Krems between April and October
→

Bike ride on the Danube Cycle Path (Donauradweg) running along both river banks through the Wachau. The flat, well-maintained route crosses vineyards, orchards, and historic villages at a peaceful pace. Bike rental shops are available in Melk and Krems.

💡 Marked bike path on both sides of the Danube

At number 9 Getreidegasse, an unpretentious yellow façade conceals the apartment where Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart was born on January 27, 1756. The family lived there for 26 years. The museum displays his first childhood violin, his harpsichord and clavichord, his concert wig, family portraits, and a collection of letters. The third floor, recreated as an 18th-century bourgeois interior, brings the young prodigy's daily life to life.

Hohensalzburg Fortress (Festung Hohensalzburg) crowns the hill dominating the Old Town since 1077. It's one of Central Europe's largest still-intact medieval fortresses. The funicular takes you straight up from the Old Town. The princely apartments, guard halls, and court life museum offer a plunge into the history of Salzburg's prince-archbishops.

The baroque Cathedral of St. Rupert (Salzburger Dom), rebuilt between 1614 and 1628 after a fire, is the church where Mozart was baptized the day after his birth. Its white façade with two towers and imposing dome dominates Domplatz. The interior, with its stucco and 17th-century frescoes, ranks among the largest baroque religious buildings in German-speaking countries.

The gardens of Mirabell Palace (Mirabellgarten), designed in the early 18th century in baroque style, serve as a natural balcony overlooking Hohensalzburg Fortress. The geometric flower beds, mythological marble statues, and classified fountains offer an idyllic setting. These gardens were the backdrop for the film The Sound of Music (1965).

→

Long walk along the Salzach riverbanks from the old bridge (Staatsbrücke) to the quieter neighborhoods upstream. The river separates the historic Old Town with its baroque bell towers from the more tranquil residential districts on the left bank. The view of the fortress and bell towers from the banks is Salzburg's finest.

→

Classical music concert in one of the Old Town's many halls—the Mozarteum, the University Church, or one of Hohensalzburg's fortifications regularly host chamber and baroque music concerts. Salzburg is a city experienced through its sounds as well.

💡 Reservation recommended in high season (July–August)
→

Ascent to Mönchsberg, the wooded hill adjoining the Old Town, accessible on foot or by elevator from Gstättengasse. A green haven two minutes from the center: shaded trails, panoramic cafés, and terraces with views over baroque rooftops and the Alps beyond.

❓ Frequently asked questions

What weather should you expect?

Peak Summer (July-August): Perfect weather for hiking and festivals, warmest temperatures, highest prices, most crowded attractions and alpine areas.

Shoulder Seasons (April-June, September-October): Excellent balance of good weather, moderate crowds, reasonable prices, ideal for sightseeing and cultural activities.

Winter (December-March): World-class skiing season, Christmas markets, cozy atmosphere, higher accommodation prices in ski resorts, magical snowy landscapes.

Spring (April-May): Blooming flowers, mild weather, perfect for Vienna and cultural sites, fewer tourists, excellent value for money.

How many days should I plan?

We believe you will enjoy this itinerary best by dedicating between 6 and 10 days. The next step will allow you to adjust the duration of your stay.

What to discover in Austria?

Mozart's birthplace offers imperial palaces, Alpine beauty, and world-class classical music scene. Vienna's coffeehouses and Salzburg's baroque architecture create cultural magic while ski resorts provide winter adventures. The Sound of Music landscapes and Danube River cruises enchant visitors. Austria perfectly combines high culture with natural splendor.
