Midlands and Peak District Road Trip: Heritage and Wild Landscapes

Two geological worlds touching at Castleton, a castle perched on a limestone spur, a town still producing 40% of British jewelry, and a pub carved into rock since 1189. Central England saves its best surprises for those who linger.

Birmingham and its 35 miles of canals, the dark moorlands of Kinder Scout, the fortified town of Ludlow and its gastronomic tables, the UNESCO-listed cast-iron bridge at Ironbridge, the caves of Castleton, the gardens of Chatsworth, Robin Hood's forest - this loop road trip through the heart of England moves from one industrial revolution to another, from the steel Midlands to the heather plateaus of the Peak District.

Key highlights (5)
  • Birmingham: Victorian canals and the world's largest Pre-Raphaelite collection
  • Ironbridge: the first cast-iron bridge in history, birthplace of the industrial revolution
  • Ludlow: Shropshire's gastronomic capital, royal castle and 500 listed buildings
  • Castleton: four caves including the world's only Blue John Stone mine
  • Chatsworth House: Jane Austen's Pemberley, a Baroque masterpiece in the Peak District

Birmingham

Drop your bags and head straight down to Gas Street Basin - this is where Birmingham shows its best face as evening sets in. The basin is the heart of the city's canal network, 35 miles of waterways dug from 1768 onwards to carry coal and iron from the factories. More than Venice's 26 miles, which Brummies have been boasting about for two centuries. Today the narrowboats have made way for terrace tables, but the evening atmosphere is excellent - lights reflecting on the water, the sound of glasses clinking, the smell of cooking. Start here.

Birmingham Museum and Art Gallery, opened in 1885, holds the world's largest public collection of Pre-Raphaelite works - over 2,000 pieces including Burne-Jones, Rossetti, and Ford Madox Brown covering entire walls. The collection has just been reinstalled in four completely renovated galleries after years of closure. What's remarkable here is that entry is free. Allow at least 2 hours, and make sure to see the Staffordshire Hoard: the largest Anglo-Saxon gold and silver treasure ever discovered, hundreds of pieces found in a Staffordshire field in 2009.

The Jewellery Quarter deserves a good half-day. This district still produces 40% of the jewelry made in the United Kingdom and has been designated a World Craft City by the World Crafts Council - one of only eight places in Europe to receive this distinction. Walk up Vyse Street and Warstone Lane: goldsmith workshops sit alongside galleries and independent boutiques. St Paul's Square, the city's last intact Georgian square, is the perfect spot for a pause in the sun. If you have time, the Museum of the Jewellery Quarter - a former factory left untouched since its closure - offers 45-minute guided tours where you can still see tools on the workbenches. Book ahead for weekends.

→

Climb aboard a narrowboat from Gas Street Basin to see Birmingham from the water. It's the most surprising way to understand why this city dominated the global industrial revolution - from the canal, you can still see Victorian warehouses, cast-iron bridges, and locks unchanged for 200 years. Rental companies offer boats by the hour or day, no license required.

→

In the evening, stay in the Jewellery Quarter rather than heading down to Broad Street, which is more touristy. The bars around St Paul's Square have a local crowd, a more relaxed atmosphere, and the prices reflect that. If you like whisky, the Grain & Glass on Frederick Street keeps a cellar of over 300 single malts available for tasting.

Aston Hall is worth the trip for its history alone: this Jacobean mansion built between 1618 and 1635 received Charles I in 1642, the night before he left to fight at Edgehill - one of the first battles of the English Civil War. The king slept here. Today managed by Birmingham Museums, the rooms have remained virtually unchanged since the 17th century. It's one of the few interiors from this period still accessible to the public in England.

Digbeth, east of the center, is Birmingham's creative quarter - Victorian warehouses converted into galleries, music studios, and street art spaces. The Custard Factory, a former Bird's factory that made custard powder, has become the symbol of this transformation: independent shops, cafes, artist workshops. If you like this kind of place, set aside the afternoon here and explore on foot.

The Thinktank Birmingham Science Museum, in the former Curzon Street goods station - the world's oldest surviving railway station building - offers exhibitions on the industrial history of the Midlands. It's both an interactive science museum and a tribute to the ingenuity that made the city's reputation. Ideal if you're traveling with children, or simply if you like machines.

→

Six kilometers south of the center, the village of Bournville is a unique Victorian urban planning experiment: chocolatier Cadbury had it built from 1879 onwards to house his workers in better conditions than the industrial slums of the time. Cadbury World traces the entire family history and chocolate-making process, with tastings. Avoid the weekend - queues are long, weekdays are better.

→

If you're there on a Tuesday, Friday, or Saturday, don't miss Birmingham's indoor market in Digbeth. It's one of England's oldest, and it gives a very accurate picture of working-class Birmingham - textiles, spices, local producers. Not at all touristy, and far more interesting than the Bullring.

Head down toward the Severn and you'll see it all at once - the cast-iron bridge spanning the gorge since 1781. Abraham Darby III had it cast with 378 tons of metal in his own Coalbrookdale foundry, using a traditional timber carpentry technique transposed to iron: mortises, dovetails, traditional joinery. The whole thing cost over £6,000 and changed the history of construction. A UNESCO site since 1986, you can walk across it for free. Go early in the morning - the low light on the wooded gorge and the metal is magnificent, and you'll avoid the coaches.

The Museum of the Gorge, housed in a Victorian warehouse on the banks of the Severn, is the best entry point into the site's history. An 8-minute video summarizes how this Shropshire valley triggered the global industrial revolution - and the collections help explain why the entire world sent its engineers here in the 18th century. ⚠️ Note: the entire Gorge museum complex was transferred to the National Trust in early 2026 and is reopening progressively - check opening times online before you go.

At Coalbrookdale, 2 km from the bridge, Abraham Darby I's old blast furnace is still there. This is where in 1709 he perfected smelting iron with coke rather than charcoal - which made mass industrial production possible and triggered everything that followed. You can see the original furnace, protected under a covered structure. Understated, but dizzying when you realize what started here.

→

Blists Hill Victorian Town, a few kilometers into the gorge, is an open-air museum that recreates a Shropshire mining town circa 1900. What distinguishes it from other museums of this kind: the buildings are authentic, salvaged and moved from the region, and at the entrance you exchange your pounds sterling for Victorian currency to shop in the stores. The costumed staff fully play their roles - the blacksmith forges, the printer prints, the pharmacist advises with period remedies. Allow half a day. ⚠️ Reopening scheduled from May 2026 under National Trust management.

→

In the late afternoon, walk along the Severn from the bridge to the Bedlam Furnaces - ruins of 18th-century blast furnaces that vegetation is beginning to reclaim. Access is free and the evening light on the red brick and ivy is striking. It's one of Ironbridge's most powerful images, and few people make this detour.

Shrewsbury

9.3 At Home

Walk down Wyle Cop slowly - this sloping street concentrates 39 listed buildings over a few hundred meters. Look for the Henry Tudor House, dated 1430-1431 by analysis of the wood's tree rings. According to local tradition, Henry Tudor slept here in 1485 before leaving to fight at Bosworth, where he killed Richard III and became Henry VII. Architectural historian Nikolaus Pevsner called it one of the most complete and spectacular medieval timber-framed houses in England. It's partly open to visitors today.

On The Square, the Old Market Hall from 1596 is an Elizabethan building in ashlar and timber framing that served the wool trade - Welsh drapers came to sell their fabrics to English merchants. Today converted into an independent cinema with a cafe, it screens films under an original Tudor coffered ceiling. Go in for a coffee there: it's an Elizabethan building still in daily use, not museumified.

Lose yourself in the "shuts" - those covered medieval passages that cut through buildings from one street to another, some still named after the trades practiced there 600 years ago. Butcher Row, Milk Street, Grope Lane. Shrewsbury's medieval fabric is better preserved than most English towns', precisely because the town experienced a long period of economic stagnation in the 18th and 19th centuries - what economists considered decline ultimately saved everything.

Shrewsbury is Charles Darwin's birthplace, born here in 1809. The family home is occasionally open. The abbey, founded in 1083 by Roger de Montgomery, is known to Ellis Peters readers as the setting for the Brother Cadfael novels - the 13th-century effigy of its founder is still there, in the nave.

→

The Quarry Park, 12 hectares on the banks of the Severn, is where locals gather in the late afternoon. The Dingle, a former quarry turned into a sunken garden with a lake and winding paths, is particularly pleasant. Small curiosity: the large cast-iron gates at the park entrance were cast at Coalbrookdale - a thread connecting your two days.

→

Shrewsbury's indoor market was voted "Britain's favorite market" three years running, including 2025 - a record. On market days (Tuesday, Wednesday, Friday, Saturday) you'll find artisan cheesemongers, butchers, and bakers from Shropshire and neighboring Wales. Ideal for preparing a picnic for tomorrow.

Climb up to Ludlow Castle and look for a discreet panel about Prince Arthur - eldest son of Henry VII, first husband of Catherine of Aragon, who died here of tuberculosis in 1502 at 15, just months after his wedding. His premature death allowed his younger brother to inherit the throne and become Henry VIII. The castle had been the administrative capital of Wales and the Marches since 1472, and remained royal property until 1811. The ruins are well preserved, and the view from the towers over the Teme valley makes the climb worthwhile.

Walk up Broad Street at a leisurely pace - poet John Betjeman called Ludlow "probably the loveliest town in England," and this street proves his enthusiasm justified. Five hundred listed buildings, an improbable mix of Norman, Tudor, Georgian, and Victorian facades that coexist without crowding each other. The Buttercross at the top of the street marks the location of the old medieval butter market.

St Laurence's Church deserves 30 minutes. It's one of the largest parish churches in Shropshire, in English Perpendicular style, with a remarkable carved rood screen. The ashes of poet A.E. Housman, author of A Shropshire Lad, were scattered here at his request. The tower offers a viewpoint over the entire town.

→

Ludlow is Shropshire's gastronomic capital - the town has had up to three Michelin-starred restaurants for 10,000 inhabitants, making it the most starred per capita in England. What's more striking in practice is that even the grocers and pubs play the local card: menus display the names of local producers, sausages, cheeses, cider from neighboring Herefordshire. In September, the Ludlow Food and Drink Festival takes over the castle courtyard - if you're there that weekend, book well in advance.

→

Whitcliffe Common, a 10-minute walk from the castle, offers the best panorama of the town: red tile roofs, church spires, castle ruins on their spur above the Teme. Go in the late afternoon for the light. This is where locals walk their dogs - an unpretentious place, and all the more pleasant for it.

Drive 12 km north and stop at Stokesay Castle - one of the best-preserved medieval fortified manor houses in England. Built around 1291 by a wool merchant named Laurence de Ludlow who had made his fortune supplying the crown, it has undergone no major modifications since the 17th century. English Heritage manages the site: you can enter the halls as they were in the Middle Ages, with original floorboards, fireplaces, and dovecote. Allow 1h30 on site.

Continue to Church Stretton, nicknamed "Little Switzerland" by the Victorians who came here to recuperate - the comparison is flattering but the setting is indeed spectacular. The village sits between the hills of the Long Mynd to the west and Caer Caradoc to the east. If the weather is clear, the view from the Long Mynd ridges reaches Wales.

→

Cardingmill Valley, a National Trust property 5 minutes from Church Stretton, is the ideal starting point for a hike on the Shropshire hills. A path follows the stream up to the moorlands of the Long Mynd plateau - allow 2h round trip for the panorama from the summit, with no major technical difficulty. The National Trust cafe at the bottom of the valley is perfect for recovering afterwards.

→

Back in Ludlow in the evening, take the opportunity to dine properly. The town deserves a serious table - good addresses abound on Church Street and Broad Street, and the kitchens all rely on local producers from Shropshire and Herefordshire. Book if you're coming on a weekend.

Buxton is England's highest market town - 300 meters elevation, a cutting cold in winter, and geothermal springs flowing at 28°C for 5,000 years. The Romans already understood the deal when they settled here in AD 78 under the name Aquae Arnemetiae. The golden age came in 1780 when the 5th Duke of Devonshire built the Crescent, an imposing Georgian facade directly inspired by Bath's Royal Crescent - some believe Buxton's is actually more accomplished. Restored and reopened in 2020 as a five-star hotel-spa, the building can also be visited via an exhibition on its thermal history.

Buxton's Opera House is a gem of Edwardian architecture - designed in 1903 by Frank Matcham, the prolific architect who designed dozens of the UK's finest theaters. The renovated interior is sumptuous: ceiling paintings, gilding, semicircular balconies. It's also, according to its inscription, the highest theater in England. The Beatles performed here in 1963 in the adjacent Octagon. If there's a show during your visit, get a ticket.

The Devonshire Dome - a former ducal stable converted into a hospital in the 19th century, now a university campus - houses a 44-meter diameter dome, one of the largest unsupported in Europe, wider than Rome's Pantheon. The adjacent Pavilion Gardens, 9 hectares laid out in 1871 along the Wye, are ideal for a late afternoon stroll.

→

Poole's Cavern, west of town, is a natural limestone cave formed two million years ago. It contains Derbyshire's largest stalactite and poached-egg-shaped stalagmites - a rare formation due to aragonite deposits. Guided tours last about 45 minutes. On the way out, climb through Grin Low Country Park to Solomon's Temple, a Victorian folly from 1896 erected on a Bronze Age burial mound: the view over the town below and the Peak District hills is the region's best panorama.

→

Before leaving, fill your water bottle at St Ann's Well fountain, opposite the Crescent - it's Buxton mineral water, the same sold in bottles throughout the UK, flowing here at 28°C with free access. Nothing stops you from joining the queue with locals filling their containers.

Climb up to Peveril Castle - the view from the keep over the Hope Valley alone justifies the climb. The castle was built in 1086 by William Peveril, a knight of William the Conqueror, then the square keep added by Henry II in 1176. The enclosed valley just behind the castle is called Cave Dale, a limestone gorge whose vertical walls served as the setting for the hill scene in The Princess Bride (1987). If you have the film in mind, recognizing it is a small pleasure.

Blue John Stone is a semi-precious fluorite with blue and yellow reflections - its name supposedly comes from the French "bleu-jaune." It exists in only two caves in the world, both at Castleton. The Blue John Cavern, perched on the flank of Mam Tor, contains 8 of the 14 known veins. Miners still work it each winter in areas closed to the public. The 40-minute guided tour descends into massive natural chambers: the Crystallised Cavern, the Variegated Cavern. Bring a jacket - it's 7°C inside year-round.

Peak Cavern - nicknamed Devil's Arse by locals for centuries, officially renamed in 1880 to avoid shocking Queen Victoria - has the largest cave entrance in the British Isles: 18 meters high, 30 wide. Rope-makers lived in the cave mouth until 1915 and sold their ropes to the village. Speedwell Cavern, at the entrance to Winnats Pass, is explored by boat on an underground canal that miners dug by hand in the 18th century hoping to find lead - they never found any.

→

Mam Tor is called the Shivering Mountain because its unstable shale layers constantly slide. In 1977 a landslide permanently closed the road that circled it - the buckled tarmac is still visible. Climb to the summit from Mam Nick pass: 20 minutes on a paved path, 517 meters, simultaneous views of the Hope valley and Edale valley. If you have energy, continue along the Great Ridge to Lose Hill for a 360° panorama.

→

Winnats Pass deserves a stop even briefly: a vertiginous limestone gorge, a collapsed ancient cave, whose walls rise on each side of the road. It's been the only access to Castleton from the west since the Mam Tor road closed. In 1758, five miners murdered a young couple who had come to marry clandestinely at Peak Forest - the last survivor confessed to the crime on his deathbed, 40 years later.

Edale is a handful of farms and two pubs lost in a valley encircled on all sides by the Dark Peak summits. Since 1965 it's also been the official starting point of the Pennine Way - England's first national trail, 431 kilometers to the Scottish border, reputed as one of the UK's toughest because of the peaty moorlands and exposure to the elements. The start is marked by the Old Nags Head, a pub from 1577, at the end of the village. In 1932, the Kinder Scout Mass Trespass started from here: hundreds of walkers invaded private moorlands to claim the right to walk in natural spaces - an act that directly led to the creation of Britain's national parks.

→

The hike up to the Kinder Scout plateau is the main reason to stop at Edale. Ascend via Grindsbrook Clough - a rocky gorge with some short sections of easy scrambling - to the moor and peat plateau at 636 meters. The view from the rocks of Crowden Tower over Edale valley and the Great Ridge is one of the Peak District's finest. Full loop via Jacob's Ladder: 13 km, 3h30 to 4h. Bring a waterproof jacket even in good weather - the plateau can be covered in fog within minutes, and the wind never really stops up there.

→

If you prefer something shorter, climb onto the Great Ridge from Hollins Cross - 30 minutes from the village - to the summit of Mam Tor, then descend on the Edale side. The view north, over the dark moorlands of the Dark Peak, is radically different from what you saw from Castleton the day before: more austere, vaster, wilder.

Chatsworth House has belonged to the Dukes of Devonshire since 1552, when the formidable Bess of Hardwick and her husband William Cavendish established their residence here. Mary Queen of Scots was imprisoned here by order of Elizabeth I from 1569 - the apartments assigned to her can still be visited. Jane Austen, who stayed in Bakewell in the late 1790s, supposedly drew inspiration for Pemberley from here - a scene from the 2005 film was shot here. The 25 open rooms house Rembrandts, Roman sculptures, family portraits by Lucian Freud. Book your tickets online, queues in high season are serious.

Chatsworth's 105 acres of gardens are worth as much as the house. The Great Cascade, designed in 1696 and fed purely by gravity from a high-altitude reservoir, still operates its 24 stepped pools as it did from day one. The Emperor Fountain can shoot to 87 meters. There's also a maze, contemporary sculptures in the park, a Victorian greenhouse. Allow at least 2h just for the gardens.

Bakewell, 5 km from Chatsworth, is a compact, no-nonsense market town. Its local obsession: the Bakewell Pudding - not the Bakewell Tart sold in the rest of the country, which deeply irritates the locals. The original version is a soft pastry with a jam base and almond filling, born here by accident in the 19th century. The Bakewell Pudding Shop still makes them according to the original recipe.

→

Haddon Hall, 3 km from Bakewell, is one of the most accomplished examples of a medieval and Tudor manor house in England - and unlike Chatsworth, it has been virtually unchanged since the 17th century. The medieval kitchens, the great hall with its timber roof, the terraced gardens with their roses are intact. The Duke of Rutland still lives there. Jane Eyre and several Pride and Prejudice adaptations were filmed here - you might recognize the angles if you've seen the films.

→

The Monsal Trail is a 13 km path laid out on a Victorian railway line through the Wye valley, with spectacular brick viaducts over the limestone meanders of the river. Access from Bakewell is immediate. By bike (rental on site), the round trip to Monsal Head viaduct takes 2h and offers viewpoints you couldn't reach otherwise. Ideal if you still have energy after Chatsworth.

Nottingham

Top hotels

9 The Old School Room - The Lace Market
9.4 Modern 2 Bed House with Parking and Garden

The real medieval castle of Nottingham was demolished by Cromwell after the Civil War - what stands today is a 17th-century ducal palace converted into a museum. Don't come for a castle: come for the caves. Beneath the building, a network of medieval tunnels carved into the red sandstone burrows into the rock - dungeons, wine cellars, secret passages, some occupied since Anglo-Saxon times. The underground guided tour is the best thing to do here.

The Lace Market is the former heart of the global lace industry - in the 19th century, Nottingham manufactured the machine lace worn throughout Europe. The red brick Victorian warehouses are now bars, galleries, and restaurants. St Mary's Church, in Perpendicular style, is the church where Robin Hood supposedly married Maid Marian according to legend. The National Justice Museum, in a former Lace Market courthouse, displays 14th-century courtrooms and dungeons from 1449.

Sherwood Forest, 30 km north, is home to the Major Oak: England's largest oak tree, estimated at 800 to 1,000 years old, 10 meters in girth, 28 meters canopy spread, 23 tons. Its branches have been supported by metal frameworks for decades. The forest that once covered a quarter of Nottinghamshire is reduced today to a 420-hectare nature reserve - but the trail from the visitor center to the tree takes 15 minutes and the atmosphere of the old oak forest is real.

→

Ye Olde Trip to Jerusalem is carved directly into the sandstone rock beneath the castle - rooms hewn by hand, low ceilings, with raw rock ceiling above the tables. The sign claims 1189 as its founding date, when crusaders stopped here before joining Richard the Lionheart. The exact date is debated, but the pub has existed at least since the 17th century and the underground atmosphere is perfectly authentic. Stop here for a drink before hitting the road.

→

To return: direct train from Nottingham Station to London St Pancras in 1h40, frequency every hour. The M1 motorway links Nottingham to London in about 2h depending on traffic. East Midlands airport is 20 km southwest of the city - connections to major European cities.
