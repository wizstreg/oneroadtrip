Moselle Circuit 10 Days: Metz, Bitche Citadel and Chagall Stained Glass

Between sacred light and military stone, Moselle unfolds its treasures: a cathedral with 6,500 m² of stained glass, an impregnable citadel that resisted for 230 days in 1870, centuries-old glassmaking expertise, and transatlantic memory.

📏 Daily average 33 km

Discover Moselle, a land of contrasts where the 6,500 m² of stained glass windows in Metz Cathedral, including Chagall's creations, rival Vauban's fortifications in Bitche. From Sarreguemines faience to the Saint-Avold American Cemetery (10,489 graves), via the Saint-Louis-Arzviller inclined plane and Centre Pompidou-Metz, this 10-day itinerary reveals unexpectedly rich border heritage between France and Germany.

  • Metz Cathedral: 6,500 m² of stained glass, France's record, Chagall's creations in the north transept (1958-1970)
  • Bitche Citadel: heroic 230-day siege in 1870, audiovisual tour through 500 m of underground passages
  • Sarreguemines Faience Museum: 1880 Winter Garden listed as historic monument, 200 years of ceramics
  • Chagall's Peace stained glass in Sarrebourg: 12 m high, the largest ever designed by the artist (1974-1976)
  • Saint-Avold American Cemetery: 10,489 graves on 46 ha, largest US WWII cemetery in Europe
  • Centre Pompidou-Metz: hexagonal roof of 8,500 m² in woven wood, 77 m spire, Shigeru Ban architecture
  • Saint-Louis-Arzviller inclined plane: unique boat lift in Europe, 44.55 m vertical drop in 4 minutes
  • Amnéville Zoo: 2,000 animals of 360 species on 18 ha, one of France's most beautiful wildlife parks

Discover

1
Saint-Étienne Cathedral displays its 6,500 m² of stained glass windows, an absolute record in France ahead of Chartres' 2,600 m². Nicknamed the 'Good Lord's Lantern', it bathes in changing light where Marc Chagall's mystical blues dialogue with Hermann de Münster's medieval windows. Its naves rise 42 meters under vault, France's third highest. Between 1958 and 1970, Chagall created stained glass windows inspired by the Old Testament for the ambulatory and north transept, featuring the Sacrifice of Isaac and Jacob's Dream in tones of supernatural blue, cosmic green, and mystical red.
⏱️ 90 min
2
Centre Pompidou-Metz emerges like a spaceship set down on the landscape, with its hexagonal roof of 8,500 m² in woven wood inspired by a Chinese hat. Designed by Japanese architect Shigeru Ban and Jean de Gastines, inaugurated in 2010, it deploys 5,000 m² of exhibition spaces under a structure of 18 km of glued laminated beams. Its spire rises to 77 meters, a nod to the year 1977 when the Paris Centre Pompidou was created. The 15-meter-wide galleries frame views of the cathedral, the train station, and the Seille park.
⏱️ 120 min
3
Place Saint-Louis, the 13th-century medieval arcades draw a setting of blond stone where the footsteps of money changers and Lombard merchants still echo. Half-timbered facades and wrought-iron signs tell eight centuries of international trade. Take time for a coffee under the vaults, where the city of Metz has been beating since the Middle Ages.
⏱️ 45 min
🎯

Activities

→
Stroll through the German Imperial Quarter, an architectural laboratory built between 1902 and 1918 under Wilhelm II. Avenue Foch lines up its neo-Romanesque and Art Nouveau villas with polychrome facades, while the monumental 1908 train station in gray sandstone evokes Rhine castles with its 40-meter central tower. This kaleidoscope of Wilhelminian styles offers a unique immersion in Prussian urbanism of the Belle Époque, with over 800 listed buildings.
⏱️ 75 min
→
The Musée de la Cour d'Or unveils 2,000 years of Metz history in the former Petits Carmes convent. 1st-century Gallo-Roman baths, Roman mosaics, and Romanesque sculptures dialogue under centuries-old vaults. The Merovingian collections are among the richest in France, with the treasure from the Metz-Sablon necropolis. Don't miss the medieval Greniers de Chèvremont integrated into the route.
⏱️ 90 min

Discover

1
The Tour aux Puces, a massive 14th-century donjon 30 meters high, dominated the Moselle valley with its 4-meter-thick walls. This remnant of the medieval fortifications houses the Thionville municipal museum, with Gallo-Roman collections and exhibits on 1,000 years of border history. From the top, the panorama extends from Luxembourg to Germany. The tower owes its name to a flea market that was held at its feet.
⏱️ 60 min
2
Vauban's casemates, designed between 1679 and 1687, plunge into 500 meters of underground galleries dug into the rock. These defensive tunnels could accommodate 1,200 soldiers and controlled access to the Luxembourg border. The vaulted chambers, powder magazines, and countermine wells tell the story of 17th-century military engineering. The temperature stays constant at 12°C year-round.
⏱️ 75 min
🎯

Activities

Walk along the Moselle riverbanks, redesigned between 2010 and 2015 into green promenades. The riverside path connects the medieval Cloister Park with contemporary gardens, passing by the 18th-century Château de Guentrange and its French garden. Plane trees, rose gardens, and playgrounds line the 3 km of walkways. In summer, outdoor cafes and river shuttle boats enliven the quays.
⏱️ 60 min
→
Explore the historic center where the Saint-Maximin church (12th-16th centuries) displays a flamboyant Gothic facade topped by a 54-meter bell tower. The pedestrian streets of the old town preserve 18th and 19th-century bourgeois houses with colored facades. The Place du Marché, bordered by 18th-century arcades, hosts a market on Wednesday and Saturday mornings, perpetuating a 700-year tradition.

Amnéville Zoo spreads across 18 hectares with 2,000 animals from 360 species in natural habitats. Gorilla Island houses a family of 12 lowland gorillas, the Tropical Vivarium shelters 500 reptiles, and the polar bear enclosure recreates the Arctic tundra. The African savannah, Asian jungle, and American plains offer immersive scenographies. Educational shows (sea lions, birds of prey) punctuate the 5 km walk, about 4 to 5 hours for a complete visit.
⏱️ 240 min
2
The Amnéville thermal baths draw their 34°C water from a 1,500-meter-deep source rich in trace elements. The 6,000 m² complex features 25 pools (wave pool, whirlpools, aquatic jets), 14 saunas and hammams, and a wellness area. The outdoor lagoon, open year-round, offers winter bathing under snow. Aquagym sessions, underwater massages, and aquabike complement the facilities.

Family areas (Octopus, Magic Carousel), water attractions (Colorado River, Gold Mine), and themed shows. May to September, 10 am to 7 pm, full day needed.

Discover

1
The Faience Museum occupies the former Moulin de la Blies, reconstructed in 2007 around the listed 1880 Winter Garden. This 750 m² metal and glass structure shelters 2,500 pieces tracing 200 years of ceramic production: earthenware services, decorative panels, Art Nouveau vases, and Art Deco creations. The collections present the evolution of techniques and styles from 1790 to the 1990s, when the last Sarreguemines kilns shut down.
⏱️ 90 min
2
The Casino des Faïenceries, an Art Nouveau jewel from 1927, amazes with its ceramic decorations: mosaics, friezes, and monumental panels by master ceramist Alexandre Sandier. The grand hall, completely covered in earthenware, evokes Sarreguemines' golden age when 3,000 workers produced 25 million pieces per year. The building now houses temporary exhibitions on ceramic history and industrial heritage.
⏱️ 60 min
3
The Faience Garden (Jardin des Faïenciers) displays monumental ceramic panels in an 8,000 m² landscaped park. The route presents 150 decorative pieces: Art Nouveau friezes, company signs, stove tiles, and architectural elements salvaged from demolished buildings. Each panel illustrates a facet of earthenware art: advertising, architecture, technical virtuosity. Free access year-round.
⏱️ 45 min
🎯

Activities

Stroll through the German district (Deutsches Viertel) built between 1870 and 1918 during the German annexation. Neo-Gothic buildings, half-timbered houses, and bourgeois villas with Art Nouveau facades line the streets. The Sainte-Croix church (1886) in red sandstone, the town hall (1895) with its 46-meter tower, and the Protestant temple (1897) illustrate Wilhelminian architecture. A unique cross-border architectural heritage.
⏱️ 60 min

Cross the Saar footbridge to Germany and walk along the riverbanks connecting France and Saarland. The promenade links Sarreguemines to Saarbrücken through 15 km of greenways. Medieval mills, locks, and old factories punctuate the route. Cycling possible, bike rentals available on the French side. A border walk illustrating the Franco-German reconciliation.

Discover

1
The Citadel of Bitche, a masterpiece by Vauban and Cormontaigne, dominates the town from its 330-meter-high sandstone peak. Redesigned between 1679 and 1754, it resisted the 230-day siege of 1870 without surrendering, an exceptional feat in the Franco-Prussian War. The audiovisual route 'L'Héroïque Siège' immerses visitors in 500 meters of underground tunnels: casemates, powder magazines, wells, and galleries where soldiers' voices echo. Above, the ramparts offer a 360° panorama of the Northern Vosges.
⏱️ 120 min
2
The Northern Vosges Regional Natural Park stretches over 130,500 hectares of sandstone forests and rock formations. The Park House in Bitche presents the local geology, biodiversity, and Alsatian-Lorraine traditions. Interactive exhibitions on endemic species (lynx, black grouse, peregrine falcon), hiking trails in preserved forests, and discovery of rock castles perched on sandstone peaks. UNESCO World Biosphere Reserve.
⏱️ 75 min
🎯

Activities

→
Hike the Simserhof fortified line, the best-preserved Maginot Line fort. This underground city built between 1930 and 1935 housed 600 soldiers with dormitories, kitchens, infirmary, electric power plant, and ammunition depot. Electric trains circulated in 3 km of galleries connecting 17 combat blocks. The guided tour, enhanced with lighting and sound, recreates the daily life of soldiers. Bring warm clothing, constant 10°C.
⏱️ 120 min
→
Discover the Saint-Louis-lès-Bitche crystal works, founded in 1586 by Lorraine glassmakers. The museum showcases exceptional pieces: 19th-century chandeliers, Art Deco vases, and contemporary creations. Crystal blowing demonstrations by master glassmakers reveal ancestral gestures: harvesting, blowing, cutting, engraving. The company store sells signed pieces. France's oldest crystal works still in operation.
⏱️ 90 min

Discover

1
The fortified city of Phalsbourg, founded in 1570 by Count Palatine George John I, preserves its 17th-century monumental gates. The French Gate and the German Gate, rebuilt by Vauban in 1680, frame the historic center with their classical stone facades topped by clock towers. The ramparts, partially intact, protected the city during the siege of 1814 when 2,200 defenders resisted 30,000 allied soldiers for 7 months. The military heritage path circles the fortifications.
⏱️ 75 min
2
The Saint-Louis crystal works museum, located 8 km away, presents 430 years of glassmaking excellence. The collections showcase masterpieces: the 1878 Universal Exhibition chandelier, Art Nouveau creations by Emile Gallé, and contemporary pieces by world-renowned designers. Immersive exhibitions explain techniques: blowing, cutting, engraving, gilding. The boutique sells numbered pieces signed by master glassmakers.
⏱️ 90 min
🎯

Activities

→
Visit the Saint-Louis-Arzviller inclined plane, a unique boat lift in Europe. Built between 1965 and 1969, it replaces 17 locks with a 44.55-meter vertical lift traversed in 4 minutes. The 108-meter-long steel tank carries 350-ton boats on rails at a 41% gradient. The visitor center explains the hydraulic and mechanical system. Boat rides available from April to October, approximately 1 hour round trip.
⏱️ 90 min
→
Hike in the Northern Vosges forests around Phalsbourg, with trails leading to sandstone peaks. The Circuit of the Three Borders (12 km) passes by the vestiges of the Lutzelbourg castle perched on a 320-meter cliff, offering a panorama of the Zorn valley. Marked trails, variable difficulty, maps available at the tourist office. Beech and oak forests, typical Vosges sandstone rock formations.
⏱️ 120 min

Discover

1
The Peace stained glass window by Marc Chagall, created between 1974 and 1976 for the 13th-century Cordeliers chapel, rises 12 meters high and spans 7.5 meters wide. This is the largest stained glass window ever designed by the artist, a hymn to life and reconciliation in celestial blue tones, emerald green, and flamboyant red. Allegorical figures (tree of life, prophets, animals) intertwine in poetic composition. Natural light animated by 600 m² of glass transforms throughout the day.
⏱️ 60 min
2
The Sarrebourg museum, installed in the former Cordeliers convent, presents Gallo-Roman archaeology and regional collections. Mosaics from the 2nd-3rd centuries, funerary steles, and ceramics attest to Roman Sarrebourg's importance. The medieval section displays sculptures, liturgical furniture, and paintings from the 15th to 18th centuries. The lapidary garden brings together fragments of ancient buildings.
⏱️ 75 min
3
The Sarrebourg canal marina, on the Marne-Rhine canal, offers boat rides and canal cruises. The port hosts 120 pleasure boat berths and welcomes cruise boats. Waterside promenades, nautical activities (canoe, pedal boat), and cycle paths along the canal. The Boat Museum traces the history of canal navigation and traditional trades. Boat rentals available for exploring the canal.
⏱️ 45 min
🎯

Activities

→
Walk in the forest of Sarrebourg, 3,500 hectares of oaks and beeches crisscrossed by 50 km of marked trails. The Sentier des Sources (8 km) discovers the springs feeding the Sarre, with footbridges over streams and forest clearings. The Lorraine sandstone peaks offer panoramic views. Medium difficulty, 2h30 walking, signposting and maps at the tourist office.
⏱️ 90 min
→
Explore the historic center where the Saint-Barthélémy church (18th century) displays baroque altarpieces and an 18th-century organ. The pedestrian streets preserve 18th and 19th-century bourgeois houses. The Place du Marché, surrounded by arcaded facades, hosts a market on Tuesday and Friday mornings. The town hall (1770) in classical style faces the park with centuries-old trees.

Discover

1
The Saint-Avold American Cemetery and Memorial, inaugurated in 1960, is the largest American World War II cemetery in Europe. On 46 hectares of perfectly maintained lawns, 10,489 white marble crosses and Stars of David align in impeccable symmetry, honoring American soldiers who fell during the liberation of France, Luxembourg, and Germany in 1944-1945. The memorial chapel in Vosges sandstone displays battle maps and the Wall of the Missing with 444 names. An overwhelming place of remembrance.
⏱️ 90 min
2
The Saint-Nabor basilica, built between 1755 and 1764, is a baroque jewel in Vosges sandstone. Its 60-meter bell towers dominate the city, topped by bulbous domes typical of Central Europe. The interior amazes with stucco decorations, gilded woodwork, and trompe-l'oeil frescoes. The main organ, built in 1782 by Jean-Conrad Sauer, is one of the most beautiful baroque organs in Lorraine. The crypt houses the relics of Saint Nabor.
⏱️ 60 min
🎯

Activities

→
Visit the Saint-Avold mining museum, installed in the former Merlebach mine. The exhibitions retrace 150 years of coal extraction (1856-2004): miners' equipment, gallery reconstruction, pneumatic tools, and documentation on working conditions. Educational circuit in surface buildings, testimonies from former miners. The mining towers, industrial heritage from the 20th century, dominate the landscape.
⏱️ 75 min
→
Stroll in the Vallon du Parc, a 15-hectare landscaped park surrounding the Château de Beausoleil. Tree-lined paths, ornamental ponds, rose gardens, and century-old trees compose a green setting. Children's playgrounds, picnic areas, and sports facilities. The Baroque-style castle (18th century) houses administrative services. Free access year-round, ideal for family walks.

Discover

1
The Georges de La Tour museum, inaugurated in 2003 in a contemporary building, pays tribute to the master of chiaroscuro born in Vic in 1593. The permanent exhibition presents a collection of 17th-century paintings, contextualizing La Tour's work within the Baroque movement. Temporary exhibitions explore influences and contemporaries. Didactic multimedia spaces explain the painter's revolutionary technique: controlled light, mystical atmospheres, and humble models treated with nobility.
⏱️ 75 min
2
The Saint-Marien collegiate church from the 13th-15th centuries preserves Renaissance stained glass windows from the 16th century and remarkable liturgical furniture: 18th-century stalls, 1749 Wegmann organ, and baroque paintings. Its squat 50-meter bell tower dominates the mechanical tile roofs of the village, a sandstone sentinel watching over the former salt marshes that made Vic's fortune from the 11th to 17th century with 27 salt works producing 60,000 tons of salt per year.
⏱️ 45 min
🎯

Activities

→
Discover the history of salt in the Saulnois region, land of 400 salt springs exploited since Gallic then Gallo-Roman times. The salt marshes of Vic, Moyenvic, and Marsal produced 'white gold' in the Middle Ages by solar evaporation in lead pans. A little-known heritage that shaped the Lorraine economy until the 18th century, controlled by the bishops of Metz who drew immense revenues from it. The flat country of shimmering ponds as far as the eye can see preserves this unique landscape.
⏱️ 60 min
→
Stroll through the streets of Vic-sur-Seille, a characterful village of 1,200 inhabitants with houses of pink Vosges sandstone. Ancient salt works including Gallic briquetages, 16th-17th-century Renaissance mansions, sandstone fountains, and wash houses tell the story of the prosperity of a city that exported its 'white gold' by river to Nancy, Metz, and even Burgundy. Partially preserved medieval ramparts.
⏱️ 45 min

Discover

1
The Temple Neuf, a neo-Romanesque Protestant church built between 1901 and 1904 on a small island in the Moselle, is reflected in the waters with its twin 58-meter spires. Erected during the German annexation, it illustrates the Wilhelminian will to mark the urban landscape. Gray sandstone facades, copper roofs turned green, and stained glass windows by Wilhelm Pfeffer create a romantic decor. The interior, restored after World War II, displays Rhine Romanesque architecture.
⏱️ 45 min
2
The Saint-Pierre-aux-Nonnains church, built in the 4th century, is one of the oldest religious buildings in France. Originally a Gallo-Roman basilica then a Benedictine church, it preserves walls 1,600 years old. The nave, austere with exposed stones, evokes late Antiquity. Archaeological excavations revealed Roman mosaics and Merovingian foundations. The building now houses exhibitions and concerts, its millennial acoustics attracting musicians.
⏱️ 60 min
🎯

Activities

→
Take a boat cruise on the Moselle to admire Metz's architecture from the water. The 1-hour routes pass under medieval bridges (Moyen Pont, Pont des Roches), along the cathedral and the Temple Neuf, and the imperial port district. Commentary explains the city's 3,000-year history. Evening cruises at sunset offer a romantic atmosphere. Departures from the port quays April to October.
⏱️ 60 min
→
Explore the covered market of Metz, a metallic structure from 1831 renovated in 2007. The 40 stalls offer regional products: Lorraine mirabelle plums, Munster cheese, Metz sausages, and local wines. Butchers, fishmongers, bakers, and produce sellers perpetuate artisanal traditions. The central square hosts a market on Tuesday, Thursday, and Saturday mornings. The wrought iron and glass architecture evokes 19th-century Les Halles.
⏱️ 45 min
