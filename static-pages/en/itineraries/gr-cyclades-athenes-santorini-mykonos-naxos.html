Cyclades Journey: Athens, Santorini, Mykonos & Naxos

From the Acropolis to Santorini's volcanic cliffs, from festive Mykonos to Naxos' marble villages - ten days in the classic Cyclades.

Begin in Athens with its Acropolis built between 447 and 432 BC under Pericles' orders. Fly to Santorini and its volcanic cliffs born from a cataclysmic eruption around 1600 BC. Ferry across to Mykonos, its 16th-century Venetian windmills and essential excursion to sacred Delos, a UNESCO site. End at Naxos - the largest Cyclades island - between giant kouroi, marble villages and deserted beaches.

Key highlights (4)
  • Athens: Acropolis, Parthenon and historic Plaka and Monastiraki neighborhoods
  • Santorini: volcanic caldera, Fira-Oia hike and Oia sunset
  • Mykonos: Venetian windmills, Little Venice and UNESCO-listed Delos excursion
  • Naxos: Portara, Venetian kastro, giant kouroi and wild beaches

Start at Syntagma Square - literally 'Constitution' - the beating heart of modern Athens since 1843. From the café terraces, watch the changing of the Evzone Guard in front of Parliament: the ritual happens every hour, with the full ceremonial change on Sunday mornings at 11am. Then head up Ermou Street toward Monastiraki market, where stalls overflow with copper icons, ceramics and ancient replicas.

The Plaka neighborhood, wedged between the Acropolis and Monastiraki, is one of the oldest continuously inhabited districts in Europe - its alleyways date back to Ottoman times and beyond. You'll find neoclassical houses, tavernas that have been around for decades, and some of the city's rare pedestrian zones. Avoid restaurants with touts at the entrance - the best tavernas don't need hawkers.

The Ancient Agora, at the foot of the Acropolis, is the birthplace of Athenian democracy: this is where Socrates debated, political assemblies convened, and philosophers strolled beneath the colonnades. The Temple of Hephaestus on the site's edge is one of the best-preserved Greek temples in the world - converted to a church in the 7th century, then preserved as is.

The Temple of Olympian Zeus, south of the Acropolis, was ancient Greece's largest temple once completed. Construction began in 515 BC and wasn't finished until six centuries later, under Emperor Hadrian in 132 AD. Of the original 104 columns, only 15 remain standing - a sixteenth collapsed during a storm in 1852 and still lies on the ground.

→

Explore Monastiraki flea market on Sunday morning - that's when it's most lively and well-stocked. Vendors spread out everything and anything from dawn: antiques, old books, vinyl records, jewelry. It's also the best place to grab a hot gyros, standing up, before starting your day.

→

Late afternoon, head up to the Psyrri and Gazi neighborhoods for drinks: these former industrial districts converted to bars and restaurants are today's hip Athens, far less touristy than Plaka. Locals dine late here - before 9pm, restaurants are half empty.

→

Try pork or lamb souvlaki at one of the artisan grill houses around Monastiraki - it's Athens' quintessential street food, far better than at tourist restaurants. Pair it with tzatziki and grilled pita.

The Acropolis sits at 156 meters elevation on a rocky plateau measuring 300 by 150 meters. The Parthenon, built between 447 and 432 BC under Pericles by architects Ictinus and Callicrates under sculptor Phidias' supervision, required 22,000 tons of marble quarried from Mount Pentelicus 17 km away. In 1687, a Venetian bomb ignited gunpowder stored inside by the Ottomans - the roof collapsed. Arrive at opening (8am): crowds double after 10am. Book tickets online to skip the line.

The Erechtheion, north of the Parthenon, is the last monument erected on the Acropolis before the end of the 5th century BC. It's famous for its Porch of the Caryatids: six draped maidens serving as load-bearing columns. The five originals are in the Acropolis Museum - the statues on site are replicas. Lord Elgin took a sixth to London in 1803; it remains in the British Museum despite repeated restitution requests.

The Acropolis Museum, opened in 2009 at the hill's base, preserves the Parthenon's sculptures and friezes that weren't stolen or destroyed, as well as the original Erechtheion Caryatids. From its windows, you get a direct view of the Acropolis. Allow at least 90 minutes.

The Panathenaic Stadium is the world's only stadium built entirely of white marble. Inaugurated in 330 BC for the Panathenaic Games, it was renovated in 1896 to host the first modern Olympic Games. It can accommodate 50,000 spectators. You're allowed to run on the track.

→

Climb Mount Lycabettus by funicular or on foot via the trail (about 45 minutes' walk from the Kolonaki neighborhood). At 277 meters, it's Athens' highest point - the view embraces the entire city from Piraeus port to the Acropolis. In the evening, the illuminated city and Acropolis views are striking.

→

In the evening, if programming permits, the Odeon of Herodes Atticus has hosted the Athens Festival since 1955 beneath its marble seats built in 161 AD. Concerts, operas and dance performances take place under the stars, with the illuminated Acropolis as backdrop. Check the program in advance.

Fira, Santorini's capital, clings 250 meters above the caldera. The caldera itself resulted from one of the largest eruptions in human history: around 1600 BC, the island's central volcano exploded and collapsed, creating a 12 km diameter crater now filled with sea. The multicolored cliffs - black basalt, red, white pumice - tell the stratigraphy of this catastrophe. If you're afraid of heights, walk carefully along the edges.

The Museum of Prehistoric Thera in Fira displays objects discovered during Akrotiri excavations - a Minoan city buried under volcanic ash around 1600 BC, comparable to Pompeii. The original frescoes from the city are among the finest in the Aegean Antiquity: fishing scenes, navigation, young men in action. Allow an hour for the visit.

Oia is Greece's most photographed village - its cave houses carved into the cliff, cobalt domes and flowered alleyways make it a worldwide postcard. Arrive early morning, before 9am, to see it almost deserted. Oia's Byzantine castle is the best viewpoint for sunset - plan to arrive 90 minutes early to find a spot.

→

In the evening, settle in Oia for sunset. It's the most famous in the Cyclades: in July-August, hundreds of people crowd the cliff edge. Go anyway, but arrive early and position yourself slightly back from the castle for an unobstructed view without fighting for space.

→

Santorini fava, yellow split pea purée grown on the island since antiquity and protected by PDO, has incomparable sweetness. Tomatokeftedes, fritters made from cherry tomatoes concentrated by volcanic sun, are worth trying as meze. You'll find both in all Fira and Oia tavernas.

→

Santorini assyrtiko, a white grape variety cultivated in ground-level baskets to protect it from wind, produces a mineral, dry and slightly saline wine - unique in the world. The vines have thrived on the island's volcanic soils for centuries. Several estates offer tastings with caldera views.

The Akrotiri archaeological site is often compared to Pompeii: a Middle Bronze Age Minoan city, buried under meters of volcanic pumice around 1600 BC and preserved intact. Unlike Pompeii, no bodies were found here - the inhabitants had time to flee. The two-story alleyways, oil jars still in place, and house structures are protected by a massive covered structure. Allow 90 minutes.

Kamari beach, on the east coast, is Santorini's main black sand beach. This volcanic sand heats up very quickly in summer - wear shoes to walk to the water. The water is clear and quite deep quickly. The beach is long, well-equipped, with seaside tavernas.

Red Beach, west of Akrotiri, is one of Greece's most spectacular beaches: an enclosed cove between bright red lava cliffs plunging into deep blue sea. The footpath from the parking lot takes 10 to 15 minutes on rocky terrain - wear good shoes. The beach is small and packed in summer, arrive early.

Pyrgos is Santorini's best-preserved medieval village, perched on the island's highest point at 350 meters. Built around a 13th-century Venetian kastro, it has retained its labyrinthine alleyways and crenellated houses. 360° view over the entire island from the chapel at the summit. Much less crowded than Oia.

→

The Fira-Oia hike along the caldera is one of the most beautiful in the Mediterranean: 10.5 km on the cliff edge, between 250 and 350 meters height, with constant views over the 12 km diameter crater and volcanic islets at the center. Allow 3h30 to 4h with breaks. Start early morning in summer - midday heat on the volcanic stones is crushing. The trail passes through Firostefani and Imerovigli before reaching Oia.

→

Amoudi Bay, at the foot of Oia's cliffs accessible by 300 steps, is the island's most authentic spot for fish. A few fishing tavernas serve dried octopus, sea urchins and grilled fish at the water's edge. You can also dive from the rocks directly into the sea. Avoid going between noon and 3pm - the return climb in the heat is exhausting.

Megalochori is one of Santorini's most authentic villages, built in the 17th century by merchants and shipowners. Its main square with vaulted arches and small church is one of the island's most beautiful. Much less crowded than Fira or Oia, this is where you'll see the island as it was before mass tourism.

Imerovigli, between Fira and Oia, is nicknamed 'the balcony of the Aegean Sea'. Skaros Rock, a former 13th-century Venetian fortress partially collapsed into the sea during the 1956 earthquake, offers a 270° view over the Kameni islets and caldera - less crowded than Oia castle for sunset.

→

Boat excursion to Nea Kameni volcano in the caldera's center: shuttles depart from Fira's old port (accessible by 580 steps or funicular). On the islet, a trail climbs to the volcanic cone's summit at 150 meters - sulfurous fumaroles remind you the volcano remains active, its last eruption dating from 1950. You can then swim in neighboring Palaia Kameni's natural hot springs, slightly ferruginous and sulfurous water.

→

Wine tasting in Santorini is a unique experience: vines are cultivated in ground-level basket coils to protect from wind, a millennial technique unique here. The mineral, dry assyrtiko white is most renowned - but vinsanto, sweet wine from sun-dried grapes, is also worth experiencing. Several estates offer tastings with caldera views.

Chora Mykonos is a deliberate labyrinth: the alleyways are intentionally winding to confuse pirates. Its whitewashed houses, bougainvillea and blue domes form the quintessential Cycladic scene. Explore it early morning - after 10am, cruise groups invade the port and the alleys become highways.

The seven Kato Mili windmills were built in the 16th century by Venetians to grind wheat transported from neighboring islands, thanks to the Meltemi blowing 300 days a year. Their whitewashed cylindrical walls, topped with wooden and straw roofs, are Mykonos' most recognizable image. Go at sunset - the orange light on the immaculate white is photogenic, but beware the wind at the hilltop.

Little Venice - the Alefkandra district - owes its name to 18th-century captains' houses whose colorful balconies overhang the Aegean Sea directly. It's the island's only waterfront district. In the evening, the bar terraces fill up quickly - book if you want a waterfront spot.

Panagia Paraportiani is a complex of five chapels built between 1425 and the 17th century, merged into an asymmetrical white mass. It's the oldest of Mykonos' 400 churches and one of Greece's most photographed. Its name - 'Our Lady of the Side Gate' - comes from its position in the former medieval kastro gate. The interior is usually closed, but the exterior alone is worth the trip.

→

In the evening at Mykonos port, bars and terraces come alive late. The island's nightlife is renowned throughout the Mediterranean. If clubs aren't your thing, settle for a drink at Little Venice watching the sun descend over the Aegean.

→

Try kopanisti, Mykonos PDO spicy cheese - a soft fermented paste with intense flavor. Louza, dried spiced pork charcuterie, is another local specialty unavailable elsewhere in Greece. Ask for both as meze in an old port taverna.

The village of Ano Mera, 8 km from Chora, is authentic Mykonos - far from luxury boutiques and party beaches. Its Panagia Tourliani monastery, founded in 1542, houses a beautiful carved wooden iconostasis and flowered courtyard. Tavernas on the main square serve traditional family cooking at prices far below those at the port.

→

Paradise and Super Paradise beaches, south of the island, are Mykonos' party playground: loud music, beach clubs, international atmosphere. Buses serve them from Chora. If you're seeking calm, neighboring Agrari is less developed and less crowded - same fine sand, same turquoise water.

→

Psarou beach, smaller and quieter than Paradise, is ideal for enjoying the sea without techno music. The water is particularly clear and surrounding rocks are good for snorkeling.

→

Late afternoon, head to Armenistis lighthouse at the island's northwest tip - built in 1891, it measures 19 meters. From the site, panoramic views over the Aegean Sea and Tinos island. Arrive before sunset: it's one of the island's best spots, and much less crowded than Oia or the windmills.

Delos is 30 minutes by boat from Mykonos' old port. The entire 3.5 km² island has been a UNESCO site since 1990: no accommodation, no commerce, no modern construction. According to mythology, it's the birthplace of Apollo and Artemis. The French School at Athens has excavated here since 1873 and only a third of the site has been uncovered.

The Terrace of the Lions is Delos' emblematic image: Naxos marble lions, offered by the Naxians in the 7th century BC to guard Apollo's sanctuary. Of the 16 originals, only 5 survive as replicas on site - the originals are preserved in the island's museum. A sixth has stood outside the Venice Arsenal since the 17th century.

Delos' ancient theatre, built between 314 and 250 BC, could accommodate 5,000 to 6,500 spectators. This is where performances took place during the Delia, a Panhellenic festival held every four years in Apollo's honor. Sit in the stone seats to imagine the atmosphere.

Mount Cynthus, at 113 meters, is Delos' highest point. From its summit, the view embraces the entire Cyclades on clear days. This is where, according to tradition, Apollo was born. The rocky path takes 20 to 25 minutes - wear good shoes.

→

Take the early boat from Mykonos' old port - the site closes at 3pm in low season. There's no water to buy or restaurant on the island after opening: bring water and snacks. The sun is merciless, with no trees for shelter.

→

Don't leave without visiting the island's Archaeological Museum - built in 1904, it preserves the original terrace lions, a 6th-century BC kouros torso, and mosaics, jewelry and inscriptions found during excavations. Allow 45 minutes for the nine rooms.

Naxos' Portara is visible from the ferry long before docking: a massive marble doorway perched on Palatia islet at the port entrance. Its four blocks each weigh 20 tons - too heavy for the Venetians to move, which explains why it's the only standing remnant of Apollo's temple begun around 530 BC under tyrant Lygdamis and never completed. According to mythology, this islet is where Theseus abandoned Ariadne. Go at sunset: the sun descends exactly through the doorway's axis.

Chora Naxos' Kastro, founded in 1207 by Marco Sanudo, first Duke of Naxos, dominates the port from a hill. Its outer walls are formed by the backs of Venetian palaces, many retaining their coats of arms. Inside: the 13th-century Catholic cathedral, an archaeological museum housed in the former 1627 Jesuit lyceum - where Nikos Kazantzakis, author of Zorba the Greek, was a student - and a Venetian museum.

The Temple of Demeter at Sangri, 15 km south of Chora, is a 6th-century BC Doric temple partially reconstructed with its original materials. Small site museum with carved fragments found. The place is quiet and non-touristy - worth seeing if you have a vehicle.

→

The beaches on Naxos' southwest coast - Agios Prokopios, Agia Anna, Plaka - are among the Cyclades' finest: fine, pale sand, shallow turquoise water. Agios Prokopios is the most equipped; Plaka stretches 5 km and is wilder. All are accessible by bus from Chora.

→

Arseniko (Naxos graviera), a hard aged cheese with powerful flavor, is produced under PDO on the island. Taste it with local honey at Chora cheese shops. Naxos potatoes - small, flavorful, PDO - are renowned throughout Greece: roasted with olive oil and lemon, they accompany all taverna dishes.

Halki, the island's former capital, is a marble village in the heart of Tragea plain, surrounded by olive groves and vineyards. Its main square is animated by a few tavernas and the Vallindras distillery, producing kitron since 1896 - a unique liqueur distilled from the leaves of a particular citrus tree that grows only in Naxos.

Apiranthos is one of Naxos' best-preserved villages: alleyways entirely paved with marble, Cycladic stone houses, four small museums. The inhabitants largely descend from Cretan refugees who arrived in the 17th century. Don't leave without stopping at the café on the main square - local life unfolds there effortlessly.

The Kouros of Apollonas, at the island's northern tip, is an 11-meter, 80-ton giant statue abandoned in its quarry in the 7th century BC - probably due to a crack in the marble. It's lain on its back in the rock for over 2,700 years. Access is free and open, roadside.

→

The route between Chora, Halki, Apiranthos and Apollonas crosses the mountainous heart of Naxos - olive trees, vineyards, marble villages and Aegean Sea views. It's the best way to see the island in its reality. Allow half a day with stops.

→

Aliko beach, southwest of the island, is one of Naxos' most isolated and wild: fine sand, dunes covered with cedars, crystal-clear waters and very few people. A sand path leads through the dunes to the water's edge. Bring supplies - there's nothing on site.

→

Late afternoon, return to Chora for the Portara at sunset - it's even more beautiful than on arrival, from the pedestrian causeway crossing the port. Then settle into a waterfront taverna for your last evening in Naxos.
