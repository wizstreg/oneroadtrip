Grand Est Road Trip: Vineyards, Ramparts and Forests

Where sun-drenched vineyards cascade down golden slopes, thermal waters bubble from ancient stone, and half-timbered villages spiral through time—Grand Est awaits.

Journey through Eastern France between fortified towns and wine villages. Explore Plombières-les-Bains and Langres, discover Joinville and Domrémy, wander through Wissembourg, Obernai and Riquewihr, stroll Kaysersberg and Eguisheim, then follow the wine terraces of Mittelbergheim and Bergheim.

Key highlights (5)
  • Riquewihr: where Renaissance splendor crowns Alsace's wine heart
  • Kaysersberg: medieval alleys flowing with centuries of winemaking soul
  • Obernai: a Market Square where baroque fountains dance with storks
  • Langres: limestone heights crowned by Europe's grandest ramparts
  • Plombières-les-Bains: where Roman waters still heal beneath Empire grandeur

The Thermes Napoléon rises with its Second Empire façade at the heart of town. Inside, an 18-meter vaulted ceiling crowns a 65-meter stone corridor bathed in light. The entire ensemble is listed as a Historic Monument.

Descend to the Bains Romains, Gallo-Roman remains from the 1st century. Roman legions captured the hot springs here by diverting the Augronne river and pouring a concrete foundation pierced with chimneys to channel the water upward.

The Pavillon des Princes, a sober 18th-century building, hosted a meeting that changed history: the 1858 interview that decided Italy's fate.

The Saint-Amé church blends flamboyant neo-Gothic with imperial heritage: its spire bears the crown of the Empire. It's listed as a Historic Monument.

Step into the museum dedicated to the landscape painter born here in 1817, a figure of the Barbizon School. Canvases, sketches and views of the Vosges valley compose the collection.

Plombières is nicknamed the "city of a thousand balconies": wrought iron, molded cast iron or wooden balconies adorn 19th-century façades. A marked circuit leads from alley to stairway through these architectural treasures.

→

Climb to the Jardins en Terrasses, the town's "1001st balcony." Pools, sculptures and rare species punctuate the ascent to the panorama over the Augronne valley.

→

Stroll through the Parc Impérial, a green haven laid out during the Second Empire. The Stanislas Fountain, adorned with baroque motifs, marks the park entrance.

→

Taste the Plombières ice cream, a dessert born during the 1858 Interview: a blend of candied fruit and cream that became a local pride.

→

Follow the Coteau de la Vierge trail for a viewpoint overlooking the spa town and the rooftops of old Plombières.

Saint-Mammès Cathedral (1150-1196) combines the last Burgundian Romanesque influences with the first Gothic practices. Measuring 94 meters long, it rises 23 meters under the vault. Two Renaissance tapestries illustrate the legend of Saint Mammès.

Climb the 227 steps of the cathedral's south tower. At the summit, the view embraces the rooftops of the old town, the Langres plateau and distant forests.

Enter the Sainte-Croix chapel (1549), a Renaissance masterpiece: sculpted coffered vault, altar and floor in polychrome Rouen faïence.

The Maison des Lumières traces the universe of the 18th century and displays an original copy of the Encyclopédie. The philosopher Diderot was born here in 1713.

The Art and History Museum occupies the former Jesuit convent. Gallo-Roman collections, paintings and art objects cover twenty centuries of local history.

Admire the Porte des Moulins, a monumental entrance pierced in the ramparts in 1647. Its two round towers frame a vaulted passage adorned with rustication.

→

Walk the sentry path along the ramparts: 3.5 km of curtain walls punctuated by 12 towers and 7 gates. Viewpoints open onto the plateau, Lake Liez and distant mountain ranges.

→

Follow the golden studs of the "Pas à Pas" circuit on the ground: they guide through medieval and Renaissance alleys of the old town to the main monuments.

→

Visit the Tour de Navarre, a 16th-century cannon tower with a unique helical ramp inside France, designed to raise artillery pieces.

The Château du Grand Jardin is a pleasure pavilion built between 1533 and 1546 by the first Duke of Guise. The Renaissance façades rank among the most finely sculpted in the region. The Saint-Claude chapel preserves two caryatids rescued from the Guise tomb.

Enter the Renaissance-style garden classified as a Remarkable Garden: geometric parterres, verdant labyrinth, large orchard and a national collection of 500 boxwood specimens.

Notre-Dame church presents a remarkable Renaissance portal. Around it, half-timbered houses and private mansions recall that the Guise made Joinville the capital of their principality.

The Sainte-Croix chapel, in the cemetery, offers flamboyant Gothic style and magnificent stained glass from the early Troyes school.

The Auditoire de Justice testifies to the Renaissance momentum brought by the Guise. Its architecture expresses the judicial and political power of the lords of Joinville.

→

Take the "Joinville, medieval city" circuit: from the site of the château d'en Haut, vanished stronghold of the Guise on the heights, down to the quai des Peceaux where the Marne flows.

The Centre Culturel Renoir, on the town hall square, opens the tour. The permanent exhibition traces the life of the painter and his three sons: an actor, a filmmaker and a ceramist. A film completes the visit.

On rue de l'Extra, step through the door of the painter's studio. Easels, brushes and the wheelchair he used late in life remain in place. Upstairs, a rare film shows the artist at work.

The family house, acquired in 1896, was the painter's first property. Rooms restored in period style and a garden promenade planted with fruit trees extend the immersion into family intimacy.

The Saint-Rémi church, 19th-century neo-Gothic, presents a Latin cross nave, stained glass and an organ donated by a family of local philanthropists.

In the village cemetery rest the painter, his wife and their three sons. The choice of this final resting place speaks volumes about the family's attachment to Essoyes.

The 13th-century press, a listed Historic Monument, is one of the oldest in Europe. Made of solid oak without metal parts, it weighs 30 tons and could crush 3,000 kg of grapes. It remained in operation until the late 19th century.

Saint-Rouin church, rebuilt in 1880 in neo-Gothic style, houses the relics of Saint-Rouin and Saint-Maurice in two reliquaries on either side of the transept.

Reach the orientation table at the summit of the promontory. The view embraces the Meuse valley, the Argonne forests and the wind turbines that dot the horizon.

The half-timbered houses of this street-village testify to Argonne's architectural identity. Some are built with cut stone recovered during the demolition of the abbey in 1790.

In the forest, the Saint-Rouin chapel (1954) surprises: cubic concrete blocks on stilts, inspired by Le Corbusier. Colored stained glass by a Japanese artist softens the geometric rigor. An 18th-century Assumption altarpiece, the only vestige of the abbey church, stands in the open air nearby.

Take the Abbey loop starting from the village. The marked trail crosses the state forest to the Saint-Rouin hermitage and its miraculous fountain.

Saint-Étienne church, flamboyant Gothic with Renaissance façade (1513), houses Ligier Richier's Transi: a standing skeleton holding his heart toward the sky. This 16th-century funerary sculpture, listed as a Historic Monument, is one of the most striking of the French Renaissance.

Climb Place Saint-Pierre, nerve center of the Upper Town. 16th-century private mansions align blonde stone façades, sculpted pediments, fluted pilasters and gargoyles. The ensemble forms a Renaissance quarter of rare unity in Eastern France.

The Collège Gilles de Trèves (1573-1575) presents an interior courtyard with two galleries on fluted pilasters, of Flemish influence. Built to compete with Protestant universities, it testifies to 16th-century religious tensions.

The Musée Barrois occupies the former Château-Neuf of the Dukes of Bar. Archaeological collections from the Nasium site, Lorraine paintings and sculptures from the 14th to 16th centuries compose the tour.

The Tour de l'Horloge, last vestige of the ramparts, has borne a clock face since 1381. Take the 183-step stairway descending to the lower town for a panorama over the Ornain valley.

Push open the door at no. 73, rue des Ducs de Bar: an imposing 15th-century seigneurial press, a listed Historic Monument, recalls the wine tradition of Barrois and the production of Pineau de Bar.

→

Follow the marked heritage circuit of the Upper Town. Explanatory panels line the Renaissance alleys, from the castle esplanade to the viewpoints on rue des Grangettes.

→

Descend to the lower town to taste the redcurrant jam seeded with a goose quill, an artisanal specialty of Bar-le-Duc perpetuated since the Middle Ages.

→

Walk along the banks of the Ornain below the Upper Town. The contrast between Renaissance façades above and the watercourse below offers a pleasant promenade setting.

The citadel of Montmédy dominates the town from a rocky promontory. Erected in 1545 under Charles V to defend the Spanish Netherlands, it was conquered by French troops in 1657 then remodeled by Vauban. More than 1.5 km of walls encircle the upper town.

The Jules Bastien-Lepage museum, in the citadel's former school, presents drawings, paintings and objects of this naturalist painter from Meuse, internationally recognized for his rural life scenes.

The Fortification Museum traces the evolution of Meuse defensive systems, from Prehistory to the 18th century. Models and plans explain the successive works of Charles V, Vauban and Séré de Rivières.

Saint-Martin church, at the heart of the citadel, blends Romanesque and Gothic influences. Renovated, it occasionally hosts concerts and exhibitions under its restored vaults.

Montmédy was the destination chosen by the king during his flight from Paris in 1791: the garrison had remained loyal to him. A piece of history to ponder while walking through the casemates.

→

Follow the marked circuit of the ramparts. Bastions, underground passages, covered way and casemates succeed one another. The viewpoints offer a panorama over the Meuse countryside and the Chiers valley.

→

The Upper Town houses artists' studios installed in former casemates. Wander between small squares and winding alleys that thread through the heart of the stronghold.

→

Push on to the basilica of Avioth, 15 km away: a Gothic masterpiece nestled in a tiny village, it surprises with its dimensions and its "Recevresse," a unique outdoor baldachin.

Joan of Arc's birthplace, a listed Historic Monument and Maison des Illustres, presents four rooms including the girl's bedroom. On the façade, a sculpted tympanum with 15th-century coat of arms remains.

The "Visages de Jehanne" center extends the visit: permanent exhibition, audiovisual scenography, "Chamber of Kings" reconstructing the small theater of the Hundred Years' War. A film traces Joan's childhood, her epic and her trials.

Climb the Bois-Chenu hillside. The Basilica of Sainte-Jeanne d'Arc (1881-1926) stands where Joan is said to have heard her voices. Stained glass from Chartres workshops, gilded mosaics and a cycle of paintings trace her life.

Begin with the crypt Notre-Dame des Armées, opened in 1891. The Stairway of Peace, adorned with coats of arms from cities marked by the epic, leads to the upper church.

The parish church of Saint-Rémy, Joan's baptismal site, preserves baptismal fonts and a 15th-century statue of Saint Margaret. Modern stained glass completes the Johannine narrative.

→

Follow the "In Joan's Footsteps" themed trail between the village and the basilica. The path crosses meadows and woods above the Meuse valley.

→

From the basilica forecourt, the panorama embraces the Meuse valley and wooded hills. Statues of Joan and her parents punctuate the esplanade.

→

The second Sunday in May, the Joan of Arc festival animates the village: procession, wreath-laying and historical performance. In June-July, a sound and light show takes place on the basilica forecourt.

The priory church (1722) surprises with its three onion-domed bell towers that draw a baroque silhouette in the hollow of the Vosges hills. Inside, the Silbermann organ of 1746, a listed Historic Monument, is the only authentic survivor of Jean-André Silbermann's work.

The Haute Chapelle, a 12th-century Romanesque construction, overlooks the village from a wooded relief. According to legend, the mule transporting Saint Quirin's relics from Rome stopped here in 1049 and refused to go further.

Walk around the priory to find the miraculous fountain below. For centuries, it has been attributed with healing powers for skin diseases.

The archaeological site of Croix Guillaume, accessible by a marked forest trail, reveals a Gallo-Roman hamlet: traces of dwellings, necropolis, sandstone quarries and cult remains.

In Lettenbach, the Verriers chapel (1756), rococo style, sports an onion-domed bell tower and folk art stained glass. The monks had established a glassworks here in the 15th century that became a Royal Manufacture under Louis XV.

→

Take the Club Vosgien marked trails around ponds and deciduous forests. The village counts 75 listed or registered buildings, from Lorraine farms to wayside crosses.

→

Seven churches and chapels scattered around the village form the "7 Roses" circuit. Each tells its story, between Christianized Celtic sanctuaries and forest hermitages.

→

In summer, the Silbermann Musical Days offer organ recitals in the priory church: a rare moment in an exceptional setting.

The abbey church Saints-Pierre-et-Paul, founded around 660, is the second largest Gothic edifice in Alsace after Strasbourg Cathedral. Its Romanesque belfry from the 11th century is the oldest part. The Dubois baroque organ (1766), the largest in Alsace, was restored in 2012.

The adjoining Gothic cloister houses a reproduction of the "Christ of Wissembourg," considered the world's oldest figurative stained glass (the original is at the Musée de l'OEuvre Notre-Dame in Strasbourg).

The Maison du Sel (1448), former bourgeois hospital, impresses with its proportions and atypical roof. Extensively restored in the 18th century, it's one of the town's most remarkable civic buildings.

Reach the Bruch quarter, former marshland. The Maison de l'Ami Fritz (16th century) mirrors itself in the Lauter, its richly carved half-timbering framing mullioned windows.

The Musée Westercamp traces local archaeology and border history (1870). It houses an exceptional collection of popular imagery from the Wentzel manufactory.

The Hôtel Stanislas housed the exiled King of Poland from 1719 to 1725. Its sober façade conceals an interior that was long that of a sovereign in residence.

→

Walk the tour of the ramparts: abbey defensive walls, imposing Schartenturm tower, urban enclosure and north moat. A fitness trail with 13 stations completes the loop.

→

Stroll along the Lauter quays, the river that flows through the old town. Half-timbered houses reflect in the water, forming Wissembourg's "Little Venice."

→

Wissembourg is the gateway to the Northern Vosges Regional Nature Park, a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve. Hiking trails and mountain bike paths leave directly from town toward the Mundat forest.

Saints-Pierre-et-Paul church (12th century) is one of the finest Romanesque edifices in Alsace. Its frieze of human and animal figures runs along the external cornice. Inside, sculpted capitals blend foliage, masks and fantastical motifs.

The Maison romane (circa 1152), on rue du Général-de-Gaulle, is considered one of the oldest stone civic constructions in Alsace. Its façade preserves Romanesque arcading and twin windows.

Four medieval gates survive in the ramparts: Porte du Lion, Porte de l'École, Porte Basse and Porte de la Vierge. Each retains sculpted door crowns, a unique vestige of Rosheim's medieval décor.

The six-bucket well on Place de la Mairie, and the 16th and 17th-century half-timbered houses complete a dense and homogeneous intra-muros heritage.

Follow the marked heritage route: panels explain the history of this former free imperial city, fortified as early as the 13th century.

→

The Alsace Vineyard Green Way leaves from Rosheim on a disused railway line. By bike or on foot, it crosses vineyards and orchards to Saint-Nabor.

Obernai's Market Square is one of the most photogenic in Alsace. Colorful half-timbered houses, geranium-filled windows and winstub terraces crowd around the double-gabled Halle aux Blés.

The Kapellturm belfry, a 60-meter tower crowned with a Gothic spire, served both as bell tower of the Chapel of the Virgin and as watchtower. Its astronomical dial indicated lunar phases.

The six-bucket well (1579), on the Market Square, is adorned with Renaissance sculptures representing Obernai's coat of arms and allegorical figures.

The neo-Gothic Saints-Pierre-et-Paul church (19th century) houses remarkable stained glass and a baroque-style altar. Its openwork spire bell tower dominates the town's silhouette.

Walk the ramparts: the double medieval enclosure preserves several towers including the Storks' Tower, where the wading birds come to nest each spring.

→

Mittelbergheim, classified as one of France's Most Beautiful Villages, aligns Renaissance winemakers' houses in pink sandstone along its main street, dating from the 16th and 17th centuries. Sculpted carriage gates display construction dates and family names.

The former communal press, on the main street, testifies to the village's ancestral wine activity. Engraved lintels and mullioned windows recall the Renaissance prosperity of the winemakers.

The Renaissance town hall, with its overhanging oriel and sculptures, is one of the village's most photographed buildings.

The village backs onto Grand Cru Zotzenberg, the only Grand Cru in Alsace to authorize the Sylvaner grape in addition to Riesling and Gewurztraminer.

Châtenois's fortified cemetery, rare in Alsace, preserves its medieval enclosure pierced with loopholes. At the center, the 12th-century Saint-Georges church presents a squat Romanesque bell tower and a porch with sculpted décor.

The Tour des Sorcières (13th century), former rampart keep, owes its name to the witchcraft trials held in the region. Its massive silhouette dominates the old town.

The partially preserved ramparts and medieval gates frame an ancient core of half-timbered houses. The village is a southern gateway to the Alsace Wine Route.

A few kilometers away, Sélestat houses the Humanist Library, registered in UNESCO's Memory of the World registry, and the Romanesque Sainte-Foy church (12th century).

Bergheim's fortified enclosure is one of the best preserved in Alsace: the 14th-century double enclosure still circles the town over almost its entire perimeter. The Porte Haute (1300), flanked by its tower, marks the main entrance.

The Gothic Notre-Dame-de-l'Assomption church, built in the 14th-15th centuries, contains medieval wall frescoes and rich liturgical furnishings.

The three-hundred-year-old linden tree, on the Market Square, shades half-timbered houses and fountains. Painted façades and sculpted oriels compose a preserved setting.

The Maison des Sorcières traces the history of witchcraft trials that struck the village between 1582 and 1683: forty women were condemned. The exhibition blends archival documents and immersive scenography.

The intra-muros alleys conceal 16th and 17th-century half-timbered houses, with interior courtyards, wells and spiral staircases typical of the Alsatian vineyard.

The fortified church of Saint-Jacques-le-Majeur (15th-16th century) stands in the middle of the vines, surrounded by its cemetery encircled by a hexagonal crenellated wall. This enclosure served as refuge for villagers in times of danger. The building, simultaneously Catholic and Protestant, is a rare case of simultaneum.

Half-timbered winemakers' houses line the main street, adorned with dated carriage gates and sculpted signs. The village, classified as one of France's Most Beautiful Villages, has barely 600 inhabitants.

The village backs onto Grand Cru Rosacker, a terroir of muscats and rieslings renowned for their minerality. The vines descend in gentle slopes toward the plain.

The 18th-century washhouse, fed by a spring, and Renaissance fountains punctuate the stroll through flower-filled alleys.

Push through the Porte Haute and climb rue du Général-de-Gaulle, Riquewihr's single artery. The 16th and 17th-century half-timbered houses, painted yellow, blue or red, display oriels, wrought-iron signs and interior courtyards with wooden galleries.

The Tour Dolder (1291), a 25-meter-high sandstone belfry, guarded the town's main entrance. It houses a small local history museum with models and old engravings.

The Tour des Voleurs, corner tower of the ramparts, preserves a torture chamber and medieval dungeon. The exhibition traces judicial practices of the period.

The Maison Liebrich (1535), with its half-timbered courtyard, Renaissance well and old press, is one of the village's most complete architectural ensembles.

The ramparts, nearly intact around the entire perimeter, enclose an urban fabric frozen since the 16th century. Riquewihr suffered no major destruction during wars.

Kaysersberg's fortified bridge, spanning the Weiss, is one of the rare defensive bridges surviving in Alsace. Its central chapel and crenellated parapets compose an emblematic image of the village.

Sainte-Croix church (12th-15th century) houses a sculpted and polychrome wooden altarpiece from 1518, representing the Passion of Christ. The Romanesque tympanum of the portal depicts the coronation of the Virgin.

Voted "France's Favorite Village" in 2017, Kaysersberg aligns polychrome half-timbered houses, sculpted oriels and galleried courtyards along rue du Général-de-Gaulle. Wrought-iron signs signal former guilds.

The communal museum, in a Renaissance house, presents medieval religious art objects and a 15th-century polychrome wood Christ of Pity.

The village is linked to a historical figure born here in 1875, who received the Nobel Peace Prize in 1952 for his humanitarian work in Africa.

→

Climb to Kaysersberg castle (13th century) via the winding trail. The cylindrical keep, rare in Alsace, offers a 360° panorama over the vines, the Weiss valley and the Vosges crests.

Kaysersberg winemakers cultivate the Grand Crus Schlossberg (first Grand Cru classified in 1975) and Kaefferkopf. Taste the chiseled Rieslings in town center cellars.

→

In December, Kaysersberg's authentic Christmas market is held around the fortified bridge: local crafts, bredele and mulled wine in an intimate atmosphere.

Eguisheim nestles in concentric rings around its 13th-century octagonal castle. The circular plan, unique in Alsace, reveals itself with every step: curved alleys draw perfect circles around the central keep.

On the central square, the Renaissance fountain bears the statue of Pope Leo IX, born here in 1002. This son of Eguisheim became one of the most important reforming popes of the 11th century.

The chapel of Saint-Léon IX, inside the castle, preserves a 12th-century Romanesque portal and ancient frescoes. 14th-century opening Virgin in the treasury.

The half-timbered façades of the circular streets vie for floral displays: geraniums, petunias and Virginia creeper cover the walls. Eguisheim was voted "France's Favorite Village" in 2013.

Saints-Pierre-et-Paul church houses a polychrome Romanesque tympanum depicting Christ between Saint Peter and Saint Paul, and a Virgin and Child called the Opening Virgin.
