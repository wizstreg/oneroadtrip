Nyhavn, the canal dug between 1671 and 1673 by Swedish prisoners of war on orders from King Christian V, linking the sea to Copenhagen's city center. The oldest house, number 9, dates from 1681. Hans Christian Andersen lived successively at numbers 20, 67, and 18 - it was at number 20 that he wrote his first fairy tales, including The Tinderbox. Arrive before 9am to have the quay to yourself.

Rosenborg Castle, built between 1606 and 1633 by Christian IV as a summer residence - the king loved this palace so much that when he was dying in 1648, they transported him by sledge from Frederiksborg so he could breathe his last here. The Dutch Renaissance red-brick building has housed the royal treasury since 1838. In the cellars, the Danish Crown Jewels: four sets of diamonds, rubies, pearls, and emeralds. The Knights' Hall guards three life-size silver lions. A curiosity: beneath the Winter Chamber, acoustic conduits allowed hidden musicians to make their music resonate above - guests believed they were hearing music appear from nowhere.

Christiansborg, the parliament palace on Slotsholmen island, has burned down twice: in 1794 during a court ball (candles set fire to the decorations), then in 1884 during construction work. The current building, in grey granite, dates from 1928. The tower (106 meters) offers the best panoramic view over Copenhagen - palaces, copper roofs, and harbor.

→

Canal tour departing from Nyhavn - one hour cruising past the Opera House, Christianshavn, and the Royal Library, nicknamed the 'Black Diamond' for its polished black granite facade.

Tivoli Gardens, opened on August 15, 1843. Georg Carstensen convinced King Christian VIII to grant the license by telling him: 'When the people are amusing themselves, they do not think about politics.' Tivoli is the world's second-oldest amusement park - the first, Dyrehavsbakken, lies 12 km north. Walt Disney drew direct inspiration from it when creating Disneyland. The wooden Rutschebanen roller coaster, built in 1914, operates with a brakeman on board for each descent - one of only seven remaining of this type worldwide.

The National Museum of Denmark (Nationalmuseet), housed in the former Crown Prince's Palace. The collection spans 14,000 years of history. Don't miss: the Trundholm sun chariot (circa 1400 BC), a bronze wheel bearing a gold disc representing the sun - one of the most fascinating objects from the European Bronze Age - and the lurs, bronze Viking trumpets.

The Little Mermaid (Den Lille Havfrue) on Langelinie pier, a 1.25-meter bronze sculpture perched on a rock since 1913. She's been beheaded twice (1964 and 1998) and had her right arm torn off in 1984. Continue to Amalienborg Palace - the changing of the guard takes place at noon.

→

Stroll through the Nørrebro and Vesterbro neighborhoods - the real living quarters of Copenhagen locals, with independent cafés, street markets, and local designer shops.

Frederiksborg Castle in Hillerød, built between 1602 and 1620 by Christian IV - the largest Renaissance castle in Scandinavia, set on three islets in the middle of a lake. A fire in 1859 destroyed the interiors, but brewer J.C. Jacobsen (founder of Carlsberg) financed the restoration. Since 1878, it has housed the Museum of National History: 500 years of royal portraits, tapestries, and furniture. The chapel from 1617 survived the fire and remains the coronation site for Danish monarchs.

Frederiksborg's Baroque Garden, created in the 1720s and restored identically in 1996, descends in four terraces toward the lake with a central cascade. Clipped hedges form the monograms of four sovereigns in boxwood. An optical illusion: the pathways narrow as they descend to make the garden appear larger than it is.

Kronborg Castle in Helsingør, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2000. At the narrowest point of the Øresund - 4 km between Denmark and Sweden - Danish kings collected the 'Sound Dues' from 1420, a tax imposed on all ships entering the Baltic Sea. Frederick II transformed it into a Renaissance castle between 1574 and 1585. A fire in 1629 destroyed everything except the chapel; Christian IV rebuilt it. In 1658, the Swedes occupied and plundered the castle. Shakespeare probably never visited Kronborg, but accounts of the extravagant feasts held there - the Great Hall at 62 meters is the longest in Northern Europe - traveled across Europe and inspired Hamlet. In the casemates, a statue depicts Holger the Dane, the legendary knight said to awaken if Denmark is threatened.

→

Walk the ramparts of Kronborg with views across the strait to the Swedish coast. In summer, Shakespeare festival performances take place outdoors in front of the castle.

Roskilde Cathedral, inscribed as a UNESCO site since 1995. Its brick construction began around 1170 - Scandinavia's first Gothic brick cathedral, an unprecedented technical challenge at the time. Its vaults reach 25 meters. Forty Danish kings and queens are buried here, including Margaret I who unified the three Scandinavian kingdoms in the 14th century. Queen Margrethe II's tomb is already in place.

The Viking Ship Museum, facing the fjord. The five Skuldelev ships had been intentionally sunk around 1070 to block the fjord channel and protect Roskilde from seaborne attack. Excavated in 1962, they represent five different types: warships, trade vessels, fishing boats, and cargo carriers. In 1997, excavations to expand the shipyard uncovered nine additional medieval ships, including Roskilde 6 - at 37 meters, the longest Viking ship ever discovered.

→

Rowing trip on a reconstructed Viking ship from the museum harbor (May through late September). Tickets sold on-site in the morning - limited spaces available.

Hans Christian Andersen's birthplace, in the small yellow house on Hans Jensens Stræde where he was born in 1805. The new immersive museum opened in 2021, designed by Japanese firm Kengo Kuma, recreates the universe of the fairy tales through light installations and labyrinthine spaces built around The Little Mermaid, The Snow Queen, and The Ugly Duckling.

St. Canute's Cathedral in Odense (Sankt Knuds Kirke), built between the 13th and 15th centuries. In the crypt rest the remains of King Canute IV, assassinated in 1086 in St. Alban's Church by peasants revolting against his taxes - he was canonized in 1101. The gilded wooden altarpiece from 1521 is one of the finest examples of late Gothic sculpture in Scandinavia.

Den Fynske Landsby, the open-air museum on the edge of Odense. Some twenty authentic 18th-19th century buildings have been relocated here from the Funen region: farms, windmill, forge. In summer, craft demonstrations bring the site to life.

→

Walk through Odense's pedestrian center following the footprints drawn on the cobblestones, which lead to addresses connected to Andersen's life.

ARoS, the Aarhus Art Museum, inaugurated in its current cubic building in 2004. Architect Schmidt Hammer Lassen designed the interior around Dante's Divine Comedy: in the basement the nine circles of Hell, then the ascent through Purgatory across the floors, and at the summit Paradise. This Paradise takes the form of 'Your Rainbow Panorama', Olafur Eliasson's circular walkway opened in May 2011: 150 meters long, 3 meters wide, set on columns 3.5 meters above the roof, with a 52-meter diameter. The glass panels cover the entire visible spectrum - walking the full circle, you see Aarhus in every color of the rainbow. The work cost 60 million kroner, financed by the Realdania foundation.

Den Gamle By, the open-air museum founded in 1909, recreates a Danish market town across three eras: 1864 (half-timbered houses, horse-drawn carts), 1927 (cobbled streets, Art Nouveau shop fronts), and 1974 (apartments with Tandberg televisions and Ikea furniture). More than 75 historic buildings have been relocated here from across Scandinavia. Michelin awards it three stars.

→

Walk along Aarhus's waterfront to Dokk1, Scandinavia's largest library, opened in 2015. The white concrete and glass building is part of the industrial harbor's regeneration. On the outdoor deck, the Kloden (Globe) playground is open to all.

Moesgaard Museum, 10 km south of Aarhus, houses Denmark's most remarkable prehistoric collection. The centerpiece is Grauballe Man, a body discovered in a peat bog in 1952, who died around 290 BC - his face, fingernails, and fingerprint ridges are still visible after more than 2,300 years preserved in the bog's acid. The building itself, inaugurated in 2014, is built into a hillside: its gently sloping roof is accessible and extends into the landscape.

Aarhus Cathedral (Aarhus Domkirke), Denmark's longest church with its 93-meter nave. Built in the 12th-15th centuries, it preserves an exceptional set of medieval frescoes on its vaults, revealed beneath whitewash during the Reformation. The altarpiece from 1479, painted by Bernt Notke of Lübeck, is one of the most important in Scandinavia.

→

Salling Rooftop terrace, atop the Salling department store, for coffee with 360° views over Aarhus's rooftops and the bay.

Lindholm Høje, just north of Aalborg - Scandinavia's largest Viking cemetery, with 682 graves dug between 400 and 1000 AD. Stones arranged in the shape of a ship's hull mark male burials; rounder forms indicate women's graves. Sand drifts covered the site in the 11th century and preserved it intact until excavations in 1952-1958. The adjacent museum displays two superimposed Viking villages - one from the 8th century, the other from the 11th - and skeletons discovered on site. Arab silver coins testify to the trading network that connected Aalborg to the wider world.

Aalborghus Castle, a Renaissance fortress built between 1539 and 1555 under Christian III, on the edge of the Limfjord. One of the few medieval fortresses still standing in Denmark, with its moats and underground dungeons visible on guided tours.

Kunsten Museum of Modern Art Aalborg, built in 1972 according to plans by Alvar Aalto and Jean-Jacques Baruël. This is one of the great Finnish architect's rare Danish realizations - a white marble building with light carefully directed through roof wells.

→

Walk through the cobbled streets of Aalborg's historic center, among 16th-17th century half-timbered houses, including Jens Bang's House (1624) - the largest private Renaissance merchant house in Denmark.

Skagens Museum, founded in 1908 in the dining room of Brøndums Hotel - the inn where the Skagen Painters had gathered since the 1870s. This historic room, with its portraits on the walls, is preserved unchanged. The artists' colony, led by P.S. Krøyer, Anna and Michael Ancher, Laurits Tuxen, and Swedish and Norwegian painters, came each summer to Skagen to paint en plein air in the particular light of northern Jutland - the famous 'blue hour' of evening when sea and sky merge. Krøyer created his celebrated painting 'Summer Evening on Skagen's Southern Beach' (1899) here. The museum now holds over 1,900 works.

Grenen point, 3 km north of Skagen - the exact spot where the North Sea (Skagerrak) and Baltic Sea (Kattegat) meet. You can see the two currents colliding in opposing waves. No ship can round the point: the combination of shifting sands and opposing currents makes it dangerous to approach.

Den Tilsandede Kirke, the sand-buried church, built in the 14th century - the region's largest at the time. Dunes began invading it in the 17th century despite attempts to clear them. In 1795, the church was closed and its precious possessions evacuated. Today only the tower emerges from the dunes.

→

Tractor ride (Sandormen) from Grenen center to the point - the vehicle drives along the sandy beach to reach Denmark's northernmost tip.

Hanstholm Fortress, Northern Europe's largest World War II fortification. On the night of April 9, 1940 - the day Denmark was occupied - the Germans laid mines in the Skagerrak. The main battery comprised four 38 cm guns (110 tons each, identical to those on the Bismarck) capable of firing 55 km, hermetically sealing access to the Baltic Sea. The complex counted up to 450 bunkers. The Bunkermuseum is installed in one of the giant 2,500 m² bunkers - the quarters of the 90 soldiers who lived there are restored to original condition. In summer, a narrow-gauge ammunition train runs through the bunkers.

Thy National Park, Denmark's first national park (2008), extends around Hanstholm - shifting dunes, heather moorland, wild beaches, and stone pine plantations. The bunkers blend into the dune landscape, creating a unique atmosphere between military history and preserved nature.

→

Hike through the dunes of Thy National Park along the North Sea coast - marked trails, coastal wildlife (sea otters, roe deer, migratory birds).

Ribe, Scandinavia's oldest town, founded around 710 as a trading market between Frisians and Scandinavians. The cathedral, whose construction in Cologne tufa stone (stone was locally scarce) began in 1110, is Denmark's best-preserved Romanesque monument - the only five-naved one. The Citizens' Tower (Borgertårnet), from the 14th century, served as the town's watchtower and in 1644 they hoisted cannons up to fire on Swedish ships. Climb the 248 steps to see the Wadden Sea marshes on the horizon. The tomb chamber of King Christopher I, buried here in 1259, contains Northern Europe's oldest royal sarcophagus.

Ribe VikingeCenter, 3 km south of town - a full-scale reconstruction of a 700-1000 AD Viking village on 12 hectares, with costumed actors, longhouses, a blacksmith's workshop, and craft demonstrations. One of Europe's finest living Viking museums.

Ribe's medieval center: cobbled streets, 16th-17th century half-timbered houses (a major fire in 1580 destroyed earlier buildings). The old town hall, built before 1496 and used as city hall until 2007, is one of Denmark's oldest.

→

Birdwatching in Wadden Sea National Park, a UNESCO site. The area is one of the world's largest tidal flats and a major stopover for millions of migratory birds each autumn.

Vejle

Vejle's Fjordenhus, inaugurated in 2018, is one of Olafur Eliasson's rare architectural constructions - a 4-story office building in concrete and steel, partially built in the waters of Vejle Fjord. Its curved facades, pierced with circular portholes of different sizes, evoke basalt formations. Unlike typical usage, the building is accessible on foot and by boat - public spaces have been arranged around the structure to allow Vejle residents to reclaim the fjord.

Vejle Church (Skt. Nicolai Kirke), built in the 13th century. In a glass sarcophagus preserved in the church, you can see the mummy of the 'Haraldskær Woman', a Viking queen discovered in a peat bog in 1835, who died around 490 AD - one of Denmark's best-preserved bog bodies.

→

Walk along Vejle Fjord, between Fjordenhus and the marina - view of Eliasson's building from the water.

❓ Frequently asked questions

What weather should you expect?

Summer (June-August): Warmest weather, longest daylight hours, peak tourist season, highest prices, perfect for cycling and outdoor activities.

Spring (April-May): Pleasant temperatures, blooming flowers, fewer crowds, moderate prices, excellent for sightseeing and cultural activities.

Fall (September-October): Comfortable weather, autumn colors, fewer tourists, good value, cozy indoor attractions, harvest season.

Winter (November-March): Cold and dark, Christmas markets, hygge season, lowest prices, indoor cultural experiences, northern lights possible.

We believe you will enjoy this itinerary best by dedicating between 10 and 16 days. The next step will allow you to adjust the duration of your stay.

What to discover in Denmark?

Hygge lifestyle with stunning coastlines, Viking heritage, and modern design excellence. Experience Copenhagen's cultural scene, fairy-tale castles, and sustainable living practices. The country offers excellent quality of life with beautiful architecture and progressive values. Denmark provides Scandinavian charm with cultural sophistication and environmental leadership.
