From the wild promontory of Gargano to the ancient sassi of Matera, from the trulli of Alberobello to the turquoise beaches of Salento, this grand loop from Bari crosses Puglia in all its diversity. Eighteen days to connect oak forests and Adriatic cliffs, Lecce baroque and Tremiti islands, whitewashed towns and the lanes of Gallipoli.

Key highlights (5)
  • Gargano: wild peninsula with cliffs, forests, and the Tremiti Islands
  • Alberobello: over 1,500 UNESCO-listed trulli since 1996
  • Matera: troglodyte sassi listed by UNESCO, continuously inhabited since the Neolithic
  • Lecce: capital of Apulian baroque, nicknamed the Florence of the South
  • Gallipoli: baroque limestone island on the Ionian Sea, the Mediterranean's leading olive oil exporter in the 18th century

Vieste historic center (Vieste Vecchia): perched on the eastern tip of the Gargano promontory, this labyrinth of whitewashed lanes sprawls across two rocky headlands. The Cathedral of Santa Maria Assunta, episcopal seat from 918 to 1817, dates to the 11th century. Inside stands a wooden statue of the Virgin of Merino, object of local pilgrimage for centuries.

Pizzomunno monolith and Castello Beach: this 25-meter limestone sea stack rises directly from the sand at the town's edge. Local legend tells of a lover turned to stone when sirens dragged his beloved into the sea. The nearby Swabian castle, built by Frederick II then rebuilt by the Spanish in 1537, commands the promontory from its triangular perch.

→

Sunset walk along Lungomare Enrico Mattei in Vieste: this seaside boulevard runs between the town's two beach zones. Watch fishermen haul in their nets while the trabucchi — ancient wooden fishing machines on stilts, now protected heritage of Gargano National Park — cast their silhouettes across the water.

Baia delle Zagare and Gargano north coast: between Vieste and Mattinata, the coastal road traces white limestone cliffs plunging into the Adriatic. Baia delle Zagare (Bay of Orange Blossoms) is framed by two faraglioni rising straight from the sea. Spiaggia di Vignanotica, reached via a steep path from a clifftop parking area, offers white pebbles and turquoise water hemmed by towering cliffs riddled with caves.

Peschici historic center: ranked among Italy's most beautiful villages, this medieval town clings to a cliff above the sea, its whitewashed houses topped with Arab-influenced flat roofs. A small harbor at the cliff's base serves as departure point for excursions to surrounding coves.

→

Boat excursion from Vieste to the sea caves: the coast between Vieste and Mattinata hides dozens of limestone caves accessible only by water — Grotta Sfondata, Grotta dei Due Occhi, Grotta delle Campane. San Nicola Island — Abbey of Santa Maria a Mare: San Nicola concentrates the entire history of the archipelago. The Benedictine abbey, founded in the 9th century and rebuilt many times since, has crowned this rock for a thousand years. Inside, a mosaic floor and a Byzantine-era wooden cross bear witness to its long past. The island served as a place of exile since Roman times — Emperor Augustus banished his granddaughter Julia here for adultery, where she died after twenty years of confinement.

San Domino Island — pine forest and coves: largest of the five islands, San Domino is blanketed in Aleppo pine that descends to the sea. Three marked trails cross the island end to end. The archipelago's only sandy beach lies here: Cala delle Arene. Cars are forbidden — only residents may drive.

→

Ferry crossing Vieste → Tremiti (about 2 hours) and boat tour of the archipelago: from San Domino, boat excursions circle the five islands along limestone cliffs, faraglioni, and sea caves — Grotta del Bue Marino, Grotta delle Viole, Grotta delle Rondinelle. Waters here have been a protected marine reserve since 1989.

4
Day

Trani

Foresta Umbra: at the heart of the Gargano promontory, this 10,000-hectare forest is Puglia's only large broadleaf woodland. Beech, oak, yew, and pine cover a territory that was once an island joined to the peninsula by alluvial deposits. The visitor center offers an introduction to local fauna — roe deer, foxes, buzzards.

Trani — Cathedral of San Nicola Pellegrino: built from 1099 in response to the canonization of a young Greek pilgrim who died in Trani, this is one of the finest examples of Apulian Romanesque. Trani stone, a pinkish limestone tufa quarried locally, gives it a unique color that shifts with the hour. The 59-meter bell tower and double flight of stairs leading to the central portal make the first impression. The bronze door, a masterpiece by Barisano da Trani completed in 1175, is preserved inside — a faithful replica stands in the facade. The construction was consecrated in 1143 before even being finished.

Trani — Swabian castle and seafront: built by Frederick II of Swabia in 1233 on the waterfront, this square-plan castle with corner towers faces the cathedral from the same esplanade. The two buildings sum up the medieval duality of the city: spiritual power versus temporal power, stone against stone.

Trani — old port and Jewish quarter: Trani was one of the most active commercial ports on the Adriatic during the Middle Ages. Its Jewish quarter, among the best preserved in southern Italy, houses the Scolanova Synagogue from the 12th century, converted to a church then returned to the Jewish community in 1993.

Monte Sant'Angelo — Sanctuary of the Archangel Michael: carved into the rock of Mount Gargano at 796 meters elevation, this sanctuary is one of the oldest Christian pilgrimage sites in the West. According to tradition, the Archangel Michael appeared here to the Bishop of Sipontum at the end of the 5th century. Pope Gelasius I formalized the cult. The Lombards made it their dynastic sanctuary — King Rothari (died 652) is buried here. Listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site with other Lombard sites in 2011.

Manfredonia — Swabian-Angevin castle and archaeological museum: founded by Manfred of Sicily in the 13th century, the city takes its name from its founder. The castle holds the Daunian stelae in its collections — sculpted funerary slabs dating from the 8th to 6th century BC, among the oldest figurative testimonies of Puglia.

→

Scenic descent from Gargano to the plain: the road from Monte Sant'Angelo to Manfredonia offers views across the eponymous gulf and the distant Murge. This crossing marks the passage from mountainous, forested Gargano to the agricultural plain of central Puglia.

Caves of Castellana: discovered on January 23, 1938, by speleologist Franco Anelli who first descended into the chasm called "la Grave," this karst network extends 3,348 meters in length and 122 meters in depth, at 330 meters elevation on the Murge plateau. La Grave itself measures 60 meters deep, 50 wide, and 100 long — a hall so vast Anelli's acetylene lamp couldn't illuminate it entirely. The complete route (about 3 km) ends at the Grotta Bianca, considered among the purest calcite caves in Europe. Constant temperature of 16.5°C inside.

Alberobello — Rione Monti: this neighborhood of more than 1,030 trulli slopes down the southern hillside and has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1996. The trulli, built of dry limestone without mortar from the 16th century onward, answered a fiscal trick: the Counts of Acquaviva d'Aragon allowed peasants to build these dismantleable houses to escape the royal tax on permanent dwellings — until 1797 and the end of feudalism. Look for the Trulli Siamesi, two cones fused at the base but with separate entrances, associated with the legend of a quarrel between two brothers.

Alberobello — Rione Aia Piccola and Trullo Sovrano: the second historic quarter has 590 less-touristy trulli, still inhabited. The Trullo Sovrano is the town's only two-story trullo: its walls reach 2.7 meters thick. Built in the 18th century, it now houses its original furnishings.

Alberobello — Belvedere Santa Lucia and Sant'Antonio church: from this terrace on the hill opposite Rione Monti, you overlook a sea of conical roofs. The Church of Sant'Antonio (1926) is the only church in the world built entirely in trullo style, with a 21-meter interior dome height.

→

Evening stroll through the lanes of Rione Monti: fading light emphasizes the silvery gray of the chianche (limestone slabs) and the symbols painted in white lime on the pinnacles — crosses, stars, primitive figures, characters originally apotropaic to ward off the evil eye.

Locorotondo — circular historic center: the town's name contracts "Luogo rotondo" (round place), referring to the circular plan of the medieval borgo perched on its hill. Characteristic houses bear the name cummerse: pitched roofs covered with flat stone tiles, an architecture unique to Locorotondo that distinguishes it from its flat-roofed neighbors. The mother church of San Giorgio (17th-19th century) occupies the highest point of the promontory. Ranked among Italy's most beautiful villages, the town commands the entire Itria Valley.

Cisternino — old town: another village ranked among Italy's most beautiful, Cisternino presents a medieval center with whitewashed lanes of almost Oriental appearance. Its Romanesque campanile from the 12th century, remnant of the church of Santa Maria, towers over Piazza Garibaldi. The village is also known for its macellerie con fornello — butcher shops with built-in grills where you choose your meat and have it cooked on the spot, a culinary tradition unique to Puglia.

Ostuni — Cathedral of Santa Maria Assunta and ramparts: perched at 218 meters on its three hills, Ostuni owes its white color to a medieval health decision — facades were covered with lime during the 15th-17th century plague epidemics, lime having disinfectant properties. The late Gothic cathedral, built in 1400, stands out for its facade with three portals and its rose window in limestone lacework. Founded by the Messapians around 600 BC, the city was destroyed by Hannibal during the Punic Wars then rebuilt by the Greeks.

Ostuni — Museum of Civilizations and burial of Delia: in the former church of San Vito Martyr is preserved the burial of Delia, a 25,000-year-old pregnant woman found in the cave of Santa Maria di Agnano, a few kilometers from the city. The nearly complete skeleton, with the fetus visible, is accompanied by a headdress adorned with hundreds of small shells and flint tools. One of the best-preserved Paleolithic testimonies in Italy.

Ostuni — sea and olive grove viewpoints: from the Aragonese ramparts built in 1539 to resist Turkish incursions, the view extends across millennial olive groves and, in clear weather, to the Adriatic Sea 8 kilometers away. Piazza della Libertà marks the entrance to the historic center and serves as the town's heart.

→

Driving loop through the Itria Valley between Locorotondo and Ostuni: secondary roads cross agricultural properties dotted with isolated trulli, dry stone walls, and centuries-old olive trees with twisted trunks. Some of these olives are more than 1,000 years old according to botanical assessments.

Polignano a Mare — clifftop old town: founded by Greeks as Neapolis apula, the town perches on 20-to-25-meter tufa cliffs. Romans ran the Via Traiana through here — the stone bridge spanning Lama Monachile beach is a direct remnant. Venetians resided here from 1506 onward — the Palazzo del Doge on the main square preserves the memory. The old town is a maze of lanes where poetic inscriptions are painted on the walls.

Polignano a Mare — Lama Monachile and viewpoints: this white-pebble cove framed by limestone cliffs takes its name from the monk seal (monachile) that populated the coast until the Middle Ages. The Bourbon bridge spanning it dates to the 19th century. Terrazza Santo Stefano and the Modugno belvedere offer the best elevated views. Polignano's coast holds 80 natural caves carved into the limestone, including Grotta Palazzese — converted into a ballroom in 1730 by the Duke Leto of Naples.

Monopoli — Charles V Castle and old port: built between 1552 and 1567 on Charles V's orders to defend the Adriatic coast against Ottoman incursions, the Castello di Carlo V is integrated into the urban fabric of the waterfront. The old port, ringed by medieval ramparts, maintains an animation of fishing and pleasure boats. Cala Porta Vecchia beach opens directly beneath the old ramparts.

Monopoli — historic center and Cathedral of Maria Santissima della Madia: in the 16th century, according to local legend, a raft bearing a Byzantine icon of the Virgin washed ashore at Monopoli. The baroque cathedral (17th-18th century) was built in her honor. The icon, named Madonna della Madia, is still preserved inside.

→

Boat excursion from Polignano a Mare: coastal outings let you see the municipality's 80 caves from the water, including Grotta Azzurra Monachile and Grotta Palazzese viewed from the sea. Kayak or paddleboard options available from Cala Paura beach.

Coastal drive Monopoli → Brindisi: the route follows the Adriatic coast for 45 km, with possible stops at Spiaggia di Capitolo or Villanova, two uncrowded coves between the two cities.

Manduria — Messapian city and Primitivo wines: Manduria's Messapian ramparts, built between the 5th and 2nd centuries BC, are among the best preserved on the Italian peninsula. They extend for several kilometers around the ancient city and are accompanied by a necropolis of several thousand tombs. Manduria is also the capital of Primitivo di Manduria DOC, a powerful red wine produced from the local Zinfandel grape since the 17th century.

Brindisi — Roman column of the Via Appia: atop the Virgilio stairway overlooking the port, an 18.74-meter marble column traditionally marks the end of the Via Appia, the road that linked Rome to Brindisi from 244 BC onward. Originally two columns stood here. The second collapsed in a 1528 earthquake — its fragments were gifted to Lecce in 1659, where architect Giuseppe Zimbalo incorporated them into the column in Piazza Sant'Oronzo. The original capital of the remaining column is preserved in Palazzo Granafei-Nervegna.

Brindisi — Church of Santa Maria del Casale: 3 km north of the city center, this 13th-century church in Apulian Romanesque-Gothic style is one of the best-preserved medieval monuments in the region. Its interior frescoes from the early 14th century cover the entirety of the walls — notably the Last Judgment in the apse, attributed to Rinaldo da Taranto. The facade in bichrome limestone (light and dark stone alternated) is characteristic of late Romanesque Puglia.

Brindisi — lungomare and historic port: Brindisi's natural harbor, whose shape recalls a deer's antlers (Brunda in Messapian, whence the city's name), was one of the Roman Empire's principal embarkation ports to the East. Virgil died here in 19 BC on his return from Greece — a small house on Piazza Vittorio Emanuele marks the traditional site of his death. The Lungomare Regina Margherita runs along the inner harbor.

→

Primitivo di Manduria DOC tasting: estates around Manduria offer vineyard visits and tastings of this powerful red wine (14 to 17 degrees), produced for centuries on limestone and reddish clay soils. Primitivo is genetically identical to Californian Zinfandel — its presence in Italy is documented from the 17th century onward.

Lecce — Basilica of Santa Croce: begun in 1549 and completed in 1695, this facade is the defining monument of barocco leccese — a style born from the marriage between the malleability of Lecce stone (pietra leccese), a soft yellowish limestone easy to carve, and competition between religious orders. The facade, work of several successive architects including Gabriele Riccardi (lower part) and Cesare Penna (upper part), arrays ranks of grotesque figures, telamons, fantastic animals, and floral motifs across two levels.

Lecce — Piazza del Duomo: this enclosed square constitutes one of Italy's most coherent baroque ensembles. The Cathedral of Maria Santissima Assunta, rebuilt by Giuseppe Zimbalo between 1659 and 1670, has two facades — one sober on the north side, the other richly decorated on the west. The 68-meter bell tower (1661-1682), also by Zimbalo, dominates the ensemble. The Palazzo del Seminario, built between 1694 and 1709 by Giuseppe Cino, closes the square on the right.

Lecce — Piazza Sant'Oronzo and Roman amphitheater: Lecce's central square layers testimonies from five eras. The Roman amphitheater, discovered by chance in 1929 during construction of the Bank of Italy, measured 102 by 83 meters and could seat about 25,000 spectators. It dates to the 2nd century AD. The column of Sant'Oronzo, of Roman origin, was brought from Brindisi in the 17th century — it's one of the two columns that marked the end of the Via Appia at Brindisi. The Sedile, former Venetian town hall (1588-1592), now houses the tourist office.

Lecce — Church of San Matteo and baroque quarter: built from 1667 to the plans of Giovann'Andrea Larducci, San Matteo is the only church in Lecce to adopt a convex facade in the lower part and concave in the upper part, in the style of Borromini. Porta Napoli, a baroque triumphal arch erected in 1548 at the northern entrance to the city in honor of Charles V, marks the beginning of the baroque route from the north.

→

Baroque walking loop: from Piazza del Duomo to Santa Croce, passing through Castello Carlo V, Porta Napoli, and Corso Vittorio Emanuele. The golden evening light on pietra leccese is the best hour to photograph the facades.

Galatina — Basilica of Santa Caterina d'Alessandria: built from 1384 by Raimondello Orsini del Balzo upon his return from the Holy Land, this basilica is one of the best-preserved Gothic-Byzantine fresco ensembles in the Mezzogiorno. Frescoes cover the entire interior walls in multiple registers: scenes from the life of Saint Catherine, the Apocalypse, the life of Christ. The Orsini family, who controlled Salento in the 15th century, made the church their dynastic pantheon.

Galatina — historic center and Piazza Orsini: the town retains a medieval center with Piazza Orsini as the central space. Galatina is also known as the cradle of Salento tarantella (la pizzica), a ritual dance traditionally practiced during the Feast of Saint Paul in late June. According to tradition, people bitten by a tarantula (or "tarantate") healed through dance.

Nardò — Piazza Salandra and baroque cathedral: Piazza Salandra, with its Colonna dell'Immacolata (1769) and baroque palaces, is considered one of the most beautiful squares in Salento. The cathedral, founded in 1080 by the Normans and remodeled in the 17th-18th centuries, retains a Byzantine crypt with rupestrian frescoes. Nardò is also the starting point for the beaches of Porto Selvaggio and Santa Maria al Bagno on the Ionian coast.

→

Olive oil and Salento wine tasting at a masseria: the territory around Lecce produces Primitivo and Negroamaro. Estates offer tours of centuries-old olive trees and tastings of Terra d'Otranto DOP olive oil.

Otranto — Cathedral of Santa Maria Annunziata: built by Normans in 1068 on an early Christian site, the cathedral is listed on UNESCO's tentative World Heritage list for its floor mosaic. Commissioned in 1163 by the archbishop and executed between 1163 and 1165 by the monk Pantaleone using 600,000 limestone tesserae, this 1,000-square-meter mosaic represents the tree of life of Gilgamesh supported by two Indian elephants. Around the tree mingle biblical, mythological, and historical figures: Adam and Eve, King Arthur, Cain and Abel, Alexander the Great, the signs of the Zodiac. In the Chapel of Martyrs are preserved the bones of 800 citizens beheaded by the Turks in 1480 for refusing to convert to Islam — canonized by Pope Francis in 2013.

Otranto — Aragonese castle and ramparts: built between 1485 and 1498 on Ferdinand I of Aragon's orders to prevent another Ottoman attack after the 1480 massacre, the castle is the work of architects Ciro Ciri and Francesco di Giorgio Martini. Its bastioned ramparts constitute an early example of military architecture transitioning between medieval and artillery-era design. The walk along the ramparts overlooks the old town and harbor.

Cava di Bauxite di Otranto: 2.6 km south of the center, this former bauxite quarry operated from 1940 to 1976 has left an emerald lake at the bottom of an excavation with rust-red walls. The chromatic contrast between oxidized rock and green water results from the water table and runoff. View from the elevated belvedere.

Torre Sant'Andrea — faraglioni and Otranto's north coast: 14 km north of Otranto, this coastal site is known for its natural arches and limestone faraglioni rising straight from turquoise water. The coastal path between Torre Sant'Andrea and the site of Roca Vecchia (Messapian archaeological site with the Grotta della Poesia, a cave with rupestrian inscriptions from the 2nd millennium BC) runs on foot along the cliffs.

→

Swimming and snorkeling around the faraglioni of Torre Sant'Andrea: the natural arches create shallow zones with crystalline water, accessible by swimming from the rocky shore. Water shoes recommended.

→

Swimming on the Adriatic coast of Salento north of Otranto: between Torre Sant'Andrea and Santa Cesarea Terme, the coast alternates rocky coves and small beaches accessible on foot. Santa Cesarea Terme, 20 km south, is a former spa town with Moorish-style villas from the early 20th century.

Santa Maria di Leuca — cape viewpoint and basilica: the southernmost point of Puglia, where the Adriatic meets the Ionian Sea, is crowned by the Basilica of Santa Maria de Finibus Terrae. The Apulian Aqueduct, 524 kilometers long, ends here with an artificial cascade pouring into the sea from the basilica's terrace. Many pilgrims arrive on foot from cities as distant as Lecce or Taranto.

Gallipoli — old town on limestone island: the city's name derives from Greek Kallè Polis ("beautiful city"). The historic center occupies a limestone islet connected to the mainland by a 17th-century bridge. Thanks to its port, Gallipoli became from the late 16th century onward the leading olive oil export center in the entire Mediterranean, the oil serving to fuel lamps throughout Europe. The baroque palaces of Via Antonietta De Pace testify to this wealth.

Gallipoli — Aragonese castle and Greek fountain: at the bridge entrance, the square-plan castle with its four corner towers was remodeled many times from its Byzantine construction onward, through Aragonese modifications of the 15th century and the addition of a fifth circular tower on the sea side in the 16th century. The Fontana Greca, on Piazza Alda Moro between the island and the Borgo, is considered the oldest monumental fountain in Italy still preserved in place, dating to the Greco-Roman period and remodeled in 1560.

Gallipoli — Cathedral of Sant'Agata: built in the 17th century of Lecce stone (local carparo), the building is the most representative example of Salento baroque in Gallipoli. The interior holds twelve baroque altars, a coffered ceiling in gilded wood, and canvases by Giovanni Andrea Coppola, a local painter trained in Naples, as well as works by Neapolitans Nicola and Giovanni Malinconico. The polychrome marble altar of the presbytery is the work of Cosimo Fanzago of Bergamo.

→

Sunset from Spiaggia della Purità: this town beach runs directly along the old town ramparts on the Ionian side. It's the hour when fishermen ready their blue boats in the old port for the next morning's outing.

Porto Cesareo — marine reserve and torre: this small fishing village is surrounded by a marine reserve since 1997. The 16th-century Aragonese tower, built on an islet facing the harbor, is accessible by boat. The shallow waters of the bay are among the clearest on the Ionian coast.

Pescoluse (Marina di Morciano), nicknamed the "Maldives of Salento" by Italian guides for its sandbars emerging in turquoise waters, lies 35 km south.

Manduria — Messapian ramparts and Primitivo wines: Manduria's ramparts, built between the 5th and 2nd centuries BC, are among the best preserved in Italy. They extend for several kilometers around the ancient city with a necropolis of several thousand tombs. Manduria is also the capital of Primitivo di Manduria DOC, a grape genetically identical to Californian Zinfandel, whose presence in the region is documented from the 17th century onward.

Taranto — MArTA National Archaeological Museum: founded in 1887 in the former convent of the Alcantarini, this museum is the second most important archaeological museum in southern Italy after Naples. Its collection of Ori di Taranto — Hellenistic gold jewelry from the 4th and 3rd centuries BC from monumental tombs — has made it world-famous. The museum also holds the world's largest collection of Greek terracotta figurines, and a 6th-century BC bronze statue of Zeus found at Ugento.

Taranto — Temple of Poseidon and old town: the two Doric columns of the Temple of Poseidon (6th century BC) are the oldest vestiges of Tarentum and among the first Doric-style columns in Magna Graecia. The old town occupies an islet between the Mar Grande and the Mar Piccolo — the natural inner harbor that made the city's fortune since antiquity. The Ponte Girevole, a 19th-century swing bridge, links the island to the mainland.

Drive Taranto → Matera (60 km, ~1h): the road leaves Puglia to enter Basilicata. Matera appears at the edge of the limestone plateau, first invisible, then suddenly revealed from the canyon rim. *(stage in Basilicata, outside Puglia)*

Matera — viewpoints and Gravina: the Belvedere Giovanni Pascoli, on the opposite rim of the canyon, offers the most complete frontal view of the two sassi quarters. The Gravina, the deep ravine separating the city from its plateau, is visible from several viewpoints in the historic center. Across the way, caves carved into the rock served as dwellings, sheepfolds, and places of worship.

Matera — rupestrian churches: Matera's territory holds more than 150 rupestrian churches, about fifty of which are accessible. The Madonna de Idris (12th century), carved into the rock of the Idris monolith, is one of the best known. Its Byzantine frescoes are partially preserved. The Cripta del Peccato Originale, a few kilometers away, preserves the best-preserved rupestrian frescoes of Puglia-Basilicata (8th-9th century).

Matera — Piazza Vittorio Veneto and underground museum: Matera's main square overlies a vast complex of Byzantine cisterns and canals from the 8th-10th centuries, brought to light during excavations. Descending into these underground spaces gives a measure of the city's stratification. Some of the sassi's troglodyte houses have up to four levels carved into the rock, the deepest dating to the Neolithic.

Matera — Church of San Pietro Caveoso and surroundings: one of the few built rather than carved churches in the sassi, San Pietro Caveoso (12th century, baroque facade remodeled in the 17th) overlooks the Gravina ravine from its rock. Across the canyon, the caves of the former villanelle constitute an uninhabited but accessible troglodyte ensemble on foot. Matera was European Capital of Culture in 2019.

Matera — Ridola National Museum: installed in a former 17th-century convent, this museum preserves archaeological collections from Matera and its territory, from the Paleolithic to the classical period. Ceramics from the Serra d'Alto civilization (4th millennium BC) are particularly well represented.

Bari — Basilica of San Nicola: built from 1087 onward to house the relics of Saint Nicholas of Myra, stolen from Myra (present-day Turkey) by sailors from Bari who beat the Venetians to the same prize. The Romanesque basilica, completed in 1197, is the model that inspired Trani Cathedral and several other Apulian Romanesque buildings. The crypt preserves the saint's relics, venerated by both Catholic and Orthodox Christians. Pope Urban II partially consecrated it in 1089.

Bari Vecchia — old town and Cathedral of San Sabino: the labyrinth of lanes in Bari's old town, built to an irregular plan of Byzantine and medieval origin on a rocky promontory, is one of the densest historic centers in Puglia. The Cathedral of San Sabino (12th century) neighbors the basilica. Orecchiette Street — Via dell'Arco Basso — is known for the women who make pasta by hand in front of their doors.

→
