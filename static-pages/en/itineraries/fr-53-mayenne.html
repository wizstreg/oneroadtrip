Sainte-Suzanne crowns a rocky spur rising 70 meters above the Erve valley. Listed among France's Most Beautiful Villages and as a Small Town of Character, this fortress was the only stronghold to successfully resist the assaults of William the Conqueror in the 11th century.

Step into the castle courtyard to discover the Renaissance lodge, built between 1608 and 1613 by Guillaume Fouquet de la Varenne, Henri IV's Postmaster General. It now houses the CIAP (Centre for Architecture and Heritage Interpretation) with scale models, audiovisuals and tactile displays exploring Mayenne's heritage.

The 11th-century quadrangular keep, erected by the Beaumont family from around 1020, stands protected by round towers equipped with arrow slits—the flour tower and the powder tower. The entrance was on the first floor via a ladder, designed to protect the lord during attacks.

Walk the patrol path along the double rampart walls. The 360° view sweeps across the Coëvrons hills, the Mayenne hedgerow countryside and the Erve valley. Head to the Watch Tower and West Tower for the finest panoramas over the town's rooftops.

The church, remodeled in 1884, preserves a Renaissance portal and remarkable statues from the 14th to 17th centuries, including those of Saint John the Baptist and Saint Suzanne, patron saint of engaged couples.

Push open the door to the municipal museum, housed in the former 17th-century courthouse. Nine rooms trace 3,000 years of local history through models, weapons from the Hundred Years' War and elements of Celtic vitrified walls. The centerpiece: France's oldest suit of armor, dating from 1410-1430, restored by the Musée des Invalides.

Descend toward the Grand Moulin, the oldest mill in the lordship. Transformed from a grain mill into a paper mill, it's still run by a master papermaker who crafts pure rag paper by hand using 13th to 18th-century techniques. The town once boasted 16 paper mills from the 17th to 19th centuries.

→

The Mills Walk follows the Erve for 3.6 km below the fortified town. This family-friendly path passes the vestiges of old water mills installed along the river.

→

Visit the Erves dolmen, Mayenne's oldest monument. This Neolithic megalith, approximately 6,500 years old and classified in 1889, once sheltered the burials of 14 individuals.

→

Climb to Tertre Ganne in the late afternoon. This former strategic post from the Hundred Years' War offers the finest panorama of the medieval town and castle—spectacular at sunset.

The Basilica of Notre-Dame de l'Épine in Évron is Mayenne's most significant medieval religious building. According to legend, a 7th-century pilgrim returning from Jerusalem deposited a relic of the Virgin's milk in a hawthorn bush; its miraculous overnight flowering led Saint Hadouin to found a monastery here in 642.

The architecture offers a complete panorama of medieval religious art: a 10th-century crypt, an 11th-century Romanesque nave, a 12th-century fortified tower-porch, and a 13th-century Gothic choir with soaring proportions. Listed as a Historic Monument since 1840.

Don't miss the 12th-century Saint-Crépin chapel, a stunning Arabo-Byzantine jewel inspired by Spanish Moorish architecture—unique décor in western France. Also admire the mural paintings, stained glass and ornate reliquary containing the Virgin's milk.

The basilica's history is turbulent: destroyed by Bretons and Vikings in the 9th century, rebuilt in the 10th, pillaged during the Wars of Religion in 1562. A Festival of Sacred Arts brings the naves to life each summer in July.

→

Hike in the Charnie Forest, southeast of Évron. This forest massif is one of Mayenne's most extensive, offering numerous marked trails through oak and beech groves.

→

The Coëvrons territory around Évron offers over 500 km of marked trails for hiking, mountain biking or horseback riding through hedgerow countryside and green hills.

Ancient Noviodunum, now the commune of Jublains, is western France's most important Gallo-Roman site. Capital of the Gallic Diablintes people and founded in the 1st century AD, the city counted 3,000 to 4,000 inhabitants at its peak and served as a road junction connected to a dozen cities.

The Gallo-Roman temple on the road to Mayenne marks the town's origin. Rebuilt in stone after 65 AD, it testifies to the acceleration of Romanization in the mid-1st century.

The fortress is the site's most spectacular element and a monument unique in the Roman Empire. This fortified trapezoid measuring 117.50 × 104.25 meters wasn't a military camp as long believed, but probably a warehouse where taxes in kind and precious metals transited to Rome.

The theater, built around 80 AD and gifted to the city by merchant Orgétorix, sits on the plateau slope facing the Coëvrons hills. Initially fully circular in the 1st century, it was rebuilt as a semicircle in the 2nd. It still hosts cultural events today.

Beneath the current church hide the Roman baths, transformed into a place of worship as early as the 5th century. They occupied a square urban block 60 meters on each side with porticos, bathing rooms and shops—the heart of Roman social life.

The departmental archaeological museum, labeled Tourism and Disability, presents collections from the Bronze Age to the early Middle Ages: pottery, jewelry, coins, game pieces, terracotta Venus statuettes and fragments of the 'pillar with masks' evoking a theater scene.

→

Follow the archaeological trail by spotting the 12 bronze discs set into the ground. They mark a pedestrian circuit between the temple, baths, theater and fortress, crossing the ancient Roman town's layout.

→

Continue to the Crafts Forum, a venue dedicated to artisanship built after the discovery of an ancient artisan quarter whose activity continues today.

→

Excavations have been ongoing since 2010 at Jublains: archaeologists and student volunteers are uncovering a vast Gallo-Roman complex—domus or macellum—along the Grazay road. A temporary exhibition accompanies each year's excavation season closing.

Mayenne Castle overlooks the river of the same name. Over 1,100 years old, it houses Europe's best-preserved Carolingian palace, classified as an archaeological site of national interest. The reception hall, cellar and residential tower are still legible in the masonry.

The museum, labeled Museum of France and starred in the Michelin Green Guide, traces 2,000 years of history through an interactive journey. The collections from excavations are exceptional: chess and backgammon pieces from the 10th-12th centuries, ceramics, coins, arrowheads and funerary furnishings testifying to the life of Mayenne's lords.

In the upper courtyard, a medieval-inspired garden has been recreated from period documents. Plants and shrubs are classified as in the Middle Ages: medicinal herbs, aromatics, edibles, medicinals or 'magical' plants.

The Basilica of Notre-Dame-des-Miracles, on the right bank, is a composite edifice from the 12th, 16th and 19th centuries. The west façade is original Romanesque (circa 1110). The 19th-century choir draws inspiration from Le Mans Cathedral's chevet. A chapel houses the 15th-century polychrome wooden statuette of Our Lady of Miracles.

The basilica's current stained glass windows, designed by Maurice Rocher and installed between 1952 and 1962, replaced those destroyed during the June 1944 bombings that devastated the town. The basilica remained closed for thirteen years for restoration.

Stroll through Place de Hercé with its octagonal fountain, then reach Place Cheverus surrounded by 17th-18th century townhouses. On the left bank, the 11th-century Saint-Martin church stands out with its russet granite.

The Calvary Sisters Chapel, last vestige of a vast 17th-century convent, now houses an art center devoted to contemporary creation.

→

Walk along the Mayenne quays for a riverside stroll. The castle park, right in the town center, offers a bandstand, towering trees and a stunning view from the ramparts.

→

Drive 5 km to Fontaine-Daniel, a village of character nestled in a clearing in the Salair woods. Founded around a Cistercian abbey in 1205, it became the birthplace of Toiles de Mayenne in 1806 when the abandoned abbey was transformed into a spinning mill. The factory, labeled Living Heritage Company, still weaves on site.

→

In season, board the pleasure boat departing from the river stop for a narrated cruise on the Mayenne River. Wildlife, flora, local history and manual lock operation on the program.

Lassay-les-Châteaux, a Small Town of Character, boasts three castles. Victor Hugo, passing through in 1836, wrote to his wife that he'd seen 'Lassay, a charming half-wild little town with three castles, two of them admirable, which I sketched.'

The fortress, rebuilt in one campaign in 1458-1459 by Jean II de Vendôme after the Hundred Years' War, is a remarkable example of 15th-century military architecture. Its eight massive towers linked by curtain walls and ramparts, its barbican and drawbridge form an irregular octagon that has survived intact to this day—an exceptional occurrence in France.

The guided tour opens furnished rooms: bedroom, armory, kitchen with its granite cooking range and collection of antique copper. Arrive at the first tour to witness the drawbridge opening, or at the last to see it close.

A kilometer from the center, the ruins of Bois-Thibault castle raise their century-gilded towers amid the ivy. Built on a castle mound, it was home to the Du Bellay family—that of poet Joachim. Classified as a Historic Monument in 1925, it's maintained by a volunteer association.

Saint-Fraimbault church is dedicated to the 6th-century evangelizing hermit buried at Lassay, who allegedly inspired Chrétien de Troyes to create the character of Lancelot of the Lake.

The historic center in local granite can be explored on foot: picturesque lanes, houses from the 14th-18th centuries, colorful storefronts and preserved period signs. Spot the lintel carvings and turrets at street corners.

→

Wander through Lassay's rose garden, near the old convent, where some 300 rose varieties offer an olfactory experience in season.

→

The medieval garden, connected to the castle, presents plants and herbs cultivated according to Middle Ages practices—aromatic, dye, medicinal.

→

Follow the Lassay river to discover the numerous old wash houses lining the waterway, witnesses to daily life of yesteryear.

→

The King Arthur legend interpretation trail connects the town's three castles (Lassay, Bois-Thibault and the Bois-Frou vestiges) through verdant landscapes and built heritage.

Gorron is a small character town set on the granite foundations of the Armorican Massif, at the historic crossroads of the Breton and Norman marches. The Colmont river flows through it, creating a green setting bordered by old wash houses.

Saint-Martin church, rebuilt from 1868 to 1874 in neo-Romanesque style, dominates the town from its tall square bell tower that forms the entrance porch. Inside, an altarpiece depicting the stoning of Saint Stephen deserves attention.

Rue des Chauvinettes, quite steep, runs within the outline of the old fortress built around 1162 by Henry II Plantagenet, King of England. The granite flanks of the Colmont riverbanks served as natural fortification. The castle, contested between the lords of Mayenne and the English crown, was destroyed during the Hundred Years' War.

Stroll the lanes to spot the town hall housed in the old market halls, 19th-century bourgeois houses and the metal footbridge above the street—remnant of the former furniture factory that brought industrial fame to Gorron after the clog makers.

→

The Colmont trail (n°11, yellow markers, FFRandonnée certified) starts from the church and circles the town between sunken lanes, hedgerows and Colmont riverbanks. Along the way, the Roche menhir has stood since approximately 4,000 BC beside a stream: 4.40 meters tall, classified Historic Monument. Legend says that on Christmas night, at the twelve strokes of midnight, the stone turns twelve times on itself.

→

The Colmont Leisure Park offers in season treetop adventure courses, kayaking, swin-golf, outdoor laser tag and paintball in a preserved natural setting.

Laval, prefecture of Mayenne and a Town of Art and History, has sprawled for a millennium along the riverside. Dominated by its medieval castle, the historic city overflows with half-timbered houses, steep lanes and townhouses.

The Old Castle, founded around 1020 by Guy de Dénéré on a hill overlooking a ford, is the town's emblematic monument. Its 34-meter cylindrical keep, crowned with a defensive hoarding, houses an exceptional 13th-century timber frame. The 12th-century Romanesque chapel boasts beautiful carved capitals.

The MANAS (Museum of Naïve Art and Singular Arts), installed in the castle since 1967, is France's first naïve art museum. It displays major collections across 1,100 m² including three canvases by Le Douanier Rousseau, a Laval native born in 1844, and works by Séraphine de Senlis. Free admission to permanent collections.

Pass through the Beucheresse Gate, one of the old fortified gates: it was in this tower that Le Douanier Rousseau was born. From the other side of the Old Bridge, the panorama of the castle and quays is Laval's most photographed view.

The Basilica of Notre-Dame d'Avesnières, on the right bank, is a fine Romanesque building from the 11th-12th centuries. Its chevet with radiating chapels is one of the most harmonious in western France.

The municipal Public Baths, labeled '20th-century Heritage,' are a jewel of Laval Art Deco. Their mosaics by Isidore Odorico play with blue and yellow contrasts in each cabin, following a pattern inspired by Viennese art.

The Saint-Julien wash-boat, last survivor of the fleet that animated the Mayenne riverbanks, recalls the time when washerwomen beat their laundry along the water.

→

Climb up to the Perrine Garden, a haven of peace in the heart of town. This park overlooking the Mayenne shelters century-old trees, a rose garden and panoramic views of the river. At Le Douanier Rousseau's grave, read the verses engraved by his friend Guillaume Apollinaire.

→

Descend to Laval's port for an electric boat ride on the Mayenne. Southward, the river leads to the Abbey of Notre-Dame du Port-du-Salut (birthplace of the famous cheese) and the Gallo-Roman baths of Entrammes.

→

The towpath along the Mayenne is part of the Vélo Francette (from Caen to La Rochelle). Rent a bike to pedal along the river between manual locks and hedgerow landscapes.

Craon Castle, nicknamed 'little Versailles of Mayenne,' stands on Guinefol hill overlooking the town. This elegant late 18th-century tuffeau edifice, built by the Marquis d'Armaillé on the ruins of a feudal lodge destroyed by the Wars of Religion then by Henri IV, is still inhabited by the same family.

The guided tour opens salons with finely carved woodwork and furnishings that belonged to Marie-Antoinette. The architecture, inspired by Versailles' Petit Trianon, illustrates the art of living in the Age of Enlightenment.

The French formal garden surrounds the castle with perfect symmetry. Water mirrors reflect the façade and light. The English-style park, planted in 1830, unfolds 47 hectares of greenery and over 150 plant species along 6 km of tree-lined alleys with century-old specimens.

Don't miss the large walled kitchen garden (over a hectare), the 19th-century greenhouses, the ice house, the wash-laundry and the dovecote—all preserved in their original state.

In town, Saint-Nicolas church and the market halls merit a detour. Craon was also a major center for Mayenne horse racing, a tradition rooted in this horse-breeding land symbolized by Pegasus, the red winged horse, emblem of the department.

→

Walk the castle park following the 6 km of alleys. A treasure hunt is offered to children. Along the paths, encounter horses, goats, swans, geese and perhaps deer or squirrels that populate the estate.

→

Follow the Oudon river that borders the park. Its two bridges offer romantic viewpoints of the castle through the grove.

Château-Gontier-sur-Mayenne, classified among France's Most Beautiful Detours, was born around the year 1000 from Count of Anjou Foulque Nerra's strategic decision to establish a fortress facing the Bretons. The castle, entrusted to officer Gontier, has disappeared, but the town has preserved abundant heritage on both sides of the river, with 18 protected historic monuments and a 58-hectare Remarkable Heritage Site.

Saint-Jean-Baptiste church, built in the 11th century in flint and roussard stone by Benedictines from Saint-Aubin Abbey in Angers, is the town's oldest church. Its Romanesque interior strikes with its purity: wooden-vaulted nave, rare dome on pendentives above the choir, ten-column crypt and fine ensemble of 12th-13th century mural paintings.

Hôtel Fouquet, an elegant 17th-century townhouse at the intersection of Grande Rue and Rue Jean Bourré, houses the Museum of Art and Archaeology. Nearby, spot the Hôtel de Lantivy (17th c., classified MH), Hôtel du Tertre de Sancé (15th c., listed MH) and half-timbered houses in medieval lanes.

Cross the Mayenne to discover the Ursuline convent in the Azé suburb. Founded in 1634 in a 15th-century manor house, expanded by Laval architects Pierre and Gilles Corbineau from 1642, it boasts a majestic cloister, a Jesuit-style church and convent cells. Since 1999, it has housed the National Stage Le Carré in a 530-seat theater integrated into the cloister.

Push open the gate to the Garden of the End of the World, perched on a rocky spur above the Mayenne. This flower-filled park with century-old trees shelters a 17th-century ice house and mischievous goats. The view over the river and town rooftops is worth the visit.

The Garden of Scents, on the Mayenne's banks, displays 80 varieties of fragrant and medicinal plants: lavender, mint, oregano, thyme. The Medieval Garden of the Spring, at the foot of old ramparts, completes this plant journey. Château-Gontier has earned four flowers in the Flowered Towns and Villages competition.

→

Walk down the Mayenne quays along the river from the river stop. In summer, ephemeral riverside cafés set up on the banks with concerts and dining.

→

The Perrettes circuit (marked) winds between the Mayenne quays, the Ursuline convent, old quarters and the Garden of the End of the World—an ideal loop to embrace all the town's faces.

→

Thursday mornings, Château-Gontier's traditional market invades the town center squares. It's also the continent's largest calf fair—a vibrant tradition of this livestock-raising land.

Saulges

The Saulges cave valley is the only prehistoric site presenting decorated caves north of the Loire. Carved into 320-million-year-old limestone by the Erve river, this small canyon shelters 22 known cavities, classified Natura 2000, frequented by humans for at least 300,000 years.

Margot cave, classified Historic Monument since 1926, is the only decorated cave open to visitors in western France. In 2003, archaeologist Romain Pigeaud discovered over 200 prehistoric engravings from the Upper Paleolithic (~25,000 years ago): horses, bison, woolly rhinoceros and engraved birds—the latter extremely rare in cave art.

Rochefort cave, a human-sized chasm, reveals an archaeological excavation site, spectacular concretions (stalactites, stalagmites) and an underground lake. Excavations have uncovered a Solutrean habitat dated 25,000 to 20,000 years before present.

The Prehistory Museum, opened in 2017, traces the life of the Erve valley's first inhabitants through original archaeological collections: laurel leaves, spear points, reindeer antler harpoons, ibex plaquette. An immersive 3D dome allows virtual visits to Mayenne-Sciences cave, closed to the public, which contains drawings of bison and mammoths 27,000 years old.

Saint-Pierre church, in Saulges village, is a building constructed between the 8th and 10th centuries. A few minutes' walk away, the Céneré oratory perpetuates the memory of the hermit who evangelized the valley.

→

Take hiking trail n°33 from the museum. It crosses the canyon passing Japanese stepping stones over the Erve, follows the limestone cliffs and offers plunging views of cave entrances nestled in the rock faces.

→

The canyon cliffs count a hundred climbing routes from 3a to 7c, on walls reaching 25 meters. A popular venue for initiation and recreational practice.

→

The Natura 2000 valley shelters wild orchids, dragonflies, rare butterflies and one of western France's most important bat colonies. Ouessant sheep maintain the site ecologically. Take time to observe wildlife at dusk.
