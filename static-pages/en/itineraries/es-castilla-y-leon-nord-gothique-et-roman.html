Castile and León Road Trip: Cathedrals and Medieval Villages

Gothic spires pierce the sky at dizzying heights, medieval stained glass blazes with color, Romanesque arches stand in timeless silence. Castile and León is cathedral country.

From Covarrubias, a medieval jewel with juniper half-timbering, to Astorga and its Gaudí palace, this itinerary traverses the historic heart of Castile and León. Five stops along the Camino de Santiago to discover Burgos's UNESCO-listed Gothic cathedral, the pure Romanesque art of Frómista, the dazzling stained glass of León, and Castilian gastronomy—from roasted lechazo to Maragato chocolate.

Key highlights (6)
  • Burgos Cathedral: UNESCO-listed Gothic masterpiece with the Golden Staircase and El Cid's tomb
  • Frómista: Church of San Martín, one of the purest Romanesque buildings in Europe
  • León: 1,800 m² of medieval stained glass in the Pulchra Leonina
  • Basilica of San Isidoro: Royal Pantheon and Romanesque frescoes, the 'Sistine Chapel of Romanesque art'
  • Astorga: Gaudí's neo-Gothic episcopal palace and Gothic-Renaissance cathedral
  • Covarrubias: medieval village with juniper half-timbering and a collegiate church filled with Flemish treasures

Covarrubias

The Collegiate Church of San Cosme y San Damián, a 15th-century Gothic gem, houses the tombs of Count Fernán González and his wife in 4th-century Hispano-Roman sarcophagi. The 16th-century cloister preserves the sepulcher of Princess Kristina of Norway, who journeyed from Scandinavia in 1257 to marry a Castilian prince.

Step inside the Collegiate Treasury Museum to admire the Adoration of the Magi triptych, a polychrome wooden masterpiece attributed to Gil de Siloé. Also on display: a Virgin painting attributed to Van Eyck, a canvas by Pedro Berruguete, and the 1620 organ—the oldest still functioning in Castile.

The Torreón de Fernán González, a 10th-century Mozarabic defensive tower, rises at the end of half-timbered streets. Its stone walls are the oldest remnants of the fortified enclosure that once protected the capital of the first County of Castile.

Enter the old town through the arch of the Archives of the Adelantamiento de Castilla, a 16th-century Renaissance building adorned with Philip II's coat of arms. In front of the arch stands a Gothic stone calvary.

Wander around Plaza de Doña Urraca, bordered by the town hall housed in the former palace of Fernán González. The cobbled streets around the square reveal half-timbered houses built with juniper wood on stilts, like the Casa del Obispo Peña with its carved corbels and the house of Doña Sancha with wooden balconies.

The Church of Santo Tomás, dating from the 15th century, preserves a Renaissance pulpit and beautiful altarpieces. Less visited than the collegiate church, it's worth a look for its peaceful atmosphere and quality woodwork.

→

Walk down to the banks of the Arlanza for a riverside stroll. The path follows the remains of the old walls and offers a sweeping view of the village and its tiled roofs.

→

The Arlanza Valley produces wines under the AO Arlanza designation. Pair them with local sheep's cheese and valley cherries, a village specialty for centuries.

→

Late in the day, the half-timbered facades take on golden hues in the setting sun. Plaza de Doña Sancha and the triangular streets form the perfect setting to capture the village's intact medieval atmosphere.

The Cathedral of Santa María de Burgos—the only cathedral in Spain individually designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site—unfolds behind a facade crowned by two 84-meter towers with 15th-century openwork spires. The Sarmental Door features a Christ in Majesty surrounded by apostles, one of the finest sculptural ensembles in Spanish Gothic art.

Inside, the Chapel of the Constable, designed by Simon of Cologne between 1482 and 1494, forms a cathedral within the cathedral. Beneath its eight-pointed star vault lie the recumbent effigies in Carrara marble of the Constable and his wife. The sacristy houses a Magdalene attributed to the school of Leonardo da Vinci.

Climb the Golden Staircase, a Renaissance masterpiece with a double stairway designed by Diego de Siloé in 1519. Its gilded railing inspired Charles Garnier for the grand staircase of the Paris Opera. Beneath the neighboring lantern tower, a Mudéjar star vault shelters the tombs of El Cid Campeador and his wife Doña Jimena.

The Arco de Santa María, a 14th-century fortified gate remodeled in the 16th century, welcomes visitors with its six monumental statues including Charles V and El Cid. The interior, open to visitors, displays Mudéjar plasterwork from the castle.

The Museum of Human Evolution, a contemporary building designed by Juan Navarro Baldeweg, presents over 200 original fossils from Atapuerca, including remains of Homo Antecessor (the oldest in Europe), the Miguelón skull, and the Excalibur hand axe. Four floors trace 1.5 million years of evolution.

The ruins of Burgos Castle, on the hill overlooking the city, offer a 360° panorama over the rooftops, cathedral spires, and the Castilian meseta. A network of restored underground galleries can be toured beneath the fortress.

The Charterhouse of Miraflores, a Gothic monastery east of the city, houses a polychrome altarpiece said to have been gilded with the first gold brought back from the Americas. The royal tombs of John II and Isabella of Portugal, sculpted by Gil de Siloé, rank among the finest in Spain.

→

The neighborhood around Plaza Mayor is packed with traditional mesones. Sample morcilla de Burgos, black pudding with rice and spices, and lechazo asado, milk-fed lamb roasted in a wood-fired oven—two pillars of Castilian gastronomy.

→

The Paseo del Espolón follows the Arlanzón River beneath a double row of plane trees. This shaded promenade, the city's most popular, connects the Arco de Santa María to the San Pablo bridge where an equestrian statue of El Cid stands.

→

The Monastery of Las Huelgas, founded in the 12th century by Alfonso VIII, served as both royal pantheon and coronation site. Its Gothic cloister, the Museum of Rich Fabrics, and the banner captured from the Moors at Las Navas de Tolosa in 1212 are all worth the detour.

The Church of San Martín de Frómista, founded in 1066 by Doña Mayor of Castile, is one of the purest Romanesque buildings in Europe. Three naves, three apses, two cylindrical towers on the gable, and an octagonal dome. The 315 sculpted corbels beneath the cornices depict fantastic animals, human faces, and vegetal motifs of exceptional finesse.

The Church of San Pedro, with its Renaissance portal and Gothic interior, houses the Museum of Sacred Art of Frómista. Its centerpiece: 29 Hispano-Flemish panels from the 15th century, recovered after a theft, which belonged to the main altarpiece of Santa María del Castillo.

Santa María del Castillo, built on the site of a former castle, blends late Gothic with elements from the 15th to 18th centuries. It has been classified as a Site of Cultural Interest since 1944.

The set of four locks on the Canal de Castilla (locks 17 to 20) forms the largest lock system of the canal's three branches, overcoming a drop of more than 14 meters. The aqueduct over the Ucieza River, with three round arches, completes this 18th-century hydraulic engineering work.

The former lock keeper's house, transformed into an interpretation center, traces the history of the Canal de Castilla, an Enlightenment project launched in 1753 under Ferdinand VI to connect the Castilian plateau to the northern ports. More than 350 boats plied its waters in the 1850s.

→

The towpath along the canal offers a flat, shaded route perfect for cycling. Pedal between Frómista and Boadilla del Camino following the path once traveled by mules pulling barges.

→

The wetlands formed around the canal harbor bitterns, warblers, and other aquatic birds. Dawn and dusk are the best times to observe them from the banks.

→

A river cruise of about an hour departs from the Frómista lock keeper's house toward Boadilla del Camino. Onboard commentary presents the local wildlife and the operation of the locks.

The Cathedral of Santa María de León, nicknamed 'Pulchra Leonina' for its beauty, is the most luminous cathedral in Spain. Its 737 stained glass windows cover 1,800 m² of glazed surface, spanning from the 13th to the 20th century, distributed across 125 panels and 57 medallions. Filtered light transforms the soaring naves into a kaleidoscope of colors.

Built in the 13th century atop Roman baths of the Legio VII Gemina, the cathedral draws direct inspiration from the French models of Reims and Amiens. The main altarpiece, painted by Nicolás Francés in the 15th century, the walnut choir stalls, and the White Virgin at the west portal rank among its treasures.

The Basilica of San Isidoro, consecrated in 1063, houses the Royal Pantheon: a vaulted chamber whose 12th-century Romanesque frescoes have earned it the nickname 'Sistine Chapel of Romanesque art.' Biblical scenes, an agricultural calendar, and fantastic animals cover the ceilings above the tombs of 23 kings, queens, and princes of León.

Casa Botines, on Plaza San Marcelo, resembles a fairytale castle with its four turrets and neo-Gothic stained glass windows. Designed in 1891 as a textile shop and residence, it's one of only three works by Gaudí outside Catalonia. The building now houses a museum dedicated to Gaudí and 19th-20th century Spanish art.

The Convent of San Marcos, a former pilgrims' hospital on the Camino turned Parador, displays a 100-meter Plateresque facade adorned with medallions, scallop shells, and mythological figures. Its 16th-century church and the adjoining archaeological museum merit a stop.

The Roman walls of León, built by the Legio VII Gemina in the 1st century and expanded in the 3rd-4th centuries, remain visible in several sections. The San Isidro tower and the medieval sections from the 13th-14th centuries complete the enclosure.

→

The Barrio Húmedo, around Plaza San Martín, is the realm of tapas. Every drink ordered comes with a free tapa. Try cecina de León, cured and aged beef, and cocido leonés, the local pot-au-feu.

→

MUSAC, the Museum of Contemporary Art of Castile and León, occupies a building whose multicolored 3,000 m² glass facade echoes the colors of the cathedral's stained glass windows. Temporary exhibitions fill vast modular spaces.

→

On weekends and holidays, the cathedral's stained glass windows are illuminated from within between midnight and 2 a.m. From Plaza de Regla, the nocturnal spectacle offers a completely different vision of the Pulchra Leonina.

The Episcopal Palace of Astorga, designed by Gaudí in 1887 after the old palace burned down, blends castle, temple, and manor house in white granite from El Bierzo. Four circular towers, moats, battlements, and multicolored stained glass windows inspired by León Cathedral compose this unique neo-Gothic setting. It now houses the Museum of the Ways, dedicated to the pilgrimage to Santiago.

The Cathedral of Santa María de Astorga, built from the 15th to the 18th century, juxtaposes Flamboyant Gothic, Renaissance, and Baroque with surprising harmony. On a pinnacle of the apse, the statue of Pedro Mato, a Maragato surrounded by legends, watches over the city. The sacred art museum preserves the reliquary of San Genadio and the chalice of Alonso de Portillo.

The Chocolate Museum traces Astorga's history as Spain's chocolate capital since the 17th century, when Maragato muleteers brought back cocoa beans from the ports of Galicia. Antique utensils, molds, and period advertisements make up the collection.

Plaza Mayor, square and arcaded, is dominated by the Baroque facade of the Town Hall. Its automaton clock, in place since 1748, features a Maragato couple striking the hours—a spectacle to watch out for at noon.

The Roman walls of Astorga, dating from the 3rd century, testify to the city's importance under the name Asturica Augusta, a crossroads of Roman roads in the northwest of the peninsula. The Roman Museum, housed in a former ergástula, completes the visit.

Between Gaudí's palace and the cathedral, the wall connecting the Church of Santa Marta to the Chapel of San Esteban conceals a discreet passage. A few steps lead to the cell of the walled-in women, a recent discovery and the city's most unusual site.

→

Start the day with a chocolat à la taza accompanied by mantecadas, small lard-and-flour cakes that have been Astorga's pastry fame for centuries. Hojaldres puff pastries complete the ritual.

→

Astorga is the crossroads of two great pilgrimage routes: the Camino Francés and the Vía de la Plata. Walk a few kilometers of the path toward Castrillo de los Polvazares, a Maragato village with reddish stone houses considered one of the most beautiful in Spain.

→

Maragato cuisine peaks with cocido maragato, a pot-au-feu served backwards: you start with the meat, then the vegetables, and finish with the broth. Cecina de León and local charcuterie accompany the meal.
