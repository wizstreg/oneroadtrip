Twenty days to traverse Piedmont in depth - from Garessio and its medieval gateways on the ancient salt road between Liguria and the plains, to Vogogna and its Visconti castle guarding the Ossola valley. Between them, a journey among villages officially ranked among Italy's most beautiful: Chianale with its Swiss pine forest, Ostana the Occitan alpine architecture laboratory, the UNESCO-inscribed hills of Barolo and Neive, the underground infernot of Cella Monte, the enigmatic Rosazza decorated with Masonic symbols, and Orta San Giulio facing its millennial island.

Key highlights (5)
  • Barolo: Castello Falletti and WiMu museum at the heart of the UNESCO Langhe
  • Orta San Giulio: mystical island and Sacro Monte on the lakeshore
  • Rosazza: Italy's most mysterious village, covered in Masonic symbols
  • Cella Monte: underground infernot carved from the Monferrato's marine stone
  • Ostana: worldwide model of contemporary Occitan alpine renaissance

Garessio

Top hotels
9.5 Il Fienile
9.7 B&B Ameglia
9 Casa vacanza IL FARINELLO
9.5 Crosa Rosella

Garessio spills across four distinct quarters - Borgo Maggiore, Ponte, Poggiolo, and Valsorda - divided by the rushing Tanaro River. Enter the medieval center through Porta Rose, the ancient main gateway where legend tells of a lady who fired a fatal arrow from the battlements at an enemy knight during the sixteenth-century Spanish Wars. The partially medieval tower crowns Piazza San Giovanni, where the Confraternity church displays a monumental painting gifted by designer Giorgetto Giugiaro - a native son - depicting the Deposition of Christ. This ritual, the Mortorio, written in 1433 and performed every four years, ranks among Piedmont's oldest sacred reenactments.

In Borgo Ponte, the parish church of Santa Caterina, built between 1723 and 1740 by architect Francesco Gallo, presents a terracotta facade animated by four empty niches - a remarkable example of late Piedmontese baroque. Beyond the stone bridge spanning Rio Mauro, the church of Santa Maria Extra Moenia has stood since the eleventh century; it's one of the region's oldest buildings. The town hall houses a pinacotheca and the Libro della Catena, a tenth-century legal text bound by a chain to the judge's seat and consulted during trials - an exceptionally well-preserved document.

Five kilometers south in the Casotto valley, the Charterhouse of Santa Maria del Casotto was founded in 1183 through donations from local lords. Pillaged under Napoleon, it was purchased by the House of Savoy in 1847 and transformed into a hunting lodge for Victor Emmanuel II. Now owned by the Piedmont Region and part of the UNESCO Royal Residences circuit since 1997, it can be visited with its original furnishings intact.

→

Hike to the Gray caves and visit Garessio's geo-speleological museum, which displays fossils and prehistoric tools attesting to human occupation in the region for millennia, including Ursus spelaeus bones recovered from local caves.

Chianale

9.2 Casa Sasia

Chianale - La Cianal in Occitan - clings to the mountainside at 1,800 meters in the upper Varaita valley, a stone's throw from the Colle dell'Agnello pass leading into France. The Varaita torrent splits the village in two halves connected by a stone bridge that forms the village's true heart. The slate houses with lauze stone roofs reflect an unbroken Occitan building tradition dating back to the tenth century, when Saracen raiders ravaged the region. In 1343, Chianale belonged to the five Free Escartons Republics - a Franco-Italian alpine confederation enjoying considerable autonomy granted by the last Dauphin of Viennois, Humbert II. The 1713 Treaty of Utrecht definitively attached the valley to Savoy.

The church of Sant'Antonio, erected in the fourteenth century, features a double-bay bell gable and a triple-crowned Romanesque portal. Inside, an eighteenth-century baroque altar of carved Swiss pine illustrates the richness of local craftsmanship. The Museum of Costume and Textile Crafts, opened in 2009 in the former eighteenth-century Capuchin Mission, exhibits the clothing and traditions of this alpine community.

Two kilometers from the village, the Alevè forest extends across 825 hectares between 1,500 and 2,500 meters elevation. It's the largest Swiss pine forest in the Alps and one of Europe's largest - trees several centuries old that Virgil already mentioned in Antiquity. Start from the hamlets of Castello or Villaretto for the 5-kilometer trail along Castello Lake, created by a dam built in the twentieth century.

→

In summer, hike to Colle dell'Agnello (2,744m) with plunging views over the French Queyras and Monviso (3,841m), nicknamed the "Stone King." In winter, cross-country skiing and ski mountaineering from Chianale (15km of trails). Every January 17th, the blessing of the animals marks Saint Anthony's Day; in February, carnival perpetuates the wolf-return ritual with costumed men and nocturnal village celebrations.

Ostana counts barely fifty permanent residents today - down from 1,200 in 1921 - yet this Occitan village perched at 1,250 meters beneath Monviso has become an international model of alpine renaissance. In 1985, mayor Giacomo Lombardo and architect Renato Maurino launched a restoration project following strict rules respecting original materials and volumes. This continuous effort transformed Ostana into an "open-air alpine architecture laboratory" in Legambiente's words, which awarded it the Bandiera Verde in 2005. In 2008, Ostana joined the Borghi più belli d'Italia network.

Wander through the hamlets scattered across the slope: La Villo (the administrative center with the town hall flying the Occitan flag), Champanho surrounded by maples and ash trees, Miribrart completely restored with European funds and home to a film school founded in 2012. In the small chapel of San Bernardo, a recently uncovered medieval fresco represents a rare testament to high-mountain sacred art. The ethnographic museum I Rënèis, housed in the town hall, presents traditional ways of life in the Po valley.

→

Hike the Monviso Horizon route, a 50-kilometer loop connecting all the upper Po valley villages, also suitable for mountain biking. In winter, ski touring and snowshoeing at the foot of Monviso. Ostana is also a recognized climbing destination with summer routes and winter ice cascades.

Barolo

Top hotels
9.1 CasaCastello Barolo
9.5 La Rosa Gialla Bio Apartments & rooms
9.6 Eleìra Luxury Accomodations
9.5 Casa Colbert, Barolo

Barolo is a village of 700 souls whose name has traveled the world. Castello Falletti, with foundations dating to the tenth century, served as the residence of the powerful Falletti family until the nineteenth century. Juliette Colbert, who married Marquis Carlo Tancredi Falletti in 1807, transformed Barolo from a sweet wine into a dry, age-worthy red, then gifted 325 bottles to King Carlo Alberto of Savoy to introduce it at court - a founding gesture of its worldwide fame. In May 2015, Pope Francis recognized Juliette Colbert as Venerable in her beatification process.

The castle has housed the WiMu (Wine Museum) since September 2010, designed by French museographer François Confino. The route descends from the panoramic third floor to the ancient cellars, encountering multimedia installations that weave wine together with literature, painting, cinema, and gastronomy. The castle's ancestral cellars host the Regional Enoteca of Barolo, representing all 11 production communes of the appellation. For an unusual curiosity, the neighboring Corkscrew Museum displays hundreds of pieces from all eras.

Three kilometers away, climb to the village of La Morra (559m), nicknamed the "balcony of the Langhe." Its panoramic terrace offers one of the widest views over the hills classified as UNESCO World Heritage since 2014. Along the way, don't miss the Cappella Brunate - a small country chapel reinterpreted in contemporary art with vivid colors by two New York artists - and the majestic Cedar of Lebanon planted in 1856 before the chapel. The Big Bench Community project has installed giant benches in the surrounding vineyards, offering viewpoints at the height of the hills.

→

Barolo DOCG tasting in the village's historic cellars or at the castle's Regional Enoteca. Barolo ages a minimum of 38 months in oak (62 months for Riserva) and develops aromas of faded roses, tar, and spices. The Barolo wine festival takes place every second Sunday in September.

Monforte D Alba

Top hotels
9.3 Hotel Villa Beccaris

Monforte d'Alba - the Mons Fortis - takes its name from the ramparts that once crowned the hill at 480 meters. In 1028, Milan's Archbishop Aribert d'Intimiano besieged the village to eradicate a Cathar community. The deported population, condemned to the stake in Milan after refusing to recant, suffered an episode that preceded the Béziers massacres by two centuries. The medieval center has preserved its fan-shaped plan following the contour lines, with tortuous alleys bordered by carefully restored stone buildings.

The Auditorium Horszowski is an open-air venue carved into the hill's natural morphology - a 800-seat cavea whose exceptional acoustics are produced by the terrain configuration between the Romanesque bell tower (sole remnant of the old church demolished in the early twentieth century), the baroque oratory of Santa Elisabetta, and the walls of Palazzo Scarampi. Pianist Mieczysław Horszowski, then 91 years old, inaugurated it in 1986. Since then, the Monfortinjazz festival has welcomed internationally renowned artists every summer, from Paolo Conte to Caetano Veloso.

Palazzo Scarampi, built in the eighteenth century on the ruins of the medieval castle (itself rebuilt after 1706 by the Del Carretto family then restored in 1833), now houses the town hall. Its facade opens directly onto the auditorium and serves as a backdrop for concerts. Right beside it, the oratory of Saint Augustine and Saint Boniface presents a brick facade and a bronze portal with medieval symbols.

→

Wine walk through the UNESCO-classified Langhe vineyards (2014) - Monforte is one of the 11 Barolo DOCG production communes. Local estates offer cellar visits with tastings of Barolo, Nebbiolo, and Dolcetto d'Alba.

Guarene

9.8 Vista Langhe - CerratoHouses
9.7 Cascina Cortine

Guarene dominates the right bank of the Tanaro from a strategic position that allowed the Radicati to establish a fortress here in the Middle Ages. In the eighteenth century, Count Carlo Giacinto Roero di Guarene - friend and student of court architect Filippo Juvarra - decided to transform the fortress into a prestige residence. Work began on September 13, 1726, the date and hour recorded in the archives. The building, 25 meters high over three levels and surrounded by Italian gardens begun in 1740, is a rare example of Piedmontese baroque commissioned by an aristocrat who was himself an amateur architect.

In 1773, King Victor Amadeus III of Sardinia and the Queen visited the castle, leaving traces in the furnishings of several rooms. From the terraces, the view embraces 60 kilometers of UNESCO-classified territory - from the Langhe of Barolo to Barbaresco, the Monferrato, and the Alpine chain from the plain of Alba and beyond. Today converted into a luxury hotel and listed as a historic monument since 2011, the castle preserves all its period furniture, tapestries, and family portraits of the Roero.

On the hill of San Licerio, the Parco d'Arte presents works by Italian and international artists installed among Nebbiolo vines and woods. Born in 1975 from a local painting competition, the collection has grown to constitute a unique free open-air museum in the Roero.

→

Tasting of Roero Arneis - Piedmont's great dry white - and Roero Rosso made from Nebbiolo in local cellars. Walk through the Roero vineyards along the "Rocche," spectacular sandy cliffs carved by erosion that characterize this north side of the Tanaro.

Neive

+2
Top hotels
9.5 Borgese Camere e Suites
9.5 Ca Dei Currà

Neive - the pais di sgnuret (village of little lords) - perches in the heart of the western Langhe, divided between an old town on the hilltop and Borgonuovo below. The upper old town was the chosen residence of nobility, who left a series of aristocratic palaces and mansions in terracotta along the steep alleys. The original castle was destroyed in 1276 during a war between the communes of Asti and Alba, but remnants of the medieval ricetto partially survive.

Piazza Italia, a true eighteenth-century drawing room bordered by elegant palaces, is the village heart. The thirteenth-century Clock Tower rises here - a Roman funerary plaque embedded in its wall testifies to the continuity of occupation since Antiquity. The fortified house of the Conti Cotti di Ceres, built in the thirteenth century near this tower by a banking family, houses the text by Francesco Cotti (late seventeenth century) that constitutes one of the oldest Piedmontese writings on vine cultivation. The church of the Archconfraternity of San Michele, built in the second half of the eighteenth century by architect Giovanni Antonio Borgese, is worth visiting for its interior stuccoes.

The Casa della Donna Selvatica museum, with free entry, traces local history and presents the production of Donna Selvatica grappa, the typical brandy of the Langhe. The hills around Neive produce the sector's four great DOCG wines: Barbaresco, Barbera, Moscato d'Asti, and Dolcetto d'Alba.

→

Visit the cellars of Barbaresco (the "neighboring village" 5km away) where the Regional Enoteca of Barbaresco occupies the nave of the former church of San Donato. Barbaresco's medieval tower, 36 meters high and the tallest in Piedmont, offers in season a 360° panorama over the Alpine arc and the vines below.

Castagnole Delle Lanze

+2

Castagnole delle Lanze takes its name from the Lancia family - from the nickname Lancifero (lance-bearer) awarded to Lord Manfredo at Frederick Barbarossa's court in the twelfth century. Its history then follows that of the commune of Asti from the thirteenth century, then the Visconti in the fourteenth, finally Emanuele Filiberto of Savoy from 1573. The village sits in the UNESCO buffer zone of the Vineyard Landscapes of Langhe-Roero and Monferrato. Its vineyards primarily produce Moscato d'Asti, Barbera d'Asti, and Ruchè - a rare aromatic red wine from a grape variety typical of this zone.

Climb to the historic borgo of San Pietro, perched on the hill at 298 meters. The parish church of San Pietro in Vincoli, built in 1681 in Ligurian-Piedmontese baroque style, houses 230 carved cherubs, gilded stuccoes, and wooden altars inside - unexpectedly rich decor for a village of this size. The nineteenth-century panoramic tower, commissioned by Count Paolo Ballada di Saint Robert for his astronomical observations, dominates the Rimembranza park and offers a 360° view over the UNESCO buffer zone.

Along Via Ener Bettica, the arcades of the historic center are adorned with murals by local artist Vincenzo Piccato - thirteen arches in the colors of the seasons and wine constitute an open-air gallery on the nature of the Langhe and Monferrato. The church of the Confraternity of the Battuti Bianchi (1668), now deconsecrated, hosts exhibitions and concerts; it houses the Museum of Peasant Civilization of the Alto Monferrato.

→

Tasting of Moscato d'Asti DOCG (sweet sparkling wine) and Barbera d'Asti in local cellars. The Adopt a Row of Vines initiative, launched in 2010 by the town hall, allows visitors to adopt ten meters of Barbera and receive ten bottles of wine - unique in Italy.

Mombaldone

+2
Top hotels

9.4 La Terrazza sul Borgo
9.7 Casa Victoria

Mombaldone is the only village in the Langa Astigiana still encircled by its intact original walls, with its urban plan unchanged since foundation. The first written mention dates to 991, in the foundation act of the abbey of San Quintino in Spigno Monferrato. In 1208, the fief passed to the Del Carretto family, Marquises of Finale Ligure. In the seventeenth century, the village alone resisted Napoleonic troops - armor and muskets from French soldiers are still found in the Bormida plain - while the rest of Piedmont opened its doors to the victorious army. A once-imposing square tower, now partially ruined, still dominates the village summit.

Walk through the single main street from which branch alleys and vaulted passages, interior courtyards and portals carved from centuries-old wood. Notice the engraved lintels and stone arches testifying to unchanged medieval craftsmanship. The Palazzo Fortezza, partially inhabited by descendants of the Del Carretto family - whose name spans more than eight centuries of continuous presence - faces the main street.

Leaving the village, climb a few hundred meters toward the surrounding countryside. The calanchi are deep ravines carved by erosion in the gray sandstone of the Langa Astigiana hills - a lunar and overwhelming landscape, unique in this wine region. Herds of goats whose milk produces Robiola di Roccaverano DOP graze these slopes.

→

Hiking or cycling in the Bormida di Spigno valley, on trails crossing fields and vineyards. Canoeing on the Bormida for the more athletic, possible depending on water levels. In August, the Historia Montis Baudonis historical reenactment and classical music festival animate the village.

Volpedo is the birthplace of Giuseppe Pellizza (1868-1907), divisionist painter whose work Il Quarto Stato - The Fourth Estate - became the worldwide icon of twentieth-century workers' struggle. Pellizza spent ten years on this monumental canvas (5.45m × 2.93m), completed between 1898 and 1901: he drew inspiration from his native village's Piazza Malaspina as the backdrop for a workers' strike. Bernardo Bertolucci chose this painting for the poster of Novecento (1978). The painting is now preserved at Milan's Museo del Novecento.

The Pellizza museum circuit comprises three sites: the painter's studio (via Rosano, donated to the town hall by his daughters in 1966), the didactic museum presenting preparatory studies for major works (including three large versions of the Fourth Estate), and the Romanesque parish church of San Pietro, built in the tenth century and remodeled in the fifteenth, which holds a painting of San Luigino painted by Pellizza in 1894. Marked trails connect the three sites.

The Romanesque Pieve of San Pietro, whose square bell tower has marked Volpedo's horizon since the tenth century, houses Renaissance frescoes in its side chapels. The historic village nestles on a small hill on the right bank of the Grue torrent, around which the medieval castrum walls were built - some remnants survive at the village edge.

→

Walk through the rolling countryside of the Colli Tortonesi, production zone of Timorasso - a local white grape variety revived in the 1990s after nearly disappearing - and Barbera d'Asti. The vineyards extend to the hamlet of Vho (frazione of Tortona), 10km away.

Cella Monte

+6
Top hotels
9.1 Living Monferrato
9.2 Dimora Storica La Corte Delle 4 Stagioni
9.5 Castello Mellana
9.7 The Glass House & SPA - The Orginal One

Cella Monte is mentioned as early as 1116 in a diploma of Emperor Henry V. Its name derives either from ancient wine cellars dug into the sandstone (cella in Latin), or from cells of small medieval monasteries that existed on these hills. Cella Monte's geological peculiarity is unique: 17 million years ago, the Monferrato was a sea, and house walls are literally built with marine sandstone containing fossils and shells still visible to the naked eye. This stone, called Pietra da Cantoni, has been the exclusive building material for all local construction since Antiquity.

The infernot are hypogean cellars carved entirely into this Pietra da Cantoni, without light or ventilation, by Monferrato peasants during winters since the late eighteenth century - the era when wine began to be bottled thanks to falling glass costs. These underground storage spaces maintain constant temperature and humidity ideal for conservation. Cella Monte sits in the central zone of the UNESCO Monferrato degli Infernot site, a component of the Piedmont Vineyard Landscapes inscribed in 2014. The Ecomuseum of Pietra da Cantoni, installed in Palazzo Volta (an extraordinary fifteenth-century loggia rediscovered during restoration), opens onto the history of this unique architecture.

The parish church of Santi Quirico e Giulitta, consecrated in 1633, preserves wall frescoes, canvases, and a precious polychrome marble altar. The Casaforte, a fourteenth-century fortified dwelling, and Palazzo Radicati - home of Pietro Secondo Radicati (1671-1729), bishop of Casale Monferrato - testify to the village's seigneurial past. In May, the Le Colline sono in Fiore event transforms facades into a flower competition.

→

Guided visit of an infernot with a local producer, combined with tasting of Barbera d'Asti, Grignolino, and Freisa - the three main Monferrato wines. The first weekend of November, the White Truffle Fair of Valle Ghenza brings together producers and gourmets.

9 Appartamenti Borgo Piazza

Cocconato perches at 491 meters above the lower Astigiano, commanding valleys that extend to the Alps on clear days. Its exceptionally mild microclimate, due to protection from surrounding hills, allows palms, olive trees, and mimosas to flourish - hence the nineteenth-century nickname Riviera del Monferrato. The name likely derives from the Latin cum conatu, the effort required to climb to the summit. The counts Radicati family ruled Cocconato from the tenth to fifteenth centuries, granting it de facto quasi-autonomy.

The Torre dei Radicati, last remnant of the medieval castle rebuilt in the nineteenth century, served under Napoleon as an optical telegraph relay, then as a windmill - two successive functions engraved in the crenellated stone that make it the village symbol. The fifteenth-century Gothic Palazzo Comunale, with its elegant terracotta windows and ogival arcades, is one of the rare examples of civil Gothic style in Piedmont. A stone's throw away, the Pieve della Madonna della Neve (twelfth century), surrounded by vineyards, offers a striking panorama over the countryside.

Eight kilometers away, the abbey of Santa Maria di Vezzolano in Albugnano is a Romano-Gothic masterpiece whose legendary foundation is attributed to Charlemagne. The interior presents a carved fourteenth-century rood screen and a fresco cycle of exceptional artistic value - one of medieval Piedmont's most important monuments.

→

Every last Sunday in September, the Palio degli Asini (donkey race) celebrates a tradition dating to the Middle Ages: legend has it that donkeys carried water to extinguish a fire at the castle. In June, the Cocco7 wine and food walk offers 15km through vineyards with seven tastings. Cocconato's Robiola - soft cow's milk cheese - and local prosciutto benefit from the village's particular microclimate.

Ingria

Top hotels

9.1 La Cürt Brandon
9.3 Camere Villa Lunardini

Ingria, at 827 meters elevation in Val Soana, counts 46 permanent residents - but groups together some thirty hamlets scattered across the slopes, linked by ancient trails. This commune in Gran Paradiso National Park is one of Canavese's most isolated and best-preserved villages. The uncertain etymology suggests either the Lombard Ingrich, or the Latin ingredire, linked to the Roman bridge spanning the Soana torrent - true gateway to the valley. The local language, Valsoanin, is a variant of Arpitan (Franco-Provençal), still spoken in the hamlets.

The 1719 bell tower, standing opposite the parish church of San Giacomo, is the village symbol. The permanent outdoor exhibition Le case raccontano presents panels with old black-and-white photographs captioned in Arpitan and Italian, along the alleys. The most typical hamlets like Camprovardo (three- to four-story houses connected by exterior wooden staircases) and Pasturera (sixteenth-seventeenth century round-arch stone arcades) are discovered on foot.

→

Hike to Punta Arbella or Monte Betassa from Ingria, in Gran Paradiso National Park - one of Italy's oldest parks (1922), created to protect the ibex after Carlo Felice's 1821 hunting ban. The Soana torrent is suitable for canoeing depending on water levels. In August, Cose d'altri tempi (Tchouse d'auti ten) is a Franco-Provençal festival with music, dance, and ancient crafts.

Rosazza

+5
Top hotels
10 Colosseo Apartment
9.6 Casa Home COMPIANI Apartment
9.1 Relax a due passi dal mare dall'aeroporto"casettaStella"
9.3 B&B Il viaggio

Rosazza is a village of about a hundred inhabitants in the upper Cervo valley (province of Biella), carved from local syenite. It owes its fame to Federico Rosazza Pistolet (1813-1899), Kingdom senator, Mazzinian philanthropist, and Grand Master of Biellese freemasonry. After the deaths of his wife and daughter, he devoted the last thirty years of his life to architecturally refounding his native village, in collaboration with painter-architect Giuseppe Maffei, according to an aesthetic blending Masonic symbolism and romantic neo-Gothic style. Today Rosazza is twinned with Rennes-le-Château in France.

Decipher the symbols carved into every building: compasses and squares, five-petaled roses (lodge emblem), hourglasses, five-pointed stars, and a swastika - a Neolithic cosmic symbol used by freemasons - adorn the facade of the parish church of Saints Peter and George, built between 1876 and 1880 on a former cemetery. Its interior ceiling is painted midnight blue and scattered with thousands of stars, like an invitation to spiritual elevation. The church connects to the village by a bridge decorated with crosses and five-pointed stars.

Federico Rosazza's neo-Gothic castle, completed in 1899 the year of his death, is a labyrinth of esoteric references with its false romantic ruins, high tower, and moss-covered walls - a composition seemingly extracted from a fantasized medieval tale. The monumental cemetery, accessible via a three-arch stone bridge adorned with hourglasses, houses the Rosazza family tombs in a setting of crypts and Masonic funerary symbolism. The Palazzo Comunale, where Rosazza held his lodge meetings, still houses period decor.

→

From Rosazza, panoramic road to the Sanctuary of Oropa (one of Italy's most important Marian sanctuaries, at 891 meters) via a road built between 1889 and 1898 by Federico Rosazza himself, passing through a 367-meter gallery dug by hand. Hike to the alpine pastures and Alpe Desate.

Rassa

+3

Rassa sits at the confluence of the Sorba and Gronda torrents in Valsesia, at 917 meters elevation. Since November 2024, it has joined the Borghi più belli d'Italia circuit - the first and only village in Vercelli province to receive this distinction. The village divides into five cantons each bearing its own name. Its territory forms a special nature protection zone. The region proudly carries the nickname La valle dei tremendi (the valley of the terrible ones) - a reputation built notably by the inhabitants' resistance to the troops of Fra Dolcino and his Dulcinian disciples, medieval heretics who took refuge in these valleys around 1304 and held out against the crusade of the bishop of Vercelli for two winters.

The parish church of Santa Croce, existing since 1596 and partially rebuilt in the nineteenth century after a fire, preserves eighteenth-century frescoes by Antonio Orgiazzi - an artist who also worked at the Sacro Monte of Varallo. The single-arch stone bridge over the Sorba is described by specialists as a jewel of alpine civil engineering. The Ecomuseum of Wood in Rassa is installed in the Brasei hydraulic sawmill, built in the seventeenth century and operating until the 1980s - last survivor of a series of sawmills that once paced the village economy. The Bottega del Patel, a small carpentry workshop with its period tools, completes the visit.

→

Hike in Val Sorba and Val Gronda, both classified as special protection zones. The bike path linking Alpe di Mera to Sorbella and Rassa allows a cycling outing in an unaltered mountain landscape. Outside the village, an old lime kiln and a carbonera (wood carbonization oven) can be observed on the trail to rifugio Heidi Rassa.

Orta San Giulio spreads across a wooded peninsula on the eastern shore of Lake Orta - the smallest of northern Italy's great lakes. Friedrich Nietzsche stayed here in 1882 and walked the hills arm-in-arm with Lou Andreas-Salomé; here he began conceiving Thus Spoke Zarathustra. Piazza Motta, closed to traffic and open to the lake, is bordered by the Palazzo della Comunità della Riviera (1582) whose first floor houses a baroque fresco representing the Virgin with Saints Francis and Giulio. The alleys that suddenly open onto the lake or interior courtyards form a preserved medieval labyrinth. The village ranks among the Borghi più belli d'Italia.

The island of San Giulio, 400 meters offshore (275m long, 140m wide), is reached by boat from Piazza Motta. Legend has it that the Greek missionary Giulio, wanting to build his hundredth church here in 390 AD, crossed the lake on his cloak, drove out the serpents and dragons infesting the island, and founded the edifice where he was buried. The Romanesque basilica of San Giulio, with foundations dating to the fourth century, houses salvaged ancient columns and medieval frescoes. The Benedictine abbey Mater Ecclesiae - a cloistered community - occupies most of the island. The perimeter path is marked by the Via del Silenzio and the Via della Meditazione.

The sanctuary of Madonna del Sasso, perched west of the lake, offers a panorama over the entire body of water.

The Sacro Monte of Orta, inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage list in 2003, rises on the wooded hill dominating the village at 400 meters elevation. Commissioned by Capuchin friar architect Padre Cleto, the sanctuary was built between 1590 and 1760: it comprises 20 chapels decorated with frescoes and adorned with more than 900 life-size painted terracotta statues illustrating the life of Saint Francis of Assisi - from birth to canonization. It's the only alpine Sacro Monte entirely dedicated to a single saint. The church of San Nicolao at the summit, built in 1602-1607 modeled on the lower basilica of Assisi, houses a fourteenth-century wooden Pietà at the origin of local worship.

Back in the village, Via Bossi (n°11), the seventeenth-century Villa of the same name hides a coffered ceiling adorned with frescoes and a lakeside garden. The parish church of Santa Maria Assunta (fifteenth century, facade remodeled in 1940) presents a three-nave interior covered with seventeenth-eighteenth century frescoes. The Torre di Buccione, an isolated medieval tower in the woods on the eastern shore, is accessible on foot from the panoramic road. To the north, the Sanctuary of Madonna del Sasso (eighteenth century) dominates the wild western shore and offers a panorama over the entire body of water.

→

Complete circuit of Lake Orta by car or bike (40km panoramic road). Swimming in the lake waters from Gozzano beach to the south. Garden enthusiasts can visit the villas and botanical gardens dotting the shores.

Vogogna appears in a parchment dated 970 and preserves much older traces: a Celtic steatite mask from the third century BC (the Mascherone Celtico) and a Roman inscription commemorating the construction of the Ossola road in 196 BC are displayed in Palazzo Pretorio. Vogogna reached its apex in the fourteenth century: in 1348, Giovanni Visconti, bishop of Novara and lord of Milan, made it the administrative capital of Lower Ossola, rival to Domodossola for Upper Ossola.

Castello Visconteo, commissioned in 1348 by Giovanni Visconti, dominates the village with its semi-circular round tower. Its irregular plan follows the terrain's orography and its battlements still evoke the defensive role the duke assigned it to bar Swiss access to the lakes. In 1798, after three centuries under the Borromeo family, the castle passed to the municipality which used it as a prison until the nineteenth century. Restored from 1990, it now hosts permanent exhibitions on the Celts, Leonardo da Vinci, and the wolf's return.

Palazzo Pretorio (1348), with ogival arches on squat columns in the Lombard broletto tradition, was the seat of Lower Ossola government until 1819. It still preserves traces of original painted decoration, including the Visconti coat of arms on the facade. The mule trail Il respiro della storia starts from the castle, climbs to the hamlet of Genestredo with its oratory of San Martino, then to la Rocca (eleventh century) - a medieval watchtower from which the view over the Ossola valley and the Alps is striking.

→

Hiking in Val Grande National Park - Italy's largest wilderness area, over 70,000 hectares without any permanent human infrastructure in the park's core. Marked trails from Vogogna cross the primeval forests. The Vogogna-Premosello geological trail, marked by the Park, descends to Premosello station. In November, the Montagna e Dintorni festival animates the valley's cultural life for ten days.
