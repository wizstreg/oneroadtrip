Sicily's Island Circuit: Aeolian, Aegadian & Pantelleria in 14 Days

Seven volcanic islands, three turquoise-water gems, one black island between Sicily and Africa — the entire Sicilian archipelago, car-free, in fourteen stages.

Depart from Milazzo to Lipari, hub of the Aeolian Islands, then journey through Vulcano's sulfurous mud baths, verdant Salina, chic Panarea, eruptive Stromboli, and remote Filicudi and Alicudi. Return to Sicily, then ferry to Favignana, Levanzo with its 10,000 BC prehistoric cave paintings, and Marettimo. Finish at Pantelleria, 70 km from Africa, with its Arab Dammusi and DOC Passito wine.

Key highlights (4)
  • Stromboli: eruptions every 20 minutes, night ascent to the summit
  • Alicudi: no roads, no cars, 2,000 steps from the port to the summit
  • Favignana: Cala Rossa, tuff caves, and Grotta del Genovese on excursion from Favignana
  • Pantelleria: Arab Dammusi, 40°C volcanic lake, DOC Passito wine

Lipari

Lipari, the largest Aeolian island with 10,000 residents, serves as the transportation hub for the entire archipelago. Its citadel crowns a 40-meter obsidian rock that has witnessed Greek settlement (580 BC), Roman fortification, Arab conquest, Norman rule, and Spanish occupation. The current ramparts date from the 16th century, erected under Charles V.

The Aeolian Archaeological Museum, housed within the citadel walls, holds the Mediterranean's most comprehensive collection of prehistoric and ancient island artifacts. Its obsidian trade section reveals Neolithic-era commerce routes stretching from Lipari's volcanic glass quarries to Provence and Spain — evidence of the first maritime trade networks in the Inner Sea.

The Quattrocchi belvedere, 3 km from the port, frames the Aeolians' most photographed vista: twin rock arches rising from the sea with Vulcano's smoking cone on the horizon. The name references a local tradition where couples come to see with "four eyes rather than two."

→

Tasting of Malvasia delle Lipari DOC at island cellars — a naturally sweet wine made from grapes dried on straw mats, cited in Homer's Odyssey and among the Mediterranean's most ancient wines still produced today.

Lipari's pumice quarries on the island's northern flank supplied volcanic stone throughout the Mediterranean for millennia. White pumice cliffs plunge directly into the sea — since the 2000s, extraction has been regulated to preserve the dramatic landscape.

Spiaggia Bianca, a beach of white pumice sand and pebbles at the quarry base, lies a 30-minute walk from Canneto village. Its shallow, crystal-clear waters are perfect for snorkeling among obsidian rock formations.

→

Hike to Spiaggia Valle Muria, a secluded beach of black lava sand and white pumice accessible only on foot (1h30 one-way) or by boat. One of the archipelago's most pristine snorkeling spots.

Vulcano's Gran Cratere, the summit of a stratovolcano at 391 meters, last erupted in 1890 but continuously vents bright yellow sulfur fumaroles. The 500-meter-wide crater releases plumes visible from the sea. The ascent from Porto Ponente takes 45 minutes up a trail of ash and consolidated lava.

The Laghetto di Fanghi, a pool of gray-yellow mud heated to 35°C by geothermal springs, is the Aeolians' most popular therapeutic site. Bathers come for the sulfurous clay's renowned dermatological properties.

→

Thermal bathing in the warm waters of Porto Levante, where underwater hot springs heat the sea to 40-50°C between the rocks. Free and accessible on foot from the port.

→

Bike rental for the island circuit (17 km) to the Belvedere del Gelso in the south, facing Filicudi.

Salina, the greenest Aeolian island with its twin extinct volcanic cones — Monte Fossa delle Felci (962 m) and Monte dei Porri (860 m) — takes its name from ancient salt pans. Its dense vegetation of capers, vines, and fig trees contrasts sharply with neighboring islands' arid landscapes.

The Pollara cove on the northwest coast served as the natural set for Michael Radford's film Il Postino (The Postman, 1994). Its volcanic tuff cliffs plunge into emerald waters, creating one of the Mediterranean's most stunning natural amphitheaters.

Lingua village in the south is the production center for Salina IGP capers — considered Italy's finest by top chefs — cultivated on volcanic terraces since antiquity.

→

Tasting granita alle more di gelso (mulberry sorbet) and Malvasia delle Lipari DOC at Santa Marina's cafés — specialties exclusive to Salina.

Monte Fossa delle Felci (962 m) is the Aeolian Islands' highest peak. The ascent from Santa Marina through vineyards and giant ferns (felci) takes 2 hours to reach a 360° panorama of all seven islands. On clear days, the Calabrian coast appears on the horizon.

→

Swimming at Rinella, the black sand beach in the village of the same name on Salina's south coast, accessible by local bus from Santa Marina.

The prehistoric village at Capo Milazzese, perched on an eastern promontory, dates to the Bronze Age (15th-13th century BC). Its 23 elliptical dry-stone huts rank among the Mediterranean's best-preserved prehistoric settlements.

Cala Junco, a cove accessible on foot (20 minutes from the port), forms a natural pool enclosed by granite and obsidian cliffs. Its intense blue-green water makes it Panarea's most photographed beach.

→

Boat excursion around the satellite islets — Basiluzzo, Lisca Bianca, Dattilo, and Bottaro. The islets harbor underwater thermal springs where the sea bubbles between the rocks.

Stromboli

Top hotels

9.5 La Rosamarina

Stromboli, in continuous eruption for 2,000 years, ranks among the world's most active volcanoes. Its summit eruptions — every 15 to 30 minutes — hurl lava bombs down the Sciara del Fuoco, a scree slope plunging directly into the sea. Ancient mariners called it the "Lighthouse of the Mediterranean" because its glowing eruptions served as a navigation beacon.

Ginostra, a hamlet of 30 permanent residents on the island's southwest flank, is accessible only by sea and claims Europe's smallest port — a natural cleft that fills with just two boats. No roads, no cars, no ATMs.

→

Night ascent of Stromboli (924 m) with mandatory licensed guide above 400 meters: departure at 5 PM, summit arrival at 8 PM to observe eruptions after dark, descent at 11 PM via the Sciara del Fuoco.

→

Swimming at Ficogrande black sand beach, 10 minutes from the port, where volcanic lava sand retains heat until nightfall.

Filicudi, a 9.5 km² island with 200 permanent residents, remains one of the least touristy Aeolian islands. Its agricultural terraces, abandoned in the 1950s, now bloom with wild capers and fig trees. A single road connects the port to Valdichiesa at 390 meters elevation.

The Grotta del Bue Marino (Sea Ox Cave, named for the now-extinct Mediterranean monk seal), accessible only by sea, is a 35-meter-deep marine cavern whose walls reflect turquoise water in shimmering patterns.

→

Hike to Monte Fossa Felci di Filicudi (773 m) from the port: 2h30 round-trip on a trail through terraced vineyards and caper plants, with views of Alicudi and Salina.

9
Day

Alicudi

Alicudi, the most isolated Aeolian island with 100 permanent residents, has no roads, no cars, no motorcycles — only steep trails and donkeys for transport. Electricity arrived only in 1990, telephones in 1991.

Monte Filo dell'Arpa (675 m), accessible via 2,000 steps carved into the rock from the port, is the only way to reach the agricultural terraces. The climb takes 2 hours and offers views of all the Aeolian Islands.

→

Snorkeling and swimming in the port waters, among the clearest in the archipelago thanks to the absence of pollution and maritime traffic.

Favignana

Favignana, the main Aegadian island, witnessed the Battle of the Aegates in 241 BC — antiquity's first decisive naval battle, where Rome defeated Carthage at sea for the first time. Since 2005, 200 bronze rams from sunken warships have been recovered from the seabed.

The yellow tuff quarries, excavated since the 16th century, have created a unique landscape of canyons and vertical terraces. Some have been transformed into hanging gardens; others, now flooded, form natural swimming pools.

Cala Rossa, a cove with red-orange waters caused by iron oxides in the submerged tuff rocks, is Favignana's most spectacular swimming spot. Accessible by bike from the port (4 km).

→

Bike rental for the island circuit (27 km): flat, well-marked route connecting Cala Azzurra, Cala Rossa, and Cala Rotonda. Favignana is flat and bans motorcycles in high season.

The Ex-Stabilimento Florio, a 19th-century industrial tuna processing plant converted into a museum, documents the mattanza — the ritual bluefin tuna fishing practiced since Arab times. The labyrinth of nets, the rais (fishing chief) ritual, and period tools are preserved through archival audiovisual materials.

→

Ferry excursion to Levanzo (20 min, Liberty Lines) for the morning: visit the Grotta del Genovese with its 10,000 BC prehistoric frescoes — the Mediterranean's most important cave paintings. Afternoon return.

→

Snorkeling at Cala Rotonda, east of Favignana, with seagrass meadows and visibility up to 25 meters.

Marettimo

Marettimo, the westernmost Aegadian island, is the wildest. Its 800 residents live by fishing and low-key tourism. No roads — only marked trails — the island is explored on foot. The water clarity reaches 35-40 meters visibility.

The Castello di Marettimo, a 9th-century Arab fortress expanded by the Normans in the 11th century, houses a small 11th-century Norman church in original condition — one of Sicily's oldest and least-known Christian edifices.

→

Boat tour of the sea caves — Grotta del Cammello, Grotta della Bombarda, Grotta Perciata — whose limestone ceilings are pierced with light shafts illuminating emerald waters.

Pantelleria, a volcanic island 70 km from Tunisia, is nicknamed "the Black Pearl of the Mediterranean" for its basalt rock. Its Dammusi — domed stone dwellings inherited from 9th-century Arab architecture — collect rainwater and maintain coolness through 90 cm-thick walls.

Specchio di Venere (Venus's Mirror), a crater lake at 836 meters elevation, reaches 40°C in geothermal zones. Its gray-green silt, used as a body mask since antiquity, is harvested directly by bathers.

The Mursia prehistoric site includes a Bronze Age village (2000-1000 BC) and a sesi — a funerary monument unique to Pantelleria, built of dry-stacked volcanic stone, whose function remains debated by archaeologists.

→

Bathing at the Thermae San Calogero, natural 60°C geothermal springs in a black stone cave on the north coast, used since the Bronze Age.

→

Tasting Passito di Pantelleria DOC, a sweet white wine produced from Zibibbo grapes sun-dried in July — the only Sicilian wine awarded the grand prize by the International Union of Oenologists.

Cala Gadir on the northeast coast is a small black rock cove where underwater geothermal springs heat the sea to 30-35°C. Accessible on foot from Gadir village in 10 minutes.

Montagna Grande, the island's highest point at 836 meters, offers views of the Tunisian coast on clear days from its hiking trail. The Mediterranean forest of kermes oak and mastic that covers it is among the most intact in the central Mediterranean.

→

Island circuit by scooter (50 km, circular coastal road) to reach Cala dei Cinque Denti — a cove with five finger-like lava rocks emerging from the sea, accessible on foot via a 20-minute trail.
