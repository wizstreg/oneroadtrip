Verdun

Descend into 7 kilometers of galleries carved beneath Vauban's citadel at the end of the 19th century. An augmented reality headset puts you in the boots of a 1916 soldier: discover the ovens that produced 28,000 rations daily and the ardent chapel where Corporal Thin designated the Unknown Soldier on November 10, 1920.

The World Center for Peace occupies an 18th-century episcopal palace, partially destroyed in 1916 and later restored. Its temporary exhibitions explore Franco-German relations and human rights. The formal French garden at the rear offers an open view over the city.

Step inside Notre-Dame Cathedral, built from the 10th century onward. Its Romanesque-Gothic blend houses a gilded baroque baldachin, restored after the Great War bombardments. Modern stained glass filters colored light into the nave.

Fort Douaumont, the most powerful structure in the fortified complex, fell to German hands on February 25, 1916. Walk through the command room, chapel, combat galleries, and inscriptions soldiers left on the walls. Interior temperature hovers around 7 to 10°C.

The Douaumont Ossuary, inaugurated in 1932, rises like a gigantic shell above 15,000 white crosses. Its 46-meter tower dominates the battlefield. Through the oculi in the base, you glimpse the remains of 130,000 unidentified French and German soldiers.

Renovated in 2016 for the centenary, the Verdun Memorial displays over 2,000 authentic objects at the former station of the destroyed village of Fleury-devant-Douaumont. A 100-square-meter shattered screen reconstructs the battlefield's convulsions. From the top-floor terraces, the panorama reveals scars still visible beneath the forest.

The Trench of Bayonets preserves the memory of June 12, 1916: according to legend, a company of the 137th regiment was buried alive by bombardment, their bayonets left stuck in the ground. A sober monument has protected the site since 1920.

→

Walk the Meuse quays at dusk, between stone bridges and facades rebuilt after 1918. The reflection of lights on the river offers a soothing contrast after visiting the battlefields.

→

Follow the Sacred Way, the former supply route that linked Bar-le-Duc to Verdun during the battle. Milestones carved with poilu helmets mark the 56-kilometer historic route.

→

Pass through the Porte Chaussée, a 14th-century fortified gate that was part of the city's original ramparts. Used as a prison from 1755 to 1860, it marks the entrance to old Verdun.

→

The Princerie Museum, housed in a 16th-century mansion, brings together archaeological and artistic collections spanning from prehistory to the 19th century. Sacred art enthusiasts will find remarkable pieces here.

→

Visit Fleury-devant-Douaumont, a village taken and retaken 16 times in two months. Signs indicate the location of the town hall, school, and church. It's one of nine villages classified as "died for France."

Perched on a rocky spur of the Meuse Heights, Hattonchâtel village overlooks the Woëvre plain by 80 meters. Fortified in the 9th century by Hatton, Bishop of Verdun, it preserves the 12th-14th century vaulted house, the 14th-century arcaded house, and a 1921 washhouse inspired by medieval and Japanese design.

The Saint-Maur collegiate church houses the Hattonchâtel altarpiece (1523), attributed to sculptor Ligier Richier. This polychrome stone triptych depicts three Passion of Christ scenes in a setting borrowed from the Italian Renaissance: scroll pilasters, interlaced motifs, and basket-handle arches.

The 14th-century cloister, of which only one gallery remains, adjoins the church. Stained glass windows by Jacques Grüber, Nancy master glassmaker, illuminate the nave with colored reflections. The 16th-century calvary outside completes this ensemble classified as Historic Monuments.

The castle, rebuilt in the 1920s thanks to American patron Belle Skinner, blends medieval fantasy with Norman checkerboard stonework. A private property housing a hotel, it can be visited by appointment. From the esplanade, the panorama extends over the Meuse Heights, mirabelle orchards, and Lake Madine.

Climb to the Montsec Butte (375 m) to discover the American memorial erected in honor of troops who reduced the Saint-Mihiel salient in September 1918. The bronze orientation table and circular colonnade offer a 360° panorama over the Woëvre plain and Lake Madine.

→

Cycle around Lake Madine: a 20-kilometer bike path circles this large body of water ideal for water sports, swimming, and observing migratory birds.

→

Wander through Hattonchâtel's lanes following the arcaded street. Bucolic gardens border village houses and viewpoints offer views over the vineyards and orchards of the northern Meuse Heights.

→

Want to taste the terroir? Producers of the Meuse Heights offer gray, white, and red wines from Lorraine grape varieties with tangy and fruity aromas.

Pass through the Porte de France, a medieval remnant through which Joan of Arc left Vaucouleurs on February 23, 1429, to join Dauphin Charles VII at Chinon. Over 13 meters high with 2-meter-thick walls, it's the last of the four gates in the fortified enclosure.

Climb to the castle chapel on the town heights. The crypt houses the statue of Notre-Dame des Voûtes, before which Joan came to pray. The 26 stained glass windows from the Nancy workshop Höner, Benoît et Janin trace Joan's epic journey.

The Joan of Arc Museum, housed in the Town Hall wing, preserves over 300 pieces: drawings, prints, stained glass cartoons, bronze sculptures, and posters. The museum's four sections explore Joan's different symbolisms - virgin, warrior, and martyr.

Next to the Porte de France, the Pagis Towers testify to the city's fortified past. The first castle, built in 1026 by Étienne de Vaux, had 17 rampart towers. Only the King's Tower and the English Tower survive in the lower town.

The 1848 Town Hall contains a monumental painting by Scherrer (1886) depicting Joan's departure, and six Aubusson tapestries inaugurated in 1990 illustrating six stages of Joan's epic.

→

Follow the discovery circuit of Joan of Arc heritage through Vaucouleurs streets: from the Porte de France to the linden tree where Joan reportedly rested, each stop features explanatory panels.

→

Saturday's weekly market brings together local producers and artisans. Find mirabelles, jams, cheeses, and charcuterie from the Meuse terroir.

→

Stroll along the Meuse valley. The communal forest surrounding Vaucouleurs offers shaded trails for a nature break between historical visits.

Château Stanislas de Commercy, classified as a Historic Monument, was embellished by the Prince of Vaudémont then by Stanislas Leszczynski, former King of Poland and Louis XV's father-in-law. Architects Boffrand and Dorbay gave it its current appearance. Partly destroyed in 1944, it was identically rebuilt from 1957 to 1977.

Cross the semicircular Place du Fer à Cheval, bordered by two symmetrical buildings extending the castle. The stables redesigned by architect Héré recall the pavilions of Place Stanislas in Nancy: continuous rustication, semicircular bays, and balustrades adorned with trophies.

Saint-Pantaléon church blends a 17th-century nave with a 19th-century choir in Champagne Gothic style. Its sober interior contrasts with the richness of liturgical furnishings.

The Museum of Ceramics and Ivory is housed in the former municipal bathhouses in Art Deco style (1930), inspired by Stanislas's royal pavilion. It holds one of France's most important ivory collections, from the 17th to 19th centuries, complemented by porcelains and earthenware.

Place Charles de Gaulle houses the former Town Hall built under Stanislas. Nearby, a 1903 pharmacy with Art Nouveau decor testifies to the town's elegance at the turn of the century.

→

Step into an artisanal madeleine factory to watch the making of these small shell-shaped cakes, born in the kitchens of Stanislas's castle in the 18th century. Tasting on site.

💡 Free self-guided visit, production visible through a window
→

Walk along the Meuse following the Promenade Stanislas, which crosses the town's different districts within 18th-century boundaries. The river draws meanders bordered by willows and classical architecture.

→

Visit the Priory of Breuil, founded in the 11th century, which occupies an important place in Commercy's religious history. Guided tours trace its successive transformations.

Saint-Étienne church preserves Ligier Richier's absolute masterpiece: the Sepulcher, composed of thirteen figures slightly larger than life, carved from Euville stone between 1554 and 1564. The dead Christ, carried by Nicodemus and Joseph of Arimathea, is surrounded by Mary Magdalene, the Virgin, and the Holy Women. Classified as a historic monument as early as 1840.

The Benedictine Library, on the second floor of the former abbatial palace, is considered one of France's five most beautiful libraries. Its two architecturally remarkable rooms contain 8,780 books, including 74 manuscripts from the 9th to 16th centuries and 86 incunabula. Some volumes still bear the scars of a 1915 shell.

Saint-Michel abbey church, founded in the 8th century, preserves a Romanesque tower-porch. Inside, Ligier Richier's Swoon of the Virgin - two life-size walnut wood figures - shows Mary fainting in Saint John's arms after Christ's death.

The Museum of Sacred Art, in the monks' former refectory, gathers religious objects, paintings, and sculptures from the 13th to 19th centuries deposited by departmental communes. The Saint Elisabeth attributed to Ligier Richier is an exceptionally refined piece.

Walk through Saint-Mihiel's Renaissance streets: the Gondrecourt mansion with its gargoyles representing a ram, dog, and lion; the Bousmard mansion (1520); Ligier Richier's house; the 16th-century Café des Arcades. An open-air museum of Lorraine architecture.

→

Hike to the Seven Rocks, limestone cliffs over twenty meters high overlooking the Meuse valley. The 7-kilometer trail crosses forests and rock formations with panoramic viewpoints.

→

The Meuse Greenway follows the river: a bike and pedestrian path crossing several picturesque villages. Bike rental shops offer equipment suitable for the whole family.

→

Taste local specialties: croquets and rochers de Saint-Mihiel, traditional dry cookies that accompany Lorraine coffee.

Bar-le-Duc's Upper Town district constitutes one of France's most remarkable Renaissance urban ensembles. Clinging to a rocky promontory, it unfolds mansions with sculpted pediments, fluted pilasters, and 16th-century gargoyles along Place Saint-Pierre and rue des Ducs de Bar.

Step through the doors of Saint-Étienne church in Flamboyant Gothic style. It houses Ligier Richier's Transi, a macabre sculpture depicting a flayed skeleton holding its heart toward heaven - one of the most striking works of the Lorraine Renaissance, commissioned for René de Chalon's funerary monument.

The Barrois Museum occupies remnants of the former castle of the Dukes of Bar and Lorraine. Nicknamed the "little Louvre," it presents archaeological objects from ancient Nasium, paintings from the Middle Ages to the 20th century, weapons and armor, and works by Ligier Richier and Ipoustéguy.

The Clock Tower, one of the rare remnants of 10th-century medieval fortifications, dominates the upper district. Circular at its construction, it received a clock oriented toward castle inhabitants in 1381. Its arrow slits and embrasures recall its defensive purpose.

The Gilles de Trèves college, built from 1573 to educate the duchy of Bar's youth, is a remarkable example of Renaissance school architecture. Its ornate roofs are discovered during a walk around the ramparts, between Upper and Lower Town.

Head to Lower Town and rue du Bourg, the city's Gallo-Roman cradle. Saint-Antoine and Notre-Dame churches preserve stained glass that, in the evening, shine with vivid colors.

→

Discover the centuries-old know-how of seeding currants with a goose quill, an artisanal tradition attested since 1344. Local jam makers perpetuate this technique that gives "Bar caviar" its incomparable texture.

💡 Tasting available on site
→

Stroll along the Ornain, the river crossing Bar-le-Duc. The bridges, squares laid out in the 18th century, and boulevard de la Rochelle reveal another facet of the town, more intimate and bucolic.

→

Visit the Sacred Way's zero milestone, the historic starting point of the supply route to Verdun during the 1916 battle. The monument recalls Bar-le-Duc's strategic role as a rear-front zone.

Climb Dun-Haut hill (81 m above the town), an ancient Gallic oppidum then Roman castrum. Notre-Dame de Bonne Garde church, built in 1346 by the lords of Apremont, is the only surviving monument from the old fortress. This Gothic jewel possesses a sumptuous 18th-century organ case and furnishings.

Inside the church, two monumental sculptures by contemporary artist Ipoustéguy, native of Dun-sur-Meuse, surprise with their expressionism: Death of Bishop Neumann (1976) and a tormented Christ. The church is part of the European "Open Churches" network.

Walk through remnants of ramparts built from the 10th century. A round tower and a square tower survive, along with the 1402 Porte des Chevaux. From the church square, the view plunges over the Meuse valley, the river's meanders, and surrounding fields.

In the lower town at Doulcon, the Ipoustéguy cultural center exhibits marbles and bronzes by this sculptor who won the Bright Award at the Venice Biennale in 1964 and installed a monumental bronze on the Berlin Congress Palace forecourt in 1978.

Five kilometers away, Mont-devant-Sassey's 12th-century Romanesque church stands beside the Meuse in a majestic setting. Its young heritage ambassadors offer passionate summer tours.

→

Follow the Dun-le-Chastel ramparts circuit, a one-hour pedestrian walk punctuated with explanatory panels between archaeology, heritage, and gardens. Medieval remains blend with valley panoramas.

→

The "Old Meuse and Green Lake" green circuit winds along ponds and the old Meuse. The Natura 2000 classified zone shelters remarkable fauna and flora, between gray herons and water lilies.

→

Lake Dun offers supervised summer swimming and daily boat rentals. The marina allows peaceful navigation on the water body.

The European Museum of Beer occupies the former provisions store of Stenay citadel, transformed into a malthouse in the 19th century. Labeled "Museum of France" and listed as Historic Monuments, it displays 55,000 objects across 2,500 square meters. Six sequences trace brewing history from Mesopotamian origins (12,000 years ago) to the present day.

The museum's highlight: the advertising posters room reveals how graphic art served brewers. It notably features a lithograph by Czech artist Alfons Mucha (1897), created for Bières de la Meuse of Bar-le-Duc, in Art Nouveau style.

Stenay's Saint-Joseph church, rebuilt after Great War destruction, stands out for its sober architecture. The city's coat of arms, adorned with a devil, recalls this former stronghold of the Counts of Bar's rich past.

→

The museum's botanical garden presents Lorraine's only hop garden, surrounded by aromatics and spices used in brewing. The visit stimulates sight, smell, and touch.

→

End at the museum tavern for a tasting of craft beers with top, bottom, or spontaneous fermentation. Local specialties accompany the glasses: beer sausage, cheese, and Stenay madeleines.

💡 Three-beer tasting available by reservation
→

Hop on a bike along the Meuse Greenway, a cycle path part of EuroVelo 19. The section from Stenay follows the river through a Natura 2000 zone rich in birds and valley-floor landscapes.

Montmédy citadel, erected in 1545 by Charles V to defend the Spanish Netherlands, is one of northeastern France's vastest fortresses with over 1.5 kilometers of walls. Conquered by Louis XIV's troops in 1657, it was remodeled by Vauban who added bastions, demi-lunes, and a second ring of fortifications.

At the upper town's heart, the Fortification Museum presents the evolution of Meuse defense systems from prehistory: scale models of works, ancient weapons, aerial views of fortified sites. Maps and reconstructions help understand attack and defense strategies through the centuries.

The Jules Bastien-Lepage Museum, housed in the citadel's former school, exhibits drawings, prints, and paintings by this 19th-century Meuse artist from Damvillers, who painted rural life scenes with realism influenced by the Impressionists.

Upper town lanes twist around small squares where artists' workshops animate the citadel's cultural life. The upper town church houses a remarkable organ and temporary exhibitions in certain casemates transformed into galleries.

Fifteen kilometers away, Avioth basilica is a Gothic marvel rising in the middle of a small village. A highly visited pilgrimage site, it surprises with its dimensions and sculpture finesse. Don't miss the "Recevresse," a structure unique in France designed to receive offerings.

→

Follow the ramparts circuit, a marked route of about 1 kilometer circling the fortifications. Explanatory panels, videos, and quizzes trace the citadel's history. The 360° panorama embraces the Meuse countryside to the Belgian border.

→

In summer, torchlight visits transform the citadel at nightfall. Underground galleries, artillery casemates, and caponnières take on a striking atmosphere by torchlight.

→

Make a detour to Marville, 10 kilometers away, a village known for its Renaissance architecture and Spanish-style houses. The cemetery and church merit a stop for old stone enthusiasts.
