The Most Beautiful Villages of León Province

The pallozas of Balouta have roofs woven from rye thatch. The streets of Castrillo de los Polvazares have been paved since the 17th century. León Cathedral holds 737 stained-glass windows across 1,765 square meters. The Romans moved 500 million cubic meters of earth to extract gold from Las Médulas. León is a province on a human scale—and that's why it stays with you.

A loop from León through three worlds: medieval El Bierzo with Villafranca and its church of indulgences, the wild Ancares where hamlets of prehistoric pallozas survive at 1,000 meters elevation, and La Maragatería where Castrillo de los Polvazares has frozen the era of the great muleteers in its stone streets. With the climax being the 10th-century Mozarabic church of Peñalba de Santiago in the Valle del Silencio, and Gaudí's palace in Astorga.

Key highlights (4)
  • Balouta: Ancares hamlet with prehistoric pallozas
  • Peñalba de Santiago: 10th-century Mozarabic church, jewel of the Valle del Silencio
  • Castrillo de los Polvazares: muleteer village frozen in 17th-century architecture
  • León: cathedral with 737 stained-glass windows and Romanesque Royal Pantheon of San Isidoro

León Cathedral—affectionately called the 'Pulchra Leonina'—was built starting in 1205 atop the ruins of 2nd-century Roman baths, which King Ordoño II had earlier transformed into a royal palace in the 10th century. What sets it apart from every other cathedral in Spain is its stained glass: 737 individual panels covering 1,765 square meters, created between the 13th and 20th centuries. This is the largest collection of medieval stained glass in Europe after Chartres. Visit late morning—that's when sunlight streams through the main rose window, flooding the naves in red, blue, and gold. Its twin Gothic towers soar to 65 and 68 meters.

Just steps from the cathedral, the Basilica of San Isidoro ranks as the most important Romanesque complex in Spain. King Fernando I and Queen Sancha had the relics of Saint Isidore of Seville transferred here in 1063—the remains of this Visigothic Church Father have rested here ever since. The Royal Pantheon, burial place of eleven kings of León and several queens, is adorned with 12th-century frescoes so perfectly preserved they've earned it the nickname 'the Sistine Chapel of Romanesque art.' Legend has it that Jimena, wife of El Cid Campeador, came here to attend Mass. The museum also houses the Nodicia de Kesos, considered the earliest written text in Spanish.

Walk along the 3rd-century Roman walls on Avenida de los Cubos—their semicircular towers, known as 'cube towers,' are the best preserved in Spain and have been protected since 1931. The wall originally encircled the Roman camp in a perfect quadrilateral. Then make your way to Casa Botines, one of the few buildings Gaudí designed outside Catalonia, commissioned in 1891 by textile merchants from León and now a museum.

→

The Barrio Húmedo is León's beating heart. This labyrinth of medieval lanes around Plaza de San Martín earned its name—the 'Wet Quarter'—from the countless wine cellars and taverns that have packed it for centuries. The rule is simple: every drink comes with a free tapa. Midday belongs to vermouth, evenings to lively bars. Must-tries: morcilla de León (blood sausage), cecina (cured beef), caldo gallego (Galician soup), and botillo del Bierzo. The neighboring Plaza Mayor, rebuilt after a 1654 fire, has hosted a market every Wednesday and Saturday since 1660.

The Valporquero Caves, 45 km north of León, rank among the most spectacular in the Iberian Peninsula. This 3,300-meter network of underground galleries carved into Carboniferous limestone harbors giant stalactites, translucent draperies, and underground lakes with turquoise reflections. The grand chamber—the Sala de las Maravillas—soars over 20 meters high. Guided tours last about 75 minutes. Unlike the Collegiate Church of Arbas, the caves stay open year-round except January and February.

Back in León, don't miss the San Marcos Convent: its 16th-century Renaissance façade stretches 100 meters and is covered in scallop shells sculpted in bas-relief—one of the most sumptuous in Spain. This was the headquarters of the Order of Santiago, charged with protecting pilgrims on the Camino. The main façade is freely accessible; the interior now houses a Parador, but you can enter the cloister and adjacent church.

Right next door, the Palacio de los Guzmanes is León's most elegant Renaissance palace, commissioned in the 16th century by the Guzmán family from architect Rodrigo Gil de Hontañón. Its two-story interior courtyard gallery is remarkably understated. The provincial government offers free guided tours—inquire at the entrance.

→

Wander the lanes of the Barrio Romántico, north of Calle Ancha. Less touristy than the Barrio Húmedo but equally authentic, it's packed with excellent restaurants and some of the city's finest wine bars. This is the best place to dine with locals.

Balouta is the last village in León province before the borders with Galicia and Asturias converge. With about twenty inhabitants, this Ancares hamlet preserves the highest concentration of pallozas in the entire region—those circular stone houses with thatched rye roofs that look like giant haystacks. Pallozas date back at least to the Middle Ages; their round form helped them withstand the winds and snows of these mountains over 1,000 meters up. Some once sheltered both people and animals under the same roof, the livestock's body heat warming the dwelling. One restored palloza now serves as a bar—make sure to go inside to see the interior. Book your casa rural well in advance; accommodations are extremely scarce.

The neighboring village of Suarbol, 4 km away, has only seven permanent residents (about thirty in summer) and also preserves pallozas. The road between the two villages follows the valley floor of the Ancares, flanked by slopes thick with oaks and chestnuts. You'll also find a small church dedicated to San Justo, with a noble coat of arms on one of the neighboring façades—one of the rare witnesses to the valley's former prosperity.

→

Hiking in the Ancares Biosphere Reserve. Marked trails from Balouta lead to the brañas—high-altitude pastures—where views sweep across multiple valleys simultaneously. Wildlife is remarkable in this seldom-visited area: brown bears (presence confirmed), wolves, deer, and remarkable bird density. Bring proper hiking boots and water—there are no services outside the village.

→

Watch the sunset from the heights above Balouta. The late-day light on the rye-thatched palloza roofs and green slopes creates one of the Ancares' most striking images. Bring a flashlight for the return.

Villafranca del Bierzo owes its name to the French pilgrims who settled here in the 11th century at King Alfonso VI's invitation—'villa francorum' became Villafranca. The Cluniac monks the king brought from Occitania founded Santa María de Cluniaco and introduced viticulture to the valley. The Romanesque church of Santiago, built between the 12th and 13th centuries, is one of only two in the world—along with the Cathedral of Santiago de Compostela—to possess a Puerta del Perdón (Door of Pardon): pilgrims who could not physically continue received here the same indulgences as at Compostela. Pope Callixtus III granted this extraordinary privilege, acknowledging that the climb to the Puerto de Cebreiro was the Camino's most formidable stretch.

The castle of the Marquises of Villafranca, a 16th-century construction with massive lateral towers, dominates the town from above. Part is still inhabited—you can't visit inside, but the view from its surroundings over the confluence of the Burbia and Valcarce rivers is magnificent. In 1822, Villafranca briefly became capital of Bierzo province—a status that lasted only a decade.

Calle del Agua is the town's historic spine: an arcaded street lined with Renaissance and Baroque palaces, a 15th-century Mudéjar manor, and several convents. It's now an open-air heraldry museum—the coats of arms carved on façades tell five centuries of Bierzo noble families. The old town has been classified as a Cultural Heritage Site.

→

Tasting Bierzo wines—the protected designation produces reds from mencía, a grape unique to this region, prized for its notes of red fruit and volcanic minerality. The vineyards cling to the valley's slate-covered slopes. Several producers offer tastings at their estates around Villafranca.

→

Dinner around Plaza Mayor to try botillo del Bierzo—a local butcher describes it as 'smoked pork ribs and backbone, stuffed into the stomach.' Served boiled with vegetables, it's the region's emblematic dish, recognized with protected geographical indication status.

Ponferrada

The Templar Castle of Ponferrada is one of the most impressive medieval fortresses in northwest Spain. In 1178, the kings of León ceded the town to the Order of the Temple to protect pilgrims on the Camino. The Templars remained until 1312, when the Order was dissolved. The castle spreads over 8,000 square meters with a complex system of courtyards, barbicans, and towers—the original twelve reproduced constellations. In 1850, the town council sold the walls as a quarry—it took designation as a National Monument in 1924 to halt the destruction. Climb the rampart walk for views over the city and the Bierzo mountains.

The 16th-century Renaissance Basilica de la Encina houses the statue of the Virgin de la Encina—patroness of El Bierzo. According to legend, the Templars found her hidden in an oak trunk (encina) during the Reconquista. Right next door, the Renaissance Clock Tower and the Baroque Town Hall form one of the city's best-preserved civic ensembles.

→

Visit the Museum of Bierzo, housed in the former 15th-century Convent of San Francisco. It traces the comarca's natural and human history, with a remarkable room devoted to the Roman mines—Ponferrada sits at the heart of Las Médulas territory, the most productive gold mine in the Roman Empire.

→

Evening stroll along the Río Sil. The riverbank has been turned into a tree-lined promenade; it's locals' favorite spot for jogging and aperitifs. The illuminated castle viewed from the riverbanks is the city's signature photo.

Molinaseca is one of the most beautiful villages on the Camino Francés in El Bierzo. Declared a Historic-Artistic Site in 1975, this stone village belonged in the Middle Ages to the lordship of Ramiro Froilaz, nephew of El Cid Campeador. Its seven-arched medieval bridge over the Meruelo River—built of ashlar and cobblestone in the 12th century—is the traditional entrance to the village for pilgrims. In summer, the river below becomes a natural swimming hole. Calle Real, which leads from the bridge, is lined with armorial palaces, stone houses, and the 17th-century sanctuary of Nuestra Señora de las Angustias, whose Virgin is the village's patroness.

Twelve kilometers beyond Molinaseca on the LZ-24 road, the village of El Acebo deserves a stop: slate houses aligned along a single street, wooden balconies overhanging the Camino. This mountain village at 1,150 meters elevation was the dreaded stage for pilgrims before descending to Molinaseca. A wooden cross at the path's edge pays tribute to Camino cyclists—a tradition established by the riders themselves. The return descent to Molinaseca offers panoramic views over the Bierzo valley.

Lombillo de los Barrios, en route to your next stop, has been classified as a Historic-Artistic Site since 2014. Its church of San Martín and the chapel of Nuestra Señora de la Encarnación form the village's monumental core. The Mirador del Bierzo on the heights offers a panoramic view over the entire comarca—stop for 20 minutes to take in the vista.

→

Swimming in the Meruelo River at Molinaseca in late afternoon—the terraces of bars overlooking the bridge and river are the perfect spot to end the day with a glass of Bierzo mencía. In summer you need to arrive before noon to get a good spot on the riverbanks.

→

Hiking the section of the Camino Francés between El Acebo and Molinaseca: 7 km descending from the mountain hamlet, first through oak forest then along the river. It's one of the Camino's most physical stages—you'll do it in reverse from the pilgrims, downhill, which changes everything.

Ponferrada

Peñalba de Santiago perches at 1,110 meters elevation at the bottom of the Valle del Silencio—a valley so deep-cut into the Aquilanos mountains that the Oza River briefly disappears underground, hence the name. Saint Genadio, bishop of Astorga, withdrew here starting in 920 to live as a hermit until his death in 936. He had founded a monastery of which only the Mozarabic church of Santiago remains, completed in 937 by Abbot Salomon—one of the rare and most beautiful examples of preserved Spanish Mozarabic art. Its south portal, with its double horseshoe arch supported by three marble columns with Corinthian capitals, is remarkably graceful. The interior harbors early-medieval wall paintings uncovered during recent restorations. The village itself, with its stone and slate houses with wooden balconies, has been classified as a Cultural Heritage Site since 1998. Plan for the road—narrow and winding, it can be closed in winter.

From the village, a 30-minute trail leads to the Cueva de San Genadio, the cave carved into the vertical cliff at the valley's entrance where the saint withdrew each night to pray. The view from the cave over Peñalba's slate roofs below is the iconic image of the Valle del Silencio.

→

Hiking in the Valle del Silencio from Peñalba: the trail continues beyond the Cueva de San Genadio toward the monastery of San Pedro de Montes, founded in the 7th century by Saint Fructuoso and restored by San Genadio in 895. The complete round trip from Peñalba is about 10 km for a half-day in oak forest.

The Episcopal Palace of Astorga is one of Gaudí's rare works built outside Catalonia. After the old palace burned in 1886, Bishop Joan Grau i Vallespinós—a Catalan and Gaudí's personal friend—commissioned him to rebuild. The project began in 1887: Gaudí designed a neo-Gothic building in white granite that blends castle, temple, and palace, with turrets, battlements, and multicolored stained glass inspired by León Cathedral. The bishop's death in 1893 caused a rupture between Gaudí and the church authorities—he walked off the job, leaving the top floor unfinished, which another architect completed more soberly. Today the palace houses the Museum of the Ways, dedicated to pilgrimage routes. Allow at least 90 minutes.

Astorga's Cathedral of Santa María, built from the 15th to 18th centuries, is one of Spain's most composite monuments—Gothic, Renaissance, Baroque, and Plateresque succeed each other on its façades and interior. On one of its apsidal pinnacles, look for the statue of Pedro Mato, a Maragato about whom nothing is certain but local legends are numerous. The cathedral museum houses a magnificently chiseled silver casket by Alfonso III the Great.

Astorga was the Roman capital of Asturica Augusta, crossroads of several Roman roads. The Roman walls preserved in several sections throughout the city are among the best preserved on the peninsula. Don't miss the Roman Museum to understand the city's importance in imperial times—and the Chocolate Museum, since Astorga was historically one of the first European cities to import and process cacao from the Americas via Maragato muleteers.

→

Bar and restaurant tour around Plaza Mayor to dine on Maragatería specialties: cocido maragato (the Spanish stew eaten backwards—meat first, soup last), mantecadas de Astorga (butter and cinnamon cakes), and artisanal chocolate that's made the city's reputation since the 18th century. Book a restaurant for the cocido—it's a meal lasting several hours.

Castrillo de los Polvazares is La Maragatería's most perfectly preserved village. Its cobbled streets, red-stone houses with large round-arched doorways and windows painted blue or green form a homogeneous 17th-century ensemble declared a Historic-Artistic Site in 1980. Those oversized doorways weren't architectural whimsy: they allowed carts and mules to enter interior courtyards. For Castrillo was from the 16th to 19th centuries the heart of the Maragato muleteer network—those semi-nomadic transporters who linked Galicia and Madrid with their mule caravans. English writer Richard Ford described them in 1846: 'as nomadic as Bedouins, but with mules instead of camels; they cost dearly, but their honesty compensates.' The arrival of the railroad in Astorga in 1866 spelled their end. The village had 1,200 inhabitants in 1870; it has about a hundred today.

Santa Colomba de Somoza, 13 km away, is another preserved Maragato village, inhabited since the 11th century and linked to the muleteering trade since the 16th. The Casa Maragata—an ethnographic museum set in a real muleteer house—recreates the domestic and professional spaces of Maragato life: kitchen with large hearth, cart shed, agricultural tools, and traces of the chocolate trade. Each August, residents open their courtyards for a crafts fair.

→

Lunch at Castrillo de los Polvazares with cocido maragato: the legend about the inverted eating order (meat before soup) is tied to the War of Independence—Spanish soldiers ate the most nourishing parts first before imminent battle against the French. Book ahead, especially weekends—the restaurant is often full.

→

Hiking the 'Senda Maragata' from Santa Colomba de Somoza—several marked circuits let you discover remnants of Roman mines and the Maragatería landscapes between oak groves and heathland. The Mirador de la Laguna Cernea offers views over the worn hills and Roman gold workings of the region.

Use the morning to visit what you missed on your first visit: the Palacio de los Guzmanes, a 16th-century Renaissance building by Rodrigo Gil de Hontañón, now the provincial seat and open to the public during guided tours. Its interior courtyard is one of the city's finest. Or the cathedral one last time, this time lit differently depending on the hour—the light shifts throughout the day through the 737 stained-glass windows.

→

Plaza Mayor market (Wednesdays and Saturdays since 1660) to pick up León products: vacuum-packed cecina, mantecadas, mountain cheeses, and a few bottles of Bierzo mencía or Canary malvasía if you've extended the trip. One last tapa tour in the Barrio Húmedo before hitting the road.
