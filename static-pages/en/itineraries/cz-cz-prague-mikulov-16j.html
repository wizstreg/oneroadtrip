The Most Beautiful Places in the Czech Republic

A 26-meter-wide sandstone arch, a 138-meter underground abyss, 40,000 bones arranged in a chapel, a pale lager invented in 1842, an 18th-century Baroque theatre frozen in time. The Czech Republic stretches far beyond Prague.

Prague and its 600 years of Gothic cathedral construction, the sandstone cliffs of the Bohemian Paradise, the Giant Mountains summit at 1,603 meters, the natural arches of Bohemian Switzerland, the thermal springs of Karlovy Vary, the pale lager beer invented in Plzeň in 1842, Český Krumlov and its intact Baroque theatre, the village of Holašovice in rural Baroque style, Třebíč's Jewish Quarter—the only Jewish monument outside Israel listed as UNESCO World Heritage—Telč's Renaissance square, Mies van der Rohe's Villa Tugendhat, the Punkva Caves, Olomouc's Baroque column, and Mikulov's vineyards. The Czech Republic conceals far more treasures than just its capital.

Prague

Prague Castle, founded in the 9th century, is the world's largest medieval castle complex at 70,000 square meters. Its three successive courtyards lead to St. Vitus Cathedral, begun in 1344 under Charles IV to designs by French architect Matthias of Arras and completed only in 1929—a 585-year construction project. Inside, the Chapel of St. Wenceslas glitters with semi-precious stones and 14th-century frescoes. The seven-lock vault, each key held by a different state dignitary (the President, Prime Minister, Archbishop, etc.), houses the Bohemian Crown Jewels.

Golden Lane (Zlatá ulička), a row of colorful 16th-century cottages tucked against the castle's northern ramparts, owes its name to legends of Rudolf II's alchemists. Leave the castle via the Old Castle Stairs down into Malá Strana. Charles Bridge (Karlův most), laid on July 9, 1357 at 5:31 a.m.—a palindromic date chosen by Charles IV's astrologer—stretches 515 meters and is flanked by 30 Baroque statues added between 1683 and 1714. Beneath the statue of St. John of Nepomuk, martyred by being thrown into the Vltava in 1393 on King Wenceslas IV's orders, a bronze plaque polished by millions of hands is said to bring good luck.

St. Nicholas Church in Malá Strana, built between 1704 and 1755 by Christoph and Kilian Ignaz Dientzenhofer, stands as a pinnacle of Central European Baroque. Its 75-meter dome dominates the left bank. Mozart played the organ here during his Prague visits.

→

In the evening, stroll along the Vltava embankments between Charles Bridge and Legion Bridge to see the illuminated castle reflecting on the river from Kampa Island.

Prague

Top hotels
9.5 The Grand Mark Prague - The Leading Hotels of the World
9.1 Hotel Paris Prague
9.1 Falkensteiner Boutique Hotel Prague
9.1 Art Deco Imperial Hotel

Old Town Square hosts the Astronomical Clock (Orloj), built in 1410 and modified around 1490. The oldest functioning astronomical clock in Europe, its dial represents Earth at the center of the cosmos according to Ptolemy and displays local time, sidereal time, and the positions of the Sun and Moon. Every hour, the twelve apostles parade past. Legend claims the town councilors blinded the creator to prevent him from reproducing his masterpiece elsewhere. The Church of Our Lady before Týn, its asymmetrical 80-meter towers dominating the square, served as Prague's main Hussite church. Astronomer Tycho Brahe is buried here.

Explore Josefov, the former Jewish ghetto. The Old-New Synagogue, built around 1270 in Cistercian Gothic style, is the oldest active synagogue in Central Europe. It's linked to Rabbi Löw (1520–1609), to whom tradition attributes the creation of the Golem. In 1389, over 3,000 Jews were massacred in this quarter. The Old Jewish Cemetery, with its 12,000 gravestones layered up to 12 deep, dates from the early 15th century.

In the afternoon, take an excursion to Karlštejn Castle, 30 km from Prague. Built between 1348 and 1365 to Matthias of Arras's designs, it was conceived by Charles IV as a vault for the Imperial crown jewels. The 60-meter Great Tower houses the Chapel of the Holy Cross: its walls are inlaid with semi-precious stones and adorned with 129 panels painted by Master Theodoric—the world's largest collection of Gothic panel paintings. Charles IV himself entered this chapel barefoot as a sign of humility.

→

Sample Czech beer in a traditional Old Town pub—some establishments in the center still serve Pilsner Urquell or Kozel directly from wooden barrels.

Kutna Hora

Top hotels

9.4 Penzion U Hrádku

The Sedlec Ossuary, an underground chapel containing 40,000 to 70,000 bones, dates back to the 14th century. An abbot returning from Jerusalem in 1278 is said to have scattered Golgotha earth across the cemetery, rendering it so sacred that families from across Europe wanted to be buried here. In 1870, woodcarver František Rint bleached the bones with lime and fashioned them into chandeliers, coats of arms, and garlands that entirely line the chapel.

St. Barbara's Church, whose construction began in 1388 under Petr Parléř, is dedicated to the patron saint of miners who died on average at age 35 in Kutná Hora. Its triple tent-shaped vault, a masterpiece by Benedikt Ried, and its frescoes depicting miners' work and coin minting testify to the wealth of a city that was once the second-largest in the Kingdom of Bohemia. UNESCO-listed since 1995.

The Italian Court (Vlašský dvůr), founded in the 13th century by King Wenceslas II, served as the kingdom's mint for two centuries. Kutná Hora's mines then produced a third of Central Europe's silver.

→

Descend into a reconstructed medieval silver mine beneath Kaňk Hill, whose galleries plunge to 250 meters. Helmets and miners' smocks provided on-site.

Jicin

Top hotels

Prachovské Skály, 5 km from Jičín, are the most renowned rock formations in the Bohemian Paradise (Český ráj), the world's first UNESCO Global Geopark in the Czech Republic. These sandstone pillars and labyrinths formed over 100 million years ago in a shallow sea. Erosion has sculpted towers reaching 30 meters and corridors so narrow that one passage bears the name 'Mouse Hole.' A gorge is called 'Emperor's Passage' since Emperor Francis I of Austria was carried through it by palanquin around 1804.

The ruins of Trosky Castle, perched on two basalt volcanic plugs 47 and 57 meters high named Baba (the Old Woman) and Panna (the Maiden), are the symbol of the Bohemian Paradise. Founded in the second half of the 14th century, never conquered by the Hussites, it was burned by the imperial army in 1648 and abandoned.

The Hrubá Skála rock city is a labyrinth of 400 sandstone towers up to 60 meters high. Hrubá Skála Castle, mentioned as early as 1353 as the seat of the Valdštejn family, was rebuilt in Renaissance style in the 16th century. Its panoramic terrace offers views over the ensemble of sandstone towers and the Trosky ruins.

Valdštejnovo Square in Jičín, dominated by the 52-meter Baroque Valdická Gate tower, was the project of Duke Albrecht von Wallenstein, who dreamed of making it the capital of his duchy in the 17th century. The avenue of 1,200 lime trees, 2 km long, leads from the gate to Valdice Castle.

→

Hike through Prachovské Skály on marked trails. The red circuit (2.5 km, 1.5h) is recommended for families; the green circuit (3.5 km, 2h) is the most spectacular with its viewpoints over the rock towers.

Krkonoše National Park (Giant Mountains), a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve since 1992, is the Czech Republic's highest mountain range. It covers 550 km² along the Polish border and hosts 1,300 plant species, including the Bohemian bellflower, the park's emblem. At the summit of Sněžka (1,603 m), the country's highest point, a weather observatory, a wooden chapel, and a post office allow you to mail a postcard stamped from the summit. The Czech-Polish border cuts the summit in two.

Obří důl Valley (Giant Valley), which leads from Pec pod Sněžkou to Sněžka's summit, traverses an old mining gallery (Kovárna mine), traditional wooden chalets, and subalpine landscapes. It's the classic route to reach the summit on foot, with 800 meters of elevation gain from Pec pod Sněžkou (altitude 750 m).

→

Take the cable car from Pec pod Sněžkou to Sněžka summit (1,603 m). The current cable car, inaugurated in 2014 to replace a two-seater chairlift in service since 1949, transports passengers in enclosed four-person cabins with a 700-meter elevation gain in 16 minutes.

→

Hike via Obří důl Valley to the summit, then return by cable car. The blue-marked trail from Pec pod Sněžkou parking lot follows the Úpa River, passes the Giants' Mine Chapel (built in 1873), and gradually climbs to the ridge.

Pravčická Gate is Europe's largest natural sandstone arch: 26.5 meters wide, 16 meters high inside. Created in 2000, Bohemian Switzerland National Park (79 km²) owes its name to 18th-century Swiss artists struck by the resemblance to their Alps. In 1881, Prince Edmund Clary-Aldringen built a 50-bed hotel near the arch, now renamed Sokolí hnízdo (Falcon's Nest). Since 1982, climbing on the arch has been prohibited due to erosion. In July 2022, a forest fire—the worst in modern Czech history—ravaged over 1,000 hectares of the park. Edmund Gorge reopened on July 19, 2025, under limited access.

The village of Hřensko, population 280, nestles at the confluence of the Kamenice and Elbe rivers on the German border. Its half-timbered houses exemplify Czech-German border architecture. From here radiate all the park's trails.

→

Hike from Hřensko to Pravčická Gate via the red-marked trail (about 3 km, 1.5h ascent). Then descend into the Kamenice Gorges for the flat-bottomed boat crossing, guided by a boatman with a pole on translucent waters between 150-meter-high walls.

Karlovy Vary

Top hotels

Karlovy Vary received royal town status from Charles IV in 1370. The town has 12 main hot springs. The most powerful, Vřídlo, shoots up at 73°C with 2,000 liters per minute, forming a 12-meter natural geyser. Listed as a UNESCO Great Spa Town of Europe in 2021, the city has hosted Goethe, Chopin, Karl Marx, and Peter the Great. The Mill Colonnade (Mlýnská kolonáda), built between 1871 and 1881 by Josef Zítek in neo-Renaissance style, is the most imposing: 124 columns over 132 meters shelter five springs.

The Imperial Spa (Císařské lázně), completed in 1895 in French neo-Renaissance style by Viennese firm Fellner & Helmer, is the town's most monumental thermal building. After a long restoration completed in 2023, its interior adorned with carved woodwork and murals is once again accessible. The Church of St. Mary Magdalene, built between 1732 and 1736 by Kilian Ignaz Dientzenhofer, is the town's Baroque masterpiece.

→

Take the funicular up to Diana Tower for panoramic views of the town nestled in its ring of hills. Then sample a glass of Becherovka, a liqueur made from 20 herbs and spices produced exclusively in Karlovy Vary since 1807.

→

Sip thermal water from different springs at the colonnades—each spring has a different temperature and mineralization. Spa tradition calls for drinking it slowly from a small spouted cup.

Plzen

St. Bartholomew's Cathedral, with its 102-meter tower—the tallest church tower in the Czech Republic—dominates Republic Square, the country's largest medieval square after Prague. The Great Synagogue of Plzeň, built in 1893, is the world's third-largest synagogue after Budapest and New York, with its two 50-meter towers.

Pilsner Urquell Brewery produced its first batch on October 5, 1842, under Bavarian brewmaster Josef Groll. By combining local soft water, Saaz hops, and pale malt, Groll invented the world's first pale lager beer—a style that today represents over 80% of global production. The name Pilsner Urquell ('original source of Pilsen') was trademarked in 1898. Visit the historic cellars beneath the city where beer still fermented in beechwood barrels.

→

Guided tour of the brewery cellars with tasting of unfiltered Pilsner Urquell drawn directly from the barrel—the only chance to taste the beer before filtration and pasteurization.

Šumava National Park, often called 'the green heart of Europe,' stretches 80 km along the Bavarian border. A UNESCO Biosphere Reserve since 1990, it harbors five glacial lakes, centuries-old primeval forests, peat bogs, and populations of lynx, wolf, and capercaillie. During the Cold War, the region was long a military zone closed to the public, which allowed nature to remain virtually pristine.

Černé jezero (Black Lake) is the Czech Republic's largest and deepest natural lake (40 meters deep). Its black color comes from the reflection of the surrounding spruce forests. One kilometer away, Čertovo jezero (Devil's Lake), with clear, transparent waters, owes its name to a legend where the devil, dragged to the bottom by a girl cleverer than him, is said to have carved this valley. By geographical curiosity, the two lakes separated by a few hundred meters drain into different basins: Černé jezero toward the North Sea, Čertovo jezero toward the Black Sea.

→

Hike to both glacial lakes from Špičácké sedlo parking lot. The yellow-marked trail descends in 1h to Čertovo and Černé jezero lakes (8.3 km loop, 328 m elevation gain).

→

In the evening, cycle on forest trails in the park from Železná Ruda—several rental shops offer e-bikes for marked trails around the village.

Český Krumlov Castle—the Czech Republic's second-largest castle complex with 40 buildings over 17 hectares—was founded in the 13th century by the Vítek family. Passed to the Rosenbergs in 1302 (their five-petal rose is the town's emblem), it was transformed into a Renaissance residence in the 16th century. Its moat has housed live bears since the 16th century. UNESCO-listed since 1992.

In the fifth courtyard, the Baroque theatre of 1765–1766 is one of only two in the world entirely preserved in their original state (the other is at Drottningholm, Sweden). Its stage machinery, painted sets, costumes, and orchestra pit date from the 18th century.

The Old Town, encircled by a meander of the Vltava, features cobblestone lanes lined with Gothic, Renaissance, and Baroque facades. St. Vitus Church, founded in 1309, houses the tomb of William of Rosenberg. The Egon Schiele Art Center chronicles the Austrian Expressionist artist's 1911 stay—his mother was from Krumlov.

→

Canoe or kayak down the Vltava meanders around the castle—a loop of several kilometers offering unique views of the complex from the river.

Holašovice, 30 km from Český Krumlov, is a village of 140 inhabitants UNESCO-listed since 1998. Founded in the 13th century, it was entirely repopulated after a plague epidemic between 1520 and 1525 that left only two survivors. Its 23 farms with colorful stucco gables, arranged around an oval square, form the most complete example of South Bohemian Baroque rural architecture—a unique style fusing Czech and Austrian vernacular traditions. It's a living village, not a museum.

Zlatá Koruna Cistercian Monastery, founded in 1263 by King Přemysl Otakar II 8 km north of Český Krumlov, is one of Central Europe's best-preserved Gothic complexes. Its name ('golden crown') refers to the Crown of Thorns relic brought from Paris by the king.

→

Stroll through Český Krumlov Castle's Baroque gardens—11 hectares of terraces, a Rococo summer pavilion, and a cascade fountain featuring Neptune. The revolving open-air theatre installed in the gardens hosts performances during summer season.

Zachariáš of Hradec Square is one of Europe's most beautiful Renaissance squares. Rebuilt after a 1530 fire, it presents a continuous row of arcaded houses with pastel facades decorated with sgraffito. Zachariáš of Hradec, returning from a trip to Genoa in 1551 enthused by the Italian Renaissance, undertook to transform Telč with Italian master architects. UNESCO-listed since 1992.

Telč Castle, transformed from a Gothic fortress into a Renaissance residence from 1553, possesses a Golden Great Hall with an exceptional 16th-century carved coffered ceiling. The work was financed by the mining dowry of Zachariáš's first wife, Kateřina of Valdštejn.

Climb the 153 steps of St. James Church tower for panoramic views of the square and the three ponds encircling the old town.

→

Walk or cycle along the ponds surrounding Telč for changing perspectives of Renaissance facades reflected in the water.

Trebic

Třebíč's Jewish Quarter is the only Jewish monument listed as UNESCO World Heritage outside Israel (2003). Developed from the 12th century, it comprises 123 houses, two synagogues, and a cemetery with 4,000 graves. Its particularity is remarkable: unlike other European ghettos, coexistence with the Christian community here was exceptionally peaceful. In 1723, when a law imposed the creation of closed ghettos, Christian residents living in the territory voluntarily exchanged their houses with their Jewish neighbors. All residents were deported and killed during World War II.

St. Procopius Basilica, built in the 1240s–1260s in Romanesque-Gothic style for the Benedictine abbey founded in 1101, measures 72 meters long. Its crypt with 50 columns, each capital different, dates from 1220. It was consecrated in 1704 to St. Procopius on the 500th anniversary of his canonization.

→

Walk through the maze of lanes in the Jewish Quarter, along the Jihlava River, then climb to the castle for views over the entire site.

Špilberk Castle, founded around 1277 by Přemysl Otakar II, was transformed into a Baroque fortress in the 17th century then into Central Europe's most dreaded prison from the 18th century. Italian writer Silvio Pellico spent eight years here and wrote My Prisons. The Cathedral of St. Peter and Paul, whose twin spires reach 84 meters, hides a legend: in 1645, defenders rang the noon bells an hour early, fooling the Swedes. Since then, they ring noon at 11 a.m.

Villa Tugendhat, built by Ludwig Mies van der Rohe between 1929 and 1930, has been UNESCO-listed since 2001. Its large living room open to the garden, its golden onyx partitions, its motorized bay windows retractable into the floor were revolutionary. The Gestapo requisitioned it in 1939. In 1992, Václav Klaus and Vladimír Mečiar signed the separation of Czechoslovakia here.

St. James Church ossuary, discovered in 2001 beneath a downtown square, is Europe's second-largest after the Paris Catacombs: 50,000 bones—victims of plague epidemics and Hussite wars from the 14th to 18th centuries.

→

Wander through the Cabbage Market (Zelný trh), a lively Baroque square that has hosted a market since the Middle Ages. In the evening, explore the bars and breweries in the Veveří student quarter.

Punkva Caves, located in the Moravian Karst (the Czech Republic's largest karst system with over 1,000 cavities), allow access to the bottom of Macocha Abyss—138 meters deep, formed by the collapse of a cave ceiling in the 17th century. Two small lakes formed there, fed by the Punkva River whose underground flow is visible from two observation walkways. The stalactite named Romeo and the stalagmite Juliet, no longer fed by water for centuries and thus destined never to touch, illustrate the guides' imagination.

→

Guided circuit through Punkva Caves (about 1h) followed by a motorboat crossing on the underground river between stalactite domes. Access to the Masaryk Dome—considered the most beautiful in the cave—is only possible by boat.

Olomouc

The Holy Trinity Column, erected in 1740 at the end of a plague epidemic and consecrated in 1754 in the presence of Empress Maria Theresa, is the most monumental example of this monument type in Central Europe. Rising 35 meters high, adorned with religious sculptures by Moravian artist Ondřej Zahner, it houses a small chapel at its base. UNESCO-listed since 2000. Its square, Horní náměstí, is also graced with six Baroque fountains dedicated to mythological gods (Neptune, Hercules, Jupiter, Triton, Caesar, Mercury)—a unique ensemble recalling the legend that Olomouc was founded by Julius Caesar.

St. Wenceslas Cathedral, whose neo-Gothic towers reach 100 meters (the second-tallest in the Czech Republic), was founded in 1104–1107 by Prince Svatopluk. It witnessed tragedy in 1306: the assassination within its walls of King Wenceslas III, the last Přemyslid, ending the oldest Czech dynasty. Beethoven composed his Missa Solemnis for this cathedral in 1823; Mozart, taking refuge here in 1767 to flee a smallpox epidemic in Vienna, composed his Symphony in F major.

The Town Hall astronomical clock of Olomouc is the most original in the Czech Republic: designed in socialist realist style after 1945 (the original was damaged by the Germans in 1945), it depicts not Apostles but Czech workers, scientists, and athletes parading every hour.

→

Sample Olomoucké tvarůžky, ripened cheeses typical of Olomouc with a very pronounced smell and taste, in a traditional downtown restaurant. These small farmhouse cheeses have been produced in the region since the 15th century and are a protected designation.

Mikulov

+2
Top hotels

9 Hotel Ryzlink

Mikulov's Baroque castle dominates the town from a limestone spur. Rebuilt in the 17th century under Cardinal Franz von Dietrichstein, it was burned during the German troop withdrawal in April 1945. Restored after the war, it now houses Moravia's most comprehensive wine exhibition, as well as an enormous 18th-century wine barrel. Napoleon made a brief stop here on December 12, 1805, traveling from Brno to Vienna after the Battle of Austerlitz.

Mikulov's Jewish Cemetery, with its 4,000 graves dating from the 15th century, is the Czech Republic's second-largest after Prague. For 300 years, Mikulov was the seat of Moravia's rabbis. The Jewish community—up to 1,500 people in the 19th century—was entirely deported and exterminated during World War II.

The Pálava Hills, a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve since 1986, extend north of Mikulov on white limestone ridges where endemic plant species grow. From the Holy Hill trail (Svatý kopeček), a Baroque Way of the Cross installed in 1622 by Cardinal Dietrichstein to give thanks for the end of a plague epidemic, the view embraces Mikulov, the vineyards, and on clear days, the Austrian Alps.

→

Taste Moravian wines in a Mikulov cellar—the region concentrates 5,000 of the Czech Republic's 18,000 hectares of vineyards. Typical grape varieties from the Mikulov sub-region are Welschriesling, Grüner Veltliner, and Pálava, a local variety producing aromatic, full-bodied wine.
