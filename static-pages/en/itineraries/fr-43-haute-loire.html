Polignac

Polignac Fortress rises from a 3-hectare basalt platform at 2,644 feet elevation. The 14th-century keep soars 105 feet high, commanding a 360° panorama across the Puy basin and the Meygal and Mézenc massifs.

Follow the 14-stop circuit through the ruins: half a mile of ramparts, 6 fortified gates, the armory hall, a vertiginous 274-foot-deep well, and the medieval-inspired garden.

On the keep's ground floor, the mask of Apollo testifies to an ancient Roman temple. Pilgrims once consulted an oracle here—a priest hidden behind the mask dictating prophecies.

Step inside Saint-Martin Church, a Romanesque jewel classified as a Historic Monument since 1902. The choir shelters 14th-century frescoes depicting the Last Judgment, paradise, and hell.

In the south apsidiole, 15th-century paintings illustrate the Annunciation, the Visitation, and the Nativity. Notice the polychrome statue of Saint Anne from the 13th century, typical of medieval folk art.

The village counts over 100 rural heritage features scattered across twenty hamlets: crosses, fountains, bread ovens, and wash houses. The one at Bilhac preserves a cross inscribed as a Historic Monument since 1930.

→

Climb to the top of the keep to embrace the Velay volcanoes. On clear days, the view reaches all the way to the Chaîne des Puys.

→

Follow the Pinatelle du Zouave trail winding around the basalt dike. The walk reveals the volcanic geology of this site, over 2.5 million years old.

→

Wander the lanes of this village labeled one of France's Most Beautiful Villages. Houses built from volcanic breccia and lava stone fountains punctuate your stroll.

Lavaudieu

Lavaudieu's Romanesque cloister is the only one in Auvergne to have survived destruction intact. Its single or paired columns, crowned with sculpted capitals, support rounded arches beneath a 12th-century wooden gallery.

Enter the refectory adjoining the cloister. A monumental Byzantine-influenced fresco depicts Christ in Majesty above the Virgin surrounded by the Apostles. Classified as a Historic Monument as early as 1862.

The Abbey Church of Saint-André, crowned by its octagonal Romanesque bell tower, conceals a collection of 14th-century murals from the Italian School: Passion scenes and a curious allegory of the plague, the "Black Death."

The Benedictine abbey was founded in 1057 by Robert de Turlande, also founder of La Chaise-Dieu. Nuns lived here until the Revolution, in this place baptized Lavaudieu—"Valley of God."

Visit the Museum of Folk Arts and Traditions, housed in the former baker's home. Over a thousand objects recreate an Auvergnat interior from days past.

Notice the abbey's bell tower, truncated during the Revolution: a pike and Phrygian cap still ornament its summit, an unusual republican vestige.

→

Stroll along the Senouire River and cross the Romanesque bridge. From both sides, the view of the golden stone wine-growers' houses and terra-cotta tiles is exceptional.

→

Meander through the cobbled lanes of the village, classified as one of France's Most Beautiful Villages. Kitchen gardens nestled around the abbey and an 18th-century iron cross punctuate the walk.

→

In summer, the village hosts its traditional wine festival, a custom born during the Revolution when locals seized barrels destined for the monks of La Chaise-Dieu.

Brioude

The Basilica of Saint-Julien is the largest Romanesque church in Auvergne, built between the 11th and 12th centuries over a 4th-century martyr's tomb. Its polychromy of red sandstone, basalt, granulite, and gray marbles is utterly unique.

Look up: roughly 300 Romanesque capitals populated with foliage, symbolic animals, and knights follow one another down the nave. The sculpted cycle in the choir evokes the Passion and Resurrection.

Look down at the pavement of basalt and quartz pebbles, rediscovered in 1963. Arranged in flowers, rosettes, and waves, these river stones form a two-tone mineral carpet.

The 37 contemporary stained-glass windows by Father Kim En Joong, created by the Loire Workshops of Chartres in 2008, flood the nave with colored light. The dialogue between Romanesque and contemporary art is striking.

Take rue du 4-Septembre to admire the Maison de Mandrin (12th–20th c.), its half-timbered brick façade with corbelling, and its vaulted room with painted coats of arms.

The Saint-Julien Fountain, on Avenue d'Auvergne, marks according to legend the site of the saint's beheading. Its capitals reportedly date back to the 10th century.

→

Brioude sits on the Via Arverna linking Clermont-Ferrand to Santiago de Compostela. Walk a stretch of this thousand-year-old pilgrimage route in the surrounding area.

→

Wander the lanes of this "Petite Cité de Caractère" (Small Heritage Town): mansions, turrets, half-timbered houses, and shaded squares punctuate your stroll.

→

Need some fresh air? Head down to the banks of the Allier. The river, one of the last wild rivers in Europe, flows at the town's doorstep at the entrance to its gorges.

La Chaise Dieu

The Abbey of La Chaise-Dieu (Casa Dei, "House of God") was founded in 1043 by Robert de Turlande. The 14th-century Gothic abbey church shelters the white marble tomb of Pope Clement VI, surrounded by 55 sculpted figures.

The famous Danse Macabre, a 15th-century fresco painted on the north aisle, is one of the few surviving in France. Three dead and three living dance together: "Death spares neither small nor great."

Admire the 14 Flemish tapestries from the early 16th century, totaling 213 feet in length. Commissioned by Abbot Jacques de Saint-Nectaire, they illustrate biblical scenes from the Poor Man's Bible.

The 144 carved oak choir stalls surround the choir. Chimeras, monks in grotesque poses, grimacing faces: Flemish sculptors let their imaginations run wild here.

Visit the Echo Room, a genuine acoustic curiosity. Stand in one corner and whisper: your companion, in the opposite corner, hears you perfectly.

The flamboyant Gothic rood screen separates the nave from the monks' choir. Above the main entrance, the 17th-century Baroque organ case adorns the interior façade with sculpted musical angels.

The Clémentine Tower, an imposing 14th-century fortified keep at the church's chevet, once housed the abbey's treasures. The neighboring stables have been converted into an auditorium.

→

Wander through the medieval town built below the abbey. The rue des Casernes, narrow and paved with pebbles, is the oldest. Some façades preserve bartizans, mascarons, and coats of arms.

→

Each August, the La Chaise-Dieu Music Festival, founded in 1966, hosts classical music concerts beneath the abbey's vaults. Check the program.

→

Step inside the Chapelle des Pénitents, the former monks' refectory transformed into a chapel after the Revolution. Free access year-round.

The "Potence" of Allègre: this name designates the spectacular ruins of the 14th-century feudal castle, built atop the Baury volcano. Two towers linked by a cornice of trefoil machicolations remain balanced above the void. Classified as a Historic Monument in 1935.

The original castle counted 23 towers across 3 enceintes. Built by Morinot de Tourzel starting in 1385, it was one of Auvergne's most imposing. A chimney fire ravaged it on November 15, 1698.

Pass through the Porte de Monsieur, the south entrance to the fortified town. It has retained its two towers, the portcullis housing, and the hinges of the original gate.

On the Place du Marchédial, surrounded by fortified houses, the Chapelle Notre-Dame-de-l'Oratoire shelters a 16th-century Pietà.

Allègre, labeled a "Petite Cité de Caractère," reveals itself through its charreyrons: narrow lanes once used by water carriers between houses staggered on the slopes of Mont Baury.

→

Climb to the summit of Mont Bar (3,855 ft), unique in Europe: it's the only strombolian volcano sheltering a peat bog in its crater. An interpretive trail circles this Natura 2000 classified zone.

→

From the orientation table at the foot of the Potence, take in the Devès volcanoes, Mont Bar facing you, and the Velay mountains in the distance.

→

Allègre is the gateway to the Livradois-Forez Regional Natural Park. Several marked trails radiate from the village to explore the surrounding countryside.

The Château d'Arlempdes, the first castle on the Loire from its source, rises from an 260-foot volcanic dike that the river wraps in a meander. Its ruins date from the 12th to 14th centuries.

Climb up to the Saint-Jacques castle chapel, built in red stone and dating from the 11th or 12th century. Inside, the keep and the Guards' Hall were renovated in 2016.

The 11th-century postern, inscribed as a Historic Monument in 1971, served as the entrance gate to the castle town. Its high pointed barrel vault marks the entrance to the fortified village.

The Romanesque Church of Saint-Pierre (12th century) features a single nave and a gable bell tower with four arches. Outside, a 15th-century Gothic calvary in sculpted stone is the sole vestige of the old cemetery.

Arlempdes is classified as one of France's Most Beautiful Villages. Its volcanic stone houses huddle at the foot of the basalt spur whose vertical walls plunge toward the Loire.

The site is recognized by the BRGM (French geological survey) as rare in France: the rocky eminence is a "dike," a feeder conduit of an ancient volcanic chimney exposed by erosion. The stacks of basalt prisms heighten the spectacle.

→

Take the "Rocher d'Arlempdes" trail for a spectacular viewpoint over the village, its castle, and the Loire's meander from the heights.

→

Descend to the pebble beach by following the castle town's enclosure. The wild Loire, barely 25 miles from its source, flows between basalt flows sometimes resembling organ pipes.

→

In summer, the Loire offers a cool oasis for waterside picnics. Anglers find grayling, trout, and minnows here.

Pradelles, classified as one of France's Most Beautiful Villages, overlooks the Upper Allier valley from 3,786 feet elevation. Nicknamed the "balcony of the Midi," it offers a panorama over Lake Naussac and the Margeride hills.

Cross the Place de la Halle with its stone arcades. The noble dwellings with tall stone façades, braced against the Cévennes squalls, testify to the town's medieval prosperity.

The Chapel of Notre-Dame de Pradelles, built in the early 17th century, is the village's spiritual heart. Several French kings stopped here, including Saint Louis in 1254.

Spot the vestiges of medieval fortifications: towers, corner turrets, shadowy porches. The cobbled lanes climb in tiers through this ancient town of tawny stone.

Pradelles was a major stop on the Via Régordane (GR 700) linking Le Puy-en-Velay to Saint-Gilles-du-Gard. Merchants and pilgrims passed through for centuries.

→

Walk in the footsteps of Robert Louis Stevenson: the GR 70, the famous Stevenson Trail, crosses Pradelles. The Scottish writer stopped here in 1878 with his donkey Modestine.

→

Try the Pradelles vélorail: pedal along the old Transcévenol railway line that crosses the volcanic Devès plateau toward Langogne.

→

Lake Naussac, visible from the village heights, offers water activities in season: sailing, paddleboarding, canoeing, and swimming at developed beaches.

The Cluniac Priory of Sainte-Croix was founded in 1025 by Odilon de Mercoeur, 5th Abbot of Cluny. The buildings visible today date mainly from the 15th and 18th centuries, their hemicycle form hugging the Allier's meander.

Step inside the Priory Church of Sainte-Croix, in Gothic style (15th c.). It preserves a Romanesque door called Saint Odilon's Door (12th c.) and a polychrome wooden Christ from the mid-12th century.

Look for the statuette of Notre-Dame-Trouvée: in 1496, two girls playing by the Allier discovered this 0.6-inch Virgin inside a broken pebble. She is preserved in a silver reliquary.

Cross the humpbacked medieval bridge linking the village's two quarters. One of its arches dates back to the 11th century—it has resisted centuries of violent Allier floods.

The Maison des Oiseaux et de la Nature, in the priory courtyard, presents around fifty bird species from the Allier gorges, sculpted life-size in wood against a fresco backdrop.

Explore the Barrys quarter, the village's oldest, with its traboules—narrow characteristic passageways allowing quick descent to the river.

→

Descend the Allier gorges by canoe or kayak. Outfitters offer trips suited to all levels departing from the village.

→

Take the belvedere loop (PR001, 2 km / 1.2 mi) for a unique view of the Allier's perfect loop around the priory. Family-friendly.

→

In summer, swim in the Allier from the beach near the bridge. This river, one of the last wild rivers in Europe, is also ideal for family fishing.

→

The GR 470 "Source et Gorges de l'Allier" crosses the village. The Lavoûte-Chilhac–Langeac stage (19 km / 12 mi) offers gorge landscapes hemmed between volcanoes and forests.
