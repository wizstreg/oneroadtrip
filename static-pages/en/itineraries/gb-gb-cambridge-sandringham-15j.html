East Anglia Circuit: Cambridge, Suffolk Coast and Royal Norfolk

From Cambridge colleges to Blakeney Point seals: East Anglia reveals itself slowly, and every turn holds something unexpected.

From Cambridge and its thousand-year-old colleges to Suffolk's wild beaches and Norfolk's royal estates, this itinerary traverses England's least-visited corner. Punt on the Cam, follow Constable's footsteps at Flatford, explore the coast from Southwold to Aldeburgh, then finish on Holkham's vast beaches and in Sandringham's gardens.

Key highlights (5)
  • Cambridge, its 31 colleges and punting on the Cam since 1209
  • Dedham Vale, the mills and meadows that made Constable a painter
  • Southwold with its town-centre lighthouse, Victorian beach huts and eccentric pier
  • The Norfolk coast between Blakeney seals, Holkham's immense beach and steam train to Walsingham
  • Sandringham, royal residence since Edward VII, open to the public spring through autumn

Cambridge was born from a brawl. In 1209, Oxford students fled to the Cam after a deadly town-and-gown conflict and founded a university here on the riverbanks. Since then, this place has produced more than 120 Nobel laureates—more than most countries. Start by getting lost in the streets: King's Parade, Senate House, Trinity Street. Every facade teaches architectural history, every gateway conceals a courtyard. Plan to enter at least two colleges—opening hours vary, so check the day before.

The Backs is the ribbon of gardens and lawns behind the colleges, riverside—the finest view of Cambridge, visible only from a punt or on foot. King's College Chapel (begun 1446 under Henry VI) dominates everything with its vertical limestone facade. Look up at the carved fan-vaulted ceiling inside—some historians consider it the finest Gothic ceiling in the world. Clare Bridge, the oldest surviving bridge over the Cam, dates from the 1630s: examine its 14 decorative stone balls closely. One is deliberately incomplete—legend says the architect, inadequately paid, sliced off a piece as his fee.

→

Don't leave Cambridge without punting on the Cam—it's the experience that captures the soul of this city. A punt is a flat-bottomed boat propelled with a pole. You can hire one yourself or book a guided tour: local guides pull out improbable anecdotes about each college you glide past. Allow 45 minutes to an hour for the classic College Backs route. In summer, arrive early morning or late afternoon—between 11am and 3pm it's mobbed.

The Fitzwilliam Museum is one of Britain's finest art and antiquities museums—and admission is free. Its foundation story alone is worth the visit: in 1816, Viscount Richard Fitzwilliam bequeathed Cambridge 144 paintings (including works by Rembrandt, Titian, and Veronese), over 10,000 printed books and 130 illuminated manuscripts, plus £100,000 to build the museum. Architect George Basevi won the 1834 competition with a neoclassical design inspired by ancient Greece—then fell to his death from scaffolding at Ely Cathedral in 1845, never seeing the 1848 opening. Today the collection exceeds 500,000 objects: Egyptian antiquities worthy of the British Museum, canvases by Monet, Picasso, Cézanne, Van Dyck, and a major collection of Turner watercolours. Allow at least 90 minutes.

Cambridge University Botanic Garden spans 40 acres south of the city centre. Founded in 1831 by John Stevens Henslow—the professor who encouraged Darwin to board the Beagle—it shelters over 8,000 plant species. It's also an active climate change research site. Late afternoon light on the beds is magnificent. Allow 1 to 1.5 hours.

Grantchester is a small village 3km south of Cambridge, reachable on foot along the river in 45 minutes. Poet Rupert Brooke lived here just before World War I and wrote in exile the poem 'The Old Vicarage, Grantchester' that made him famous. The village and its riverside pub remain remarkably unspoiled. If you want speed, take a punt from Cambridge.

→

Spend the evening wandering central Cambridge outside peak hours. The Round Church (Church of the Holy Sepulchre), built around 1130, is one of only four rare round Romanesque churches still standing in England. From there, climb toward the market—it runs weekdays and offers local produce and crafts. The pubs on King's Parade or Bridge Street are perfect for ending the day.

Houghton Mill is one of the last working watermills on the Great Ouse, managed by the National Trust. Built in the 17th century, it was still grinding grain into the 1930s. You can watch the millstones turn on certain weekends and buy flour milled on site. The adjoining village of Houghton is a concentration of half-timbered cottages—ideal for a morning stroll.

St Ives is a small town on the Great Ouse with a 15th-century stone medieval bridge—on which stands a chapel, extremely rare in England. Wander along the waterfront to the central market, active since the Middle Ages. The town owes its name to a 4th-century Persian bishop whose relics were supposedly discovered here in the 10th century—one of Cambridgeshire's most unlikely stories.

Wimpole Hall, about fifteen kilometres southwest of Cambridge, is Cambridgeshire's largest mansion. Its architectural history reads like a soap opera: built from 1640, remodelled by James Gibbs in the 1710s, then Henry Flitcroft around 1749, then John Soane in the 1790s. But the real story is its last owner: Elsie Bambridge, Rudyard Kipling's only daughter. She bought the ruined estate with her father's inheritance and literary royalties—so The Jungle Book and Kim paid to restore the roof. She had a notorious temperament: when a couple once picnicked too near the house, she had her own picnic prepared on their neighbours' lawn. Upon her death in 1976, she left everything to the National Trust. The 3,000-acre park was designed by Capability Brown in 1767. The recently reopened historic library and the 1794 Home Farm with rare-breed animals complete the visit.

→

The countryside between Houghton, St Ives and surrounding villages is best explored on foot or by bike along well-marked paths beside the Great Ouse. The banks are flat, shaded, and popular with locals on weekends—a relaxed way to see rural Cambridgeshire away from Cambridge tourists.

Ely is visible from miles away—the cathedral dominates the flat Fens like a beached liner, earning its nickname 'Ship of the Fens'. Its history begins in 673 when Princess Æthelthryth founded an abbey here. The current structure dates mainly from 1081, but the highlight is the Octagon Tower at the nave's centre: in 1322, the Norman tower collapsed in the night with such a crash the monks thought it was an earthquake. Architect Alan de Walsingham had the brilliant idea of widening the space into a 23-metre octagon, crowned by a painted-wood and lead lantern weighing 400 tonnes. A medieval engineering masterpiece that took 18 years to build. The cathedral measures 164 metres long and its nave is one of England's longest. Book a tower tour: climbing inside the structure is well worth the effort.

Oliver Cromwell's House sits two minutes from the cathedral. Cromwell inherited an Ely estate in 1636 from his uncle and lived here about ten years as a tithe collector for the cathedral—a cruel irony, since he later shut it down for a decade during the Commonwealth. The 16th-century timber-framed house is now a living museum. The town's name, 'Ely', may derive from Old English Eilig, 'island of eels'—you can still find them on some local restaurant menus.

→

After the cathedral, head down to the Great Ouse banks for a walk along the quay. This is where you truly understand the Fens landscape: a plain drained by Dutch engineers in the 17th century, with barges moored side by side. Ely market runs twice weekly and sells local produce, crafts and street food.

5
Day

Dedham

En route to Dedham Vale, stop in Bury St Edmunds—an elegant town built on a grid pattern laid out in the 11th century by Benedictine monks. Here in 1214, English barons gathered to seal the agreement that would force King John to accept Magna Carta—one of the founding acts of modern liberty. The Benedictine abbey founded in 1020 by Canute the Great was one of England's most powerful: its ruins, in the freely accessible Abbey Gardens, stretched over 500 feet in length—among the country's largest.

Bury St Edmunds Cathedral (St Edmundsbury Cathedral) adjoins the abbey ruins. Its nave dates from 1503 and was built by John Wastell, the same mason who worked on King's College Chapel in Cambridge. It only received cathedral status in 1914 and its current bell tower wasn't completed until 2005. Inside, look for the tomb of Mary Tudor, Queen of France and sister of Henry VIII, in the adjacent church of St Mary.

Ickworth House is a neoclassical mansion whose 30-metre-diameter central rotunda is one of England's most eccentric constructions, commissioned in the late 18th century by the Bishop of Derry Frederick Hervey, an avid collector and compulsive traveller. The 1,800-acre park designed in the English landscape style is open year-round. If you have time before reaching Dedham, it's a 7km detour from the town centre.

→

Arriving in Dedham late afternoon, take an initial walk around the village. St Mary the Virgin church displays an original Constable painting, The Ascension. Dedham Hall, dating from 1400, stands at the village entrance—it regularly runs painting courses in Constable's footsteps. Take time to spot panoramas over the valley to better appreciate them tomorrow.

6
Day

Dedham

Walk down to Flatford, about twenty minutes from Dedham along the River Stour. Here John Constable (1776-1837), born in nearby East Bergholt, painted his most famous canvases: The Haywain (1821), Flatford Mill (1817), The Cornfield. The mill belonged to his father, a wealthy miller and grain merchant. The property is managed by the National Trust—you can see Willy Lott's House, the farmhouse appearing in the background of The Haywain, virtually unchanged since 1820. The small visitor centre at Bridge Cottage presents an exhibition on Constable's life and work. Allow 1.5 to 2 hours on site.

East Bergholt is Constable's birthplace. In a letter to his friend Fisher, he wrote: 'the sound of water escaping from mill dams, willows, old rotten planks, slimy posts... I love such things'. Push open the door of St Mary the Virgin church: it houses a 16th-century wooden bell cage, unique in England, because the planned tower was never built for lack of funds. Inside, don't miss the original of The Ascension by Constable.

→

The circular walk between Flatford, East Bergholt and Dedham (about 8km, 2.5 hours) is one of Suffolk's finest rambles. You cross meadows beside the Stour, pass scenes that inspired Constable and encounter views still recognizable today from the paintings. Bring waterproof shoes outside summer. Maps available at Flatford National Trust.

7
Day

Dedham

Lavenham is England's best-preserved medieval village—not just a marketing line. In the 16th century, this small town of fewer than 2,000 residents was England's 14th richest, paying more taxes than York or Lincoln. The fortune came from trading blue cloth ('Lavenham Blew'), exported as far as Russia. Merchants built timber-framed mansions inadvertently preserved: when trade collapsed in the early 16th century, nobody could afford to demolish or rebuild. Result: over 340 listed buildings. Wander the Market Place and enter the Corpus Christi Guildhall (16th century)—Lavenham cloth's characteristic blue came from the woad plant, its leaves producing a natural blue, hence the English expression 'dyed in the wool'. Each piece had to measure exactly 28 yards 28 inches long, checked by royal inspectors.

Long Melford, 8km north of Lavenham, has a long thoroughfare over a kilometre long lined with Tudor brick houses. Holy Trinity church is a masterpiece of perpendicular Gothic style, financed by 15th-century wool merchants, with wide windows flooding the nave with light. Melford Hall, a National Trust property, is an Elizabethan red-brick mansion built in the 1550s where Queen Elizabeth I stayed.

Kentwell Hall, at Long Melford's north end, is a 16th-century red-brick Tudor mansion surrounded by moats, at the end of a 700-metre lime-tree avenue. The property regularly hosts highly immersive Tudor historical re-enactments—check the programme before visiting.

Don't miss Sutton Hoo en route to Southwold—one of Europe's most important archaeological sites. In June 1939, self-taught archaeologist Basil Brown uncovered beneath a burial mound a 27-metre-long ship and a burial chamber filled with early 7th-century Anglo-Saxon treasures: 37 solid gold coins (Frankish tremisses), 5th-century Byzantine silverware, garnets from Sri Lanka and a human-faced helmet that became the symbol of the Anglo-Saxon period. The deceased is probably King Rædwald of East Anglia, who died around 625. The originals are in the British Museum, but the National Trust-managed site offers an excellent exhibition and visits to the mounds. Allow 1.5 hours.

Framlingham Castle is one of England's best-preserved medieval castles, with a wall-walk on the battlements offering clear views across the Suffolk countryside. Built between 1190 and 1210 by Roger Bigod, it's famous as the place where Mary Tudor awaited the battle result that would carry her to the English throne in 1553. The town of Framlingham invites a stroll: its market, pubs and quiet atmosphere are typically Suffolk.

→

Arriving in Southwold late afternoon, take an initial tour of the seafront. The white 31-metre lighthouse, built in 1887 right in the town centre, is open for visits—113 steps for a panoramic coastal view. On Gun Hill, six 18th-century cannons face the sea: they commemorate the 1672 Battle of Sole Bay where the Anglo-French and Dutch fleets clashed right here. An excellent appetizer for the rest of your stay.

Southwold Pier, built in 1900 to welcome steamships from London, has been rebuilt several times after storms. Its current 623-foot version dates from 2001. Enter the Under the Pier Show: a collection of deliberately absurd slot machines, the work of artist Tim Hunkin, where you can notably 'sell modern art with a machine gun'. The pier is free to access, games cost extra.

Southwold's multicoloured beach huts are the town's postcard image—there are 266, all privately owned. Originally they were Victorian 'bathing machines' on wheels allowing ladies to change unseen. The golden sand beach is one of Suffolk's finest. Heading back to the centre, explore the lanes around Market Place—independent shops, pubs, and the local brewery producing beers since 1872.

Walberswick, on the other bank of the Blyth, is a peaceful fishing village with a long sandy beach and RSPB-managed bird marshes. Much quieter than Southwold, it's a good counterpoint for anyone seeking solitude.

→

The small rowing ferry crossing between Southwold and Walberswick is one of the little joys of this coast. The service is seasonal (generally April to October)—otherwise take the bridge on foot, 20 minutes. On the other side, walk along the beach to the RSPB marshes for birdwatching, then return via the dunes. Allow 2 hours round trip.

Orford Castle is one of England's most fascinating castles, with its 21-sided polygonal keep—unique in the country. Henry II had it built between 1165 and 1173 to counter the power of Suffolk barons, notably Hugh Bigod of Framlingham. Construction cost £1,413 sterling, a colossal sum at the time, and detailed accounts are preserved in the royal Pipe Rolls. Climb to the roof for views over Orford Ness. Inside, read the legend of the Wildman of Orford: in 1167, fishermen supposedly caught in their nets a wild man covered in hair, held six months in the castle without ever speaking a word, before escaping to the sea.

Aldeburgh is a former Tudor port town turned discreet and elegant seaside resort, intimately linked to composer Benjamin Britten who founded the Aldeburgh Festival here in 1948—still one of Britain's major classical music festivals. On the shingle beach, the 16th-century Moot Hall now sits at the water's edge, after coastal erosion swallowed everything around it. Fishermen's huts sell smoked fish and crabs directly on the beach.

Thorpeness, 2km north of Aldeburgh, is an entirely artificial village designed from 1910 by eccentric Stuart Ogilvie: a neo-Tudor holiday community with a 65-acre artificial lake (the Meare). The 'House in the Clouds' perched at 21 metres actually concealed a 50,000-gallon water tower—decorated on the facade to look like an ordinary house so as not to spoil the village aesthetic. Impossible not to smile seeing this.

→

Small pedestrian ferry crossing between Southwold and Walberswick

→

Walk on Southwold Pier, built in 1900 as a landing stage for steamers bringing tourists from London. Dynamited during World War II to prevent a German landing, struck by a drifting mine in 1941, hit by storms in 1955, it was completely rebuilt and now extends 190 metres. It houses a collection of eccentric coin-operated machines created by inventor Tim Hunkin.

→

Visit the local brewery, established since 1872 on East Green, on a site where beer has been brewed for at least 670 years. Tasting of craft beers and spirits distilled on site.

Norwich was England's second city after London at the time of the Norman Conquest—that's why William the Conqueror immediately built a castle here. The current castle, with its Victorian facade (19th-century cladding replaced the original Norman stone), is now an excellent museum covering regional history, the Iceni and Boudica, the Vikings, and Norfolk arts. Take time to explore the dungeons and galleries.

Elm Hill is Norwich's most complete medieval street, and one of England's finest. Nearly all the buildings you see were constructed after the 1507 fire that ravaged over 700 houses in the city—only Briton's Arms, a thatched inn dating from 1347, survived the flames. Walking up the cobbled street, glance at the blue plaque of Pettus House (1550) and the stairs of merchants' houses whose backs gave directly onto the River Wensum.

Norwich Cathedral is one of England's great medieval cathedrals, begun in 1096 by Bishop Herbert de Losinga. Its 96-metre spire is surpassed only by Salisbury. It houses the world's largest collection of carved medieval vault bosses—over 1,000 painted bosses adorn nave and cloisters. The cloisters, England's largest, are worth a good half-hour alone. During summer, Shakespeare plays are performed here.

→

Late afternoon, arrive in Burnham Market to explore the village. This 'Chelsea of Norfolk' as locals call it is organized around a long grassy green flanked by Georgian brick houses. The independent shops, galleries and pubs make it a pleasant stop for an evening. It's an excellent base for the next two days on Norfolk's north coast.

Holkham Beach is breathtaking—at low tide, an expanse of white, smooth sand opens as far as the eye can see, framed by pine forests planted in the late 19th century by the 2nd Earl of Leicester to protect farmland from wind. It was in this golden light that Gwyneth Paltrow walked during the final scenes of Shakespeare in Love (1998). Holkham National Nature Reserve covers 3,900 hectares and welcomes large formations of pink-footed geese in winter. Arrive early in summer.

Holkham Hall is a Palladian mansion built between 1734 and 1764 by Thomas Coke, 1st Earl of Leicester, to designs by William Kent. The marble hall with its 15-metre pressed-stucco dome is one of 18th-century England's finest interiors. The state rooms house canvases by Rubens, Van Dyck, Claude Lorrain and Gainsborough. The 25,000-acre park with its lake and red deer can be visited by bike or on foot.

Wells-next-the-Sea is an active port with a colourful quay lined with fishing boats and smoked fish shops. The town added 'next-the-sea' to its name in the 19th century to distinguish itself from other Wells. Stroll along the quay, climb to the beach huts at the end of the embankment, and listen for the tide siren—a wartime relic that still sounds to warn bathers when the sea rises.

Walsingham is a pilgrimage village active since the Middle Ages—its 12th-century Augustinian priory ruins and Catholic and Anglican shrines make it one of England's most important Marian devotion sites. To reach it from Wells, take the Wells & Walsingham Light Railway, a 4-mile narrow-gauge steam train—the longest railway of this gauge in England.

Blakeney is one of Norfolk's most beautiful coastal villages, with its flint houses and quay opening onto an estuary of great beauty. At low tide, the harbour empties almost completely, leaving boats stranded on mudflats. Climb to St Nicholas church, built in 13th-century flint brick with a secondary tower that served as a lighthouse for sailors.

Cley next the Sea, just east of Blakeney, has a 17th-century windmill on the edge of the marshes that has become the photographic symbol of North Norfolk. The mouth of the River Glaven and surrounding marshes are a premier bird reserve—bring binoculars.

→

The boat trip to Blakeney Point to observe grey and common seals is one of Norfolk's most popular nature outings. The sand spit of Blakeney Point, managed by the National Trust since 1912, hosts one of Britain's largest grey seal colonies. Trips depart from Blakeney quay and last about 1.5 hours. Book ahead in high season.

→

The North Norfolk coast is best walked on the Norfolk Coast Path. From Burnham Market, the section to Holkham or Wells offers magnificent views over salt marshes and dunes. Allow 2 to 3 hours for a loop.

Sandringham Estate has been the British royal family's private residence since 1862, when Queen Victoria purchased it for her eldest son, the future Edward VII, for £220,000. The current red-brick Jacobean-style house was built in 1870 by architect A.J. Humbert. George V wrote of Sandringham: 'the place I love better than anywhere in the world'. George VI died here in 1952. Eight ground-floor rooms, virtually unchanged since Edwardian times, open to the public from late March to early October. The 24-hectare Country Park is open year-round for free.

St Mary Magdalene church, on the Sandringham estate, is the royal family's parish church—where the Christmas service watched by Britons on television each year is held. Built in the 14th century and extensively remodelled by Edward VII, it houses several royal memorials. Don't miss the Silver Jubilee Gate in solid silver, a gift from the royal family in 1977.

→

After the house and church, wander freely in Sandringham Country Park—24 hectares of woods and heathland open year-round. In season, the estate's organic farm sells its own produce at the shop. It's a good moment to catch your breath before the final day.

15
Day

Kings Lynn

King's Lynn, 15 minutes from Sandringham, is a medieval merchant town often ignored by tourist circuits—which makes it all the more pleasant. During the Middle Ages, it was one of England's busiest ports, exporting East Anglian wool to Flanders. The 1683 Custom House, on the Great Ouse quay, is one of 17th-century England's finest merchant buildings. Tuesday Market Place, one of the country's largest Georgian market squares, is surrounded by remarkably well-preserved 18th-century brick houses.

Before catching the train, climb to see the Trinity Guildhall on Saturday Market Place—a black-and-white chequered 15th-century building housing a town museum with King John's gold crown (brother of Richard the Lionheart), lost in the Fens marshes in 1216 and found by chance. King's Lynn station serves London King's Cross in 1h45 and Cambridge in 45 minutes.

→

End of the East Anglia circuit. From King's Lynn, the train takes you directly back to London or Cambridge without changing. If you're driving back, the A10 runs along the Fens and offers a few more kilometres of that flat, silent landscape that is the signature of the entire region.
