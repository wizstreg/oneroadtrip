The twin towers of the Keep rise 28 meters above the Sèvre Niortaise River. Listed as a historic monument since 1840, it's one of the finest Romanesque keeps in France.

Climb the spiral staircase to the terrace to contemplate the green expanses of the Marais Poitevin.

The refectory hall displays sculpted capitals and traditional Poitevin furniture with its elevated beds.

The museum presents the ethnographic collection and graffiti of English sailors imprisoned in the 18th century.

Walk along the banks of the Sèvre Niortaise to the Épona space where remains of the ancient sanctuary of Port Boinot are exhibited.

→

Stroll through the historic quarter between market halls and sculpted dragons that animate the city center.

→

The Bernard d'Agesci Museum brings together fine arts, decorative arts, and natural history collections in a grand cabinet of curiosities.

The Maison du Marais Poitevin occupies the former Maison de la Coutume on the square of the same name. The illuminated maraiscope traces the draining of the marsh.

The Church of the Holy Trinity dates from 830. Its watchtower on the side and its 18th-century funeral frieze testify to a turbulent history.

The narrow streets reveal low houses with whitewashed walls and blue or green shutters.

→

Board a traditional flat-bottomed boat from the Quai Louis Tardy. The boatmen tell stories of the marsh, its wildlife, and the mystery of fire on water.

The ruins of Maillezais Abbey dominate the marsh from a former island. It was a fortress, Romanesque abbey, then Gothic cathedral from the 11th to 17th centuries.

The cathedral measured 105 meters long and reached 60 meters high. Only 25 meters of the north wall's height and the two bell towers remain.

Climb the tower to contemplate the surrounding marsh excavated by monks as early as the 11th century.

The refectory and salt cellar testify to monastic life. François Rabelais stayed here for five years during the Renaissance.

An immersive film on three walls traces the history of the abbey in the former refectory.

→

Picnic in the shaded area with a view of the majestic ruins.

→

Board from the abbey's embarkation point to navigate the canals of the wet marsh.

The Royal Abbey of Celles-sur-Belle was rebuilt in the 15th century by order of Louis XI, then restored in the 17th century by architect François Le Duc, known as Toscane.

The Romanesque portal with six polylobed arches and sculpted capitals are remains of the first 12th-century construction.

The off-center monumental staircase leads to the nave. The north wing was never completed, hence the imbalance of the façade.

The ribbed-vaulted refectory extends over six bays. The cloister gallery with groined vaults dates from the 17th century.

The museum recreates the abbot's chamber and a monastic cell. Touchscreen tablets facilitate the discovery of liturgical objects.

→

Stroll through the formal French gardens, the insect garden, and the herb garden.

→

Discover the vintage motorcycle museum which presents models from 1903 to the present day.

The abbey church founded in the 5th century was rebuilt in the 17th century in Gothic style by the Benedictines of Saint-Maur after the Wars of Religion.

The 17th-century rood screen, sculpted woodwork, and sixteen-lobed monolithic baptismal font testify to the richness of the building.

The 7th-century crypt houses the relics of Saint Léger. Its barrel vaults and Romanesque capitals are listed as Historic Monuments.

The cloister and chapter house now host exhibitions and cultural events.

→

Visit the National School for Active Non-Commissioned Officers created in 1963, a unique training ground for the French Army.

💡 The town took the name Saint-Maixent-l'École in 1926
→

Stroll along the banks of the Sèvre Niortaise between half-timbered houses and 16th-century mansions.

The Saint-Jacques Gate opens onto the Way of Saint James. Its two massive elliptical towers and 13th-century fortified bridge dominate the Thouet River.

Climb the spiral staircase to the top of the tower to admire the panorama over the medieval town and the Thouet.

The Rue de la Vau Saint-Jacques displays its 15th-century half-timbered houses along the largest medieval street in Europe.

The ramparts lined with circular towers extend for three kilometers to the north and west, overlooking the rocky plateau.

The Georges Turpin Museum in the Maison des Cultures traces the town's history from the Middle Ages to the 20th century.

→

Follow the path along the ramparts with views of goats grazing near the preserved towers.

→

Cross the bridge of the Saint-Jacques Gate to contemplate the reflection of the fortifications in the calm waters of the Thouet.

The Abbey Church of Saint-Pierre from the 11th-13th centuries is a jewel of Poitevin Romanesque art. Its historiated capitals and Plantagenet vaults are exceptional.

The 19th-century mechanical cylinder organ, listed as a Historic Monument, resonates in the nave during performances.

The cenotaph of Pierre de Saine Fontaine, founder who died in 1110, testifies to the antiquity of the site.

The underground fountain can be discovered with the key lent by the Tourist Office.

The 19th-century market halls and 15th-16th century half-timbered houses border Place Saint-Pierre.

→

Climb up to the Old Castle for a superb panorama over the town and the Thouet Valley.

→

Follow Aldéarde's paths, ground-marked routes with medallions to discover the heritage.

The Château d'Oiron has housed the Curios & Mirabilia collection since 1993, contemporary artworks inspired by Renaissance cabinets of curiosities.

The 55-meter-long Renaissance Gallery illustrates the history of Troy. Its 16th-century frescoes are among the most important after Fontainebleau.

Christian Boltanski photographed Oiron's schoolchildren between 1993 and 2000, subverting the tradition of portrait galleries.

The painted and sculpted 17th-century woodwork dialogues with creations by 63 contemporary artists.

The adjoining collegiate church completes the visit to the estate of the Gouffier family, Grand Equerry of Henri II.

→

Stroll through the park designed by Pascal Cribier between 2005 and 2008 where contemporary art inhabits the green spaces.

→

Discover the village of Oiron where contemporary creation has taken over public squares and alleyways.

The castle of the Dukes of La Trémoille has dominated the rocky promontory since the 8th century. Jacques Lemercier redesigned it in the 17th century for Marie de La Tour d'Auvergne.

The Collegiate Church of Notre-Dame was built by Gabrielle de Bourbon-Montpensier from 1503 to 1515 in Flamboyant Gothic style with Renaissance influences.

The Church of Saint-Médard preserves its 12th-century Romanesque façade and sculpted portals. The 56-meter Gothic bell tower dominates the town.

The half-timbered houses of Place Saint-Médard and Rue du Château include the 15th-century Hôtel des Trois Rois.

→

Descend toward the collegiate chapel via Rue Imbert and Rue du Château where small houses and mansions stand side by side.

The 11th-century castle of the Lords of Beaumont unfolds 700 meters of walls punctuated by 38 towers on a rocky promontory.

Follow the restored ramparts and admire the romantic ruins of the 15th-century manor house.

The 19th-century neo-Gothic mansion hosts temporary exhibitions and sculpture symposiums.

The Church of Notre-Dame, founded by Thibault de Beaumont in the 11th century, features 12th-century Angevin domed vaults.

The 56-meter Renaissance bell tower inspired by Italy dominates the city. 19th-century frescoes adorn the choir.

→

From the castle esplanade, enjoy the view over the Chapel of Saint-Cyprien and the 19th-century former slaughterhouses.
