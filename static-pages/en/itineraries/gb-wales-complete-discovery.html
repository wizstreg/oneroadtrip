Cardiff

4 photos
Day 1
🔍

Discover

1
Explore Cardiff, the vibrant capital of Wales, where centuries of history blend seamlessly with modern Welsh culture. Cardiff Castle stands at the heart of the city, its medieval keep and Victorian Gothic apartments revealing layers of history from Roman times to the extravagant 19th-century makeover by the Marquess of Bute. The castle's fairy-tale towers and opulent interiors decorated with gold leaf, murals and intricate carvings create one of Britain's most remarkable heritage sites.
2
Wander through Cardiff's beautiful Victorian and Edwardian arcades, elegant covered shopping passages that have served the city for over a century. These ornate galleries with their independent boutiques, vintage shops and cozy cafes represent some of the finest examples of arcade architecture in Britain. The intricate ironwork, glass roofs and decorative tiles transport visitors back to the golden age of retail.
3
Marvel at the Wales Millennium Centre in Cardiff Bay, a stunning architectural masterpiece that serves as the home of Welsh national opera, ballet and theatre. The distinctive bronze-colored steel and slate facade features a poetic inscription visible from across the bay, while the interior showcases exceptional acoustics and contemporary Welsh design. Cardiff Bay itself offers waterfront dining, boat tours and the impressive Senedd building housing the Welsh Parliament.
4
Visit Castell Coch, the fairy-tale red castle perched in woodland on the northern outskirts of Cardiff, rebuilt in Victorian Gothic fantasy style by the same architect who transformed Cardiff Castle. The conical towers rising above the beech forest create a scene straight from a medieval romance, while the lavish interiors feature hand-painted walls and ceilings inspired by medieval manuscripts. Bute Park, stretching from the castle to the bay, offers peaceful green spaces along the River Taff.
🎯

Take a walking tour through the historic city center to discover hidden architectural gems and local legends

Tenby

10 photos
Day 2
🔍

Discover

1
Discover Tenby, a picturesque harbor town in Pembrokeshire whose rows of multi-colored Georgian houses have made it one of Wales' most beloved holiday destinations. The pastel-painted facades cascading down to the golden beaches and bustling harbor create postcard-perfect scenes that have attracted visitors for generations. This charming seaside resort offers the perfect blend of historic character, sandy beaches and traditional Welsh hospitality.
2
Explore the medieval town walls that still encircle much of Tenby's historic center, offering elevated walks with views over the harbor and out to Caldey Island. The narrow cobbled streets within the walls are lined with independent shops, galleries and restaurants serving fresh local seafood. St Catherine's Island, accessible at low tide, adds a touch of adventure with its Victorian fort perched dramatically above the waves.
3
Relax on Tenby's beautiful beaches, including the sheltered North Beach and the expansive South Beach backed by dramatic limestone cliffs. The clear blue waters and golden sands rival any Mediterranean resort, yet maintain an authentically Welsh character with traditional beach huts and family-friendly atmosphere. The harbor comes alive with fishing boats and pleasure craft, offering boat trips along the stunning Pembrokeshire coast.
🎯

Take a boat trip to Caldey Island to visit the Cistercian monastery and sample the monks' famous chocolate and perfume

St Davids

4 photos
Day 3
🔍

Discover

1
Marvel at the Green Bridge of Wales, a spectacular natural limestone arch dramatically jutting into the sea on the Pembrokeshire coast. This remarkable geological formation, carved by millennia of wave action, creates one of Wales' most photographed natural wonders. The nearby Elegug Stacks, two giant pillars of stone rising from the sea, add to this dramatic coastal landscape that showcases the raw power of nature.
2
Explore St Davids, Britain's smallest city and the most westerly point of Wales, built around its magnificent medieval cathedral. St Davids Cathedral, nestled in a peaceful hollow, is the final resting place of Wales' patron saint and has been a site of pilgrimage for over 1,500 years. The purple-hued stone building with its distinctive square tower represents one of the finest examples of medieval religious architecture in Britain.
3
Watch a spectacular sunset at Whitesands Beach near St Davids, one of the most stunning coastal experiences in Wales. This beautiful Blue Flag beach offers pristine golden sands backed by dramatic headlands, with views stretching across St Brides Bay. The sunsets here paint the sky in extraordinary colors, creating magical moments that visitors remember long after leaving.
🎯

Activities

→
Book a boat tour with Thousand Island Expeditions to Skomer Island, home to the largest Atlantic puffin colony in southern Britain

Aberaeron

2 photos
Day 4
🔍

Discover

1
Fall in love with Aberaeron, a gorgeous Georgian coastal town on Cardigan Bay that ranks among Wales' most photogenic destinations. The colorful townhouses lining the harbor in shades of pink, blue, yellow and green create a rainbow of facades reflected in the calm waters below. This planned Regency town, built in the early 19th century, retains its elegant proportions and charming character.
2
Stroll along Aberaeron's picturesque harbor, where traditional fishing boats bob alongside pleasure craft against a backdrop of painted Georgian houses. The harbor promenade offers perfect vantage points for photography, especially during the magical golden hour when sunset light illuminates the colorful facades. Local seafood restaurants serve the day's catch in this authentic Welsh fishing community.
3
Discover New Quay, a seaside town just 20 minutes south along Cardigan Bay famous for its layered colorful houses cascading down to sandy beaches. This charming village, said to have inspired Dylan Thomas's Under Milk Wood, offers spectacular coastal scenery and excellent opportunities for dolphin watching in the bay. The tiered rows of brightly painted cottages create stunning compositions against the blue waters of the Irish Sea.
🎯

Activities

→

Aberystwyth

3 photos
Day 5
🔍

Discover

1
Ride the historic Aberystwyth Cliff Railway, a charming funicular that has carried passengers up Constitution Hill since 1896. This Victorian engineering marvel offers one of the most spectacular viewpoints in Wales, revealing panoramic vistas over the town, Cardigan Bay and distant mountain peaks on clear days. The gentle ascent provides time to appreciate the beautifully maintained original carriages and mechanisms.
2
Explore Aberystwyth, a vibrant university town and seaside resort that serves as the cultural capital of Welsh-speaking Wales. The sweeping Victorian promenade curves around Cardigan Bay, lined with colorful hotels and guesthouses that have welcomed visitors since the railway age. The ruins of Aberystwyth Castle, perched on a headland, add historic drama to the coastal views.
3
Visit the National Library of Wales, one of only six copyright libraries in the UK, housing a vast collection of Welsh literature, manuscripts and historical records. The impressive Edwardian building on Penglais Hill overlooks the town and contains treasures including medieval Welsh poetry and the earliest surviving manuscript of the Mabinogion. The library's exhibitions bring Welsh culture and history vividly to life.
🎯

Activities

→
Walk along the Victorian promenade and watch the sunset over Cardigan Bay from the seafront

Tywyn

10 photos
Day 6
🔍

Discover

1
Journey aboard the Talyllyn Railway, the world's first preserved railway, known affectionately as 'The Railway with a Heart of Gold.' This historic steam train, operating since 1865, carries passengers on a scenic 55-minute journey from the coastal town of Tywyn seven miles into the heart of Snowdonia National Park. The gentle pace allows time to absorb the stunning Welsh landscapes, from pastoral valleys to dramatic mountain scenery.
2
Travel to Nant Gwernol, the railway's terminus deep in a wooded ravine surrounded by ancient oak forests and cascading waterfalls. The narrow-gauge locomotives puff and whistle through countryside that has changed little since Victorian times, creating an authentically nostalgic experience. Walking trails from the station lead to spectacular viewpoints and the atmospheric ruins of old slate quarries.
3
Explore Tywyn, a traditional Welsh seaside town that serves as the gateway to the Talyllyn Railway and the southern reaches of Snowdonia. The town's long sandy beach stretches for miles, backed by dunes and offering views across Cardigan Bay to the Llŷn Peninsula. St Cadfan's Church houses the Cadfan Stone, bearing the earliest known written Welsh, connecting visitors to 1,400 years of Welsh linguistic heritage.
🎯

Activities

→
Hike from Nant Gwernol station through the forest to discover hidden waterfalls and old quarry workings

Harlech

3 photos
Day 7
🔍

Discover

1
Stand in awe before Harlech Castle, a magnificent 13th-century fortress built by King Edward I that dominates the landscape from its dramatic clifftop position overlooking Tremadog Bay. This UNESCO World Heritage site, one of the finest examples of late 13th-century military architecture in Europe, inspired the famous Welsh anthem 'Men of Harlech.' The castle's massive walls and towers seem to grow from the rocky outcrop itself, creating an unforgettable silhouette against sea and mountain.
2
Explore the castle's impressive gatehouse, one of the strongest built during the medieval period, designed to withstand the most determined siege. The views from the battlements stretch across to the Llŷn Peninsula, along the coast to the mountains of Snowdonia, and on clear days all the way to Ireland. The atmospheric ruins evoke centuries of Welsh history, from Edward's conquest to Owain Glyndŵr's legendary siege.
3
Discover Harlech's beautiful beach, a vast expanse of golden sand stretching below the castle cliffs that offers a dramatic contrast to the fortress above. The beach, backed by dunes and with the castle as a constant backdrop, provides unique photographic opportunities combining medieval architecture with natural beauty. The small town of Harlech itself retains a quiet Welsh character, with traditional shops and welcoming local pubs.
🎯

Activities

→

Portmeirion

3 photos
Day 8
🔍

Discover

1
Enter the whimsical world of Portmeirion, an enchanting Italianate village on the North Wales coast that feels like a slice of the Amalfi Coast transported to the shores of Snowdonia. Designed by visionary architect Sir Clough Williams-Ellis between 1925 and 1975, this unique village features colorful buildings, campaniles, and piazzas nestled in subtropical gardens cascading to the Dwyryd Estuary. The pastel-painted facades and Mediterranean architecture create an otherworldly atmosphere unlike anywhere else in Britain.
2
Wander through Portmeirion's labyrinthine paths discovering hidden courtyards, ornate fountains and architectural salvage from across Europe incorporated into Williams-Ellis's grand design. The village gained worldwide fame as the setting for the cult 1960s television series 'The Prisoner,' and fans still visit to explore the iconic locations. Every corner reveals unexpected details, from classical statues to whimsical follies, creating endless opportunities for exploration.
3
Explore the extensive woodland gardens surrounding the village, where exotic plants thrive in the mild coastal climate created by the Gulf Stream. The wild gardens, planted with rhododendrons, hydrangeas and rare specimens from around the world, offer miles of scenic coastal walks along the estuary. The views across to Snowdonia's peaks provide a stunning backdrop to this unique architectural fantasy.
🎯

Activities

→
Take a scenic coastal walk through the woodland gardens with views across the estuary to Snowdonia

Snowdonia

3 photos
Day 9
🔍

Discover

1
Immerse yourself in the breathtaking landscapes of Snowdonia National Park, home to Wales' highest peaks and most dramatic mountain scenery. The park encompasses 823 square miles of rugged mountains, glacial lakes, ancient forests and wild moorland that has inspired artists, poets and adventurers for centuries. On clear days, the views across the peaks create some of the most spectacular panoramas in Britain.
2
Challenge yourself on Mount Snowdon, the highest mountain in Wales at 1,085 meters, whose summit can be reached by several hiking trails of varying difficulty or by the famous Snowdon Mountain Railway. The Pyg Track and Miners' Track are popular routes offering stunning views over Glaslyn and Llyn Llydaw, glacial lakes that shimmer like jewels in their mountain bowls. On the summit, on clear days, views extend to Ireland, Scotland and England.
3
Discover the park's hidden valleys, cascading waterfalls and ancient oak woodlands that create a landscape of endless variety and beauty. Historic slate quarries, their terraces carved into mountainsides, tell the story of the industry that shaped North Wales and exported Welsh slate across the world. The mountain villages, with their stone cottages and Welsh-speaking communities, maintain traditions stretching back centuries.
🎯

Activities

→
Take the Snowdon Mountain Railway from Llanberis for a scenic ascent to the summit without the hiking effort

Caernarfon

6 photos
Day 10
🔍

Discover

1
Marvel at Caernarfon Castle, the mightiest of Edward I's Welsh fortresses and a UNESCO World Heritage site that has dominated this strategic spot for over 700 years. The castle's unique polygonal towers and banded masonry were deliberately designed to echo the walls of Constantinople, emphasizing Edward's imperial ambitions. This is where the investiture of Princes of Wales has taken place since 1911, most recently for Prince Charles in 1969.
2
Climb the castle's towers for spectacular views over the Menai Strait, the town's slate rooftops, and the mountains of Snowdonia rising dramatically to the east. The Eagle Tower, the largest of the castle's distinctive towers, offers the most impressive panorama and contains exhibitions on the castle's history and the Welsh princes it was built to suppress. The castle walls, remarkably intact after seven centuries, allow visitors to walk above the medieval town.
3
Explore the charming town of Caernarfon, where the castle serves as a dramatic backdrop to narrow streets lined with independent shops, cafes and traditional Welsh pubs. The town walls, largely intact, connect to the castle and offer elevated walks with views over the harbor where boats depart for trips along the Menai Strait. The Welsh language thrives here, giving visitors an authentic taste of Welsh culture and heritage.
🎯

Activities

→
Photograph the castle from the town streets where medieval towers frame views of shops and daily Welsh life

Conwy

5 photos
Day 11
🔍

Discover

1
Explore Conwy Castle, one of the finest medieval fortresses in Europe and a masterpiece of 13th-century military architecture that dominates this historic walled town. Built by Edward I between 1283 and 1289, the castle retains all eight of its massive towers and much of its curtain wall, creating an atmospheric monument that feels authentically medieval rather than over-restored. The combination of ruined sections and intact towers gives visitors a genuine sense of stepping back through centuries of history.
2
Climb the castle towers for extraordinary views over the harbor, the medieval town, and Snowdonia's peaks rising in the distance. The vantage point allows photography across to other towers, creating dramatic landscape shots that appear almost drone-captured. On sunny days, the light plays across the ancient stones while boats bob in the harbor below, creating scenes of timeless beauty.
3
Walk the complete circuit of Conwy's medieval town walls, the most complete in Britain, which protected the English settlement Edward I created alongside his castle. The walls stretch for over three-quarters of a mile, studded with 21 towers and offering elevated views over the town's slate rooftops, the castle and the estuary. The Smallest House in Great Britain, a tiny red fisherman's cottage on the quay, adds a quirky footnote to the town's history.
🎯

Activities

→
Arrive early when the castle opens to photograph the towers without other visitors for the most dramatic shots

Llandudno

3 photos
Day 12
🔍

Discover

1
Discover Llandudno, Wales' largest seaside resort, a beautifully preserved Victorian town nestled between two limestone headlands on the North Wales coast. The elegant sweep of the promenade, lined with pastel-painted hotels and guest houses, curves around the bay with the Great Orme rising dramatically at its northern end. This classic British seaside town has maintained its Victorian character while offering modern attractions.
2
Ride the Great Orme Tramway, Britain's only cable-hauled street tramway, which has carried passengers up the headland since 1902. The vintage trams climb through residential streets before emerging onto the open hillside with spectacular views unfolding across the bay, town and mountains beyond. At the summit, panoramic vistas stretch across the Irish Sea to the Isle of Man on clear days.
3
Take the Llandudno Cable Cars for a different perspective on the Great Orme's dramatic landscapes and the town spread out below. The longest passenger cable car system in Britain glides over limestone grasslands grazed by wild Kashmir goats, descendants of a herd gifted by the Shah of Persia in 1837. The summit offers walking trails, an ancient copper mine dating back 4,000 years, and endless views over sea and shore.
🎯

Activities

→
Walk or drive to the summit of the Great Orme for sunset views over the Irish Sea and Snowdonia mountains

Anglesey

4 photos
Day 13
🔍

Discover

1
Journey to South Stack Lighthouse on the island of Anglesey, perched dramatically on a rocky islet at the northwestern tip of Wales. Built in 1809 to guide ships through the treacherous waters around Holy Island, this iconic lighthouse requires a descent of 400 steps to reach, rewarding visitors with spectacular clifftop scenery along the way. The lighthouse itself can be climbed for views across to Ireland and along the rugged Anglesey coastline.
2
Marvel at the dramatic sea cliffs surrounding South Stack, home to thousands of seabirds including guillemots, razorbills and, during the right season, adorable puffins that nest in the rocky crevices. The RSPB nature reserve offers walking trails along the clifftops with observation points for watching the incredible birdlife and the waves crashing against the rocks far below. The wild, windswept landscape represents Wales at its most dramatically beautiful.
3
Explore Anglesey, the largest island in Wales, connected to the mainland by two bridges including Thomas Telford's historic suspension bridge. Beaumaris Castle, another of Edward I's mighty fortresses, represents the most technically perfect medieval castle in Britain, while the island's beaches, prehistoric sites and charming villages reward further exploration. The island's Welsh name, Ynys Môn, reflects its importance as the heartland of Welsh language and culture.
🎯

Activities

→
Visit during spring or early summer for the chance to spot puffins nesting on the cliffs
