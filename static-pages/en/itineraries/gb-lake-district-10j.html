Lake District and North-East Loop: Lakes, Dales and Cathedrals

Mist rising from Ullswater, a Norman cathedral on its rocky spur, bridges that tilt like winking eyes - northern England isn't a destination, it's an accumulation of firsts.

From industrial Manchester to Cumbria's mirror-still lakes, across Yorkshire's wild dales to the medieval walls of York, Durham's towering cathedral and Newcastle's iconic bridges - this 12-day circuit cuts through northern England's deepest history and most dramatic landscapes. You'll walk in Wordsworth's footsteps at Grasmere, touch stones raised 5,000 years ago at Castlerigg, read England's story in York Minster's stained glass, and learn that Newcastle's Tyne Bridge served as the full-scale prototype for Sydney's harbour crossing.

Key highlights (7)
  • Castlerigg Stone Circle, raised around 3200 BC on a plateau with 360° views of the fells
  • Wordsworth's Dove Cottage at Grasmere, where I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud was written
  • Richmond's Norman castle, with more 11th-century architecture than any other in England
  • York Minster and its 128 medieval windows, built over 252 years
  • Durham Cathedral, England's greatest Norman monument, UNESCO-listed since 1986
  • Newcastle's Tyne Bridge, which served as full-scale trial for Sydney Harbour Bridge
  • Leeds' Royal Armouries, national arms collection with the world's only Indian elephant armour

Manchester

9.2 Stock Exchange Hotel, Manchester, Autograph Collection
9.2 Staycity Aparthotels Manchester Piccadilly
9.2 Leven Manchester

Start at the Science and Industry Museum - it's free, and you're standing on the site of the world's very first passenger railway station, Liverpool Road, opened in 1830. The station and its warehouse are Grade I listed, and it shows. Look for the Manchester Baby: this 5-metre-long monster is a faithful replica of the world's first stored-program computer, built here in 1948. The original was dismantled piece by piece to feed other machines - nothing remains. Volunteers run the replica at fixed times, using the same original vacuum tubes. Note that some galleries are under renovation right now - check the website before you visit.

Take a wander through the Northern Quarter - think Brooklyn or Paris's Marais, colder and more direct. The streets around Oldham Street are plastered with street art, record shops and specialty coffee houses. This is where Factory Records launched Joy Division, New Order and Happy Mondays in 1978. If you like rummaging, Afflecks Palace on Church Street is a sprawling vintage indoor market that captures what Manchester has in its bones.

If you've got an hour to spare, push open the door to the John Rylands Library on Deansgate - entry is free and the neo-Gothic hall from 1900 is worth the trip on its own. In the display cases, you'll find the oldest known page of the New Testament: a 2nd-century papyrus fragment, a few square centimetres that have survived 1,900 years. Few people know this library exists; it's one of Manchester's most astonishing places.

→

Manchester is a city passionate about football to its core. Old Trafford (Manchester United, 74,879 capacity) and the Etihad Stadium (Manchester City, 53,400 capacity) offer guided tours of changing rooms and pitches. If you're lucky enough to catch a match, the atmosphere is unmatched - book tickets several weeks ahead.

→

Deansgate and the Castlefield area offer plenty of lively pubs for a properly Mancunian evening. Local beers from Marble Brewery or Cloudwater represent the current British craft beer scene at its best.

Windermere

Welcome to the Lake District - UNESCO-listed since 2017, but this landscape has been changing people for far longer. This is where the English Romantics invented, in the 18th century, the very idea of going to the mountains for pleasure. Stop first at Ambleside to get your bearings and kit up if needed: it's the gateway to the park and outdoor shops are plentiful. Spot the Bridge House as you pass - a tiny 17th-century house perched on a bridge over Stock Ghyll, so narrow you wonder how a family could have lived there.

Dove Cottage in Grasmere is where it all began for English Romantic poetry. Wordsworth and his sister Dorothy lived here from 1799 to 1808 - eight years during which he wrote I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud, inspired by an entry in his sister's journal describing daffodils seen near Ullswater. The building was previously an inn, the Dove and Olive, open since 1617 - which explains the low ceilings and thick walls. Wordsworth paid £8 annual rent and received Coleridge, Walter Scott and Thomas de Quincey. The visit is guided and in small groups: book online ahead, slots fill fast in summer.

Take an hour to stroll around Grasmere: St Oswald's church, where Wordsworth attended Sunday service and where he's buried in the churchyard, creates a particular atmosphere. Grasmere lake a stone's throw away, with its rowing boats for hire at the north end, is a good spot to decompress after visiting Dove Cottage.

→

The hike around Loughrigg Fell from Grasmere is one of the park's most accessible: 8km, 200 metres elevation gain, with views over Grasmere and Rydal Water. Allow 2h30 at an easy pace. The path is waymarked and walkable year-round, but waterproof boots remain essential.

→

Grasmere Gingerbread is a unique specialty: half-biscuit half-cake, spiced, sold since 1854 from the same shop beside the church. The original recipe is kept in a safe at the local bank. Join the queue - it's worth it.

Hill Top at Near Sawrey goes far beyond Peter Rabbit nostalgia. Beatrix Potter bought this farmhouse in 1905 with her book royalties, then transformed herself into a fierce Herdwick sheep breeder and Lake District conservation campaigner. When she died in 1943, she bequeathed to the National Trust 4,000 acres and 15 farms on condition they remain worked according to traditional methods - it's largely thanks to her that the park still looks as it does. The house has remained almost unchanged since 1943. Book your slot online ahead - it's mandatory in high season and places go quickly.

Hawkshead is the Lake District's best-preserved medieval village - its cobbled lanes and whitewashed 16th-century houses haven't been disfigured by modern rebuilding. This is where the young Wordsworth studied at the Grammar School between 1779 and 1787; his desk, carved with his name, is still visible. Push open the door to the Beatrix Potter Gallery - housed in the former office of the solicitor she married, it displays original illustrations from her books. Hawkshead is quieter than Windermere and deserves lingering.

→

The ascent of Catbells from Keswick Landing Stage or Hawse End is one of the Lake District's most popular hikes: 451 metres elevation gain, a narrow ridge with 360° views over Derwentwater, Borrowdale and the surrounding fells. Alfred Wainwright described it as 'a hill for all'. Allow 3h with photo stops. The descent can be made on the Newlands Valley side to vary the route.

→

A cruise on Lake Windermere - England's largest natural lake at 17km long - is the most contemplative way to end the day. Boats run back and forth between Ambleside, Bowness and Lakeside. Take the last departure in the evening for the light.

Arrive at Castlerigg early morning, before the tour coaches - at 7am mist still covers the fells and you'll be alone with 38 stones raised 5,000 years ago. This circle of volcanic lava, built around 3200 BC, is probably older than Stonehenge. From inside, you see Helvellyn, Skiddaw and Blencathra in every direction - the Neolithic builders chose this plateau like a natural amphitheatre. Coleridge had visited it with Wordsworth in 1799 and described it as a place where 'the mountains stand in order as if summoned by magicians'. Free access, managed by the National Trust. From Keswick centre, allow 30 minutes on foot.

The Derwent Pencil Museum in Keswick tells a story you wouldn't expect - it's free, by the Leeds Dock, and frankly impressive. Here, in Borrowdale a few kilometres away, the world's first pure graphite deposit was discovered in 1565. The material was so strategic that miners slept on-site to prevent theft and the Crown banned its export. The first pencils were carved locally, wrapped in string. Allow 45 minutes - it's an original stop 10 minutes' walk from the centre, and makes a change from fells.

Make the detour to Ullswater, 30 minutes south-east of Keswick - many consider it the Lake District's most beautiful lake, and they have good reasons. It's precisely on its shores that Dorothy Wordsworth noted in 1802 having seen 'a crowd of golden daffodils' - the entry that inspired her brother's most famous poem. Take the steamer one way and return on foot via the lakeside path: the Ullswater Steamers have been in service since the 1850s and the combination of both gives you the best perspectives on the lake.

→

A cruise on Derwentwater with the Keswick Launches allows you to circuit the lake in 50 minutes, with stops at several landings that open onto paths of varying lengths. The full circuit gives access to Friars Crag, a promontory that John Ruskin described as 'one of the three most beautiful scenes in Europe'.

→

For experienced walkers, the ascent of Helvellyn via the Striding Edge ridge is the Lake District's most spectacular hike: 950 metres elevation gain, a rocky arête exposed on both sides, and a view from the summit that embraces the entire park. Allow 6 to 7h from Patterdale. Not recommended in bad weather or winter without proper equipment.

Aira Force is worth the detour even in rain - especially in rain, actually. This 20-metre waterfall cascades through a wooded gorge at Ullswater's northern tip, National Trust property since 1906. Two stone bridges span it at different heights: climb first to the upper bridge to see the fall from above, then descend to the lower bridge to feel the spray. Wordsworth drew inspiration from it for several poems. The car park is paid but entry to the falls is free. Allow 1h30 round trip from the car park.

Glenridding is a tiny village at the foot of Helvellyn, on Ullswater's edge. This is where the Ullswater Steamers and summit hikes depart from. If you didn't climb Catbells yesterday and your legs still have something to give, Place Fell from Glenridding is a less-known but superb alternative - fewer crowds, same lake views.

→

The Ullswater Steamers are steam-powered boats in service since the 1850s, England's oldest regular steamboats. The cruise between Pooley Bridge and Glenridding takes about 35 minutes each way. Combine the outbound cruise with the lakeside path for the return for a perfect half-day.

Did you know that Richmond in Virginia, Richmond in Australia and Richmond-upon-Thames in London all owe their names to this small Yorkshire town? Henry Tudor had inherited the title Earl of Richmond; becoming Henry VII, he baptised his palace at Sheen with the name of his Yorkshire property - and the London district has borne that name ever since. Climb to Richmond Castle to understand why: perched on a cliff above the Swale, built by Alan the Red after the Norman Conquest around 1071, it possesses more 11th-century Norman architecture than any other castle in England. Local legend claims King Arthur and his knights sleep beneath the foundations - the English love their legends.

Don't miss the Georgian Theatre Royal just below the castle - it's Britain's most intact Georgian theatre, founded in 1788, closed in 1848 and used as a warehouse for a century before being restored and reopened in 1963. Its 200-seat auditorium with original painted decor is tiny and fascinating. If there's a performance during your visit, it's a rare experience not to be missed.

Take the road west from Richmond and you enter Swaledale - the wildest of the Yorkshire Dales. Dry-stone walls divide pastures to infinity, 18th-century hay barns punctuate the hills, villages like Reeth and Muker seem unchanged for two centuries. Drive to Keld at the valley's end for the Kisdon Force waterfalls - it's the road's terminus and everything beyond is on foot.

→

Hiking in Swaledale to the hidden waterfalls around Keld: several short trails (2-4km) lead to Kisdon Force and Catrake Force from the village. The landscape of moorland and dry-stone walls is different from anything you've seen in the Lake District.

→

The Wensleydale Creamery at Hawes, heading up the valley of the same name from Richmond, offers creamery tours with tasting. Wensleydale is a crumbly, slightly honeyed cheese, produced in this valley since Cistercian monks from Roquefort settled here in 1156 and brought their maturing techniques. Hawes is about 45km from Richmond.

Aysgarth Falls in Wensleydale is the Thames in Yorkshire version: the River Ure tumbles over three levels of limestone slabs through an oak forest. Robin Hood: Prince of Thieves (1991) was filmed here. Make the detour to the Lower Falls - less frequented than the Upper and considerably more impressive after rain, which in England isn't a rare problem.

Skipton Castle deserves more than a glance from the street. Built from 1090, remodelled in the 14th century, it withstood a three-year siege during the Civil War (1642-1645) before being partially dismantled on Cromwell's orders. Lady Anne Clifford restored it in the 1650s - the yew tree she planted in the inner courtyard is still there, three and a half centuries later. Climb onto the ramparts for views over the town and canal. It's one of England's best-preserved medieval castles and tickets remain reasonable.

Make the detour to Bolton Abbey, 8km east of Skipton on the banks of the Wharfe - one of England's most beautiful monastic ruins, and entry is free for pedestrians. The 12th-century Augustinian ruins sit alongside a priory still in service for Sunday worship, creating a strange and endearing mix. The estate belongs to the Duke of Devonshire. The 5km walk around the river's meander is easy and magnificent - allow 1h30.

→

Skipton market is one of Yorkshire's oldest and liveliest, held since 1204. It runs Monday, Wednesday, Friday and Saturday on the High Street. If you pass on one of those days, you'll find local cheeses, charcuterie, artisan breads and produce from Dales farms.

💡 Market on Monday, Wednesday, Friday and Saturday
→

The walk along Skipton's ramparts follows the Leeds-Liverpool Canal - Britain's longest single-lock canal (204km, completed 1816). A 3km round trip from the castle gives you a sense of the peaceful atmosphere of Yorkshire's canals.

York Minster stops you dead as you enter the city - it dominates everything. It's northern Europe's largest medieval cathedral, and its construction lasted 252 years (1220-1472). The first church on this site was built in wood in 627 for the baptism of King Edwin of Northumbria. Inside, look up: 128 medieval stained glass windows, the UK's largest collection - including the Great East Window, surface-equivalent to a tennis court. In 1984, lightning cracked the Rose Window into 40,000 pieces; restoration traces are visible if you look closely. Climb the 275 steps of the central tower - the view over York from up there is the city's best. Allow 2h minimum.

The Shambles is mentioned in the Domesday Book of 1086 - back then, butchers hung meat from hooks still visible on some facades. The overhanging upper storeys lean so close from one side to the other you could almost shake hands from window to window. The street is often cited as the inspiration for Diagon Alley in Harry Potter, which J.K. Rowling has never confirmed or denied. Avoid it between 11am and 4pm in summer - the flow is relentless; early morning or evening, it's a different atmosphere.

The National Railway Museum is the world's largest railway museum - and it's free. Look first for Mallard: this locomotive set the world speed record for steam in 1938, 202.8 km/h, a record that still stands. Move on to Queen Victoria's and Edward VII's royal silk-and-wood carriages - the gap between royal comfort and third-class carriages in the same era is striking. A 1960s Japanese Shinkansen sits alongside a Eurostar in the same shed: 150 years of engineering in a single glance.

→

The Jorvik Viking Centre is installed exactly where a Viking quarter was excavated in the 1970s - 1,000 years of organic waste perfectly preserved in clay, including shoes, combs, food and even intestinal parasites. You board a cart that transports you to the archaeological level, with sound and smell reconstruction - yes, smells are included. Book online: without booking, the queue can exceed an hour, and it's for about 45 minutes of visit.

→

York's walls are England's best-preserved: 3.4km continuous and freely accessible, built in the 13th-14th centuries on Roman and Viking foundations. The stretch between Bootham Bar and Monk Bar is best - direct views of York Minster, gardens below, medieval lanes visible from the battlements. Allow 1h30 for the full circuit, less if you only do the northern section.

York Castle Museum occupies the city's former prisons, including the one where Dick Turpin, the famous highwayman, awaited his execution in 1739. Inside, you'll find a fully reconstructed Victorian street - Kirkgate - with its shops, blacksmith and horse-drawn omnibus. This isn't a dusty museum: the reconstruction is dense enough to linger in. The 20th-century toy room is northern England's most nostalgic collection - allow an hour and a half.

York's Chocolate Story answers a question everyone should ask: how did a provincial Yorkshire town become the capital of British chocolate? Terry's opened here in 1767 - the first shop was near Bootham Bar - and Rowntree's followed in 1862, inventing the KitKat, Smarties and Aero. The guided tour takes you from the ritual origins of cocoa in Central America to Yorkshire production lines, with tasting included. Book online - tickets are cheaper there and there may be a wait in high season.

→

Don't laugh: York is officially England's most haunted city according to the Society for Psychical Research - it's not marketing. Walking Ghost Tours run every evening through the historic centre's lanes. The best-known stop is the Treasurer's House, where a plumber claimed in 1953 to have seen a legion of Roman soldiers marching through the basement - at mid-leg height, as if following a Roman road buried since. Archaeological investigation did indeed find Roman paving at that depth. Departures generally at 8pm, allow 1h30.

→

To finish the evening gently, descend to the Ouse from Lendal Bridge and walk to Skeldergate Bridge - 2km round trip along the riverbanks. The riverside pub terraces are the right addresses for a last pint before heading back.

Leeds

The Royal Armouries Museum is Leeds' big surprise - free, by the Leeds Dock, and frankly impressive. What was crammed into the Tower of London for lack of space was transferred here in 1996 and fills five floors. Start with the Hall of Steel: 2,500 pieces of military equipment arranged over the building's full height like an immense tapestry of arms. Then find the 16th-century Indian elephant armour on the fourth floor - the only one known in a public museum worldwide, mail and plates for an entire animal. And Henry VIII's tournament armours: they prove he was, when young, slim and athletic. Late portraits show only half the story. Allow a good half-day.

The Victoria Quarter in Leeds centre is the best lesson in Victorian architecture you'll find in a shopping centre. Architect Frank Matcham - better known for his theatres - designed in 1900 arcades in marble, mahogany and rose terracotta, with a canopy that's Europe's largest in coloured glass. The Art Nouveau golden mosaics under the central dome represent the trades that made Leeds' fortune. Harvey Nichols opened its first shop outside London here in 1996. You can enter just to look at the ceiling.

If you have a car and 30 minutes, detour via Kirkstall Abbey - 5km west of the centre along the River Aire. Founded in 1152 by Cistercian monks from Fountains Abbey, dissolved by Henry VIII in 1539, its ruins are among England's best preserved: in some parts, walls stand to original roof level. Turner and Girtin painted these ruins when Romanticism had transformed old stones into artistic subjects. It's a public park, free entry, quiet, by the water.

→

Leeds Corn Exchange - a grain exchange built in 1863 - is a masterpiece of cast iron and glass with an elliptical dome reminiscent of Paris's Bourse de Commerce. Converted into a market of independent shops: record stores, local designers, cafés. Right next door, Leeds Kirkgate Market has been active since the Middle Ages and remains one of Europe's largest covered markets - hundreds of stalls, real neighbourhood life.

→

Leeds is 2h15 from London King's Cross by direct train - one of northern England's best-served routes. The station is a 20-minute walk from the Dock. If you're continuing elsewhere, Birmingham, Manchester and Edinburgh are all accessible in under 2h30 from here.

Turn the corner at Palace Green and you'll stop dead - Durham Cathedral is there, in golden sandstone, perched on its promontory above the Wear as if it had always been there. And it almost has: built between 1093 and 1133, in just forty years, it's England's greatest Norman monument. What few people know: the pointed ribs of its ceiling are the first of their kind in the world - Durham invented the structural system that would become Gothic, before Notre-Dame de Paris. Find the Galilee Chapel to the west: a simple stone vault houses the relics of Bede the Venerable, the man who in the 8th century laid the foundations of historical writing in Europe. Allow at least 1h30 - there's much to see and the place deserves unhurried time.

Right beside the cathedral on the same rocky spur, Durham Castle has been inhabited by students since 1837 - it's the world's most improbable university residence. Built in 1072 on William the Conqueror's orders, it was the base of Durham's Prince-Bishops, who had near-total autonomy: their own army, their own taxes, their own coinage. They guarded the border with Scotland. The Norman chapel from 1078 is the oldest part you can visit. Warning: entry by guided tour only, students really do live there.

Descend to the Wear's banks and walk around the peninsula - 2km of wooded path below the ramparts, with upward views of cathedral and castle. This is where you understand why the Normans chose exactly this spot: the river protects three sides of the plateau, leaving only one point to fortify. Take the path on the east side in early afternoon, the light falls well on the golden stones.

→

Durham students have a superstition: climbing the cathedral tower's 325 steps before getting your degree brings bad luck. You don't have that problem - climb, the view over the peninsula and surrounding hills is worth the effort. If you arrive on a Saturday morning, the market around Palace Green offers local cheeses and breads from North Yorkshire. The rest of the time, Saddler Street and its pubs are where Durham really lives.

Newcastle Upon Tyne

Go down to the Quayside and look at the bridges. The Tyne Bridge - the great green arch inaugurated in 1928 by King George V - is Newcastle's icon, and it hides a story almost nobody knows. The same engineering firm, Dorman Long of Middlesbrough, later built Australia's Sydney Harbour Bridge. At the opening, their representative said clearly that Newcastle had served as a 'full-scale trial for the Sydney bridge'. Compare the two photos: the arches are virtually identical. Another detail: at its construction, the Tyne Bridge was Britain's longest steel arch bridge.

Cross the Gateshead Millennium Bridge on foot - it's the world's only tilting bridge of this type, and it's as simple as that. Its two steel arches tilt together like a blinking eye, to let boats pass with 25 metres clearance. The manoeuvre takes 4 minutes and a half: 36,000 people gathered on the riverbanks to watch the first tilt in June 2001. On the other side on the Gateshead bank, the BALTIC Centre for Contemporary Art is in a former flour mill converted in 2002 - it's free, and exhibitions change regularly. Go up to the café on the top floor: you have the best view of the Tyne's seven bridges.

Newcastle Castle Keep deserves 45 minutes - and not because it's grand, but because it's strange. The castle that gave the city its name is today wedged between two Victorian railway viaducts that literally pass through its former bailey. Henry II had it built in 1167 on an earlier Norman fortification. Climb to the top of the keep for views over the Tyne and bridges - and to see how thoroughly the Victorians had no qualms about running their trains straight through the middle of a medieval castle.

→

If you want to see Newcastle off the beaten track, head to the Ouseburn Valley - 15 minutes' walk east of the centre. This is the creative quarter: artist studios, micro-breweries, concerts in converted Victorian warehouses. The Victoria Tunnel, a former 19th-century coal transport tunnel dug beneath the city, offers headlamp tours. Book ahead, slots go quickly.

→

The Quayside in the evening is a Newcastle institution. Bars and restaurants line both banks between the Tyne Bridge and Swing Bridge, and the Geordie atmosphere - that's the nickname for Newcastle residents - is direct and warm. If you want to try local food: the stottie is a thick round bread typical of the region, and pease pudding (split pea purée) is eaten in sandwiches. You won't find this anywhere else in England.
