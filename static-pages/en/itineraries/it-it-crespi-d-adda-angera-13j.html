Lombardy's Most Beautiful Villages

UNESCO company towns, 15th-century Hebrew presses, Sforza fortresses, and lakeside strongholds where Napoleon slept. Lombardy connects every chapter of its history.

From Crespi d'Adda, a UNESCO company town frozen in 19th-century industrial amber, to the ramparts of Soncino where European Hebrew printing was born, from the Mincio marshes and the sanctuary of Grazie to the morainic hills of Castellaro Lagusello, then from the limonaie of Limone sul Garda to the medieval forges of Bienno and the Roman baths of Bormio—Lombardy unfolds its villages across ten centuries of history. Lake Como concludes the journey between the Cistercian abbey of Piona, the gardens of Varenna and Bellagio, before the Rocca Borromea of Angera closes this grand itinerary on Lake Maggiore.

Key highlights (4)
  • Crespi d'Adda: Europe's only complete UNESCO-listed company town
  • Varenna and Bellagio: Lake Como jewels with terraced botanical gardens
  • Bormio: thousand-year-old thermal baths at the gateway to Stelvio Pass
  • Soncino: birthplace of Hebrew printing in 15th-century Italy

Crespi D Adda

Top hotels
9.1 Crespi Apartments Six
9.6 Crespi d'Adda a "colori"
9.2 B&B Casa Lulù
9.4 Le vigne sull’Adda

Walk down Crespi d'Adda's main avenue to grasp the master plan of this workers' village founded in 1877 by Cristoforo Benigno Crespi, one of Italy's greatest textile industrialists. His son Silvio, trained in England and Germany, designed its final layout: identical workers' houses arranged in a perpendicular grid, each with a garden and vegetable plot, following a paternalistic model inspired by British company towns.

Step inside the textile factory and its grounds: inaugurated in 1878, it sprawled across nearly two square kilometers and counted 120 mechanical looms, 4,000 workers, and a daily output of 50,000 meters of fabric at its peak. Its monumental entrance features a red wrought-iron gate with floral decorations crafted by Milanese artist Alessandro Mazzucotelli. Power came from a hydroelectric plant Crespi himself built on the Adda River.

Explore the village's communal buildings: the school (where books, meals, and teachers' lodging were covered by the company), the Renaissance-style church faithfully copied from the church in Busto Arsizio, the Crespi family's birthplace, the theater, the food cooperative, the hospital, and the public baths with a covered pool and hot water—all financed entirely by the family. The village was also the first in Italy equipped with Edison-system electric street lighting.

Climb the cypress-lined path to the workers' cemetery, where the community's social hierarchy still reads in stone. The simplest workers' graves stand in rows like a military cemetery, while foremen's and managers' tombs occupy the sides. At the far end, dominating the ensemble, rises the Crespi family's pyramid-shaped mausoleum, encircled by walls that seem to embrace 'their' workers protectively. The entire complex was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1995.

→

Bike ride or walk along the Adda riverbanks around the village, following the industrial canal Crespi dug to power his hydroelectric plant. The landscape blends Lombard meadows, poplars, and industrial ruins—a setting that illuminates why UNESCO called Crespi 'the most complete and best-preserved example of a company town in southern Europe.'

Wander through Soncino's medieval center, encircled by red-brick ramparts that Venice rebuilt in 1454 to mark its border with the Duchy of Milan. The village developed at a strategic crossroads between the republics of Venice and Milan, which earned it multiple sieges during the 12th and 13th centuries. Its urban fabric is among the best-preserved in the Lombard plain.

Visit the Rocca Sforzesca, a brick fortress built between 1473 and 1475 on orders from Galeazzo Maria Sforza, Duke of Milan. Architect Bartolomeo Gadio of Cremona—the same who designed Milan's Castello Sforzesco—conceived the structure in three years. Four corner towers flank the inner courtyard with its siege well; a unique cylindrical tower testifies to adaptation to preexisting walls. The main hall's ceiling is painted as a pergola, just like in the Sforza castle in Milan.

Push open the door to the Hebrew Printing Museum, housed in the former print shop founded in 1483 by physician Israel Nathan, who came from Speyer in the Rhineland. His son Joshua Solomon printed the first Talmud treatise ever to emerge from a press here on December 19, 1483—the Berakhot. The Soncino family—who adopted the town's name—produced 137 Hebrew works here between 1483 and 1547, including the first complete vocalized Hebrew Bible (1488). Legend has it that the Sforzas invited Jewish financiers to raise funds for the Rocca's construction.

→

Walk the ramparts and cobbled alleys of Soncino: the circuit around the 15th-century walls lets you measure the defensive system's scope and discover the fortified gates still standing. Soncino today draws pilgrimage from scholars and Jewish communities worldwide, who come tracing the footsteps of the Soncino printers.

Grazie Mantoue

Top hotels
9 Hotel Casa Poli
9 Hotel Leon D'Oro
9.5 B&B Casa del Teatro

Enter the basilica of the Sanctuary of the Beata Vergine delle Grazie, built between 1399 and 1406 by Francesco I Gonzaga, Captain of Mantua, as an ex-voto for the end of a plague epidemic. The architecture is austere Lombard Gothic with a single nave. Inside, however, the density is staggering: a wooden gallery carved in 1517 by Brother Francesco da Acquanegra runs along all the walls, its niches holding some sixty statues in wax, papier-mâché, and wood—illustrious figures who obtained graces, miraculous pilgrims, and ex-votos of every sort. From the ceiling, suspended among Murano glass chandeliers, hangs a stuffed crocodile: several legends circulate about its origin, including one about a reptile pulled from the Mincio by fishermen.

Seek out the tomb of Baldassarre Castiglione (1478-1529), author of The Courtier, in the first chapel on the right—designed by Giulio Romano. Also observe, on the outer portico facade, commemorative plaques from the Battle of Curtatone on May 29, 1848, where a handful of Tuscan volunteers resisted Marshal Radetzky's army for seven hours before surrendering when ammunition ran out. Pope Pius II and Emperor Charles V numbered among the illustrious visitors whose statues adorn the interior.

Stroll through the hamlet of Grazie di Curtatone, part of the Curtatone municipality 9 km from Mantua. Its lanes open onto a vast esplanade facing the basilica, which comes alive each August 15th for the Fiera delle Grazie—a fair instituted in 1425 by Marquis Gian Francesco Gonzaga—and for the Raduno Nazionale dei Madonnari, a gathering of street artists who cover the asphalt with large sacred reproductions in chalk. Pope John Paul II visited the sanctuary on June 22, 1991.

→

Explore the marshes and reedbeds of the Mincio around the sanctuary: a secret passage to the right of the portico provides access to the natural panorama where the Mincio begins widening to form the three lakes surrounding Mantua. In July and August, the water surface becomes covered with lotus flowers. The area forms part of the Mincio Park.

Pass through the fortified gate of Castellaro Lagusello, a hamlet of Monzambano in the heart of Mincio Park. The village owes its name to its origin: a 'castelliere' (defensive enclosure empty of dwellings) on a 'lagusello' (small lake). The Scaligeri of Verona built a castle here in the 13th century; the site appears first in a diocesan document from 1145. It then passed to the Visconti, the Gonzaga, and in 1441 to Venice, before being auctioned in 1637 for 545 ducats to Count Arrighi, who transformed the fortress into an inhabited noble court.

From the Clock Tower at the north entrance, walk the cobbled lanes to Villa Arrighi-Toccoli, the hamlet's only patrician residence—still owned by descendants today. The original crenellated walls, four towers, and two medieval rustic houses remain intact. Napoleon I stayed here in 1796 to direct his Po Valley operations; in 1859, Napoleon III and Mac Mahon would establish their headquarters here during the Battle of Solferino.

Visit the San Nicola church, mentioned as early as the 12th century, which preserves a 15th-century gilded wooden sculpture of the Virgin and several 17th-century canvases. From the recently restored bell tower, open April through October on weekends, the view plunges onto the heart-shaped lake ringed with reeds and the morainic hills of the Lake Garda amphitheater.

→

Descend to the heart-shaped lake via the small stairway south of the village: a wooden boardwalk crosses the protected wetland of the Castellaro Lagusello Morainic Complex Nature Reserve (271 hectares). Excavations have uncovered Bronze Age pile-dwelling settlements, now displayed at the Archaeological Museum of Alto Mantovano in Cavriana.

Explore the historic center of Limone sul Garda, nestled between limestone cliffs and the lake. The village name doesn't derive from lemons but probably from the Latin 'limes' (border) or the Celtic 'lemos' (elm). Until 1932, the village was road-inaccessible and could only be reached by boat or mountain paths—a fact that contributed to an exceptional genetic peculiarity: in 1974, researchers from the University of Milan discovered ApoA-1 Milano in local residents, a mutation of the apolipoprotein A-I protein inherited from a common ancestor born around 1780. This protein eliminates arterial fats at high speed, conferring on its carriers natural resistance to cardiovascular disease despite very low HDL cholesterol. About 60 out of 1,100 residents still carry it today.

Climb to the Limonaia del Castèl, an 18th-century lemon greenhouse bought and restored by the municipality in 1995. Limonaie are open-air structures facing due south, with thick walls, tall white stone columns, and wooden planks placed in winter to protect the trees from cold. Franciscan monks introduced lemon cultivation on these shores as early as the 13th century, making Limone one of the northernmost points in the world for commercial citrus growing. Goethe described these terraced gardens in September 1786 during his Italian Journey. The limonaia now houses an educational pathway and more than a hundred producing lemon trees.

Descend to Limone's small historic harbor, the village's original heart. The San Rocco church, on its terrace by the water, offers a vantage point on Monte Baldo across the lake. At the northern edge of the village, a museum recounts the history of fishing and PDO olive oil production, another local specialty.

→

Walk the Busatte-Tempesta panoramic walkway, inaugurated July 14, 2018, after two years of construction costing 7 million euros. Suspended from the cliff face about 50 meters above the lake on a 2-kilometer route, it follows the municipality to the Trentino border. The round-trip from the village center is entirely on foot.

Enter Bienno's historic center, built on the geometric grid of a 1st-century BC Roman foundation. The name comes from the Latin 'biennium,' a term designating the canals that channeled waters from the Grigna torrent to the workshops. The defensive Avanzini tower (dating from 1075) and Morandini tower flank the oldest nucleus. Renaissance palace facades preserve exterior frescoes; Palazzo Francesconi Rabajoli, two 16th-century noble houses united, shelters frescoes attributed to Fiamminghino.

Follow Via Artigiani along the Vaso Re, an artificial canal built around the year 1000 to channel water from the Grigna to the forges. In the 17th century, fifteen forges operated in the village. Visit the Fucina Museo, a forge-museum acquired by the municipality in 1985: the enormous hydraulic hammers, driven by the waterwheel according to Archimedes' principle, still function and are demonstrated. This is the heart of the Ethnographic Museum of Iron, Arts, and Popular Traditions of Bienno, founded in 1988.

Visit Bienno's Mill-Museum, the other pillar of the village's industrial heritage, adjacent to the Fucina. Together, these two buildings form the museum's founding nucleus and illustrate the complete chain of metal transformation from ore in the prehistoric Camunian mines to the finished tool.

Climb to the Colle di Cristo Re to see the golden statue of Christ the Redeemer, a 1929 work by Timo Bortolotti. Nearby, the church of the Maddalena is adorned with frescoes. The Gothic church of Santa Maria Annunciata, built in the 15th century with its exposed-stone rose window, preserves frescoes by Romanino and Pietro da Cemmo, two great Lombard painters.

→

Thematic walk along the Vaso Re canal titled 'Discovering Vaso Re, along the story told by water': this marked trail crosses the village's old forges converted into a Forge School and Iron Playroom, allowing you to follow Bienno's industrial history from the Middle Ages to the 1980s, when the last active forges closed.

Bormio

+3

9 Palace Hotel Wellness & Beauty
9.5 Chalet Heidi

Stroll through Bormio's historic center—the 'Magnifica Terra'—and its Piazza Cavour surrounded by noble palaces with painted facades. The first mentions of Bormio date back to a letter from Cassiodorus; in the Middle Ages, the town was the seat of an autonomous county covering the present territories of Valfurva, Valdidentro, and Livigno. The name Bormio probably derives from the Gaulish 'bormo,' meaning thermal spring. The Bajona Tower and the old Via Roma testify to Lombard medieval architecture. Bormio will be one of the Alpine sites for the Milano-Cortina 2026 Olympics for alpine skiing.

Take time for a soak at the Bagni Vecchi, perched 3 km above the center on the Stelvio road. These thermal baths have been known since pre-Roman antiquity: a bas-relief discovered during the demolition of a local church represents a water god and is dated between the 5th and 1st century BC. Pliny the Elder reportedly mentioned the springs in his writings. Nine natural springs emerge between 1,280 and 1,421 meters altitude at temperatures of 37 to 43°C. The Grotte di San Martino, a natural Turkish bath carved into the rock, is one of the oldest springs; an infinity pool overlooks the entire valley.

→

Ski Bormio's slopes in winter—the domain extends 50 km between 1,225 m and 3,000 m altitude on the flanks of Monte Vallecetta. The Stelvio piste, inaugurated in 1982, hosted the World Alpine Ski Championships in 1985 and 2005; it's part of the World Cup circuit each December. In summer, hike or mountain bike in Stelvio National Park, the largest of Italy's Alpine national parks, created in 1935.

→

Excursion to Stelvio Pass (Passo dello Stelvio, 2,757 m): the second-highest paved mountain pass in the Alps, it connects Bormio to Bolzano through a succession of 48 hairpin turns on the Lombard side. In summer the pass is open to road traffic and hikers; the summit glacier enables year-round skiing. In cycling, the Stelvio is one of the legendary climbs of the Giro d'Italia.

Descend the paved road of the Olgiasca peninsula to the Cistercian Abbey of Piona, nestled at the foot of Monte Legnone on the edge of a sheltered Lake Como cove. A monastic community is attested at this site as early as the 7th century, founded by Bishop Agrippino of Como. The current Romanesque church, a single nave 27 meters long, was consecrated in 1138 after reconstruction by Cluniac monks from San Pietro in Vallate. Its cloister, built between 1252 and 1257 by Prior Bonaccorso de Canova, features columns with varied capitals mixing Romanesque, nascent Gothic, and transalpine decorative influences—one of the best-preserved examples of Lombard Romanesque art in a lakeside monastic setting. Byzantine frescoes from the 12th-13th centuries survive in the apse.

Visit the shop of the Cistercian monks of Piona—the same ones who still inhabit the abbey—where liqueurs distilled according to traditional monastic recipes, medicinal herbs, honeys, and cosmetics produced within the priory are offered. The chapter house, meeting place and site of the superior's election, retains its wooden benches from the sacristy of San Zeno in Verona.

→

Easy loop hike on the Olgiasca peninsula from the village, following trail #7 through woods and clearings to the abbey in about 45 minutes. The return route takes the paved road, with plunging views of the Laghetto di Piona—a nearly enclosed cove of Lake Como—and the snow-capped Orobie Alps. Total circuit about 1h30, without particular difficulty.

Varenna

+1
Top hotels
9 Villa Varenna

9.4 Varenna Sweet Home

Arrive at Varenna by ferry to immediately take the Passeggiata degli Innamorati, a 1.3 km suspended corniche linking the landing stage to the village center by following the lake beneath red iron arches draped with climbing plants. Then climb toward Piazza San Giorgio, the medieval heart of the village, dominated by the 13th-century church of San Giorgio whose austere facade shelters medieval frescoes.

Visit Villa Monastero and its botanical gardens. The villa takes its name from a Cistercian monastery founded in the 12th century and closed by papal decree in 1567 for the nuns' indecorous behavior. Bought by the Mornico family then resold several times, it was enlarged at the end of the 19th century by a Hamburg family and completed in 1904. Now state-owned, its 14 furnished rooms constitute a house-museum. The botanical garden extends nearly 2 kilometers along the shore, in terraces, with some 900 plant species: African palms, Lebanon cedars, cypresses, magnolias, wisteria, citrus trees, and some rarities like Erythea armata.

Climb to Castello di Vezio, a medieval fortress perched on the hill overlooking Varenna for over a thousand years. The tower offers 360° panoramas of the lake and mountains. The castle houses a falconry with regular demonstrations and a permanent exhibition dedicated to the Lariosaurus, a primitive marine reptile whose first fossils were discovered in the province of Lecco and which, according to local legend, was spotted in the lake in 1946.

Stroll through the gardens of Villa Cipressi, dating from the 15th century: terraces descend to the lake, planted with cypresses, exotic flowers, and aromatic plants. Paired with Villa Monastero, it completes Varenna's botanical circuit.

→

Ferry crossing between Varenna and Menaggio or Bellagio: Lake Como's public boat service connects the three points of the 'golden triangle' in 15 to 30 minutes. It's the best way to grasp the lake's inverted-Y geography and appreciate the villas and villages from the water.

Explore the steep lanes and cobbled stairways of Bellagio's old town—'Bellus Loci' (beautiful place) to the Romans, and already a summer residence of Pliny the Younger who loved fishing here. The village occupies the tip of the Lariana promontory, at the exact point where the lake's two southern arms meet in an inverted Y. From Piazza della Chiesa, the 12th-century basilica church of San Giacomo, dedicated to Bellagio's patron saint, displays a characteristic bell tower and triumphal arch. From the Punta Spartivento viewpoint, the panorama embraces both lake branches and the Alps simultaneously.

Visit the gardens of Villa Melzi d'Eril, commissioned in 1808 by Francesco Melzi d'Eril, Duke of Lodi and vice-president of the Italian Republic under Napoleon. The neoclassical villa was completed in 1810 in simple, severe lines; the English-style park surrounding it was designed to harmonize with the lakeshore, and a Japanese garden with pond and maples was added in the northern section. Neoclassical, Renaissance, Egyptian, and Etruscan-Roman sculptures are scattered among Lebanon cedars, camellias, azaleas, and centuries-old trees. The orangery now houses works by Appiani and sculptures by Canova.

Book a guided tour of the gardens of Villa Serbelloni, which extend over 18 hectares atop the promontory with panoramic views of both lake arms. Designed in the 18th century, these Italian-style gardens are accessible only by guided tour organized from the village (Tuesday-Sunday). Napoleon, Austrian Emperor Francis I, and Franz Liszt number among their illustrious 19th-century visitors.

Descend to the hamlet of Pescallo east of Bellagio, a former fishing village accessible from the cemetery via via per Pescallo. Its small beach and sailboats anchored in the bay offer an authentic view of Lake Como's eastern branch.

→

Rent a license-free boat from Bellagio's landing stage to explore small coves inaccessible from shore, cruise along historic villas, and reach isolated beaches on the lake's central branch.

Stroll along Menaggio's lakefront, a promenade bordered by flowerbeds and painted houses extending from Piazza Garibaldi. Menaggio forms the third point of the 'golden triangle' with Bellagio and Varenna; its ferry port connects these three villages in 15 to 30 minutes. The upper part of the town, medieval in aspect with its alleys and stone buildings, preserves the ruins of a 12th-century castle. The local baroque church, painted pink, is adorned with paintings on columns and walls up to the dome above the altar.

Climb to Loveno, a hamlet of Menaggio, to visit the gardens of Villa Mylius Vigoni. The villa was purchased in 1829 by Enrico Mylius, fascinated by its position. Designed between 1855 and 1865 by architect Giuseppe Balzaretti over 8 hectares in an amphitheater, the romantic park is one of the most intact examples of this type in Lombardy: centuries-old trees, fountains, marble statues, sculptural groups, and small commemorative temples. The villa was bequeathed in 1983 to the Federal Republic of Germany and now houses the Villa Vigoni Italian-German Center for European Excellence.

→

Hike in Val Sanagra, a natural wildlife reserve accessible from Menaggio, following the Sanagra torrent to the Tobi waterfalls through forests and ancient rural villages. The trail is family-friendly for the first few kilometers, then becomes more technical at the height of Sass Corbee. A shorter variant from Croce leads to the Chiesetta degli Alpini and Monte Crocetta (views of Lake Como from the WWI Cadorna Line defense positions).

Angera

+2
Top hotels
9.1 Hotel Ponti
9.7 Cascina Canée
9 A Casa di Ale Lago Maggiore Private outdoor sauna
9.1 La Casa sul Lago

Stroll through Angera's center and along its quays bordering the southern tip of Lake Maggiore on the Lombard side. The small town is dominated from afar by the crenellated silhouette of the Rocca whose swallowtail merlons can be recognized from the lake.

Visit the Rocca Borromea of Angera, a medieval fortress owned by the Borromeo family since 1449. The earliest traces of the building date back to 1066, when it belonged to the archbishops of Milan. The Visconti took control in 1277 after the Battle of Desio—a victory commemorated in the spectacular frescoes of the Hall of Justice, painted shortly after the event by the 'Master of Angera' and constituting one of the major examples of secular painting from the late Lombard Gothic. Vitaliano I Borromeo bought the complex in 1449 for 12,800 imperial pounds. The Rocca is the harmonious result of five building sections constructed between the 11th and 17th centuries.

Spend an hour at the Doll and Toy Museum, the largest in Europe, founded in 1988 by Princess Bona Borromeo Arese. Its 12 rooms distributed across the Visconti and Borromeo wings display over 1,300 pieces—dolls, French and German automatons from the 19th century, toys from around the world—from the 18th century to contemporary creations. A separate room is dedicated to dolls and toys from non-European cultures.

Explore the Rocca's medieval garden, 2,000 m² enclosed between the vineyards and the crenellated walls overlooking the lake. This garden was reconstructed from historical sources—illuminated codices and period documents—and presents the three types of medieval gardens: the Princes' Garden, the Verzière (vegetable garden), and the Herb Garden.

→

Climb to the Rocca's tower viewpoints to admire the panorama over Lake Maggiore and the Alps. The Torre Castellana offers the highest view of both lake shores—the Lombard side with Angera, and the Piedmont side with Arona, where the Rocca d'Arona once stood before Napoleon ordered its demolition in 1800. Together with Angera, it formed the lake's historic lock.
