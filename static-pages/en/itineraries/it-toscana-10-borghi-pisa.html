The 14 Most Beautiful Villages of Pisa Province

Fortresses crowning olive-covered hills, alabaster gleaming under summer light, abandoned villages between calanchi and silence. The Pisan hinterland in all its authenticity.

From the walls of Calci's charterhouse to the ramparts of Castelfranco, passing through Brunelleschi's fortress at Vicopisano, the castle of the vicars at Lari, and the Buddhist enclave of Pomaia, this itinerary traverses fourteen villages that shaped the history of Pisan hinterland. Volterra and its Etruscan alabaster, Lajatico's Teatro del Silenzio, Peccioli's contemporary art, Palaia's ghost villages, and San Miniato's white truffles compose a journey through terracotta, medieval stone, and Tuscan memory.

Key highlights (4)
  • Volterra: Etruscan city perched at 550 meters, capital of Tuscan alabaster
  • Vicopisano: Brunelleschi's military masterpiece between Arno and Monte Pisano
  • San Miniato: medieval imperial borgo and Tuscan capital of white truffles
  • Peccioli: Tuscan village that transformed a landfill into a contemporary art museum

Calci

9.1 Hôtel La Caravelle

The Certosa Monumentale di Calci, founded in 1366 by Pisan noble families, stands as one of Italy's best-preserved Carthusian monastic complexes. Transformed into a sumptuous Baroque residence during the 17th century—complete with frescoes, Carrara marble, and elaborate woodwork—it houses 15 monk cells arranged around a grand cloister, a refectory adorned with Bernardino Poccetti's Last Supper (1597), and the sacristy where the Calci Bible is displayed: an illuminated four-volume codex from 1168 that returned here in 2015 after a decade at Pisa's San Matteo Museum. Visits are guided only, by reservation.

The Natural History Museum of the University of Pisa occupies the certosa's former service quarters—granaries, cellars, wine press, stables—converted since 1979. Founded in 1591 by Grand Duke Ferdinand I de' Medici, it preserves four centuries of scientific collections: zoology, paleontology, minerals, and one of Europe's most important cetacean skeleton galleries. The granary, nearly 100 meters long, houses the collection's largest specimens.

The Pieve dei Santi Giovanni ed Ermolao, in the hamlet of La Pieve, is a late 11th-century Romanesque-Pisan building surrounded by terraced olive groves. First documented in 934, the church faces not the canonical west but toward the gate of the old castle—a detail suggesting it predated the fortified village itself.

→

Hike to the Rocca della Verruca, a 13th-century Pisan Republic fortress perched at 537 meters on a rocky spur. Leonardo da Vinci surveyed the site in 1503 for the Florentine army, which made it their final stronghold during Pisa's siege—the fortress capitulated on June 18, 1503, sealing Pisan independence. Two trails connect the site from Calci or Vicopisano; ascent takes about an hour one-way. Views sweep across the Pisan plain, the Arno, and on clear days, the sea.

→

Stroll through the Valle Graziosa around the charterhouse. Along the stream crossing Calci, more than a hundred mills still operated a century ago. Ancient olive trees on the slopes create one of the most peaceful landscapes in the Pisan Mountains.

The Rocca del Brunelleschi is one of the few military fortresses designed by Filippo Brunelleschi, commissioned by Florence after conquering Vicopisano in 1406—the siege had lasted nine months, with Florence even using a galley rowed up the Arno to cut supply lines. Brunelleschi presented his design in clay and wood before a commission that included the young Francesco Sforza, future Duke of Milan. Construction began in 1435: a 31-meter keep, a 60-meter crenellated wall descending to the Torre del Soccorso at the Arno's edge, and a drawbridge system designed to isolate sections of the fortress if enemies penetrated. Restored in 1995, open to the public since 1998.

The Palazzo Pretorio, adjacent to the Rocca, houses a permanent exhibition of excavations from the San Michele Arcangelo alla Verruca monastery. Its walls still bear marks from centuries of different uses: seat of the podestà, prison, residence of the Florentine vicar.

Vicopisano's medieval center preserves 13 towers from the 11th-15th centuries, including the 12th-century Clock Tower, converted into a bell tower. Walking the narrow streets reveals layers of successive defenses: Pisan walls, Florentine additions, filled moats. Dante mentions the nearby Caprona castle in the Divine Comedy.

→

Panoramic view from the Rocca's wall-walk across the Arno plain, the Pisan Mountains, and on clear days, to the sea. From the signal tower, messages to Florence once departed in visual code using flags, smoke, and artillery fire.

→

Olive oil tasting from the Pisan Mountains at San Giovanni alla Vena, a Vicopisano hamlet known for its Arno clay and ceramic tradition. Olives are hand-harvested following the tradition of the Pisan Mountains Oil Route.

The Castello dei Vicari has dominated Lari since the year 1000—the first documented mention dates to 1043, when land was sold for a sword. After Florence took Lari in 1406, it became the seat of a Florentine vicariate administering almost the entire Tuscan coast from Livorno to Pontedera. The inner courtyard bears 92 ceramic coats of arms of the vicars who governed here for over four centuries. The cistern built in 1448, topped with the Pitti and Scali family arms, sits halfway up the 95 access steps. The complex served as a prison until World War II, damaged by bombing. Open to visitors since 1991.

Grand Duke Leopold II, during his visit, wrote: "I arrived at Lari and from its fortress was shown a vast country, the most beautiful part of Tuscany." The terrace indeed offers a 360° panorama: from the Pisan Mountains to the Balze of Volterra, from the Livorno coast to Peccioli.

The Porta Pisana, also called Maremmana, is the only one of the three original gates still equipped with a counter-gate. The tight space between the two openings created a trap for attackers who had breached the first defense. The Porta Volterrana, demolished in 1798, still had a drawbridge at that date.

The 15th-century parish church of San Leonardo and Santa Maria Assunta preserves paintings by Melani and two marble statues attributed to Andrea Pisano. It was severely damaged by the 19th-century earthquake that also remodeled the neighboring market loggia.

→

Tasting of Lari cherries: 19 local varieties, some found only in this area of the Pisan hills. Local producers sell baskets, jams, and liqueurs in the village square. The cherry festival is one of Tuscany's oldest.

→

Stroll through the borgo's medieval alleyways, whose original stone structure and Renaissance-Gothic dwellings have remained intact—the world wars never touched the village.

The Istituto Lama Tzong Khapa, founded in 1977 by Lamas Thubten Yeshe and Thubten Zopa Rinpoche, is Europe's largest center for Tibetan Buddhist studies. It bears the name of Tsongkhapa, founder of the Gelugpa monastic order. The Dalai Lama has taught here on several occasions. The library, meditation halls, gompa (prayer room), and retreat cottages spread across the Pisan hills 40 km south of Pisa. Courses in Buddhist philosophy, psychology, and meditation are offered year-round.

The statue of Cenresig (Avalokiteshvara, the Buddha of Compassion), inaugurated by the Dalai Lama in June 2014, is the tallest in Europe. Its story is singular: created by film set designer Dante Ferretti for Martin Scorsese's Kundun (1997), it risked destruction after filming in Morocco. Saved and transported to Italy, it was restored by sculptor Alessio Pazzini according to strict Buddhist iconographic canons.

→

Participate in a guided meditation session in the institute's gompa, open to visitors on certain days according to the Tibetan calendar. Prayer flags, white stupas, and prayer wheels surrounding the main building create an unexpected atmosphere in the heart of Tuscany.

Volterra

Top hotels
9 Hotel La Locanda
9.3 Casale con vista colline
9.5 Hotel Residence Villa Rioddi
9.1 Albergo Etruria

The Palazzo dei Priori, begun in 1208 and completed in 1254, is Tuscany's oldest town hall—it directly inspired the design of Florence's Palazzo Vecchio. Its local limestone façade bears three rows of mullioned windows and the glazed terracotta coats of arms of Florentine governors. The Council Hall is entirely adorned with 14th-century frescoes. From the monumental tower, views embrace the entire city and hills to the sea.

The Museo Etrusco Guarnacci, founded in 1761 through nobleman Mario Guarnacci's donation of his private collection—over 50,000 volumes and hundreds of funerary urns—is one of Europe's oldest public museums. It houses the world's largest collection of Etruscan alabaster funerary urns, spanning the 9th to 1st century BC. Among the masterpieces: the Urn of the Married Couple and the famous "Shadow of the Evening," a bronze statuette with an elongated body that fascinated Giacometti.

The Alabaster Ecomuseum, installed in the medieval Minucci-Solaini tower, traces the history of this translucent stone from the Etruscans to today's workshops. Volterra has worked alabaster for 2,500 years—the Etruscans already sculpted funerary urns from it. In the 18th century, the local industry reached its peak; today, artisans still work by hand in the center's workshops. The neighboring Pinacoteca preserves Rosso Fiorentino's Deposition from the Cross (1521), considered one of the major works of Tuscan Mannerism.

The Fortezza Medicea, built between 1472 and 1475 at the Medici's request at the city's highest point, is one of the Renaissance's best-preserved defensive works. Its penitentiary remains in operation—a rare European feature. The Roman Theater, rediscovered in the 1950s, dates from the late 1st century BC and could accommodate 1,700 spectators. Names of great Roman families are engraved on the marble seats of the front rows.

Visit Palazzo Incontri-Viti, a 16th-century private residence acquired in 1850 by a wealthy alabaster merchant. Its 12 rooms, still inhabited by descendants, display period furniture, Oriental porcelain, and alabaster pieces, including a candelabra designed for Emperor Maximilian of Mexico—who was executed in 1867 before he could collect it.

→

The Balze: spectacular erosion formations on Volterra's northwest edge, where layers of clay and sand slowly collapse into cliffs and ravines. Several medieval churches have already been engulfed; a 10th-century abbey remains visible on the cliff face. Walk along the edge on foot from the center in 20 minutes.

→

Alabaster carving workshop: several artisans in the historic center offer demonstrations or introductions to this fragile, translucent material, worked exclusively by hand—a mechanical lathe would shatter the stone. A tradition the Etruscans had already mastered 25 centuries ago.

The Teatro del Silenzio is a natural amphitheater created in 2006 at the initiative of Alberto Bartalini and tenor Andrea Bocelli, born in Lajatico on September 22, 1958. The concept: a theater that remains silent 364 days a year and comes alive once each summer for a single concert. Tuscan travertine block terraces surround an artificial basin at whose center a different contemporary artist's sculpture is installed each year. The first edition, July 27, 2006, was dedicated to Bocelli's father. Since then, Placido Domingo, José Carreras, Dua Lipa, and Zubin Mehta have performed here.

The borgo of Lajatico, perched on a spur overlooking the Valdera, was first a castle of the Bishop of Volterra before being conquered by Pisa then Florence. The civic tower, Palazzo Pretorio, and cobbled alleyways form the heart of a village of about 1,000 inhabitants that claims, statistics in hand, to be Italy's municipality with the highest average per-capita income.

→

Hike to the Rocca di Pietracassia, a ruined medieval fortress at 550 meters altitude in the Valdera woods, about 40 minutes' walk from Lajatico. Built in the Lombard era to control the route to Montecatini's copper mines, it was disputed and sold several times before passing definitively to the Pisans in 1434. Its name comes from a fissure (cassia) in a limestone spur.

→

The mofeta di Borboi, between Lajatico and Orciatico: a geothermal carbon dioxide emission typical of ancient volcanic zones. The stream water appears to boil from gas rising from underground. Best seen between October and May, when the watercourse is fuller.

Terricciola developed around a hill inhabited since Etruscan times, as evidenced by 4th-century BC hypogea (underground chamber tombs) discovered beneath Via della Fonte. These underground vaults still served as wine cellars last century. Several are being reopened to the public thanks to European funds. The clayey, sandy soil, rich in Pliocene marine fossils, is ideal for Sangiovese, Trebbiano, and Merlot.

The Pieve di San Verano is the main parish church of the Terricciola territory, a few kilometers from the borgo. A sober Romanesque building whose interior houses medieval decorative elements. The hamlet of Morrona, in the municipality, contains a Hellenistic-era necropolis with hypogeal chamber tombs among the best documented in the Pisan hills.

→

Terricciola has about 25 independent wineries and is a member of the National Association of Wine Cities. The municipality is part of the Pisan Hills Wine Route. Several estates offer year-round tastings: red Sangiovese, white Vermentino and Trebbiano, with a mineral signature linked to sea breezes rising up the valley. The White Night of Wine takes place every first weekend in July.

→

Panorama over the Pisan hills and Valdera from the heights of the medieval borgo, whose alleyways and Renaissance houses have retained their original appearance.

Chianni

Top hotels

9.3 Casa Laura

The church of San Donato has been documented since May 8, 1277. Pope Boniface VIII himself sent it a papal bull in 1301 ordering it to claim the property of Morrona Abbey. The current building dates from complete reconstruction in 1812, which incorporated a castle tower into the apse and moved the early 17th-century baroque high altar, in precious polychrome marble, from the demolished church of San Pietro a Massa. The apse is covered with frescoes by Antonio Gaioni.

The Cappella della Santissima Annunziata is an underground chapel whose construction probably dates to parish expansion work in the early 18th century. It is entirely frescoed by Giovanni Battista Tempesti of Volterra, a cycle covering the large barrel vault and side walls—one of Tempesti's rare works outside major cities.

A few kilometers away, the medieval hamlet of Rivalto (320 m altitude) was built on the ruins of an ancient fortress. This is where Blessed Giordano da Rivalto was born in 1260, a Dominican preacher who, in a sermon delivered in Florence in 1305, mentioned eyeglasses as a recent invention whose inventor he had met—one of the first documented historical mentions of this object. The 18th-century Sanctuary of the Madonna del Carmine, nestled in the woods, offers the wildest viewpoint in the area.

→

Chianni is one of the last municipalities on the Pisan Hills Wine Route. The local extra virgin olive oil, with its intense color and very low acidity, has been produced for centuries; local mills open during the autumn harvest. In November, the Sagra del Cinghiale (wild boar festival) transforms the village into a gastronomic fair, one of Tuscany's oldest.

→

Hike toward Monte Vaso from Chianni: marked trails through oak and olive woods, with views stretching from Pisa to Volterra. Rivalto's chestnut groves produce superior quality marroni already mentioned in 1428 cadastral records.

Villa Baciocchi occupies the site of the former castle of the Della Gherardesca counts, who founded Capannoli in the Middle Ages. The current building, in late Baroque and Neoclassical style, was restructured in the mid-18th century, then acquired in 1833 by Count Felice Baciocchi, husband of Elisa Bonaparte, Napoleon's sister. The villa now houses a Zoological Museum (taxidermied animals and dioramas) and an Archaeological Museum of the Valdera. The botanical park contains 160 plant species and 12 monumental trees.

The hamlet of Santo Pietro Belvedere preserves the urban structure of a medieval castle-borgo mentioned as early as 1175 in a treaty between Pisa and Lucca. The bell tower of San Pietro church, built by Luigi Bellincioni between 1881 and 1883, is founded on the remains of the old castle tower—whose Ghibelline battlements were still visible in 1789. The Oratorio di San Rocco contains a painted panel of the Virgin and Child attributed to the school of Benozzo Gozzoli.

→

The municipality of Capannoli is part of the Pisan Hills Wine Route. Valdera wine estates offer tastings of local wines (Sangiovese, Vermentino, Trebbiano) paired with typical products: olive oil, truffles, pecorino.

→

Hiking or cycling on marked CAI trails crossing the hills between Capannoli, Peccioli, and Terricciola. The landscape of vineyards, olive groves, and isolated farms corresponds to the classic model of 16th-century Tuscan rural settlement.

Peccioli

The MACCA (Museo d'Arte Contemporanea a Cielo Aperto) of Peccioli brings together over 70 permanent installations scattered throughout the municipal territory. The Anfiteatro Fonte Mazzola, built in 2007 in tuff slabs without concrete using bio-construction techniques, can accommodate 2,000 spectators with the hills as backdrop. In July it hosts the 11 Lune festival. In 2024, Peccioli won RAI's national "Il Borgo dei Borghi" competition.

The medieval borgo of Peccioli has been inhabited since the Neolithic, with attested Etruscan and Roman occupations. Its Piazza del Popolo brings together the Palazzo Pretorio, the Pieve di San Verano, and the Archaeological Museum—excavations have yielded Greek and Etruscan ceramics. Walking Patrick Tuttofuoco's Endless Sunset footbridge, which connects the centro storico to the modern part in a ribbon of colors, is one of the most surprising strolls in the Pisan hills.

The hamlet of Ghizzano houses permanent works by Alicja Kwade and Patrick Tuttofuoco, and house façades bear the signature of British artist David Tremlett. The entire territory of Peccioli functions as a museum without walls—the "Sistema Peccioli": revenues from the waste treatment and recovery plant (biogas, recycling) have financed culture, art, and public facilities since the 1990s, a model cited as a reference at the European level.

→

11 Lune Festival at the Anfiteatro Fonte Mazzola, every July: theater, music, and dance by national and international artists, with the Valdera hills as natural backdrop. The festival attracts about 15,000 spectators per edition.

→

Wine and local product tasting in the cellars of the Pisan Hills Wine Route. In October, the Sagra del Tartufo brings Peccioli alive with markets, tastings, and performances.

Palaia

Top hotels

Toiano is a ghost village perched among the Pisan hills and tuff calanchi reminiscent of Volterra's Balze. Tuff spurs up to 40-50 meters high line the access track, 3 meters wide. The village, inhabited until the 1960s (521 souls in the 19th century), was gradually abandoned when Valdera factories—including Piaggio—drew labor to the plain. Its history was marked by the unsolved 1947 murder of "beautiful Elvira" (Elvira Orlandini, 22), a case that mobilized all of postwar Italy and remains a cold case. The borgo is listed by FAI (Fondo per l'Ambiente Italiano) among the "places of the heart" to be preserved.

The Pieve di San Martino of Palaia, outside the walls, was built between 1270 and 1300 to Andrea Pisano's designs—it is listed in the catalog of Italian national monuments. A Romanesque building with three naves of five bays, ending in a raised presbyterium with three chapels. On the floor, funerary slabs of the village's illustrious figures; on the walls, those of Palaia's parish priests.

Villa Saletta, another ghost village near Palaia, is an example of a completely self-sufficient 16th-17th century villa-fattoria belonging to the Florentine Ricciardi family. The main square, manor villa, and clock tower have remained virtually intact. Films have been shot here, including the Taviani brothers' La Notte di San Lorenzo and Virzì's Me and Napoleon. In summer, a popular theater festival brings it to life.

→

Visit the Museum of Rural Civilization in Montefoscoli, installed in a house dating from the year 1000: cellars, oil mill, agricultural tools, reconstruction of the Valdera peasant world. Next door, the "Casa Museo Vaccà Berlinghieri" celebrates Andrea Vaccà, famous surgeon at the University of Pisa and personal physician to Paolina Bonaparte. Best visited early morning before heading to Toiano and Villa Saletta.

→

Hike on wooded trails between Palaia and its hamlets—Alica, San Gervasio, Colleoli. The Tuscan expression "Peggio Palaia" (worse than Palaia) dates, according to tradition, from the 1435 siege by Niccolò Piccinino, who took the castle by starvation after failing to force it.

San Miniato

Top hotels
9.3 Agriturismo Cascina Cipressa

9.7 Appartamento Casa Nina

The Rocca di Federico II of San Miniato, whose tower was erected between 1217 and 1221 (reconstructed in 1958 after bombing), is the borgo's emblem. From the summit, views carry across the Valdarno, the Volterra hills, the Apennines, and the sea. In the Middle Ages, the borgo was called San Miniato al Tedesco: Otto I of Saxony made it a center of imperial administration in 962, and Frederick II of Swabia in 1218 a tax collection point for all central Italy. Bishop Sigeric of Canterbury stopped here on the Via Francigena around 990, confirming its place among the route's historic stations.

The cathedral of Santa Maria Assunta and San Genesio, begun in the 12th century and elevated to cathedral status in 1622, presents a sober façade adorned with 26 ceramic bowls mainly of Tunisian origin (the originals are preserved in the Diocesan Museum). The Baroque interior houses several large altarpieces, including works by Francesco d'Agnolo known as Lo Spillo, Aurelio Lomi, and Cosimo Gamberucci. A tragic episode: on July 22, 1944, an American artillery shell killed 55 people gathered in the cathedral cemetery by German soldiers.

The Palazzo Comunale houses on the first floor the Sala delle Sette Virtù, the building's oldest room, decorated with frescoes by Cenni di Francesco di Ser Cenni: the Virgin nursing the Child surrounded by the four cardinal virtues and three theological virtues. The Palazzo del Seminario, on Piazza della Repubblica, is recognizable by its length and façade entirely painted with repetitive motif frescoes and Latin maxims.

The archaeological area of San Genesio, a few kilometers from the historic center, is one of the historic stages of the Via Francigena mentioned by Sigeric. Recent excavations have uncovered the foundations of an important medieval city, now an archaeological site open to visitors.

→

White truffle hunting in the forests of San Miniato, with a guide and his dogs. The white truffle of San Miniato (Tuber Magnatum Pico) is one of Italy's most renowned. Local production represents about one-fifth of national production. The National White Truffle Fair has taken place the last three weekends of November since 1969; in 1954, a truffle weighing over 2 kg was offered to President Eisenhower.

→

Tasting of local products in the historic center: white truffle tagliatelle, mallegato (medieval charcuterie still produced by peasant families), spuma di gota di maiale (pink pork pâté). San Miniato is a Slow Food member.

Montopoli In Val Darno

Top hotels

Montopoli was nicknamed the "castello insigne" by Boccaccio, impressed by its powerful fortifications. Disputed between Pisa, Florence, and Lucca since the 11th century—the Arno once flowed beneath its eastern walls—the town resisted numerous sieges. The Torre di San Matteo (15th century), built by the Florentine Republic, is today the best viewpoint over the Pisan Valdarno, reaching the Apuan Alps on clear days. The Arco di Castruccio Castracani marks the spot where the famous condottiere was wounded during the 1328 siege.

The Battle of San Romano (1432), fought near Montopoli between Florence and the Milanese troops of Niccolò Piccinino, was immortalized by Paolo Uccello in three paintings now preserved at the Uffizi in Florence, the Louvre, and London's National Gallery. The town voluntarily submitted to Florence in 1349, after the decisive battle. In 1412, podestà Jacopo degli Albizzi divided Montopoli into two peoples—Santo Stefano and San Giovanni—whose rivalry is reenacted every September during the Disfida con l'Arco.

The Museo Civico in Palazzo Guicciardini displays archaeological finds dating back to the 3rd century BC (Etruscan period, Volterran style), medieval collections, and a section devoted to local ceramics—Montopoli had a tradition of kilns and potters from the 16th century, perpetuated in the 19th century by Dante Milani.

→

Disfida con l'Arco: every second Sunday in September, Montopoli's two historic quarters (Santo Stefano and San Giovanni) compete in an archery tournament in medieval costume, preceded by a parade. The event commemorates the 1412 decision that divided the town.

→

Stroll through the centro storico: 17th-century noble palaces, Villa Capponi Piaggio (16th century, acquired in 1953 by Enrico Piaggio), and the Tenuta di Verramista with its oaks, chestnuts, and white firs exceedingly rare in the Pisan plain.

Castelfranco Di Sotto

Castelfranco di Sotto was founded in the first half of the 13th century by four hamlets on the banks of the Arno and Usciana who united to defend themselves from struggles between Guelphs, Ghibellines, Pisa, Lucca, and Florence. Lucca encouraged settlement by exempting from taxes any inhabitant who settled there—hence the name "Castelfranco" (free castle). The original borgo was ringed with walls and 16 towers; the original walls were destroyed by an Arno flood in 1333 and completely rebuilt. The Roman castrum plan—two main axes crossing at the center, four gates oriented to the cardinal points—is perfectly legible today.

The Palazzo Pretorio, on the main square, is the former seat of Florentine dominion. The historic center still preserves three of the four original gates—each corresponding to a contrada—and the medieval underpasses scattered through the alleyways. The Archaeological Museum assembles objects from prehistory to the Middle Ages, including two unique finds: remains of a Roman bridge and a medieval canoe used on the former Lake Bientina.

→

The Riserva Naturale di Montefalcone, the most important of the Cerbaie, is crossed by a section of the medieval Via Francigena. Pine and oak woods, rare fauna, marked trails connecting Castelfranco to Orentano. A one to two-hour walk is enough to reach the first edges from the town center.

→

Palio dei Barchini con le Ruote: every third Sunday in May, the four contrade (San Michele in Caprugnana, San Martino in Catiana, San Bartolomeo a Paterno, San Pietro a Vigesimo) compete in a race of wheeled little boats through the borgo's streets, accompanied by itinerant dinners. Tradition established since 1987.
