Grand Alpine Valleys of Piedmont: Occitan Alps, Lakes & Baroque Savoy

Occitan Alps, ibex of Gran Paradiso, UNESCO Sacred Mountains, and Savoy baroque: Piedmont unfolds its full diversity in eleven unforgettable stages.

Eleven days across Piedmont's most varied alpine terrain: from the Occitan valleys of Maira and Stura to the UNESCO Sacred Mountains of Varallo and Orta, passing through Italy's first national park (Gran Paradiso), the Walser villages of Valle del Lys, and the medieval Marquisate of Saluzzo. The circuit concludes in Turin, baroque capital of the House of Savoy, with its UNESCO-listed royal museums, Mole Antonelliana, and historic cafés.

Key highlights (6)
  • Dronero: Occitan gateway to Piedmont with its Devil's Bridge from 1428
  • Fort of Exilles: Susa Valley citadel and legend of the Man in the Iron Mask
  • Gran Paradiso: Italy's first national park and its 3,000 ibex
  • Sacred Mountains: Varallo (1491) and Orta (1583), two UNESCO jewels
  • Saluzzo: capital of the medieval marquisate with Gothic frescoes
  • Turin: UNESCO royal residences, Egyptian Museum, and Mole Antonelliana

Demonte

The Forte Albertino di Vinadio, built between 1834 and 1859 by the Kingdom of Sardinia under King Charles Albert, stands as one of Europe's last great star fortifications. Its pink granite bastions, pierced by 8 km of galleries, could house up to 4,000 soldiers and withstand a six-month siege. The fort was designed to guard the Maddalena Pass (1,996 m) against any French incursion from Dauphiné. It was abandoned at the end of the 19th century without ever facing assault.

The Sanctuary of Sant'Anna di Vinadio (2,035 m altitude) is Europe's highest car-accessible Marian sanctuary. Construction dates to the 15th century at the initiative of the upper valley communes. On the night of July 25-26, the annual Saint Anne gathering draws thousands of pilgrims—a torchlit procession descends toward the plain through darkness. The site sits surrounded by alpine meadows with direct views onto the Franco-Italian border ridges.

The Casa del Contrabbandiere in Argentera is a museum-ecomuseum dedicated to the smugglers of the upper Stura: salt, tobacco, alcohol, and textiles crossed the Maddalena Pass by human or mule back, by night, throughout the 18th and 19th centuries. Exhibits include period equipment, oral testimonies, and maps of clandestine routes. In Aisone, caves carved into molasse have yielded traces of human occupation dating back 9,000 years.

The Terme di Vinadio thermal baths capture sulfurous water at 31°C flowing at 620 liters per minute from a geological fault at 900 meters altitude. The current facility, rebuilt in the 20th century, builds on a spa tradition dating to the 16th century, documented in the House of Savoy archives.

→

Hike to Rifugio Migliorero from Vinadio (Valle Stura): ascent through alpine pastures between 1,400 and 2,100 meters, spotting chamois and marmots. Return via abandoned shepherd huts of the valley.

→

Session at the Terme di Vinadio after the hike: outdoor and indoor sulfurous water pools, thermal treatments. Reservation recommended in high season.

The Ponte del Diavolo of Dronero, built in 1428 over the Maira torrent, is the village's centerpiece. Three unequal arches, crenellated in swallowtail style (Ghibelline pattern), rise 18 meters above two irrigation canals. The main arch measures 27 meters in diameter and 6 meters wide. A drawbridge existed on the village side until 1810, its iron hinge discovered beneath the paving. Legend tells that the mayor, desperate as floods swept away every bridge, struck a pact with the devil for the soul of the first being to cross. He threw a bread crust—a stray dog bolted across and fled. The devil, cheated, abandoned the bridge. The original construction receipt is preserved in the municipal archives: 700 florins paid to one magister Antonius.

Espaci Occitan, headquarters of the Piedmont Occitan cultural association, houses the Museo Sòn de Lenga devoted to Occitan history and culture in the region. Dronero appears as Dragonerium from the 12th century, its coat of arms displaying a rampant dragon. The historic center preserves medieval arcades, Gothic capitals, and 15th-century noble palaces.

Climb the Valle Maira toward Chialvetta and Viviere on the old paved mule trail. Along 177 km of marked paths, the Occitan Routes traverse hamlets, votive chapels, and sites linked to the former Marquisate of Saluzzo. The San Salvatore chapel in Macra preserves 14th-15th century frescoes depicting the Dance of Salome and Genesis.

Viewpoint above Acceglio facing the Monviso massif (3,841 m), highest peak of the Cottian Alps. The Maira rises in a glacial basin nearby, its waters flowing between pine forests before carving caves in travertine. The village of Acceglio is one of the valley's most isolated, at 1,220 meters altitude.

→

Hike the Cyclamen Trail starting from Bedale (hamlet of Macra): between July and September, meadows of orchids and cyclamens beneath ash and wild cherry trees. Duration about 3h, moderate elevation gain.

→

Introduction to rock climbing on the valley's equipped cliffs (beginner to intermediate routes, top-ropes installed). Local providers offer half-day guided sessions.

→

In June, don't miss the Fiera degli Acciugai (Anchovy Fair) in Dronero, recalling the valley inhabitants' old trade: in winter, they descended to Liguria to buy salt-preserved anchovies, which they resold throughout the region. It's believed the anchovies originally served to conceal smuggled salt.

Caraglio, at the entrance to Valle Grana, houses the Filatoio Rosso, one of Europe's oldest silk mills, built in 1678. The red brick building now hosts the Piedmont Silk Museum. Valle Grana stretches twenty kilometers between the Cottian and Maritime Alps, climbing to Punta Tempesta (2,679 m).

Climb to the Sanctuary of San Magno at 1,761 meters altitude, at the head of Valle Grana. In 1475, Enrico Allemandi built the first Gothic chapel. Growing devotion required expansion in 1514—the decorations by Giovanni Botoneri, still visible, retain their original colors. The current sanctuary was completed in 1716 with the high altar added in 1775 and side porticos between 1861 and 1868. Excavations in 1894 unearthed a Roman altar dedicated to Mars and coins dating to around 250 AD, proof of occupation since imperial Roman times. Among the ex-votos hung inside, numerous portraits of cattle: the saint is considered protector of livestock.

The alpine pastures surrounding Castelmagno are the birthplace of Castelmagno DOP, a whole cow's milk cheese considered one of Italy's oldest. Produced only in the communes of Castelmagno, Pradleves, and Monterosso Grana according to aging techniques passed down through generations, it obtained protected designation of origin status. The village of San Pietro Monterosso preserves a settlement of dozens of Babaciu, life-size wooden figures inhabiting the alleyways.

→

Ascent to Colle Fauniera (2,481 m) from the Sanctuary of San Magno, a pass famous for Giro d'Italia stages. A monument honors Marco Pantani. 360° views over the plain and border ridges.

→

Castelmagno DOP tasting with alpine producers in Castelmagno or Pradleves, with visit to an artisanal cheese dairy (aging in natural caves).

Saluzzo

Top hotels

9.2 Hotel & Apartments " Perpoin "
9.1 Hotel Antico Podere Propano

Saluzzo served as capital of an independent marquisate for over three centuries (founded by Manfred I around 1125, annexed to France in 1548, then to Savoy in 1601 by the Treaty of Lyon). Its medieval historic center climbs cobbled lanes toward the Castiglia, the marquises' fortress built in 1270 and radically remodeled in 1492 by Ludovico II for the arrival of his bride Marguerite de Foix. The 55-meter circular tower dominates the entire plain to Monviso. The castle houses two museums unique in Italy: the Museum of Chivalric Civilization and the Prison Memory Museum. The civic tower of 1462 and its 14th-century ramparts are remarkably preserved.

Casa Cavassa is a 15th-century Renaissance palace, residence of the Cavassa family in service to the marquises. Its interior courtyard features wall frescoes, wooden balconies running the full width of facades, and coffered ceilings. It houses the Civic Museum with the Labors of Hercules in grisaille by Flemish painter Hans Clemer and the Madonna della Misericordia. The pediment bears the French motto 'droit quoi qu'il soit,' trace of the period of French rule.

The Abbey of Staffarda, 9 km from Saluzzo in the commune of Revello, is one of Italy's most complete medieval monastic complexes. Founded in 1135 by Manfred I on marshland given to the Cistercians, it was for three centuries a major agricultural and commercial center—the monks drained 1,300 hectares and created a covered market whose Gothic arcades survive. The Romano-Gothic church with three naves alternates gray stone and red brick; each pillar differs from the others. The high altar preserves the Polyptych of Pascale Oddone (1531), eight ovals of gilded and polychrome carved wooden statues. In 1690, French General Catinat took the abbey during the Battle of Staffarda, burning the archives and refectory.

Castello della Manta, 5 km south of Saluzzo, is a 13th-century fortified house transformed into a lordly residence around 1420 by Valerano di Saluzzo, son of Marquis Thomas III. Its baronial hall is covered with a late Gothic fresco cycle depicting the nine Heroes and nine Heroines in near life-size, with the Fountain of Youth—a rare example of early 15th-century secular painting. Belongs to FAI (Italian Environment Fund).

→

Stroll through Saluzzo's medieval center: from Piazza Castello, climb to the castello via cobbled lanes with views of Monviso. Pass through Piazzetta dei Mondagli and the birthplace of Silvio Pellico, poet and patriot whose My Prisons inspired Risorgimento revolutionaries.

→

Wine tasting from the Marquisate: Pelaverga, Quagliano, and Chatus, native grape varieties from the Saluzzo hills in DOC Colline Saluzzesi. Accompany with tomini (small valley cheeses) and local hazelnut gianduja.

The Palazzo Reale di Torino, erected from 1584 on the foundations of the old bishop's palace, was the main residence of the House of Savoy until Italian Unification. Listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1997 as part of the Residences of the Royal House of Savoy, the complex (Polo Reale) groups the palace with its royal apartments and baroque frescoes, the Galleria Sabauda (Italian and Flemish paintings), the Armeria Reale (weapons and armor), the Biblioteca Reale, and the royal gardens designed in part by André Le Nôtre. Piazza Castello is framed by Palazzo Madama—a blend of Roman gate, medieval castle, and Juvarra's baroque facade (1721)—and the church of San Lorenzo with Guarino Guarini's dome (1680).

Turin's Museo Egizio is the world's second-largest museum devoted to ancient Egypt, after Cairo. Founded in 1824, it assembles over 3,000 pieces including mummies, sarcophagi, papyri, and the reconstruction of the rock temple of Thutmose III (around 1450 BC), the statue of Amenhotep I, and the stele of Ramses II. Explanatory panels are translated into French.

The Mole Antonelliana (167.5 meters), designed by Alessandro Antonelli from 1863 as a synagogue then reclaimed by the city, is Turin's architectural symbol and one of Europe's tallest masonry structures. It houses the National Cinema Museum, whose spectacular scenography exploits the building's height across seven levels. The central panoramic elevator rises to 85 meters with views of the Alps on clear days.

Piazza San Carlo, nicknamed Turin's 'drawing room,' was inaugurated in 1638. Surrounded by columned porticos, it's flanked by the near-twin churches of San Carlo Borromeo and Santa Cristina. At its center, the equestrian statue of Emanuele Filiberto of Savoy. Under the arcades stand the historic 18th-19th century cafés, meeting places for royalty and Turinese writers. The pedestrian Via Roma links the square to Palazzo Reale over 700 meters of porticos.

→

Wander the Quadrilatero Romano, old Roman Turin with its grid plan dating to Augustus. Maze of narrow streets linking 17th-century palaces, Romanesque churches, and interior courtyards. In the evening, the district comes alive at aperitivo hour (Negroni, Martini, Punt e Mes—born in Turin or Piedmont).

→

Sample Turinese specialties at the Porta Palazzo market (Europe's largest open-air market, Piazza della Repubblica): Piedmont wines (Barolo, Barbaresco, Moscato), gianduja chocolates, vitello tonnato, agnolotti del plin. The market runs every morning except Sunday.

→

Optional excursion to Sacra di San Michele, a Romanesque abbey perched at 962 meters on Monte Pirchiriano in Valle di Susa (40 km from Turin). Founded around 983, it was one of Western Europe's most important abbeys and reportedly inspired Umberto Eco's The Name of the Rose. The Porta dello Zodiaco (12th century) and the Stairway of the Dead lead to the three-nave church.

The Ponte del Diavolo of Lanzo Torinese, built from 1378 to 1380 over the Stura di Lanzo, rises 16 meters above the river. Its construction cost 1,400 florins, financed by a wine tax for ten years. It allowed travelers to avoid the hostile territories of the princes of Acaja and Montferrat when reaching Turin. In 1564, a gate was installed on the bridge to block travelers during plague epidemics. Its name comes from a natural cavity 7 meters high and 6 meters at the base carved by waters in the left wall—the Giant's Cauldron—which locals attributed to the devil.

The Civic Tower of Aymon de Challant (1329-1357), 20.50 meters tall, was the gateway to the medieval borough. It bears the name of its builder, castellan of Lanzo from 1332 to 1357 in service to Margaret of Savoy. The church of Loreto was built in 1618 in just three months by the inhabitants, modeled on the Santa Casa of Loreto, at the initiative of Margaret of Savoy. Via San Giovanni Bosco is the old heart of town with its terracotta porticos, ogival windows, and medieval decorations.

The Ecomuseum of the History of Alpinism of the Valli di Lanzo, installed at the headquarters of the Italian Alpine Club of Lanzo, traces the history of these valleys considered the cradle of Piedmontese alpinism. Over 360 marked trails covering 1,000 km constitute the hiking network. The ethnographic museum of textile art is housed in the 17th-century Palazzo degli Estensi.

The Casa del Grissino in Lanzo recalls the invention of the Turin breadstick: physician Pecchio reportedly developed this thin, crispy bread to treat young Vittorio Amedeo II, unable to digest ordinary bread. The Lanzo grissino is distinct from the rubatà grissino of Chieri.

→

Hike to Lago Monastero from Ala di Stura (Val Grande di Lanzo): alpine lake surrounded by rhododendrons, panorama over high-altitude pastures. Ala di Stura preserves medieval buildings and was connected to Turin by the Turin-Ceres railway in 1916.

→

Via ferrata or mountain safety workshop in the Valli di Lanzo (several equipped sectors available depending on season, inquire with local providers).

Valle del Lys (Gressoney Valley) was colonized in the 13th century by the Walser, a Germanic population originating from the Valais canton in Switzerland. Departing from Zermatt via the Theodul Pass (3,317 m) to the west and Monte Moro (2,984 m) to the east, they settled in a then-inhospitable region in exchange for rights and freedoms granted by local feudal lords. They still speak titsch (in Gressoney) and töitschu (in Issime) today, Alemannic dialects close to German—in Issime, titsch is still taught in primary school.

Castel Savoia in Gressoney-Saint-Jean was built between 1899 and 1904 by Queen Margherita of Savoy, wife of King Umberto I, who particularly appreciated this valley for her summer stays. Its neo-Gothic towers and English gardens contrast with the alpine landscape. The queen received botanists and alpinists here. Period furniture is largely preserved.

The hamlet of Alpenzu (1,776 m), above Gressoney-La-Trinité, is one of the best-preserved Walser villages in the Alps. Its stadel—larch granaries on stilts—date from the 16th-17th centuries. The Walzer Weg (Walser Path) links Gressoney-Saint-Jean to Alpenzu crossing alpine pastures and larch forests. The Walser Kulturzentrum in Gressoney-Saint-Jean documents the architecture, traditional costume, and language of this community.

→

Hike the Walzer Weg from Gressoney-Saint-Jean to Alpenzu: ascent through alpine pastures, view of the Monte Rosa glacier. Duration about 3h round trip.

→

In summer, the cable car from Gressoney-La-Trinité climbs to 3,200 meters on the Monte Rosa massif, offering direct access to glaciers and high-mountain routes.

The Sanctuary of Oropa, at 1,200 meters altitude in the Biella Prealps, is the largest Marian sanctuary in the Alps and has been part of UNESCO World Heritage since 2003 (Sacri Monti). Its origins reportedly date to the 4th century, when Bishop Eusebius of Vercelli placed a statue of the Virgin there according to tradition. In 1620, during the first coronation of the Black Virgin statue, baroque works accelerated. Filippo Juvarra designed the Porta Regia in the 18th century, Guarino Guarini participated in the overall design. The twelve chapels of the Sacro Monte (built from 1620) recount the life of Mary in polychrome statues and frescoes by Giovanni d'Enrico. The new basilica, consecrated in 1960, rises over 80 meters to its dome.

The medieval quarter of Piazzo, the upper part of Biella accessible by funicular from the lower town, preserves a Piazza Cisterna surrounded by 15th-century arcades, the Romanesque church of San Giacomo, and Palazzo Gromo Ternengo (16th century). The cathedral baptistery, built between the 7th and 11th centuries with a central plan and four apses, is one of the most significant examples of Piedmontese Romanesque art. Above the door, a Roman bas-relief depicting Hercules in reuse testifies to continuity of occupation since antiquity.

Biella is first mentioned in 826 as Bugella, in a donation act by Louis the Pious to a Count Busone. From 1245, city statutes reference weaver and wool worker guilds. In 1695, a silk manufactory was built there; in 1835, the same building was converted to a mechanical wool mill, placing Biella at the forefront of European textile industry. Cittadellarte - Fondazione Pistoletto, installed in a former mill on the Cervo river, is a contemporary art center founded in 1996 by artist Michelangelo Pistoletto.

→

Cable car from Oropa to Rifugio Savoia (1,900 m), then 15-minute hike to Lake Mucrone: panorama over the plain to Turin and Milan on clear days. Return via old shepherd trails.

→

Forest hike in Burcina-Felice Piacenza Natural Park, south of Biella: reserve known for its spectacular rhododendron collection (blooming May-June). Bronze Age tools have been found in this massif.

Varallo

The Sacro Monte of Varallo is the oldest and most important of Piedmont's Sacred Mountains, inscribed as UNESCO World Heritage in 2003. Founded in 1491 by Franciscan friar Bernardino Caimi, who had lived in the Holy Land, it recreates Jerusalem's Holy Places on the hillside for pilgrims who couldn't travel there. Over 40 chapels spread across the cliff, decorated with frescoes and polychrome terracotta sculpture groups depicting scenes from Christ's life, were created between the 15th and 19th centuries. Gaudenzio Ferrari, who executed his major works here between 1517 and 1530, developed a narrative and expressive style that prefigures baroque.

Varallo, founded in the 13th century, is the main town of Valsesia. Its historic center preserves arcaded medieval houses, the Pinacoteca del Sacco (15th to 18th-century paintings including several works by Gaudenzio Ferrari), and the Collegiata di San Gaudenzio. Valsesia climbs about 80 km from the plain to the foot of Monte Rosa (4,634 m), second highest summit in the Alps.

Upper Valsesia opens onto Alagna Valsesia, a Walser village at the foot of Monte Rosa at 1,191 meters altitude. As in Valle del Lys, the Walser built their typical stadel here from the 13th century. The local Walser museum traces the history of these pioneers from Valais. Alagna is the starting point for Monte Rosa ascents and the Monterosa Ski domain.

→

Rafting or kayaking on the Sesia torrent from Varallo or Borgosesia: the rapids of the Sesia (class III to IV depending on season and sector) are among Piedmont's most popular. Providers offer guided 2 to 4-hour runs.

→

Canyoning in gorges of a Sesia tributary (several equipped sectors in the valley, beginner to intermediate level, wetsuit and helmet provided by outfitters).

→

Catch-and-release sport fishing on authorized Sesia stretches (salmonids in alpine setting, daily permit required, available from local fishing associations).

Lake Orta (13.4 km by 2.5 km, elevation 292 m) is the westernmost of the great Piedmontese lakes, carved by the Simplon glacier. At its center, the island of San Giulio—275 meters long by 140 wide—is reached by boat from Piazza Motta. Legend tells that Saint Julius, a Greek missionary, crossed the lake on his cloak around 390 to drive out dragons and serpents infesting the island, then built a basilica. The Lombards erected a fortress there in the 6th century; Berengar II took refuge there; Otto I besieged it in 962. The current Romanesque basilica (12th century) preserves a serpentine ambo from 1130, carved with figures of Saints Julius and John the Evangelist, and a crypt housing Saint Julius's relics. The interior contains 15th-century frescoes and nave ceilings painted by 18th-century local masters. The island circuit follows the Via del Silenzio, skirting the Mater Ecclesiae Benedictine abbey founded in 1973.

The medieval village of Orta San Giulio centers on Piazza Motta and the Broletto, a Renaissance communal palace from 1582 whose portico is covered with frescoes. Cobbled lanes preserve their intact character: 16th-17th century mansions, pointed-arch doors, villa gardens overlooking the lake. Villa Bossi (17th century) opens onto terraced gardens reflecting in the water. The village is listed among Italy's 'Most Beautiful Villages.' One kilometer away, the painted hamlet of Legro is an open-air gallery whose facades are covered with murals by artists from around the world.

The Sacro Monte of Orta (400 m altitude, about 100 m elevation from the village) is the only Sacred Mountain in Piedmont and Lombardy dedicated to the life of Saint Francis of Assisi. It was founded from 1583 at the initiative of the Orta community and Abbot Amico Canobio of Novara, near a pre-existing church of Saint Nicholas. Twenty chapels string along a spiral route through the woods, each covered with micaceous gneiss slabs and containing frescoes and life-size terracotta sculptures. The ensemble counts 900 frescoes and 376 statues by 16th-18th century artists. In May 1882, Nietzsche met Lou Salomé here. UNESCO listed since 2003.

→

Boat crossing to the island of San Giulio from Piazza Motta (regular small boat service, 5-minute crossing). Walk around the island in 15-20 minutes on the Via del Silenzio.

→

Blue Ring hike starting from Sacro Monte: descent toward Via Francisca, then climb to Monte Mesma (San Francesco convent) with panorama over the lake and Monte Rosa in the background. Loop of about 10 km.

→

In June, the Cusio Ancient Music Festival gives concerts in the 18th-century Casa Tallone on the island of San Giulio, with international musicians playing period instruments. In May, Orta in Fiore transforms Piazza Motta into a flower market.

Parco del Valentino (50 hectares along the Po) houses the Borgo Medievale, an 1884 historical recreation of a Piedmontese and Valdostan village inspired by actual regional castles. The Rocca reproduces 14th-century military architecture. Castello del Valentino (17th century, Savoy royal residence, UNESCO listed) adjoins the park.

The Reggia di Venaria Reale, 8 km north of Turin, is one of Europe's largest royal residences (80,000 m² of buildings, 80 hectares of gardens). Designed from 1658 by Amedeo di Castellamonte for Vittorio Amedeo II, remodeled by Juvarra, it was abandoned in the 19th century and restored over 25 years to reopen in 2007. UNESCO listed since 1997. Juvarra's galleries, the Sant'Uberto chapel, and the French gardens are highlights.

The Basilica of Superga (672 m altitude), accessible by rack railway from Turin (30 min), is Filippo Juvarra's masterwork, built between 1717 and 1731 by order of Vittorio Amedeo II in thanksgiving for a 1706 victory over the French. Its 65-meter dome is visible from across the Po plain. The royal crypt houses the tombs of most Sardinian kings of the House of Savoy. At the rear, a plaque commemorates the May 4, 1949 crash that killed the entire Torino Football Club team, then Europe's best.

→

Turin is a major rail hub: direct TGV Paris-Turin in under 4h, connections to Milan (1h), Florence (2h30), Rome (4h30). Turin-Caselle airport is 16 km from downtown (shuttle, 45 min).

→

Closing lunch in the San Salvario district or in one of Via Roma's historic cafés. Specialties not to miss: tajarin (fine pasta with 30 egg yolks), bagna cauda (hot anchovy-garlic sauce), bonet (chocolate and amaretti flan).
