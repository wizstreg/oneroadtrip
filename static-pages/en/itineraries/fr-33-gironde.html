Terraced vineyards facing the estuary, oyster parks battered by sea spray, honey-stone villages between river and ocean. Gironde marries land and sea with natural elegance.

Bordeaux

Place de la Bourse is the symbol of the Port of the Moon, a UNESCO World Heritage site since 2007. Built in the 18th century, it reflects Bordeaux's maritime trade prosperity with symmetrical façades framing the Three Graces fountain. The location becomes particularly photogenic at sunset when lights reflect in the Miroir d'Eau (Water Mirror) just opposite.

La Cité du Vin is an interactive museum dedicated to wine cultures from around the world. Its architecture evokes a decanter swirling wine. The permanent exhibition Via Sensoria takes you through the four wine seasons via immersive sensory installations. The 8th-floor belvedere offers panoramic views over Bordeaux and the Garonne River, with a complimentary wine tasting included in the admission ticket.

Saint-André Cathedral has dominated old Bordeaux since the 12th century. This UNESCO World Heritage monument hosted the wedding of Eleanor of Aquitaine and Louis VII in 1137. The royal portal features remarkable medieval sculptures. The Pey-Berland Tower, standing separate from the main building, can be climbed for panoramic city views (231 steps).

Rue Sainte-Catherine is Europe's longest pedestrian shopping street at 1.2 kilometers. It connects Place de la Comédie to the historic center, running through the commercial districts. Covered passages like Galerie Bordelaise add architectural charm. It's the main shopping artery in Bordeaux.

The Grand Théâtre de Bordeaux, built by Victor Louis in 1780, is a masterpiece of neoclassical architecture. Its columned façade is topped with twelve statues representing muses and goddesses. The interior houses a sumptuous Italian-style auditorium that inspired the Paris Opera Garnier. Guided tours reveal the backstage and the building's history.

The Chartrons district, once home to wine merchants, preserves its 18th-century bourgeois atmosphere. Elegant façades line Rue Notre-Dame, now filled with antique shops, art galleries, and wine cellars. The Chartrons market on Sunday mornings buzzes with energy. It's a pleasant neighborhood for strolling along the quays.

→

The Miroir d'Eau (Water Mirror), facing Place de la Bourse, is the world's largest reflecting pool at 3,450 m². Inaugurated in 2006, it alternates mirror phases (2 cm of water) and mist phases every 15 minutes. Children love running through it barefoot in summer. It's one of Bordeaux's most photographed spots, especially at sunset.

→

Garonne River cruises let you discover Bordeaux from the water. Several companies offer departures from the quays, with commentary on the city's maritime history. Cruises typically last 1.5 to 2 hours. Some include a snack or aperitif with local wine tasting.

→

Wine tasting in Bordeaux can be enjoyed at several downtown cellars. The Bar à Vin du CIVB, near the Grand Théâtre, offers guided Bordeaux wine tastings. Les Bassins des Lumières, in a former submarine base, combines immersive art exhibitions with tastings. For a pedagogical approach, the École du Vin de Bordeaux organizes introductory workshops.

→

Cycling along Bordeaux's quays. The city has a well-developed network of bike paths. Several rental shops offer classic or electric bikes by the day or half-day. The quayside route, from La Cité du Vin to the Pont de Pierre, is particularly pleasant with views of the river and monuments.

💡 VCub (bike-sharing) is also an option
→

Visit the Marché des Capucins, Bordeaux's largest market. Open every morning except Monday, it gathers local producers, fishmongers, greengrocers, and artisans. The atmosphere is lively and authentic. Perfect for discovering regional products: Arcachon Bay oysters, cheeses, charcuterie, wines. Several stalls offer on-site tastings.

💡 Arrive early for the best atmosphere and products

The medieval village of Saint-Émilion has been a UNESCO World Heritage site since 1999. Perched on a rocky promontory, it preserves cobbled streets, ramparts, and honey-colored stone houses. The village welcomes over one million visitors annually. Take time to wander the lanes discovering artisan shops, wine cellars, and ancient buildings.

Saint-Émilion's Monolithic Church is Europe's largest underground church, carved from limestone in the 11th-12th centuries. The Gothic bell tower, standing 68 meters high, can be climbed for panoramic views of the village and vineyards (118 steps). The church interior can only be visited on guided tours organized by the tourist office. The dimensions are impressive: 38 meters long, 20 meters wide, 11 meters high.

The King's Tower, a 13th-century Romanesque keep, is the only remaining vestige of Saint-Émilion's château. Standing 32 meters tall, it's open for self-guided visits. The 118 steps lead to the top where you'll discover 360° views over the village, tiled roofs, surrounding vineyards, and the Dordogne Valley. It's Saint-Émilion's best viewpoint.

The Cordeliers Cloister is a 14th-century former Franciscan convent transformed into a sparkling wine cellar. The underground galleries, carved from limestone, now house Crémant production. The visit combines historical heritage with discovery of traditional sparkling winemaking methods. Tasting included in the visit. The cloister garden is highly photogenic.

Saint-Émilion's catacombs form a network of underground galleries developed during the Romanesque period. These former limestone quarries served as burial sites and ossuaries. The guided tour reveals the fascinating history of these mysterious places. The atmosphere is special with constant coolness (14°C) and plays of light and shadow.

→

Visit and tasting at a Saint-Émilion wine château. The appellation counts 82 classified grands crus in a small area, creating an exceptional concentration. Family estates and grand domains alike offer guided tours of cellars and vineyards. Tastings help you understand the appellation's typical blends (predominantly Merlot, with Cabernet Franc and Cabernet Sauvignon). Reservations essential.

→

Saint-Émilion Wine Route circuit. Several marked routes cross the vineyard between prestigious châteaux and family properties. The UNESCO-listed vineyard landscape remains intact. You can follow the circuit by car, bicycle (rentals available), or tourist train. Views over the hillsides are magnificent. Plan stops for tastings at châteaux that interest you.

💡 Circuit maps available at the tourist office
→

Tasting Saint-Émilion macarons, a local specialty since the 17th century. These small almond cakes, different from Parisian macarons, have a soft texture and pronounced almond flavor. Several village pastry shops offer them. It's a pleasant sweet break after visiting the village. They keep well and make good souvenirs to bring home.

→

Cycling through Saint-Émilion's vineyards. The Roger Lapébie bike path, built on a former railway line, connects Sauveterre-de-Guyenne to Bordeaux passing through the vineyard. The route is flat and easy, perfect for family outings. Bike rentals available in Saint-Émilion or neighboring villages. Allow 2-3 hours for a leisurely loop with photo stops.

Pauillac is the capital of Médoc wine country, world-renowned for its 18 classified grands crus. The village stretches along the Gironde estuary on 1.3 km of quays lined with 18th and 19th-century bourgeois houses. The Pauillac appellation includes 3 of the 5 premiers grands crus classés from the 1855 classification: Château Lafite Rothschild, Château Latour, and Château Mouton Rothschild. The vineyard covers approximately 1,200 hectares cultivated like French formal gardens.

The Pauillac Tourist and Wine Center is the ideal starting point for discovering the vineyard. You'll find a selection of wines from the 8 Médoc appellations at estate prices, advice for organizing château visits, and tourist information. The team can book visits to wine estates for you. A shop offers wine accessories and local products.

Saint-Martin Church in Pauillac, built in the 19th century on the site of a former church, overlooks the waterfront. Its neo-Gothic architecture contrasts with the classic quayside façades. The interior is sober yet luminous. The forecourt offers lovely estuary and marina views. The weekly market takes place in the adjacent square every Saturday morning.

Pauillac marina welcomes boats passing through the estuary. The landscaped quays are pleasant for strolling with views over the Gironde. On clear days, you can see the right bank and the Blaye vineyard. Several restaurants and cafés line the waterfront. It's a peaceful spot to pause while watching the movement of boats and tides.

→

Visit a Médoc wine château. Prestigious properties and more accessible estates alike offer guided tours of their facilities. You'll discover the vines, the cellars where wines age, and the property's history. The final tasting helps you understand the Médoc wine style, marked by Cabernet Sauvignon. Reservations required, often several weeks in advance for classified grands crus.

💡 Some châteaux only accept professionals
→

The Médoc Château Route (D2) crosses the prestigious appellations of Margaux, Saint-Julien, Pauillac, and Saint-Estèphe. This 60-kilometer route between Bordeaux and Pointe de Grave runs along the estuary passing dozens of wine châteaux. Landscapes of meticulously aligned vines and varied château architecture make this route unmissable. Plan a full day to enjoy the journey with some estate visits.

💡 Designate a driver or rent a vehicle with chauffeur
→

Pauillac Châteaux hike. Two marked walking loops of 8 and 14 km let you discover the Pauillac AOC estates on foot. The circuit crosses vineyards between prestigious châteaux and offers beautiful estuary views. It's an enjoyable and sporty way to discover the terroir. The route is flat, accessible to all. Maps available at the Tourist Center.

→

Boat tour on the Gironde estuary. Several companies offer cruises departing from Pauillac to discover the estuary, Europe's largest (75 km long). You'll pass vine-planted banks, glimpse islands and port facilities. Some cruises include guided commentary on river heritage and Médoc wine tasting. Duration typically 2 to 3 hours.

→

Tasting and lunch at a wine estate. Several Médoc domains offer gastronomic experiences combining cellar visits, guided tastings, and lunch with food and wine pairings. Dishes highlight local products: Pauillac lamb, garden vegetables, regional cheeses. It's a convivial way to deepen your wine knowledge while enjoying the exceptional château setting.

The Margaux appellation is one of six communal Médoc appellations, renowned for elegant and aromatic wines. The vineyard extends over 1,500 hectares across four communes: Margaux-Cantenac, Soussans, Arsac, and Labarde. The appellation counts 21 grands crus classés from 1855, including the celebrated Château Margaux. The gravelly terroir (pebbles) brings finesse and complexity to the wines. Margaux village itself is small but charming.

The Margaux Wine and Tourism Center welcomes you to discover the appellation. The museum space explains the terroir, vineyard history, and the 1855 classification. A wine selection is available for tasting and purchase. Staff can advise you on châteaux to visit and help with reservations. It's the ideal starting point for organizing your vineyard discovery.

The Margaux-Cantenac Châteaux loop is a 15 km route to walk or cycle. This marked itinerary crosses the vineyard passing 18 of the 21 classified grands crus in the appellation. You'll pass prestigious properties like Château Margaux, Château Palmer, Château Lascombes. The path offers beautiful estuary and Île Margaux views. Allow 2 hours by bike, 4 hours on foot without visit stops.

Saint-Michel Church in Margaux, built in the 19th century in neo-Romanesque style, stands in the village center. Its square bell tower is visible from afar across the flat Médoc landscape. The sober interior houses some interesting stained glass. The forecourt opens onto the main village square where the market is held. It's a useful landmark for orientation in Margaux.

→

Visit and tasting at a Margaux château. The appellation offers a wide diversity of wine tourism experiences, from classified grands crus to more accessible family properties. Guided tours help you understand vineyard work, vinification, and barrel aging. Tastings reveal the elegance characteristic of Margaux wines. Reservations essential, sometimes several weeks in advance for the most prestigious estates.

💡 Family properties are often more accessible
→

Cycling through Margaux vineyards. The Médoc's flat topography and quiet small roads make cycling very pleasant. Several rental shops offer classic or electric bikes. You can follow the château loop or create your own itinerary. Landscapes of vines aligned as far as the eye can see are magnificent. Plan breaks to admire châteaux and take photos.

💡 Electric bikes help with wind
→

Margaux Golf offers an 18-hole, 6,000-meter course spread over 45 hectares. Designed in a natural setting amid vineyards, it's renowned for its beauty and impeccable maintenance. Greens are bordered by century-old elms and ash trees. The clubhouse offers views over the course and surrounding vineyards. On-site restaurant. Green fees accessible with reservations recommended.

→

Margaux Saveurs is an annual event held the third weekend of November. Many appellation châteaux open their doors for tastings, entertainment, concerts, and gourmet meals. It's a festive and convivial opportunity to discover the Margaux appellation differently. The program blends wine discoveries with musical ambiance. Tickets and reservations on the event website.

Arcachon's Winter Town is a unique quarter created in the 19th century to welcome spa visitors. Over 300 Belle Époque villas with eclectic architecture hide in a wooded park on the heights. Styles vary: colonial, Moorish, neo-Gothic, Swiss chalet. A stroll through the lanes reveals these architectural gems. Costumed guided tours are regularly organized by the tourist office.

The Dune du Pilat is Europe's highest dune at 104 meters tall and 3 km long. A member of the Grands Sites de France network, it's in perpetual motion, advancing 1 to 5 meters per year toward the forest. A 160-step staircase facilitates the climb from April to November. The panorama from the summit is spectacular: on one side the Atlantic Ocean and Banc d'Arguin, on the other the Landes forest stretching endlessly. Allow 30-45 minutes to climb and descend.

Arcachon Bay is a 155 km² lagoon connected to the ocean by channels. At its center, Île aux Oiseaux (Bird Island) and its two tchanquées cabins (cabins on stilts) are the bay's emblems. Strong tides (up to 4 meters amplitude) transform the landscape: at low tide, oyster parks are accessible on foot. The bay circuit by road measures about 80 km and lets you discover different oyster-farming villages.

Jetée Thiers extends 250 meters into the bay from Arcachon's central beach. It's the embarkation point for boat excursions to Île aux Oiseaux, Banc d'Arguin, and Cap Ferret. Built in 1865, it has been rebuilt several times after storms. The pier's end offers beautiful views of the Winter Town and villas. Pleasant stroll at any hour, particularly at sunset.

Cap Ferret's oyster villages (L'Herbe, Le Canon, La Vigne) have preserved their authenticity. Colorful oyster farmers' cabins line up along the piers. You can taste fresh oysters directly from producers, feet in the water facing the bay. The atmosphere is peaceful, far from tourist bustle. L'Herbe village, with its small Algerian chapel, is particularly photogenic.

Pereire Beach is one of Arcachon's four beaches, on the bay side. Quieter than the central beach, it's appreciated by families for its shallow calm waters and absence of waves. Colorful beach cabins line the seafront. The beachfront promenade offers beautiful bay and boat views. Several restaurants and ice cream shops are located along the waterfront.

→

Oyster tasting in an oyster farmer's cabin. Arcachon Bay produces over 8,000 tons of oysters annually. Oyster farmers welcome visitors directly to their cabins for tastings. You'll taste oysters facing the bay, accompanied by bread, butter, and lemon, with a glass of white wine. Some cabins also offer seafood platters. Authentic atmosphere and affordable prices.

💡 Arrive outside peak hours
→

Boat excursion on Arcachon Bay. Several companies offer guided outings to Île aux Oiseaux and its tchanquées cabins, Banc d'Arguin, or Cap Ferret. Some cruises include onboard oyster tasting. Duration varies from 1 to 3 hours depending on circuits. Boats generally depart from Jetée Thiers in Arcachon. Remember to check tide schedules which influence departures.

💡 Check tide-dependent schedules
→

Cycling around Arcachon Bay. The bike path network totals over 220 km around the bay. The complete circuit measures 80 km but you can choose shorter sections. The route is mostly flat and crosses pine forest. You'll pass through oyster villages, beaches, and the Dune du Pilat. Bike rentals available in all bay communes.

💡 Bring water and sunscreen
→

Excursion to Banc d'Arguin, a nature reserve at the foot of Dune du Pilat. This 4 km long sandbank emerges at low tide and hosts numerous migratory bird species. Access is by boat only (departures from Arcachon or Cap Ferret). On site, you can swim in shallow waters, picnic, and birdwatch. Watch tide schedules for the return.

💡 Bring picnic and sun protection
→

Climbing Cap Ferret Lighthouse. This red and white striped lighthouse, 53 meters tall, can be visited from Easter to All Saints' Day. You must climb 258 steps to reach the summit but the 360° panorama is worth the effort. You'll discover Arcachon Bay, Conche du Mimbeau, Dune du Pilat, oyster parks, and the ocean. On clear days, views extend up to 40 km. Souvenir shop at the lighthouse base.

💡 Go in morning or late afternoon
→

Low tide outing with an oyster farmer. This unique experience lets you discover the oyster farmer's trade from the inside. You'll board the boat to work on oyster parks at low tide. The oyster farmer explains farming stages, daily work, and trade challenges. The outing typically ends with a tasting. Duration 3 to 5 hours depending on tides. Reservations required several days in advance.

→

Swimming at Arcachon beaches. The town has four bay-side beaches (Eyrac, Thiers, Pereire, Abatilles) and several ocean beaches nearby (Moulleau, Pyla). Bay beaches are ideal for children with calm shallow waters. Ocean beaches offer waves for surfing and bodyboarding. All beaches are supervised in season. Deckchair and parasol rentals available on main beaches.

The Basilica of Notre-Dame-de-la-Fin-des-Terres is a 12th-century Romanesque art jewel, UNESCO World Heritage listed. Built to welcome pilgrims on the Way of Saint James, it was buried by dunes in the 18th century then completely excavated in the 19th. The Romanesque architecture is remarkably preserved with sculpted capitals. The sober luminous interior invites contemplation. Classical music concerts are regularly organized here.

Soulac's Old Village counts about 500 Belle Époque villas built between 1860 and 1930. These pink brick houses, with their sculpted eaves, canopies, arabesques, and colored stained glass, testify to the seaside resort's heyday. Two visitor circuits reveal the finest: the Soulac villas circuit (1.5 hours) and the architectural circuit (1 hour). Maps available at the tourist office. Some villas bear pretty feminine names.

Soulac's seafront stretches over 7 kilometers of fine sandy beaches. The landscaped promenade runs along supervised beaches with ocean views and distant Cordouan Lighthouse. Breakwaters—concrete slabs installed in the 1930s to combat erosion—create natural seawater pools at high tide. Several accesses descend to beaches from the promenade. Restaurants and ice cream shops line the seafront.

A 4-meter-tall replica of the Statue of Liberty stands on the seafront facing the ocean. Like the New York original, it was created in the Bartholdi workshops. She gazes toward America and the Côte d'Argent dunes. It's a friendly landmark and popular photo spot. A memorial to the northern Médoc fortress stands nearby, recalling German occupation.

→

Excursion to Cordouan Lighthouse, nicknamed the Versailles of the Seas or King of Lighthouses. Built starting in 1584 under Henri IV, it's France's oldest lighthouse still in operation. Standing 68 meters tall, it's located at sea 7 km from shore. UNESCO World Heritage listed since 2021, it can be visited from April to September. The boat excursion lasts 3.5 hours with 1.5 hours on-site visit. Landing is done feet in the water. Impressive interior with royal apartments and chapel.

💡 Shoes that can get wet
→

Swimming at Soulac beaches. The resort has several supervised summer beaches: central beach, Naïades beach, Amélie du Nord beach. Atlantic waves attract surfers and bodyboarders. Surf schools offer lessons on two beaches. Beaches are wide and clean, with fine sand. Beware of baïnes, dangerous currents typical of the Atlantic coast. Always swim in supervised areas.

→

Visit to Pointe de Grave, the northern tip of the Médoc peninsula where the Gironde meets the Atlantic. A monument commemorates the 1917 American troop landing. The site offers spectacular views over the estuary, ocean, and Cordouan Lighthouse. It's the starting point of the GR8 trail running up the entire Atlantic coast. A ferry connects Pointe de Grave to Royan in Charente-Maritime (20-minute crossing). Wild landscapes of dunes and forests.

→

Cycling through pine forest. Soulac has a network of bike paths winding through the Landes forest or leading toward the estuary. Routes are shaded and pleasant, away from beach heat. Several marked circuits reveal heritage and nature. Bikes, rosalies, and tandem rentals available in town. Paths are flat and accessible to all.

💡 Shaded routes, pleasant even in summer
→

Visit the Soulac Archaeology Museum. This small museum presents the history of the Médoc Point from Antiquity to medieval times. Collections include objects discovered during local excavations: ceramics, tools, coins, architectural elements. Several rooms trace the basilica's history and the Saint James pilgrimage. Modest admission. Guided tours possible by reservation.

Blaye Citadel is a military fortress designed by Vauban between 1685 and 1689. With 38 hectares of fortifications, 1.5 km of ramparts, and four bastions, it's an impressive defensive complex. UNESCO-listed along with Fort Pâté and Fort Médoc, it forms the Estuary Lock that was meant to protect Bordeaux from maritime invasions. The citadel now houses shops, restaurants, and accommodations. Free entry, paid guided underground visit.

The citadel ramparts offer spectacular panoramas over the Gironde estuary, Europe's largest. On clear days, you can see Fort Pâté on its islet and Fort Médoc on the opposite bank. The rampart walk circles the entire citadel. Several viewpoints and orientation tables help you get your bearings. Sunset from the ramparts is particularly beautiful with reflections on the estuary.

Château des Rudel, inside the citadel, is a 12th-century medieval vestige. It was the fortress dominating Blaye before Vauban integrated its ruins into the new fortifications. The famous troubadour Jaufré Rudel was reportedly born here. Part of the towers and curtain walls survive. The site now houses the Estuary Conservatory and a museum on citadel history. Panoramic views from the tower remains.

The Minimes Convent, built under Henri IV in the early 17th century, served as a place of worship for garrisons. Integrated into the citadel by Vauban, it retains its original architecture with cloister and convent buildings. Now transformed into a vacation center and event venue. The chapel can be visited and hosts temporary exhibitions. The cloister is a peaceful haven with its central garden.

The citadel's underground passages form a network of defensive galleries carved into limestone rock. The guided tour (about 45 minutes) reveals this ingenious system designed by Vauban. You'll explore galleries, vaulted rooms, and understand the defense strategy. Constant temperature of 14°C year-round, bring warm clothing. Reservations recommended through the tourist office.

→

The Flowered Corniche Road connects Blaye to Bourg-sur-Gironde over 10 kilometers. This small road winds between limestone cliffs, dry stone walls, and the estuary. Landscapes are magnificent with views of carrelets (fishing huts on stilts), silvery Gironde waters, and vineyards. You'll pass through the troglodyte villages of Plassac and Mombrier. Many spots to stop and photograph. Narrow road, drive slowly.

💡 Better in late afternoon for light
→

Blaye wine tasting. The Blaye Côtes de Bordeaux appellation produces mainly powerful red wines and dry whites. Several cellars and châteaux in the region offer tastings. The Blaye Wine Center, in the citadel, presents an appellation selection and organizes guided tastings. Blaye's Spring Wines event in April gathers over 300 winemakers in the citadel for the season's biggest wine event.

→

Cruise on the Gironde estuary. Boat excursions depart from Blaye to discover the Vauban Lock: you'll pass Fort Pâté on its islet and Fort Médoc on the Médoc bank. Guided cruises explain the defensive system's history and estuary navigation. Some outings include a stop at Fort Médoc. Duration 2 to 3 hours. Departures vary by season and tides.

→

Blaye Market. The market is held three times weekly (Wednesday, Saturday, and Sunday mornings) on the citadel's alleys and in the lower town. You'll find local products: fruits and vegetables, cheeses, honey, wines, estuary oysters, fish. The atmosphere is friendly and authentic. It's a good opportunity to chat with producers and discover regional specialties. Saturday's market is the largest.

→

Horse-drawn carriage or tourist train ride through the citadel. These guided tours reveal the citadel's history and monuments in a fun way. The tourist train circles the ramparts with audio commentary. Carriages offer rides in and around the citadel. It's a pleasant option for families with children or people with walking difficulties. Regular departures in season.

La Réole is a medieval city founded in 977, one of Gironde's oldest urban centers. Classified as a City of Art and History, it's France's smallest commune to carry this label. The town clings to a rocky promontory overlooking the Garonne. It was protected by three successive walls, of which vestiges remain. Wander cobbled lanes to discover half-timbered houses, vaulted passages, and small squares. The atmosphere is authentic and peaceful.

The Benedictine Priory of Saint-Pierre majestically dominates the Garonne. Founded in the 10th century, it's at the origin of the town's development. The 18th-century classical façade hides older buildings. The priory now houses the town hall, municipal services, and library. The 18th-century wrought iron entrance gate is an ironwork masterpiece. The forecourt offers beautiful river and hillside views.

La Réole's Town Hall is one of France's oldest civic buildings, dating from the 12th century. This remarkable Gothic edifice originally served as market halls and common house. The architecture testifies to La Réole's political and economic importance in the Middle Ages. The square-facing façade preserves its Gothic arcades. The interior houses municipal services. The building can be visited on guided tours organized by the tourist office.

Château des Quat'Sos is an imposing 13th-century medieval fortress. Built to protect the town, it had four corner towers (hence its name of Four Sous). The château was dismantled on Richelieu's orders in 1629. Two imposing round towers and part of the curtain walls survive. Transformed into a bourgeois residence in the 18th century, it's now private. Visible from outside, it can be visited exceptionally during Heritage Days or on reserved guided tours.

Saint-Pierre Church in La Réole combines several architectural styles from the 12th to 15th centuries. The Gothic building presents Romanesque and Flamboyant elements. The sculpted capitals and stained glass merit attention. The church preserves interesting furnishings including a Baroque altarpiece. It's part of the Vézelay Way stage on the Santiago de Compostela pilgrimage routes. Remarkable acoustics host classical music concerts.

Guided tour of La Réole. The tourist office and City of Art and History service offer themed guided tours: medieval heritage, town history, architecture. The classic tour lasts 1.5 hours and covers main monuments and neighborhoods. You'll discover the fascinating history of this city that alternated between French and English control during the Hundred Years' War. Passionate guides bring La Réole's past to life. Reservations recommended.

→

Stroll along the Garonne banks. The quays developed in the 19th century offer a pleasant riverside walk. You'll enjoy views of vineyard-covered hillsides on the opposite bank and the Rouergue suspension bridge. A towpath follows the river upstream and downstream. It's a peaceful spot for walking or picnicking. Some benches let you settle in facing the river. Sunset here is particularly beautiful.

→

La Réole Market is held every Saturday morning on the Garonne quays. This traditional market gathers local producers and artisans. You'll find seasonal fruits and vegetables, cheeses, charcuterie, honey, Entre-deux-Mers wines. The atmosphere is friendly and authentic. It's an opportunity to chat with producers and discover regional specialties. Some stalls offer on-site dining for breakfast.

→

Discovering La Réole's gastronomy. Several restaurants highlight Gironde terroir products: duck, foie gras, porcini mushrooms, asparagus, nearby Agen prunes, regional cheeses. Entre-deux-Mers wines perfectly accompany these dishes. The town also preserves pastry specialties. For an authentic experience, choose an old town restaurant with summer terrace overlooking the quays.

→

Walk in Charros Park, developed at the foot of medieval ramparts. This peaceful landscaped garden follows the path of the former Charros stream that served as château moats. Interpretation panels and benches invite relaxation and heritage discovery. The park offers lovely views of fortifications and third wall vestiges. It's a green haven appreciated by locals. Children's playground.
