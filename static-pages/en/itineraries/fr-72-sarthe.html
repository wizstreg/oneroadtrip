Riverside stones catch the golden light. Abbey bells ring through morning mist. Medieval bridges span emerald valleys. The Sarthe breathes with timeless tranquility, entirely customizable to your journey.

Discover the Prytanée at La Flèche, the pottery workshops of Malicorne, hear Gregorian chants at Solesmes Abbey, and meander through Sablé-sur-Sarthe. The Sarthe region offers the gentle charm of the Maine along the water.

  • Sablé: medieval river town with storied bridges

Founded in 1603 by King Henry IV, the Prytanée National Militaire impresses with its classical architecture. The Saint-Louis church holds the heart ashes of both the king and Queen Marie de Medici.

The formal French gardens of the Prytanée extend into a centuries-old park. You'll discover the courtyards of Austerlitz and Sebastopol, witnesses to a prestigious military heritage.

Established in 1946, the La Flèche Zoo is home to 1,600 animals representing 150 species. White lions, elephants, and lemurs roam through carefully designed habitats.

The ruins of Château des Carmes line the banks of the Loir River. This protected natural site offers a verdant riverside walk.

The 14th-century Bruère mill produced natural ice using water from the Loir. A unique testament to pre-industrial ingenuity.

→

Stroll along the Loir River from Parc des Carmes. The landscaped riverbanks invite leisurely exploration.

→

The Monnerie Lakes offer swimming, a cycling path, and a beach. A fishing lake completes the offerings.

→

The Cré-sur-Loir marsh, a regional nature reserve, features several trails for discovering local wildlife.

Malicorne Sur Sarthe

Established in 1747, the Bourg-Joly Pottery carries on the time-honored craft of master potters. The openwork baskets are Malicorne's signature piece.

The Faïence and Ceramics Museum, designated a Museum of France, showcases antique ceramics and contemporary creations. Tour the interior of the firing kilns.

The 11th-century Saint-Sylvestre church houses a magnificent Gothic-style recumbent statue. Stained glass windows illuminate the Romanesque nave.

The Château de Malicorne, listed as a Historic Monument, once hosted Madame de Sévigné. The gardens overlook the Sarthe River.

The mills bear witness to a past when the river provided energy. The former color mill ground pigments for pottery painters.

→

Cruise the Sarthe in an electric boat. A unique perspective on the village and its riverbanks.

→

Paddle down the Sarthe by canoe-kayak from Malicorne. Locks punctuate the navigation route.

Join slip-casting pottery workshops at the museum. A professional leads these sessions for all ages.

The 18th-century Château de Sablé commands the river from its promontory. It replaced the former strategic medieval fortress.

The neo-Gothic Notre-Dame church preserves exceptional late 15th-century stained glass windows: the Crucifixion, the life of Christ, and Mary Magdalene.

The half-timbered medieval houses of the Saint-Nicolas quarter reveal intricate ironwork and decorative roof finials. The winding lanes captivate visitors.

The Marble Ecomuseum traces marble-working activity from the 17th to 20th centuries. Discover the quarries, tools, and iconic creations.

The cookie workshops perpetuate the tradition of golden, crumbly sablé biscuits. Tours available to savor the scent of fresh butter.

→

Walk along the banks of the Sarthe from the marina, the most vibrant in western France.

→

Take a river cruise departing from Sablé. The navigable Sarthe reveals its rolling landscapes.

→

Visit the weekly market to sample rillettes, goat cheeses, and homemade pastries. Authentic Sarthe flavors.

Founded in 1010, Saint-Pierre Abbey at Solesmes dominates the banks of the Sarthe. A sacred center of Gregorian chant since 1837.

Attending a sung office provides an unforgettable spiritual experience. The voices of the monks resonate throughout the chapel.

The "Saints of Solesmes," two groups of statues, are masterpieces of 15th and 16th-century French sculpture.

The village of Solesmes reveals itself on foot. Attentive strollers discover colorful sculptures by Claude and Marie-Madeleine Gruer adorning the facades.

From the bridge, enjoy the view of the abbey commanding the Sarthe. An exceptional panorama over the valley.

→

Continue the walk along the tranquil path beneath the abbey to Cri Cri Beach. A peaceful riverside escape.

→

Explore the Alpes Mancelles from Solesmes. Gentle hills, steep peaks, and a wild river compose this unique landscape.

The 12th-century Notre-Dame church boasts a remarkable carved oak door from 1528. The Tree of Jesse stands alongside the twelve apostles on this masterpiece.

Climb to the castle park to admire the panorama over the Sarthe and the river's meanders. The keep ruins dominate the site.

The 14th-century castle gate consists of two circular towers. This defensive element also served as a prison.

The market hall, now a performance venue, once hosted sales of the famous Fresnay hemp cloth.

The Coiffe Museum displays 180 19th-century Sarthe bonnets worn by local women. Meticulous restoration by traditional seamstresses.

Wander the narrow lanes lined with weavers' cottages. The local roussard stone gives the town its distinctive character.

→

Walk along the Rue du Bourgneuf beside the Sarthe. This cobbled street offers calm and tranquility along the water.

→

Hike through the Perche Sarthois and the Bonnétable Forest. Walking trails depart from Fresnay.

→

The twice-weekly markets at the foot of the church offer local produce and everyday goods.

The 11th-century Romanesque church commands the village from its rocky spur. Its 12th and 14th-century frescoes, restored in 2006, are masterpieces.

The church's stations of the cross, created by Christian Malézieux, features fourteen stations instead of the usual twelve to mark the year 2000.

Walking around the choir, discover the beehive carved into the stone. Bees have protected the church since the 9th century.

The 15th-century Chapel of Little Saint-Céneri rises in a vast meadow. Its chestnut framework forms an inverted boat hull.

Legend has it that young women who stick a pin in Saint Céneri's statue feet will find a husband within the year.

Cross the stone bridge for the iconic perspective of the perched church. A magnificent view over the Sarthe.

The Auberge des Soeurs Moisy, converted into a museum, evokes the Saint-Céneri School. 19th-century painters lodged here.

→

The miraculous fountain, born from Saint Céneri's prayer, supposedly heals certain eye diseases according to legend.

→

Follow the 'Saint-Céneri sur le Motif' educational trail leading to locations favored by painters of the Saint-Céneri School.

💡 Activity booklet available
→

Hike in the Alpes Mancelles from Saint-Céneri. The Haut-Fourché rocks, Toyères cliff, and natural amphitheater await.

Mamers

The 13th and 14th-century Saint-Nicolas church towers over the town. Its remarkable bell tower and Renaissance portal are emblematic.

The Grain Hall, built in 1818, still hosts weekly markets. Its stone columns and wooden framework provide an authentic setting.

The Italian-style theater, on the ground floor of the festival hall, forms with the market hall and church the Place Carnot 'triptych.'

The Flamboyant Gothic Notre-Dame church possesses a remarkable porch and rare triforium. Its stained glass illuminates the nave.

The Convent of the Visitation, founded in 1630, houses a former state prison in its chapel. Prisoners' graffiti remain visible.

Stroll through the 'coulées,' narrow alleyways typical of Mamers. The weavers' houses on Rue de Cinq-Ans testify to the hemp industry.

→

Visit Place de la République to see Mamers' Eiffel Tower, a 19th-century 1/100th scale replica.

→

Taste the authentic Sarthe rillettes. Mamers is the rillettes capital and headquarters of the Brotherhood of Sarthe Rillettes Knights.

→

Weekly markets (Monday, Friday, and Saturday) under the halls offer local produce and Perche specialties.

→

The Maine Saosnois Greenway, a former 12 km railway line, invites family-friendly bicycle or walking outings.

Rebuilt in the 15th century, the Château de Bonnétable was redesigned in the 19th century by architect Henri Parent for the Duke of Doudeauville.

The 11th and 19th-century Saint-Sulpice church houses a wooden organ from 1850 and magnificent stained glass windows by François Fialeix depicting the life of Saint Sulpice.

The vegetable garden, created by the Bühler brothers in 1885, remained functional for 80 years. Restored in 2002, it showcases heirloom vegetables and diverse plant species.

The castle's former stables now house the Tourist Office. Two tack rooms have been recreated and the stalls renovated.

The market hall, built in 1856, adorns the Place du Vieux Château. Its bell tower features two Wagner clocks.

→

Stroll through the castle park bordered by the Tripoulain. Water features, re-excavated moats, and shaded pathways await.

→

Explore the Bonnétable Forest. Hiking circuits on foot or by bicycle through this Perche woodland.

→

Ride the Transvap, a steam train on the former Mamers-Saint-Calais line. An 18 km railway journey between Bonnétable and Beillé.
