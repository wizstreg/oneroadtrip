This 21-day grand tour crosses Greece from north to south before looping back through the Ionian Islands. From Thessaloniki and its fifteen UNESCO monuments to the cliff-top monasteries of Meteora, from the Oracle at Delphi to the Acropolis of Athens, from the Mycenaean and Byzantine Peloponnese to the wild Mani, before catching the ferry to Corfu and island-hopping through the Ionians to Ithaca — the island of Odysseus. A single car rental covers most of the route; Athens is best explored on foot.

Key highlights (5)
  • Thessaloniki: a city with 15 UNESCO monuments, the Roman Arch of Galerius and the Byzantine upper town of Ano Poli
  • Meteora: 14th-century monasteries perched 400 m high on rock pillars — one of the most striking sights in Greece
  • The Acropolis of Athens and Epidaurus: two pinnacles of ancient architecture, separated by 2,500 years of history
  • Mystras, the Mani and Monemvasia: the medieval and untamed Peloponnese that few travellers ever discover
  • Corfu, Kefalonia and Ithaca: three Ionian islands with radically different personalities

The White Tower is the first thing you'll spot as you reach the waterfront — a cylindrical 15th-century fortification rebuilt under Suleiman the Magnificent. It initially served as a prison and site of mass Ottoman executions before being renamed the 'White Tower' when Greece reclaimed the city in 1912. Today it houses a museum dedicated to Thessaloniki's history, and from the top you get a sweeping view over the Thermaic Gulf — on a clear day, you can make out Mount Olympus.

The Basilica of Agios Dimitrios is the largest church in Greece. Built between 629 and 634 on the ruins of the Roman bathhouse where Saint Demetrius was imprisoned and executed under Diocletian, it stretches 53.6 metres long and has five naves. It was converted into a mosque from 1493 to 1912 — known then as the Kasimiye Camii. Don't miss the 7th-century mosaics on the pillars and the underground crypt, which occupies the site of the ancient Roman baths.

The Galerius complex brings together two UNESCO-listed Roman monuments. The Arch of Galerius, inaugurated in 303 to celebrate victory over the Sassanid Persians, stands right in the middle of Egnatia Street — its marble bas-reliefs are still visible. 125 metres to the northeast, the Rotunda — started in 306 as a mausoleum for Galerius — went on to serve as a temple, then a church, then a mosque. The city's last surviving minaret still stands next to it.

Head up to Ano Poli, the old upper town — one of the few neighbourhoods to survive the devastating fire of 1917. Its narrow alleyways lined with Ottoman wooden-balcony houses offer plunging views over the Thermaic Gulf. Make sure to see the Heptapyrgion, the seven-tower Byzantine fortress, and the church of Hosios David, whose 5th-century mosaics are UNESCO-listed.

→

In the late afternoon, stroll down to Aristotelous Square, the beating heart of the city — designed after the 1917 fire in a style blending Art Deco with classical references. Order a café frappé — Thessaloniki claims the invention of this iced drink in 1957 at the Dimitris Vakondios coffee house — and watch local life unfold around you.

💡 For dinner, head to Modiano Market or the Ladadika district — colourful converted warehouses, Macedonian cooking, lively atmosphere. Try the bougatsa, a cream-filled pastry that is a Thessaloniki speciality.

The Archaeological Museum of Thessaloniki covers Macedonian treasures from prehistory through late Antiquity. The hall dedicated to the royal tombs of Vergina is essential preparation before visiting the actual site tomorrow: you'll see reproductions of the gold crowns, weapons and ivories that were the pride of the Macedonian court. The collection of Macedonian coins and Hellenistic jewellery alone make the visit worthwhile.

The Museum of Byzantine Culture, in the same neighbourhood, is one of the most important in Greece for this period. Its collections span Early Christian and Byzantine art from the 4th to 15th century: icons, frescoes, sacred metalwork, detached mosaics. The chronological layout helps you trace the stylistic evolution of Byzantine art from its Roman origins through to its peak in Thessaloniki.

→

Spend the afternoon along the waterfront and the lively central neighbourhoods. The promenade running along the Thermaic Gulf from the White Tower to the port is where Thessaloniki does its evening passeggiata. In the evening, explore the streets around the ancient Roman Agora — excavations are open and visible from street level — and finish up at a tsipouradiko in Ladadika.

💡 Pick up your rental car the next morning — no need to have it for the two days in Thessaloniki. The city is best explored on foot.

75 km southwest of Thessaloniki, the site of Vergina holds the royal tombs of ancient Aigai, the first capital of the Kingdom of Macedon. Tomb II, discovered by archaeologist Manolis Andronikos in 1977, is attributed to Philip II, father of Alexander the Great — assassinated in 336 BC during his daughter's wedding. It had never been looted. The underground museum built directly beneath the burial tumulus displays the treasures found in situ: a 313-gram gold wreath, an iron cuirass adorned with gold, swords, carved ivories and the marble sarcophagus. It is one of the most moving museums in Greece.

→

After Vergina, drive south towards Kalambaka — allow about 2.5 hours. Late in the afternoon, settle in Kastraki rather than Kalambaka: this small village sits just 2 km from the monasteries, right at the foot of the rock pillars. The sunset from the viewpoints above the village paints the conglomerate columns in shades of orange and pink — one of the most photographed sights in Greece, and it's free.

💡 Book your accommodation in Kastraki rather than Kalambaka to be as close to the monasteries as possible for the next morning.

The Great Meteoron, the first monastery built here in 1382 by the monk Athanasios, is also the largest of the six still active today. These geological formations took shape during the Tertiary period: a river flowing into the ancient sea of Thessaly deposited pebbles cemented by sand over millions of years, creating conglomerate pillars that erosion then sculpted into their current shapes. Before going inside, look at the pulley systems still in place — until the early 20th century, monks were hoisted up in nets pulled by hand. Allow 30 minutes to climb the 200 steps carved into the rock.

Varlaam Monastery, founded in 1517, is often the favourite of returning visitors: more intimate than the Great Meteoron, it houses an impressive capstan mechanism with a storage net still visible. Its 16th-century frescoes in the main church — martyrdom scenes with strikingly precise anatomical detail — are among the finest in Meteora.

Roussanou, the convent perched on a 60-metre pillar and linked to the cliff by a footbridge, was restored and reoccupied by nuns in 1988. The interior is strikingly beautiful: 16th-century frescoes and a flowering garden suspended over the void. Holy Trinity (Agias Triados), reached by 140 steps through a tunnel, is the most dramatically positioned of all — this is where the final scene of the James Bond film 'For Your Eyes Only' was filmed in 1981.

→

Marked trails between the monasteries let you skip the tarmac roads and enjoy constantly shifting views of the rock pillars. The loop from Kastraki to the Great Meteoron and Varlaam on foot takes about 3 hours. Set out at dawn — mist sometimes clings to the rocks for an unforgettable spectacle, and tour groups don't arrive until after 9am.

💡 Strict dress code in all monasteries: shoulders and knees must be covered. Bring cash. The monasteries close on a rotating schedule — check the weekly timetable online before you go.

Delphi was 'the navel of the world' according to the ancient Greeks — Zeus released two eagles from opposite ends of the earth and they met right here. On the slopes of Mount Parnassus, between the two red cliffs of the Phaedriades, climb the Sacred Way that pilgrims once followed from across the Mediterranean. The Temple of Apollo (4th century BC) is now just a rectangle of columns, but the view from its foundations over the valley and the Gulf of Corinth is one of the finest in Greece. The Pythia delivered her oracles from an underground chamber while in a trance, likely induced by ethylene fumes rising from a geological fault — archaeologists have confirmed the presence of these emissions.

The Archaeological Museum of Delphi is unmissable. Its star piece is the Charioteer of Delphi — a bronze statue from 478 BC in extraordinarily good condition, with enamel eyes and bronze eyelashes. The Sphinx of Naxos (570 BC) and the friezes from the Treasury of the Siphnians (525 BC) show the sheer scale of donations made to the sanctuary. The marble omphalos — the 'navel of the world' — is also on display. Allow 1.5 hours.

Before entering the main sanctuary, make a detour to Marmaria below the road: the Sanctuary of Athena Pronaia is home to the Tholos of Delphi, a circular 4th-century BC temple whose three Doric columns have been re-erected. It is the most photographed image of Delphi — yet archaeologists still don't know what it was used for.

→

Arachova, 10 km from Delphi on the slopes of Mount Parnassus at 960 metres altitude, is your base for the night. Its grey stone houses with red tile roofs cling to the mountainside on several levels. Settle in on a terrace and try the formaela PDO cheese — a semi-hard goat's cheese whose production has been regulated since 1996 — with a glass of local wine.

💡 Buy the combined site + museum ticket at the entrance to Delphi. The full visit (site + museum + stadium) takes 3–4 hours. Arrive before 9am to beat the cruise-ship crowds.

Head up to the Acropolis early in the morning — a daily cap of 20,000 visitors has been in place since 2023, and tickets sell out fast in peak season. Book online the night before. The Parthenon, built between 447 and 432 BC on the orders of Pericles by architects Ictinus and Callicrates under the supervision of Phidias, measures 69.5 metres long by 30.88 metres wide — 22,000 tonnes of white marble from Mount Pentelicus. The statue of Athena it once housed, 12 metres tall and made of gold and ivory, vanished long ago. Wear non-slip shoes: the marble paving is polished and slippery.

On your way down, don't skip the Acropolis Museum, opened in 2009 at the foot of the hill. This is where you'll find the real Caryatids from the Erechtheion (the ones outside are copies), the original Parthenon friezes and the polychrome archaic pediments. The glass building is designed so you can see the Parthenon in the background throughout the visit. Allow at least 1.5 hours.

The Temple of Olympian Zeus, whose construction began in the 6th century BC and was only completed under Hadrian in 131 AD — more than six centuries of building work — retains just 15 of its original 104 Corinthian columns, each standing 17 metres tall. Right next to it, Hadrian's Arch marked the boundary between classical Athens and the Roman city.

→

At the end of the day, lose yourself in the Plaka district, tucked between the Acropolis and Syntagma Square. It is the oldest continuously inhabited area of Athens. Skip the restaurants on the main streets — hunt for the ones hidden down side alleys, where you'll pay half as much. Climb up to Anafiotika, the tiny Cycladic-style neighbourhood clinging to the rock face, built in the 19th century by workers from the island of Anafi.

💡 Drop off your rental car at the agency when you arrive in Athens — you won't need it for the next two days. Athens is best explored on foot or by metro.

The National Archaeological Museum is one of the world's most important collections of ancient art. Don't miss the Mask of Agamemnon — actually three centuries older than the Homeric king — found by Schliemann at Mycenae in 1876, the Santorini frescoes from Akrotiri, the Hellenistic bronzes including the Youth of Antikythera and the Jockey of Artemision, and the collection of Attic black-figure and red-figure vases. Allow at least 2 hours.

The Ancient Agora, below the Acropolis, is where Socrates debated and where Athens' earliest courts sat. The Temple of Hephaestus, built around 450 BC, is one of the best-preserved classical Greek temples in the world — its roof is still intact. The museum housed in the Stoa of Attalos displays ostraka, the clay shards on which Athenians voted to banish citizens who had grown too powerful.

→

Explore the Monastiraki market — one of the liveliest squares in Athens, a mix of flea market stalls, souvenir shops and bustling tavernas. In the evening, the souvlaki joints around Monastiraki stay open until 3am. For a panoramic view of the illuminated Acropolis, take the funicular up Lycabettus Hill from the Kolonaki neighbourhood — the Acropolis seen at night from up there is one of the great sights of Athens.

💡 Pick up a new rental car tomorrow morning from an Athens agency for the Peloponnese leg. Book in advance during peak season.

First stop on the road: the Corinth Canal. Dug between 1882 and 1893 (Nero had started the work in 67 AD before abandoning it), it connects the Gulf of Corinth to the Aegean Sea over 6.4 km. What really strikes you are the vertical walls: 79 metres of sheer limestone. The width is just 21 metres. Park on the road bridge and look down — the vertigo is guaranteed.

The Theatre of Epidaurus is often described as the most perfect in all of Antiquity. Built around 330 BC by the architect Polykleitos the Younger, it originally had 34 tiers seating 6,200, with 21 additional rows added in the Roman period to reach 12,000. Climb to the very last row and ask someone at the bottom to whisper — you'll hear them perfectly from 60 metres away. Researchers from the Georgia Institute of Technology found that the limestone seats filter out low-frequency background noise, allowing voices to carry without any amplification.

The Sanctuary of Asclepius, the ancient world's first major healing centre, has been a UNESCO site since 1988. Patients travelled from across Greece to practise incubation: they slept in the abaton (a 70-metre portico) hoping that Asclepius would appear to them in a dream. The complex included a gymnasium, baths, a stadium and a library — built on the idea that body and soul should be treated together.

→

End the day in Nafplio, the first capital of independent Greece in 1829. Its Venetian old town is one of the most beautiful in the Peloponnese: paved lanes, ochre and burgundy façades. If you still have energy, climb up to Palamidi Fortress — the stairs lead in 30 minutes to an outstanding panorama over the bay and the floating fort of Bourtzi. The golden light on the gulf in the late afternoon makes the effort worthwhile.

💡 In summer, the Epidaurus Festival stages ancient tragedies on Friday and Saturday evenings in the theatre — an experience like no other.

Tiryns is often overlooked in favour of Mycenae — unfairly so. This Mycenaean citadel from the 14th century BC has the best-preserved Cyclopean walls in the ancient world: up to 14 metres thick, with corbelled galleries carved right into the masonry. The ancient Greeks believed only the Cyclopes could have assembled such colossal blocks. UNESCO-listed and far less crowded than Mycenae.

Mycenae is the city of Agamemnon, the king who led the Greeks against Troy. As you enter through the Lion Gate, erected around 1250 BC, you pass beneath the oldest monumental relief in Europe. The Treasury of Atreus, 300 metres from the main gate, is a royal tholos tomb dating to 1250 BC: the circular chamber measures 14.50 metres across and 13.20 metres high. It was the largest dome in the Western world until the Pantheon in Rome was built five centuries later. The acoustics inside are eerie and mesmerising — clap your hands and listen.

→

Nafplio deserves a proper stroll. Cross Syntagma Square, framed by Venetian and Ottoman houses, pass the Vourliotis mosque — now a cinema — then walk along the harbour to the Bourtzi fort. Water taxis make the short crossing from the port. The tavernas along Nafplio's harbour are among the best in the region — go for the ones without laminated menus displayed at the entrance.

The Palace of the Despots, in the upper town of Mystras, is the most imposing palatial complex on the site. Its two recognisable wings — the Cantacuzene and the Palaeologue — bear witness to the power of the governors of the Morea. On the second floor of the Palaeologue wing stood the throne room, the Chrysotriklinos. It was in this palace that Constantine XI was crowned the last Emperor of Byzantium in 1449, two years before the fall of Constantinople. Founded in 1249 by William II of Villehardouin, Mystras passed to the Byzantines in 1262 and became the capital of the Despotate of the Morea. UNESCO-listed since 1989.

The Monastery of Peribleptos is literally clamped to the rock face. Inside, the 14th-century frescoes — the Christ Pantocrator in the dome, narrative scenes from the life of Christ — rank among the finest examples of late Byzantine art. The colours remain vivid after seven centuries.

The Monastery of Pantanassa is the only one in Mystras still inhabited: a small community of Orthodox nuns maintains the grounds. Its intricately carved stone façade and 15th-century frescoes already show the influence of the Italian Renaissance — Mystras and Florence were exchanging artists and intellectuals at the time. Modest dress required: shoulders and legs must be covered.

→

A visit to Mystras unfolds in three parts: the lower town (monasteries and the Metropolis), the upper town (Palace of the Despots) and the fortress at the top (360° panorama over the Laconian plain and Mount Taygetos). Allow at least 3 hours. In summer, start at opening time (8am) to avoid the heat on the exposed hillside. Bring plenty of water — there is no refreshment point on site. Modest dress required in the monasteries.

Gerolimenas

Vathia is the ghost village of the deep Mani: a collective fortress perched on a hilltop, made up of stone tower houses packed so tightly together they form a continuous rampart against rival clans. Vathia had as many as 200 inhabitants in the 19th century; today a few houses have been restored, but it's the abandoned section that is most haunting. The atmosphere is unique — beauty and melancholy intertwined, and not a tourist in sight outside July and August.

Cape Tenaro is the southernmost point of mainland Greece. From the car park, walk 45 minutes along a coastal trail running between two seas: the Ionian Sea to the west and the Aegean to the east merge here. The ancients regarded this cape as one of the gates to the Underworld. You'll find the ruins of a 4th-century BC Temple of Poseidon and a small chapel built on its foundations. The white lighthouse stands 41 metres above the sea.

→

The Diros Caves, 11 km from Areopoli on the road to Gerolimenas, are explored by rowboat along an underground river — one of the most extraordinary experiences in the Peloponnese. The boatman guides you through 1,200 metres of flooded galleries, past stalactites that almost graze the water's surface. The total mapped network stretches beyond 14,700 metres. Book ahead in summer — tickets are limited.

💡 Leave early for Cape Tenaro — the heat is stifling after 11am. Bring at least 2 litres of water.

Monemvasia makes an immediate impression the moment you catch sight of it: a 300-metre rock planted in the Aegean, connected to the mainland by a single 130-metre causeway. Its name — moni emvasia, 'single entrance' — says it all. Founded in 583 AD by Laconians fleeing Slavic invasions, it was held in turn by Byzantines, Franks, Venetians and Ottomans. In 1821, it was one of the first fortresses liberated during the Greek War of Independence.

Climb to the upper town on the paved path — allow 30 to 45 minutes. You'll pass the remains of an Ottoman hammam before reaching the church of Agia Sofia, built in the 12th century under Andronikos I Komnenos, right at the cliff's edge. The view from its forecourt over the Aegean and the Peloponnese is probably the finest panorama in the southern Peloponnese.

→

Get lost in the lower town's alleyways without a map — there's only one main shopping street (Mesi Odos) and a labyrinth of narrow lanes branching off it. Pick up some Malvasia wine from the small shops: the grape variety takes its name from Monemvasia — it was from this fortress's harbour that the Venetians exported this sweet wine across Europe during the Middle Ages. Legend has it that George Plantagenet, condemned to death in 1478, chose to die by drowning in a barrel of Malvasia — Shakespeare immortalised the scene in Richard III.

💡 Day trippers leave by about 5pm — after that hour, Monemvasia recovers a calm and authentic atmosphere that most journalists never get to experience.

The drive from Monemvasia to Olympia passes through Kalamata — allow about 4 hours. Make a stop at the Kalamata market if you're there on a weekday or Saturday morning: Kalamata PDO olives — purple, fleshy, with a fruity, slightly tangy flavour — and the olive oil from this region rank among the best in Greece. The area produces 45,000 tonnes of oil per year from over 40 million olive trees.

On the coastal road before Kalamata, the village of Kardamyli is worth a quick stop. Homer mentions it in the Iliad as one of seven cities offered by Agamemnon to Achilles. The writer Patrick Leigh Fermor settled here in the late 1960s and wrote 'Mani', the book that introduced this peninsula to the wider world. Its stone architecture and small beach make it one of the most appealing villages in the outer Mani.

→

Arrive in Olympia late in the afternoon. Settle in and have dinner at a taverna in the modern village — honest and practical after a long day on the road. Try the kokoretsi (lamb offal roasted on a spit) and a wine from Mantinia — the moschofilero, a white and fruity grape variety produced in the Arcadia region.

💡 Kalamata market: Wednesday and Saturday mornings. If you miss those days, the delicatessens in the town centre sell the same products.

The ancient site of Olympia has been inhabited since the 3rd millennium BC, and the Games were held here without interruption from 776 BC to 393 AD — a span of 1,169 years. The Temple of Zeus, built between 470 and 456 BC, once housed the chryselephantine statue of Zeus by Phidias: 12 to 13 metres tall, gold and ivory over a wooden frame, one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World. The Olympic flame is still lit here every four years in front of the Temple of Hera during the official ceremony.

The ancient stadium measures 212.54 metres long by 28.50 metres wide. At the entrance, you pass through the same vaulted tunnel that athletes used in Antiquity. The marble starting line is still visible. Run the length of the track — this is the stadion, the original Olympic event: 192 metres, one Greek stade.

The Archaeological Museum of Olympia is one of the finest in Greece. Its masterpieces: the Hermes of Praxiteles (c. 343 BC), one of the few original Greek sculptures to survive, and the Nike of Paionios (c. 420 BC) — a winged victory soaring through the air. The reassembled pediments of the Temple of Zeus depict the preparations for the chariot race and the battle of the Lapiths against the Centaurs. Allow at least 1.5 hours.

→

After the sites, take an evening walk along the banks of the Alpheios downstream from the site: the river flows between plane trees and reeds in a landscape free of cars and tourists. This is where ancient athletes came to purify themselves before the Games. If you arrive in the early afternoon, also explore the Museum of the History of the Ancient Olympic Games — more accessible and particularly well suited for children.

💡 Combined site + archaeological museum ticket available at the entrance. The full visit takes 4 to 5 hours — arrive early to avoid the afternoon heat.

The Old Town of Corfu, a UNESCO site since 2007, is a maze of paved alleyways called kantounia, lined with pastel-hued neoclassical façades inherited from centuries of Venetian rule. Venice held the island almost without interruption from 1386 to 1797. Enter through the Royal Gate and head for the Spianada — the largest esplanade in Greece — flanked on its western side by the arcades of the Liston, built during the French occupation and directly inspired by the Rue de Rivoli in Paris.

→

The Patras–Corfu crossing takes about 7 hours by fast ferry. Settle in on deck and enjoy the passage through the Gulf of Patras and out into the Ionian Sea. On arrival, head straight for the UNESCO-listed old town. For your first Corfiot dinner, order the pastitsada — rooster braised in a Venetian-spiced sauce — or the bourdeto, fish in a tomato sauce spiked with hot paprika.

💡 Ferry Patras → Corfu: book ahead in July–August. The crossing takes 7 hours — consider the night boat if you want to save a day.

The Old Fortress (Palaio Frourio) sits on a rocky promontory separated from the town by a sea moat that the Venetians dug to turn the citadel into an artificial island. It successfully repelled three major Ottoman sieges: 1537, 1571 and 1716. Climb up to the lighthouse at the summit for the best panoramic view over the sea and the town.

The Achilleion, 10 km to the south, is Empress Sisi's palace: built between 1889 and 1891 in a neoclassical Pompeian style, set on 200,000 m² of grounds overlooking the Ionian Sea. She had taught herself ancient Greek in order to read Homer in the original and wanted to pay tribute to Achilles. After her assassination in 1898, Kaiser Wilhelm II purchased the palace. In 1981, the first floor served as the casino set in the James Bond film 'For Your Eyes Only'.

Paleokastritsa, 25 km to the northwest, is one of the most beautiful natural spots in the Ionian Islands: six interlocking bays, turquoise waters and limestone cliffs rising 30 to 50 metres. The Monastery of Panagia Theotokos, founded in 1225 on a rocky promontory, offers one of the island's finest views from its gardens.

→

Book a boat trip from Paleokastritsa to explore the sea caves along the northwest coast. The light boats slip into limestone cavities where the light creates shimmering blue and green reflections — the local guides call one of them the 'Blue Light Cave'. Morning, when the sea is calm, is the best time.

💡 Rent a scooter or a small car to explore Corfu independently — public buses to Paleokastritsa are limited.

The ferry from Corfu to Igoumenitsa followed by the drive to Lefkada (about 3.5 hours total) brings you to the only Greek island linked to the mainland by a bridge. Start with a stop at Agia Mavra Castle, set by the channel — built in the 14th century by the Orsini counts, it gave the island its medieval name (Sainte-Maure).

Porto Katsiki — 'the goats' port' — is one of the most photographed beaches in the Mediterranean. A concave limestone cliff plunges sheer to a beach of white pebbles and sand, lapped by water of an almost unreal blue-turquoise. From the car park at the top of the cliffs, about a hundred steps cut into the rock lead down to the beach.

Cape Lefkatas, at the island's southwestern tip, rises 72 metres above the Ionian Sea. In Antiquity, those suffering from impossible love would leap from this promontory into the sea — surviving the jump was said to cure you of your passion. The poetess Sappho is said to have died here for love of Phaon, according to legend. A lighthouse built in 1913 now occupies the site of the ancient Temple of Apollo.

→

Vassiliki, your base for the night, is a world-renowned windsurfing destination — the reliable afternoon thermals funnelling through the bay attract competitors from around the globe. In the morning, the bay is a glassy mirror ideal for beginners. Dozens of windsurfing and kitesurfing schools cater to all levels.

💡 Get to Porto Katsiki before 10am in July–August — the beach fills up quickly and the steps become a queue. Bring water and food — there are no facilities at the bottom.

Melissani Cave — also called the 'Cave of the Nymphs' — is a limestone cenote whose ceiling collapsed during the 1953 earthquake, letting sunlight pour directly onto the underground lake. The lake is about 160 metres long and 30 to 40 metres deep. Time your visit between 11am and 1pm: that's when the sun strikes the opening head-on and turns the water into a kaleidoscope of blues and violets. The tour is by rowboat and lasts 10 to 15 minutes.

Drogarati Cave, 5 km from Sami, was discovered about 300 years ago when an earthquake caused its entrance to collapse. It measures 65 metres long, 45 metres wide and 20 metres high, with a constant temperature of 18°C. The main hall, known as the 'Hall of Apotheosis', has such remarkable acoustics that it has hosted concerts — including a performance by the Bavarian Philharmonic in 2014.

Antisamos Beach, 4 km from Sami, is one of the most beautiful on Kefalonia: a long stretch of white pebbles framed by pine forest running right down to the sea, with deep turquoise waters. Several scenes from the film 'Captain Corelli's Mandolin' were shot here in 2001. Bring your snorkelling gear — the seabed is rich.

→

The combined visit of both caves (Melissani + Drogarati) works best as a half-day outing: head to Drogarati first thing in the morning to beat the cruise-ship coaches, then reach Melissani around 11:30am for peak sunlight. Spend the afternoon swimming at Antisamos. In the evening, dine in Sami with riganada: dry bread rubbed with fresh tomato, olive oil, feta and dried oregano — simple and excellent.

💡 The ferry from Lefkada to Kefalonia departs from Nydri or Vassiliki. Check timetables depending on your departure point.

The ferry from Sami to Ithaca takes about 45 minutes. Vathy, the capital of Ithaca, is a small town of 1,700 inhabitants tucked at the end of a horseshoe-shaped gulf — one of the best-sheltered natural anchorages in the Ionian Sea. The archaeological museum houses a ceramic fragment inscribed with the name 'Odysseus', found during excavations. Stroll around the port and have lunch at a waterfront taverna.

The hill of Aetos, 4 km from Vathy, rises to 380 metres. A steep trail climbs in 20 minutes to the ruins of the ancient city of Alalkomenes — this is where Schliemann excavated in 1868 and 1878, convinced he had found the palace of Odysseus. Modern archaeologists are more cautious: the fortifications date to the 7th century BC. But the view from the top alone, over both coasts of the island and Kefalonia across the water, is worth the effort.

→

Catch the afternoon ferry from Ithaca to Patras (via Kefalonia/Sami) — the full crossing takes about 3 hours. In Patras, return the rental car. The city is lively in the evening along its waterfront and around the Byzantine kastro. Have dinner before turning in early — tomorrow it's a bus or train to Athens.

💡 Check the Ithaca → Patras ferry timetable (via Sami) in advance — direct services are rare, often just 1 to 2 departures per day depending on the season. Return the car in Patras before 8pm.

Stop on the old sliding bridge in the heart of town: you're standing at the narrowest point of the Euripus Strait, where just 38 metres separate the island from the mainland. Stay a few minutes and watch the water: the current reverses direction roughly every six hours, reaching 12 km/h, with eight minutes of dead calm between each change. This phenomenon, driven by the moon, has fascinated scientists since Antiquity — Aristotle is said to have come here to study what seemed to him to defy the laws of nature.

Karababa Castle, built by the Ottomans in 1686, dominates the town from the hill opposite the bridge. It offers a striking panorama over the strait, the bridge and the rooftops below. The climb from the centre takes about 20 minutes on foot. It's the best vantage point for photographing the current phenomenon from above.

→

Settle in on a terrace facing the old bridge to watch the current reverse — the locals do the same; it's a free and fascinating show. On 'disorder' days (6 to 7 days per lunar month), the current can change direction up to 14 times in a single day. If you happen to be there on one of those days, you'll understand why the ancients spoke of the 'mad waters' of Chalcis. Dine at one of the waterfront tavernas — a quiet night before the airport.

💡 Getting here: bus or train from Patras to Athens (2h15), then train from Athens to Chalcis (1h). Chalcis is 80 km from Athens airport — allow 1h15 by taxi or rental car.
