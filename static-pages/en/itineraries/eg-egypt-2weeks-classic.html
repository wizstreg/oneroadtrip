Egypt Journey: Nile Cruise and Red Sea Beaches

Nile cruise from Luxor to Aswan, Giza pyramids, Grand Egyptian Museum, Abu Simbel and 4 days snorkeling on Hurghada's coral reefs: the grand classic Egypt in 14 days.

This 14-day circuit combines the best of ancient Egypt and the Red Sea. From the Grand Egyptian Museum to the Giza pyramids, from a Nile cruise between Luxor and Aswan (with stops at Edfu and Kom Ombo) to the Abu Simbel colossi, then 4 days of diving and snorkeling in Hurghada on the planet's richest coral reefs. The cruise brings a different rhythm—you fall asleep facing the Nile and wake up before a temple.

Key highlights (6)
  • Grand Egyptian Museum: 100,000 objects including Tutankhamun's complete collection, opened in 2025
  • Nile cruise Luxor-Aswan: 2 nights on the river with stops at Edfu and Kom Ombo temples
  • Karnak: 134 columns rising 23 meters in antiquity's largest hypostyle hall
  • Abu Simbel: four 20-meter colossi, solar alignment at equinoxes for 3,200 years
  • Giftun Island: marine national park, coral reefs and 800 fish species in the Red Sea
  • Valley of the Kings: 63 tombs carved in rock, wall paintings 3,000 years old

Arrive at Cairo International Airport (CAI). Transfer to your downtown or Giza hotel—expect 45 minutes to 1.5 hours depending on traffic, which is legendarily chaotic in Cairo. Take time to settle in and acclimatize to the heat and noise of the city.

If your flight lands early afternoon, head up the Cairo Tower on Gezira Island—187 meters tall, built in 1961, offering the best 360° panorama over the city. On clear days you'll spot the Giza pyramids on the horizon. The Zamalek neighborhood on the same island is Cairo's most pleasant district for a first stroll—colonial villas, art galleries, quiet Nile-side cafés. It's a good decompression chamber before diving into Cairo's chaos.

→

First Egyptian dinner in Zamalek or along the Nile corniche. Egyptian street food is worth trying from day one: koshari (lentils, rice, pasta, spicy tomato sauce—the national dish), ful medames (stewed fava beans), ta'ameya (Egyptian falafel made with fava beans). The corniche restaurants offer views of the illuminated Nile—a proper introduction.

Cairo

Begin at the Citadel of Saladin, perched 75 meters above the city on the Mokattam hills. Built from 1176 by Saladin to defend Cairo against the Crusaders, it served as Egypt's seat of power for nearly 700 years. Inside, push through the doors of the Mohammed Ali Mosque—nicknamed 'the Alabaster Mosque' for its interior alabaster cladding rising 11 meters. Its central dome reaches 52 meters and its Ottoman silhouette dominates Cairo's skyline. From the terrace, views sweep across all of Old Cairo, the minarets, and on clear days the Giza pyramids on the horizon. Allow 1.5 hours here.

Descend to the Sultan Hassan Mosque below the Citadel—one of the masterpieces of Mamluk architecture. Built between 1356 and 1363 under Sultan An-Nasir Hassan, it measures 150 meters long and its southern minaret reaches 68 meters, the tallest in medieval Cairo. The interior strikes with its proportions: the central courtyard opens onto four iwans (vaulted halls), each dedicated to one of the four schools of Sunni jurisprudence. Opposite, the Al-Rifai Mosque (1912) forms a striking contrast—same Mamluk style but built 550 years later. It houses the tombs of Iran's last shah and several members of the Egyptian royal family.

Al-Azhar Mosque, founded in 970 by the Fatimids, is Cairo's oldest mosque and home to the second-oldest continuously operating university in the world. Its 12,000 square meters blend Fatimid, Mamluk and Ottoman architectural layers—spot the three minarets, built respectively in 1340, 1469 and 1510, each in a different style. The white marble courtyard dates from the original construction. If you're here late morning, you might see students studying Quranic interpretation under the arcades, as they have for over a thousand years.

→

Dive into the Khan el-Khalili souk, founded in 1382 by Emir Djaharks al-Khalili on the former cemetery of the Fatimid caliphs. Over 900 shops pack a labyrinth of covered alleys—goldsmiths, spice merchants, hammered copper, perfumes. Walk up Al-Muizz Street, one of Cairo's oldest (10th century), lined with 29 UNESCO-listed Islamic monuments. Don't miss mint tea at El-Fishawy café, open continuously since 1773. The souk is at its best late afternoon when the heat drops and the alleys come alive.

💡 Haggle systematically—the initial price is often 3 to 5 times the real price. Favor side alleys over main thoroughfares to find genuine Egyptian artisans.
→

Dinner in the Old Cairo district—Egyptian street food is a must: koshari (lentils, rice, pasta and spicy tomato sauce), ful medames (stewed fava beans), ta'ameya (Egyptian falafel made with fava beans rather than chickpeas). The eateries around Khan el-Khalili serve generous portions. If you prefer a more relaxed experience, several terrace restaurants overlook Al-Hussein Square.

The Grand Egyptian Museum (GEM), opened November 1, 2025, is the world's largest archaeological museum dedicated to a single civilization—500,000 square meters total, 100,000 objects displayed across 12 permanent galleries covering seven millennia of history. It sits 2 km from the pyramids, on the Giza plateau. Right at the entrance, the colossal statue of Ramses II (11 meters, 83 tons, 3,200 years old) greets you in the atrium. The Grand Staircase, spanning six levels and 6,000 square meters, is lined with some sixty monumental statues with direct pyramid views through the glass facade. The complete Tutankhamun collection—5,398 pieces, many displayed for the first time—occupies 7,500 square meters alone. Allow at minimum 4 hours, ideally 6 hours for a complete visit. Tickets are no longer sold on-site—book online.

After the museum, head to the Giza plateau 2 km away—a pedestrian bridge now connects the two sites. The Great Pyramid of Khufu, sole survivor of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World, has stood for over 4,500 years. The numbers are staggering: 230 meters per side, 146 meters tall originally (138 today), 2.3 million limestone blocks averaging 2.5 tons each, for a total weight of 5 million tons. It was the world's tallest building for 3,800 years. If you go inside (extra fee), expect a narrow, sloping corridor leading to the king's chamber—10.5 by 5.2 meters in solid granite. It's physical and claustrophobic, but the experience is worth it. Arrive at opening (8am) to avoid crowds and heat.

The Sphinx, carved directly from a rocky outcrop of the plateau, measures 73.5 meters long and 20 meters high—the largest sculpted monolith of antiquity. Its face, probably that of Pharaoh Khafre (Fourth Dynasty, circa 2500 BC), gazes due east toward the sunrise. Don't miss the Panoramic Point (Nine Pyramids viewpoint) on the road circling the plateau to the south—this is where you'll get all three great pyramids and the three small queens' pyramids aligned in the same field of view.

→

Sunset over the pyramids from the Panoramic Point or from one of the terrace restaurants on the Fayoum road that border the plateau. The golden light on the pyramids in late afternoon is the best photographic moment—tourist buses have left and the heat is bearable. Some organize camel or horseback rides around the plateau in late afternoon.

💡 Bring lots of water, a hat and sunscreen. There's virtually no shade on the plateau. In summer, temperatures regularly exceed 40°C.

Early departure to Saqqara, 30 km south of Cairo—the necropolis of ancient capital Memphis. The Step Pyramid of Djoser, built around 2660 BC by vizier Imhotep, is Egypt's oldest pyramid and humanity's first monumental stone structure. Six stacked mastabas of decreasing size form its 62 meters of height. The entrance through the colonnade—40 carved papyrus-shaped pillars—is striking in its modernity. Beneath the pyramid, a 5 km network of underground corridors descends 28 meters deep to the burial chamber. It's possible to visit part of the underground network for an extra fee. The site is far less crowded than Giza—take advantage.

Continue to Dahshur, 15 km south of Saqqara—an even quieter site. Here you'll see the evolution of construction techniques: the Bent Pyramid (105 meters, circa 2600 BC), recognizable by its mid-height slope change—Sneferu's architects had to reduce the angle mid-construction to prevent collapse. Right next door, the Red Pyramid (104 meters), the first successful smooth-sided pyramid in history. It served as the direct model for Khufu's Great Pyramid. You can enter the Red Pyramid through a 63-meter corridor—the ammonia smell is strong, but the three interior chambers in red stone are worth the visit.

Stop by the Imhotep Museum at Saqqara before leaving—small but remarkable. The blue faience tiles found inside Djoser's pyramid, Merenre I's mummy, and Imhotep's wooden coffin are on display. It's a good complement to understanding the necropolis.

→

Cairo → Luxor flight in late afternoon (1-hour flight, several airlines offer daily departures). Upon arrival, settle on the east bank of the Nile. If your flight arrives before sunset, head straight to Luxor Temple—it's magnificent at night, illuminated. The pylon of Ramses II, his two seated colossi of 15 meters, and the remaining obelisk (the second has been in Paris's Place de la Concorde since 1836) form the entrance. Amenhotep III's processional colonnade, 20 meters high, gives chills under the spotlights. The avenue of human-headed sphinxes, restored and reopened in 2021 after decades of work, connects Luxor Temple to Karnak over 2.7 km—you can walk along it in the cool evening air.

💡 Book the Cairo-Luxor flight in advance. Luxor Temple is open until 9pm in winter—the night visit is more beautiful and pleasant than by day.

Karnak is the largest religious complex ever built—over 2 square kilometers of temples, pylons, obelisks and sanctuaries erected over 2,000 years by dozens of pharaohs, from Senusret I (circa 2000 BC) to the Ptolemies. Arrive at opening (6am) to have the Great Hypostyle Hall almost to yourself. This forest of 134 columns, built by Seti I and completed by Ramses II, measures 103 meters long by 52 meters wide—it could contain Notre-Dame de Paris. The 12 central columns rise to 23 meters with capitals 3.5 meters in diameter. In the raking morning light, the reliefs carved in stone take on striking depth. Allow at minimum 2.5 hours for Karnak, 3 hours if you want to explore the corners.

Don't leave Karnak without circling the sacred lake (120 × 77 meters), where priests purified themselves before ceremonies. The obelisk of Hatshepsut, the tallest still standing in Egypt (29 meters, carved from a single block of Aswan pink granite), rises between the 4th and 5th pylons. At the end of the main axis, the sanctuary of Amun's barque—the temple's spiritual heart—preserves remarkably well-preserved wall paintings. At the entrance, 40 ram-headed sphinxes (criosphinxes) line the processional avenue that once led to the Nile.

The Luxor Museum, on the Nile corniche between Karnak and Luxor temples, is a small, remarkably designed museum—far more pleasant than Cairo's old museum. Among the major pieces: two royal mummies (Ahmose I and Ramses I), a colossal granite head of Amenhotep III, and the talatat wall—283 painted sandstone blocks from Akhenaten's Temple of Aten at Karnak, demolished by his successors. Allow 1 to 1.5 hours.

→

Late afternoon stroll along the Nile corniche between Luxor Temple and downtown. Luxor's east bank comes alive at sunset—feluccas glide on the river, restaurant terraces light up. If you haven't yet seen Karnak by night, the sound and light show (nightly, multiple languages) takes you through the illuminated temple—it's kitschy but the atmosphere in the hypostyle hall under the spotlights is worth it.

💡 Karnak at 6am for the light and absence of crowds. Luxor Temple in the evening. Don't do both by day—the heat is crushing between 11am and 4pm.

Wake at 4:30am for a hot air balloon flight over the west bank—one of Egypt's most spectacular experiences. The balloon lifts off at sunrise and soars over the Valley of the Kings, Hatshepsut's temple and the sugarcane fields along the Nile for 45 minutes to 1 hour. Reservations are made the day before—flights are cancelled in high winds.

The Valley of the Kings holds 63 tombs carved into the rock between 1539 and 1075 BC—New Kingdom pharaohs had abandoned pyramids in favor of underground tombs to escape looters. Your ticket includes 3 tombs of your choice. The most impressive for their paintings: Ramses IV (KV2), Ramses IX (KV6), Merenptah (KV8). Seti I's tomb (KV17), considered the valley's most beautiful, requires a substantial supplement. Tutankhamun's tomb (KV62), discovered in 1922 by Howard Carter, is small and its paintings less spectacular than others—the 5,398 objects of the treasure are now at the GEM in Giza. Arrive before 7am—there's no shade in the valley and the heat quickly becomes unbearable.

The Colossi of Memnon, two seated statues of Amenhotep III standing 18 meters tall each, guard the entrance to what was Luxor's largest mortuary temple—now vanished. Carved from 720-ton quartzite blocks each, transported from the Gebel el-Ahmar quarries near Cairo 675 km away. In antiquity, the northern colossus emitted a sound at sunrise (probably due to expansion of stone cracked by an earthquake in 27 BC)—Greeks and Romans came from afar to hear it. The stop is quick—15 to 20 minutes suffice.

→

Free afternoon to recover from the 4:30am wake-up. If you still have energy, detour to Deir el-Medina, the village of artisans who built and decorated the Valley of the Kings tombs for 500 years. The small workers' tombs—notably those of Sennedjem and Inherkhau—are paradoxically better painted than some royal tombs, with colors of astonishing freshness. The site is little visited and gives insight into the daily life of the royal tomb builders.

💡 Start the west bank as early as possible. After 11am the heat in the Valley of the Kings is barely tolerable. Bring lots of water.

Hatshepsut's mortuary temple at Deir el-Bahari is backed against a 300-meter cliff that serves as its natural backdrop—the architectural spectacle is unique in Egypt. Three monumental terraces connected by ramps rise against the rock face. The first measures 120 meters deep by 75 wide. The temple, built in 15 years by architect Senenmut, tells on its walls the trade expedition to the land of Punt (present-day Somalia/Eritrea)—you can see the frankincense trees transported by boat—and the divine birth of the queen. Hatshepsut, the only woman to have reigned as pharaoh for 22 years, saw most of her representations hammered out by her successor Thutmose III after her death in 1458 BC. Arrive early—the temple is in full sun from 9am.

Continue to Medinet Habu—Ramses III's mortuary temple, less crowded than Karnak but whose painted reliefs are the best preserved on the entire west bank. The battle scenes against the Sea Peoples (circa 1178 BC) still retain their original colors—blue, red, yellow. The ceiling of the second portico preserves traces of vivid pigments after 3,000 years. The outer enclosure, inspired by a Syrian fortress (the Migdol), is unique in Egyptian temple architecture. If you only see one 'off-the-beaten-path' temple in Luxor, this is the one.

The Ramesseum, Ramses II's mortuary temple, is worth a stop for its monumental remains—notably a broken colossus of Ramses II that weighed over 1,000 tons and measured about 18 meters standing. This is the colossus that inspired Shelley's poem Ozymandias in 1818. The hypostyle hall still retains part of its ceiling with astronomical paintings. The site is less restored than Karnak, giving it a romantic ruin charm.

→

If your budget allows, visit the Valley of the Queens and Nefertari's tomb (QV66)—considered the most beautiful tomb in all Egypt for the vividness of its wall paintings. Every inch of wall is painted with exceptional finesse—Nefertari guided by the gods, scenes from the Book of the Dead, star-studded ceiling in deep blue. The supplement is steep and visitor numbers limited, but it's a moment apart. The Valley of the Queens holds about a hundred tombs, several open with the standard ticket.

💡 Nefertari's tomb (QV66) is the most expensive in Luxor but unanimously considered the most beautiful. Visitor numbers limited—book early.
→

Late afternoon boarding on the cruise boat moored at Luxor. Cabin installation and first dinner on board facing the sunset over the Nile. The Luxor-Aswan cruise lasts 2 nights and 3 days, with stops at Edfu and Kom Ombo. Navigation happens mainly at night—you fall asleep at one stop and wake at the next.

💡 The cruise is the classic mode of transport between Luxor and Aswan. Boats generally navigate at night and stop at temples in the morning.

8
Day

Edfu

+6

Wake on the Nile—the boat navigated overnight from Luxor and passes through the Esna lock in early morning (sometimes with a 1-2 hour wait depending on river traffic). Arrival at Edfu mid-morning. The Temple of Edfu, dedicated to the falcon god Horus, is the best-preserved Ptolemaic temple in Egypt—and the second largest after Karnak. Built between 237 and 57 BC, it took 180 years to complete. Its entrance pylon rises 36 meters high with reliefs showing Ptolemy XII smiting his enemies before Horus. Inside, the hypostyle hall with 18 columns is intact, with its ceiling still in place—extremely rare in Egypt. The gray granite naos of the sanctuary, which housed Horus's statue, remains in place. Allow 1 to 1.5 hours for the visit.

→

Afternoon navigation on the Nile between Edfu and Kom Ombo. The cruise passes through a landscape of palm groves, sugarcane fields and Nubian villages on both banks. This is the moment to enjoy the boat's upper deck—tea, reading, birdwatching (herons, egrets, kingfishers). The late afternoon light on the Nile is extraordinary for photography.

Morning stop at Kom Ombo—the temple sits spectacularly on a promontory overlooking the Nile, meters from the boat dock. Its particularity is unique in Egypt: it's a double temple, dedicated simultaneously to two deities—Sobek (the crocodile god) on the south side and Haroeris (Horus the Elder) on the north. Everything is symmetrical: two entrances, two sanctuaries, two series of reliefs. On the outer wall, don't miss the panel showing surgical instruments—one of the rare representations of ancient medicine in Egypt. The small adjacent crocodile museum displays Nile crocodile mummies found in the area. Allow 45 minutes to 1 hour.

→

Navigation to Aswan, arriving early afternoon. Disembark and check into your hotel. The city has a slower rhythm and more Nubian atmosphere than Luxor—the border with historical Nubia passes here. Stroll through Aswan's souk, Egypt's most colorful: spices in pyramids (cumin, saffron, henna), woven Nubian baskets, fabrics and perfumes. Vendors are less aggressive than in Cairo.

💡 The souk is at its best late afternoon. Bring back henna, spices and karkadé (hibiscus)—these are the best souvenirs from Aswan.
→

First felucca ride on the Nile at sunset—Aswan is where this experience is most beautiful. The river is dotted with granite islands, golden light falls on the sand dunes of the west bank, and the white sails of feluccas glide silently. Allow 1 to 1.5 hours. Negotiate the price before boarding—feluccas depart from the south corniche.

Depart at 3am for Abu Simbel—280 km of desert road (3 to 3.5 hours), in an organized convoy. Arrive at sunrise at the temples, when the raking light sets the four colossi of Ramses II ablaze. The great temple, carved directly from the cliff around 1265 BC, strikes with its dimensions: the facade measures 33 meters high and 38 wide. The four seated statues of Ramses II reach 20 meters each. Inside, a hypostyle hall of eight Osiride pillars leads to the deepest sanctuary, 63 meters into the rock. Twice a year, at the equinoxes (February 22 and October 22), the sun's rays penetrate to the back of the sanctuary to illuminate three of the four statues—an astronomical alignment calculated 3,200 years ago.

Right next door, the small temple of Nefertari—one of the rare Egyptian temples dedicated to a queen—is consecrated to Nefertari and the goddess Hathor. Six 10-meter statues adorn the facade. Inside, the wall paintings showing Nefertari crowned by the goddesses are among the best preserved in Egypt. In 1964-1968, both temples were cut into 1,042 blocks and reassembled 65 meters higher to save them from Lake Nasser's rising waters—a UNESCO operation financed by 50 countries.

Return to Aswan around 1-2pm. Afternoon devoted to Philae and Aswan sites. The Temple of Philae, dedicated to the goddess Isis, stands on Agilkia Island, accessible only by water taxi (10-15 minutes from Shellal). Built between 380 BC and the 3rd century, it too was dismantled and reassembled on a neighboring island between 1972 and 1980 to escape flooding. Trajan's Kiosk, with its 14 columns each with different capitals, is Aswan's most photographed structure.

The Unfinished Obelisk, in the pink granite quarries, would have measured 42 meters and weighed 1,200 tons if completed—a crack in the rock forced the stone cutters to abandon it around 1450 BC. The site shows ancient extraction techniques: tool marks, chisel trenches, traces of wooden wedges. The Aswan High Dam (3,600 m long, 111 m high), built with Soviet assistance between 1960 and 1970, created Lake Nasser—500 km long. Both can be visited quickly (30 min each).

→

Felucca crossing to Elephantine Island to discover the Nubian village with brightly painted houses and the ancient Nilometer that measured the Nile's flood. If time permits, push on to the botanical garden of Kitchener Island, created in the 19th century—pleasant for a shady break before evening.

💡 Very packed day (3am wake-up). Abu Simbel morning, Philae + obelisk + dam afternoon. It's doable but intense—plan a quiet evening.

11
Day

Hurghada

Flight Aswan → Hurghada (1 hour) or road transfer (4-5 hours via the Eastern Desert). Arrive at the Red Sea coast—the contrast with the Nile Valley is radical: here it's turquoise blue as far as the eye can see, coral reefs and desert plunging into the sea. Check into your hotel and take your first swim. Red Sea water stays 24-28°C year-round, with visibility often exceeding 30 meters.

→

Decompression day after 10 days of intensive sightseeing. Enjoy your hotel beach or head down to Hurghada marina for a walk—restaurants, cafés and shops line the port. If you still have energy, take a tour of the old El Dahar quarter to discover authentic Hurghada—local souk, mosques, neighborhood life far from the resorts.

→

First snorkeling from the beach—most Hurghada hotels have a reef accessible directly from shore or from a jetty. The Red Sea harbors over 800 fish species and 200 coral species. Even from the beach, you'll see butterflyfish, clownfish, surgeonfish and probably a moray eel or two.

💡 Biodegradable sunscreen mandatory—regular creams destroy corals. UV-protection shirt recommended to avoid sunburn while snorkeling.
12
Day

Hurghada

Boat excursion to the Giftun Islands—a marine national park 45 minutes by boat from Hurghada. The two islands (Giftun Kebir and Giftun Soraya) are surrounded by some of the northern Red Sea's most beautiful coral reefs. The water is turquoise and crystal clear, the white sand beaches nearly deserted. Snorkeling here is exceptional: parrotfish, napoleons, butterflyfish, stingrays, and with luck green sea turtles. Multiple snorkeling stops are planned at different reefs throughout the day.

→

Full day at sea with lunch on board. Excursions typically include 2 to 3 snorkeling stops at different reefs—gardens of hard and soft corals, drop-offs, shallow lagoons. The natural lagoon of Giftun Soraya, with waters 0.5-1.5 meters deep, is perfect for beginners and families. The reefs on the south face offer more spectacular drop-offs for experienced snorkelers. Return to Hurghada late afternoon.

💡 Book the excursion the day before through your hotel or a local operator. Prefer small boats (max 20 people). Bring your own mask if you have one.
13
Day

Hurghada

Last day in Hurghada—several options depending on your interests. For underwater enthusiasts: a discover scuba diving experience (no experience required, full instruction, 1.5 hours total) lets you descend to 6-10 meters on a reef—an incomparable experience to surface snorkeling. The reefs around Giftun and Abu Ramada are perfect for a first dive, with weak currents and exceptional visibility.

→

Alternative: excursion to Dolphin Reef (Sha'ab el-Erg), about 1.5 hours by boat north of Hurghada. A pod of spinner dolphins permanently resides in the area—chances of sighting are high, especially in the morning. Swimming in open water near the dolphins (without touching or feeding them), then snorkeling on surrounding reefs.

💡 The dolphins are wild—no sighting guarantee, but probabilities are very good (80%+ according to operators). Departure around 7am, return around 2pm.
→

Late afternoon relaxation at the beach or hotel pool. Last sunset over the Red Sea from the marina. Hurghada is also known for its Eastern Desert excursions (quad, 4x4 safari, Bedouin dinner)—an option if you want to mix things up for this last evening.

14
Day

Hurghada

Last morning in Hurghada. If your flight is late in the day, enjoy a final swim or early morning snorkel on the hotel reef. Hurghada International Airport (HRG) is 10-15 minutes from downtown—far simpler than Cairo. Direct flights to Paris, Lyon, Brussels, Geneva and most European capitals, as well as connections via Cairo.

→

Transfer to Hurghada Airport (HRG) for your return flight. The airport is small and quick—1.5 hours before flight is enough for international flights. If you're returning via Cairo, allow a comfortable connection (3 hours minimum) to absorb any potential delays.

💡 Hurghada has direct flights to Europe—no need to go back through Cairo. Check direct flight options before booking.

❓ Frequently asked questions

What weather should you expect?

Winter (November-March): Perfect weather, comfortable temperatures, ideal for sightseeing, peak tourist season, higher prices.

Spring/Fall (April-May, September-October): Good weather, moderate crowds, reasonable prices, still hot but manageable.

Summer (June-August): Extremely hot, challenging conditions, lowest prices, Red Sea diving still excellent, indoor attractions preferred.

Red Sea Season: Year-round excellent diving, summer best underwater visibility, winter more comfortable surface conditions.

How many days should I plan?

We believe you will enjoy this itinerary best by dedicating between 11 and 17 days. The next step will allow you to adjust the duration of your stay.

What to discover in Egypt?

Ancient civilization with pyramids, Nile River cruises, and incredible archaeological treasures. Experience pharaonic heritage, modern Cairo, and Red Sea diving. The country offers world-famous historical sites with warm hospitality and cultural depth. Egypt provides life-changing encounters with human civilization's greatest achievements and ongoing archaeological discoveries.
