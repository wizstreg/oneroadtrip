Yenne is a medieval town built between the Rhône and the Dent du Chat peak. A marked trail with 29 points of interest winds through the historic center: the Chambéry Gate, Novalaise Gate, salt granary, and noble houses from the 15th-18th centuries reveal layers of history in weathered stone.

Step inside Yenne's church, a listed Historic Monument. Its baroque furnishings blend with older elements in a hushed atmosphere that invites contemplation.

Follow the Balcon de Yenne trail—part of the Santiago de Compostela pilgrimage route—up to the Notre-Dame de la Montagne viewpoint. The vista plunges into the Pierre-Châtel gorge, a protected natural site where the Rhône squeezes between cliffs at the border of Ain and Savoie.

The Pierre-Châtel gorge shelters ruins of a 13th-century charterhouse that became a military fortress in the 1800s. From the trail's second viewpoint, you can clearly make out the structure nestled in the canyon below.

Head to the Jongieux-Marestel hillsides, a vineyard classified as national heritage for its scenic beauty. Terraced vines overlook the Rhône. The slopes produce Roussette de Savoie (Altesse grape) and Mondeuse, a bold red wine.

→

Balcon de Yenne hike: the trail climbs through boxwood forest to three successive viewpoints over the Rhône and Pierre-Châtel gorge. Return via the hamlets of Chevru and Curtelod.

→

Wine tasting in the Jongieux cellars. Marestel vintages and Mondeuse reds are sampled at local producers' estates, set between manicured slopes and Lake Bourget panoramas.

💡 Several estates offer cellar visits by appointment
→

Canoe-kayak on the wild upper Rhône at the foot of the Pierre-Châtel gorge. Whitewater threads through a landscape of cliffs and forests, ranked among the region's most acclaimed paddling routes.

💡 Intermediate level required on certain sections

The Thermes Nationaux of Aix-les-Bains, built in 1783 over Roman bath ruins, closed in 2008 and became a historic monument. Enter freely: glass elevators offer a plunging view into the 1st-century AD thermal remains.

The Arc de Campanus, a 1st-century monumental arch, rises opposite the baths. Erected by a Roman notable to honor his dead, it recalls Aix's importance since antiquity. The Temple of Diana, integrated into the town hall, completes this Gallo-Roman heritage.

The Musée Faure occupies Villa des Chimères, a Genoese-style mansion opened in 1949. It holds France's second-largest Rodin sculpture collection, plus canvases by Cézanne, Degas, Pissarro, and Sisley.

The Grand Cercle Casino, inaugurated in the mid-19th century, merits a visit for its Belle Époque decoration: mosaic ceilings, stained glass, and gilded frescoes evoke the spa's golden age.

Cross the lake by boat to Hautecombe Abbey. Founded in the 12th century by Cistercian monks, this necropolis of the Princes of Savoy was restored in the 19th century in flamboyant neo-Gothic style. Frescoes, marble sculptures, and princely tombs form a striking ensemble.

Stroll the Esplanade du Lac, an 800-meter promenade between the small and large ports. Plane trees shade the view across Lake Bourget—France's largest natural lake—and the Dent du Chat peak opposite.

→

Narrated cruise on Lake Bourget. Departures from Aix's Grand Port, with a possible stop at Hautecombe Abbey. Panoramas of the Alps and the Épine range unfold over one to two hours.

→

Hike to Mont Revard viewpoint (1,538 m). From the summit, the vista sweeps over Lake Bourget, Aix-les-Bains, Mont Blanc, and the Bauges range. Picnic tables dot the trail.

→

Swimming or paddle-boarding at Lake Bourget. Several supervised beaches (Rowing, Grand Port, Mémard) let you enjoy waters that reach 26°C in summer.

💡 Rowing Beach is the most accessible from downtown

The Fontaine des Éléphants, voted France's most beautiful fountain in 2023, towers 17 meters high. Four cast-iron elephants arranged in a Savoy cross support the statue of Count de Boigne, the town's benefactor who made his fortune in India. Locals call it "les quatre sans cul" (the four without butts).

Walk up Rue de Boigne, a 19th-century avenue with Turin-style arcades, to the Château des Ducs de Savoie. Residence of the Counts of Savoy since 1232, the castle houses the Sainte-Chapelle, which sheltered the Holy Shroud before its transfer to Turin. The Grand Carillon—70 bells totaling 41 tons—ranks among Europe's largest.

The Cathédrale Saint-François-de-Sales, a former 13th-century Franciscan chapel, conceals a surprise: Europe's largest surface of trompe-l'oeil paintings covers its vaults. The treasury includes a 12th-century Byzantine diptych.

Lose yourself in the maze of old-town alleys: traboules, covered passages, inner courtyards of mansions with bare molasse-stone facades. Rue Basse du Château, the oldest street, preserves a wooden footbridge spanning the lane.

Place Saint-Léger, the city's original heart, lines up colorful facades with Italian influences. Café terraces nestle under arcades, between shops and 15th-18th-century townhouses.

The Musée des Beaux-Arts de Chambéry, near the old town, presents a collection spanning the Middle Ages to contemporary art: paintings, sculptures, and temporary exhibitions in a setting that reflects Savoyard identity.

→

Guided tour "Castle, Alleyways and Secrets": a heritage guide opens the doors to inner courtyards, reveals hidden spiral staircases, and recounts the histories of noble Savoyard families.

→

Savoie wine tasting in downtown cellars. Apremont, Roussette, Mondeuse: local grape varieties are discovered alongside Tomme des Bauges cheese and diots (Savoyard sausages).

💡 Downtown wine shops offer tasting platters
→

Bike ride on the Voie Verte linking Chambéry to Le Bourget-du-Lac. The flat route follows the shoreline and passes the Prieuré du Bourget-du-Lac, an 11th-century Cluniac priory with a Gothic church and French formal gardens.

Pass through the Porte de Savoie, a remnant of the 14th-century ramparts, to enter the medieval citadel of Conflans. Built on a rocky spur at the confluence of the Arly and Isère rivers, this upper town guarded the entrance to Tarentaise. Cobbled lanes, artisan workshops, and colorful facades have kept their charm intact.

The Maison Rouge, a listed 14th-century building, houses the Musée d'Art et d'Histoire d'Albertville (labeled Musée de France). Gallo-Roman archaeology, baroque sacred art, rural Savoyard objects, and early skiing equipment form a diverse collection.

The église Saint-Grat de Conflans, recognizable by its onion-dome bell tower, was rebuilt in the early 18th century. Its baroque interior—gilded altarpiece, carved woodwork—illustrates the importance of this art in the Savoyard valleys.

The Tour Sarrasine, built between the 11th and 12th centuries, is the sole vestige of the Château de la Cour. From the garden surrounding it, the panorama embraces Albertville, the Isère valley, and alpine summits.

Descend to the lower town to discover the "Tremplin 92, Mountain and Olympism" visitor center. The space recounts the 1992 Winter Olympics through documents, artifacts, and testimonies. The Halle Olympique, renovated in 2015, remains a venue for cultural and sporting events.

→

Guided tour of Conflans with a guide from the Pays d'Art et d'Histoire. The route details the fortified gates, Château Manuel de Locatel (16th century, built by a noble merchant from Bergamo), and the 1390 Château Rouge.

→

Want to gain altitude? A paragliding flight above the valley lets you soar over the confluence of the Arly and Isère, with the Tarentaise peaks as backdrop.

→

Taste Savoyard specialties in Conflans inns: crozets gratinéed with Beaufort, diots in white wine, and polenta. Terraces offer plunging views over the valley.

Beaufort-sur-Doron stretches along its river, between footbridges and mountain-architecture houses. The "Beaufort Through Time" trail, marked by 18 panels, guides your walk: Château de Randens (15th century, now town hall), Pont Morens repeatedly swept away by floods and rebuilt, the former imperial gendarmerie of Napoleon III.

The baroque église Saint-Maxime, built on the rock since 1171 and remodeled in the 18th century, strikes with its carved wooden doors and gilded altarpiece. The interior testifies to the baroque exuberance of the Savoyard valleys.

The Beaufortain dairy cooperative reveals the secrets of Beaufort AOP, a pressed cooked cheese made from raw milk of Tarine and Abondance cows. Tour of the aging cellars followed by a tasting of summer and winter Beaufort, paired with a Savoie wine.

Take the road to Lac de Roselend (1,557 m altitude). The dam, built between 1955 and 1962, measures 804 meters long and 150 meters high. Its turquoise waters mirror the peaks of Beaufortain, including Pierra Menta.

The Romanesque Chapelle Sainte-Marie-Madeleine de Roselend, sole vestige of the old hamlet submerged when the dam was filled, was dismantled stone by stone and rebuilt on the lake's shores. A place of contemplation facing the mountains.

→

Hike around Lac de Roselend. The trail follows turquoise shores through alpine pastures where herds of Tarine cattle graze. From Plan de la Laie, a 15-minute detour leads to an unusual curiosity: a red telephone booth perched in the middle of the mountains.

→

Scenic drive over the Cormet de Roselend pass (1,968 m). This pass links Beaufortain to the Mont Blanc massif, famous for its Tour de France appearances. The hairpin turns offer spectacular views of the lake and snow-capped peaks.

💡 Road open roughly June to October
→

Discover the Beaufortain alpine pastures. In summer, Tarine cows—brown coats and gentle eyes—climb to altitude to produce milk for Beaufort d'alpage. Trails cross flower-filled meadows with Mont Blanc in the background.

💡 The Grand-Mont chairlift (1,700 m) provides summer access to the pastures

Val d'Isère stretches a kilometer in the heart of Haute-Tarentaise, ringed by summits topping 3,700 m. Wander the village center's lanes and admire stone chalets with lauze-slab roofs that remain faithful to traditional rural architecture.

The église Saint-Bernard-de-Menthon, built in the 11th century of cut stone, towers at the village center. Simple and massive, it has marked the rhythm of Avalin life for nearly a thousand years. In front, the larch mission cross (1847) marks Chapelle Saint-Jean, built to protect the village from avalanches descending from Solaise.

Continue to Le Fornet, an authentic hamlet at the valley's far end (2,000 m). Stone chalets pressed close together, absolute silence: here you touch raw mountain, before the road attacks the hairpin turns of the Iseran pass.

Take the Col de l'Iseran road (2,764 m), the highest paved pass in the French Alps. The panorama sweeps across the glaciers of Vanoise and Gran Paradiso on the Italian side. At the summit, a stone chapel marks the border between Tarentaise and Maurienne.

Parc National de la Vanoise, France's first national park (created 1963), protects 535 km² of core zone. More than 1,400 plant species and remarkable fauna are recorded: ibex, chamois, marmots, bearded vultures, and golden eagles.

Chapelle Saint-Barthélemy, in the hamlet of Laisinant, shelters a restored painting of the Virgin and Child. Built before 1633, it offers a fine starting point for park trails. Nearby, a tapped spring supplies the resort with drinking water.

→

Hike to Refuge du Fond des Fours. From Pont Saint-Charles, the trail crosses the Malpasset gorges, rejoins Val d'Isère village, and climbs the Manchet valley in the heart of the National Park. The refuge (2,537 m) offers a panorama of the Méan Martin glacier.

→

Family hike to Lac de l'Ouillette (2,350 m). This turquoise alpine lake is reached in 45 minutes from the top of the Solaise cable cars, significantly reducing effort. Beautiful panorama over the Bellevarde rock.

💡 Cable cars open in summer, check dates
→

Mountain biking or e-biking on the bike park trails linking Tignes and Val d'Isère. Over 220 km of marked trails between technical descents and panoramic rides overlooking mountain lakes.

💡 Rental and lessons available on-site

Bonneval-sur-Arc is the only Savoyard village classified among France's Most Beautiful Villages. Perched at 1,800 m altitude at the far end of Haute-Maurienne, it lines up stone houses with 80-cm-thick walls, roofed with lauze slabs on larch timbers. No antenna or satellite dish disturbs the village silhouette.

The parish church raises its tower bell-spire of stone above the rooftops. Originally a simple chapel, it was enlarged in the 16th century to accommodate the parish newly established by the Bishop of Maurienne.

Push on to the hamlet of l'Écot, 4 km from the village (2,000 m). Classified as a historic site in 1971, this cluster of abandoned chalets and its Chapelle Sainte-Marguerite (Romanesque, adorned with medieval frescoes) served as a film set for Belle et Sébastien adaptations.

The Espace Neige et Montagne recounts daily life in the high mountains of yesteryear: wood crafts, pottery, weaving, and the beginnings of mountaineering at Bonneval. The old-timers heated themselves with "grébons," molded briquettes made from sheep dung.

Stop at the cooperative fromagerie to taste Bleu de Bonneval, a local blue-veined cheese, and Beaufort AOP d'alpage. Cows graze here between 1,800 and 2,500 m altitude, in the pastures of Vanoise National Park.

→

Hike to the Cirque des Évettes. From l'Écot, the trail climbs alongside the Arc torrent to a glacial amphitheater dominated by the Albaron (3,637 m). Facing the Évettes glacier, silence and immensity reward every step.

→

Family walk on the Sentier des Agneaux, between the village and the hamlet of l'Écot. The path follows pastures where flocks spend the summer, with views of the border peaks of Italy.

→

Canyoning in the gorges of l'Écot. One of the Alps' finest spots: descent in the Arc's waters with natural slides, jumps, and rappels supervised by instructors.

💡 Supervision required, wetsuit provided

The Forts de l'Esseillon form an ensemble of five fortifications built between 1819 and 1834 by the Kingdom of Piedmont-Sardinia on a natural glacial lock. Listed Historic Monuments, they bear the names of the royal family: Victor-Emmanuel, Marie-Thérèse, Marie-Christine, Charles-Albert, and Charles-Félix.

Fort Victor-Emmanuel, the most imposing of the complex, could house 1,500 soldiers. You wander through casemates, vaulted corridors, and guard posts. A scenario-based treasure hunt invites families to slip into the role of a 19th-century French spy.

Redoute Marie-Thérèse, horseshoe-shaped, houses a fortified heritage interpretation center. Scale models, panels, and artifacts trace the history of the military lock, from its construction to Savoy's annexation in 1860.

Walk down to the Pont du Diable, a suspension bridge above the Arc gorges, linking Fort Victor-Emmanuel to Redoute Marie-Thérèse. The plunging view into gorges more than 100 meters deep is dizzying.

At Modane's outskirts, on the Valfréjus road, spot the "maison penchée." This former 1939 blockhouse, an observation post at the railway tunnel entrance, was hurled dozens of meters by the explosion of two explosives wagons in 1944. It replanted itself in the ground, nearly intact but tilted.

The monumental entrance to the Fréjus railway tunnel (the Alps' first boring, 1857-1871) stands on the Modane-Valfréjus road. The cut-stone portal marks the beginning of one of the 19th century's great engineering projects.

→

Via Ferrata du Diable. Seven routes of increasing difficulty spread across the Esseillon site, between rock walls, monkey bridges, and metal ladders overhanging the Arc gorges.

💡 On-site equipment rental, supervision available
→

Hike on the old Esseillon road, starting from Modane. The trail winds between Le Bourget and Aussois via the Saint-Benoît waterfall and chapel, then passes the forts one by one. Drinking water points all along the route.

→

Treetop adventure course in the Parc du Diable, near Redoute Marie-Thérèse. Walkways between the trees and zip lines above the gorges, in a spectacular setting blending forest and military heritage.

💡 Family-friendly, multiple course levels

The Cathédrale Saint-Jean-Baptiste, founded in the 6th century, rebuilt in the 11th, and enlarged in the 15th, possesses one of France's oldest timber frames (wood dated to 1075). In the choir, 82 walnut Gothic stalls carved in 1498 depict saints, apostles, fauna, and flora—each one different from its neighbor.

The cloister, built around 1450 between the cathedral and the canons' refectory, unfolds its tufa vaults reinforced with local gypsum ribs. From the south bay, you access the Romanesque crypt, rediscovered in 1958 beneath the Gothic choir.

The neoclassical portico (1771) shelters the cenotaphs of three of the first princes of the House of Savoy: Humbert the White-Handed, founder of the dynasty, Amadeus I, and Boniface. The bell tower, a former 11th-century capitular keep, lost its Gothic spire during the Revolution.

The Musée Opinel occupies nearly 700 m² dedicated to the famous Savoyard knife. From the old forge to modern workshops, the exhibition traces the adventure of a tool created in 1890 in the nearby hamlet of Gévoudaz, today distributed in over 70 countries. Free entry, personalized engraving in the boutique.

Opposite the cathedral, the former bishop's palace (listed Historic Monument) houses the Musée des Costumes, Arts et Traditions Populaires. The collection of traditional Maurienne costumes, among the Alps' most original, displays embroidered headdresses, festive aprons, and dresses from the Arvan valleys.

Stroll through the historic center: the cathedral square hosts the market, lanes reveal the Tabellion block (medieval tribunal), the Art Deco festival hall (1933) that became the Théâtre Gérard Philipe, and at the town entrance, two giant Opinel knife sculptures mark local pride.

→

Excursion to the Lacets de Montvernier. This spectacular road chains together eighteen tight hairpins on a vertical wall above the Maurienne valley. Plunging view guaranteed, a legendary Tour de France passage since 2015.

→

Cycling on the Maurienne greenway. The bike path follows the Arc through the valley, linking villages between orchards and valley-floor meadows. E-bike rental available on-site.

💡 Flat route, family-friendly
→

Tasting of Mauriennais specialties: Saint-Jean local cheese, diots (smoked sausages cooked in white wine), corn polenta, and crozets (small square buckwheat pasta). Restaurants around the cathedral square offer these traditional dishes.

💡 Local market Saturday morning on cathedral square
