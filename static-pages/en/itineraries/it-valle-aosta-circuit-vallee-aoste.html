Aosta Valley Circuit: From Roman Gateway to Mont Blanc

Two thousand years of history between medieval castles, Roman ruins, and eternal glaciers: the Aosta Valley from the devil's bridge to the roof of Europe.

Nine days to traverse Italy's smallest autonomous region end to end: from Pont-Saint-Martin's Roman bridge (1st century BC) to the Skyway Monte Bianco at 11,358 feet, passing through Fort Bard rebuilt by Charles Albert, the ruins of Augusta Praetoria at Aosta, Italy's first national park at Cogne, the Lion Ridge beneath the Matterhorn, the Great Saint Bernard hospice founded in 1050, and the pre-phylloxera vineyards of Morgex at Mont Blanc's foot.

  • Fort Bard: the fortress that held off Napoleon for two weeks
  • Augusta Praetoria: Aosta, the Rome of the Alps with 1st-century arch, gate, and theater
  • Gran Paradiso: 3,000 ibex in Italy's first national park
  • Matterhorn: the Lion Ridge and the saga of Jean-Antoine Carrel
  • Great Saint Bernard: a thousand years of hospitality and the rescue dogs
  • Skyway Monte Bianco: 11,358 feet facing the glaciers from Courmayeur

Pont Saint Martin

The Roman bridge at Pont-Saint-Martin, built around the 1st century BC under Augustus to span the Lys torrent, stands as one of Italy's best-preserved Roman arches. Anchored directly into bedrock on both banks, it soars 82 feet above the water with a single span stretching 115 feet. At its base, you can still see slots carved into the rock where wooden support beams were fitted during construction. The bridge formed part of the Route des Gaules linking Eporedia (Ivrea) to Augusta Praetoria (Aosta), then onward to the Great and Little Saint Bernard passes. Though replaced for traffic in 1836, it survived even the Allied bombing of May 25, 1944, which devastated much of the village. Legend, celebrated annually during the historic carnival, tells how Saint Martin negotiated the bridge's construction with the devil: he threw bread across the gorge, and a starving dog raced over first, claiming the promised soul. Outwitted, the devil vanished into the Lys waters.

Fort Bard rises from a rock outcrop commanding the valley's narrowest point. The first defensive works date to the 10th century (first documented in 1034, belonging to Viscount Boso of Aosta). In May 1800, the fort—defended by just 400 soldiers under Captain Stockard von Bernkopf—held up Napoleon's reserve army for two weeks, forcing 40,000 men and 50 cannons to bypass the fortress under cover of night, their cannon wheels wrapped in hay to muffle the sound. Napoleon ordered the fort razed after his victory. King Charles Albert of Sardinia had it rebuilt between 1830 and 1838, designed by engineer Francesco Antonio Olivero. Decommissioned in 1975, purchased by the Region in 1990, and renovated over 10 years at a cost of 45 million euros, it reopened in 2006 as a cultural hub. The Museum of the Alps (in the Carlo Alberto building) offers an interactive journey through Alpine geography, cultures, and glaciers from Slovenia to France. The medieval village of Bard at its foot is accessible by panoramic elevator.

Two miles from the fort at Donnas, the Route des Gaules is carved directly through living rock for 725 feet, topped by an arched passage 13 feet thick, 13 feet high, and nearly 10 feet wide. It's one of the most spectacular testimonies to Roman road engineering in the Alps, still visible in its original state.

→

Hike in Mont Avic Natural Park, established in 1989 and accessible from Pont-Saint-Martin: over 30 lakes and peat bogs, vast Swiss pine forests, wild landscapes untouched by tourist infrastructure. One of the Aosta Valley's most pristine natural spaces.

→

Historic Carnival of Pont-Saint-Martin (February/March): over 1,000 costumed performers recreate the town's Roman history. On Shrove Tuesday night, an effigy of the devil is burned beneath the Roman bridge arch in a spectacular pyrotechnic display.

Graines Castle at Brusson (4,511 feet) ranks among the Aosta Valley's oldest medieval fortresses, built in the 11th century on a rocky spur at the mouth of Freudière Valley. Classified as a "primitive" castle by Valdostan architectural historians, it consists of a high crenellated tower on a quadrangular plan, a chapel, and walls following the cliff's contours. Its position commanded visual control of the Ayas valley floor and access to the upper Gressoney valley. Around 1200, the Graines family's lands passed to the powerful House of Challant, viscounts of Aosta.

The Ayas valley was colonized by the Walser from the 13th century onward: these mountain people of Germanic origin from the Upper Valais crossed the Alpine passes and settled in the upper valley, leaving their mark in the villages of Mascognaz and Saint-Jacques-des-Allemands (Canton of the Germans). Their rascard—larch granaries on stilts with stone-slab roofs—are still visible at Antagnod and Champoluc. By the 19th century, the Walser language had vanished from this valley, supplanted by Valdostan Franco-Provençal patois.

Verrès Castle (14th century), at the valley entrance, rises from a 260-foot rock dominating the confluence of the Evançon torrent and the Dora Baltea. A rectangular fortress with four corner towers topped by cupolas, built around 1390 by Ibleto de Challant, it now houses a museum devoted to the Aosta Valley's medieval history.

→

Ride the cable car from Champoluc (5,144 feet) to Crest station (6,496 feet), then hike the alpine pastures of the Monterosa domain: panoramas of Monte Rosa glaciers (15,203 feet). In summer, the Tour of Monte Rosa trails depart from this station.

→

Tour the Chamousira gold mine at Brusson (by reservation): since 1899, over a mile of galleries have been excavated, some accessible with a guide. The mine was still active in the 1970s.

Ussel Castle, built by Ébal II de Challant in 1343 (dendrochronology-confirmed date) on a serpentinite promontory above Châtillon, marks the Aosta Valley's first monobloc castle—an architectural rupture between castles articulated in multiple volumes (like Fénis) and austere single-mass fortresses (like Verrès). Rectangular in form with a summit band of small blind arches, paired windows with floral decoration, and two cylindrical towers to the south, it passed through Challant hands until François de Challant's death in 1470, then served as prison and barracks before abandonment. In 1983, Baron Marcel Bich—inventor of the Bic pen and Châtillon native—purchased and donated it to the Region, which restored it as an exhibition space. From the terraces: sweeping views across the main valley to the Valtournenche entrance.

Saint-Vincent, just over a mile from Châtillon, has been a renowned spa town since the 18th century. Its waters feed the thermal station, and its casino—Casino de la Vallée—funded the Valdostan regional budget for decades. The village preserves the fresco dedicated to Saint Maurice on the market square, with the motto "in adversity, good heart," added to the coat of arms in 1565 by Duke Emmanuel Philibert.

Fénis Castle, six miles west of Châtillon, is the Aosta Valley's most photographed castle. Built by Aimone de Challant in the 14th century, it features double concentric walls, semi-circular towers, and an inner courtyard decorated with frescoes depicting Saint George slaying the dragon and a court scene with philosophers holding phylacteries—the work of Giacomo Jaquerio, circa 1415-1420.

→

Wine tasting along the Aosta Valley Wine Route: terraced vineyards at Chambave, Nus, and Montjovet produce local DOC wines like Chambave Muscat, Nus Malvoisie, and Torrette made from Petit Rouge. These "heroic" vineyards clinging to Alpine slopes between 1,300 and 2,950 feet have been cultivated by hand for centuries.

→

Hike the Cammino Balteo trail from Châtillon: discovery route through the Aosta Valley's natural and cultural heritage at mid and low elevations, accessible year-round.

The Matterhorn (14,692 feet), the Alps' fifth-highest summit, is the world's most recognizable mountain for its nearly perfect pyramidal shape—the result of four intersecting glacial cirques. From Breuil—renamed Cervinia in 1936-37 at the urging of Senator Dino Lora Totino to assert the resort's Italian identity—the Lion Ridge rises toward the summit. On July 17, 1865, Valtournenche guide Jean-Antoine Carrel opened the first Italian route up the Matterhorn via this ridge, three days after Englishman Edward Whymper's triumph from Zermatt. Whymper's descent cost four companions their lives. In 1890, Carrel perished on the mountain he had conquered, caught in a storm while descending after bringing his clients to safety.

Cervinia's Blue Lake, a 45-minute walk from the village, offers the Matterhorn's reflection in turquoise waters. This glacial cirque lake at 7,064 feet is surrounded by a mineral amphitheater whose hues shift from turquoise to cobalt depending on the hour. It's the starting point for trails to the Oriondé (Duca degli Abruzzi, 9,193 feet) and Carrel (12,566 feet) refuges—the two stages of the Matterhorn ascent by the Italian route.

The Matterhorn Alpine Crossing, inaugurated July 1, 2023, links Breuil-Cervinia to Zermatt by cable car for the first time without skis, crossing the Alps' highest border crossing. The Plateau Rosa (11,417 feet) to Klein Matterhorn (12,739 feet) section crosses the Italian-Swiss frontier above the glaciers, with direct views of the Matterhorn from both flanks.

→

Hike to Oriondé Refuge (Duca degli Abruzzi, 9,193 feet) from Breuil: ascend the route of the first alpinists, pass the Carrel cross, view the Matterhorn's south face. Round trip approximately 5-6 hours.

→

In summer, ride the cable car to Plateau Rosa (11,417 feet): panoramas of Matterhorn and Monte Rosa glaciers, summer ski slope, walking access to the seracs. In winter, international ski domain linked to Zermatt.

Aosta (Augusta Praetoria Salassorum) was founded in 25 BC by the Romans following the final defeat of the Salassi people. The city preserves its Roman grid plan intact: today's streets still follow the ancient cardines and decumani orientation. The Arch of Augustus, erected immediately after the foundation to honor the alliance with the empire, stands 37 feet high with a single 30-foot arch, flanked by ten Corinthian half-columns on a Doric entablature. In the Middle Ages, a crucifix was placed there and the arch took the name Saint-Voût (from Latin votum)—the original 15th-century crucifix is now preserved in the Cathedral Treasury Museum.

The Porta Praetoria, built in 25 BC, was the Roman city's main eastern entrance. It comprises two parallel arches, each with three openings—the central arch for chariots, the two lateral ones for pedestrians. The outer façade partially preserves its original travertine marble facing, revealed during 2012 excavations 10 feet below the current level. The adjacent Roman theater, whose scenic façade reaches 72 feet, the underground cryptoporticus with triple gallery (formerly the Romans' medieval market), and the 2,375 × 1,712-foot wall complete the archaeological circuit.

The Collegiate Church of Sant'Orso, founded on a 4th-5th century paleo-Christian basilica, preserves a Romanesque cloister (1460) with grey marble columns decorated with historiated capitals among northern Italy's most remarkable. Ottonian frescoes from the 11th century adorn the church vaults, depicting Gospel scenes in an expressive style rare at this altitude. The neighboring cathedral (12th century, remodeled 15th-16th centuries) houses in its choir two floor mosaics from the 12th and 14th centuries representing the months of the year, fantastic animals, the Tigris and Euphrates.

The megalithic area of Saint-Martin-de-Corléans, west of Aosta, is one of the Alps' most important prehistoric sites. Used from the fifth millennium BC, it has yielded anthropomorphic stelae remarkably similar to those of Petit Chasseur at Sion (Valais), testifying to trans-Alpine cultural links well before Roman times.

→

Ascend to Pila from Aosta by gondola (17 minutes, 5,905 feet elevation): in summer, 43 miles of marked trails, panoramas across the entire Gran Paradiso and Mont Blanc massifs. Mountain biking domain with possible return on foot to Aosta.

→

Market at Piazza Chanoux (Aosta's pedestrian heart): local cheeses including Fontina DOP (aged in natural caves), the valley also produces Lard d'Arnad and mocetta (dried chamois meat). Valdostan blood sausage with potatoes and Jambon de Bosses ham (Saint-Rhémy) round out the must-try specialties.

Gran Paradiso National Park, inaugurated December 3, 1922, is Italy's first national park. Its history is inseparable from the Alpine ibex: in 1856, King Victor Emmanuel II declared these mountains a Royal Hunting Reserve, saving the species from extinction—only a few hundred remained. In 1919, his son Victor Emmanuel III offered the 5,189 acres to the State on condition that a national park be created. In 1945, after World War II reduced the ibex population to 416 individuals, commissioner Renzo Videsott obtained autonomous park management in 1947, enabling gradual recovery. The park today shelters nearly 3,000 ibex—Europe's largest population—along with 8,000 chamois, marmots, lynx, and wolves.

The Cogne valley is the main Valdostan access to the park. At Valnontey, the Paradisia alpine botanical garden (mid-June to mid-September) assembles Alpine flora species, medicinal plants, and lichens. Its name comes from the white lily Paradisea liliastrum, endemic to the park. Cogne's historic center perpetuates the tombolo tradition—bobbin lace—transmitted for centuries.

The Lillaz waterfalls, a 10-minute walk from the eponymous hamlet, drop 492 feet in three successive rocky leaps on the Urtier torrent. Rifugio Vittorio Sella (8,478 feet), originally a royal hunting lodge built in 1861 by order of the king, was converted to a mountain refuge in 1922 at the park's opening.

→

Hike from Valnontey to Rifugio Vittorio Sella (8,478 feet), the park's heart: virtually guaranteed encounters with ibex (males with long curved horns averaging 36 inches) and chamois. Return by the same route.

→

Short hike to Lillaz waterfalls and Valnontey pastures: wildlife observation in late afternoon, flowering alpine meadows, views of the Grivola (13,022 feet) and its glacier.

Courmayeur

The Great Saint Bernard Pass (8,114 feet), Switzerland's third-highest road pass, has been traveled since antiquity—the Romans built a road here and constructed a temple dedicated to Jupiter Poeninus whose excavations yielded over 40 bronze ex-votos. The hospice was founded in 1050 by Bernard of Aosta, archdeacon of the cathedral, to welcome and protect travelers crossing the Alps—a mission uninterrupted for nearly a thousand years. In May 1800, Napoleon crossed the pass with 40,000 men, 5,000 horses, and 50 cannons during his second Italian campaign; the episode was immortalized in Jacques-Louis David's painting. The tomb of General Desaix, who died at Marengo weeks later, stands in the hospice stairway.

Saint Bernard dogs were bred at the hospice from the 17th century onward, first as guard dogs, then to accompany the canons on daily patrols searching for travelers in distress in the snow. The breed was selected from dogs probably gifted by Valais families in the 1660s-1670s—first mentioned in 1709. Breeding has been entrusted since 2005 to the Barry Foundation in Martigny. Each summer, a dozen dogs return to the pass where visitors can accompany them on hikes. The hospice museum traces the pass's history from ancient cults to contemporary hospitality.

→

Hike around Lake Great Saint Bernard (8,114 feet): 45-minute loop between the lake's two shores, 1931 stone Italian-Swiss border marker, views of the Pennine Alps. In summer, possibility of guided hikes with Saint Bernard dogs.

→

Visit the hospice treasury: 11th-century objects, travelers' offerings, numismatic and Roman archaeological collections found at the Jupiter Poeninus temple site. The baroque church is Europe's highest and hosts ceremonies for the canons' community.

The Little Saint Bernard Pass (7,178 feet), open in summer, links Courmayeur to Bourg-Saint-Maurice in Savoie. It was one of the main Roman routes to Gaul (Alpis Graia). The hospice of Little Saint Bernard, founded in the 11th century by Bernard of Aosta simultaneously with Great Saint Bernard, houses an alpine botanical garden created by Abbot Chanoux in the late 19th century with over 500 alpine species. A circle of pre-Roman standing stones (Cromlech), analogous to Stonehenge but smaller scale, borders the hospice.

La Thuile, a village at 4,728 feet at the entrance to Val Grisenche, harbored until 1967 a silver-lead mine exploited since the 16th century. Its Franco-Italian ski domain (linked to La Rosière in Savoie) is one of the rare ones crossing an Alpine border. The Château de la Crête, a medieval fortress on the mountainside, guards the Dora de la Thuile gorge.

Morgex and La Salle, in the Valdigne, produce the Blanc de Morgex et de La Salle DOC, Europe's highest vineyard wine (between 2,950 and 4,265 feet elevation). The Prié Blanc grape is cultivated on low pergolas on glacial terraces—one of Europe's rare pre-phylloxera vines, spared by the parasite thanks to altitude. The Cave Mont Blanc de Morgex et de La Salle offers tastings and vineyard tours.

→

Pré-Saint-Didier thermal baths, 5.6 miles from Courmayeur: outdoor pools at 99°F with views of Mont Blanc's snow-covered peaks, rock treatments, waterfalls and open-air basins. The sulfurous springs have been exploited since the 16th century.

→

Departure toward France (Mont Blanc tunnel, 7.2 miles, open 24/7) or Switzerland (Great Saint Bernard pass or tunnel, depending on season). Courmayeur is 2 hours from Turin, 1.5 hours from Geneva, 3 hours from Milan.
