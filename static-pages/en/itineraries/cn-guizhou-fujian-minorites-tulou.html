The walled town of Qingyan, 29 km south of Guiyang, was built in 1378 as a Ming military outpost to control the empire's southwestern frontier. Constructed entirely from local limestone—walls, streets, houses—it sprawls across 3 km² and preserves four monumental gates rising 9.5 meters high. A rare feature in China: Buddhism, Taoism, Catholicism, and Protestantism have coexisted within its walls since a French missionary arrived in 1851.

Step through the gate of Ciyun Temple in Qingyan, where stone carvings on column bases rank among the finest in Guiyang. Wanshou Palace, a former Jiangxi merchant's trading house converted into a Taoist temple in 1778, preserves a theater stage adorned with bas-reliefs depicting historical legends.

Jiaxiu Tower, in central Guiyang, stands on a rock in the middle of the Nanming River. This three-story Ming dynasty wooden structure with upturned eaves served as a study and meditation retreat for scholars. It remains the city's most recognizable symbol.

→

Street food tasting in Qingyan's alleyways: braised pork trotters in soy sauce (a local specialty since the Qing dynasty), Qingyan tofu, and rice cake porridge rank among Guizhou's most celebrated snacks.

Anshun

Top hotels
10 Four Points by Sheraton Anshun, Huangguoshu
9 DoubleTree by Hilton Hotel Anshun

Huangguoshu Waterfall, on the Baishui River, anchors a cluster of 18 cascades spread across 163 km². The main waterfall measures 67 meters high and 83.3 meters wide—the largest single-drop waterfall in Asia. The 17th-century traveler Xu Xiake described it as mist bursting from rock. Behind the curtain of water, Water Curtain Cave extends 134 meters with six natural windows, five chambers, and three springs. On sunny days, a rainbow forms above Rhinoceros Pool.

Doupotang Waterfall, 1 km upstream, is the widest in the group at 105 meters. It served as the filming location for the 1986 television series "Journey to the West," where monk Tang and his disciples cross the river. Before floods, it emits a low rumble that earned it the nickname "roaring waterfall."

Tianxingqiao scenic area, 7 km downstream, covers 4.5 km² of karst landscape where stones, trees, and water form natural compositions. Silver Chain Waterfall falls in parallel threads like a pearl necklace into a turquoise pool.

→

Walk through Gaodang Stone Village, 6 km from the waterfalls: houses, bridges, roofs, and kitchen utensils carved from stone by the Buyi people. The village, founded during the Ming dynasty, forms a circle crossed by a river spanned by a five-arch bridge whose reflection draws a perfect circle.

Upper Langde Miao Village, 30 km from Kaili, is a hamlet of wooden stilted houses clinging to the mountainside. Visitors are greeted with a welcome ceremony: women in embroidered costumes offer rice wine in water buffalo horns. The village preserves songs, lusheng (bamboo mouth organ) dances, and traditional embroidery without compromising to mass tourism.

Matang Gejia Village, 19 km from Kaili, houses 400 members of an officially unrecognized Miao subgroup—they consider themselves China's "57th ethnic group." According to their legend, they descend from Hou Yi, the mythical archer who shot down nine suns. The women practice beeswax batik with unique geometric patterns.

The Qiandongnan Nationalities Museum, in central Kaili, presents costumes, silver jewelry, musical instruments, and agricultural tools of the Miao and Dong peoples across eleven halls. The silver ornament collection—crowns, breastplates, necklaces—illustrates the metal's importance in Miao culture: a complete bridal set can weigh over 10 kg.

→

Batik workshop in Matang: artisans teach how to draw patterns on fabric using a stylus dipped in hot beeswax, before indigo dyeing. The complete process (drawing, soaking, rinsing, drying) takes about 2 hours.

Shiqiao Miao Village, in Danzhai County, is the last place in China where paper is made by hand using Tang dynasty (7th century) techniques. More than 60 families still practice this millennial craft: local tree bark soaked, boiled in lime, beaten into pulp, then spread on a bamboo screen. The resulting paper can be preserved for 1,500 years—the National Library and National Museum of China use it to restore ancient books and paintings. The process, inscribed on the national intangible heritage list in 2006, involves more than ten steps.

Chuandong Cave, adjacent to Shiqiao village, houses an underground paper mill fed by a spring whose slightly alkaline water is considered ideal for papermaking. Stalactites grow at an unusual rate here, giving the walls a soft texture to the touch.

→

Flower paper (huacao) workshop in Shiqiao: visitors dip a screen into bark pulp, place dried flowers and leaves on the wet layer, then add a second layer of pulp. Drying takes a week—the paper is then shipped.

→

Kaili Sunday Market gathers Miao villagers from the surrounding area every Sunday. Embroidered textiles, hammered silver jewelry, medicinal herbs, and sour fish soup (suantangyu) mingle in a rural fair atmosphere.

Xijiang, the "Thousand Household Village," is the world's largest Miao village: 1,432 families, over 6,000 inhabitants of whom 99.5% are Miao. Wooden fir stilted houses climb in tiers on both sides of the Baishui River, linked by Wind and Rain Bridges. The village is divided into ten natural hamlets at the foot of Mount Leigong.

The Miao Nationality Museum, in the village, traces the history of this people through costumes, orally transmitted "old songs," and agricultural tools. Special emphasis is placed on silver jewelry making—hammering, filigree, granulation—practiced by village silversmiths for generations.

→

Climb to Xijiang viewpoint in late afternoon: at dusk, thousands of lights illuminate the valley, transforming the stilted houses into a luminous amphitheater.

→

Miao song and dance performance on the central square (around 11:30 am and 5:00 pm). The "Miao old songs" are polyphonic vocals without choir leader or instrumental accompaniment, transmitted by word of mouth for centuries.

→

Long table banquet (changzhuo yan): guests sit around a table several dozen meters long. Pickled sour fish, salted vegetables, and chili-boiled chicken, accompanied by rice wine served in buffalo horns.

Zhaoxing

Top hotels
9.6 White Swan Guest House
9 Chaoyangli Folk Resort Hotel

Zhaoxing, according to the oral tradition of the Lu clan, was founded around 1160 during the Southern Song dynasty—over 850 years of history. It's China's largest Dong village: more than 800 families, 4,000 inhabitants. The village is divided into five quarters bearing the names of Confucian virtues—Ren (Benevolence), Yi (Righteousness), Li (Propriety), Zhi (Wisdom), Xin (Honesty). Each quarter has its own drum tower, covered bridge, and theater stage.

Zhaoxing's five drum towers, built entirely of fir wood without a single nail, measure from 14.9 to 25.9 meters. Four central columns rise to the top, twelve peripheral pillars symbolize the twelve months. Lintels bear carvings of dragons, serpents, turtles, and tigers—Dong totems. Originally, a drum at the summit served to sound the alarm in case of attack or fire.

→

Dong Grand Song (Dong Da Ge) concert at the drum tower square. Inscribed on UNESCO's intangible heritage list since 2009, this multi-voice polyphonic singing is performed without choir leader, without sheet music, and without instrumental accompaniment. The melodies imitate nature's sounds—flowing water, birdsong, wind in the pines.

Tangan village, 6 km from Zhaoxing, is an ecomuseum created jointly by China and Norway. Its 160 Dong families live in stilted houses surrounded by terraced rice paddies. The village preserves a traditional gateway, drum tower, ancient tombs, wells, rice granaries, and stone pathways—a coherent ensemble of Dong rural life.

→

"Eight villages and one mountain" hike from Zhaoxing: the trail crosses Tangan, Xiage, Shangdiping, Dengjiang, Jitang, and Jilun, passing through the sacred mountain of Sa Sui, a female deity revered by the Dong. Rice terraces, bamboo forests, and wooden hamlets punctuate the route.

Sanjiang

Top hotels

Chengyang's Yongji Bridge, known as the "Wind and Rain Bridge," completed in 1912, is China's most famous Dong covered bridge. Measuring 64.4 meters long, 3.4 meters wide, and 10.6 meters high, it rests on five stone piers topped with multi-tiered wooden pavilions. The entire structure was assembled without a single nail—only through interlocking fir beams. The Sanjiang region counts more than 160 drum towers and a hundred covered bridges.

The eight villages of Chengyang (Ma'an, Pingzhai, Yanzhai, Dazhai, Pingpu, Pingtan, Jichang, Guandong) form an ensemble of stilted houses, drum towers, and water mills along the Linxi River. From the bridge, tea-planted hills, rice paddies, and water mills compose a preserved Dong rural landscape.

Excursion to Biasha Miao Village (2.5-hour drive each way from Sanjiang via Congjiang), nicknamed the "gunmen's village." The men still wear the topknot shaved on the sides, a knife at the belt, and a flintlock rifle—the only community in China authorized to carry firearms. Burials take place under a tree planted at each child's birth.

→

Hike between Chengyang villages along the Linxi River and tea hills. The short circuit (Chengyang → Pingtan → Guandong) crosses rice paddies and plantations over approximately 12 km.

Yongding Tulou

Top hotels
9.6 Tulou Herongzhuang Inn
9.5 Tulou Fuyulou Changdi Inn

The Hongkeng tulou cluster gathers Fujian's most famous earthen fortresses. Zhencheng Lou, nicknamed the "Prince of tulou," is a four-story circular structure built according to the Eight Trigrams plan. Its outer rammed-earth walls mixed with glutinous rice and brown sugar reach 1.5 meters thick at the base. The interior combines Chinese and Western elements—neoclassical columns in the courtyard testify to the influence of Hakka emigrants to Southeast Asia.

Chengqi Lou in Gaobei village is the "King of tulou": four concentric rings, 400 rooms across four floors, capable of housing 600 people from the same clan. The central courtyard shelters the ancestral temple. Each family has the same number of rooms on each level—a Hakka principle of strict equality.

In 2008, 46 tulou were inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage list, including 23 in Yongding district alone. These communal dwellings, built from the 12th to the 20th century, could shelter up to 800 people behind walls 1 to 3 meters thick and up to 18 meters high. Single door, no ground-floor windows, loopholes on upper floors: fortresses against bandits.

→

Overnight in an inhabited tulou, with a local family. Hakka families offer rooms on the upper floors. Dinner follows Hakka recipes: braised pork with pickled mustard greens, stuffed tofu, and local tea.

Nanjing Tulou

Top hotels
9.6 Tulou Herongzhuang Inn
9.5 Tulou Fuyulou Changdi Inn

The Tianluokeng cluster forms the "four dishes and one soup": four round or oval tulou surrounding a square tulou, seen from above. It was by spotting these circular shapes on satellite images in the 1960s that the CIA believed it had discovered nuclear missile silos—an anecdote systematically cited in guidebooks, probably exaggerated but now inseparable from the place.

Taxia village stretches along a stream bordered by stone bridges and 24 ten-meter-high honor poles erected to celebrate clan members who obtained academic titles. The village counts 47 tulou of varied shapes among the mountains.

Yuchanglou, in Xiaban village, is a 14th-century round tulou whose interior pillars lean up to 15 degrees without the structure having collapsed in over 700 years. This "leaning tower" of Fujian intrigues architects.

→

Bike ride between tulou in Nanjing valley. Country roads cross tea plantations, groves of 13 centuries-old banyans (over 300 years old) along the river, and inhabited Hakka hamlets.

Tianyou Peak ("Heavenly Tour") is Wuyi Mountain's iconic summit: 848 steps carved into the cliff lead to a viewpoint above the 5th and 6th bends of the Nine Bend River. The panorama over red Danxia sandstone peaks crowned with subtropical vegetation justifies the climb.

The Da Hong Pao area shelters the six mother bushes of this legendary oolong tea, clinging to the cliff of Jiulongke ravine. These plants, estimated at over 350 years old, are World Heritage listed. Since 2006, all harvesting from these mother plants has been prohibited. The Tea Trail (4.5 km) descends through rock walls where hundreds of varieties of rock tea (yancha) grow to Water Curtain Cave.

→

Bamboo raft ride down the Nine Bend River, 9.5 km in about 2 hours. The raft passes between 200 to 400-meter red sandstone cliffs. The raftsman comments on rock formations and legends. The river owes its name to its nine sharp turns between the peaks.

Thread of Sky (Yixiantian) is a passage between two walls over 50 meters high, so narrow that the tightest point measures only 30 centimeters. You progress sideways, glimpsing a thin line of sky above your head.

Tiger Roaring Rock (Huxiao Yan) owes its name to wind rushing into a giant cavity in the cliff. The trail climbs steeply along the wall via stairs clinging to the rock, with plunging views over the subtropical forest.

Wuyi Palace, at the mountain's foot, was an 11th-century Neo-Confucian center. Philosopher Zhu Xi developed thought here that would influence all of East Asia for centuries. The UNESCO site covers 999 km² and includes ruins of a 1st-century BC Han city.

→

Tea tasting at a teahouse at the foot of Wuyi Mountain. Tea masters prepare Da Hong Pao using the gongfu method: Yixing clay teapot, water at 95-100°C, successive infusions of 10 to 15 seconds. The aromas evolve from infusion to infusion—roasted notes first, then floral and fruity. Wuyi produces over 300 varieties of rock tea.

Gulangyu Island, 600 meters from Xiamen's shore, has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2017. The 2 km² island was an international concession from 1903 to 1943, explaining its 1,000 buildings mixing colonial, Art Deco, and traditional Chinese styles. Cars are prohibited. The island once counted up to 500 pianos in the early 20th century, earning it the nickname "Music Island."

Nanputuo Temple, at the foot of Mount Wulao, is a Buddhist monastery founded during the Tang dynasty and rebuilt under the Qing. Dedicated to Guanyin, it serves an active monastic community and houses a Buddhist school. The four main halls climb up the hillside.

Sunlight Rock (Riguang Yan) is Gulangyu's highest point at 93 meters. At the summit, a 360° panorama embraces the island, the Taiwan Strait, and Fujian's mountains.

→

Ferry crossing to Gulangyu from Dongdu terminal (20 minutes). Free stroll through pedestrian alleys, between colonial facades covered with bougainvillea and local pastry shops.

Shuzhuang Garden, created in 1913 by an exiled Taiwanese scholar, integrates the sea into its landscape composition—coastal rocks are part of the garden. The Piano Museum, within the grounds, displays over 100 antique pianos from Europe and America, from the 17th to the 20th century.

Zhongshan Road, Xiamen's main shopping artery, stretches 1.2 km under 1920s-1930s colonial-style arcades (qilou). These covered galleries, typical of southern Fujian and influenced by Southeast Asian architecture, blend European and Chinese elements across three to five stories.

→

Seafood tasting at Xiamen harbor market. Fujian is renowned for oyster omelets (haolicuijian), clam vermicelli, and crab spring rolls. The market bustles in late afternoon.
