Sevilla

Seville Cathedral is the world's largest Gothic cathedral. Construction began in 1403 on the site of the former Almohad mosque, preserving the Patio de los Naranjos with its 60 orange trees. Inside, Christopher Columbus's tomb—repatriated from Havana—rests on the shoulders of four bronze bearers.

Climb the 35 ramps of La Giralda, a 104-meter minaret built in 1184 by architect Ahmad Ben Baso. Muezzins once rode horses up these ramps for the call to prayer. At the summit, El Giraldillo—a 4-meter bronze statue representing Faith—has been spinning in the wind since 1568.

The Royal Alcázar is Europe's oldest inhabited royal palace. In 1364, Peter I summoned Mudéjar craftsmen from Toledo, Granada, and Seville to create this masterpiece in just two years. Step through the doorway of the Patio de las Doncellas: its rectangular pool flanked by marble columns and intricate stucco rivals the Alhambra itself.

The Alcázar gardens spread across 7 hectares of palms, orange trees, and fountains. The Mercury Pool, the Dance Gardens, and the Grotesque Gallery unfold in welcome coolness. Summer concerts at 'Noches en los Jardines' extend the magic as dusk falls.

Lose yourself in the Barrio de Santa Cruz, the former Jewish quarter where lanes are so narrow two people barely pass. Patios reveal fountains, azulejo tiles, and bougainvillea cascading from wrought-iron balconies. Find your way to Plaza de los Venerables to discover the 17th-century baroque hospice.

The General Archive of the Indies occupies the former Lonja de Mercaderes, a 1598 Renaissance building. Its vaults hold 43,000 files and 80 million pages documenting four centuries of Spanish colonization. The building, cathedral, and Alcázar together form the UNESCO World Heritage site inscribed in 1987.

→

Catch a flamenco performance at a peña in the historic center. Seville is the birthplace of trianero style, born in the Triana neighborhood across the Guadalquivir.

→

Hop between tapas bars from Alfalfa to Alameda de Hércules. Seville keeps alive the tradition of free tapas with every drink: montaditos, espinacas con garbanzos, carrillada ibérica.

→

Cross the Puente de Isabel II toward Triana at sunset. The former quarter of potters, sailors, and gypsies offers the finest views of the Torre del Oro and La Giralda's silhouette gilded by slanting light.

Step into the Mezquita-Cathedral and let your eyes adjust to the half-light: 856 columns of granite, jasper, and marble support a double arcade of horseshoe and rounded arches. The alternating red brick and white stone creates an infinite forest across 23,400 square meters. Construction began in 786 under Abd al-Rahman I, on the ruins of the Visigothic Basilica of Saint Vincent.

Make your way to the mihrab at the back of the mosque: this octagonal prayer niche gleams with Byzantine mosaics sent by Emperor Nicephorus II at the request of Caliph Al-Hakam II in the 10th century. At the building's center, the major chapel erected in 1523 by architect Hernán Ruiz thrusts its Gothic vaults and baroque altarpiece into the forest of columns.

Cross the Roman Bridge over the Guadalquivir, 331 meters long and composed of 16 arches. In use since the 1st century BC, it offers the most iconic panorama of the Mezquita and the Puerta del Puente. The Calahorra Tower on the far bank houses a museum on the three cultures.

Dive into the Judería, the former Jewish quarter where whitewashed lanes twist around the mosque. The Calleja de las Flores—a narrow passage opening onto a flower-filled plaza with views of the Mezquita's bell tower—is Córdoba's most photographed street.

The Alcázar de los Reyes Cristianos served as the residence of the Catholic Monarchs for eight years as they prepared the reconquest of Granada. Its terraced gardens align pools, fountains, and cypress hedges across grounds that once rivaled those of the Alhambra.

Seek out the Chapel of San Bartolomé, hidden in the Judería's lanes. This jewel of early-15th-century Mudéjar architecture features ribbed vaults and faience-tiled walls in an intimate space that contrasts starkly with the Mezquita's grandeur.

→

Córdoba is Spain's capital of flowering patios. Wander the San Basilio neighborhood to admire these interior courtyards overflowing with geraniums, jasmine, and bougainvillea, inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list.

💡 Outside the festival, some patios remain open to the public
→

Treat yourself to an evening at the Arab baths. Córdoba counted more than 600 hammams during the caliphate era. Modern baths recreate the alternation of hot, warm, and cold rooms in a setting of arches and candlelight.

→

Taste salmorejo, a cold tomato-and-bread soup thicker than gazpacho—Córdoba's signature dish. Pair it with flamenquín, breaded pork rolls stuffed with serrano ham.

The Alhambra commands Granada from the Sabika hill. Start with the Alcazaba, the primitive citadel: from atop the Torre de la Vela, the panorama sweeps across the city, the fertile Vega plain, and the snow-capped peaks of the Sierra Nevada. Here in 1492, the Catholic Monarchs raised the cross and flag of Castile.

The Nasrid Palaces reach the summit of Hispano-Moorish art. In the Patio de los Arrayanes, a rectangular pool bordered by myrtles reflects the 45-meter-high Comares Tower. The Hall of Ambassadors, crowned by a cedar ceiling representing Islam's seven heavens, served as Yusuf I's throne room.

Cross into the Patio de los Leones, the Alhambra's most celebrated courtyard: 124 slender columns of white Macael marble encircle a fountain borne by 12 lions. Four channels symbolize the four rivers of Paradise. Mohammed V built this ensemble in the 14th century as his private residence.

The Generalife, the Nasrid sultans' summer residence, sprawls across the Cerro del Sol. Its Arabic name Jannat al-Arif means 'garden of the architect.' The Patio de la Acequia aligns its arching water jets above a long canal fed by Sierra Nevada snowmelt. Water is everywhere: channels, rills in the stair ramps, low fountains.

Follow the Carrera del Darro, considered Granada's most beautiful street, running along the Darro River at the foot of the Alhambra. The ancient Arab baths of El Bañuelo, dating from the 11th century, are among the best-preserved in Spain with their star-pierced vaults.

Climb to the Mirador de San Nicolás in the Albaicín for sunset. The Alhambra rises against the backdrop of the Sierra Nevada in golden light. The Albaicín itself—a UNESCO-listed Moorish quarter—is a labyrinth of steep lanes, small plazas, and carmenes, those garden-houses hidden behind high white walls.

→

Push open the door of a tetería on Calle Calderería Nueva, nicknamed 'tea street.' This steep Albaicín lane lines up craft shops and Moroccan tea rooms where you can savor mint tea and honey pastries.

→

Granada keeps alive the tradition of free tapas: every drink comes with a plate that grows with each round. The Navas quarter and the streets around Plaza Nueva concentrate the best bars.

→

Attend a zambra performance in a Sacromonte cave. This gypsy dance, heir to primitive flamenco, is performed in troglodyte dwellings carved into the hillside since the 15th century.

The Puente Nuevo spans the Tajo gorge 98 meters above the riverbed. Its construction lasted 42 years, from 1751 to 1793, under the direction of architects Domingo Lois de Monteagudo then José Martín de Aldehuela. The chamber at the bridge's center—once used as a prison during the Civil War—now houses an interpretation center.

El Tajo gorge plunges 120 meters deep over 500 meters of length, carved by the Guadalevín River. Descend to the Jardines de Cuenca, arranged in terraces on the cliff face, to photograph the bridge from below. The Mirador de Aldehuela offers the most spectacular perspective.

Ronda's Plaza de Toros, completed in 1785 by the same architect Aldehuela, is one of Spain's oldest bullrings. Its 68 arches and 136 pillars encircle a 5,000-seat arena. Here Pedro Romero codified the rules of modern bullfighting in the 18th century.

Cross through the old town, the Ciudad, to the Palacio de Mondragón. This 14th-century palace—former residence of the Moorish governor then the Catholic Monarchs—preserves three patios including a Mudéjar garden with plunging views over the valley.

The Church of Santa María la Mayor rises on the site of Ronda's former main mosque. Its minaret converted to a bell tower, its mihrab arch still visible in the wall, and its Renaissance balcony illustrate the layered history of this town perched at 750 meters altitude.

The Arab baths of Ronda, near the Puente Viejo, date from the 13th-14th centuries. Their barrel vaults pierced with stars of light and their three rooms (cold, warm, hot) make them the best-preserved Moorish baths on the Iberian Peninsula.

→

Take the Tajo trail at the base of the cliffs. The Camino de los Molinos descends to the Guadalevín riverbed passing former flour mills, with vertiginous views of the Puente Nuevo's arches and the waterfall.

→

The Serranía de Ronda has produced recognized wines since Roman times. Surrounding estates cultivate Syrah, Cabernet, and Garnacha between 700 and 900 meters altitude, yielding concentrated reds shaped by thermal amplitude.

💡 Several estates 15-20 min drive away
→

Settle in at the Paseo de Ernest Hemingway to watch the sunset over the gorges. The writer, passionate about bullfighting, spent the summer of 1959 in Ronda and immortalized the town in several of his writings.

Malaga

Málaga's Alcazaba, built between 1057 and 1063 by Berber king Badis of Granada, is nicknamed 'the little Alhambra.' Its three concentric enclosures blend Roman columns and capitals recycled from the neighboring theater with Nasrid patios adorned with pools and gardens. From the ramparts, the view embraces the port and the Mediterranean.

At the Alcazaba's foot, the Roman Theater dates from the 1st century under Emperor Augustus. Discovered by chance in 1951 after centuries of burial, it unfolds its cavea of 31 meters diameter and 16 meters height. The adjacent Interpretation Center traces the history of ancient Malaca.

Climb to the Castillo de Gibralfaro, connected to the Alcazaba by the fortified path of La Coracha. This 14th-century fortress, built by Nasrid sultan Yusuf I to protect the Alcazaba, offers Málaga's finest panorama: the city, port, bullring, and Mediterranean in 360 degrees.

The Picasso Museum occupies the Palacio de Buenavista, a 16th-century Renaissance building in the heart of the old town. Born at 36 Plaza de la Merced in 1881, Picasso lived his first ten years in Málaga. The museum presents over 200 works covering all his periods.

Málaga Cathedral, begun in 1528 on the former mosque, blends late Gothic, Renaissance, and baroque styles. Its south tower was never completed, earning it the affectionate nickname 'La Manquita' (the one-armed lady). The rooftop climb offers a bird's-eye view of the city.

Stroll down Calle Marqués de Larios, a pedestrian artery lined with Belle Époque buildings from the late 19th century. It opens onto Plaza de la Constitución, Málaga's beating heart since the 15th century. Stop at Café Central, an institution offering ten ways to prepare coffee.

→

Head to La Malagueta beach in the morning for a swim facing the port. The chiringuitos lining the sand serve espeto de sardinas—sardines grilled vertically over olive-wood fires, a quintessential Málaga tradition.

→

Wander along Muelle Uno, a promenade developed on the port docks. Between boutiques, restaurants, and the Centre Pompidou Málaga—the first outpost outside France, inaugurated in 2015—this modern waterfront contrasts with the historic city.

→

As evening falls, head to a rooftop in the center for a final drink with views of the illuminated Alcazaba, the port, and the Mediterranean at twilight.
