10 Natural Wonders Around Bologna

Rose gypsum hills, centuries-old beech forests, waterfalls and glacial lakes: the secret nature of Bologna's hinterland.

From Po plain wetlands to the Dardagna waterfalls on the flanks of Corno alle Scale, Bologna's hinterland delivers startling contrasts. Brisighella with its three rose-pink gypsum pinnacles, the Rocchetta Mattei and its Moorish castle lost in the Apennines, the haunting silence of Monte Sole—each stop pulls you farther from the red city and its porticoes, plunging deeper into unspoiled nature.

Key highlights (5)
  • Brisighella: village perched on three rose-pink gypsum spires, unique medieval covered street
  • Rocchetta Mattei: Moorish castle of the electrohomeopathic count in the Apennines
  • Monte Sole: forest and memory of Western Europe's largest civilian massacre in 1944
  • Cascate del Dardagna: seven successive cascades in the Bolognese Apennine beech forest
  • Dozza: medieval village with 80 outdoor murals, regional wine cellar in the rocca

Parco di Villa Ghigi - 28 hectares designated as an urban nature reserve on the first hills south of Bologna. The park preserves wildflower meadows dotted with native orchids, heritage fruit orchards (pears, almonds, figs), and hedgerows that have vanished from the flatlands below. The 18th-century villa was acquired by the municipality in 1965 and now serves as an environmental education center.

Due Torri - Bologna's two iconic medieval towers: the Torre degli Asinelli (97.2 m, built 1109-1119) and the Torre Garisenda (48 m, leaning 3.2 m off-plumb). In the 12th century Bologna bristled with over a hundred towers erected by noble families as symbols of power and prestige. Dante references the Torre Garisenda in the Divine Comedy (Inferno, XXXI). The Torre degli Asinelli is open for climbers: 498 wooden steps lead to a 360° panorama over the city.

Basilica di San Petronio - the fifth-largest church in the world by floor area (132 m long, 66 m wide), begun in 1390 and still unfinished: the facade wears its marble cladding only halfway up, as funding was diverted by the pope to build the university instead. Inside you'll find Giovanni Cassini's meridian line (1655), a 66.8 m solar trace across the floor—the world's longest when it was installed.

Portico di San Luca - the world's longest covered arcade: 3.8 km, 666 arches, linking Porta Saragozza to the hilltop sanctuary of the Madonna di San Luca at 289 m elevation. Built between 1674 and 1793 to shelter pilgrims during the annual procession. Inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2021 alongside Bologna's other porticoes.

→

Hike the waymarked trails through the Parco di Villa Ghigi, wandering among wildflower meadows and heritage orchards with clear-day views to the Contrafforte Pliocenico.

→

Walk up to the San Luca sanctuary via the portico—allow 45 minutes for the 3.8 km climb beneath the arches. On clear days, the terrace offers sweeping views across Bologna and the Po plain to the distant Alps.

→

Sample mortadella IGP and tagliatelle al ragù in the city's covered markets—the Mercato di Mezzo on Via Drapperie, housed in a medieval building, gathers Bologna's finest artisan producers under one roof.

Area Naturalistica La Bora - a 10-hectare wetland managed by the province of Bologna, featuring breeding ponds for the European pond turtle (Emys orbicularis), a protected species threatened with extinction across northern Italy. The site also hosts a center dedicated to amphibians and aquatic insects.

Oasi del Quadrone - a 270-hectare nature reserve established in 1985 by Italy's bird protection league (LIPU), one of Emilia-Romagna's first protected wildlife oases. The artificial wetland, surrounded by cereal fields and sunflowers, has recorded over 180 bird species, including little egrets and black-crowned night herons.

→

Follow the 3 km waymarked trail from the visitor center through diverse habitats: reed beds, wet meadows, and willow copses. Interpretive panels explain the wildlife and ecosystems.

Dozza

Rocca Sforzesca di Dozza - a 14th-century fortress perched atop a 190 m hill, remodeled by the Sforza family in the 15th century. The interior preserves period-furnished rooms and Renaissance frescoes. Since 1960, the rocca's cellars have housed the Enoteca Regionale Emilia Romagna, the region's official wine showcase with over 1,000 labels.

Centro storico and Muro Dipinto - since 1960, every two years artists from around the world paint the facades of Dozza's medieval houses during the Biennale del Muro Dipinto. The village now boasts over 80 outdoor murals, turning its alleyways into an open-air museum.

→

Taste Emilia-Romagna wines at the Enoteca Regionale in the rocca's vaulted cellars—Sangiovese, Albana DOCG, and Pignoletto are the regional flagships.

→

Wander the village lanes to discover the Biennale murals, from the earliest works dating back to the 1960s to the most recent additions.

Brisighella - a village perched on three selenite gypsum pinnacles in the Lamone valley, ranked among Italy's most beautiful villages. The three spires are crowned respectively by the Rocca (14th-century fortress), the Torre dell'Orologio (17th-century clock tower), and the Santuario del Monticino. The rose-pink gypsum rock lends the buildings their distinctive hue.

Via del Borgo (Via degli Asini) - a 14th-century covered medieval street built one story above street level, which allowed donkeys laden with gypsum to pass sheltered from the weather. Unique in Emilia-Romagna, it connects Piazza Carducci to the medieval quarter.

Parco della Vena del Gesso Romagnola - a regional park protecting Italy's longest continuous gypsum outcrop: 25 km of ridges between Imola and Faenza. The karst massif, formed 6 million years ago during the desiccation of the Mediterranean Sea (Messinian salinity crisis), harbors caves, sinkholes, and rare rock-dwelling flora.

Grotta Tanaccia - a 500 m karst cave accessible from Brisighella, inhabited during the Bronze Age (archaeological remains date to the 2nd millennium BC). The translucent selenite gypsum formations create crystals unlike anything found in ordinary limestone caves.

→

Hike the CAI trail 503 along the Vena del Gesso ridge—plunging views over the Senio and Lamone valleys, passages between rose-pink gypsum cliffs.

→

Sample Brisighella DOP olive oil—produced on the clay-gypsum hills of the Lamone valley, this fruity, slightly bitter oil is one of Emilia-Romagna's rare protected designations.

Ponte Alidosi - a humpback medieval bridge spanning the Santerno, built in 1499 by Giovanni II Alidosi in a single 41 m arch. Considered one of Emilia-Romagna's best-preserved medieval bridges, it served the Via Emilia montana linking Bologna to Florence for five centuries.

Palazzo Alidosi - a 16th-century Renaissance palace, seat of the Alidosi family who ruled the Santerno valley for two centuries. Houses the Museo della Civiltà del Castagno, dedicated to the history of local chestnut groves, and the Museo della Linea Gotica, documenting the 1944-1945 fighting across the Bolognese Apennines.

Castagneti di Castel del Rio - centuries-old chestnut groves producing the Castagna di Castel del Rio IGP, awarded in 2009. The chestnuts, some over 300 years old, blanket the Apennine slopes between 400 and 900 m altitude. Historically nicknamed 'l'albero del pane' (the bread tree), they sustained mountain communities for centuries.

→

Walk the waymarked trails through the chestnut groves around the village—in autumn (October-November), you can watch the harvest and some producers open their traditional drying sheds to visitors.

Parco Storico di Monte Sole - a 6,300-hectare park between the Reno and Setta valleys, established in 1989 as a memorial to the Marzabotto massacre. Between September 29 and October 5, 1944, SS troops from the 16th Panzergrenadier Division murdered 770 civilians (women, children, elderly) in the Monte Sole villages—the largest massacre of civilians in Western Europe during World War II. The devastated villages of Marzabotto, Caprara, and Casaglia are preserved as ruins.

→

Walk the 8 km waymarked memorial trail linking the park's ruined villages, with interpretive panels at massacre sites. The silence of the oak and chestnut forest that has reclaimed the ruins makes the visit particularly haunting.

Rocchetta Mattei - an eclectic castle built from 1850 onward by Count Cesare Mattei (1809-1896) atop the ruins of a medieval fortress at 370 m elevation. Mattei, inventor of electrohomeopathy (an alternative medicine he marketed worldwide), financed the construction of this residence-laboratory for 46 years. The architecture blends medieval Gothic, Moorish style (domes, horseshoe arches, Hispano-Moorish ceramics), and Renaissance elements in a labyrinth of 60 rooms.

Museo Giorgio Morandi a Grizzana - the Bolognese painter Giorgio Morandi (1890-1964), famous for his still lifes of bottles and carafes, spent every summer in Grizzana from 1913 to 1964. The municipal museum presents reproductions of his works and documents his relationship with the Bolognese Apennine landscape that influenced his earthy color palette.

→

Take a guided tour of Rocchetta Mattei—the route winds through decorated halls, the Moorish room with Spanish ceramics, the neo-Gothic chapel, and terraces with views over the Reno valley.

→

Hike the ridge trails around Grizzana with views across the Reno and Setta valleys—the wooded hillscape that inspired Morandi for half a century.

Lago di Suviana and Lago del Brasimone - two artificial lakes created in the 1920s-1930s for hydroelectric power in the Bolognese Apennine valleys. Lake Suviana (capacity 32 million m³) is ringed by beech and conifer forests between 500 and 900 m altitude. Lake Brasimone, smaller, is managed as a nature reserve with restricted access to protect aquatic wildlife.

→

Swimming and boating on Lake Suviana from the Bargi beaches—canoes and pedalos in summer, forest trail hiking in any season.

Terme di Porretta - a spa town in the upper Reno valley, whose sulfurous waters have been known since antiquity and exploited in organized fashion since the 14th century. The current spa buildings, built in late-19th and early-20th-century liberty style, offer treatments for respiratory and digestive ailments. The town developed as a resort for the Bolognese bourgeoisie from 1864 onward with the opening of the Porrettana railway line.

→

Take the waters at the Porretta spa—treatments using sulfurous-saline-bromine-iodine waters recommended for respiratory conditions, open March to November.

→

Discover the Porretta Soul Festival—since 1959, every July Porretta Terme has hosted a soul, rhythm and blues, and gospel festival that drew artists including James Brown, Otis Redding, and Wilson Pickett. The festival, born from a local spa doctor's passion for African-American music, is now one of Europe's oldest soul festivals.

→

Hike the waymarked trails through the Reno valley from Porretta—chestnut and oak forests, perched villages, and views to the Apennine ridge.

Santuario della Madonna del Faggio - a hermitage built in the 17th century in the Bolognese Apennine beech forests at 1,150 m elevation, according to legend at the spot where woodcutters discovered a terracotta image of the Virgin beneath a great beech tree. The 4 km access path from Lizzano passes through dense beech forest.

Castello Manservisi - a 19th-century neo-Gothic castle overlooking the village of Lizzano, restored and open for visits. It preserves works by the local painter Giacomo Lolli (1859-1940), including landscapes of the Bolognese Apennines.

→

Hike to the Santuario della Madonna del Faggio from Lizzano—4 km through beech forest with 300 m elevation gain.

Parco Regionale del Corno alle Scale - a 5,000-hectare regional park around Corno alle Scale (1,945 m), the Bolognese Apennines' highest peak. The name comes from the sandstone strata that form natural steps (scale) on the summit's flanks. The park shelters primary beech forests, alpine meadows, and peat bogs, with fauna including red deer, roe deer, and peregrine falcon.

Cascate del Dardagna - a series of seven successive waterfalls along the Dardagna stream in a beech forest at 950 m elevation. The main cascade plunges 35 m over sandstone ledges before forming a natural pool. Reachable on foot from the Pianaccio parking in 30 minutes.

Lago Scaffaiolo - a glacial lake at 1,790 m elevation on the ridge between Emilia-Romagna and Tuscany, one of the Apennines' highest lakes. Known since medieval times for its sudden storms, it was considered an evil place by local populations. The adjacent CAI refuge is open June to September.

→

Hike to the Cascate del Dardagna from Pianaccio—an easy 3 km round-trip trail through beech forest along the stream to the seven cascades.

→

Climb Corno alle Scale from the Rifugio Cavone—a 5 km hike with 600 m elevation gain, 360° views across the Apennines, the Po plain, and on clear days the distant Alps to the north.

→

Extend to Lago Scaffaiolo from Corno alle Scale—a 2 km extension from the summit along the ridge to reach the glacial lake at 1,790 m.
