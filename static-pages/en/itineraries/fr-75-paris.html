Louvre Museum — Former medieval fortress that became a royal palace then museum in 1793, the Louvre houses over 35,000 works across 60,600 m². The glass pyramid designed by I.M. Pei in 1989 marks the entrance to this temple of art where the Mona Lisa by Leonardo da Vinci, the Venus de Milo and the Winged Victory of Samothrace reign.

Tuileries Garden — Created by Catherine de' Medici in 1564 then redesigned by Le Nôtre in 1664, this French formal garden extends over 23 hectares between the Louvre and Place de la Concorde. Sculptures by Maillol and Rodin punctuate the paths lined with century-old chestnut trees.

Place Vendôme — Jewel of elegance designed by Hardouin-Mansart in 1699, this octagonal square with classical facades houses the world's greatest jewelers. The central column, cast from bronze of 1,200 cannons captured at Austerlitz, rises to 44 meters.

→

Palais Royal — Pass through the arcades to discover Buren's columns in the courtyard of honor and the secret gardens surrounded by galleries where antique dealers and fashion boutiques line up. This haven of peace was the theater of the beginnings of the French Revolution.

→

Comédie-Française — Founded in 1680 by Louis XIV, the world's oldest theater company perpetuates French dramatic art. Admire the neoclassical facade of the Salle Richelieu where Molière performed his final shows.

→

Rue de Rivoli Arcades — Stroll under the 2.5 km of Napoleonic arcades lined with shops and cafés. This monumental perspective offers protection from the weather while revealing the uniform facades desired by Percier and Fontaine.

Notre-Dame Cathedral — Masterpiece of French Gothic built between 1163 and 1345, the cathedral reopened its doors in December 2024 after five years of restoration following the 2019 fire. Its 12,000 oak timbers of the new framework, its spire rebuilt identical to Viollet-le-Duc's and its restored 13th-century rose windows once again dazzle visitors.

Île de la Cité — Historical cradle of Paris where the Parisii settled in the 3rd century BC, this island houses the vestiges of royal and religious power. Stroll along the Quai des Orfèvres immortalized by Simenon and discover the century-old flower market.

Sainte-Chapelle — Jewel of Rayonnant Gothic erected in just seven years (1241-1248) by Saint Louis to house the Crown of Thorns. Its 1,113 m² of 13th-century stained glass, among the oldest in Paris, diffuse celestial light depicting 1,113 biblical scenes.

→

Place des Vosges — Paris's oldest square inaugurated in 1612, this perfect 140-meter square charms with its red brick and blonde stone pavilions. Victor Hugo lived there for six years at No. 6, now a free museum dedicated to the writer.

→

Centre Pompidou — Cathedral of modern and contemporary art conceived by Renzo Piano and Richard Rogers, inaugurated in 1977. Its "inside-out" architecture exposes colored pipes and escalators on the facade, housing Europe's largest modern art collection.

→

Musée d'Orsay — Former train station transformed into a temple of Impressionism, the museum displays masterpieces by Monet, Renoir, Van Gogh and Cézanne under a monumental glass roof. The great clock offers a unique view of Montmartre.

Bois de Vincennes — Paris's largest green space with 995 hectares, this royal forest landscaped by Haussmann contains four lakes, a racetrack, a velodrome and the award-winning Parc Floral. Its bridle paths and bike trails offer a natural escape at the gates of Paris.

Château de Vincennes — Medieval fortress dominated by Europe's tallest keep (52 meters), this royal castle served as residence to seven kings of France before Versailles. The 14th-century Sainte-Chapelle, replica of Paris's, preserves remarkable Flamboyant Gothic stained glass.

Bercy Village — Former 19th-century wine warehouses converted into a commercial and cultural village, this pedestrian district with original cobblestones houses cinemas, restaurants and boutiques under century-old plane trees. Frank Gehry's Cinémathèque française completes the ensemble.

→

Coulée Verte René-Dumont — World's first elevated linear park (1993) built on the former Bastille railway line, this 4.7 km tree-lined promenade crosses the 12th arrondissement at 10 meters height before descending to the Bois de Vincennes.

→

Parc Floral de Paris — This 35-hectare botanical garden in the heart of the Bois de Vincennes features over 3,000 plant species around a central pond. Its seasonal flower exhibitions and summer jazz festival make it unmissable.

→

Bastille Quarter — This historic crossroads where the fortress was stormed on July 14, 1789 now vibrates to the rhythm of trendy bars and clubs on rue de Lappe. Carlos Ott's Opéra Bastille and Port de l'Arsenal add a contemporary cultural touch.

Père-Lachaise Cemetery — Paris's largest necropolis at 44 hectares established in 1804, this garden cemetery with romantic paths houses the graves of Jim Morrison, Édith Piaf, Oscar Wilde, Chopin and Molière. Its neo-Gothic chapels and Art Nouveau mausoleums make it an open-air museum.

Parc de Belleville — This 4.5-hectare park built in 1988 on Paris's highest hill (108 meters) offers an exceptional panorama of the capital from its belvedere terrace. Its waterfalls, playground and Maison de l'Air make it a popular green lung.

Ménilmontant Quarter — This working-class village absorbed by Paris in 1860 preserves its secret passages, artists' studios and authentic bistros. The walls covered in street art and the guinguettes of Parc des Buttes perpetuate its bohemian and creative spirit.

→

Rue Oberkampf — Trendy artery of eastern Paris, this street with colorful facades lines up cocktail bars, world cuisine restaurants and concert venues. In the evening, its giant terrace invades the sidewalks for a festive and cosmopolitan atmosphere.

→

Street Art in Belleville — The streets of Belleville form an open-air gallery where the greatest names in world street art express themselves. Rue Dénoyez with entirely covered walls and murals on Boulevard de Belleville constantly renew this free urban museum.

→

Place Gambetta and 20th arrondissement town hall — This working-class neighborhood with village charm is organized around Place Gambetta and its Second Empire town hall. The Belleville market, one of Paris's largest and cheapest, animates its streets on Tuesdays and Fridays.

Sacré-Cœur Basilica — Built between 1875 and 1914 in Romano-Byzantine style by Paul Abadie, this white stone basilica from Château-Landon rises to 130 meters altitude on Montmartre hill. Its 475 m² mosaic of Christ in Glory is one of the world's largest. Since 1885, perpetual prayer has been maintained day and night.

Village of Montmartre — Former wine-growing village annexed to Paris in 1860, Montmartre has preserved its cobbled lanes, secret stairways and bohemian atmosphere. Picasso, Van Gogh and Toulouse-Lautrec lived there, immortalizing its windmills and cabarets in their works.

Place du Tertre — Artistic heart of Montmartre since the 19th century, this cobbled square gathers painters and portrait artists under umbrellas. Despite tourist crowds, it retains the charm of Belle Époque studios where Utrillo painted white facades.

Montmartre Vineyards — Last wine-growing vestige of Paris planted in 1933 on 1,556 m², this vineyard produces about 500 bottles of Clos Montmartre each year during festive October harvest. A reminder of the wine-growing tradition that once covered the entire hill.

→

Moulin Rouge — Founded in 1889, this legendary red-windmill cabaret saw the birth of the French cancan and hosted Toulouse-Lautrec who immortalized its dancers. Its feathered revues perpetuate the tradition of Parisian music hall in Pigalle.

→

Fondation Louis Vuitton — Glass vessel conceived by Frank Gehry and inaugurated in 2014 in the heart of the Bois de Boulogne, this contemporary art museum unfolds twelve translucent sails over 46 meters high. Its terraces offer panoramic views of La Défense and Paris.

→

Palais de Tokyo — Europe's largest contemporary art center with 22,000 m², this 1937 Art Deco palace programs bold exhibitions and performances until midnight. Its Monsieur Bleu restaurant facing the Eiffel Tower attracts creative Paris.

Avenue des Champs-Élysées — Nicknamed "the most beautiful avenue in the world," this 1.9 km artery connecting Concorde to the Arc de Triomphe was laid out by Le Nôtre in 1667. Its wide sidewalks planted with chestnut trees host July 14th military parades and the Tour de France finish.

Arc de Triomphe — Commissioned by Napoleon in 1806, this 50-meter-high monument celebrates the victories of the Grande Armée. The Tomb of the Unknown Soldier's flame has burned there since 1923, rekindled each evening at 6:30 pm. The panoramic terrace offers a 360° view of the twelve radiating avenues.

Grand Palais — Built for the 1900 Universal Exhibition, this Beaux-Arts palace features Europe's largest glass roof covering 13,500 m². Its 45-meter-high nave hosts major international exhibitions and fashion shows from the great houses.

Petit Palais — Harmonious counterpart to the Grand Palais, this City of Paris Fine Arts Museum presents collections from Antiquity to 1914 for free. Its peristyle inner garden, inspired by Pompeii, offers an unexpected bucolic break.

→

Église de la Madeleine — Neoclassical temple surrounded by 52 Corinthian columns 20 meters high, this church without bell tower or cross was desired by Napoleon as a temple to the glory of the Grande Armée. Its richly decorated interior hosts concerts and prestigious ceremonies.

→

Musée de Cluny — Set in a 15th-century medieval mansion and 3rd-century Gallo-Roman baths, this National Museum of the Middle Ages preserves the famous Lady and the Unicorn tapestry, six 15th-century Flemish tapestries illustrating the five senses and a mysterious sixth.

→

Arènes de Lutèce — Vestige of the 1st-century Gallo-Roman amphitheater that could hold 15,000 spectators, these arenas rediscovered in 1869 during Haussmann's works now form a peaceful garden where Parisians play pétanque.

Trocadéro — This monumental esplanade facing the Eiffel Tower offers the most beautiful viewpoint on the Iron Lady from the Palais de Chaillot gardens. Its Trocadéro cascading fountains, created for the 1937 Exhibition, illuminate at dusk in a water show.

Musée Marmottan Monet — This 19th-century private mansion houses the world's largest collection of Claude Monet works, including Impression, Sunrise that gave its name to the Impressionist movement. The monumental Water Lilies and Berthe Morisot collection complete this unique ensemble.

Bois de Boulogne — Former 846-hectare royal hunting ground landscaped by Haussmann, this green lung of western Paris contains two romantic lakes, the Longchamp racecourse, the Auteuil greenhouses and the Bagatelle rose garden with 10,000 rose bushes.

→

Serres d'Auteuil — This 1898 botanical garden houses five tropical greenhouses where palms, orchids and exotic plants thrive under Art Nouveau glass roofs. The Japanese garden with koi carp and century-old azaleas offers an unexpected zen break.

→

Musée de l'Homme — Set in the Passy wing of the Palais de Chaillot, this museum renovated in 2015 explores human evolution through 700,000 years of history. Its ethnographic and prehistoric collections coexist with reflections on our species' future.

→

Cité de l'Architecture — World's largest architecture center set in the Palais de Chaillot, this museum presents life-size casts of Romanesque and Gothic portals, models of radiant cities and reconstituted Le Corbusier witness apartment.

Eiffel Tower — Built in 2 years, 2 months and 5 days by Gustave Eiffel for the 1889 Universal Exhibition, this 330-meter, 7,300-ton "Iron Lady" of puddled steel assembled by 2.5 million rivets was meant to be dismantled after 20 years. Saved by its usefulness for wireless telegraphy, it now welcomes nearly 7 million visitors per year.

Champ-de-Mars — This 780-meter esplanade extends from the Eiffel Tower to the Military School where Bonaparte studied. Former parade ground, this English-style garden hosted five Universal Exhibitions and remains the preferred spot for Parisian picnics facing the Iron Lady.

Hôtel des Invalides — Commissioned by Louis XIV in 1670 to house wounded soldiers, this masterpiece by Libéral Bruant then Hardouin-Mansart houses under its 107-meter golden dome Napoleon I's tomb, in six nested coffins of red quartzite.

→

Musée Rodin — Set in the 18th-century Hôtel Biron where the artist lived, this museum presents The Thinker, The Kiss and The Burghers of Calais in a verdant setting. The sculpture garden is among Paris's most romantic.

→

Musée du Quai Branly — Designed by Jean Nouvel and inaugurated in 2006, this museum of indigenous arts houses 300,000 objects from Africa, Asia, Oceania and the Americas. Its 800 m² plant wall signed by Patrick Blanc makes it a unique vertical garden.

→

Place de la Concorde — Paris's largest square where Louis XVI was guillotined in 1793, it displays the Luxor obelisk offered by Egypt in 1836. Its monumental fountains and eight statues representing great French cities frame a unique perspective toward the Champs-Élysées.

Saint-Germain-des-Prés — Paris's oldest church founded in 543 by Childebert I, this neighborhood was the heart of postwar existentialism. Jazz caves, art galleries and antique dealers perpetuate the bohemian atmosphere dear to Boris Vian and Juliette Gréco.

Saint-Sulpice Church — Paris's second-largest church after Notre-Dame, this 17th-century monument impresses with its asymmetrical facade and two unfinished towers. Its astronomical gnomon and Delacroix frescoes in the Chapel of the Holy Angels fascinate visitors.

Historic Cafés — Café de Flore and Les Deux Magots face each other on Boulevard Saint-Germain, witnesses to 20th-century intellectual ferment. Their heated terraces under red umbrellas perpetuate the tradition of the Parisian philosophical café.

→

Canal Saint-Martin — This romantic 4.5 km canal dug under Napoleon I crosses northeast Paris through nine locks and two swing bridges. Its tree-lined banks, immortalized in the film Amélie, attract picnickers and pétanque players.

→

Parc des Buttes-Chaumont — This 25-hectare park landscaped by Haussmann in 1867 on former gypsum quarries offers Paris's unique steep terrain. Its Temple of the Sibyl perched on a 50-meter rock dominates the lake and artificial waterfalls.

→

Montmartre Cemetery — Third-largest Parisian necropolis, this romantic cemetery with shaded paths houses the graves of Dalida, François Truffaut, Edgar Degas and Émile Zola. A peaceful haven overlooked by the Caulaincourt Bridge.

Latin Quarter — Paris's intellectual heart since the Sorbonne's founding in 1257, this neighborhood owes its name to Latin spoken by medieval students. Its cobbled lanes lined with bookshops, art cinemas and bistros perpetuate the bohemian and rebellious spirit of May '68.

Panthéon — Commissioned by Louis XV in 1744 as Sainte-Geneviève church then transformed into a republican mausoleum in 1791, this neoclassical temple by Soufflot houses the remains of Victor Hugo, Voltaire, Rousseau, Marie Curie, Jean Moulin and Simone Veil. Foucault's pendulum demonstrated Earth's rotation there in 1851.

La Sorbonne — Founded in 1257 by Robert de Sorbon to train theologians, this legendary university rebuilt under the Third Republic preserves its 17th-century chapel where Cardinal Richelieu rests. Its amphitheaters have seen the greatest thinkers pass through.

Luxembourg Gardens — Created by Marie de' Medici in 1612 to accompany her Florentine palace, this 25-hectare garden blends French and English styles. Its iconic metal chairs, pond where small sailboats glide and statues of French queens make it Parisians' favorite green lung.

→

Latin Quarter Bookshops — Explore the legendary Shakespeare and Company facing Notre-Dame, Gibert Joseph and its seven floors, or the bouquinistes along the Seine quays listed as UNESCO heritage. These temples of books perpetuate the neighborhood's literary tradition.

→

Boulevard Saint-Germain — This 3 km Haussmannian artery crosses the intellectual heart of the Left Bank where Sartre and Beauvoir philosophized at Café de Flore and Deux Magots. Antique dealers, galleries and publishing houses perpetuate this cultural heritage.

→

Théâtre de l'Odéon — France's oldest theater inaugurated in 1782, this Italian-style neoclassical hall hosted the premieres of Beaumarchais's The Marriage of Figaro. Now Théâtre de l'Europe, it programs contemporary creations and great classics.
