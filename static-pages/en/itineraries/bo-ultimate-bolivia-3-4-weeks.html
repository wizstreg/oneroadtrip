  • La Paz: vertiginous chaos of the Andean capital
  • Sucre: white colonial city and cradle of independence
  • Torotoro: dinosaur footprints and secret canyons
  • Rurrenabaque: Amazon jungle and pink dolphins of the pampas
  • Tarija: high-altitude wines and Andalusian flavors of Bolivia

Plaza 24 de Septiembre is the historic heart of Santa Cruz, named after the September 24, 1810 independence uprising. Its two-story colonial arcades, palm trees, and café terraces make it the beating pulse of local life.

The Metropolitan Cathedral Basilica of San Lorenzo towers over the plaza with its twin 40-meter ochre brick towers. Built in the 16th century, its carved wooden vaults and silver-adorned altar make it the city's most remarkable religious monument.

A ten-minute walk from the plaza, Parque El Arenal is a green haven surrounding a natural pond. A massive mural by Bolivian painter Lorgio Vaca traces the history of eastern Bolivia's civilizations.

The 186-hectare Botanical Garden preserves the flora of the eastern tropical lowlands. Two distinct forests—subtropical and Chaco—coexist here. Ecological trails wind past a lagoon where capybaras, caimans, and over 200 bird species thrive.

The Noël Kempff Mercado Natural History Museum celebrates eastern Bolivian biodiversity. It displays tens of thousands of plant specimens, insects, and fossils, plus an aquarium showcasing local aquatic species.

→

Dinner in the Equipetrol neighborhood, the epicenter of Santa Cruz nightlife and gastronomy. Terraces come alive in the evening with camba specialties: majadito (dried beef, rice, fried eggs, and plantains) and sopa de maní (peanut soup).

💡 Equipetrol is in the 4th district, take a taxi from downtown
→

Catch a peña performance at one of the bars on Plaza 24 de Septiembre. Camba music, typical of Bolivia's eastern lowlands, blends Guaraní, Spanish, and Creole influences.

→

Explore Lomas de Arena or La Rinconada parks on the city's outskirts. The first offers sand dunes surrounded by humid Chaco forest; the second features an artificial lake, natural waterfalls, and local wildlife.

💡 Go in the morning to beat the tropical heat

Santiago De Chiquitos

+2

The Jesuit mission church of Santiago de Chiquitos, founded in 1754, was the last mission established in the Chiquitania region. It holds the oldest bells in the entire area. Restored three times, it preserves numerous objects and ornaments left by the Jesuit fathers.

En route from Santa Cruz, the village of Chochis demands a stop. Dominated by the spectacular Cerro Chochis, this village is ringed by remarkable volcanic formations. The Sanctuary of Virgen Asunta, designed by architect Hans Roth—the same who restored the missions—houses a beautiful collection of wood carvings.

The Tucavaca Valley Nature Reserve protects the Bosque Seco Chiquitano, a unique dry tropical forest in South America. Santiago serves as the gateway: about a dozen marked trails start from the village.

→

Morning hike in the Tucavaca reserve. From a pass overlooking the village, climb through forest to spot toucans, red tegus, and vicuñas. The round-trip walk takes about two hours.

💡 Start early for wildlife, especially toucans
→

Swim in the natural hot springs of Río Aguas Calientes, 15 km from Santiago. These warm waters nestled in the forest are a favorite relaxation spot for locals.

💡 Vehicle access, track passable in dry season
→

Los Arcos y Cuevas circuit: a full day of trekking through unusual geological formations, caves, and rock paintings depicting animals. This circuit departs from Santiago with a local guide.

El Fuerte de Samaipata is the region's crown jewel, UNESCO World Heritage since 1998. On this hill 10 km from the village, an immense carved sandstone slab—the largest in the world—reveals seats, basins, ritual channels, pumas, and serpents hewn into stone. The site was successively occupied by the Chané, the Incas, and the Spanish.

The village archaeological museum displays artifacts excavated from El Fuerte and traces the major phases of regional occupation. Ideally visit before El Fuerte to better understand the site.

From Samaipata, a winding road leads to the entrance of Amboró National Park. This park, at the crossroads of three ecosystems—the Andes, the Amazon, and the Chaco—is one of Bolivia's most biodiverse zones.

→

Guided hike in Amboró National Park: giant ferns several meters tall, wild orchids, waterfalls in cloud forest. Local guides offer circuits from 3 to 6 hours depending on the season.

→

High-altitude wine tasting at Bodega Uvairenda. This 6-hectare estate produces whites, reds, and rosés vinified at 1,750 m. The vineyard tour takes 30 minutes, followed by a tasting.

💡 3 km from town center, accessible by taxi or on foot
→

Wander the cobblestone streets of the village. Samaipata—'resting place in the heights' in Quechua—is a bohemian village alive with artisan workshops, small cafés, and galleries. The atmosphere contrasts sharply with Santa Cruz's frenzy.

💡 On weekends, the main square comes alive with a farmers' market

Carreras Pampa, just outside the village, is the park's major dinosaur footprint site. This vast clay plain holds hundreds of fossilized tracks from the Late Cretaceous—about 65 million years ago. Guides decode the species represented and hunting scenarios.

Ciudad de Itas is a natural labyrinth of limestone blocks up to 20 meters high, eroded into animal or fantastical shapes at 3,700 m altitude. Rock paintings are visible in several caves at the site.

Umajalanta Cave, Bolivia's deepest with 7 km of galleries, is open only for its first 500 meters. You progress between stalactites, stalagmites, and narrow passages to reach an underground river inhabited by blind catfish, an endemic species.

Torotoro Canyon, nicknamed El Vergel, opens for 14 km. From viewpoints, the amphitheater of stratified rocks reveals millions of years of Andean geology. The waterfall at the canyon's base requires descending 800 steps—and climbing back up.

→

Hike to the condor viewpoint. This balcony suspended above the canyon allows you to observe the Ara rubrogenys—red-fronted parrot, the species for which the park was created in 1989—and condors gliding on thermals.

💡 Binoculars recommended
→

Swim in the crystal-clear waters of El Vergel waterfall at the canyon's bottom. The effort of the descent (800 steps) is amply rewarded by this natural pool bordered by cliffs.

💡 Bring food and water—no facilities at the bottom
→

Observe the Ara rubrogenys in the valley. This bright red-fronted parrot, endemic to Bolivian valleys, is endangered, and Torotoro is one of the rare places where you can observe it in the wild.

💡 Stay quiet—parrots flee at the slightest noise

Sucre

Plaza 25 de Mayo is the heart of Sucre. The 16th-century metropolitan cathedral and immaculate colonial buildings frame it. In the evening, locals gather here in traditional or modern dress—an authentic scene of Bolivian life not to be missed.

Casa de la Libertad is Bolivia's most important historical monument. In the chapel of this former Jesuit monastery built in 1621, Bolivia's independence was proclaimed on August 6, 1825. The Independence Hall preserves the original act, portraits of the liberators, and two of Bolívar's swords. The guided tour is essential to grasp the 50 presidents of the Republic parading across the walls of the next room.

San Felipe Neri church lets you climb onto the city's rooftops. From there, the panorama of ochre tile roofs, white bell towers, and green hills surrounding Sucre is exceptional—one of the best views of the city.

La Recoleta convent overlooks the southern heights of the city. Its colonial galleries, flowered patios, and a 1,400-year-old cedar tree proclaimed a Bolivian historical monument make it a haven of calm. The Recoleta viewpoint on Pedro de Anzúrez plaza offers a spectacular sunset view.

The ASUR Museum (Textile Art and Culture) displays hand-woven textiles by indigenous communities of the Chuquisaca region. Each piece tells a cosmology: the geometric Jalq'a motifs from the north represent chaos of the underworld; those of the Tarabuco to the south illustrate victory over the conquistadors.

Cal Orck'o Cretaceous Park, 5 km from downtown, displays 5,000 dinosaur footprints on an 80-meter-high vertical limestone wall. It's the world's largest deposit of dinosaur tracks, with 294 species identified.

→

Tarabuco artisan market, 64 km from Sucre, every Sunday morning. Yampara communities sell their textiles with unique warrior motifs, musical instruments, and traditional dishes. Arrive early to see peasants in traditional costume coming down from the villages.

💡 Allow 1.5h travel from Sucre
→

Hike to the Maragua crater in the Cordillera de los Frailes, 50 km from Sucre. A full day's walk (7h round trip) leads to the edge of this spectacular geological crater, among corn fields, dinosaur tracks, and Andean landscapes.

→

Gastronomic stroll through Sucre's markets. Mercado Central offers salteñas (meat-filled pastries with olives) from 7am, then full Bolivian almuerzos at noon. Don't miss tropical valley fruits and Singani, the national grape brandy.

Potosi

+4
Top hotels

9.1 VIRREYES HOTEL

Arriving from Sucre (2,790 m) to Potosí (4,070 m): take time to acclimatize. The 1,300 m climb in 2 hours can trigger soroche (altitude sickness). Rest the first two hours at your hotel, drink coca tea, and avoid any physical effort before afternoon.

Casa Nacional de la Moneda was built in 1572 to mint silver coins for the Spanish Empire. Its colonial wooden machinery, heavy mills, and baroque sacred art galleries form one of South America's richest museums. Between 1545 and 1810, Potosí's mines produced some 150,000 tons of silver. The guided tour lasts 2 hours.

Cerro Rico (Rich Mountain) towers over Potosí at 4,782 m. Its flank is riddled with tunnel entrances—miners still work there today extracting tin and zinc in the same galleries dug since 1545. At night, the small lights from miners' lamps slowly descend the mountain.

San Lorenzo church (18th century) is the jewel of Andean baroque in Potosí. Its pink carved stone façade blends indigenous and Christian motifs with exceptionally rich detail. From the terraces of neighboring San Francisco convent, the view of Cerro Rico and the old town's colorful rooftops is striking.

Santa Teresa convent, founded in 1685, opens for guided visits. Its silent cloisters, colonial sacred art collections—including a Virgin by Murillo—and gilded altars evoke Potosí's extreme wealth at its peak.

→

Guided tour of Cerro Rico's active mines. Enter the galleries where miners still extract tin and zinc under extreme conditions. Before descending, stop at the miners' market to buy offerings for the workers: coca leaves, dynamite, cane juice. Inside, meet El Tío, the demonic protective figure of the underworld.

💡 Wear clothes you don't mind getting dirty
→

Stroll through the UNESCO-listed historic center. Cobbled streets, colonial houses with carved wooden balconies, and 36 baroque churches testify to the city's 16th-century splendor, when Potosí had 160,000 inhabitants—as many as Paris or London at the time.

💡 Take a local guide for hidden historical anecdotes
→

Thermal bath at Ojo del Inca, a natural hot spring a few kilometers from downtown. A welcome break after physical efforts at 4,000 m altitude.

💡 Ideal late afternoon for recovery

The train cemetery lies 3 km from Uyuni. About fifty early 20th-century locomotives and wagons rust on abandoned tracks. These trains hauled minerals from Andean mines to Chilean ports—a grand Bolivian railway network that never came to be.

In Colchani, a village at the salt flat's edge, artisans work salt extracted from the flats: decorative objects, salt slabs, raw furniture. A small artisan refinery explains the salt transformation process. Just ahead, the Ojos de Sal—underground water springs piercing the crust—reveal geothermal activity beneath the desert.

Incahuasi Island rises from the absolute white of the salar at its center. A remnant of a volcanic formation covered in petrified coral, it's colonized by candelabra cacti 10 to 12 meters tall. From the summit viewpoint, the 360° view over the salt immensity is breathtaking.

Salar de Uyuni spans 10,582 km²—the world's largest salt desert—at 3,663 m altitude. Its white crust, formed about 10,000 years ago when an inland sea dried up, conceals beneath its halite polygons 40% of the world's lithium reserves.

→

3-day 4x4 circuit through the salar and Sud Lipez. Day 1: train cemetery, Colchani, Incahuasi, night in salt hotel. Day 2: Laguna Colorada and its flamingos, Arbol de Piedra, Sol de Mañana geysers, Polques hot springs. Day 3: Laguna Verde and Dali desert, return to Uyuni.

💡 Dry season (May–Nov) for firm crust; rainy season (Jan–Mar) for mirror effect
→

Perspective photography on the salar. The perfectly flat surface tricks the eye: play with miniature objects in the background to create optical illusions. The best angles are in late afternoon when low light tints the white with pink.

💡 Bring polarized sunglasses—salt reflection is intense
→

Stargazing from the salar on a dark night. At 3,663 m altitude with no light pollution, the Milky Way is visible to the naked eye. New moon nights between May and September offer the best conditions.

💡 Dress warmly—nights can drop below -10°C

Plaza Luis de Fuentes, named after the city's founder (1574), is the center of Tarija life. Its palm trees, terrace cafés, and white-façaded colonial buildings breathe a serenity that contrasts with the bustle of Bolivia's big cities.

Casa Dorada is Tarija's most emblematic building. This eclectic Art Nouveau mansion built in 1930 by wealthy landowners now houses the city museum and cultural center. Its golden-yellow façade is instantly recognizable.

San Roque church, atop its hill, is Tarija's most important religious monument. Its blue and white façade, completed in 1887, was visible from afar to travelers. Its balcony once served as a lookout post for city guards.

Castillo Azul is one of Tarija's architectural curiosities: a villa with brilliant blue walls, built in the 19th century by a wealthy landowner. Its improbable neo-Gothic silhouette contrasts with the surrounding colonial architecture.

Mirador Loma de San Juan offers a 360° panorama over Tarija and the Guadalquivir valley, a river bearing the same name as Andalusia's—and the resemblance doesn't end there: landscapes, terraces, and vineyards recall southern Spain.

→

Wine route through the Concepción and Los Cintis valleys. Bolivian vineyards, between 1,700 and 2,400 m altitude, are among the world's highest. Bodegas offer tours led by oenologists: Malbec, Tannat, Cabernet Sauvignon with intense aromas thanks to high-altitude UV.

💡 Book through Tarija agency; Concepción valley is 30 km away
→

Singani tasting, the national brandy distilled from muscat grapes. Campos de Solana, one of the most awarded at the Brussels World Competition, offers cellar tours and tastings. Singani is drunk neat or as Chuflay, mixed with ginger ale.

💡 Campos de Solana Bodega is in Uriondo, 20 km from Tarija
→

Lunch at Mercado Central. This covered market is the ideal place to try Saice, a Tarija specialty: spicy minced meat served with rice and peas. Tropical fruit and spice stalls complete the local cuisine discovery.

The Witches' Market stretches along Calle Linares between Sagarnaga and Santa Cruz streets. Stalls overflow with dried llama fetuses, magic stones, medicinal herbs, and mysterious powders: offerings to Pachamama and Aymara remedies. The yatiris, in their black hats, read the future in coca leaves.

San Francisco church dominates the plaza of the same name. Its 18th-century baroque mestizo façade blends indigenous motifs and Spanish ornaments. The interior nave houses gilded sculptures and religious paintings. The bell tower offers a stunning view over La Paz.

Plaza Murillo is Bolivia's political heart. The neoclassical Government Palace, Legislative Palace, and 19th-century metropolitan cathedral frame it. Illuminated at night, the plaza bustles with newspaper vendors, masked shoe shiners, and pigeons.

Calle Jaén is La Paz's best-preserved colonial street. Its ochre and blue façades house several small museums: Museo del Litoral, Museo de Metales Preciosos, and Museo de la Coca. A peaceful parenthesis amid the urban whirlwind.

Valle de la Luna lies 10 km from downtown. Erosion has sculpted clay and sandstone into needle formations, miniature canyons, and pinnacles resembling a lunar surface. Marked trails allow a 45-minute walk through these fairy-tale landscapes.

Mirador Killi Killi, perched on Santa Bárbara hill, offers the most complete panorama of La Paz. Below, red rooftops pile into the canyon down to El Alto on the plateau. In the distance, the snow-capped peaks of the Cordillera Real close the horizon.

→

Ride the Mi Teleférico network, the world's largest urban cable car system with 30 km of cables. The red line connects downtown to El Alto; the yellow line descends toward Sopocachi. From the cabins, La Paz reveals itself in all its enormity: the colorful Chualluma neighborhood and its street art murals, terraced markets, snow-capped summits.

💡 Take the red line at sunset for spectacular light
→

Cholitas Wrestling show in El Alto, every Sunday afternoon. These women in layered skirts and long braids fight in a wrestling ring in a colorful spectacle. Beyond the show, it's a powerful message of Bolivian women's empowerment.

💡 Accessible by cable car from downtown
→

Bike descent of the Yungas Road, nicknamed 'Death Road.' 64 km of descent from 4,700 m to the Amazon foothills, between vertiginous cliffs, waterfalls, and cloud forest. An experience reserved for adrenaline seekers.

→

Walk through the Chualluma neighborhood: its façades covered in giant murals rival Medellín's Comuna 13. Descend from the Cementerio cable car station and climb back up on foot through steep alleyways.

💡 Morning for light and fewer crowds
→

Hike in Valle de las Ánimas, 30 minutes from downtown. This phantasmagoric canyon harbors obelisk rock formations and pre-Columbian worship sites. According to local beliefs, protective spirits Achachilas reside there.

Rurrenabaque

+4
Top hotels
9.4 Casa de Campo

Rurrenabaque sits on the banks of the rio Beni, 45 minutes by air from La Paz. This small town with a relaxed tropical atmosphere contrasts radically with the Altiplano. It's the departure point for two wilderness regions: Madidi National Park to the west (dense jungle) and Pampas del Yacuma to the east (open wetlands).

Madidi National Park is one of the world's most biodiverse zones. Accessible in 2.5 hours by dugout canoe from Rurrenabaque, it spans 1.8 million hectares of primary tropical forest, Andean valleys, and grasslands. Over 1,000 bird species have been recorded here.

→

3-day excursion in Madidi Park. Navigate the Beni and Tuichi rivers to a community ecolodge. On the agenda: hikes on themed trails (medicinal plants, giant trees), observation of spider monkeys, peccaries, and macaws, and building a balsa raft on the río Tuichi. Jungle nights echo with howler monkeys.

💡 Bring strong repellent and a headlamp
→

3-day excursion in the Pampas del Yacuma. Canoe navigation on the Yacuma river to observe wildlife in broad daylight: caimans lounging on banks, pink dolphins surfacing, capybaras at the water's edge, anacondas sought on foot in wetlands.

💡 Combine jungle + pampas in 4–5 days to see both ecosystems
→

Sunrise from a canoe on the rio Yacuma. At 5:30am, howler monkeys announce the day from riverside trees, followed by macaw and parrot calls. Pink light on the calm river and mist rising from the water offer an unforgettable silent spectacle.

→

Piranha fishing in Pampa del Yacuma lagoons. Guides provide lines and explain techniques. These carnivorous fish pose a real danger only if you have an open wound in the water.

💡 Activity included in most pampas circuits

Sajama

+2

Nevado Sajama dominates the park at 6,542 m—Bolivia's highest peak and one of the world's tallest volcanoes. Sajama village sits at its feet on the Altiplano at 4,200 m. The view of the snow-capped cone, framed by the twin volcanoes Parinacota (6,340 m) and Pomerape (6,240 m), is unforgettable.

The church of Curahuara de Carangas, 30 minutes from Sajama village on the Patacamaya road, dates to 1608. Its biblical frescoes covering the entire interior walls are perfectly preserved. Locals call it the Sistine Chapel of the Altiplano.

The Queñua forest—a species of Polylepis with red papery bark—climbs Sajama's flanks to 5,000 m altitude. It's the world's highest forest. The national park was created in 1939 precisely to save it from disappearing.

Putulma rock paintings trace the park's history from the pre-Hispanic period—llamas, volcano representations, cattle—to the colonial period with horsemen. Yaraque's polychrome paintings (red, black, white) were a major ritual site for Aymara communities.

→

Hike to Manasaya hot springs: from the village, a 45-minute walk across the Altiplano leads to natural pools at 35–40°C with direct views of Nevado Sajama. After a cold night at 4,200 m, one of the trip's most pleasant experiences.

💡 Bring swimsuit and towel; possible to return via Queñua forest
→

Hike to Juchusuma geysers. From the village church, an 8 km trail toward Pomerape volcano leads to geothermal fields: jets of boiling water erupting every 2–3 hours, mud pots, fumaroles. Along the way, hundreds of llamas, alpacas, and wild vicuñas graze oblivious to visitors.

→

Andean wildlife observation. The park harbors 27 mammal species: vicuñas and endangered Andean armadillos (quirquinchos), Andean foxes, pumas, and exceptional birdlife on the Lauca river banks. Flamingos, condors, and Andean geese are present year-round.

Copacabana

The Basilica of Our Lady of Copacabana stands in white on Plaza 2 de Febrero. Built in 1601 in mannerist style, it houses the Black Virgin of Copacabana, Bolivia's patron saint, introduced in 1583 in a richly decorated altar.

Go to the basilica forecourt to witness the blessing of vehicles. Bolivian families have their cars decorated and sprinkled with holy water to gain the Virgin's protection—a unique daily ritual worldwide.

Climb Cerro El Calvario: 120 meters of elevation marked by crosses and votive altars. At the summit at 3,960 m altitude, Copacabana bay and the immensity of Lake Titicaca spread before you, with the snow-capped peaks of the Cordillera Real as backdrop.

Stroll the lakefront and its wooden piers. Local fishermen unload their trout; beachfront restaurants cook them with garlic, butter, or as ceviche. Lake Titicaca sits here at 3,812 m altitude, the world's highest navigable lake.

Copacabana market occupies the corner of Pando and Jauregui streets. Aymara Cholitas in layered skirts and bowler hats sell fresh produce, coca, and local crafts. An essential stop before heading to the docks.

Isla del Sol is accessible in 1.5 hours by boat from Copacabana. The Incas' sacred island, legendary birthplace of the sun god and the Inca Empire's founding, offers ancient stone paths between the villages of Challapampa in the north and Yumani in the south. The Chincana site (northern ruins) and Fuente del Inca merit exploration.

→

Sunset from Cerro El Calvario. Golden light transforms Lake Titicaca into a golden mirror. Pilgrims light candles on votive altars while reciting their intentions.

💡 30–40 min climb from the plaza
→

Kayak or pedal boat outing on Lake Titicaca from the port. The water, intensely blue at this altitude, offers a unique perspective on the bay and mountains.

💡 Prefer morning—wind picks up in afternoon
→

Hike along the Yampupata peninsula: take a taxi to Sampaya, then walk the ridges overlooking the lake for 2 to 3 hours to the village of Yampupata, where boats depart for Isla del Sol.

→

Lake trout tasting in small waterfront restaurants. Trout with garlic, butter, or ceviche—fish pulled from the water that very morning.

What weather should you expect?

Dry Season (May-October): Best weather overall, minimal rainfall, clearer skies, ideal for Salar de Uyuni, peak tourist season.

Wet Season (November-April): Heavy rains, challenging road conditions, Salar de Uyuni becomes mirror-like, lush landscapes, fewer tourists.

Shoulder Seasons (April-May, October-November): Good weather transitions, moderate crowds, reasonable prices, excellent for most activities.

High Altitude Considerations: La Paz and Altiplano best visited during dry season, less altitude sickness risk with clear weather.
