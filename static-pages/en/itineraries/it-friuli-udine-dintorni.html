Udine & Friuli Circuit: Hidden Treasures

Palmanova's UNESCO star fortress, Udine's Venetian squares painted by Tiepolo, world-renowned Spilimbergo mosaics, the turquoise torrents of Val d'Arzino, and griffon vultures at Lake Cornino: Friuli reveals its secrets far from the beaten path.

Explore Udine and its arcaded Venetian squares, then discover Palmanova, the 1593 star-shaped fortress with three concentric walls. In Spilimbergo, step inside the mosaic school founded in 1922 whose students have decorated Washington's Library of Congress. Pause in San Daniele del Friuli, where prosciutto has cured for centuries in the breeze between Alps and Adriatic. Venzone closes the journey: a medieval borough rebuilt stone by stone after the 1976 earthquake, housing six-century-old natural mummies in its Romanesque chapel crypt.

Piazza Grande di Palmanova - At the precise geometric center of this star-shaped fortress city founded on October 7, 1593 by the Venetian Republic, the hexagonal plaza forms the heart of an architectural masterpiece. The foundation date commemorates the 1571 Battle of Lepanto victory over the Ottomans. The original name "Palma" symbolized that triumph; Napoleon later added "nova" two centuries on. Stand on the central platform to grasp the perfect symmetry of six radiating streets leading toward nine defensive bastions.

Duomo del Santissimo Redentore - Dominating Piazza Grande, this church traditionally attributed to Vincenzo Scamozzi, Palladio's pupil, features a deliberately squat bell tower: it couldn't be visible from outside the ramparts, denying potential attackers a targeting reference point. This single detail encapsulates Palmanova's dual nature as both Renaissance utopian city and military machine.

Fortezza di Palmanova - Three concentric rings form three nested nine-pointed stars. The first two were built by Venice between 1593 and 1690, the third added by Napoleon between 1806 and 1813 under General Chasseloup's direction. Each curtain wall measured roughly 400 meters—exactly matching the cannon range of the era, so a single bastion could defend the entire adjacent front. UNESCO-listed since 2017, Palmanova remains the only perfectly preserved star-shaped Renaissance fortress city in existence.

Museo della Grande Guerra e della Fortezza - Housed in the dungeons of Porta Cividale, this museum chronicles the fortress's military history from its 1593 founding through the 20th century. Weapons, uniforms, and documents illustrate the Venetian, Napoleonic, and Austro-Hungarian periods.

Bastioni e Gallerie di Contromina - Descend into the underground galleries of Baluardo Donato: these secret tunnels connected the bastions to the 4-kilometer outer moat, allowing soldiers to move under cover. The pointed bastion design deflected cannonballs, reducing impact force. Above, the rampart walkway offers a 4.3-kilometer circuit on foot or by bike around the entire fortress.

Porta Aquileia, Porta Udine, and Porta Cividale - The three monumental gates facing Aquileia, Udine, and Cividale were the fortress's only entry points. Each was protected by counter-gates and drawbridges. Push through to appreciate the wall thickness and the perspectives opening toward Piazza Grande.

→

Complete rampart circuit on foot or by bike - The Anello delle Fortificazioni (4.3 km) traces all three Venetian and Napoleonic defensive rings, past bastions, ravelins, and lunettes. Interpretive panels line the route. Each September, the "Palma alle Armi" historical reenactment draws over a thousand costumed participants in 16th-century dress.

Villa Manin - This monumental complex of 8,500 square meters and 18 hectares was built in the 17th century by Friulian nobleman Antonio Manin. The forecourt, inspired by St. Peter's Square in Rome, opens onto a semicircular colonnade. The central building was redesigned in the 18th century by Giorgio Massari, one of Venice's great Settecento architects. Napoleon Bonaparte lodged here for two months in 1797 with Joséphine de Beauharnais, conducting negotiations that led to the Treaty of Campoformio signed October 17, 1797—ending a thousand years of Venetian Republic. Napoleon's bedroom and period bed remain on display.

Cappella Sant'Andrea - Built in 1708 by Domenico Rossi outside the square courtyard, this octagonal chapel adorned with marble statues by sculptor Pietro Baratta was connected by corridors to the villa's upper floors, allowing the Manin family to attend services without mingling with Passariano villagers.

Permanent collections - The former stables house a collection of antique carriages and a 350-piece armory from Udine's Casa della Contadinanza. Since 2004, the villa has also served as a Contemporary Art Center with rotating exhibitions featuring international artists and park installations.

→

Stroll through the French-style gardens - Designed in 1714 by a French architect modeled on Versailles, the park blends centuries-old trees, small lakes, hillocks crowned with mythological statuary, and vast lawns. In spring, the central meadow carpets itself with yellow daffodils. Even Carlo Goldoni was dazzled by the site's magnificence during a visit.

Piazza della Libertà - Considered the finest Venetian square outside Venice itself, it gathers the Loggia del Lionello, Loggia di San Giovanni, and the column crowned by the Lion of Saint Mark in one space—a striking summary of four centuries of Venetian rule over Friuli.

Loggia del Lionello - This Venetian Gothic town hall was commissioned in 1441 and built between 1448 and 1456. Its architect, Nicolò Lionello, wasn't a trained architect but a goldsmith, which explains the refinement of every detail. The façade measuring 35.5 meters long and 15.2 meters high alternates bands of white and pink stone, ten pointed arches at ground level, and a central balcony above. Ravaged by fire in 1876, it was reconstructed identically. The Gothic inscription "UTINUM" recalls Udine's Latin name.

Loggia di San Giovanni and Torre dell'Orologio - Facing the Loggia del Lionello, this Renaissance loggia shelters a clock tower inspired by—though squatter than—the one in St. Mark's Square in Venice. It underscores the Serenissima's constant influence on city life.

Arco Bollani and ascent to the Castello - Pass through the arch designed by Andrea Palladio in 1556: it grants access to the ramp climbing toward the castle hill. Along the way, walk beneath the Loggia del Lippomano, an elegant 16th-century portico.

Castello di Udine - Rebuilt in the mid-16th century after the 1511 earthquake, this palace-fortress atop the hill houses the Civic Museums: galleries of Venetian and Friulian paintings from the 14th to 19th centuries, archaeological collections, and prints. From the terrace, views sweep across the city rooftops and, on clear days, the snow-capped Julian and Carnic Alps.

Museo Diocesano e Gallerie del Tiepolo - Set in the Palazzo Patriarcale, residence of the Patriarchs of Aquileia from 1593 to 1751, this museum holds around 700 works. Young Giambattista Tiepolo painted masterful frescoes here between 1726 and 1730: The Fall of the Rebel Angels in the stairwell, Rachel Hiding the Idols in the Gallery of Guests, and The Judgment of Solomon in the Red Room. An entire floor displays polychrome wood sculptures from the 12th to 18th centuries.

Oratorio della Purità - This small chapel shelters a ceiling Assumption painted by Tiepolo: a luminous Madonna surrounded by cascading angels. The intimacy of the space makes the work all the more striking.

Duomo di Santa Maria Annunziata - A Latin cross cathedral rebuilt after the 1348 earthquake, whose entire floor is covered by one of northern Italy's largest Roman mosaic pavements. The side chapels preserve works by Tiepolo and Pordenone.

Piazza Matteotti (Piazza San Giacomo) - A lively square ringed by Renaissance and Neoclassical façades, bordered by café terraces. This is Udine's social heart: locals gather here evenings for a tajut, the glass of Friulian white wine shared at the counter.

Casa Cavazzini - Museo d'Arte Moderna e Contemporanea - This restructured 16th-century palace presents a substantial collection of 20th-century Italian artists including De Chirico, Morandi, and Severini, through several donations tracing the evolution of modern art in Italy.

→

Wander the Venetian historic center - Meander through lanes between Via Mercatovecchio and its arcades, Via Vittorio Veneto and its colorful palaces. Every Thursday morning, the Mercato del Giovedì sets up stalls of fresh produce, cheeses, and Friulian cured meats on Piazza Matteotti.

→

Friulian wine tasting - Udine is the gateway to Friuli's great wines: Friulano, Ribolla Gialla, Refosco dal Peduncolo Rosso. Wine bars in the historic center offer tastings paired with frico (Montasio cheese fritter) and musetto (local cotechino sausage).

Duomo di Santa Maria Maggiore - Built in 1284 against the old fortified walls, this Gothic gem incorporated a defensive tower converted into a bell tower. Inside, the three apses preserve 14th-century frescoes: Christ the Judge, the Annunciation to the Shepherds, and scenes from the Old and New Testaments. In the crypt rests the sarcophagus of Walterpertoldo IV, 14th-century lord of Spilimbergo.

Castello di Spilimbergo - Founded in the 11th century by the Spengenberg counts of Carinthia, the castle forms a circular courtyard surrounded by noble palaces. Destroyed and rebuilt multiple times, burned in 1511 during a popular revolt, it preserves two remarkable elements: the Palazzo Dipinto and Palazzo Tadea with its 16th-century stucco salon.

Palazzo Dipinto - The sole building to survive the 1511 fire, this palace is the castle's oldest section, restored between 1390 and 1400 by Count Wanzel. Its façade is entirely covered with frescoes attributed to Andrea Bellunello (15th century) and Pordenone, with Gothic and Renaissance mullioned windows framing two balconies. Emperor Charles V stayed here with his court in 1574.

Corso Roma and historic center - The main street cuts through the medieval borough east to west between two 14th-century towers. Pastel-colored façades line up painted palaces and antique signs. Pause before Palazzo Monaco and its painted façade, then Casa Dipinta (Palazzo Ercole) adorned with 16th-century frescoes depicting scenes from the life of Hercules.

Piazza Duomo and Loggia della Macia - On the square, notice the corner column still bearing the Macia, the ancient length measure used for medieval commercial exchanges. The Palazzo del Daziario, where taxes were paid to the lords, completes the picture of a borough that was an important commercial transit center.

Scuola Mosaicisti del Friuli - Founded in 1922 by mayor Ezio Cantarutti on a suggestion from Milan's Umanitaria, this school trains mosaicists from around the world. Its graduates have created decorations for the Library of Congress in Washington, the dome of the Holy Sepulchre in Jerusalem, and the Ground Zero metro station in New York. The visitor route covers techniques from Greco-Roman to Byzantine to contemporary mosaic, with over 800 works on display. You can watch students and masters breaking tesserae with the martellina hammer.

→

Open-air mosaic trail - Spilimbergo is an open-air museum: works created by the Scuola Mosaicisti are scattered throughout town—on façades, at the entrances to the Town Hall and library, in parks, and even on tree trunks. A map available at the tourist office guides visitors from one piece to the next.

Pozze di Curnila - In the middle Arzino torrent valley, between the villages of San Francesco, Pielungo, and Pert, the water has carved natural pools in the rock in shades of emerald and turquoise. The Financial Times ranked these pozze among Europe's most beautiful wild swimming spots. The multicolored pebbles of the riverbed—pink, green, gray—have served for centuries as raw material for Spilimbergo's mosaicists.

Cascate dell'Arzino - The Arzino torrent rises near Sella Chiampon, fed by two springs: the Roggia del Nanul and the Fontanone. Shortly after its source, a succession of tiered waterfalls tumbles down a gorge (forra) carved into the Dolomia Principale, surrounded by a pre-Alpine beech forest. There's no single great waterfall, but a series of ledges that divide, rejoin, and slide over polished rock.

→

Swimming in the natural pools of the Arzino torrent - The waters are crystal-clear but cold (even in summer). The Curnila pozze are the most accessible and popular. Visit on weekdays to enjoy them in peace.

→

Pozzis - Cascate dell'Arzino loop hike - From the village of Pozzis (614 m), a trail descends toward the torrent, crosses the stream on large boulders, and follows the gorge through beeches, black hornbeams, and hazels. 7.7 km loop with 155 m elevation gain.

Centro storico di San Daniele del Friuli - Perched on a hill between the Carnic mountains to the north and the Adriatic to the south, this borough owes its fame to prosciutto DOP but conceals a far richer heritage. The position creates a unique microclimate: warm sea breezes meet cold Alpine currents, generating the gentle ventilation that cures the ham during its 13-month aging.

Biblioteca Guarneriana - Founded October 7, 1466 when humanist Guarnerio d'Artegna bequeathed his 173 manuscript codices to his community, this is Friuli's oldest public library and one of Italy's first. The idea was revolutionary: at that time, libraries were private collections. It now holds about 12,000 antique books, including a 12th-century Byzantine Bible, one of the oldest Florentine copies of the Divine Comedy (late 14th century), illuminated Books of Hours on calfskin vellum, and a wooden room with secret doors sheltering once-forbidden texts.

Chiesa di Sant'Antonio Abate - Nicknamed the "Sistine Chapel of Friuli," this single-nave church was consecrated in 1308 as the hospital chapel, damaged in 1348, then reconsecrated in 1470. Its walls are covered with three fresco cycles: those by Vitale da Bologna (late 14th century) and especially those by Martino da Udine, known as "Pellegrino da San Daniele," painted between 1497 and 1522. The choir vault presents Prophets and Evangelists, and the rear wall a large Crucifixion.

Portonat - Near Piazza Dante, this massive square-based tower (torrione) was erected around 1580, facing toward Gemona. It formed part of the borough's medieval defensive system and today offers an architectural landmark in the network of ancient lanes.

Ponte di Pinzano al Tagliamento - About ten kilometers south, this bridge spans the Tagliamento and offers one of the finest panoramas of the river. Flowing 170 km, the Tagliamento is considered Europe's last major natural braided river: its multiple channels trace gravel meanders bordered by riparian vegetation.

Castello di Villalta - On the heights between Fagagna and San Daniele, this medieval castle dominates the Friulian plain. Its towers and battlements emerge from the vegetation, offering a viewpoint over the surrounding moraine hills.

→

Prosciutto di San Daniele DOP tasting - The borough's salumifici open their doors for tastings. The prosciutto is produced solely with Italian pork leg and sea salt, without preservatives, following centuries-old know-how. In 1563, during the Council of Trent, the Patriarch of Aquileia sent 12 San Daniele prosciutti to the prelates by mule. In 1797, Napoleon's officers plundered a large quantity along with the Guarneriana's treasures.

Lago di Cornino - This tiny lake born about 10,000 years ago during glacier retreat glows intense turquoise year-round, fed by underground aquifers that maintain the water between 8 and 9°C permanently. Water laden with calcium sulfate from gypsum in the Tagliamento basin gives it crystalline clarity. The lake is oligotrophic (nutrient-poor), but still shelters the white-clawed crayfish. At Christmas, divers place an underwater nativity scene on the lake bottom.

Riserva Naturale Regionale del Lago di Cornino - This 487-hectare reserve sits at the extreme southeastern margin of the Carnic Pre-Alps. The free visitor center houses aviaries for recovering raptors, a museum dedicated to the Progetto Grifone (Griffon Vulture Project), a feeding station for vultures, and a capanno (blind) for bird observation.

→

Trail around Lake Cornino - An easy 2.5 km circuit (about 50 minutes) loops the lake, passes a panoramic platform, follows a stretch of the Tagliamento, and crosses a grove with picnic tables. Swimming prohibited.

→

Griffon vulture observation (grifoni) - The Progetto Grifone, launched in the late 1980s, is Italy's first reintroduction program for these birds. Since then, 75 vultures have been released, and the colony now numbers about 150 individuals in winter and over 200 in spring-summer, with around thirty nesting pairs in the Tagliamento valley. With nearly 3-meter wingspans and weighing 7 to 12 kg, grifoni exploit thermal currents to cover hundreds of kilometers daily. Observation is recommended in the morning, during feeding time.

Mura medievali di Venzone - The only 14th-century fortified borough preserved intact in the entire region, Venzone is ringed by a double 13th-century wall pierced by three gates: Porta San Genesio, Porta Italia, and Porta Friuli. In 1258, Patriarch of Aquileia Gregorio da Montelongo granted the borough fortified city status. Declared a National Monument in 1965 and ranked among the "Borghi più Belli d'Italia" (voted Italy's most beautiful borough in 2017), Venzone occupies a strategic position at the confluence of the Tagliamento valley and the Canal di Ferro, on the route of the ancient Amber Road linking the Baltic to Aquileia.

Duomo di Sant'Andrea Apostolo - Consecrated in 1338 by Patriarch Bertrando di San Genesio, this Gothic cathedral was almost entirely destroyed by the May 6, 1976 earthquake (magnitude 6.5), then finished off by September 15 aftershocks. Reconstruction by anastylosis—each hewn stone replaced in its exact original position—mobilized 9,500 blocks inventoried one by one thanks to photographic archives. The work, completed in 1995, is considered one of the world's greatest examples of medieval building reconstruction. The Baroque organ dates from 1792.

Cappella cimiteriale di San Michele - A 13th-century rotunda built on the Duomo's forecourt, this chapel shelters five natural mummies visible to the public in its crypt (out of some forty discovered since the 17th century). The first, nicknamed "il Gobbo" (the Hunchback), was found in 1647 during expansion work. Mummification was caused by a rare fungus, Hypha Bombicina Pers, which dehydrates tissues in about one year. The bodies, dating from the 14th to 19th centuries, weigh between 4 and 8 kg. Napoleon's soldiers in 1797 were so impressed by "il Gobbo" that they removed strips of skin as souvenirs—since then, the mummies wear small protective aprons.

Palazzo Orgnani-Martina - Tiere Motus - This historic center palace houses the permanent museum dedicated to the 1976 earthquake and Friuli's reconstruction. The route traces the stages: the shock of May 6, the summer under tents, the September aftershocks that finished destroying what May had spared, the exodus of 40,000 people to the beach resorts of Grado and Lignano, and complete reconstruction in ten years following the principle "first factories, then houses, finally churches." A multimedia room projects the sequence of tremors on a regional model and displays the names of 989 victims.

→

Walk through the borough rebuilt stone by stone - Stroll lanes where every façade results from meticulous reconstruction work. New stones are still distinguishable from original ones by their slightly different surface: this deliberate choice lets visitors "read" the earthquake's history on the walls. Four 15th-century votive chapels dot a trail on the surrounding heights, following the route of an ancient Celtic path.
