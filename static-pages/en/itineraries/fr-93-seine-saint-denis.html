Seine-Saint-Denis Circuit: Royal Basilica and Urban Culture

The basilica vault echoes with pilgrimage. Factory brick holds memory. Street art colors the walls. Seine-Saint-Denis pulses with life, fully customizable.

Saint-Denis shelters the basilica of France's kings and major Gothic heritage. Montreuil asserts a vibrant, engaged artistic scene. Pantin bears witness to the territory's industrial and manufacturing past. Bobigny embodies contemporary urban evolution. Between sacred memory and working-class legacy, the department reveals a powerful identity.

The first masterpiece of Gothic architecture, the cathedral basilica houses over 70 sculpted recumbent effigies from the 12th to 16th centuries. Forty-two kings and thirty-two queens rest beneath its vaults, from Dagobert to Louis XVI.

Descend into the crypt to discover the burial site of Saint Denis, martyred around 250 AD. Eight effigies commissioned by Saint Louis stand alongside the monumental tomb of François I and Catherine de Medici.

The Paul Éluard Museum of Art and History occupies a former Carmelite monastery founded in 1625. Its collections trace the history of the 1871 Paris Commune, the Hôtel-Dieu apothecary, and the life of the surrealist poet.

Step into the Fabrique de la Ville, an educational center dedicated to archaeology and excavations that reveal two thousand years of Saint-Denis history.

The Saint-Denis market brings together nearly 220 permanent merchants. Stalls overflow with spices, fabrics, and products from around the world.

→

Follow Saint-Denis's historic trail: 20 markers chart the route between the Stade de France and the basilica, tracing 2,000 years of history along the canal.

→

The Street Art Avenue runs along the Saint-Denis canal for 5 km between the Stade de France and Parc de la Villette. Some forty monumental murals bring color to the banks.

→

Stroll through Parc de la Légion d'Honneur in the heart of downtown. In spring, the Tulip Festival transforms the lawns into a botanical rainbow.

The Magasins Généraux, former customs warehouses built in 1929, rise in concrete silhouette along the Ourcq canal. Nicknamed the "cathedral of graffiti" before their conversion, they now host exhibitions and cultural events.

The Grands Moulins de Pantin, a former industrial flour mill, bears witness to the town's manufacturing past. Its brick facade towers over the canal banks.

The National Dance Center occupies Pantin's former administrative headquarters. Its rehearsal studios regularly open to the public for work-in-progress presentations.

The Church of Saint-Germain, in the heart of old Pantin, preserves a 13th-century bell tower and 19th-century stained glass. The square overlooks the quiet lanes of the old village.

Head to Place de la Pointe, a public space designed between the harbor basin and the Magasins Généraux. Engravings on the ground recall the site's port history.

→

Follow the Ourcq canal by bike or on foot from La Villette to Pantin. The towpath alternates between street art murals, cultural barges, and floating terraces.

→

The Bas-Pantin neighborhood teems with artists' studios installed in former warehouses. Push through open doors during dedicated events to discover ceramicists, painters, and designers.

→

The canal banks host guinguettes and barges in summer. Settle on a waterside terrace to watch the ballet of joggers, cyclists, and pleasure boats.

The Murs à Pêches (Peach Walls) span 34 hectares of undeveloped orchards. From the 17th to the early 20th century, these three-meter-high walls of plaster and flint made it possible to grow exceptional peaches served at the tables of Louis XIV and the Russian Tsar.

Within the school garden, the horticultural museum is France's only museum devoted to the tools and techniques of espalier peach cultivation from the 16th to 19th centuries.

The Maison Populaire de Montreuil, a contemporary art center, programs exhibitions, seminars, and events in a brutalist building at the heart of the town.

Climb up to Parc des Beaumonts for an open view over the capital. This 24-hectare natural park harbors limestone meadows rare in the Paris region and a bird observatory.

Bas-Montreuil blends workers' buildings in brick and millstone, former workshops converted into lofts and cultural third places. The alleyways hold surprises at every turn.

→

Wander the paths of the Murs à Pêches between plots maintained by local associations: gardening, beekeeping, theater, and land art coexist here.

→

The Croix-de-Chavaux neighborhood concentrates shops, cafés, and restaurants with flavors from around the world. Craving escape just steps from Paris?

→

Spot the murals and collages scattered throughout downtown streets. Montreuil hosts one of the most vibrant street art scenes in the eastern suburbs.

The MC93, Bobigny's national theater, occupies a building designed by architects Fabre and Perrotet in raw concrete, steel, and sandstone. The main Oleg Efremov hall seats 800. Since the 1970s, this theater has hosted creations by international directors.

The Seine-Saint-Denis prefecture, built between 1968 and 1971, marks the emergence of modern administrative architecture. Its buildings are organized around a square dominated by the triangular 1974 town hall, resembling a beehive.

The Memorial to the former Bobigny deportation station, inaugurated in 2023, occupies the site from which 21 convoys of Jewish deportees departed between July 1943 and August 1944. An educational trail traces this dark chapter of history.

The Bobigny Labor Exchange, designed by a Brazilian architect, displays characteristic curves in white concrete. Its auditorium regularly hosts cultural events.

Cross the town's wooded park to reach the Church of Saint-André. The route follows boules courts and opens onto Place de la Libération, bordered by the former town hall converted into a music conservatory.

→

The Cité de l'Abreuvoir, designed in the 1950s by architect Émile Aillaud as a garden city of 1,500 housing units, is labeled "Remarkable Contemporary Architecture." Walk its paths to understand the post-war social housing utopia.

→

Reach the banks of the Ourcq canal on the Bobigny side. The towpath links Pantin to Bondy through a changing landscape punctuated by murals and new developments.

The Drancy Shoah Memorial, opened in 2012, faces the Cité de la Muette. Video testimonies, archival documents, and period photographs trace the history of France's main internment and transit camp for Jews between 1941 and 1944.

The Cité de la Muette, built in the 1930s by Beaudoin, Lods, and Bodianski, represented the avant-garde of collective housing with its fourteen-story towers, France's tallest at the time. Requisitioned by the Nazis, it became a gathering camp from which 63,000 people departed for the extermination camps. It has been a protected historic site since 2001.

In front of the housing project, Shelomo Selinger's deportees' monument consists of three 3-meter blocks of pink granite forming the Hebrew letter Shin. The central block depicts ten figures gripped by flames.

The witness wagon, inaugurated in 1988 and protected as a historic monument, is connected to the memorial by rails. It is identical to those that transported thousands of deportees to the camps in Poland.

→

Every Sunday at 3 PM, a free guided tour of the Memorial is offered. It includes the permanent exhibition and an outdoor route facing the Cité de la Muette.

The Church of Notre-Dame-des-Vertus, listed as a historic monument since 1908, preserves a 17th-century organ still in service. In 1336, a young girl prayed for rain here and saw the eyes of the Virgin's statue streaming with tears: this miracle made Aubervilliers a major pilgrimage site until the 18th century.

The church's stained-glass windows, redone after World War I destruction, illustrate the various miracles that occurred here. The baroque facade was built by Louis XIII in 1628.

Les Laboratoires d'Aubervilliers occupy 900 m² of a former precision metalwork factory in the Quatre Chemins neighborhood. This transdisciplinary artistic creation venue features a garden designed by landscape architects, with a terrace, greenhouse, and pergola.

Aubervilliers town hall, housed in a former 19th-century farm, contains an ceremonial salon adorned with neoclassical frescoes depicting allegories of work and abundance.

The Théâtre de la Commune, a national theater, has presented contemporary creations since the 1960s. Its sober building integrates into the downtown urban fabric.

→

Walk along the banks of the Saint-Denis canal on the Aubervilliers side to admire the footbridges inherited from the Paris Games: the Pierre-Larousse footbridge in recycled steel and the Lucie Bréard footbridge, 13 meters wide, dedicated to soft mobility.

→

Square Stalingrad, in the heart of downtown, offers a green pause enhanced by a fountain. Settle on a bench before setting off to explore the shopping streets.

→

Want to discover an unsuspected world? Aubervilliers's Chinese wholesale district constitutes one of Europe's largest wholesale trading centers.

Parc Georges-Valbon spans 417 hectares: it's the Paris region's third-largest park after the Bois de Vincennes and Bois de Boulogne. Over 310 hectares are classified Natura 2000, harboring more than 140 bird species.

The park's rose garden gathers 15,000 rose bushes. In spring, the paths are fragrant and colors burst between carefully tended beds.

The Victoria Montou park house, by the Grand Lake, hosts free exhibitions and events year-round. Nearby, artist Rachid Koraïchi's sculpture Le Vigilant overlooks the water.

The Terrain des Essences, 13 hectares renaturalized after the Paris Games, has joined the park. An observatory lets you contemplate this still-young ecological reserve, a refuge for local wildlife.

→

Take the 10 km hiking loop offered by the Hiking Federation. The trail winds between seven lakes, woodlands, clearings, and viewpoints.

→

In fine weather, pedal boats and rowboats are available on the Grand Lake. Families enjoy playgrounds, the carousel, and orienteering courses.

→

The park's equestrian center offers pony introductions for children. Horse-drawn carriage rides are organized from April to September.

The Church of Saint-Sulpice, built in the 12th century by stonemasons in service to the Abbey of Cluny, is Aulnay-sous-Bois's oldest structure. Its Romanesque transept and choir are listed as historic monuments.

The Vieux Pays neighborhood preserves the atmosphere of a village within the city. Around the church, the Ferme du Vieux-Pays and a few old houses bear witness to Aulnay's rural past.

The Demeure Gainville, likely the town's oldest house, was inhabited by the chemist who contributed to the discovery of bleach. It now hosts a space dedicated to art exhibitions.

Aulnay features lovely early-20th-century millstone villas inspired by Art Nouveau. Exposed brick, ceramics above windows, wrought-iron railings: set out to discover them along the streets.

Parc Dumont, laid out around the former villa of the founding abbot of the subdivision, offers a verdant setting with an adjoining municipal library.

→

The Parc du Sausset covers 200 hectares north of the town. Its varied landscapes—meadows, woods, bodies of water—make it ideal terrain for walking, cycling, and birdwatching.

→

Reach Aulnay from Paris via the Ourcq canal bike path. A pleasant dozen kilometers through the landscapes of northeast Paris.

The Church of Notre-Dame du Raincy, the world's first church built entirely in reinforced concrete, was erected in just thirteen months in 1922-1923. Its bell tower rises 43 meters. Le Corbusier called it "the Sainte-Chapelle of reinforced concrete."

Inside, 32 slender columns support barrel vaults. Load-bearing walls are replaced by openwork concrete claustra where stained glass is set, transforming the walls into walls of light.

The stained glass, designed by a Nabis painter and created by a pioneering female master glassmaker, adorns all the walls. Ten large figurative windows illustrate the life of the Virgin and the departure of the Marne taxis toward the Battle of the Ourcq in September 1914.

Le Raincy preserves beautiful turn-of-the-century villas along its tree-lined avenues. Walk down Allée des Tilleuls to admire millstone facades and enclosed gardens.

Le Raincy high school occupies a historic property surrounded by a vast park. Its gates and courtyard of honor deserve a glance in passing.

→

Walk to the Allée de Montfermeil, a straight forest clearing that crosses the wooded heights between Le Raincy and Montfermeil. A verdant stroll far from urban tumult.

→

Look up at Le Raincy's modern architecture: the town served as an urban planning laboratory with its early-20th-century subdivisions conceived as garden cities.

The Forest Park of the Poudrerie spans 137 hectares, the last witness to the immense Bondy forest. Created by decree of Napoleon III in 1865, the national gunpowder factory produced civilian and military powders for a century. Some thirty brick and stone buildings survive among the century-old oaks.

The Technical Museum of Gunpowder and Armament traces the history of this pioneering factory. The first steam-powered gunpowder plant, it employed up to 3,300 workers during World War I, producing 28 tons of powder daily.

The entrance pavilions of the Cour d'Honneur, built in 1884, mark the threshold of the estate. Their brick architecture with stone corners is characteristic of 19th-century state factories.

The Ourcq canal crosses the park and divides it into two worlds: the forest side with the Bois des Sablons and Bois de la Tussion, and the leisure side with ponds, the educational farm, and playgrounds.

The forest ponds of the Bois de la Tussion harbor palmate newts, crested newts, and spotted salamanders. These protected amphibians find refuge in this network of old ponds, habitat extremely rare in urban environments.

→

Take the park's marked trails for a hike among giant sequoias, sessile oaks, and century-old chestnuts. Clearings left by demolished buildings welcome rare butterflies and insects.

→

In fine weather, a water sports center offers canoe introductions on the Ourcq canal in a wooded, quiet setting. Paddle in the shade of century-old trees.

💡 Summer only
→

Join free events organized year-round by the park's eco-rangers: birdwatching, nocturnal outings in search of amphibians, nature workshops for families.
