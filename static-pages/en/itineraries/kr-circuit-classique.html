Start with Gyeongbokgung right at opening—100 acres, the grandest of Seoul's five royal palaces, built in 1395. The Japanese demolished 80% of the complex between 1910 and 1945. Restoration continues to this day. Time your visit for the changing of the guard at 11 AM. Wear a hanbok and you'll get in free.

Head north to Bukchon—900 traditional houses from the 1920s-40s wedged between two palaces, built as quiet resistance during the Japanese occupation. The alleyways of Gahoe-dong offer the classic view of curved rooftops. Access is limited from 10 AM to 5 PM since November 2024—keep your voice down, people actually live here.

Finish at Changdeokgung, a UNESCO site and the best-preserved of the five palaces. It served as the seat of power for 250 years after Gyeongbokgung was destroyed. Its Secret Garden to the north—79 acres of pavilions and forest—can only be visited on a guided tour with advance reservation.

Head to the Starfield Library at COEX in Gangnam—a soaring 43-foot hall entirely lined with books, now one of Seoul's most photographed spots. Just next door, Bongeunsa Temple (794 AD) creates a striking contrast with the glass towers surrounding it.

The National Palace Museum of Korea, right at the entrance to Gyeongbokgung—collections of royal furniture, court costumes, and everyday objects from the Joseon kings. Free admission, less crowded than the palace itself. Good starting point before your visit.

→

Rent a hanbok around Anguk Station—free entry to all of Seoul's palaces and better photos in the Bukchon alleyways.

→

Lunch in the Bukchon alleyways—the sundubu jjigae (silky tofu in spicy broth) restaurants around Anguk are among Seoul's finest.

→

Explore the Seochon neighborhood west of Gyeongbokgung—artist studios, galleries in hanok houses, hidden teahouses. More authentic than Insadong.

→

Evening walk along the Cheonggyecheon—an urban stream reopened in 2005 for 3.6 miles, lit up and bustling at night. A typical Seoul local's stroll.

Begin at Jongmyo Shrine, the quietest place in Seoul. Built in 1395, it houses the memorial tablets of 19 Joseon kings and 30 queens. The main hall stretches 331 feet—the longest wooden structure in a straight line in Korea. Every first Sunday in May, the royal Jongmyo Jerye ritual unfolds here with costumes, music, and dances, inscribed on UNESCO's list.

Head to Gwangjang Market for lunch—open since 1905, Seoul's oldest covered market still in operation. Order the yukhoe (beef tartare), bindaetteok (fried mung bean pancake), or mini spicy rice rolls. Grandmothers in aprons have been at the stoves since dawn.

Stop by Deoksugung Palace in the late afternoon, right in the modern business district. Emperor Gojong lived here after 1895. The changing of the guard happens three times daily. The street along its walls is called 'Breakup Road'—legend says couples who walk here will split up.

Dongdaemun Design Plaza by Zaha Hadid—925,000 square feet with not a single right angle, open 24/7. During construction, archaeologists uncovered 1,600 artifacts from a 14th-century fortress. Heunginjimun Gate, a remnant of Seoul's old city walls, stands just beside it.

The National Museum of Korea, Asia's largest at 3.5 million square feet. Free admission, 12,000 years of Korean history. Don't miss the 7th-century Baekje bronze incense burner—one of the finest pieces of Korean art.

→

Evening in Hongdae—bars, clubs, food trucks, street musicians. Seoul's liveliest student quarter after midnight.

→

Climb N Seoul Tower on Namsan Mountain in the evening—1,571 feet elevation, 360° view over illuminated Seoul. Take the cable car or hike up the trail in 30 minutes.

→

Night shopping in Dongdaemun—the fashion markets open at 10 PM and close at dawn. Wholesalers and independent Korean designers side by side.

→

Korean barbecue in the evening in Mapo district—samgyeopsal or galbi grilled over charcoal at your table, lettuce wraps, banchan.

The Third Infiltration Tunnel, discovered in 1978 at 240 feet below the border. Secretly dug, over a mile long, capable of moving 30,000 soldiers per hour. North Korea painted the walls black to make it look like a coal mine—there's no coal in this region.

Dora Observatory, the most advanced observation point toward the north. On clear days, you can spot Kaesong and Kijong-dong, the North Korean ghost town with automatically illuminated windows. Binoculars are provided. Photography banned in certain zones.

The Freedom Bridge, which 12,773 prisoners crossed southward in 1953. The barbed wire is covered with colorful ribbons hung by families separated for 70 years. A bullet-riddled locomotive found on the Gyeongui railway line is displayed nearby.

Dorasan Station, the last South Korean station before the border, built in 2002 in hope of reunification. It has never hosted regular trains north. The departure board still displays 'Pyongyang' as a destination. The waiting room is empty.

→

Organized excursion from Seoul—passport required, booking at least 48 hours in advance. Total duration around 7 hours. JSA/Panmunjom tours suspended since 2023.

→

Visit the War Memorial of Korea on the way back—five halls on the Korean War, tanks and aircraft displayed outdoors. Free admission.

→

Dinner in Itaewon district—restaurants from around the world, lively bars. 10 minutes from the War Memorial.

→

Evening walk on Banpo Bridge—9,500 nozzles spraying illuminated water in colors over the Han. Show every hour in the evening.

Ascend to Seoul Sky observatory at Lotte World Tower, on the 123rd floor—1,821 feet, the world's 5th tallest tower. The glass floor at 1,640 feet is for thrill seekers. 360° view over Seoul, the Han River, and the mountains.

Head down to Bongeunsa Temple (794 AD) in Gangnam district—37,600 square feet of Buddhist gardens at the foot of the skyscrapers in Korea's most expensive business quarter. The contrast is striking. The temple also offers a temple stay program for one night.

The Insadong neighborhood—antique dealers, calligraphy galleries, teahouses, craft shops. On Sundays, the street is closed to cars.

Seoul Olympic Park in Songpa district—built for the 1988 Olympics, five sports venues surrounded by contemporary sculptures. Beneath the stands, archaeologists uncovered a fortress from the Baekje kingdom (4th century). Free park admission, accessible via subway line 5.

→

Dinner in Yongsan district—naengmyeon (cold buckwheat noodles) and galbi in restaurants around the station. Local neighborhood vibe, off the tourist trail.

→

Korean cooking class in Insadong district—kimchi, bibimbap, or tteokbokki depending on the program. About 3 hours, reservation required the day before.

→

Namdaemun Market for shopping—open since 1414, ginseng, artisanal noodles, clothing. Sungnyemun Gate (1398) stands at the entrance.

→

Evening in Sinchon district—student bars, pojangmacha (street tents), and grilled fish restaurants. More local and cheaper than Hongdae, two subway stops away.

Arrival in Jeonju by KTX. The hanok village gathers 800 traditional houses in the historic quarter—Jeonju was the spiritual capital of the Joseon Dynasty, birthplace of founding king Taejo. Gyeonggijeon, built in 1410, houses his official portrait. Jeondong Cathedral (1914) stands on the site where Korea's first Catholic martyrs were executed in the 19th century.

Climb to Omokdae Pavilion on the hill north of the village—this is where Yi Seong-gye celebrated his victory over Japanese invaders in 1380, 12 years before founding the Joseon Dynasty. Panoramic view over the hanok village below.

Jeondong Cathedral (1914), in Romano-Byzantine style, built on the site of the first executions of Korean Catholics in the 19th century. Same architect as Seoul's Myeongdong Cathedral.

The Jeonju National Museum—collections of Baekje Buddhist art and Joseon objects. Free admission. Good complement to the village for understanding the history of the Jeolla region.

→

Bibimbap lunch in the village—Jeonju is classified as a UNESCO Creative City of Gastronomy and birthplace of bibimbap. The rice is cooked in beef broth, not water. Try the yukhoe bibimbap version with beef tartare—it's the local specialty.

→

Rent a hanbok in the village for an evening stroll through the cobbled alleyways.

→

Makgeolli (rice wine) tasting in a traditional bar in the hanok village—Jeonju is also renowned for its makgeolli bars that serve free accompaniments (anju) with each order.

→

Evening walk through the cobbled alleyways of the hanok village—the dim lantern lighting gives the quarter another face. Fewer crowds after 8 PM.

Welcome to Gyeongju—former capital of the Silla kingdom for a thousand years, inscribed entirely on UNESCO as a 'museum without walls'. The Daereungwon site brings together 23 royal tumuli right in the city center, some reaching 72 feet in height. Cheonmachong tomb, excavated in 1973, yielded a gold crown, 11,500 objects, and the only known Silla painting—a white winged horse.

Continue to Cheomseongdae Observatory, built around 632-647 under Queen Seondeok—the oldest astronomical observatory still standing in East Asia. The 30-foot tower is assembled from 362 granite stones, the number of days in the lunar calendar of the era. A woman ruler in the 7th century: exceptional for East Asia.

End at Anapji Pond at sunset—royal Silla palace garden built in 674. In 1975, drainage work brought up 33,000 artifacts from the water's bottom: royal tableware, game pieces, boat models. The pond is illuminated at night and reflects in the water—one of Korea's most photographed panoramas.

Woljeonggyo Bridge, reconstructed in 2018 to match the 8th-century royal Silla bridge. Illuminated at night, it spans the Namcheon River in the heart of Gyeongju. One of the city's most photographed night views.

→

Rent a bike or e-bike near the station to explore the historic center—tumuli, steles, and pagodas are scattered through the city like in a park. Flat terrain, the loop is about 9 miles.

→

Ssambap tasting (rice wrapped in vegetable leaves) in a traditional Gyeongju restaurant—local cuisine is renowned for recipes passed down since the Silla era.

→

Evening walk around illuminated Anapji Pond—the reflections of the pavilions in the water are Gyeongju's most photographed panorama.

→

Night walk through the streets of downtown Gyeongju—the lighting of tumuli and historic sites creates a unique atmosphere after nightfall. Start with the illuminated Woljeonggyo Bridge.

Head toward Mount Tohamsan to Bulguksa, built in 528 and reconstructed in 751 by Prime Minister Kim Daeseong—according to legend, to appease his parents' souls. The temple houses six National Treasures, including the twin pagodas Dabotap and Seokgatap facing each other in the central courtyard for 1,250 years. UNESCO-listed in 1995.

Climb up to Seokguram, 2.5 miles from Bulguksa and 2,460 feet elevation. This artificial grotto carved from granite—Korean granite doesn't lend itself to direct carving—was built for Kim Daeseong in memory of his parents from a previous life. Its 22-foot dome shelters an 11-foot Buddha gazing toward the East Sea. The grotto was rediscovered by chance in 1909 by a man seeking shelter from the rain.

Yangdong Village, UNESCO-listed in 2010—one of Korea's best-preserved hanok villages, founded in the 15th century by two aristocratic Joseon clans. About sixty traditional houses still inhabited, nestled in a valley 30 minutes from Gyeongju.

Mount Namsan of Gyeongju, sacred mountain of the Silla kingdom covered with rock-carved Buddhas, pagodas, and temple ruins—over 150 Buddhist sites scattered across its slopes. The shortest hike (Sambulsa → Yongjangsaji) is 2.5 miles and passes the three most famous Buddhas.

→

Between Bulguksa and Seokguram, hike up the forest trail rather than taking the bus—2.5 miles through a pine forest (1.5 hours). The descent can be done by bus.

→

Visit Gyeongju Traditional Market (Jungang Market)—local vegetables, Korean snacks, artisanal tofu. Open daily, liveliest in the morning.

→

Gyeongju bread tasting (hwangnam ppang)—small pastry filled with red bean paste, the city's specialty since the 1920s. Bakeries are downtown.

→

Forest bathing on the slopes of Mount Tohamsan after descending from Seokguram—the centuries-old pines on the trail are classified as a therapeutic resource by the Korean government. Free, accessible from Bulguksa parking lot.

First stop in Busan: Gamcheon Culture Village, nicknamed the 'Machu Picchu of Busan'. This wasn't an artists' village—it was a 1950s shantytown built on a hillside by war refugees. The colorful houses are the result of an artistic renovation project launched in 2009. The maze-like alleyways preserve their residential character—families still live here.

Head down to Jagalchi Market, Korea's largest seafood market. The ground floor has auctioned catches since dawn. Head upstairs—they'll cook on the spot what you just bought downstairs.

Gwangan Bridge seen from Gwangalli Beach in the evening—a 4.6-mile suspension bridge that gradually lights up after sunset. The waterfront is lined with open-air cafes and bars frequented by locals.

BIFF Square in Nampo-dong district—the plaza of the Busan International Film Festival, where Korean cinema stars' handprints are engraved in the ground. Busan's IFEZ attracts Asia's greatest directors every October.

→

Dinner in Nampo-dong district—octopus pancakes, fresh oysters at Gukje Market, pig's feet soup. Busan is Korea's unofficial gastronomic capital.

→

Sunset on Gwangalli Beach with a view of the illuminated Gwangan Bridge—locals' favorite spot, 20 minutes by subway from Nampo.

→

Karaoke in Seomyeon district after dinner—the norebang (private booth by the hour) is a Korean institution, open until dawn.

→

Evening stroll along the Oncheon River quays in Dongrae district—less touristy than Haeundae, lively residential neighborhood atmosphere.

Begin with Haedong Yonggungsa Temple, founded in 1376—one of Korea's rare temples built directly on the seashore. The 108 steps (the number of human desires in Buddhism) descend toward the sea alongside stone lanterns. Destroyed in 1592, renamed 'Temple of the Sea Dragon Palace' in 1974 after a vision by monk Jeong-am. The seven-story pagoda guards the entrance facing the waves.

Reach Haeundae Beach—1.1 miles of sand, Korea's most famous. In summer, 1.5 million people pack in during August. In winter, the Winter Swimming Club has taken the dawn plunge here since 1964. Dongbaek Island at the beach's eastern tip is connected by a breakwater and offers views across the entire bay.

Taejongdae Park on Busan's southern tip—65-foot cliffs plunging into the sea, pine forest, 19th-century lighthouse. On clear days, you can spot the Japanese island of Tsushima 31 miles away.

Dongbaek Island at the eastern end of Haeundae, connected by a breakwater—panoramic view across the entire Haeundae Bay and the skyscraper district behind. The trail circles the island in 30 minutes.

→

Walk along the Haeundae Beach boardwalk—1.1 miles of promenade lined with cafes and seafood restaurants. Lively even off-season.

→

Haejangguk (Busan beef soup) dinner at a Jagalchi Market restaurant—the quintessential local dish, served from dawn for fishermen and night owls.

→

Visit Haeundae night market—grilled seafood, tteokbokki, pajeon (scallion pancake). Lively in the evening, right on the beach.

→

Evening walk on the Dongbaek Island breakwater—view of Haeundae lights from the sea, quiet and uncrowded after 9 PM.

Head north of Busan to Beomeosa Temple, founded in 678, on the slope of Mount Geumjeong. Less touristy than Haedong, more authentic. Its name means 'temple of the sacred carp from heaven'—according to legend, a golden carp would descend to play in the fountain from the clouds. The four-pillar main gate, dating from 1614, is a National Treasure.

Continue to Geumjeongsanseong Fortress on the ridge at 2,625 feet—10.5 miles of ramparts, four gates, 360° view over Busan and the sea. An overlooked fortress. Ascent by cable car from Beomeosa, descent on foot along the ramparts.

Busan Museum in Nam-gu district—collections tracing the city's history from prehistory through the Korean War. Free admission, rarely visited by foreign tourists.

Busan's North Port area in late afternoon—the old port warehouses renovated into galleries, restaurants, and terraces overlooking the bay and container ships. The port rehabilitation draws direct inspiration from Hamburg and Rotterdam models.

→

Hike along the ramparts from the north gate—1.2 miles with alternating views over the city and wooded hills. About 2 hours.

→

Sunset on Gwangalli Beach with a view of Gwangan Bridge—4.6 miles of suspension bridge illuminated at night. Locals' favorite spot in Busan.

→

Last evening in Busan in Seomyeon district—shopping mall, restaurants, bars. The city's liveliest district outside Haeundae.

→

Last drink in a Busan makgeolli bar—local rice wine served cold with pajeon (pancakes). Bars in Seomyeon district serve until dawn.

Return to Seoul by KTX. Before departing, visit Namdaemun Market—open since 1414, one of Asia's oldest covered markets still operating. Sungnyemun Gate stands at the entrance—Seoul's oldest wooden building (1398), burned in 2008 and restored in 2013.

Dongdaemun Design Plaza by Zaha Hadid—aluminum structure without a right angle, open 24/7. If you haven't seen it yet, it's Seoul's last major landmark. Heunginjimun Gate, a remnant of the ramparts, stands right next door.

Han River Park for a final moment by the river—order ramyeon through a Korean app, riverside delivery is a local tradition. Bikes for rent on-site to ride along the banks.

Insadong district for last-minute souvenirs—antique dealers, calligraphy, Korean craft shops. On Sundays, the street is closed to cars.

→

Last shopping in Myeongdong—K-beauty cosmetics, street food (tteokbokki, hotteok), fashion boutiques. The street is pedestrianized in the evening, when the vibe is best.

→

Last-minute Insadong run for souvenirs—pottery, hanji paper, Korean tea. Shops around Ssamziegil are open until 9 PM.

→

Last Korean barbecue in Mapo or Hongdae district before departure.

→

Final coffee in a hanok cafe in Bukchon or Seochon before heading to the airport.
