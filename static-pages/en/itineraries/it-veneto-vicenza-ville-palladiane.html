Vicenza Circuit: Palladian Villas and the Venetian Countryside

Palladio changed world architecture from these Venetian hills. Here's the circuit that explains why.

Follow in the footsteps of Andrea Palladio in Vicenza, a UNESCO World Heritage city since 1994, where the Teatro Olimpico, Basilica Palladiana and Villa Rotonda embody four centuries of an architectural revolution that shaped the Western world from the White House to Monticello. Push on to Marostica, a fortified medieval town famous for its Partita a Scacchi, and to Bassano del Grappa, where Palladio's Ponte Vecchio has spanned the Brenta since 1567 and where Italy's first grappa distillery has been running since 1779. Climb to the Asiago Plateau to read in the trenches and ossuaries the story of the Great War, before descending to Lugo di Vicenza and Villa Godi Malinverni, the very first villa ever built by Palladio.

Key highlights (5)
  • Vicenza: 23 UNESCO-listed Palladian monuments within medieval walls
  • Marostica: the town where a medieval chess game is replayed every two years with 600 costumed actors
  • Bassano del Grappa: Palladio's Ponte Vecchio and Italy's first grappa distillery
  • Asiago: the Seven Communes Plateau, Great War theater, with ossuary and trenches
  • Lugo di Vicenza: Villa Godi Malinverni, Palladio's first villa, built between 1537 and 1542

Teatro Olimpico - Climb the steps from Piazza Matteotti and step into one of the world's last surviving Renaissance theaters. Palladio designed it in 1580 for the Accademia Olimpica within the walls of the old medieval castle prison, but died just three months after construction began. His son Silla and then Vincenzo Scamozzi completed the building, inaugurated on March 3, 1585 with a performance of Sophocles' Oedipus Rex. The stage is the highlight: seven wooden and stucco corridors painted in trompe-l'oeil recreate the seven streets of Thebes in just 12 meters of actual depth, creating the illusion of perspectives stretching to infinity. This set, installed for the opening performance, is the world's oldest surviving stage scenery still in its original location. The elliptical cavea with tiered seating draws inspiration from ancient Roman theaters like those described by Vitruvius in his treatise De Architectura. The building still hosts performances and concerts today.

Basilica Palladiana - On Piazza dei Signori, this building marks Palladio's consecration as the city's architect. In 1546, the Council of One Hundred chose the 38-year-old architect to save the old Gothic Palazzo della Ragione, one corner of which had collapsed. His solution—a double marble loggia in white Istrian stone punctuated by the serliana, an arched central opening flanked by two rectangular bays—wraps the medieval building without destroying it. It took 60,000 ducats and sixty-five years of construction to complete the ensemble in 1614, thirty years after Palladio's death. The great Salone on the first floor measures 52 meters long by 22 wide, covered by an inverted ship's hull wooden ceiling. Climb to the upper loggia for views over the rooftops and the 12th-century bell tower. The ground floor houses the Jewelry Museum across 410 square meters.

Loggia del Capitaniato - Facing the Basilica across Piazza dei Signori stands this loggia that Palladio designed in 1571 to serve as residence for Venice's governor of the city. Only three of the planned five bays were built, but the facade is one of the architect's most expressive: colossal composite half-columns, projecting balconies, reliefs celebrating the victory at the Battle of Lepanto (1571) against the Ottomans. The broken entablature and sculptural details anticipate the Baroque. Palladio himself never saw it completed.

Palazzo Barbaran da Porto (Museo Palladio) - Walk down Corso Palladio to this palace built between 1569 and 1575 for Montano Barbarano, one of the few Palladian buildings that Palladio personally supervised to an advanced stage. The facade creates a dialogue between two superimposed orders—Ionic below, Corinthian above—with reliefs of exceptional precision. The interior now houses the Museo Palladio: models, original drawings and videos connect the architect's work with his heirs from the White House to the University of Virginia.

Palazzo Chiericati - At the far end of Piazza Matteotti, this palace commissioned by Count Girolamo Chiericati in 1550 stands as a masterpiece of Palladian civic architecture. Its two-level facade of colonnaded loggias was originally built at the edge of a river port. On the ground floor, Doric columns support an open colonnade; on the upper floor, the central rooms advance onto a covered Ionic terrace. The building houses Vicenza's Civic Museums with works by Memling, Tintoretto, Veronese and Giambattista Tiepolo.

Chiesa di Santa Corona - Walk up Corso Palladio to this Dominican church founded in 1261 by Bishop Bartolomeo da Breganze to house a thorn from Christ's crown, brought from Paris as a gift from Saint Louis. Its Gothic naves conceal two treasures: the Baptism of Christ by Giovanni Bellini (1502), painted when the master was over seventy, and the Adoration of the Magi by Paolo Veronese (1573). Both works remain in the chapels for which they were commissioned.

→

Stroll along Corso Palladio - The historic center's main artery connects Piazza del Castello to Piazza Matteotti over roughly 600 meters. Lined with Renaissance and medieval palaces, it's the city's pedestrian heart. Note Palazzo Porto-Colleoni at No. 19 (late 15th-century facade), Palazzo Pojana, and at the street's end, the unfinished fragment of Palazzo Porto-Breganze, Palladio's last project, of which only two of seven bays were built by Scamozzi.

→

Piazza dei Signori at dusk - The square was the Roman forum of the city founded in the 2nd century BC. At sunset, the two Venetian columns—one topped by the winged lion of St. Mark, the other by Christ the Redeemer—catch a golden light. Locals fill the terraces beneath the Basilica's arcades for aperitivo, following a habit that dates back centuries: the shops under the loggias were already active in Palladio's time.

→

Ponte di San Michele and the Retrone river - Walk down to this bridge built between 1621 and 1623 to span the Retrone River. The view from the banks reveals the rear facades of medieval palaces lining the waterway: a rarely photographed image of Vicenza, quite different from that of the grand squares.

Villa Rotonda (Villa Almerico Capra) - Take the road climbing south from the city to reach this villa commissioned in 1566 by Paolo Almerico, a retired Vatican ecclesiastic, on a hill overlooking the Vicentine countryside. Palladio himself classified it as a palazzo rather than a villa: no agricultural outbuildings, no utilitarian function, only beauty. The plan is unique in architectural history: a perfect square with four identical facades, each featuring a six-column Ionic portico, all converging on a circular central hall topped by a dome. The villa is oriented 45 degrees from the cardinal points to capture sunlight at every hour. Its influence is immense: Thomas Jefferson drew on it for his own home at Monticello in Virginia, and five copies exist in England alone, including Chiswick House in London.

Villa Valmarana ai Nani - A few hundred meters from La Rotonda, this 17th-century villa is famous for its frescoes, painted in 1757 by Giambattista Tiepolo in the main palazzina and by his son Giandomenico in the foresteria (guest quarters). Giambattista's frescoes depict scenes from the Iliad, the Aeneid, Jerusalem Delivered and Orlando Furioso with the luminous palette characteristic of Venetian Rococo. Giandomenico's in the foresteria show scenes of peasant life and commedia dell'arte characters—a radical break from his father's heroic register. The name 'ai Nani' comes from twenty-one stone dwarf statues lined along the perimeter wall, protagonists of a local legend.

Santuario della Madonna di Monte Berico - Climb on foot or by car to this sanctuary perched on Monte Berico hill, accessible from the center via a long covered portico of 150 arches built in the 18th century. The current church dates from 1703 and replaces a 15th-century Gothic building constructed following a Marian apparition in 1426, during a plague epidemic. The interior preserves a Pietà by Bartolomeo Montagna (1500) and a painting by Paolo Veronese depicting the Feast of St. Gregory (1572). The terrace offers panoramic views of Vicenza, the Venetian plain and the Prealps.

→

Giardini Salvi - These historic gardens, 500 meters from the train station, are laid out around a canal and a Palladian loggia built by Lorenzo Rubini in the 16th century. The adjacent English garden dates from the 19th century. Ideal for a break after morning visits before heading out to the villas.

→

Cycling excursion in the Colli Berici - The hills bordering Vicenza to the south, between 100 and 400 meters altitude, are crossed by quiet roads passing through medieval villages and vineyards producing DOC Colli Berici (Garganega, Tai Rosso, Cabernet). Local outfitters offer road bikes or mountain bikes with marked routes. The climb to Brendola or Barbarano offers views over the Po plain.

→

Parco Querini - This vast 13-hectare park in the heart of the city is laid out as an English garden around a small artificial lake, with wooded hills, canals and two small neoclassical temples. Locals use it for daily walks. A rosarium and several rare shrub species also make it a botanical site.

Marostica

Piazza degli Scacchi and Castello Inferiore - The main square of Marostica is paved with a giant chessboard measuring 16 meters on each side, in pink Conco marble and white Asiago marble, with 2-meter squares. This is where the Partita a Scacchi takes place every even year on the second weekend of September: a chess game played with 600 costumed actors in 15th-century dress, live horses and archers in the towers. The legend dates to 1454: two warriors, Rinaldo d'Angarano and Vieri da Vallonara, both in love with Lionora, daughter of castellan Taddeo Parisio, were forbidden to duel and instead played out their fate on a chessboard before the townspeople. The modern tradition dates back to 1923. The Castello Inferiore (14th century) dominates the square and houses a museum of the event's costumes.

Castello Superiore and medieval walls - A climb of about 20 minutes on foot along the rampart path connects the lower castle to the Castello Superiore, perched at 350 meters altitude on the hill behind the town. The upper castle dates from the 14th century, when Marostica was under the rule of Verona's Scaligeri before passing to Milan's Visconti and then to the Republic of Venice in 1404. A statue of St. Mark's lion on the square recalls four centuries of Venetian rule. The ramparts connecting the two castles are almost entirely preserved and offer panoramic views over the Brenta plain and the foothills of the Asiago Plateau.

→

Tasting Marostica IGP cherries - The cherries of Marostica, grown on the sunny hills north of town, were the first in Italy to obtain IGP (Protected Geographical Indication) status for this fruit. The cherry festival is held in May; the rest of the year, local producers sell jams, liqueurs and dried cherries in the center's grocery shops. The main variety, Durone Nero di Vignola, is harvested in June.

→

Walk along the ramparts - The rampart path connecting the two castles along the hillside can be walked as a loop from Piazza degli Scacchi. Views over the red-tiled roofs, the hillside vineyards and the Brenta plain. The climb is steep in places but accessible to all.

Ponte degli Alpini (Ponte Vecchio) - The covered wooden bridge spanning the Brenta is the symbol of Bassano del Grappa. Palladio designed its reconstruction in 1567, on a bridge existing since the Middle Ages. The wooden structure, destroyed several times by floods and wars, has always been rebuilt identically, most recently in 1948 by Alpine troops after its destruction in February 1945. It bears their name for this reason and was declared a national monument in 2019. The bridge connects the two banks where the Brenta narrows, with a direct view of Monte Grappa and the Prealps. The town changed its name from 'Bassano Veneto' to 'Bassano del Grappa' after World War I to honor the soldiers who fell on the mountain.

Museo degli Alpini - Installed in the vaulted stone cellars at the bridge's foot, this small museum preserves uniforms, weapons, documents and photographs tracing the history of the Alpini—the Italian army's mountain troops—since their creation in 1872. The battles of Monte Grappa (1917-1918) occupy a central place: this is where the front line held during the Austrian offensive of the Strafexpedition and the 1918 offensives, just a few kilometers from town.

Castello degli Ezzelini - The ruins of this medieval fortress constitute Bassano's oldest point, dominating the center from the northern hill. The Bolzonella tower, called 'di Ser Ivano', dates from the Middle Ages and served as an independent watchtower. Giuseppe Verdi's first opera, Oberto conte di San Bonifacio, is set within these walls—proof that the castle still fed the imagination in the 19th century. In summer, the open-air theater inside the ramparts hosts the OperaEstate festival.

Palazzo Sturm - This 18th-century Rococo palace, built on the Brenta downstream from the bridge, offers from its courtyard the best viewpoint of the Ponte degli Alpini and the mountains. The interior hosts temporary modern art exhibitions. The outdoor terrace is the best place to photograph the entire bridge with the snowy massif in the background.

→

Visit and tasting at Nardini distillery - Bortolo Nardini founded Italy's first grappa distillery in 1779, at the northern entrance to the Ponte Vecchio, where it still stands today. The guided tour of the historic cellars traces two centuries of distillation: copper stills, maceration jars, production line. The tasting includes the famous Mezzoemezzo, the house aperitif based on rhubarb and citrus, and Tagliatella, a ruby liqueur from a secret late-18th-century recipe.

→

Poli Museo della Grappa - On the other side of the bridge, the Poli distillery museum traces the history of marc brandy from its medieval origins to modern techniques. The visit ends in the exhibition room with tasting and shop. The Poli distillery, founded in Bassano in 1898, is today among Veneto's most renowned.

→

Stroll along Viale dei Martiri - This panoramic avenue running along the old town's northern ramparts offers an open view of Monte Grappa massif and the Prealps. Its name comes from a September 1944 episode: in reprisal for a resistance action, the Germans hanged 31 partisans from the avenue's trees. An individualized memorial honors each victim.

Museo Civico della Ceramica di Nove - Nove owes its existence to the Brenta. The river's alluvial deposits provided the clay, quartz and calcium carbonate for ceramic pastes; hydraulic power drove the preparation mills; boats transported wood for the kilns and finished products to Venice and Europe. The Antonibon manufactory, founded in the 1720s, was the first to produce Dutch-style majolica in response to the fashion for Chinese porcelain. It delivered services to the Viennese court and European embassies. The museum presents a collection of Nove ceramics from the 18th century to today, showing the evolution of styles from Baroque faience to Art Deco forms.

Villa Angarano - This patrician villa on the Brenta's left bank, two kilometers from Nove, was begun by Palladio for the Angarano family around 1548 but remained unfinished. The lateral barchesse (agricultural outbuildings) that survive are attributed to Palladio; the central body was taken up and completed by Baldassarre Longhena, architect of the Salute in Venice, in the 17th century. The villa belongs to the UNESCO perimeter of Veneto's Palladian villas. It still produces wine today and offers tastings.

→

Ceramics workshop in Nove - Artisanal ceramic production continues in Nove in about ten workshops open to visitors. Several organize throwing, glazing and firing demonstrations for groups, and offer practical workshops of one or two hours. The tradition of the cuco, the small fischietto (whistle) in terracotta shaped like a rooster or Napoleonic figure, remains a local specialty.

→

Sentiero del Tabacco along the Brenta - From Bassano's Ponte Vecchio, this path follows the Brenta's right bank for 35 km to the village of Costa di Valstagna. It owes its name to the tobacco cultivation that once occupied the dry-stone terraces (masiere) carved into the slopes. These terraces, now partly wooded, remain visible throughout the route. A one-hour round-trip section from Nove suffices to understand the landscape shaped by centuries of mountain agriculture.

Sacrario Militare di Asiago - This funerary monument built in 1938 preserves the remains of 54,286 Italian and Austro-Hungarian soldiers who fell on the Asiago Plateau during World War I. The Plateau was one of the war's deadliest theaters: the battles of the Strafexpedition (Austrian offensive of June 1916) and the Italian counter-offensives of 1917-1918 killed tens of thousands for territorial gains of a few hundred meters. The monumental ossuary, with its crypt and galleries, conveys the scale of these battles in what is today a peaceful mountain landscape.

Museo della Grande Guerra di Canove - Inaugurated in 1974, this museum in Canove di Roana presents uniforms, weapons, tactical maps and photographic documents tracing the Plateau's battles from 1915 to 1918. The collections include objects recovered from surrounding battle sites and written testimonies from soldiers on both sides. The museum is one of the most rigorous on the conflict in Veneto.

→

Museo all'Aperto di Monte Zebio - A few kilometers from Asiago, this open-air museum lets you walk through trenches and communication trenches carved into the rock during the Great War. Monte Zebio constituted a strategic Austrian strongpoint between 1916 and 1918. The firing positions, cave shelters and gallery networks are still largely visible. The discovery walk takes about two hours.

→

Ascent to Forte Verena - This Austro-Hungarian fort, built between 1908 and 1912 at 2,015 meters altitude, was captured by the Italians in 1916 and remained a strategic observation post until the war's end. The climb from Rifugio Verenetta (accessible by car) takes about 1 hour. The summit panorama embraces the entire Plateau, the Venetian plain and, on clear days, the Dolomites. The fort's remains are freely accessible.

→

Asiago historic center - The town of Asiago was entirely razed during the Great War and rebuilt between 1919 and 1925. Gabriele d'Annunzio called it 'Italy's smallest and brightest city.' Its center, remade in one piece in Liberty and neoclassical style, is exceptional in the mountain landscape. The two main piazzas—Piazza Carli and Piazza Risorgimento—and Corso IV Novembre form a cohesive urban ensemble rare in the alpine zone.

Villa Godi Malinverni - This villa built between 1537 and 1542 in Lonedo, a hamlet of Lugo di Vicenza, is the first villa designed by Andrea Palladio—he wasn't yet thirty. The commission came from brothers Girolamo, Pietro and Marcantello Godi, from a powerful noble family specializing in silk trade across Europe. The facade is sober, almost austere, with two massive projecting blocks framing a central loggia with three arches, without the classical ornaments that would characterize his mature works. Palladio himself notes in the Four Books of Architecture that it 'stands on a hill with an admirable view, beside a river that serves for fishing.' The interior is decorated with frescoes by Gualtiero Padovano, Giovanni Battista Zelotti and Battista del Moro. A park pavilion houses the Pierluigi Malinverni paleontological museum, which preserves notably the world's longest fossil palm, and a second museum is dedicated to the British presence in this territory during the Great War, with documents on the Prince of Wales (future Edward VIII) and pacifist writer Vera Brittain.

Buso della Rana - In Monte di Malo, about twenty kilometers from Lugo, opens the entrance to Veneto's longest cave. The speleological network extends over more than 10 kilometers of galleries. The portion accessible to the public, about 500 meters of equipped galleries, crosses rooms of limestone concretions—stalactites, stalagmites, columns—formed over millions of years. The constant temperature of 9°C makes it an appreciated excursion in summer. The guided visit lasts about an hour.

→

Visit to Villa Godi's historic gardens - The villa's centuries-old park, recently restored thanks to PNRR funds as part of the 'Gardens of Palladio' project, alternates Italian gardens with regular embroidery, alleys of century-old boxwood and an English wooded park with trees planted in the 19th century. A fountain, a network of paths and a view over the Astico valley and Vicentine Prealps complete the walk.

→

Exploring the Buso della Rana galleries - Guided cave tours depart every hour from the prepared entrance. The route crosses several remarkable rooms including the 'Sala del Bivio' and 'Sala delle Cascate,' with explanations of the karst geology of the Asiago Plateau, of which the Buso is one of the natural drains.
