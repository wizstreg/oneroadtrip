Built in 1274 by James II, the Palace of the Kings of Majorca commands the city from its hilltop perch. Its southern Gothic architecture unfolds around a central courtyard, with two superimposed chapels featuring pink and white marble voussoirs.

The Castillet rises with its red brick and honey-stone tower dating from 1368. This former city gate houses a Catalan museum and offers sweeping views over Perpignan's rooftops and the Pyrenees beyond.

The Cathedral of Saint John the Baptist blends Romanesque and Catalan Gothic styles. Its 80-meter single nave shelters the Devout Christ, a 14th-century crucifix carried during Easter processions.

The Campo Santo stands as one of France's largest medieval cloister-cemeteries. Its 13th-century arcades still bear sculpted coats of arms and Gothic interlacing.

The Hyacinthe Rigaud Museum occupies two renovated townhouses. Its collections span five centuries, from Catalan Gothic art to Rigaud's portraits, with works by Picasso, Maillol, and Dufy.

→

The Royal Château plunges its stones into the Mediterranean, standing since the 13th century. Built by the Kings of Majorca then remodeled by Vauban, it offers a circular view over the bay and the Albères mountains.

The Church of Our Lady of the Angels rises from the waves with its 17th-century lighthouse-bell tower. Its baroque altarpiece by Joseph Sunyer, covered in gold leaf, features 97 figurines and statues.

Fort Saint-Elme dominates the bay with 360° views. This 1538 fortress controlled the passage between Collioure and Port-Vendres.

The Fauvism Showcase displays reproductions and documents about Matisse and Derain. It's the starting point for the Fauvism Trail.

Step inside the Chapel of Saint Vincent, perched on its rocky outcrop. It houses the patron saint's relics brought back from Rome in 1700.

Expanded in 2022, the Museum of Modern Art houses 53 works by Picasso, 14 drawings by Matisse, and canvases by Chagall, Miró, and Soutine. Nicknamed the Mecca of Cubism, Céret drew all the great names of the 20th century.

The Devil's Bridge has spanned the Tech River since the 14th century. Legend has it that a cat was sacrificed to the Devil for its construction.

The Nine Jets Fountain dates from 1313. Its nine spouts supplied water to the different quarters of the medieval town.

Pass through the Porte d'Espagne, remnant of the medieval fortifications. Its arch marks the southern entrance to the old town.

The Church of Saints Peter and Paul houses a baroque altarpiece and works by Catalan sculptors.

→

Experience the Saturday morning market. Local produce, crafts, and Catalan atmosphere fill the main square.

→

In May and June, taste Céret's cherries. The first fruits are traditionally sent to the French President during the cherry festival.

Take the Sublime Paths route. Reproductions of paintings are placed in situ where the artists worked.

Fort Lagarde, built by Vauban between 1677 and 1682, guards the entrance to the Haut-Vallespir. Its star-shaped fortress surrounds a medieval signal tower. Climb via the zigzagging path or the underground passage with 142 steps.

The medieval ramparts encircle the upper town since the 14th century. Vauban remodeled them in the 17th century to create a remarkable defensive ensemble.

The Church of Saints Justus and Rufina imposes its dimensions since the 9th century. Rebuilt in the 13th and 16th centuries, its 1245 portal displays ironwork from Catalan forges.

The Tour de Mir testifies to the medieval defensive system. This watchtower offers a vantage point over the valley.

Visit La Verneda, a museum housed in a former textile factory. Exhibitions on the history, heritage, and Catalan traditions on both sides of the Pyrenees.

→

Wander through the medieval streets of the upper town. The colorful facades contrast with the rigor of Vauban's fortifications.

Drive up to La Preste, a spa town nestled at 1,130 meters altitude. Its sulfurous waters have treated rheumatism and respiratory ailments since the 16th century.

UNESCO-listed Villefranche-de-Conflent unfolds its pink marble ramparts dating from the 11th century. Vauban created a unique double covered walkway with bastions and fortified gates.

Fort Libéria towers 180 meters above the town. Built by Vauban in 1681, it's reached via an underground staircase of 734 steps. A garrison of 100 men, chapel, powder magazines, and dungeons unfold in succession.

The Canalettes Caves reveal limestone draperies, eccentric formations, and millennial concretions. From the Cupules corridor to the White Room, water has sculpted wonders.

The Church of Saint James preserves its 11th-century Romanesque architecture. Sculpted portal and capitals testify to Catalan Romanesque art.

→

Board the Yellow Train. This narrow-gauge line climbs to 1,600 meters altitude through bridges, tunnels, and spectacular Pyrenean landscapes.

The Odeillo Solar Furnace stands 50 meters tall. Built in 1969 with 1,000 adjustable mirrors, it concentrates solar energy to reach 3,500°C at the focal point. Research center for high-temperature materials.

The Hermitage of Notre-Dame de Font-Romeu dates from 1693. This baroque chapel on the Camino de Santiago houses a 13th-century statue of the Virgin. The sacred fountain gives it its name.

The 20th-century Church of Saint Louis dominates the center. Built in concrete in 1733 on the model of the citadel chapel, it features a monumental statue of Christ the King.

The National High-Altitude Training Center has welcomed Olympic athletes since 1967. Its facilities are open to the public: swimming pool, ice rink, weight room.

The Grand Hotel, built between 1910 and 1914, dominates the resort. Listed as a historical monument, this Belle Époque establishment symbolizes the rise of climate therapy.

→

Ski on the Font-Romeu Pyrénées 2000 domain. France's sunniest resort with 43 slopes between 1,600 and 2,213 meters altitude, 500 snow cannons, and 110 km of cross-country skiing.

💡 Lift pass reservations recommended

→

Hike on the Cerdagne plateau. Hundreds of kilometers of marked trails traverse pine forests, lakes, and clearings at 1,800 meters altitude.

→

Relax in the sulfurous hot spring baths. Llo, Dorres, and Saint-Thomas offer havens of peace in pristine nature.

The UNESCO-listed Citadel of Mont-Louis is France's highest fortified town at 1,600 meters. Created from scratch by Vauban in 1679, it forms a square flanked by four bastions. It has housed the National Commando Training Center since 1964.

The Convicts' Well houses a 17th-century squirrel-cage wheel. At 28 meters deep, the well was operated by two men. Only three original wheels survive in France.

The Mont-Louis solar furnace was the world's first in 1949. Installed by Félix Trombe, it enabled the experimentation that led to the large Odeillo furnace. Power output of 50 kW.

The Church of Saint Louis from 1733 was built on the model of the citadel's chapel. Architectural gem dedicated to Louis XIV's ancestor.

The star-shaped ramparts follow Vauban's plan. Three fortified gates controlled access: Royal Gate, France Gate, and northern emergency gate.

→

Walk the bird trail at the foot of the ramparts. Observe various bird and insect species of the territory with views of Vauban's genius.

→

Explore the citadel's parade ground. This esplanade bustles with military activities and testifies to the regiment's current life.

→

Wander through the streets of the department's smallest commune. Saint-Louis fountain, historic town hall, and Catalan architecture await discovery.

The Oniria Aquarium spans 3,500 m² with 83 tanks. Inaugurated in 2021, this immersive journey follows the water cycle from Canigou's summit to the ocean abyss. Over 3,500 animals from 660 species populate ten ecosystems.

The marina counts 2,200 berths, Europe's third largest. Protected by two jetties, it features three basins, a modern harbor master's office, and facilities for sailing and motorboating.

The fine sandy beach stretches for six kilometers. Blue Flag certified for over 25 years, it guarantees water quality and environmental standards.

The Canet lagoon forms a 4,019 km² marine nature park. This protected wetland shelters remarkable Mediterranean fauna and flora.

The Cathedral of Saint Eulalia and Saint Julia has dominated the town since the 11th century. A major monument of Catalan Romanesque art, it served as the bishopric seat from the 6th to 17th century. Blue marble portal and barrel-vaulted nave.

The white marble cloister veined with blue was built from the 12th to 14th century. Listed as a historical monument in 1840, it displays the evolution of medieval Roussillon sculpture between Romanesque and Gothic.

The Terrus Museum celebrates Étienne Terrus, a painter born in Elne in 1857. Friend of Matisse, Maillol, and Derain, he captured Roussillon landscapes in watercolor. Over 100 works on display.

The archaeology museum in the Saint-Laurent chapel presents artifacts from the Neolithic to the Middle Ages. Three 6th-century Visigothic marble sarcophagi adorn the east gallery.

The Swiss Maternity Hospital, built in 1901, sheltered children of all nationalities between 1939 and 1944. A historical monument bearing witness to solidarity and hope.

Stroll from the Porte Balaguer. Panoramas embrace the surrounding mountains and Canigou, the Catalans' sacred peak.

The marina features 1,885 berths, Europe's third-largest port by capacity. Developed since 1967 with three protected basins, modern harbor master's office, and services for boaters.

The Saint-Cyprien Collections display 19th- and 20th-century paintings. Eugène Boudin, Barbizon School, Utrillo, Marquet, Modigliani, and François Desnoyer enrich the galleries.

The Botanical Garden spans five hectares in the Villa des Capellans park. Over 800 plant species, bamboo grove, antique roses, and contemporary sculptures.

The Saint-Cyprien Resort Golf offers an 18-hole and a 9-hole course. Links-style atmosphere with views of the Pyrenees and Mediterranean.

→

Practice sailing or motorboating. The Yacht Club offers lessons and boat rentals for cruising along the coast.
