Pistoia Province Circuit: Hill Towns, Thermal Spas & Mountains

Medieval pulpits, condottiere fortresses, Belle Époque UNESCO thermal spas, citrus villages and Pinocchio's baroque garden. Pistoia province holds all of Tuscany in a single circuit.

Pistoia and its five medieval pulpits open this journey, before climbing to Serravalle Pistoiese and its rival towers. The Pistoiese mountains reveal Cutigliano with its coat-of-arms-covered Palazzo, then San Marcello Piteglio and its vertiginous suspension bridge. The descent to Montecatini and its UNESCO Art Nouveau thermal baths leads to the hilltop villages of the Valdinievole—Buggiano, flower-filled Pescia—before the final stop at Collodi, the stone village clinging to its hillside that gave its pen name to Pinocchio's creator.

Key highlights (4)
  • Pistoia: Tuscany's capital of medieval pulpits
  • Montecatini Terme: UNESCO-listed Art Nouveau thermal spa town
  • Serravalle Pistoiese: medieval village of two rival towers
  • Collodi: birthplace of Pinocchio author's pen name

Pistoia

Piazza del Duomo brings together the four pillars of medieval power in a single theatrical space: San Zeno Cathedral, the Baptistery, Palazzo del Podestà, and Palazzo dei Vescovi. The campanile tells the city's history in stone—originally a Lombard defensive tower, then heightened in the 12th and 13th centuries in Pisan Romanesque style. Each floor marks a different era, from the austere lower masonry to the elegant Pisan arcading at the summit.

San Zeno Cathedral shelters one of medieval Europe's most extraordinary goldsmithing treasures: the Silver Altar of Saint James, begun in 1287 and enriched over two centuries by Tuscany's finest goldsmiths, including a young Filippo Brunelleschi who worked on it around 1399-1400. The crypt holds the relics of Saint Zeno, while Coppo di Marcovaldo's monumental Crucifix from the 1270s dominates the central nave with its hieratic figures against a shimmering gold background.

The Ospedale del Ceppo, founded according to tradition in 1277—its name comes from a dry tree stump that miraculously flowered to mark the site—operated as a hospital for over seven centuries until 2013. Its early 16th-century loggia bears one of the Renaissance's finest glazed terracotta friezes: commissioned from 1511 by the monk Leonardo Buonafede, it united the rival workshops of the Della Robbia and Buglioni families. Giovanni della Robbia created the 1525 roundels depicting scenes from Mary's life; the narrative frieze illustrates the Seven Works of Mercy in 16th-century settings and costumes, a vivid documentary of daily life in Renaissance Pistoia.

The Hospital Museum del Ceppo, installed in the former wards since 2017, displays several hundred surgical instruments from the 17th to early 20th centuries, exhibited in bed-shaped vitrines along the old men's ward. The visit culminates in the neoclassical oval Anatomical Theater, built 1785-1787, where medical students learned anatomy. A wooden and leather birthing machine, loaned to London's Science Museum in 1913 and returned to Pistoia a century later, is now on display.

San Giovanni Fuorcivitas—"outside the walls," as it was originally built beyond the first Lombard ramparts—presents one of the most spectacular side facades in Pisan Romanesque architecture: a densely packed alternation of white marble and green Prato serpentine bands, constructed from the 12th to 14th centuries. The north portal's architrave, carved in 1166 by Gruamonte with the Last Supper, is one of the earliest examples of Pistoiese Romanesque sculpture. Inside, three masterpieces await: Fra Guglielmo da Pisa's pulpit (1270), disciple of Nicola Pisano; the central holy water font attributed to Nicola Pisano and his workshop; and Luca della Robbia's Visitation in glazed terracotta (1445), the first surviving freestanding sculpture created by the Della Robbia workshop using the invetriatura technique.

The Basilica della Madonna dell'Umiltà traces its origin to a miraculous image of the Virgin dated 1490. The foundation stone was laid in 1495 following plans by Giuliano da Sangallo. Cosimo I later commissioned Giorgio Vasari to roof the building: the dome he constructed from 1561, visible across the entire city, deliberately references Brunelleschi's in Florence and ranks among Tuscany's largest domes.

→

Pistoia Sotterranea: beneath the Ospedale del Ceppo's forecourt runs a network of brick-vaulted tunnels following the ancient course of the Brana torrent, channeled and covered as the city expanded. The public route covers about 650 meters of the total 1,200-meter network, with panels showing the successive phases of urban expansion and the remnants of walls, towers and bridges integrated into the underground.

→

The Pieve di Sant'Andrea houses the absolute masterpiece of Pistoiese pulpits: Giovanni Pisano's pulpit, completed in 1301. Its reliefs of shattering expressiveness—the Massacre of the Innocents, the Nativity, the Crucifixion—achieve a sculptural depth and dramatic tension unmatched in early 14th-century Tuscany. Pistoia is the only city in the world to possess so many major medieval pulpits within such a compact area.

→

The Civic Museum of Ancient Art (Palazzo del Podestà) preserves works from the 13th to 16th centuries, including the famous altarpiece by Giuliano da Rimini and panels by Pistoiese masters. The 13th-century palace facade, with its rounded arches and massive tower, stands as one of Tuscany's best-preserved examples of communal palace architecture.

→

Wander through the daily market in Piazza della Sala, where stalls cluster around a medieval well surrounded by arcades. Here Pistoia shows its living face, amid displays of citrus fruits, cheeses and cured meats from the Pistoiese Apennines.

The Antico Palazzo dei Vescovi, facing the cathedral across Piazza del Duomo, houses the Cathedral Museum with its collections of sacred goldwork and medieval art. Excavations in the basements have uncovered superimposed Roman and Lombard remains, visible in the underground archaeological route.

San Bartolomeo in Pantano—"in the swamp," as it was built on marshy ground outside the first ramparts in the 8th century—preserves the third great Pistoiese pulpit: created in 1250 by Guido da Como, it's the oldest of the three major medieval pulpits surviving in the city, with reliefs of the Annunciation, Nativity and Adoration of the Magi set against polychrome marble panels.

Marliana, a farming village at 500 meters altitude on the north slope of Montalbano, is surrounded by olive groves and vineyards producing the oils and wines of the Pistoiese hills. Its medieval Rocca commands views across the Pistoia plain to the Apennines. The village long served as one of the wine-producing outposts supplying Pistoia's merchants.

→

Pistoia Zoo, founded in 1970, occupies 7 hectares in the northern part of the city and is home to over 500 animals of 100 species. It's known for its endangered species conservation programs and naturalistic habitats, ranking among central Italy's most active zoological institutions.

→

Walk through the nursery district around Pistoia: the plain between Pistoia and Pescia forms Italy's main ornamental plant nursery zone, exporting millions of plants annually throughout Europe. Marked paths wind through greenhouses and ornamental plant fields from October to April.

→

The Embroidery Museum (Palazzo Rospigliosi) traces the history of Pistoiese embroidery, a craft of excellence that dressed European courts from the 17th century. The Rospigliosi family, one of whose members became Pope Clement IX in 1667, were among the great patrons of this art.

Serravalle Pistoiese

The Torre del Barbarossa rises 42 meters at the village's highest point, on the ruins of the Rocca Vecchia built by Pistoia in the 12th century. Its name remains enigmatic: no historical document connects it to Frederick I Barbarossa. What's certain is its function: from its summit, sentries watched over the Valdinievole to Lucca and the sea, communicating by light signals with neighboring towers at Monsummano Alto and Montecatini Alto. The entrance door sits 5.20 meters above ground level, following medieval military architecture standards.

The Rocca Nuova, also called Castruccio's fortress, was built in 1302 after Serravalle's conquest by the Lucchese, under the direction of Uguccione della Faggiola, then completed by Castruccio Castracani. It preserves its powerful walls, a central hexagonal-base tower and a corbelled turret connected by a parapet walk. The Gabella Gate—so named for its medieval toll-collection role—opens through a rounded arch on molded consoles. Entry is free.

The Oratorio della Vergine Assunta is considered by specialists a masterpiece of late Trecento Pistoiese painting. The frescoes on its walls, discovered by chance in the 1980s, reveal a Crucifixion facing the entrance, a Last Judgment on the right wall with the damned below and apostles at mid-height, and the Virgin with Christ in glory at the summit—colors of startling brilliance and expressions of rare intensity for the period.

The Pieve di Santo Stefano and San Michele Arcangelo church (12th-13th centuries) form with the two towers the characteristic panorama of Serravalle, visible from the Firenze-Mare motorway. San Michele preserves a 14th-century fresco of the Miracle of San Biagio and a triptych by Bartolomeo di Andrea Bocchi dated 1438.

→

Walk along the ramparts and through Serravalle's medieval lanes: the entire village can be explored by following the single main street linking Porta Gabella to Torre del Barbarossa, passing through stone houses with arched doorways decorated with coats of arms, remnants of the 13th-century prosperity when Serravalle controlled the obligatory passage between Pistoia and Lucca on the ancient Via Cassia.

→

Visit Serra Pistoiese (850m altitude) and the Museum of Peasant Culture: 15km to the northeast, this small Apennine village presents in its ethnographic museum the tools and farming practices of the Pistoiese mountains, from charcoal production to high-altitude haymaking.

The Palazzo dei Capitani della Montagna, built in 1377 to house the Florentine governor charged with administering the Pistoiese mountains, displays 95 stone and polychrome terracotta coats of arms on its facade—the heraldic shields of each successive Captain, dated from 1444 to 1742. The first to reside here was Baldo Altoviti, a Florentine nobleman. The Captains' Loggia opposite was where justice was dispensed and notarial acts signed. The Marzocco column, bearing Florence's lion with the Republic's lily, recalls that Cutigliano was an outpost of Florentine power in the mountains.

The Chiesa della Madonna di Piazza (15th century), steps from the Palazzo, preserves a terracotta by Benedetto Buglioni representing the Virgin and Child with Saint Anthony Abbot and Saint Bernardino of Siena. San Bartolomeo church, whose campanile rises from an ancient Lombard watchtower, houses 14th-century works.

→

Stroll through the stone lanes of Cutigliano (678m altitude), certified among Italy's most beautiful villages (Bandiera Arancione). The village preserves its intact medieval layout: green sandstone fountains, vaults, covered passages and hand-cut stone facades.

In Gavinana, the Museo Ferrucciano honors Francesco Ferrucci, condottiere of the Florentine Republic killed here on August 3, 1530, in the final battle of the Florentine republican resistance against Charles V and the Medici. The house on the square—according to tradition, the very threshold where Ferrucci was finished off with dagger blows by Fabrizio Maramaldo as he lay mortally wounded and told him: "You kill a dead man"—was transformed into a museum in 1931. The enemy commander Filiberto di Chalon, Prince of Orange, also died that day beneath Gavinana's walls, struck by two arquebuses.

→

The Ponte Sospeso delle Ferriere connects Mammiano Basso to Popiglio via a steel-cable footbridge inaugurated in June 1923: 227 meters long, 36 meters above the Lima torrent, only 80 centimeters wide. It was built on the idea of Vincenzo Douglas Scotti, director of the Mammiano Basso rolling mill, to allow workers from Popiglio to reach the factories in 3 minutes instead of a 6-kilometer detour. In 1990, the Guinness Book of Records certified it as the world's longest pedestrian suspension bridge—a title it held until 2006, when it was surpassed by Japan's Kokonoe Yume bridge.

The Terme Tettuccio constitutes the most representative building of European Belle Époque thermalism: rebuilt in the 1920s by architect Ugo Giovannozzi, it combines a circular colonnade, an English-style garden with fountains, and four drinking galleries where the waters of the Tettuccio, Rinfresco, Regina and Leopoldina springs flow. The interior frescoes and mosaics are the work of Galileo Chini, Italy's leading Art Nouveau artist. Montecatini has been inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage list since 2021 in the "Great Spas of Europe" category.

Montecatini Alto, accessible via the funicular inaugurated in 1898—the oldest still in service according to Wikipedia—is the ancient medieval village perched at 300 meters altitude above the spa town. The town walls were razed in 1554 by Cosimo I's Florentines during their conquest of the territory. The village preserves some cobbled lanes, the Rocca, and panoramic views across the entire Valdinievole. The 8-minute journey aboard the historic red carriages "Gigio" and "Gigia" is an experience in itself.

The Viale Verdi and its "Passi di Gloria": since 2011, over 200 bronze plaques embedded in the sidewalks between Piazza del Popolo and the Terme Tettuccio trace the visits of the most notable personalities—from Giuseppe Verdi to Puccini, from Ruggero Leoncavallo to Marie Curie, from Gary Cooper to Audrey Hepburn, all who came to take the waters. The early 20th-century Palazzo Municipale, designed by Raffaello Brizzi, borders this promenade.

→

Thermal cure or wellness session at Terme Tettuccio or Terme Excelsior: Montecatini's four springs, which rise from depths of over 60 meters, produce sulfate-saline bicarbonate-sodium waters, traditionally used for digestive, hepatic and metabolic conditions. The thermal season runs from May to October.

→

The Teatro Verdi, built in 1950 in the thermal park, hosts the summer music season. Puccini, who stayed regularly in Montecatini, reportedly said it was one of his favorite places to compose.

The Palazzo Pretorio of Buggiano Castello, a building from the first half of the 14th century, displays on its restored facade 57 coats of arms of the podestàs who governed the commune from the 14th to 17th centuries. It houses the municipal historical archive and 15th-century frescoes in its halls. It's one of the most complete examples of medieval civic architecture in the Valdinievole.

The church of Madonna della Salute e San Nicolao, founded as a Benedictine monastery in the 11th century, features an austere 12th-century Romanesque campanile. The three-nave interior preserves a 13th-century baptismal font, 16th-century paintings by G. Brina, and the remains of an adjoining cloister. The castle is documented from 1038 when the "da Buggiano" family founded the abbey there.

The Rocca and its clock tower, at the village's highest point, are the remnants of the defensive structure erected by the "da Buggiano" family who, in 1167, obtained from the Emperor a diploma confirming their possessions. The clock tower was completed with its mechanism in 1510.

Uzzano Castello, 3km from Buggiano, is a hilltop village perched on the hill dominating the Pescia valley. It's renowned for producing Bianco della Valdinievole, a DOC white wine from Trebbiano Toscano grapes. The medieval castle and its white limestone lanes can be visited in an hour.

→

Walk through the citrus gardens of Buggiano Castello: the exceptionally mild microclimate of the village—sheltered from the north and open to the south—has favored since the Middle Ages the cultivation of orange, lemon and citron trees in private gardens, hence the nickname "Village of Citrus." Every two years, the village cultural association organizes "La campagna dentro le mura" (The countryside within the walls), opening these secret gardens to the public.

→

Olive oil and wine tasting from the Buggiano hills: the terraced olive groves surrounding the village produce a recognized IGP olive oil. Several estates offer cellar visits and farm tastings.

Pescia Cathedral is mentioned as early as 951 in the form "Sita loco Piscia." Elevated to episcopal status by Pope Benedict XIII in 1726, it preserves its sober baroque architecture. Pescia was declared a noble city by Cosimo III de' Medici in 1699 and received on that occasion the Porta Fiorentina, a commemorative triumphal arch with the peculiarity of not being connected to any walls—built solely to celebrate the elevation in status.

The Palazzo del Vicario and Piazza Grande form the heart of Pescia's civic center, structured in the 13th century in "fifths"—four quarters on the right bank of the Pescia river and one on the left bank—with a fortified bridge ensuring the connection. This double town center (religious cathedral pole / civic palazzo pole) is an urban organization preserved intact since the Middle Ages.

San Francesco church preserves a collection of 14th-15th century paintings including the Triptych by Bonaventura Berlinghieri (1235), one of the earliest representations of Saint Francis of Assisi painted during his lifetime or shortly after his death, commissioned by the Franciscans of Pescia.

→

Pescia's flower market: the city is Italy's capital of cut flowers, primarily carnations. The tradition dates back to the 1920s-1930s when, after the decline of silk manufactures and paper mills, farmers converted their land to horticulture. The first covered reinforced concrete market, built in 1950-1951 on plans that won awards in Brazil in 1953, is now reopened as a multipurpose cultural space. The region exports millions of flowers and indoor plants throughout Europe.

→

Stroll along the lungofiume of the Pescia: the river flows through the city between the two medieval poles, beneath ancient bridges. The landscaped banks offer a pleasant walk between gardens and palace facades.

The Historic Garden of Villa Garzoni is one of Europe's best-preserved Italian baroque gardens, classified as a National Monument in 1909 and 1912, and integrated in 2020 into the European Cultural Route of Historic Gardens. The Garzoni family, originally from Pescia, bought the castle in 1333; major garden works began in 1652 under Romano Garzoni. The steep hillside was transformed into a theatrical green amphitheater: terraces, monumental staircases, artificial grottoes, white mythological statues, water jets, a boxwood theater and labyrinth succeed each other in rigorous baroque staging. Napoleon Bonaparte, King Victor Emmanuel III and the American ambassador number among the villa's illustrious visitors. This is where Angiolina Orzali, Carlo Lorenzini's mother, worked and met her future husband.

The Pinocchio Monumental Park was inaugurated in 1953 for the 70th anniversary of the book's publication, thanks to fundraising among Italian schoolchildren initiated by Pescia mayor Rolando Anzillotti. The bronze sculptures of characters from the tale are signed by major artists including Emilio Greco and Pietro Consagra; architectural installations were entrusted to Marco Zanuso in the 1960s-1970s. The world's tallest wooden Pinocchio, 16 meters high, was inaugurated in 2009 by the Fondazione Collodi.

The Pieve di San Bartolomeo, at the highest point of the medieval village of Collodi (244m altitude), is mentioned from the 12th century. The steeply sloping village—stone houses clustered like a cascade from the pieve down to the Villa Garzoni gardens below—stood on the border between the Republic of Lucca and the Grand Duchy of Tuscany, who disputed it for centuries before Collodi became a Lucchese possession in 1442.

→

Casa delle Farfalle (Butterfly House): a tropical greenhouse installed in the Garzoni garden, where hundreds of butterflies from Asia, Africa and South America fly freely in vegetation recreating their natural habitat. Included in the combined Pinocchio Park ticket.

→

Wander through Collodi's medieval village: lanes too narrow for cars, limestone staircases and stone arches lead through the traffic-free village from the gardens below to the pieve above. Carlo Lorenzini himself described the village as "a long streak of old houses blackened by time climbing up the back of a steep high hill."
