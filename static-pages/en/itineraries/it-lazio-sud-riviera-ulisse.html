Ulysses Riviera: Secret Gardens, Ancient Caves & Southern Lazio Islands

Ninfa Gardens (1,300 species on medieval ruins), cyclopean walls of Norba (1.5 miles, 94 acres), Grotta Guattari (11 Neanderthals), Cascata Grande of Isola del Liri (89 feet in town center), Montecassino (founded 529, bombed 1944), Split Mountain of Gaeta, Tiberius's Cave at Sperlonga (marble Odyssey), Grotte di Pilato at Ponza (Roman moray eel farm).

From Ninfa, the medieval Pompeii drowned in roses, to Ponza's Roman caves carved to breed sacred moray eels: this 7-day circuit plunges into Lazio's deepest secrets. At Norba, the cyclopean walls of a city that chose mass suicide in 81 BC tower over the plain; at Circeo, Neanderthals preceded Ulysses and Circe by 50,000 years. Isola del Liri offers Italy's unique spectacle of an 89-foot waterfall in the town center, while Montecassino tells the story of destruction and resurrection of the West's oldest monastery. Gaeta hides in its Split Mountain the handprint of a converted Turkish sailor; Sperlonga reveals in Tiberius's cave an Odyssey sculpted in marble by the same artists as the Laocoön. The circuit ends at sea, on Ponza island and its gelato-colored Bourbon port.

Giardino di Ninfa — Twenty acres of English-style gardens created in 1921 by Gelasio Caetani on the ruins of a medieval city abandoned after its destruction in 1382 and the spread of malaria. Over 1,300 botanical species—magnolias, ornamental cherries, Japanese maples, antique climbing roses on stone ruins—surround the remnants of towers, seven churches, bridges and city walls. The New York Times called it 'one of the most beautiful and romantic places on Earth.' The name comes from a Roman temple dedicated to the Naiad nymphs, on a small island in the lake where the Ninfa river springs forth. In the 12th century, the town had nine churches; Pope Alexander III was crowned here in 1159 at Santa Maria Maggiore. In 1298, Benedetto Caetani (Pope Boniface VIII) purchased Ninfa for his nephew Pietro II for 200,000 gold florins. The 19th-century writer Gregorovius dubbed it 'the Pompeii of the Middle Ages.' Gelasio's English mother, Ada Wilbraham, guided the first plantings. Lelia Caetani, the last heiress and a painter, tended the garden like a living canvas and founded the Roffredo Caetani Foundation in 1972. Designated a Monumento Naturale by the Regione Lazio in 2000. Guided tour mandatory (~1h), booking at giardinodininfa.eu. Adjacent: the Parco Pantanello (250 acres of wetlands restored in 2004).

Castello Caetani di Sermoneta — Medieval fortress dominating the Pontine plain from 785 feet altitude. Built in the 13th century by the Annibaldi barons, purchased in 1297 by Pietro II Caetani (nephew of Boniface VIII) for 140,000 gold florins. The Maschio (keep) rises 138 feet. In 1470, the Camere Pinte were adorned with mythological frescoes by the school of Pinturicchio, including a room depicting the seven virtues and their opposites. In 1499, Pope Alexander VI Borgia excommunicated the Caetani and confiscated the castle: Cesare Borgia transformed it into a military fortress (rivellino, camminamento di ronda, Casa del Cardinale by Antonio da Sangallo), destroying the church of San Pietro and the Caetani tombs out of pure spite. After Alexander VI's death, Julius II returned the castle in 1504. Charles V was received here on April 2, 1536 with 1,000 cavalry and 4,000 infantry. The Sala dei Baroni (72 feet, four bays of Gothic arches) served as the banquet hall. Scenes from 'Non ci resta che piangere' (Troisi/Benigni) were filmed in the stables. Guided tour mandatory (~1h), Fondazione Caetani.

Medieval Borgo of Sermoneta — One of Lazio's best-preserved medieval towns, mentioned in the Aeneid (Virgil calls it Sulmo). Intact 13th-15th century urban fabric. The Cattedrale di Santa Maria Assunta (13th century) was built on a Roman temple to Cybele; it preserves Quattrocento frescoes attributed to the school of Benozzo Gozzoli. The Loggia dei Mercanti housed the covered market. The Museo della Ceramica (Palazzo Ada Caetani, 16th century) traces the history of local ceramics.

→

Stroll through Ninfa's gardens among ruins draped in roses and wisteria—observe the ponte romano and ponte del Macello spanning the river. After the tour, access the hortus conclusus (17th-century Italian-style garden with citrus grove and swans).

→

Guided castle tour: Piazza d'Armi, Camere Pinte, Sala dei Baroni with period table and canopy bed, dungeons with prisoners' graffiti (open Sundays Oct-March). Panoramic view over Monte Circeo and the Pontine Islands on clear days.

Antica Norba — Archaeological site on a plateau in the Lepini Mountains, towering 1,640 feet above the Pontine plain with sheer cliffs. Roman colony founded in the 5th century BC, nicknamed 'the Republican Pompeii of Lazio.' Its cyclopean walls in opus polygonal (massive limestone blocks fitted without mortar) extend 1.5 miles and enclose 94 acres—among the best-preserved in ancient Italy. Four monumental gates: the Porta Maggiore (26-foot jambs, semicircular bastion) is the most spectacular, with walls standing up to 33-40 feet tall. Two acropolises (major and minor) housed temples to Diana and Juno Lucina (goddess of childbirth, bronze inscriptions found). In 81 BC, during the civil war between Marius and Sulla, the inhabitants sided with Marius. Besieged by Sulla's troops, they chose to burn their homes and kill each other rather than surrender—the 'House of Burnt Seeds' still bears traces of this conflagration. The city was never rebuilt. Free access.

San Felice Circeo — Village perched on the Circeo promontory (1,775 feet), which Greek mythology identifies as the island of the sorceress Circe (Odyssey, Book X). Homer describes Ulysses climbing to a 'lookout rock' to see the island surrounded by sea—the profile of Monte Circeo indeed resembles an island when viewed from the south. The acropolis of ancient Circeii preserves megalithic walls in opus polygonal at 985 feet elevation. The medieval borgo centers on the Torre dei Templari (13th century) in the main square. Parco Nazionale del Circeo (founded 1934, one of Italy's oldest) protects the lowland forest (one of Italy's last), coastal lakes (Paola, Caprolace, Monaci, Fogliano), dunes and the island of Zannone. Grotta Guattari, on the promontory's eastern flank, was sealed by a rockfall 53,000 years ago and rediscovered in 1939: a Neanderthal skull at the center of a stone circle, first interpreted as ritual cannibalism, later explained by hyena activity. In 2021, remains of 9 more Neanderthals were unearthed—one of Europe's most important sites for studying this species. Cave visitable a few times yearly by reservation (Soprintendenza/Pro Loco).

→

Walk the paved streets of Norba between the two acropolises, explore the baths, cisterns and underground passages. Extraordinary view over the Ninfa oasis below and across the Pontine plain to the sea.

→

Climb to the Circeii acropolis for the megalithic walls and panorama. Possible excursion in the National Park: trail to Picco di Circe (summit 1,775 feet, 360° view) or walk in the mesophilic forest. Swimming at Cala dei Pescatori, the bay where legend says Ulysses landed.

Isola del Liri — A unique phenomenon in Italy: an 89-foot waterfall in the town center. The Liri river, at the height of the Castello Boncompagni-Viscogliosi (built around 1000 AD, residence of the Dukes of Sora from 1579), splits into two arms around a travertine outcrop, creating a natural island and two waterfalls: the Cascata Grande (89 feet, vertical drop) and the Cascata del Valcatoio. In the 19th century, hydraulic power attracted French industrialists: Charles Lefebvre of Pontarlier founded a modern paper mill in 1812 whose 'endless' paper was exported to London to print the Times. The town was nicknamed 'Little Paris' or 'Manchester of Italy.' The former Cartiera Lefebvre, swallowed by vegetation, was rediscovered in 1995. The former Cartiera Boimond still preserves its continuous machine. The Liri Blues Festival (since 1988, sister-city partnership with New Orleans) animates the square in July. In May 1799, during the triennio giacobino, over 500 people were massacred in the church.

Abbazia di Montecassino — Italy's oldest monastery, founded in 529 by Saint Benedict of Nursia at 1,692 feet elevation, on the ruins of a Roman temple to Apollo and Jupiter from ancient Casinum. Here Benedict wrote his Rule ('Ora et labora') that structured European monastic life. Benedict and his sister Scholastica are buried beneath the high altar. The monastery was destroyed four times: Lombards (577), Saracens (883), earthquake (1349), Allied bombing on February 15, 1944. That day, 142 heavy and 114 medium bombers dropped 353 tons of bombs believing (wrongly) that Germans occupied the abbey. About 400 civilian refugees perished. The octogenarian abbot Gregorio Diamare survived and refused to sign a Nazi propaganda memorandum. The Battle of Cassino lasted 128 days (Jan-May 1944): over 55,000 Allied dead, 30,000 German. Polish soldiers took the ruins on May 18. Rebuilt identically (plans by Cosimo Fanzago, 17th-18th centuries), consecrated by Paul VI on October 24, 1964. Free entry. The 11th-century bronze door (survivor), the tabernacle by Nicola Salvi, the tomb of Pietro de' Medici, and the Codice Cassinese 512 of the Divine Comedy are among the treasures preserved. The Polish military cemetery (1,072 graves) bears the inscription: 'For our freedom and yours, we Polish soldiers gave our souls to God, our bodies to Italian soil, our hearts to Poland.'

→

Admire the Cascata Grande from Piazza Boncompagni. Cross the Parco Fluviale along the Liri to see the Cascata del Valcatoio. Taste finifini (ultra-thin fettuccine with ragù), a local specialty.

→

Drive up to Montecassino via the scenic road. Cross successive cloisters: entrance cloister (former Apollo temple), Cistern cloister, Loggia del Paradiso, Benefactors' cloister. Basilica rebuilt with polychrome marbles. Crypt with bronze reliquary urn. Polish military cemetery.

Medieval Gaeta — The 'Pearl of the Tyrrhenian,' cited in Virgil's Aeneid, which derives its name from Caieta, Aeneas's nurse. Autonomous duchy in the Middle Ages (9th-12th centuries) with its own currency (the Follaro), the city traded throughout the Mediterranean. The Castello Angioino-Aragonese dominates the town: 13th-century core (Charles of Anjou, 1279) expanded by Alfonso of Aragon (1440), fortified by Charles V (150,000 sq ft). Gaeta was 'the key to the Kingdom of Naples.' Pope Pius IX took refuge here from 1848 to 1850; Francis II of Bourbon capitulated here on February 13, 1861—one of the final acts of Italian unification. The Cattedrale (SS. Erasmo e Marciano) has an Arab-Norman campanile and a baroque crypt from 1620 with frescoes by Giacinto Brandi. The church of the Annunziata (1321, Gothic façade) houses the Cappelletta d'Oro, where Pius IX and John Paul II both prayed. The church of San Giovanni a Mare summarizes the architectural influences of the 'city of a hundred churches' with its Arab-inspired dome.

Montagna Spaccata and Grotta del Turco — On Monte Orlando (Riviera di Ulisse regional park). The Santuario della SS. Trinità (11th century, founded with a Benedictine convent) is built at the edge of three deep fissures in the limestone cliff: legend says they opened at Christ's death. Descending the main fissure, you see the 'Mano del Turco'—a handprint in the rock, linked to a legend of a skeptical sailor's conversion whose hand sank into the stone. Saint Philip Neri is said to have lived here; his stone 'bed' is visible. To the left of the church, a 300-step stairway descends to the Grotta del Turco, a sea cave with emerald waters (access sometimes closed for safety). At the summit of Monte Orlando: the Mausoleo di Lucio Munazio Planco, circular tomb of a general of Julius Caesar who died in Gaeta in 22 BC.

→

Get lost in the medieval quarter of Sant'Erasmo: alleys, towers, stone staircases, 12th-13th century constructions. Lunch of tiella (octopus and escarole pie, eaten with hands) and Gaeta olives. Stroll along the lungomare Giovanni Caboto.

→

Descend into the Montagna Spaccata fissure, search for the Mano del Turco. If open, descend the 300 steps to the Grotta del Turco and its turquoise reflections. Climb to Plancus's Mausoleum for the panorama over the gulf.

Villa of Tiberius and Cave — In 1957, coastal road builders stumbled upon an imperial villa identified as Tiberius's praetorium (emperor 14-37 AD), cited by Suetonius and Tacitus. The core dates to the late Republican era; the complex extended 985 feet with sea-facing terraces, baths, port and barracks. The centerpiece: a natural cave transformed into a mythological landscape, preceded by a large rectangular seawater fish pond with an artificial island serving as a summer dining room (caenatio). Inside the cave, a circular pool (40 feet diameter) was surrounded by monumental sculptural groups—a 'marble Odyssey.' Four groups identified: the blinding of Polyphemus by Ulysses, Scylla's assault on the ship, the theft of the Palladium by Ulysses and Diomedes, and Ulysses carrying Achilles's body. A Ganymede abducted by Zeus's eagle crowned the entrance. The sculptures bear the signatures of the same Rhodian artists as the Vatican's Laocoön (Agesander, Athenodorus, Polydorus). In 26 AD, Tiberius nearly died in a cave-in—Sejanus saved his life. The Julio-Claudian gens considered itself descended from Ulysses and Circe: the cave faced Monte Circeo, mythical home of the sorceress. The Museo Archeologico Nazionale (1963, Giorgio Zama) was a victory for residents who opposed transferring the works to Rome.

Borgo di Sperlonga — Listed among the 'borghi più belli d'Italia,' a whitewashed village with blue shutters clinging to a promontory between two beaches. Narrow lanes, arches, stairways, sea views at every turn. The Torre Truglia (1532), symbol of the village, was built on a coastal watchtower, destroyed by the corsair Barbarossa then by the Turks (1623), rebuilt each time. The spiaggia di Levante extends toward Tiberius's cave; the spiaggia di Ponente toward the north. Lago Lungo beach (sand spit with dunes separating an artificial lake from the sea) offers a wild atmosphere.

→

Visit the archaeological museum: plaster reconstructions of complete groups, original Greek marble fragments, Faustinus inscription describing the works. Descend through the macchia toward the villa, fish ponds and cave. Observe the circular pool and the view toward Monte Circeo.

→

Wander the whitewashed alleys of the centro storico at sunset. Climb to Torre Truglia for the panorama. Dinner of fresh fish in a seaside trattoria.

Crossing and Porto di Ponza — Ferry from Formia (2h30 on traghetto, 1h15 on aliscafo) to the largest of the Pontine Islands (2.9 sq mi, 928 feet at Monte La Guardia). The porto borbonico (1772-1793), built on a Roman port by Ferdinand IV's engineers (Antonio Winspeare and Francesco Carpi), is an amphitheater of colorful houses—one of the Mediterranean's most beautiful boat arrivals. The island, inhabited since the Neolithic, was colonized by the Phoenicians, then the Greeks (8th century BC), then the Romans (312 BC) who made it a resort and exile destination. Benedictine monks founded the abbey of Santa Maria, destroyed by Saracens. After centuries of pirate raids (the Turks blew up the port tower in 1655), the Bourbons repopulated the island in 1771 with settlers from Ischia (20 boats) and Torre del Greco (50 boats for coral fishing). Ischian surnames (Scotto, Migliaccio, Mazzella) and the Neapolitan dialect survive.

Grotte di Pilato and island tour — Hypogean and underwater archaeological complex from the 1st century AD: 5 caves carved into the tuff, connected by underwater tunnels and an external basin. Called 'Antico Murenaio Romano': the Romans bred moray eels here, a sacred fish. The main basin had 5 openings closed by sluice gates (saracinesche) to control water and transfer fish. In 1997, at the bottom of the main cave, a white marble statue (Apollo or Dionysus), a tuff altar and oil lamps were discovered—suggesting cultic use. The caves were connected by a stairway to Emperor Augustus's villa on the Collina della Madonna. The name 'Pilato' comes from a legend: young Pontius Pilate, a Roman noble, was sent to Ponza to quell a revolt and gained his surname before being sent to Palestine. The Cisterna della Dragonara (town center) is one of three large Roman cisterns. Continuing the tour by gozzo: Chiaia di Luna (white tuff cliff in a half-moon, 330 feet high), Piscine Naturali (volcanic rock pools), Cala Feola (only sandy beach), Faraglioni della Madonna.

→

Climb the colorful lanes of the centro: church of SS. Silverio e Domitilla, Giardino Botanico. Rent a gozzo (~€80-150/day) or take an organized excursion for the island tour.

Étape

Inland Ponza: trails and heritage — The hilly interior (volcanic origin) is explored by a network of trails. The Faro della Guardia, at the southwestern tip (Monte La Guardia, 928 feet), is reached by an ancient path carved into rock: panorama over the open sea and Sardinia on clear days. The Roman tunnels (cunicoli from the 3rd century BC) cross the mountain between the port and Chiaia di Luna. Bourbon fortifications (1771-1793) dot the heights. The island served as confino under Mussolini: among political prisoners, Sandro Pertini (future president), Pietro Nenni and Giorgio Amendola. The church of SS. Silverio e Domitilla bears the inscription 'Cistercium Mater Nostra, Benedictus Pater' in memory of monasteries destroyed by pirates. Ferry return to Formia. If time permits, stop at the Tomba di Cicerone (traditional tomb of Cicero, assassinated in Formia in 43 BC).

→

Morning hike to Faro della Guardia (2h round-trip). Explore the Roman tunnels (headlamp useful). Final swim in a cove before the return ferry.
