Stresa

Isola Bella (320 × 180 m) was nothing more than a rocky outcrop inhabited by a handful of fishermen until 1630, when Count Charles III Borromeo launched construction of a palace as tribute to his wife, Isabella d'Adda. The project, interrupted by a plague epidemic, was finally completed in 1671 under Vitaliano VI. The building spans four floors and contains twenty-odd rooms: the Throne Room, Napoleon's Chamber (where Bonaparte stayed in 1797), the Music Room (which hosted the Stresa Conference of April 1935 between Mussolini, Laval, and MacDonald), and the Galleria Berthier with its 130 paintings from the 17th and 18th centuries. On the ground floor, six grottoes decorated with pebbles and tufa were built to provide cool refuge for guests during summer.

The gardens of Isola Bella rise in ten terraces shaped like a truncated pyramid, culminating at 37 meters. The statues adorning the Teatro Massimo are the work of Milanese sculptor Carlo Simonetta, created between 1667 and 1677. At the summit stands the heraldic unicorn of the Borromeo family, mounted by a Cupid. Two boxwood parterres frame the stage, where white peacocks have roamed freely for centuries.

Isola dei Pescatori (350 × 100 m) is the only place in the archipelago that has never been transformed: its narrow alleyways, fishermen's houses, and San Vittore church have remained virtually unchanged since the Middle Ages. Unlike Isola Bella, no noble family ever remodeled it—making it the most authentic island on the lake.

Isola Madre (220 × 330 m) is the largest of the Borromean Islands. Its 8-hectare park shelters a Kashmir cypress considered the tallest in Europe, along with collections of exotic plants from Asia and South America. Peacocks and parrots wander freely through the park, creating an atmosphere of a tropical botanical garden in the middle of Lake Maggiore.

→

Regular boats depart from the Stresa landing dock and serve all three islands in a loop. Plan for a full day to visit the three islands in order: Isola Bella first (the longest visit), then Isola dei Pescatori for lunch, and Isola Madre in late afternoon.

Como's Cathedral of Santa Maria Assunta is considered the last great Gothic cathedral built in Italy: construction began in 1396, ten years after the Milan Duomo, following a design by Lorenzo degli Spazzi, and wasn't completed until 1770 with the addition of the Baroque dome. The building measures 87 meters long and rises 75 meters high. Its façade, created between 1447 and 1489, features an unusual detail: two statues of Pliny the Elder and Pliny the Younger flank the main portal, honoring the two scholars born in Como. Inside, the 16th and 17th-century tapestries and paintings by Bernardino Luini—a pupil of Leonardo da Vinci—deserve close attention.

Adjacent to the cathedral, the Broletto is Como's former town hall, built in the 13th century. Its façade in gray and white stripes with a medieval tower is one of the best-preserved examples of Lombard communal architecture. The cathedral-Broletto ensemble forms a rare testament to seven centuries of civic and religious life in the same square.

The Como-Brunate funicular, inaugurated on November 11, 1894, climbs in 7 minutes over approximately 1,100 meters with a maximum gradient of 55%. It reaches Brunate at 715 meters altitude, a village nicknamed the "Balcony of the Alps" because, on clear days, you can make out Mont Rose and the Apennines. Brunate was a favored destination for Milan's bourgeoisie during the Belle Époque: its Art Nouveau and neo-Gothic villas are still clearly visible. Alessandro Volta lived here during his childhood.

→

From the funicular's arrival station in Brunate, a 30 to 40-minute walk on a marked trail leads to the Faro Voltiano, a symbolic 29-meter lighthouse built in 1927 for the centenary of Alessandro Volta's death. From the summit, the panorama over Lake Como, the Prealps, and—on clear days—all the way to Mont Rose is exceptional.

Villa Carlotta in Tremezzo was built in 1690 by Marquis Giorgio Clerici in a natural hollow between the lake and mountains. In 1801, it passed to Gian Battista Sommariva, former director of the Cisalpine Republic, who assembled works by Canova, Thorvaldsen, and Hayez, making it one of the leading centers of Neoclassical art in Italy. In 1843, Princess Marianne of Nassau purchased it as a wedding gift for her daughter Charlotte of Prussia on her marriage to Georg II of Saxe-Meiningen—hence the name Carlotta. Since 1927, a royal decree has entrusted its management to the Ente Villa Carlotta.

Villa Carlotta's botanical garden covers 7 hectares and shelters 150 varieties of azaleas and rhododendrons that bloom in spring. The park includes a fern valley, a bamboo grove with black bamboo, and a series of terraces with lake views. Georg II of Saxe-Meiningen, passionate about botany, significantly enriched the plant collections in the 19th century.

Bellagio occupies the tip of the peninsula that separates the two arms of Lake Como: it's from Bellagio that the Y-shaped view of the lake is most striking. Its stairway alleys (the Salita Serbelloni), terraced gardens, and boutiques make it the most photographed town on the lake. Villa Melzi, whose gardens open directly onto the lake, was built in 1808 for Francesco Melzi d'Eril, first vice-president of the Italian Republic.

→

A regular ferry service connects Tremezzo to Bellagio in about twenty minutes. The boat crossing offers the best views of Villa Carlotta's lake-facing façade and allows you to appreciate the Y-shape of the lake from the water. Departures are frequent throughout the day from the dock in front of the villa.

Monte Isola is the largest inhabited island in European lakes: 5 km², 600 meters at its highest point, about 1,800 inhabitants spread across 11 hamlets. No cars circulate here. The first document mentioning the island dates from 905, when it was property of the monastery of San Salvatore in Brescia. The tradition of fishing net manufacturing has been documented here for over 600 years; the local company La Rete, which revived the activity in the 1970s, has become Europe's leading manufacturer of sports nets. In 2016, artists Christo and Jeanne-Claude installed "The Floating Piers" here, yellow floating walkways connecting the island to the mainland.

The Sanctuary of Madonna della Ceriola stands at the island's highest point, at 600 meters altitude. It's Monte Isola's oldest church, founded in the 13th century. It preserves a 12th-century wood sculpture. The ascent from Peschiera Maraglio by trail requires about 400 meters of elevation gain and offers views over both shores of Lake Iseo.

In Pisogne, at the northern end of Lake Iseo, the church of Santa Maria della Neve houses a fresco cycle painted by Girolamo Romanino between 1532 and 1534: the entirety of the walls and vaults is covered with scenes of Christ's Passion, with sibyls and prophets in imposing poses on the Gothic vaults. Critic Giovanni Testori nicknamed the ensemble the "Sistine Chapel of the poor." The church, built in the late 15th century at the request of Pisogne's inhabitants, is free to enter.

→

The ferry to Monte Isola departs from Sulzano, a few kilometers from Iseo. The crossing takes less than 10 minutes. On the island, the coastal perimeter loop on foot or by bike is about 9 kilometers and takes 2h30 to 3h: you pass through the fishing villages of Sensole, Carzano (where shipyards still build mahogany wooden boats according to a 350-year tradition) and Siviano.

The Rocca Scaligera was built in the 13th century by Mastino I della Scala, Lord of Verona, to control Lake Garda from its most strategic point. The keep reaches 47 meters. The castle is entirely surrounded by water and possesses a fortified interior harbor—the only surviving example of a medieval lakeside port of this type in Italy. In 1405, the Republic of Venice seized Sirmione and reinforced the walls. The castle is the sole access point to the historic center: the entire medieval town extends behind it on the narrow peninsula.

The Grotte di Catullo, at the northern tip of the peninsula, are the remains of the largest Roman villa in northern Italy. Built in the early 1st century BC, the original structure extended over nearly 2 hectares with three levels, a panoramic terrace, and a thermal installation. The name "grottos" dates back to the 15th century, when the ruins overgrown with vegetation resembled natural caverns. Tradition attributes the villa to the poet Catullus, who died in 54 BC, though the construction dating remains debated. An archaeological museum displays pieces discovered on site.

Walk back up the peninsula along both lakeshores to appreciate the contrast between the calm waters on the west side and the stronger winds on the east side. The church of San Pietro in Mavino, erected in the 8th century and remodeled in the 11th-12th centuries, is Sirmione's oldest. It preserves medieval frescoes and stands on a rocky promontory above the lake.

→

The peninsula loop on foot from the castle gate to the Grotte di Catullo is about 3 kilometers round trip on a path along Lake Garda. The morning light on the waters and the Alps in the background is particularly beautiful at sunrise.

The Arena di Verona is a Roman amphitheater built around 30 AD in pink marble from Valpolicella, the third largest in Italy after the Colosseum and the amphitheater of Capua. Its exterior dimensions reach 152 × 128 m and 32 m in height, with a cavea that could accommodate 30,000 spectators in Roman times. An earthquake in 1117 destroyed almost the entire outer ring—only a fragment of four arches survives, visible from Piazza Bra. The interior, remarkably well-preserved, has hosted an open-air opera festival since 1913 that welcomes up to 22,000 spectators per evening.

Piazza delle Erbe occupies the site of the ancient Roman forum and constitutes Verona's oldest square. It's bordered by 16th-century frescoes of Casa Mazzanti, Palazzo Maffei and its 17th-century Baroque façade, and the Torre dei Lamberti (accessible by 368 steps or elevator, panoramic view over the rooftops). At the center, the Madonna Verona Fountain dates from 380 AD. Under the arch leading to Piazza dei Signori, a suspended whale rib has served as a talisman since the Middle Ages—according to tradition, anyone who lies while touching it will have it fall on them.

Piazza dei Signori, called Dante Square due to the poet's statue at its center, was the seat of power for the Scaligeri (della Scala), lords of Verona from 1262 to 1387. On its eastern side open the Arche Scaligere, an ensemble of Gothic sarcophagi suspended among spires and pinnacles—funeral monuments of the della Scala lords, considered among the masterpieces of 14th-century Italian Gothic sculpture.

The Casa di Giulietta at 23 Via Cappello is a 13th-century medieval palace that belonged to the Dal Cappello family—Shakespeare's Capulets. The famous balcony was added in the 1930s to meet the influx of visitors. In the courtyard, the bronze statue of Juliet has been replaced by a copy: the original was too worn from the tradition of touching her right breast, supposedly bringing good luck in love. The entrance walls are covered with love messages left by visitors from around the world.

Castelvecchio is a fortress built between 1354 and 1356 by Cangrande II della Scala on the north bank of the Adige. It now houses the Civic Museum of Verona, one of the most important art collections in Veneto with works by Mantegna, Giovanni Bellini, Pisanello, Veronese, and Tintoretto. The Ponte Scaligero (1356), reconstructed identically in 1951 after its destruction during World War II, connects the fortress to the opposite bank and offers one of the city's most photographed panoramas.

→

Attend an open-air opera at the Arena: the Verona Festival runs from June to early September with productions of Aida, Carmen, or Turandot staged in this Roman setting. The performance begins at sunset and spectators traditionally bring candles to illuminate the tiers at the beginning of the evening—a truly unique atmosphere.

→

Stroll through the historic center between Piazza Bra, Piazza delle Erbe, and Piazza dei Signori in the evening. The three squares are connected by pedestrian streets animated by shops and restaurants—Verona has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2000 for the exceptional density of its Roman, medieval, and Renaissance heritage in such a compact space.

Riva Del Garda

Riva del Garda occupies the northern tip of Lake Garda, sheltered by cliffs on three sides. The Torre Apponale on Piazza III Novembre dates from 1220: standing 34 meters tall, it's topped with a copper angel blowing a trumpet, the town's emblem. Its 165 steps provide access to a plunging view over the medieval harbor and surrounding mountains. The square is surrounded by 14th-century arcades and framed by Palazzo Pretorio (1375, built by the Scaligeri) and Palazzo del Comune rebuilt by the Venetians in the 15th century.

La Rocca, a 12th-century fortress surrounded by a moat with a drawbridge, is Riva's oldest monument. Its position at the lake's edge, with its feet almost in the water, is unique in the region. Today converted into a museum (MAG Museo Alto Garda), it houses archaeological collections covering Prehistory and Antiquity, a picture gallery, and sections devoted to the region's history up to World War II. Works by Hayez and Vincenzo Vela are on display.

The Venetian Bastione, accessible on foot or by a panoramic elevator carved into the rock, is a fortress perched 160 meters up on the slopes of Monte Rocchetta. Built by the Venetians in the early 16th century to control the lake's northern access, it's now abandoned but its terrace offers a vertiginous view over Riva and the lake. Goethe, Nietzsche, Thomas Mann, and Kafka all stayed in Riva del Garda, drawn by the mild climate sheltered by the Alps.

→

The Strada del Ponale is a former service road carved into the rock in 1851 to link Riva to the Ledro valley. Closed to traffic in the 1980s after construction of a road tunnel, it's now one of the most spectacular hikes in the region: the track follows a cliff face plunging into the lake for 3 kilometers, with tunnels carved through the rock and 180° views over Lake Garda and the Trentino mountains. The starting point is at the western exit of Riva.

The Castello Scaligero of Malcesine stands on a rocky spur rising 30 meters above the lake, on the Venetian shore. The main tower, pentagonal in shape, measures 31 meters at the base and rises 70 meters above lake level. The fortress changed hands among Lombards, Franks, Della Scala, Venetians, Visconti, French, and Austrians. Classified as a national monument since 1902, it now houses a natural history museum and a Goethe Room: in September 1786, the German poet stopped in Malcesine to sketch the castle, was taken for an Austrian spy by suspicious locals, and had to explain himself to local authorities—he recorded the episode in detail in his "Italian Journey."

The Palazzo dei Capitani, built by the Scaligeri in the 13th century and beautifully restored by the Venetians in the 15th century, was the residence of the lake captain charged with controlling trade in goods. Its classic Venetian architecture façade opens directly onto Malcesine harbor, one of the most picturesque on Lake Garda.

→

The Malcesine-Monte Baldo cable car, inaugurated in 1962, is the only one in the world whose cabin on the second section performs a complete 360° rotation during ascent—a world first put into service in 2002. In 15 minutes, it climbs to 1,760 meters altitude on Monte Baldo (2,218 m), nicknamed the "Garden of Europe" due to its some 3,600 plant species, about twenty of which are endemic. The panorama from the summit embraces the entirety of Lake Garda, the Brenta Dolomites to the north, and—on clear days—the Apennines to the south.

The South Tyrol Museum of Archaeology in Bolzano is housed in a 1912 building (former bank) and has sheltered Ötzi the Iceman since 1998, discovered on September 19, 1991 at 3,210 meters altitude in the Ötztal Alps by German hikers Helmut and Erika Simon. The mummy is preserved in a cold chamber at -6°C and 100% humidity, visible through a small window. Ötzi lived around 3200 BC (Chalcolithic, Copper Age): he was about 45 years old, stood 1.60 m tall, and weighed 50 kg. He was murdered by an arrow with a flint tip piercing his left shoulder blade (discovered by X-ray). His body bore 50 tattoos, probably for medicinal purposes, and he was equipped with a copper ax, bow, arrows, and clothing made of goat, deer, and bear skin. The museum attracts about 260,000 visitors per year.

Piazza Walther is the heart of Bolzano, an elegant 19th-century square where stands the statue of medieval Germanic poet Walther von der Vogelweide (c. 1170-1230), one of the most important authors of minnelieder of the Middle Ages. The Gothic cathedral (Duomo Maria Assunta), begun in the 14th century, is distinguished by its multicolored tile roof and richly decorated tower. The adjacent chapel of San Giovanni preserves a complete cycle of Giotto-school frescoes, one of the oldest in the region.

Via dei Portici (Arcade Street) is Bolzano's main medieval artery, lined with arcades, some dating back to the 13th century. This pedestrian street, characteristic of Tyrolean architecture, houses craft shops, galleries, and cafés. At the northern end, Piazza delle Erbe hosts a morning market where South Tyrolean apples are displayed (the region is Europe's leading producer) along with Speck Alto Adige IGP, the cold-smoked and air-dried ham that defines local gastronomy.

The Renon cable car, rebuilt in 2009 by the Leitner company with 3S technology, departs steps away from Bolzano's train station. In 12 minutes, its cabins climb from 274 to 1,227 meters altitude over a length of 4,500 meters. It replaced a rack railway inaugurated in 1907, deemed too slow (1h40 journey). At the summit, a small historic narrow-gauge train takes over to cross the plateau and reach the Renon earth pyramids, also called fairy chimneys.

→

The Renon earth pyramids are clay columns formed from glacial moraines, each topped with a protective rock block that slows erosion. They can reach several tens of meters in height. Three main groups punctuate the plateau, the most impressive being in the Rivellone valley. A trail with wooden boardwalks winds through the forest to offer several viewpoints over these natural formations. From the plateau, the panorama extends over the Dolomites and the Bolzano basin.

The gardens of Trauttmansdorff Castle extend over 12 hectares in a natural amphitheater on the heights of Merano, divided into 80 themed worlds organized into four main sections: forest gardens (bamboo groves, Japanese maples, sequoias), sun gardens (Mediterranean plants, olive trees, lavender), water and terrace gardens, and South Tyrolean landscapes. Empress Elisabeth of Austria (Sisi) stayed here twice, in 1870 and 1889, for health cures. The castle, now converted into a museum (the Touriseum, the first alpine museum devoted to tourism history), was voted "most beautiful garden in Italy" in 2005 and "International Garden of the Year" in 2013.

The Sentiero di Sissi (3.2 km) connects the center of Merano to the gardens by following the empress's footsteps. The route follows the Passer river, passes by Rubein and Pienzenau castles where Sisi resided, and climbs to the terrace of Trauttmansdorff Castle where her bench and statue are located. The winter promenade (Passerpromenade) along the river, with its Belle Époque buildings, recalls the time when Merano was the most prized spa town of the Austro-Hungarian aristocracy.

Merano's medieval historic center is organized around the Lauben (arcades), a covered passage dating back to the 13th century that runs through the heart of the city. These arcades house shops and cafés. At the northern end stands the princely castle of the Princes of Tyrol since 1470, and steps away, the 14th-century Gothic Duomo San Nicolò. The contrast between Alpine-Tyrolean façades and 19th-century Art Nouveau buildings gives Merano a bicultural atmosphere, halfway between Italy and Austria.

Bressanone (Brixen in German) is the oldest town in South Tyrol. Its Gothic cathedral dedicated to Mary and Saint Cassian was rebuilt in Baroque style in the 17th and 18th centuries while preserving its 11th-century Romanesque cloister, considered one of the finest in northern Italy: its double columns frame an interior courtyard adorned with medieval frescoes. The neighboring episcopal palace, residence of prince-bishops since the 10th century, houses the Diocesan Museum with a collection of nativity scenes, some dating back to the 17th century.

Lake Braies (Pragser Wildsee in German) sits at 1,496 meters altitude in the Fanes-Sennes-Braies Nature Park, a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2009. Its intense emerald-green waters owe their color to the fine suspension of limestone particles. The lake measures 1.2 km long by 300 to 400 meters wide, with a maximum depth of 36 meters. It's framed by the vertical wall of Croda del Becco (Seekofel, 2,810 m). It's the starting point for Alta Via n°1 of the Dolomites, the great traverse from Braies to Belluno. A dock and wooden chalet on the north shore have made it the most photographed lake in the Dolomites.

→

The loop around Lake Braies on foot is 3.9 kilometers and takes 1h15 to 1h30. The west shore, wide and flat, is accessible to all; the east shore is narrower, with steps carved into the rock and ledge passages above the water—more demanding but with the most striking views. In high season (July-September), car access is regulated: shuttles required from valley-bottom parking lots.

Cortina d'Ampezzo is nicknamed the "Queen of the Dolomites." Perched at 1,210 meters altitude in the heart of a white-rock mountain cirque, it hosted the 1956 Winter Olympics and will co-host them again in 2026 with Milan. Its Corso Italia, lined with luxury boutiques, runs through a center with a Belle Époque resort atmosphere. From town, the vertical faces of the Tofane (3,244 m) dominate to the northwest, the Cristallo (3,221 m) to the northeast.

→

The Great Dolomites Road (Strada delle Dolomiti, SS48) links Bolzano to Cortina in 117 kilometers. Construction began in 1901 by decision of the 1897 Austrian provincial law—the primary objective was military, the Austro-Hungarian Empire wanting to control its southern borders. The road was completed in 1909. From Cortina, the loop by car passes through three emblematic passes: Passo Falzarego (2,105 m) with the Lagazuoi cable car (2,835 m, site of World War I battles with tunnels carved into the rock), then Passo Pordoi (2,239 m, highest pass on the route, with a monument to cyclist Fausto Coppi and a cable car to Sass Pordoi at 2,950 m, "terrace of the Dolomites"), and finally Passo Sella (2,244 m) at the foot of the Sella group, with direct views of Sassolungo (3,181 m) and Marmolada (3,343 m, highest peak of the Dolomites).

Lake Misurina (1,754 m altitude) is the first body of water you encounter while climbing toward the Tre Cime. Stretching 900 meters long, framed by the Sesto Dolomites, it reflects the silhouettes of Sorapiss and Cristallo in calm weather. It's from Misurina that the toll road (about €30) leading to Rifugio Auronzo departs—the base for the hike around the Tre Cime.

The Tre Cime di Lavaredo (Drei Zinnen in German) are the symbol of the Dolomites and one of the most photographed rock formations in the world. They consist of three distinct pillars of dolomitic limestone: Cima Grande (2,999 m, the highest, with a nearly vertical 500-meter north face), Cima Ovest (2,973 m), and Cima Piccola (2,857 m). These rocks formed 200 to 220 million years ago by sedimentation in the tropical waters of the Tethys Ocean. Cima Grande was first climbed on August 21, 1869 by Paul Grohmann. The site has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2009. During World War I (1915-1917), the front line ran exactly here: trenches, galleries, and barracks carved into the rock are still visible on the trails.

→

The classic loop around the Tre Cime starts from Rifugio Auronzo (2,320 m, accessible by car from Misurina via toll road) and covers about 10 kilometers with 500 meters of elevation gain. The trail passes Rifugio Lavaredo, climbs to Forcella Lavaredo (2,454 m) to discover the south face of the peaks, winds around the north to Rifugio Locatelli-Drei Zinnen Hütte (2,405 m) which offers the most famous view: the three towers stand directly ahead, their north walls in full verticality. The descent returns to Rifugio Auronzo via the eastern valley. Allow 3 to 4 hours of walking.

The Grand Canal is Venice's main artery: 3.8 km long, 50 to 70 m wide, 5 to 10 m deep, in an inverted S-shape. It's lined by more than 170 remarkable buildings constructed between the 13th and 18th centuries. Four bridges span it: the Rialto (1591, resting on 12,000 elm piles), the Accademia (1854), the Scalzi (1934), and the Constitution (2008). Vaporetto line 1 goes up the canal from the station to San Marco, stopping at every stop—the slowest and most beautiful way to discover the city.

St. Mark's Basilica was founded in 828 to house the relics of Saint Mark, stolen from Alexandria by Venetian merchants that same year. Rebuilt after a fire in 976, it was consecrated in 1094 in its current form. Its interior decoration covers 4,240 m² of mosaics. The Pala d'Oro, a Byzantine gold altar retable, is set with 1,300 pearls, 300 emeralds, 300 sapphires, and 400 garnets. The four bronze horses on the façade are copies—the originals, dating from the 2nd-3rd century AD, are preserved in the interior museum to protect them from pollution.

The Doge's Palace (Palazzo Ducale) was the seat of the Venetian Republic's government for nearly a thousand years. Its Venetian Gothic style—arcades in white and pink marble lace, openwork loggia—is one of the most accomplished examples of this style in the world. The Hall of the Great Council (1,300 m²), the largest room in Europe at the time of its construction, could accommodate up to 1,800 patricians. The Bridge of Sighs (1600) connects the palace to the prisons and owes its name to the supposed sighs of condemned prisoners glimpsing the lagoon for the last time.

The sestiere Dorsoduro (hard back) takes its name from the rockier nature of its soil, making it less subject to acqua alta than other districts. It concentrates three major cultural institutions: the Gallerie dell'Accademia (Venetian painting from the 14th to 18th century, including Bellini, Giorgione, Titian, and Veronese), the Peggy Guggenheim Collection installed in her unfinished palace on the Grand Canal, and the Basilica of Santa Maria della Salute—a Baroque dome built between 1631 and 1687 in thanksgiving for the end of the 1630 plague, resting on more than one million alder wood piles.

→

Cross the Grand Canal by traghetto: a large gondola with two oarsmen that connects the two banks for €2, in 3 minutes. You travel standing, like the Venetians. Several crossing points exist between Santa Sofia and San Tomà. It's the only way to cross the canal without using one of the four bridges.

→

Visit St. Mark's Basilica early in the morning (opening 9:45am) or book a slot online to avoid lines. Basic entry is free but access to the museum, Pala d'Oro, and horses is paid separately. No entry with luggage—paid lockers are available nearby.

Murano is an island 1.5 km north of Venice, famous for its glassmaking since 1291—the year the Venetian Republic ordered the transfer of all the city's furnaces to the island to reduce fire risks. Master glassmakers there enjoyed exceptional privileges: their daughters could marry Venetian nobles. The Museo del Vetro traces the evolution of the technique since Antiquity. The Basilica of Santi Maria e Donato (12th century) preserves a Byzantine floor mosaic among the best preserved in Italy.

Burano is an island 7 km from Venice, recognizable by its brightly painted houses—red, yellow, blue, green. Tradition has it that fishermen chose these colors to find their own house in the lagoon fog. The island is also known for its needle lace, inherited from the 16th century, displayed at the Scuola dei Merletti (lace school). Its bell tower leans noticeably—visible from anywhere on the island.

Torcello is the first inhabited island of the Venetian lagoon, settled as early as the 5th-6th century by refugees fleeing barbarian invasions. Today almost deserted (about ten inhabitants), it preserves the Cathedral of Santa Maria Assunta—founded in 639, the oldest in the lagoon—with its 11th-12th century Byzantine mosaics including a Last Judgment on the counter-façade. The cathedral also contains Attila's Throne, a carved stone block that tradition says served the Hun chief—actually a seat reserved for medieval judges.

→

Attend a glass-blowing demonstration in one of the workshops open to the public in Murano. Most workshops offer free demonstrations, with an attached shop. Master glassmakers work glass molten at 1,400°C—a technique whose secrets were so jealously guarded that artisans who fled Murano risked the death penalty under the Venetian Republic.

→

Organize your day by vaporetto from Fondamente Nove: Murano in the morning (line 4.1), then Burano (line 12 from Murano or Fondamente Nove), then Torcello 5 minutes from Burano by shuttle boat. The return is via Burano and Fondamente Nove. Plan a full day—boats are not very frequent to Torcello.

The Rialto Bridge was built between 1588 and 1591 by architect Antonio Da Ponte, after a long debate between a single arch (simpler for boats) and three arches (more stable). The single arch won—48 m span, without joints, the bridge has stood for over four centuries. It rests on 12,000 elm piles. It's the first stone bridge over the Grand Canal, preceded by wooden bridges that regularly collapsed from the 12th century onward. Two rows of shops frame the central passage.

The Rialto market is divided into two side-by-side parts: the Pescheria (fish market) in a neo-Gothic building from 1907, and the Erberia (vegetables and fruits). Commercial activity at Rialto dates back to the 10th century, when this slightly elevated district was the first colonized—Venice was actually called Rialto (from Rivus Altus, high shore) in its early centuries. The market is active Tuesday to Saturday, early morning until noon.

The sestiere of Cannaregio, in northern Venice, houses the world's oldest Jewish ghetto—founded in 1516 when the Venetian Republic concentrated the Jewish population in an area isolated by gates locked at night. The word ghetto comes from the Venetian term for foundry (geto), as the area previously housed a foundry. Five synagogues dating from the 16th and 17th centuries still stand and some remain active.

The sestiere of Castello, in the eastern part of Venice, houses the Arsenal—a military shipyard founded in the 12th century that enabled Venice to dominate the Mediterranean. At its peak in the 15th century, it employed 16,000 workers (the arsenalotti) capable of assembling a complete warship in a single day. Dante drew inspiration from it to describe Hell in the Divine Comedy. The Arsenal buildings are undergoing cultural reconversion.

→

Get lost in the calli (alleyways) and campi (small squares) of San Polo and Dorsoduro, far from the main tourist arteries. Venice has about 3,000 streets, most less than 2 m wide. Moving away from San Marco and the Rialto, the city becomes a village again: children play on the campi, residents do their shopping, delivery boats supply grocery stores. It's the Venice that tourists who stick to the main routes never see.

→

Head to Piazzale Roma (bus and car terminal) or Santa Lucia station by vaporetto or on foot. From Piazzale Roma, trains connect Venice to Milan (2h20), Bologna (1h40), or Rome (4h). For Marco Polo airport, the Alilaguna water bus directly connects St. Mark's Square in 1h15, or an ACTV bus from Piazzale Roma in 25 minutes.
