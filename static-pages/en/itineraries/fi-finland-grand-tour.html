Finland Grand Tour: 21 Days from Helsinki to Lapland

Finland is vast and discreet. Its treasures reveal themselves only to those who take the time—a capital founded by a Russian tsar set between two seas with its own parliament, a quartzite landscape that inspired Sibelius, wild rapids on the Russian border that nobody knows. Three weeks, and you'll understand why Finns don't leave.

Three weeks to complete the full circuit of Finland. A UNESCO fortress on water, the medieval castle where Duke John and his Polish wife introduced forks to Finland, the archipelago of 20,000 islands with free ferries, the autonomous territory of Åland with its own parliament, the Arctic Circle, Lake Saimaa and its endemic ringed seal, Savonlinna's opera castle, and the quartzite hills of Koli where Sibelius composed. A route that demands time—and rewards it with interest.

  • Turku and archipelago: medieval old towns and 20,000 islands
  • Åland: autonomous territory between Finland and Sweden, oldest champagne in the world
  • Rovaniemi: Arctic Circle, northern lights, and Santa Claus
  • Savonlinna: medieval opera castle on Lake Saimaa
  • Koli: national landscape that inspired Sibelius

Suomenlinna Fortress sits just 20 minutes by ferry from Market Square—it's the first thing you should see in Helsinki. Built by Sweden starting in 1748 under Admiral Augustin Ehrensvärd to counter Russian expansion, it mobilized over 6,500 Finnish and Swedish soldiers and was one of the largest construction projects in the Swedish empire during the 18th century. Listed as a UNESCO site in 1991, it's still home to 850 permanent residents. Plan half a day: the ramparts, tunnels, King's Gate, museums, and harbor views are all worth your time. In summer, locals picnic on the bastions in the evening—arrive early in the morning to beat the tour groups.

Helsinki Cathedral dominates Senate Square with its green dome visible from the harbor. Carl Ludvig Engel worked on it from 1818 until his death in 1840; it was completed twelve years later by other architects. Built as St. Nicholas Church in honor of Tsar Nicholas I, it was renamed at Finnish independence in 1917. Climb the steps from the square—the panorama across Engel's neoclassical ensemble is one of the most harmonious urban views in Helsinki. A bell chimes every day at 5:49 PM.

Temppeliaukio Church is carved directly into bedrock—architects Timo and Tuomo Suomalainen won the competition in 1961, and construction began in 1968. The 24-meter copper dome rests on concrete beams that allow natural light to filter between the raw rock and the cupola. The acoustics are so exceptional that the hall regularly serves as a concert venue. Nearly 500,000 people visit it every year—arrive early in the morning to see it without crowds.

Uspenski Orthodox Cathedral overlooks the harbor from its red-brick hill, crowned with golden domes. Designed by Russian architect Aleksey Gornostayev and completed in 1868, it's the largest orthodox cathedral in Western Europe. The view from the terrace across the harbor to Suomenlinna island in the background is one of the finest in Helsinki—visit in the evening when the light slants low.

→

Market Square—Kauppatori—is Helsinki's maritime heart: smoked salmon, wild berries, local handicrafts in colorful stalls by the waterfront. It's also the departure point for ferries to Suomenlinna. Arrive in the morning for the best produce; in summer, restaurant terraces serve until evening.

→

The Design District spans 25 streets and over 200 shops, galleries, and workshops in the southern quarter. The Design Museum, in an 1873 building, traces how a newly independent country built a global visual identity—Marimekko, Iittala, Fiskars. Allow 2 hours depending on your interest in design.

Kallio is Helsinki's vibrant, alternative quarter—far from the usual tourist circuits. Kallio Church, designed by Lars Sonck and completed in 1912 in National Romantic style, dominates the neighborhood from its hill; its bells play a Sibelius chorale. Just below, Hakaniemi Market Hall (1914) is housed in renovated red brick—this is where locals buy their smoked salmon and artisan cheeses, not at Kauppatori.

Ateneum, Finland's National Gallery, occupies a Neo-Renaissance building from 1887 opposite the central railway station. Its collection spans two centuries of Finnish art, from 19th-century masters—Edelfelt, Schjerfbeck, Gallen-Kallela—to 20th-century expressionists. This is where you grasp how a country built a cultural identity from almost nothing. Allow 1.5 to 2 hours.

Kiasma, the museum of contemporary art designed by Steven Holl and opened in 1998, is an architectural sculpture at the heart of the city—its curved facade plays with natural light throughout the day. The temporary exhibitions are among the sharpest in the Nordic countries. If you only have time for one museum, choose according to taste: Ateneum for classic Finnish art, Kiasma for contemporary.

→

Löyly public sauna, on the former Hernesaari docks, blends contemporary architecture (wood, glass, sea views) with Finnish tradition—plunge into the Baltic Sea directly from the terrace. It's one of the best sauna experiences in Helsinki. Book ahead on weekends: slots fill fast.

Tampere as a day trip from Helsinki—the train takes 1h45 each way, leave early. The Finlayson Quarter was born in 1820 when Scottish industrialist James Finlayson opened a textile workshop on the Tammerkoski Rapids—this is where Tampere's industrial history begins, earning it the nickname 'Manchester of Finland'. The converted red-brick buildings now house museums, cinemas, and restaurants. In the Werstas Labour Museum, the massive Sulzer engine purchased in 1900 to power the mill still sits in its original location. Free admission.

The Moomin Museum, housed in Tampere Hall since 2017, is the only museum in the world dedicated to Tove Jansson's universe. These aren't reproductions—the original ink and watercolor illustrations, and the three-dimensional dioramas that Jansson herself and her partner Tuulikki Pietilä built by hand. Jansson donated the entire collection to the city of Tampere in 1986. Allow 1.5 to 2 hours.

Pyynikki Observation Tower stands on the world's highest gravel ridge—a glacial esker. The current tower dates from 1929, and from the 26-meter summit, the two lakes that frame Tampere—Näsijärvi to the north and Pyhäjärvi to the south—are visible simultaneously. The tower cafe serves munkki (fried doughnuts), the most famous in Finland. Don't leave without trying one.

→

Tampere Market Hall (Kauppahalli, 1901) is the place to taste mustamakkara—Tampere's black sausage made with blood and oats, served with lingonberry jam. The city's culinary specialty, impossible to find in this form elsewhere. Don't grimace before you've tried it.

→

Tampere is best explored on foot from the station—the Tammerkoski Rapids flow 700 meters through the city center between the two lakes. A walk along the banks from Finlayson to the central bridge gives you the best overview of the converted industrial city. Return to Helsinki for tomorrow's flight to Rovaniemi.

4
Day

Turku

Start with Turku Castle—the largest surviving medieval building in Finland, under construction since 1280 at the mouth of the Aura River. Its golden age came between 1556 and 1563, when Duke John of Finland established a Renaissance court there with his Polish wife Catherine Jagiellon. She brought jewels, velvet, and forks—then unknown in Finland—in her dowry. Her older brother King Erik eventually besieged the castle and imprisoned them both for three years—before a coup reversed their fortunes. Allow at least 2 hours.

Turku Cathedral is the national sanctuary of Lutheran Finland—consecrated in 1300, it's the mother church of the entire country. Its 101-meter tower was rebuilt after the Great Fire of 1827. Inside, look for the sarcophagus of Queen Karin Månsdotter, wife of King Erik XIV—imprisoned here after her husband's fall and died in Turku in 1612. It's the only royal burial in Finland.

The Luostarinmäki quarter is an anomaly—the only part of Turku to survive intact from the 1827 fire. About forty 18th-century wooden houses converted into a living museum with period-costumed craftsmen in their original workshops. Quiet and authentic.

→

The Aura River flows through the center, its banks lined with restaurant boats serving until midnight in summer. Walk from the cathedral to the castle (3 km along the waterfront) and return by the opposite bank—you'll see the whole city effortlessly.

5
Day

Pargas

Naantali, 16 km from Turku (35-50 min by bus), has a wooden old town descending toward a lively marina. Naantali Church, founded on a 1443 Bridgettine convent, is the country's second-largest medieval church. From the harbor, watch for the flag on Luonnonmaa island: when it flies over Villa Kultaranta, the President of the Republic is in summer residence.

Moominworld sits on Kailo island, connected to central Naantali by a pedestrian bridge. Tove Jansson received the first key to the park on its opening day in 1993. The experience is narrative—you step into the world of the books, not an amusement park with rides. Perfect with children, but Jansson fans of all ages find something here. Open summer only—book online.

Pargas is your first leap into the archipelago. The 14th-century gray stone medieval church is typical of Finnish-Swedish coastal towns. Ten minutes' walk away, the Pargas limestone quarry—150 meters deep across 70 hectares, in operation since the 12th century—is visible from a free, completely unknown viewpoint.

→

The Archipelago Route between Pargas and Nagu is driven by car with free ferry crossings every 15-20 minutes. No tickets, no reservations—you wait, you board. Plan the whole day to wander. Nagu, the marina self-mockingly dubbed the 'Riviera of the Finnish archipelago', is tonight's stop.

6
Day

Mariehamn

The Pommern is Mariehamn's first shock—a 96-meter four-masted barque built in 1903, the best-preserved sailing cargo ship in the world. Go aboard: crew cabins, hold, deck, masts. This ship rounded Cape Horn carrying grain between Australia and Europe, some voyages lasting four months. Next door, the Åland Maritime Museum traces three centuries of maritime tradition across three floors.

Walk down Torggatan, Mariehamn's main street, and step inside the Åland parliament—an autonomous region of Finland since 1922, demilitarized, Swedish-speaking, with its own parliament, stamps, and flag. Founded in 1861 by Tsar Alexander II who named it after his wife, it's the only capital city in the world created by a Russian tsar.

At the Åland Museum, don't miss the exhibition on the 'Champagnegaleas'—in 2010, divers discovered a wreck with 168 bottles of champagne dating from 1840, the oldest champagne ever found in the world. Some bottles were still effervescent when opened.

→

The Turku→Mariehamn ferry takes 5 to 6 hours—it's not a constraint, it's a crossing to experience. The ferries are large ships with buffet, bars, and panoramic decks from which the archipelago gradually reveals itself. Book in summer, ferries fill up on weekends.

7
Day

Mariehamn

Kastelholm, 25 km northeast of Mariehamn, is Åland's only medieval castle. Built in the late 14th century, it hosted Swedish kings including Gustaf Vasa, was the scene of witchcraft trials in the 1660s, burned in 1745, served as a prison then as a stone quarry for local farmers before being restored between 1982 and 1989. Open May to mid-September.

Jan Karlsgården open-air museum adjoins the castle—about twenty 19th-century farm buildings with windmills, barns, forge, and sauna. If you're there on a Wednesday in July, craft demonstrations take place.

The Bomarsund ruins—a few kilometers away—are what remains of a massive Russian naval fortress built to house 2,000 soldiers, destroyed in 1854 by Franco-British forces during the Crimean War. Hexagonal granite blocks litter the site in a landscape of forest and fjord. Free access, practically always deserted.

→

Åland is cycling paradise—flat terrain, quiet roads, free ferries between islands. Degersand beach in Eckerö (11 km from the ferry company) is one of Finland's finest: shallow water for dozens of meters, landscape of pines and red rocks.

Kamppi Chapel of Silence, opened in 2012, is a curved block of wood set in the middle of a busy commercial square—a secular space for contemplation open to all, with no religious services. The interior of blond spruce and the curve of the walls create unique acoustics and light. Step inside for five minutes for the contrast with the surrounding bustle.

The Sibelius Monument, in a park northwest of the center, was sculpted by Eila Hiltunen and unveiled in 1967—600 hollow steel pipes welded in a wave shape. The sculptor wanted to capture the essence of Sibelius's music rather than his portrait. After criticism, the composer's portrait was added alongside. Both together, it's a typically Finnish anecdote.

→

Return from Åland by overnight ferry to Helsinki (11h, arriving in the morning) or by ferry to Turku then train. This transit day leaves time for a short excursion to Nuuksio National Park—coniferous forests and lakes 30 km from Helsinki, accessible by bus. A good way to decompress before tomorrow's flight to Rovaniemi.

Arktikum is recognizable by its 172-meter glass corridor stretching toward the Ounasjoki River—architecture that evokes a passage to the Arctic. Inside: the Arctic Centre of the University of Lapland and the Regional Museum of Lapland. Exhibitions cover the northern lights (with visual reconstruction), Sami history, life in the Far North. Allow 2 hours.

Korundi, the House of Culture, occupies one of the few buildings in Rovaniemi to survive World War II—the former postal truck depot. Nearly the entire city was destroyed during the German retreat in 1944. Korundi now houses the Rovaniemi Art Museum and the Lapland Chamber Orchestra, the northernmost professional orchestra in the entire European Union.

Santa Claus Village sits 8 km north of the center, right on the Arctic Circle. The village welcomes over 700,000 visitors per year, year-round. In winter: husky safaris, reindeer sleighs, snowmobiles, and an official post office with the Arctic postmark. If you're traveling in winter, book your activities several months in advance—slots go fast.

→

Rovaniemi's urban plan was drawn by Alvar Aalto after the 1944 destruction—shaped like reindeer antlers viewed from the sky. The main library on Hallituskatu, also by Aalto, is worth visiting for its skylights with indirect lighting and original Artek furniture. It's a working library, enter freely.

→

In winter (mid-August to early April), northern lights are visible on clear nights. Guides organize snowmobile excursions to open sites far from city lights. Patience is required—expect several attempts. In summer, the midnight sun takes over: the sky doesn't darken between early June and early July.

Ranua Wildlife Park, 80 km south of Rovaniemi, houses over 50 Arctic species in natural enclosures: polar bears, lynx, wolverines, wolves. It's one of the few places in the world where you can observe polar bears in semi-natural habitat this close to the Arctic Circle. The park walk takes 2 to 3 hours depending on your pace.

→

A visit to a reindeer farm puts you in direct contact with Sami herders—feeding reindeer, history of Arctic herding, and in winter, a short sleigh ride. The experience is authentic and far from Santa Claus Village. Book ahead in high season.

→

A husky safari through Lapland forests is the most requested winter activity in Rovaniemi. Sleds depart early morning into snow-covered, silent forests—conditions are best between December and March. In summer, walking tours with the dogs are offered. Book several days in advance, mandatory.

Ounasvaara Hill east of the city offers the best panorama over Rovaniemi and the confluence of the Kemijoki and Ounasjoki rivers. In winter, illuminated ski slopes. In summer, hiking trails cross the birch forest to the summit in 1 hour round trip.

Jätkänkynttilä Bridge (Logger's Candle Bridge) spans the Kemijoki in the city center—it's illuminated at night and symbolizes Rovaniemi's forestry history. It's the classic viewpoint over the river for dusk photos.

→

Dine in a Lappish restaurant before leaving—reindeer stew (poronkäristys) with mashed potatoes and lingonberry jam, smoked Kemijoki salmon, Arctic berries (cloudberry, lingon) for dessert. Lapland cuisine is one of the most distinctive in Finland, and you won't easily find it again back in Helsinki.

12
Day

Lappeenranta

Lappeenranta Fortress is the city's oldest quarter—founded in 1649 under Swedish rule, fortified from 1722 by Axel von Löwen to secure the border with Russia, then captured by Russian troops in 1741. The ramparts now house three museums, an art gallery, and the oldest Orthodox church in Finland, built in 1785. Wander the cobbled lanes—the smell of tar and gunpowder mentioned by the local tourist guide is no exaggeration.

Lappeenranta harbor is the heart of the city in summer—terraces, markets, concerts, and direct views over Lake Saimaa. The giant sand castle, rebuilt every summer since 2004 with 3 million kilograms of sand by international sculptors, has a new theme each year. Vety and Atomi—meat and hard-boiled egg pasties sold since the 1960s—are found in market kiosks. Order them with ketchup, mustard, and onions, like the locals.

→

Lake Saimaa is explored by kayak from the harbor—rental companies offer half-day trips in the archipelago. With its 4,400 km², 15,000 islands, and 15,000 km of coastline, Saimaa is an inland sea. If you're lucky, you'll spot from the water the Saimaa ringed seal—only about 480 individuals remain in the world, all in this lake.

13

Lappeenranta

Imatrankoski Rapids formed 5,000 years ago when Saimaa waters breached the Salpausselkä ridge. Empress Catherine II of Russia came to admire them in 1772 with her entire entourage—this is how tourism began in Imatra. In 1929, a dam dried up the riverbed. Today, from June to August (Wednesday to Sunday at 6 PM), the dam opens for 16 minutes: 400 m³ of water per second surge through the gorges to Sibelius music. The show is free. Position yourself on Koskisilta Bridge for the best view. Arrive 20 minutes early—crowds gather.

Valtionhotelli, built in 1903 in Art Nouveau style as a castle by the rapids, is Imatra's most striking building—it replaced two wooden hotels that had burned. Even if you're not staying, the facade from the park is worth a photo.

The Church of the Three Crosses in Imatra is an Alvar Aalto work, completed in 1957 and considered by specialists his most original religious creation. The main hall divides into three independent naves by sliding partitions—a typically Aalto solution: functional and unexpected.

→

Kruununpuisto Park runs along the rapids from Koskisilta Bridge to 19th-century rock carvings—royal signatures left in the rock by illustrious visitors, including Emperor Pedro II of Brazil in 1876. An hour's walk in a shaded, historic setting.

Punkaharju Ridge, en route to Savonlinna, is one of Finland's most celebrated national landscapes. A narrow strip of land formed 11,000 years ago separates two lakes of the Saimaa system—the scenic route (7 km) runs along the ridge between centuries-old pines with water shimmering on both sides. Tsar Alexander I visited in 1802 and expressed the wish that it be preserved—protected since 1843, it's one of the oldest nature reserves in the world. Drive the route slowly or by bike.

Olavinlinna Castle, perched on a rocky islet in the middle of the lake at Savonlinna, is the northernmost medieval stone castle in Europe. Built in 1475 by Danish knight Erik Axelsson Tott to defend the Savo region, Finnish soprano Aino Ackté discovered it at a political meeting in 1907 and created an opera festival here in 1912. Today, 60,000 spectators per year come to hear operas in the castle's inner courtyards in July. If you're there in July, book well in advance. Outside the festival, the castle is open year-round.

Kerimäki Church, 23 km from Savonlinna, is the world's largest wooden church—45 meters long, 42 meters wide, 27 meters high, seating over 3,000. Completed in 1847 by architect A.F. Grandstedt. An anecdote circulates: the architect worked in centimeters but the builder read the plans in inches. The reality is less amusing but more coherent: the church was meant to accommodate half the region's population.

→

A cruise on a historic steamboat through the Savonlinna archipelago lets you see Saimaa from the water. Some offer Saimaa ringed seal observation trips in season. Inquire at Savonlinna harbor—schedules change each year.

Riihisaari Museum, opposite Olavinlinna Castle, traces the history of Lake Saimaa, the steamboats that plied it, and the ringed seal that lives there. Historic boats are moored on the pier. The inner courtyard with its rocking chairs offers a peaceful view of the castle—it's one of the best places for coffee in all of Savonlinna.

Savonlinna harbor market is the place to taste muikku—vendace from Lake Puruvesi, whose local catch benefits from European Protected Geographical Indication. Try them fried on site with a little cream: it's the city's typical dish, and Lake Puruvesi is renowned for its exceptionally clear waters.

→

Linnansaari National Park, accessible by boat from Rantasalmi (about 45 km), can only be explored by water—over 130 islands, none connected by road. It's the heart of the Saimaa ringed seal breeding area. Plan the full day, book the boat in advance.

Mikkeli

Mikkeli was the seat of Finnish Headquarters during the Winter War (1939-40) and Continuation War (1941-44)—Marshal Mannerheim commanded operations from this building. The Headquarters Museum, installed in the original premises, traces these years with maps, period objects, and archival documents. For anyone interested in Finnish military history, it's one of the best-documented sites in the country.

Mikkeli Cathedral, completed in 1896, is one of the purest neo-Gothic buildings in Finland—64 meters of red brick dominating the entire city. The single-nave interior is austere and imposing.

→

Neitvuori Hill, 20 km from Mikkeli, offers the best panorama over Lake Saimaa and the South Savo forests. A 30-minute climb from the parking lot—the view from the summit embraces a landscape of lakes and islands as far as the eye can see. Go if you have energy left after Savonlinna.

Joensuu

Joensuu was founded in 1848 by Tsar Nicholas I. The 1914 city hall is by Eliel Saarinen—the architect who would later conquer the United States with the Chicago Tribune skyscraper. Hilma, the North Karelian Museum, traces the region's history with a section on Orthodox culture, the Winter War, and—unexpectedly—the Finnish rock scene including Nightwish. Allow an hour and a half.

The Orthodox Cultural Centre of Joensuu offers permanent exhibitions on Finnish Orthodox tradition—icons, liturgical objects, calendar of feasts. It's the cultural dimension that distinguishes Karelia from the rest of Lutheran Finland, and it deserves 45 minutes of attention.

→

Taitokortteli, a block of old wooden houses in the heart of Joensuu, houses artisan workshops and a Karelian souvenir shop. It's the place to buy hand-made Karelian pasties (karjalanpiirakka) before heading into the forest tomorrow.

Koli

Ukko-Koli summit at 347 meters is the highest point in the southern half of Finland—and the panorama from the white quartzite cliff over Lake Pielinen is the country's most reproduced national landscape. Jean Sibelius came here in 1904 and composed on these rocks. The rock is among the oldest in continental Europe—remnants of a mountain range formed 1.8 billion years ago. The climb from Ukko Nature Centre takes 10 minutes.

The Summit Trail (1.3 km) connects Ukko-Koli, Akka-Koli, and Paha-Koli—named after gods from Finnish mythology. The site was considered sacred and wasn't colonized until 200 years after surrounding areas. Pirunkirkko cave (Devil's Church), 34 meters long, is at the summit—look for it, it's well hidden.

→

For a full day, take the Mäkränaho loop (7 km) from the summits—it descends toward ancient forests where Eero Järnefelt painted his autumn landscapes. Slash-and-burn meadows, birch and pine forests, rock faces. Bring sturdy shoes.

Nurmes

Nurmes has a remarkably preserved wooden quarter—Puu-Nurmes—sloping streets shaded by birches and houses from the 1880s-1930s, without crowds. The Karelian village of Bomba, on the shores of Pielinen, recreates traditional Karelian wooden architecture with decorated log houses, an Orthodox chapel, and restaurants serving authentic regional cuisine. Dine at Bomba—the Karelian stew (karjalanpaisti) has been simmering for hours and you won't easily find it again when you return.

→

The shores of Lake Pielinen around Nurmes lend themselves to beautiful evening walks. The lake is 93 km long—from certain points on the shore, you can't see the opposite bank, and you understand why Finns speak of their lakes as an inland sea.

20

Lieksa

Artist Eva Ryynänen's workshop at Paateri, 20 km from Lieksa, is one of the most singular places in eastern Finland. Ryynänen (1915-2001) spent her entire life here, carving wooden figures from Karelian mythology. She built with her own hands her house, workshop, a round gallery, and a small log church by a lake. This isn't an institutional museum—it's the trace of an entire life spent in the forest.

→

Ruunaa Rapids, 30 km from Lieksa, are Finland's wildest whitewater—30 km of Lieksanjoki River with six successive rapids toward the Russian border. Local operators offer descents by traditional wooden raft or kayak with campfire lunch. Allow 4 hours. Book ahead—it's the main activity in the region.

Valamo Monastery, in Heinävesi—about 100 km southwest of Joensuu on the return route—is the heart of Orthodox life in Finland. The community took refuge here from Lake Ladoga in 1940, bringing their icons. The main church, gardens, and monastery product shop (house wines, honey, cheeses) are worth a 2-hour stop. The place is open to non-believers and the atmosphere is peaceful—there's nothing touristy about it.

→

Return to Helsinki (400 km from Lieksa, about 4 hours with the Valamo stop). The Esplanade in the evening is the best way to conclude three weeks in Finland—the pedestrian boulevard between Market Square and the Design District, summer terraces, calm and slanting light. You leave from Helsinki and you return to it. Full circle.

What weather should you expect?

Winter (December-March): Northern Lights, winter sports, Christmas magic, polar night, Aurora season, unique Arctic experiences.

Fall (September-October): Ruska (autumn colors), comfortable temperatures, fewer crowds, mushroom picking, cozy atmosphere.

Spring (April-May): Nature awakening, moderate weather, longer days, good value, excellent for cultural activities.
