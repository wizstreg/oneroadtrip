Complete Ecuador in 25 Days: Volcanoes, Amazon, Pacific & Galápagos

Snow-capped volcanoes pierce the clouds, hummingbirds burst from the mist, waterfalls thunder through gorges, vicuñas trot across the páramo, whales breach offshore, sea lions commandeer park benches. Ecuador compresses an entire continent into a tiny country — this 25-day tour proves it.

From Quito's colonial center to Otavalo's ancient market, from the Mindo cloud forest to the perfect cone of Cotopaxi, from the turquoise crater of Quilotoa to the waterfalls of Baños, from the Amazon jungle to Chimborazo, from the Inca ruins of Ingapirca to elegant Cuenca, from the humpback whales of Puerto López to the giant tortoises of the Galápagos — 25 days to cross all of Ecuador, from the point farthest from the Earth's core to the seafloor haunted by hammerhead sharks.

Start at Plaza Grande, the beating heart of the UNESCO-listed historic center — one of the world's first two cultural World Heritage Sites, inscribed in 1978 alongside Kraków. Around you: the Palacio de Carondelet (the presidential residence since the 16th century), the Metropolitan Cathedral, and the Archbishop's Palace. On Tuesdays at 11am, catch the changing of the guard — it's highly photogenic. Sit on a bench, order a fresh juice from a street vendor, and watch the city pulse around the fountain.

Push open the doors of the Iglesia de la Compañía de Jesús, five minutes away on foot. Built between 1605 and 1765 in gray volcanic stone, its interior dripping in gold leaf will leave you speechless — the finest example of Baroque art in all of South America. Construction stretched over 160 years, which explains the layered styles: Mudéjar, Churrigueresque, Neoclassical, and dominant Baroque. The main altarpiece is considered the crowning achievement of the Quito School of Art. Budget a full hour.

Head to Plaza de San Francisco and its immense Franciscan convent, founded in 1537 on the ruins of an Inca palace. At 40,000 m² — thirteen cloisters, three churches — it's been dubbed the 'Escorial of the New World.' Inside: over 3,500 colonial artworks and the famous Winged Virgin of Legarda (1734) on the main altar, the original that inspired the giant replica crowning El Panecillo.

Late afternoon, stroll down Calle La Ronda, the most atmospheric pedestrian street in old Quito. Cobblestones, colorful facades, artisan workshops and tiny cafés line the way. Come evening, the vibe transforms: street musicians, open galleries, and the warm scent of canelazo — a hot cinnamon and sugarcane spirit drink. The perfect place to end the day.

If time allows — early morning or late afternoon — make the detour to Mitad del Mundo, 25 km north of Quito. The monument marks the equatorial line measured in 1736 by Charles-Marie de La Condamine's expedition. Straddle both hemispheres for the classic photo. Next door, the Inti Ñan ethnographic museum offers hands-on experiments on the effects of latitude 0° — water drainage, egg balancing. Allow 90 minutes.

→

Wander through the Rocafuerte neighborhood for a set-menu almuerzo lunch, rubbing shoulders with traditional herbalists, chocolatiers, and restorers of religious statues — a living Quito that never makes it into the guidebooks.

The Basílica del Voto Nacional is visible from everywhere in Quito — it soars to 115 meters, with towers clearing 78 m. It's the largest neo-Gothic basilica in the Americas. French architect Emilio Tarlier drew inspiration from Bourges Cathedral when he drafted the plans in 1890, but the cornerstone wasn't laid until 1892, and construction dragged on for nearly a century. The charming twist: the gargoyles aren't medieval chimeras but iguanas, tortoises, and armadillos — local fauna. Quitenos say the day the basilica is finished, the world will end. Climb the towers — good shoes are essential, the final meters involve iron ladders. The view over the historic center is the best in Quito.

Grab a taxi to El Panecillo, the 'little bread roll' hill rising to 3,016 m. At the summit stands the Virgin of Quito — 41 meters including the base, assembled from 7,400 aluminum plates by Spanish sculptor Agustín de la Herrán Matorras and installed in 1976. It's the tallest aluminum statue in the world, taller than Rio's Christ the Redeemer, and the only winged Virgin on Earth — inspired by a passage from Revelation. The 180° panorama over Quito is magnificent. Always take a taxi up; the area around the stairway isn't safe on foot.

→

Spend the afternoon at the TelefériQo: this cable car rides 18 minutes from 3,117 m to 3,947 m on the flanks of Pichincha — one of the highest urban cable cars in the world. From Cruz Loma, the view over Quito is dizzying: the city stretches 40 km long but barely 10 km wide. On clear days, Cotopaxi, Cayambe, and Antisana appear on the horizon, all snow-capped. Bring a windbreaker. If you're well acclimatized, push on foot toward Rucu Pichincha (4,696 m, 3 hrs one-way) — the trail is steep and sandy near the top, but the summit view sweeps the entire Avenue of Volcanoes.

Head north to Otavalo, 95 km from Quito along the Panamericana — a two-hour drive with Cayambe and Imbabura volcanoes on the horizon. On arrival, go straight to Plaza de los Ponchos, South America's most famous artisan market. Its origins trace back to pre-Columbian times, when Otavaleños were already trading textiles and goods. Open daily, it peaks on Saturdays with hundreds of stalls: hand-woven ponchos, alpaca blankets, silver jewelry, and hammocks. Bargaining is expected and part of the ritual. Weekdays offer better prices and a calmer atmosphere.

Walk the perimeter of Parque Bolívar and Calle Sucre. Otavalo sits at 2,530 m at the foot of Imbabura (4,630 m) and Cotacachi (4,944 m) volcanoes. Otavaleños are recognizable by their long black braids and traditional dress — a cultural identity remarkably preserved across the centuries. If you're here on a Saturday, don't miss the livestock market 1 km from the plaza, where cattle, pigs, chickens, and guinea pigs change hands from 6am.

→

Late afternoon, head to Lake San Pablo, 10 km at the foot of Imbabura volcano. Stop at the El Lechero viewpoint, where an ancient tree marks a sacred site to which indigenous people travel from across Ecuador for purification rituals. The 360° view over the lake, the volcano, and the patchwork of farmed hillsides is well worth the detour.

Leave early for Cuicocha Lagoon, 30 minutes from Otavalo. This crater lake formed 3,000 years ago when Cotacachi volcano erupted — 3 km wide, 200 m deep, with two small islands in the center shaped like guinea pig backs (hence the Quechua name: 'guinea pig lake'). The water shifts from deep blue to emerald green depending on the light. Take a short boat ride around the islands (25 min) for a close look at the color play.

On the way back, stop in Peguche village, 3 km from Otavalo. Its 18-meter waterfall is sacred to the Otavaleños — every June 21st at Inti Raymi (the Festival of the Sun), locals gather here for nighttime ritual baths in the cold water. Off-season, it's a peaceful 20-minute walk through eucalyptus forest. The village specializes in weaving — step into a workshop to watch traditional looms in action.

Passing through Cotacachi, 20 minutes from Otavalo, stop in this small town famous for its leather crafts — jackets, bags, belts, and boots line the main street at very reasonable prices.

→

Hike the Gorki Campuzano trail around Cuicocha Lagoon: 14 km of ridgeline between 3,100 and 3,500 m, with sweeping views over the crater and — on clear days — Imbabura, Cayambe, and Pichincha. The trail is marked and rolling but technically straightforward. Bring plenty of water and snacks; there's nothing on the route.

A long drive from Otavalo to Mindo (around 4 hours via Nanegalito). The landscape shifts dramatically — you leave the dry highlands and plunge into the Chocó Andean cloud forest at 1,250 m. Mindo is a former farming village turned naturalist paradise; BirdLife International has designated it an Important Bird Area. Ecuador holds 130 of the world's 340 hummingbird species, and many make their home here. Settle in and spend the first moments watching the hummingbird feeders at your lodge — the show never stops.

Head to the butterfly farm, 2.5 km from the center. Inside an enormous glass greenhouse, over 1,000 butterflies from 25 species fly freely around you. Hold a piece of banana in your fingers and they'll land to feed. With luck you'll witness one emerging from its chrysalis. At the entrance feeders, hummingbirds put on a permanent performance.

→

Ride the tarabita — a metal gondola on a cable — across the canopy to reach the waterfall sanctuary. Three trail options: Nambillo falls (15 min one-way, easy), La Reina falls (50 min, the most powerful), or the 5-waterfall circuit (2 hrs round-trip). Trails are narrow and muddy — pack hiking shoes, a rain jacket, and a swimsuit if you plan to take a dip.

→

Late afternoon, join a chocolate-making workshop. Ecuadorian cacao (the 'fino de aroma' variety) is among the world's finest, and several Mindo producers run the full bean-to-bar experience — tasting included.

A big driving day: from Mindo, recross the Pichincha foothills and drive due south to Cotopaxi National Park (4-5 hours total). Cotopaxi awaits with its near-perfect snow cone at 5,897 m — one of the highest active volcanoes on Earth. Its last major eruption in 1877 sent lahars sweeping down to destroy Latacunga and several valley towns. Dress warmly: the park is windswept and you'll be above 3,800 m.

Stop at Limpiopungo Lagoon at 3,800 m. On clear days, Cotopaxi's snow cone mirrors itself in the still water — Ecuador's classic postcard image. The trail loops the lake in 1 to 1.5 hours, easy and ideal for spotting Andean ducks, Andean gulls, and — if you're lucky — a carunculated caracara. Walk slowly; the altitude makes itself felt.

→

If you're well acclimatized, drive to the car park at 4,500 m and hike to the José Rivas refuge at 4,864 m — a steep 30-minute slog through volcanic sand. From there, push a further 45 minutes to the glacier's edge at 5,000 m. The thin air is a given; so is the view across the ice cap and the Avenue of Volcanoes. Warm up with a hot chocolate at the refuge on the way down.

From Latacunga, drive two hours west across the Andean countryside to Quilotoa Lagoon. This volcano last erupted in 1280, leaving behind a 3 km-wide, 250 m-deep crater filled with turquoise-green water that shifts color with the light. At 3,914 m, it's among Ecuador's most photogenic spots. The crater rim view is jaw-dropping. If you have the energy, descend to the lake — 30 minutes of steep trail down, though the climb back (40-50 min) is punishing at this altitude. For those who prefer, mules are available for the ascent.

→

After Quilotoa, drive down to Baños de Agua Santa (3-4 hours). You drop from the highlands into the Pastaza River valley, falling from 3,900 m to 1,815 m — the air warms noticeably and vegetation turns tropical. In Baños, walk the central square to see the Basílica de la Virgen de Agua Santa, a pilgrimage church whose frescoes document miracles attributed to the Virgin. Then climb to the Bellavista viewpoint for your first look at the town dominated by Tungurahua volcano (5,023 m), active since 1999.

The Waterfall Route (Ruta de las Cascadas) is the headline attraction of Baños: nearly 20 km heading toward Puyo, with some sixty waterfalls tumbling alongside the Pastaza River valley. Rent a bike — the road is almost entirely downhill. First stop: Manto de la Novia ('Bridal Veil') cascade, which you can ride over in a tarabita cable car above the gorge. Continue to Río Verde and the Pailón del Diablo ('Devil's Cauldron') — 80 meters of raw power that soaks you in 30 seconds. Rock-cut passages let you get behind the curtain of water. One of the most spectacular falls in South America.

On the way back to Baños, stop at Casa del Árbol, a former Tungurahua volcano observation post turned attraction. Its famous swing dangling over the void at 2,660 m delivers vertigo-inducing photos with the volcano behind — weather permitting. The site is small; budget 30 minutes.

→

Ride the waterfall route from Baños to Río Verde by bike. The descent is easy and the scenery shifts from Andean peaks to tropical vegetation. On the return, local pickup trucks load bikes and riders for the climb back to Baños — convenient and very local.

→

End the day soaking in the Baños hot springs. The hot sulfurous water flows straight from Tungurahua volcano, filling several pools at different temperatures (18°C to 54°C). Before you leave, try melcocha — a local candy made from sugarcane syrup, hand-stretched right in front of you.

From Baños, continue the descent toward the Amazon, driving to Puyo (1.5 hrs). Altitude keeps dropping — the heat and humidity build, vegetation thickens into dense tropical jungle. Make a stop at the Indichuris Viewpoint, 45 minutes south of Puyo: perched at 1,000 m, this lookout run by the Indichuris indigenous community ('children of the sun' in Quechua) gives a panoramic view over the confluence of the Puyo and Pastaza rivers and the vast Amazon canopy below. There are also caves carved with faces, and a swing over the void.

Don't leave without stopping at Paseo de los Monos, a nonprofit rescue center deep in the rainforest. Volunteers — sometimes French-speaking — guide you among rescued monkeys, coatis, parrots, and tortoises confiscated from illegal wildlife traffickers. Some animals, too habituated to humans or with lost limbs, will never return to the wild. It's a moving place, and the welcome is genuinely warm.

→

Take a dugout canoe on the Puyo or Napo river to visit an Amazonian Kichwa community. You'll be welcomed with traditional achiote face paint (a red vegetable pigment), take part in ceremonial dances, and learn about the jungle's medicinal plants. A short but genuine immersion into the culture of the Oriente peoples.

A spectacular climb from Tena back to the highlands via the Papallacta road (3-4 hrs). You rise from 500 m to over 4,000 m at the pass, then drop back to Papallacta at 3,300 m. The landscape flips from suffocating jungle to wind-scoured Andean páramo — a striking contrast packed into just a few hours. Pause for the panorama over the high-altitude lagoons and the swaying páramo grasses before your stop at the hot springs.

Before continuing to Quito, take the short walk around the Papallacta lagoons in the páramo. The air is crystal-clear, the silence total, and the tall grasses ripple in the wind. It's an end-of-the-world landscape at 3,500 m, shared with Andean ducks and mountain rabbits. Allow 45 minutes for the loop.

→

Soak in the Papallacta hot springs, Ecuador's finest. Perched at 3,300 m, the outdoor thermal pools (36-40°C) are ringed by mountains and mist. After 10 days of driving, hiking, and altitude, this is the perfect stop to unknot your muscles before the 90-minute drive back to Quito.

Day

Riobamba

A full day on the Avenue of Volcanoes, the 400 km Andean corridor named by Alexander von Humboldt in 1802. Driving south from Quito, you roll between two rows of volcanoes — Cotopaxi, Tungurahua, Chimborazo etched on the horizon. Stop midway at Ambato, about 2.5 hours from Quito.

Ambato, nicknamed 'the city of flowers and fruit,' flies under the radar of most itineraries but deserves a stop. Founded in the 16th century, it was destroyed three times by earthquakes (1698, 1797, 1949) and rebuilt each time. Walk Plaza Montalvo and visit the Casa y Mausoleo de Juan Montalvo — Ecuador's great 19th-century writer, who died in Paris in 1889, lies embalmed in a Greek-style mausoleum. If you pass through on a Monday, the street market is in full swing: indigenous people from the Pilahuín highlands come down to sell their harvest.

Arrive in Riobamba late afternoon after another 1.5 hours of driving. Perched at 2,750 m and nicknamed 'the Sultan of the Andes,' Riobamba sits at the foot of Chimborazo. Walk Plaza Maldonado, where the cathedral stands — its facade is one of the few surviving remnants of the original city, founded in 1534 near Colta Lagoon before the 1797 earthquake forced the move.

→

Climb to Parque 21 de Abril for Riobamba with Chimborazo as a backdrop — the volcano tends to clear in the afternoon rather than morning. Stay for dinner and try hornado (whole roasted pork), the Sierra specialty. The small restaurants around Mercado La Merced are the most authentic.

12
Day

Riobamba

Head for Chimborazo, 35 minutes from Riobamba. At 6,268 m, it's Ecuador's highest peak — and more importantly, the point on Earth's surface farthest from the center of the planet, outreaching Everest thanks to the equatorial bulge. The road cuts through a nearly 60,000-hectare wildlife reserve. Keep your eyes open: you'll almost certainly spot vicuñas, the wild relatives of llamas reintroduced here in the 1990s. They're not skittish — they'll crane their necks and size you up curiously — but drive slowly, they cross the road constantly.

Drive to the Hermanos Carrel refuge at 4,800 m — no special gear needed to get here. From there, a trail climbs 40 minutes to the Edward Whymper refuge at 5,000 m, named after the British mountaineer Edward Whymper, already famous for conquering the Matterhorn, who reached this summit on January 4, 1880 with the Carrel brothers. At this altitude, every step is deliberate — go slowly and drink constantly. When the sky clears, the glacier-capped summit rises above you.

→

On the way back, stop at La Moya community (3,200 m). Their Mountain Museum tells the story of the hieleros — men who climbed Chimborazo to hack blocks of glacial ice and sell them at markets. The last known hielero, Baltazar Ushca, practiced this trade alone for decades. His son-in-law hopes to create a memorial trail in his honor. Share a meal with the locals for a rare glimpse of high-Andean life.

A long drive (4-5 hours) from Riobamba to Cuenca, but the detour through Ingapirca makes it compelling. At 3,160 m, you'll discover Ecuador's most important archaeological site. First occupied by the Cañari people from around 500 AD, then conquered by the Incas around 1487, it houses an elliptical Sun Temple — unique in the entire known Inca world. The diorite stones are fitted without mortar with surgical precision. Charles-Marie de La Condamine was the first European to describe it, in 1739.

The guided tour (mandatory, included in the ticket) runs about 40 minutes, covering the Sun Temple, agricultural terraces, and a natural rock formation resembling an Inca face. The museum inaugurated in 2018 displays ceramics, textiles, and tools from both civilizations. Don't expect Machu Picchu — the site is modest in scale — but the story of the Cañari-Inca fusion is genuinely fascinating. If you have the energy, a 30-minute trail descends into the Intihuayco valley.

Arrive in Cuenca late afternoon. Swing by the Turi viewpoint on the hill south of the city. From there you'll have the best panoramic view of Cuenca and its sky-blue domes — the evening light sets off the red-tiled rooftops and church towers perfectly. The ideal opening shot for your time in this colonial gem.

→

Spend the evening at a restaurant in this city celebrated for its Andean cuisine. Mote pillo — white hominy corn sautéed with eggs and onions — is the local specialty. The small restaurants around the market are your best bet. Cuenca is wonderful to explore on foot at night: the pedestrian streets are well lit and the atmosphere far calmer than Quito.

Begin at Parque Calderón, the heart of the city, dominated by the Catedral de la Inmaculada Concepción and its iconic blue domes — the symbol of Cuenca, visible from almost anywhere. Construction started in 1885 and wasn't completed until 1975. Two additional dome-topped towers were planned, but the architect had to abandon them: the ground could no longer bear the weight. The interior floors are Carrara marble imported from Italy. Climb the 155 steps to the domes for a bird's-eye view of the historic center.

Across the square, the Catedral Vieja (El Sagrario) is one of Latin America's oldest churches, built in 1557. Now converted into a religious art museum, it surprises with pastel ceiling paintings — a world away from the gilded interiors typical of colonial churches. Free entry.

Steps from the cathedral, the Plaza de las Flores is a delight. The permanent flower market, run by women in traditional dress, overflows with roses, lilies, and orchids — Ecuador is one of the world's largest flower exporters. The white facade of the Carmen de la Asunción church (1730) frames the scene beautifully. This square has been called one of the most beautiful flower markets on Earth.

Walk down to the Pumapungo Museum, built on the ruins of the Inca city of Tomebamba — 'the Gate of the Puma.' The open-air archaeological site shows the remains of an Inca administrative center, and the adjacent botanical garden features over 200 native Andean plants. The indoor museum covers Ecuador's ethnographic history and indigenous cultures. Free entry.

→

Late afternoon, walk Calle Larga down to the Barranco neighborhood along the Tomebamba River. The colorful facades overhanging the water make for one of Ecuador's most photographed streetscapes. Stop in to visit a Panama hat workshop — locally they're called 'sombrero de paja toquilla.' The Cuenca region is the center of this craft, inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Heritage list. A quality hat takes weeks to weave.

Full-day excursion to Cajas National Park, just 40 minutes from Cuenca toward Guayaquil. This 28,544-hectare park, created in 1977 and designated a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve, shelters over 230 glacial lakes between 3,100 and 4,450 m. The páramo landscapes — high, windswept grasslands — feel remarkably Scottish in overcast weather. A stretch of the Inca Trail runs through the park for 4 km between Luspa Cave and Mamamag Lake. Entry is free but registration with a passport is mandatory.

For an accessible hike, loop around Laguna Toreadora (trail #1, 2.2 km, ~1.5 hrs) from the visitor center. If you're fit and acclimatized, push up to the Lagunas Unidas via trail #4 (4.2 km), which winds through a polylepis forest — these gnarled trees grow higher than any other tree species on Earth. The lakes succeed one another in total silence, broken only by birdsong. Pack warm waterproof layers: the weather changes fast at this altitude.

→

On the way back, stop at the Tres Cruces viewpoint, the highest point on the Cuenca-Guayaquil road (4,167 m). On clear days you can see the Pacific coast from here — the only spot in Ecuador where both slopes are visible at once. Back in Cuenca, recover your legs at the Baños de Cuenca hot springs (8 km from the center), fed by volcanic springs. Hot water at altitude is pure luxury.

Drive from Cuenca to Guayaquil (around 4 hours) — a spectacular 2,500 m descent to sea level. You'll pass through Cajas (a quick glance if you didn't stop yesterday), then cross banana and cacao plantations as the coast approaches. The tropical heat hits you all at once — be ready for the thermal shock after the Andes.

On arrival in Guayaquil, go straight to Las Peñas, the city's oldest neighborhood. Climb the 444 steps of Cerro Santa Ana through brightly painted facades — each landing has a number and a local story. At the top, the lighthouse and tiny chapel deliver a panoramic view over the Guayas River, the Malecón, and the city's rooftops. Go in the late afternoon for golden light and the buzz of the bars and art galleries lining the staircase.

Come back down to Malecón 2000, the 2.5 km riverside promenade along the Guayas. Revamped in the early 2000s, it runs from the artisan market in the south to Las Peñas in the north. You'll find gardens, fountains, monuments, and Parque Seminario — don't miss it: its trees are draped with dozens of land iguanas basking in the sun. Kids love them, but keep your distance: they bite.

→

Evening on the Malecón: riverfront restaurants serve shrimp ceviche (the Guayaquil specialty) and encebollado, a tuna and onion soup considered the country's best hangover cure. The nighttime atmosphere is lively, especially on weekends.

17
Day

Puerto Lopez

Drive north along the Pacific coast (about 3 hours). You follow the Ruta del Spondylus, the coastal highway named after a shell sacred to pre-Columbian civilizations. The landscape changes completely: dry tropical forest, giant cacti, and small fishing villages. Arrive in Puerto López, a laid-back town that serves as the base for exploring Machalilla National Park.

Afternoon: head to Los Frailes beach, widely considered one of the finest on mainland Ecuador. Set inside Machalilla National Park, it curves in a white sand crescent framed by cliffs and turquoise water. Access is via a 20-minute trail through dry forest (free, mandatory registration at the park entrance). There's no infrastructure on the beach — bring water and a picnic. On weekdays it's blissfully empty.

→

If you're traveling between June and September, scan the ocean from the Puerto López waterfront for humpback whales. They migrate every year from Antarctica to breed in Ecuador's warm coastal waters. Boat tours leave every morning from the port — book the day before. Watching these 15-meter giants breach out of the water is something you won't forget.

18
Day

Puerto Lopez

Full day on Isla de la Plata, nicknamed the 'Poor Man's Galápagos' — a slightly unfair label, but it gives the idea. This island about 40 km offshore from Puerto López is home to colonies of blue-footed boobies, frigatebirds, and albatrosses (April to November for the latter). The boat crossing takes 1.5 hours and can be rough — take seasickness medication if you're susceptible.

On the island, two guided hiking trails (mandatory guide) of around 2 hours take you right through blue-footed booby nesting sites. These birds have zero fear of humans — you can watch them from meters away, and the male's courtship dance, lifting each blue foot one at a time, is hilarious. After the hike, a snorkeling session is included: sea turtles, rays, and tropical fish crowd the waters around the island.

→

Back at the dock mid-afternoon, settle on the Puerto López waterfront for a fresh ceviche made by the local fishermen. Colorful fishing boats on the sand, pelicans circling the market stalls — the vibe is authentic and unhurried. Pacific sunset guaranteed.

Drive south along the coast: stop in Montañita (1 hour from Puerto López), Ecuador's best-known surf village. The vibe is pure backpacker-surfer-artisan market. If you're after peace and quiet, push 5 km north to Olón and its clifftop Sanctuary. Enjoy the beach before driving on to Guayaquil (3 hours).

On arrival in Guayaquil, cross the pedestrian bridge to Isla Santay, a nature reserve in the middle of the Guayas River. This mangrove island is free to enter and offers 2 km of elevated boardwalks over the water. You'll spot herons, iguanas, and mangrove crabs. A surprising pocket of nature just a few hundred meters from the city center.

→

Last night on the mainland before the Galápagos. The MAAC (Museo Antropológico y de Arte Contemporáneo), at the southern end of Malecón 2000, houses over 50,000 archaeological pieces tracing 5,000 years of Ecuadorian cultures, from the Valdivia civilization to the colonial period. Free entry. Dinner on the Malecón del Estero Salado for a more local atmosphere.

After landing at Baltra and transferring by bus and ferry to Santa Cruz, you arrive in Puerto Ayora — the Galápagos' most populous town with 18,000 inhabitants. Get your bearings: the waterfront follows Avenida Charles Darwin, where sea lions are already lounging on the public benches. Drop your bags and head to the Charles Darwin Research Station, 20 minutes on foot from the center. Founded in 1959 and inaugurated in 1964, this is where over 150 scientists work on conservation for the archipelago. Entry is free — just show your National Park ticket.

Next door, push through to the Fausto Llerena Tortoise Breeding Center. Since 1965, over 2,000 giant tortoises have been raised here and released into the wild. You'll see hatchlings at 8 cm and adults at 250 kg — the contrast is staggering. This is also where Lonesome George rests — the last Pinta Island tortoise, who died in 2012 at over 100 years old. His embalmed body is displayed in a climate-controlled room. Allow 45 minutes, and don't miss the botanical garden with its Scalesia affinis, an endemic giant daisy.

If you have the energy, hike to Tortuga Bay — 30 to 45 minutes from the center along a paved trail through Opuntia cacti and palo santo trees. You arrive first at Playa Brava: a long white sand beach hammered by surf, off-limits for swimming but packed with iguanas. Continue 20 minutes right to reach Playa Mansa — calm, turquoise water, with small blacktip reef sharks in the lagoon. Kayaks available to rent. Bring water and snacks; there's no commerce on site. Return before 6pm — that's when sea turtles come ashore to nest.

→

At dusk, go down to the Puerto Ayora fish market. Fishermen fillet fresh catch on the dock while pelicans, sea lions, and herons jostle for scraps — a hilarious and photogenic spectacle. Street food kiosks just behind serve grilled fish.

This morning, head to the Santa Cruz highlands. Take a taxi from Puerto Ayora (20 minutes; negotiate a round-trip rate). First stop: Los Gemelos, two enormous pit craters straddling the main road. Despite the name 'Twin Craters,' these aren't calderas — they're collapse sinkholes formed when underground magma chambers caved in, each around 300 m in diameter. The surrounding Scalesia forest — the world's largest daisy, endemic to the Galápagos — forms a dense canopy above. Stay alert: 8 of Darwin's 13 finch species live here, along with the vermilion flycatcher. Arrive early; birds are most active in the morning.

Continue to El Chato Reserve, 22 km from Puerto Ayora. This private 12-hectare property is one of the few places on Earth where you can approach fully wild Chelonoidis porteri giant tortoises. They weigh up to 250 kg and measure 1.5 m — you'll find them grazing calmly or wallowing in muddy pools. Keep 2 meters back, but the photos are extraordinary. Rubber boots are available if the ground is muddy.

Right next to El Chato, descend into the lava tunnels. These underground chambers formed when surface lava cooled while molten rock kept flowing beneath, hollowing out galleries you can walk through for several hundred meters. Bring a headlamp (or use your phone). The walls carry multicolored mineral streaks and the acoustics are remarkable. Watch your head in the low passages.

→

Back in Puerto Ayora late afternoon, stop at Las Grietas canal. Get there via water taxi from the main dock, then a 10-minute walk. It's a volcanic fissure filled with crystal-clear water between two lava walls — a shallow snorkeling and swimming spot, with a guide required.

Puerto Villamil

Early morning ferry from Puerto Ayora — about 2.5 hours of crossing, often choppy. Pack seasickness tablets. Arriving at Puerto Villamil on Isabela, you find a fishing village of 2,200 people, far quieter than Puerto Ayora. Drop your bags and rent a bike toward the Wall of Tears. The track follows the coast for 5 km from the village — sand then gravel — allow about an hour by bike.

The Muro de las Lágrimas is a wall of volcanic stone reaching 5 to 7 meters high in places, 3 meters wide and over 100 meters long, built between 1946 and 1959 by prisoners of a penal colony. President Velasco Ibarra established this prison camp in 1945 on the archipelago's most remote island — a natural prison surrounded by 1,000 km of shark-infested ocean. The construction served no practical purpose: it was forced labor designed to break the inmates. Locals say the wall is haunted and that moans can be heard at nightfall. Climb the steps to the mirador at the top — the view over Isabela is magnificent.

On the way back, stop at the wetlands to watch flamingos — the largest colony in the Galápagos lives here, in the lagoons along the trail. Best window is between 6am and 9am when they're actively feeding. You'll also pass through a natural mangrove tunnel sheltering all 4 endemic mangrove species of the archipelago.

→

Late afternoon, head to Concha de Perla, a small natural lagoon accessible via a wooden boardwalk from Puerto Villamil's dock. This is Isabela's easiest free snorkeling spot: calm, shallow water, and you'll swim with sea lions, sea turtles, rays, and tropical fish. No guide required. Bring your own mask and fins — or rent them in town.

23
Day

Puerto Villamil

The highlight of Isabela — and possibly the entire trip. Morning: board a boat for Los Túneles (Cabo Rosa), 1 hour from Puerto Villamil. These submerged lava tunnels formed millions of years ago when lava flows met the ocean: the outer shell hardened while magma kept flowing inside, leaving a labyrinth of arches, bridges, and natural pools. Access is tightly regulated — the National Park limits boat numbers and visitors daily. Book the day before through a local agency; a naturalist guide is mandatory.

The snorkeling inside the tunnels is extraordinary: the water is turquoise, calm, and shallow (2 to 10 meters). You'll see white-tip reef sharks resting on the sandy bottom, enormous green turtles (up to 2 meters across), rays (eagle, golden, manta), and with luck, Pacific seahorses — Los Túneles is one of the few places on Earth where they're reliably spotted. Above water, climb onto the lava arches to observe blue-footed boobies performing their courtship dance.

→

Afternoon: hike Sierra Negra volcano — the only one of Isabela's five volcanoes open to visitors. Its 10 km-wide, 300 m-deep caldera is the second largest on Earth. The trail is about 16 km round-trip from the trailhead (21 km from Puerto Villamil by taxi), with 200 m of elevation gain — allow 5 to 6 hours. You first climb through cloud forest, then the landscape shifts dramatically: black lava fields, fumaroles, cinder cones. The last eruption was in 2018 — you'll be walking on geologically fresh lava. Bring water, sunscreen, a waterproof jacket, and solid footwear. Guide mandatory. Note: combining Los Túneles and Sierra Negra in one day is very ambitious. Both depart early (6-7am). If you have to choose, Los Túneles is the more unique experience on Isabela; plan Sierra Negra on a possible third day.

Puerto Baquerizo Moreno

Morning ferry from Isabela toward San Cristóbal. Note: there's no direct connection — you go first to Santa Cruz (about 2.5 hours), then a second ferry to San Cristóbal (another 2 hours). Budget half a day for the journey with the Puerto Ayora stopover. San Cristóbal is the archipelago's oldest island (2.4 to 4 million years old), the first where Darwin set foot in 1835, and the administrative capital of the Galápagos.

San Cristóbal's signature outing: a boat excursion to León Dormido (Kicker Rock), 1.5 hours offshore. This eroded volcanic cone rises 148 meters above the water, split in two by a 20-meter-deep channel. Snorkeling in the channel is among the most spectacular in the Galápagos: hammerhead sharks, Galápagos sharks, white-tip reef sharks, green turtles, eagle rays. Be aware: currents are strong — this is not a spot for beginners. Allow 6 hours for the full tour including lunch and a beach break.

Back at the dock, visit the Centro de Interpretación, just north of Puerto Baquerizo Moreno. Inaugurated in 1994 with support from Spanish development cooperation, it traces the natural and human history of the Galápagos. Free entry. End at Playa Mann — sea lions everywhere, sunset over the bay.

25
Day

Puerto Baquerizo Moreno

Last morning in the archipelago — make it count. Leave early on foot from Puerto Baquerizo Moreno toward Cerro Tijeretas (Frigatebird Hill). The loop is 5.5 km and starts at the Centro de Interpretación. The lava-stone paved trail climbs through an arid zone thick with cacti, then the vegetation changes with altitude. At the summit (around 200 m), the panoramic view sweeps over Wreck Bay, Lobos Island offshore, and León Dormido in the distance. This is one of the few places where both frigatebird species — magnificent and great — nest in the same colony.

On the way down, detour to Darwin's Cove (Muelle Tijeretas) — a free, sheltered snorkeling spot between the rocks. Sea lions, sea turtles, marine iguanas, and tropical fish await.

Continue the trail to Punta Carola, a crescent beach ringed by volcanic rock formations. Stop at the Charles Darwin statue on the way. The beach is popular with surfers (waves up to 3 meters at high tide) and colonized by unhurried sea lions.

→

If your flight is in the afternoon, make one last detour to La Lobería, a beach 15 minutes by taxi from the center. This is the kingdom of marine iguanas — you'll see dozens of them on the black lava rocks, snorting salt from their nostrils. The atmosphere is wild, untouched, and perfectly captures the Galápagos magic. Soak it up one final time before heading to San Cristóbal airport for the return flight to Guayaquil or Quito.
