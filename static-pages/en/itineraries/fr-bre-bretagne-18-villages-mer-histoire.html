Embark on an unforgettable 21-day journey through Brittany's most enchanting coastal villages, where medieval ramparts meet windswept shores and ancient megaliths stand sentinel over turquoise coves. From the corsair city of Saint-Malo to the mystical forests of Brocéliande, from Belle-Île's wild cliffs to the pink granite chaos of Ploumanac'h, this comprehensive road trip unveils France's Celtic heart. Discover fishing ports frozen in time, half-timbered Renaissance towns draped in flowers, and legendary abbeys overlooking the sea - all connected by dramatic coastal paths and the world-famous GR34 hiking trail.

Saint Suliac

Saint-Suliac has been classified as one of France's Most Beautiful Villages since 1999. The narrow lanes, so tight that two people cannot pass side by side, wind between granite houses with arched doorways and thatched roofs. Fishing nets still hang from the facades.

The 6th-century church, founded by the monk Suliau, houses stained glass windows, wooden sculptures, and maritime ex-votos left by the families of Newfoundland fishermen.

Walk down the Ruette des Petits Puits - a 17th-century well gives the lane its name. Legend says it concealed an underground passage connecting the church to the harbor.

At the harbor, dories - flat-bottomed boats of the Newfoundland fishermen - sway facing the Rance River. Sailors from Saint-Suliac departed each year to fish for cod off the coast of Newfoundland.

The Beauchet tidal mill, listed as a Historic Monument, used tidal energy to grind grain. Rebuilt in stone after a fire, it operated until 1980.

At low tide, stone foundations emerge in the Vigneux cove. Listed as a Historic Monument since 1986, this site is believed to be an ancient fortified Viking camp where longships sheltered on the landward side.

→

Climb to the summit of Mont Garrot (73 m) for a 360° panorama over the Rance estuary, the harbor, and the Viking remains below.

→

Reach the Grainfollet oratory via the coastal path. In 1874, fishermen promised to build this sanctuary if all returned safely from Newfoundland. Twenty years later, the vow was fulfilled. At sunset, the view over the Rance is sublime.

💡 Accessible at low tide only via the coastal path
→

The Saint-Suliac peninsula loop follows the old salt marshes, Beauchet mill, and the Chablé menhir (5 m high, nicknamed 'Gargantua's tooth'). Warning: some passages are submerged at high tide.

💡 Impassable during tides exceeding 11 m

The ramparts of Saint-Malo, listed as a Historic Monument since 1886, form a 1,754 m loop around the walled city. Built in the 12th century by Bishop Jean de Châtillon, they were reinforced by Garangeau following Vauban's plans from 1691.

The Saint-Vincent Cathedral blends Romanesque and Gothic styles. Inside, modern stained glass windows recount the history of the corsair city. Jacques Cartier's tomb is located here.

Enter through the Grand'Porte, the oldest gate in the city (12th century). Above it, the statue of Our Lady of the Grand'Porte: according to legend, she stood against the flames in 1661 and stopped the fire.

The castle of Saint-Malo, built in 1424 by the Dukes of Brittany, is recognizable by its corner turrets. Duchess Anne of Brittany wanted it 'shaped like a carriage.'

Climb the Tour Bidouane, a 15th-century horseshoe-shaped powder magazine. From there, the view encompasses Grand Bé, Petit Bé, and the entire bay.

From the Holland Bastion, spot the mastiff niches below the walls. Every evening, these guard dogs were released around the city to prevent any intrusion.

Fort National, built by Garangeau in 1689, stands on a rocky islet facing the ramparts. Accessible only at low tide via a sand causeway.

→

Walk the complete circuit of the ramparts at sunset. Allow a good hour for the 1,754 m. Start at Porte Saint-Thomas to take in views of the Sillon, Fort National, then Grand Bé.

→

At low tide, cross the sand causeway to the Grand Bé islet. Check tide times: the sea rises quickly and cuts off access in 30 minutes.

💡 Essential to consult tide times before crossing
→

The great Sillon beach stretches 3 km facing the walled city. Rows of wooden posts (breakwaters) create a unique landscape at low tide.

The Port de la Houle is the beating heart of Cancale. Fishermen's houses, pressed tightly together, cling to the cliff. The 'back streets,' closest to the rock face, date from the 18th and 19th centuries.

The oyster market operates year-round at the foot of Pointe des Crolles. Oyster farmers open the oysters in front of you. Local tradition: throw the shells into the sea, it brings good luck.

The oyster beds form a geometric grid visible at low tide, covering 366 hectares along the coast. Oyster farming in Cancale is classified as part of France's Intangible Heritage.

From Pointe des Crolles, the view extends over Mont-Saint-Michel bay. This is the official 'Kilometer 0' of the GR34, the customs officers' path that covers 2,000 km of Brittany coast.

On Place de la République, a bronze sculpture pays homage to the oyster washers - women who sorted and cleaned shellfish by hand while their husbands fished off Newfoundland.

Walk to the Houle lighthouse at the end of the jetty. The panorama embraces the oyster beds, boats, and the harbor below.

→

Take the customs officers' path (GR34) from Pointe des Crolles to Pointe du Grouin. The trail alternates between coves, secret anchorages, and panoramas over Mont-Saint-Michel bay. Facing Grouin, the Îles des Landes shelters more than 1,000 pairs of gulls.

→

Head to Pointe du Grouin for sunrise or sunset. This promontory is the most advanced point of Ille-et-Vilaine - you can see both spectacles from the same rock.

→

Explore the 'back streets' of the upper town, less touristy, with their gray stone houses and quiet lanes. The upper town was the domain of shipowners, the lower town that of fishermen.

The Château de Combourg has stood since 1016 on a natural hill, flanked by four large pepper-pot towers. The writer's family bought it in 1761. The child lived there from age 8 through adolescence - a period that marked him deeply.

Inside, the tour passes through the writer's bedroom in the Cat Tower. According to his Memoirs, a peg-legged ghost accompanied by a black cat haunted the grand staircase. During renovations, a mummified cat was found behind a 16th-century beam.

The 25-hectare park, designed in English style in the 19th century by the Bühler brothers, contains a 250-year-old false cypress labeled 'Remarkable Tree' and the cross of Lucile, the writer's sister.

The Notre-Dame church, nicknamed 'cathedral church' for its imposing size, rivals the cathedral of Dol-de-Bretagne. Its stained glass windows are remarkable.

In the streets of this Small City of Character, spot the Maison de la Lanterne (lit by torches until the Revolution), the Templars' courtyard, and the Relais des Princes. Half-timbered facades testify to the medieval past.

→

Walk around Lac Tranquille - the writer gave it this name. A literary trail marked with panels retraces his youth in Combourg. Water lilies and castle reflections on the water create the perfect setting for a walk.

→

Three walking circuits start from the town: the heritage circuit (medieval architecture), the literary circuit (along the lake), and the historical circuit (in the writer's footsteps). Choose according to your mood.

→

The trail leads to the Ménage de la Vierge, a rocky chaos hidden under the canopy. Granite blocks form narrow passages that you pass through by squeezing between moss-covered walls.

The ramparts of Dinan stretch 2,650 m - the longest in Brittany. Ten towers punctuate the wall: Beaumanoir, Vaucouleurs, Saint-Julien, Beaufort, du Connétable, de Coëtquen, Penthièvre, Longue, Sainte-Catherine, and the Jerzual gate.

The Clock Tower rises 43 m high. Erected in the 15th century by Dinan's bourgeoisie to rival the clergy, it houses a 1498 clockwork mechanism, one of the oldest in Europe. Count 158 steps for the panorama.

Walk down Rue du Jerzual, steep at 35% in places, lined with 15th-17th century half-timbered houses. Glassblowers, wood gilders, and painters occupy workshop-boutiques.

The Saint-Sauveur basilica blends Romanesque, Gothic, Classical, and Baroque styles. Its Romanesque portal deserves a stop. Inside rests the heart of Bertrand du Guesclin, Constable of France who died in 1380.

Place des Merciers and Place des Cordeliers display the most beautiful half-timbered facades in the city. Wooden pillars support overhanging upper floors, typical of the 15th century.

The 14th-century castle, built by Duke John IV upon his return from exile in 1368, has a 34 m keep. It now houses a local history museum.

→

Descend Rue du Jerzual then Rue du Petit Fort to the port of Dinan on the Rance. The climb back is steep but the port is worth the detour: pleasure boats, waterside terraces, and an old arched bridge.

→

From the port, the bike path follows the Rance southward. On foot, the riverbanks offer a peaceful walk away from the medieval center's crowds.

→

The English Garden, laid out in 1852 on the former parish cemetery, adjoins the Saint-Sauveur basilica. Continue to the Sainte-Catherine tower for a plunging view over the Rance and the port, 75 m below.

Paimpont

Top hotels

The Abbey of Paimpont, founded in the 7th century and rebuilt in the 13th, is mirrored in the pond that borders the village. Its Romanesque portal, 17th-century woodwork, and ivory altarpiece classified as National Treasure deserve an extended stop.

Merlin's Tomb - two red slate slabs leaning against each other - hides in a beech undergrowth. Destroyed in 1894 by a treasure hunter, only these remains survive, still covered with offerings and little notes.

The Fountain of Barenton springs from a natural source in the middle of the forest. Legend says that if you pour its water on Merlin's stone - a neighboring granite slab - a storm immediately rises. Bubbles regularly rise from the bottom, a real geological phenomenon.

At the Val sans Retour, purple schist cliffs overlook a sunken valley where, according to legend, the fairy Morgana imprisoned unfaithful lovers. A water mirror stagnates at the bottom - the 'Fairies' Mirror.'

The Golden Tree, a gold-leaf sculpture planted in 1991 after a devastating fire, stands among five charred trunks. This memorial recalls the 400 hectares of forest destroyed that year.

In Tréhorenteuc, the Sainte-Onenne church blends Christianity and Arthurian legend. The Holy Grail stained glass window, the Val sans Retour paintings, and the white stag mosaic make this small church a unique case in France.

→

Walk the Val sans Retour loop from the Tréhorenteuc parking lot. The trail climbs to 'Merlin's Seat' (viewpoint over the valley), descends to the Fairies' Mirror, and reaches the Golden Tree.

→

Reach the Fountain of Barenton via the forest trail from the hamlet of Folle Pensée. The path crosses a stand of centuries-old beeches bathed in a mystical atmosphere.

💡 Boots recommended in wet weather
→

Circle the Paimpont pond by bike or on foot. Abbey reflections on the water, water lilies, and gray herons create a peaceful tableau opposite to the forest's dark legends.

Moncontour, classified as a Small City of Character, preserves its medieval ramparts almost intact. Six towers and two fortified gates encircle the upper town, perched on its rocky spur since the 11th century.

The Saint-Mathurin church contains six 16th-century stained glass windows classified as Historic Monuments. The windows tell the lives of Breton saints with a palette of blues and reds of rare intensity.

Overhanging 15th and 16th-century houses line the narrow streets. Half-timbered facades, sometimes leaning to the point of almost touching, testify to the prosperity of linen cloth merchants.

On Place Penthièvre, granite mansions align their sculpted porches. The central fountain dates from the 18th century. This is where the cloth fair that made the city wealthy was held.

From the walkway, the panorama embraces the Penthièvre countryside and the Côtes-d'Armor hills. The crenellations and machicolations are remarkably preserved for a wall of this size.

→

Follow the ramparts circuit (marked in yellow) that makes the complete tour of the medieval wall. Six towers punctuate the route, each offering a different angle on the valley.

→

Descend to the medieval washhouse below the ramparts. Water still flows there. The trail joins the terraced gardens built on the former moats.

→

Stroll through the artisan workshops installed in old medieval shops. Potters, weavers, and painters occupy the vaulted ground floors of Rue des Dames.

Rochefort-en-Terre was voted 'France's Favorite Village' in 2016. Its cobbled streets lined with stone and half-timbered houses, overflowing with geraniums and hydrangeas, form a postcard setting.

The castle, built in the 12th century and remodeled in the 17th, was restored in the early 20th century by an American painter who made it his residence. He installed art collections and transformed the gardens into flowered terraces overlooking the Gueuzon valley.

The Notre-Dame-de-la-Tronchaye church houses a miraculous statue of the Virgin, found in the 12th century in a hollow oak trunk - hence its name. The 16th-century Flemish altarpiece in polychrome limestone is classified as a Historic Monument.

Place du Puits, at the heart of the town, is surrounded by 16th and 17th-century houses. Sculpted lintels, ornate dormers, and wrought-iron signs recount five centuries of craftsmanship.

The old market halls, converted into a cultural space, preserve their medieval wooden framework. Temporary exhibitions are presented there in season.

→

Take the trail that descends toward the Gueuzon valley from the castle gardens. The path follows the stream under a canopy of oaks and beeches.

Walk through the flowered streets following the marked heritage circuit. Each house competes in floral displays - the tradition dates back to the early 20th century, when an American artist launched a facade competition.

→

La Roche-Bernard, a Small City of Character, clings to a rocky spur overlooking the Vilaine. The 'Promenade du Ruicard' follows the cliff and offers a vertiginous view over the river, 30 meters below.

The old quarter preserves 16th-17th century captains' and shipowners' houses. Rue du Ruicard displays granite facades with arched doorways and exterior staircases typical of maritime Vilaine.

The port, at the foot of the cliff, once housed shipyards that built Vilaine barges. A museum recounts this fluvial maritime history - the Vilaine was navigable as far as Redon.

The current suspension bridge succeeds that of 1839 - Brittany's first suspension bridge, swept away by a storm in 1852. From its deck, the view embraces both banks and the Vilaine meanders.

The Notre-Dame chapel, perched atop the rock, dates from the 16th century. During the Revolution, the town mayor took refuge there before being executed - a tragic episode of local royalist uprising.

→

Descend to the port via the staircase carved into the rock, then follow the Vilaine on the towpath. Old warehouses and launching ramps dot the route.

→

Cross the bridge and reach the Arzal dam on foot or by bike. This maritime lock, the largest in France, separates fresh water from salt water and regulates tides on the Vilaine.

→

Thursday morning market on Place du Bouffay is a gathering of local producers. Pénestin oysters, buckwheat pancakes, and farmhouse cider compose a complete Breton breakfast.

The Broken Great Menhir lies in four pieces on the ground. At 21 meters long and 280 tons, it was the largest monolith ever erected in Europe. Transported 10 km from a gneiss quarry, it was raised around 4500 BC.

The Table des Marchands, a 30-meter-long cairn, houses a dolmen whose capstone bears a spectacular carving: a crook or hafted axe symbol. This fragment fits with another found at Gavrinis, 4 km away by sea.

The Er Grah tumulus, 140 meters long, is one of the largest funeral monuments of European Neolithic. Built in several phases between 4600 and 3900 BC, it contained chest burials accompanied by polished axes.

The Dolmen des Pierres Plates stretches 24 meters along the seaside. Its L-shaped angled corridor is covered with Neolithic carvings: axes, crooks, shield-shaped idols. One wall bears a stylized face nicknamed the 'Mother Goddess.'

The port of Locmariaquer faces the entrance to the Gulf of Morbihan. The tidal current reaches 9 knots - one of the strongest in Europe. Oyster beds grid the foreshore as far as the eye can see.

The Notre-Dame-de-Kerdro church, from the 11th century, preserves Romanesque elements. On the square, a 16th-century cemetery cross bears sculptures of the Virgin and Christ.

→

Visit the megalithic complex (Great Menhir, Table des Marchands, Er Grah) in one go. The interpretation center at the site entrance explains megalith transport and erection techniques with models and films.

The Vauban Citadel has dominated Le Palais harbor since 1549. Expanded by Fouquet then fortified by Vauban in 1683, it served as a state prison - political detainees were imprisoned there until the 20th century. The rampart walk offers a complete panorama over the harbor.

The Aiguilles de Port-Coton, rocky pyramids battered by waves, fascinated a famous Impressionist who painted them 39 times in 1886. Colors change with each hour depending on light and tide.

Pointe des Poulains, at the island's northern tip, takes its name from the 'poulains' - rock debris extending it. A 19th-century military fort is accessible at low tide via a submersible causeway.

The Grand Phare, commissioned in 1836, rises 52 meters. Its 247 steps lead to a circular panorama embracing the Wild Coast, the mainland, and on clear days, Noirmoutier island.

Le Palais harbor, protected by two jetties, displays colorful houses facing the boats. Pastel facades - pink, yellow, blue - date from the 19th century when shipowners competed in elegance.

Donnant Beach: wedged between two black schist cliffs, this wild beach receives Atlantic swells head-on. Surfers frequent it; swimmers remain cautious of currents.

Saint-Cado island, a tiny jewel set on the Étel ria, is reached by a nine-arch stone bridge. According to legend, the Welsh monk Cado had this bridge built by the devil in one night - then tricked him by giving a cat instead of the promised soul.

Saint-Cado's Romanesque chapel, from the 11th century, is one of the oldest in Brittany. Its rough granite walls, calvary, and small maritime cemetery compose a scene of overwhelming simplicity.

Behind the chapel, a stone bed placed on the foreshore is said to be 'saint Cado's bed.' Tradition holds that the deaf come to place their ear there to regain hearing.

The Étel ria forms a 9 km sea arm, alternating mudflats, islets, and oyster beds. The Étel bar at the mouth is one of Brittany's most dangerous maritime passages - currents reach 8 knots.

Small fishermen's houses with blue or green shutters line the island's quay. At low tide, cockles and flat-bottomed boats rest on the mud, creating a timeless landscape.

Pont-Aven, 'the artists' town,' owes its fame to artists who settled there from 1860, seduced by the light, mills, and washerwomen. There were then 'fourteen mills and fifteen houses' along the Aven.

The Pont-Aven Museum, renovated in 2016, exhibits works from the Pont-Aven school and Synthetism. Permanent collections trace this small town's artistic adventure as it became the capital of post-Impressionist painting.

The Trémalo chapel, 500 m from the center, houses a 17th-century polychrome wooden Christ - the very one that inspired the famous 'Yellow Christ' in 1889. The sculpture remains in place above the altar.

The port ascends the Aven estuary to the town's heart. At high tide, sailboats float among the trees; at low tide, hulls rest on mud among egrets.

Several mills survive along the Aven. The Grand Poulguin mill (15th century) preserves its water wheel. At the time, these tan mills crushed oak bark for sail tanning.

→

Stroll through the Bois d'Amour, the stand of beeches and oaks that follows the Aven upstream from the town. Here the Synthetist movement was born in 1888, when a young painter created his 'Talisman.'

→

Follow the Xavier Grall promenade along the Aven. The path passes old washhouses, wooden footbridges, and mill ruins. Painting reproductions are placed where artists set up their easels.

→

The Ville Close of Concarneau, surrounded by 14th-century ramparts, occupies a granite islet connected to land by a fortified footbridge. It's one of Brittany's last inhabited walled towns - about 100 people live there year-round.

The ramparts offer an almost complete walkway, punctuated with towers and watchtowers. From the south bastion, the view plunges over the fishing port - France's third largest - and its blue-hulled trawlers.

The Fishing Museum, installed in the Ville Close's former arsenal, traces three centuries of Breton fishing: tuna boat models, nets, navigation instruments. A floating trawler, the Hémérica, can be visited at the port.

Concarneau's fish auction comes alive each morning at dawn. Crates of tuna, sardines, and langoustines parade on conveyor belts while fish merchants bid. It's the town's economic pulse.

The Clock Tower belfry, entrance gate to the Ville Close, dates from the 15th century. Its sundial and mechanical clock have marked the hours for 500 years.

The covered halls of Quai Pénéroff, built in 1880 for fish sales, preserve their iron and glass structure. The market is held there daily.

→

Walk the complete circuit of the Ville Close ramparts. The walkway alternates views of the fishing port, pleasure harbor, and Concarneau bay.

Follow the coastal path toward Sables Blancs and Cornouaille beaches. The path passes the seafront, old fish tanks, and Pointe du Cabellou.

If you're there in August, the Blue Nets - Brittany's oldest festival, created in 1905 to support striking sardine workers - animates the quays for four days: fest-noz, costume parades, and concerts.

Saint-Corentin Cathedral, begun in 1239 and completed in the 15th century, has a nave deliberately offset from the choir - an architectural mystery. The most common explanation: builders aligned the choir with a pre-existing chapel.

The two 76-meter spires, added in 1856, were financed by public subscription. Between them, an equestrian statue of legendary King Gradlon, mythical founder of the city, has watched over the city since the 15th century.

Old Quimper displays half-timbered houses along Rue Kéréon, Place au Beurre, and Place Terre au Duc. Sculpted signs - a mermaid, a dragon, a ship - recall medieval guilds.

Quimper faience factories have perpetuated the tradition of hand-painted 'little Breton' since 1690. More than three centuries of continuous production make Quimper France's oldest operating faience manufactory.

The Museum of Fine Arts, facing the cathedral, houses a collection of French and European painting from the 14th to 20th century, as well as an important collection dedicated to the Pont-Aven school.

The English Garden, laid out in 1852 on the former parish cemetery, adjoins Saint-Sauveur basilica. Behind high granite walls, a collection of exotic plants thrives in protection from wind. Palms, banana trees, and camellias prosper in this unexpected microclimate.

→

Follow the Odet riverbanks on foot from downtown. The river, lined with bourgeois residences and overhanging gardens, has been called 'France's prettiest river.'

→

Climb Mont Frugy (70 m), a wooded hill in the city's heart. From the summit, the panorama embraces the cathedral, slate roofs, and the confluence of the Odet and Steir.

→

The Cornouaille Festival, end of July, is the largest gathering of Breton culture: fest-noz, pipers, bagadoù, and dances from all of Brittany invade the town center for a week.

Locronan, classified as one of France's Most Beautiful Villages and a Small City of Character, has served as a film set for numerous movies. Its central square, entirely paved with granite, is surrounded by Renaissance residences built by sail cloth merchants.

Saint-Ronan church (15th century) houses the Pénity chapel, attached to the south side. Inside, the recumbent effigy of Saint Ronan, in polychrome kersanton stone, dates from the 15th century. Stained glass windows recount the life of the Irish hermit saint who came to evangelize Cornouaille.

The square's houses are built of Locronan granite - a fine-grained gray stone. Sculpted dormers, dated lintels, and monumental chimneys testify to weavers' wealth in the 16th and 17th centuries.

The royal sail cloth manufactory, created by Colbert, supplied the French navy. Hemp grown in the region was spun and woven here - an industry that made the city's fortune before declining in the 19th century.

The square's calvary, from the 16th century, is among Brittany's simplest and most expressive. Its stocky, weathered figures convey raw emotion.

→

Climb to the summit of Locronan mountain (289 m) via the Grande Troménie trail. This 12 km pilgrimage, one of Brittany's oldest, occurs only every six years - but the trail is accessible year-round.

→

Wander the cobbled streets beyond the main square. Rue Moal, Rue du Prieuré, and Venelle du Four contain less-known but equally remarkable facades.

Continue to the Névet woods, former sacred druid forest converted to a park. Centuries-old chestnut trees and fern undergrowth offer a cool walk even in summer.

The monumental calvary of Plougastel-Daoulas, erected between 1602 and 1604, counts 181 figures sculpted in kersanton stone. Built to celebrate the end of a plague epidemic, it's one of Brittany's three great calvaries with those of Guimiliau and Plougonven.

Scenes of Christ's Passion, arranged in continuous frieze around the base, blend biblical figures and 17th-century Breton costumes. Look for the Roman soldier dressed as a Breton gentleman - a deliberate anachronism.

The Strawberry and Heritage Museum traces the history of this cultivation that made the peninsula's fortune in the 19th century. Strawberry plants were imported from Chile in 1714 by a naval officer, and Plougastel strawberries became an export product.

Sainte-Christine chapel (16th century), set by the sea on Tinduff beach, is accessible only at low tide. Its miniature parish enclosure includes calvary, ossuary, and devotional fountain.

Pointe de l'Armorique, at the peninsula's tip, offers a panorama over Brest harbor, the Albert-Louppe bridge, and military vessels at anchor. An orientation table identifies landmarks.

→

Hike the peninsula's coastal path, between Anse de l'Auberlac'h and Pointe de l'Armorique. The trail alternates coves, pebble beaches, and viewpoints over Brest harbor.

→

Visit Daoulas Abbey (5 min drive). Its 12th-century Romanesque cloister, the only one preserved in Brittany, houses a medicinal plant garden from five continents.

The Notre-Dame-de-Croas-Batz church (16th century) is one of Brittany's most richly decorated. Its Renaissance bell tower, with two superimposed galleries and lantern, bears ship sculptures - ex-votos from shipowners who financed construction.

Shipowners' and privateers' houses line the harbor and Rue Amiral Réveillère. Their sculpted granite facades display maritime details: anchors, rigging, ship prows. Some date from the 16th century.

The Exotic Garden, planted on a rock facing Île de Batz, houses 3,500 plant species from the southern hemisphere. The Gulf Stream warms the coast: cacti, palms, and proteas grow here outdoors, at London's latitude.

The Johnnies House traces the epic of Roscoff pink onion merchants who, from 1828, crossed the Channel by oar to sell their harvest in England. They traveled by cart, onion braids over their shoulders - the quintessential 'Frenchman' image.

The old port, protected by a long jetty, offers views of Île de Batz and the lighthouse. Sainte-Barbe chapel, from the 16th century, stands at the jetty's end - sailors came to pray before putting to sea.

→

Visit the seaweed discovery center. The Léon coast produces 70% of French edible seaweed. Cooking workshops allow you to taste them: seaweed tartare, kombu butter, sea lettuce salad.

Ploumanac'h's granite chaos displays pink granite blocks sculpted by 300 million years of erosion. Some bear nicknames: Napoleon's Hat, the Turtle, the Die, the Upturned Bottle. Each weighs between 50 and 500 tons.

The Mean Ruz lighthouse (1945), in pink granite, replaces a 19th-century semaphore destroyed during World War II. Its color blends with surrounding rocks - you must search the chaos to find it.

Saint-Guirec beach, a fine sand cove framed by pink rocks, shelters an 11th-century oratory on the foreshore. According to tradition, unmarried girls stuck a needle in the saint statue's nose to find a husband within the year.

Costaérès castle, built in 1892 on a rocky islet, is the iconic image of the Pink Granite Coast. Accessible only at low tide, it served as a summer residence for a Polish engineer passionate about literature.

The Coastal House explains pink granite geology: formed 300 million years ago at 6 km depth, it owes its color to iron-rich feldspars. Only three sites worldwide present granite of this hue.

→

Walk the customs officers' path from Ploumanac'h to Perros-Guirec. The trail winds between pink granite blocks, gorse moors, and turquoise coves. It's one of the GR34's most spectacular sections.

Embark for the Sept-Îles, France's largest bird sanctuary. Northern gannets (France's only colony), puffins, guillemots, and gray seals populate this protected archipelago.

Saint-Tugdual Cathedral, built from the 13th to 15th century in Caen stone, schist, and granite, blends three eras: the Romanesque Hastings tower (12th century), Gothic nave, and 60-meter openwork spire (1785) adorned with playing card motifs - financed, it's said, by Louis XVI's lottery.

The 15th-century cloister, blessed in 1468, is the most complete in Brittany. Its 46 Gothic arches, stone flying buttresses, and wooden vault form an irregular trapezoid. It served as a place of prayer, access to Saint Yves' tomb, and covered market.

Saint Yves' tomb, patron saint of lawyers and the poor, is the object of a great pardon every third Sunday in May. Born in 1253 in Minihy-Tréguier, canonized in 1347, he's celebrated by a proverb: 'Saint Yves was Breton, a lawyer and not a thief - an admirable thing to the people.'

The choir stalls, sculpted between 1509 and 1512 by two craftsmen, one from the Rhine valley, present a fantastic bestiary on the misericords. Hidden with residents during the Revolution, they escaped destruction.

Ernest Renan's birthplace, at 20 Rue du même nom, is a 16th-century half-timbered shipowner's house converted to a museum. The writer was born there in 1823 - his 'Life of Jesus' (1863) caused scandal and earned him the label 'European blasphemer' from the Pope.

The Calvary of Protestation (1904), in kersanton stone, was erected in response to Renan's statue installed on Place du Martray facing the cathedral. The trilingual inscription - Latin, Breton, French - proclaims 'This man was truly the son of God.' A stone summary of the war between clericals and secularists.

→

Stroll through the historic center classified as a Protected Sector. Place du Martray, Rue Renan, and Ruelle Saint-Yves display 57 half-timbered houses from the 15th and 16th centuries - one of Brittany's best-preserved ensembles.

→

Descend to Tréguier port, nestled in the Jaudy estuary. Two former shipowners' towers once marked the town entrance. The Saint-François footbridge connects both banks.

→

Reach Minihy-Tréguier on foot (2 km) to see the Minihy church, where Saint Yves was rector, and remains of Kermartin manor where he was born and died. It's the starting point of the annual pardon.

The port of Paimpol, at the town's heart, was one of the great Iceland cod fishing ports. Between 1852 and 1935, up to 80 schooners sailed each year with 20 to 25 men aboard. An estimated 2,000 sailors were lost and 100 schooners sank during this epic.

Beauport Abbey, founded in 1202 by Premonstratensian canons, stands facing the sea in a 120-hectare domain. Its overgrown cloister, open-air church where hydrangeas and old roses bloom, and refectory bays opening onto the bay compose a place of romantic beauty.

Place du Martray and its 15th-16th century half-timbered houses testify to past prosperity. In one of them, a naval officer wrote 'Mon frère Yves' in 1883 and imagined the character of Gaud, heroine of 'An Iceland Fisherman,' inspired by a young Paimpol woman.

The Notre-Dame-de-Bonne-Nouvelle Tower, dedicated to the fishermen's patron saint, is the only vestige of the old church built in 1550 and destroyed in the early 20th century. Iceland fishermen's families prayed there for sailors' return.

Maison Jézéquel (15th century), classified as a Historic Monument at 6 Rue des Huit Patriotes, displays sculpted figures at corners. The hardware and cutlery shop created in 1886 on the ground floor is still run by the same family.

The Sea Museum, installed in a former cod drying house built around 1880 (which never functioned as such), exhibits schooner models, navigation instruments, and documents tracing the Iceland epic.

→

Visit Beauport Abbey and its coastal domain. The GR34 trail crosses the site and follows the bay. The conservatory orchard contains 60 apple varieties, 80% specific to the location.

→

Embark for Île de Bréhat (10 min from Pointe de l'Arcouest). Nicknamed 'the island of flowers,' it's car-free. The circuit on foot (8 km) passes Paon lighthouse, Saint-Michel chapel, and pink granite chaos.
