The 12th-century keep towers over the village, its pale yellow stone walls glowing in the sunlight. The Knights Hospitaller of Saint John of Jerusalem established a commandery here—one of the most significant in all of Provence.

Step through the gates of the Château des Hospitaliers, meticulously restored by the French Historic Monuments Commission in 1996. The Renaissance wing adjoining the keep now hosts permanent and rotating exhibitions showcasing regional art and history.

The Chapelle Saint-Jean-des-Commandeurs dates from the early 13th century. From rue Neuve, the view of its standing nave and choir is striking—a testament to medieval craftsmanship weathering the centuries.

Wind through the covered rue de la Chalanque, then descend the lower rampart street. Vaulted passages, uneven stone steps, and sun-drenched terraces form a labyrinth blissfully free of cars and modern intrusions.

The former 17th-century Protestant temple—one of the rare survivors in France—now houses the Museum of Dauphiné Protestantism. Its exhibits trace the Reformation's rise, the bloody Wars of Religion, and the exile of Huguenot families who fled persecution.

The Yvon Morin Art Center occupies beautifully restored rooms built from materials salvaged throughout the village. Each summer it hosts major exhibitions alongside intimate evening concerts in the stone halls.

→

Make your way to the Grand Portail, the village's ancient entrance framed by a vaulted chicane passage, then climb to the benches overlooking the Jabron Valley. The panorama sweeps across forested hills and lavender fields that shimmer purple in summer.

→

Le Poët-Laval marks the starting point of the "Huguenot Trail," a 1,800-km European footpath retracing the exile route of Dauphiné Protestants who fled to Germany. The first few kilometers wind through the surrounding hills and offer a taste of this historic journey.

→

Curious about local pottery? The nearby town of Dieulefit has kept alive an ancient ceramic tradition spanning centuries. Studios open their doors to visitors, offering a glimpse into the artisan's world.

Climb to the summit of the village to reach the Église Sainte-Foy, a jewel of 12th-century Romanesque architecture. Now deconsecrated, it hosts rotating exhibitions from April through September that breathe new life into the ancient stone.

From the orientation table near Sainte-Foy, the view embraces the Rhône Valley, the Vivarais mountains, and the foothills of the Vercors. On clear days, the panorama stretches for dozens of kilometers in every direction.

Sloping lanes lead past superb stone facades. The Adhémar de Monteil family built the first fortifications in the 12th century, and the ramparts still define the silhouette of this clifftop village.

The Église Saint-Pierre, in the heart of the village, dates from the mid-19th century. Inside hangs a 17th-century painting depicting Saint Ursula and Saint Augustine contemplating the mystery of the Trinity.

Artisan workshops dot the narrow streets: ceramics, jewelry, sculptures. A cubist painter settled here in the 1920s and founded a summer academy, igniting an artistic tradition that thrives to this day.

Mirmande once flourished through sericulture—silkworm farming. Panels throughout the village recount that era when magnaneries (silkworm nurseries) provided livelihoods for local families.

→

Follow the Sainte-Foy trail winding through orchards and olive groves below the village. You'll be rewarded with panoramic views of Mirmande's rooftops against the backdrop of the Drôme hills.

→

The region is renowned for its orchards. In season, farmers open their properties to visitors: cherries, apricots, peaches, nectarines, and kiwis available for pick-your-own or direct purchase.

→

The village of Cliousclat, just a few kilometers away, preserves its pottery-making heritage. A historical trail traces the story of the kilns and artisans who have worked the clay here for generations.

Valence

The Cathédrale Saint-Apollinaire, founded in the 11th century, was faithfully reconstructed in the 17th century after destruction during the Wars of Religion. Inside, multicolored stained glass bathes the nave in gentle, shifting light.

At 57 Grande Rue stands the Maison des Têtes—a sight that stops every passerby. This mansion, built around 1530, bridges the late Gothic and Renaissance periods. Its façade sculptures represent the Winds, Fortune, Time, Theology, Law, and Medicine.

Step into the corridor of the Maison des Têtes: its vaulted ceiling sits beside medallion busts of Roman emperors. In the interior courtyard, the Church Fathers appear in medallions beneath the gallery arches.

The Musée de Valence, Art et Archéologie adjoins the cathedral. Perched at the edge of the city's first terrace, it offers sweeping landscape views while presenting collections spanning from prehistory to contemporary art.

Parc Jouvet spreads across 7 hectares below the Champ de Mars. This "Remarkable Garden" combines formal French style in its central zone with English landscape design in its winding paths. Plane trees tower over 40 meters high, while a rose garden and water features bordered by exotic plants complete the scene.

The Armenian Heritage Center traces the history of the Armenian exodus and the community's arrival in Valence during the 1920s. It's a place of memory—spare, dignified, deeply moving.

→

Head to the Champ de Mars esplanade, inaugurated in 1862. The historic kiosk, now a listed monument, frames a perspective of Crussol Castle and the Vivarais mountains—a vista protected as a natural site since 1942.

→

Wander through the lanes of old Valence around Place des Clercs. Renaissance townhouses, covered passages, and shaded squares unfold one after another. Le Pendentif, a 16th-century funerary monument, surprises with its unusual form.

→

Sample the Suisse, a shortbread cookie shaped like a little person and perfumed with orange blossom—a Valence specialty. Pastry shops in the city center keep this tradition alive.

→

The Parc Jouvet miniature train, in service since 1967, loops around the park. Kids also love the playground, trampoline, and aviary.

The abbey church of Saint-Antoine-l'Abbaye, built between the 13th and 15th centuries, ranks among the most remarkable Gothic achievements in Dauphiné. A Historic Monument since 1840, it preserves murals, walnut woodwork, and an organ dating from the 17th and 18th centuries.

The Treasury Room, housed in sacristies paneled with walnut and Hungarian oak, displays the collection of the Antonine Order: a 17th-century ivory crucifix, antiphonaries, medieval surgical instruments, and an exceptional rotating vestment cabinet.

Take the "goulets"—narrow half-covered alleys climbing to the village summit. The houses grow more elaborate as you ascend: mullioned windows, glazed tiles creating "Dauphiné roofs," carved decorative elements.

The Departmental Museum, set in the former novitiate from the 17th and 18th centuries, focuses on the Hospitallers of Saint Anthony—an order of monk-physicians who treated "St. Anthony's Fire." A medieval garden complements the visit.

In Pont-en-Royans, the suspended houses appear to spring from the rock face above the Bourne River. Built in the 16th century to facilitate timber trade from the Vercors, their ochre, yellow, and pink façades have been protected as Historic Monuments since 1944.

Cross the Pont Picard for the village's most iconic view: the colorful houses stand out against the deep green waters. The buildings are constructed directly into the rock spur, with wooden overhangs projecting into empty space.

The Musée de l'Eau occupies a former convent that became a silk mill and then a power plant. It houses a water bar featuring 1,500 bottles from around the world, plus an exhibit on aquatic biodiversity.

→

Climb to the Belvédère des Trois Châteaux above Pont-en-Royans. The ascent is short but steep, passing through the medieval quarter and its terraced gardens. The panorama takes in the Royans region and the entrance to the Bourne gorges.

→

The landscaped banks of the Bourne invite relaxation: picnic tables, a waterfall, and swimming areas in summer. The water is crystal-clear and cool, flowing straight down from the Vercors.

💡 Water stays cool even in summer
→

The Gorges de la Bourne road connects Pont-en-Royans to Villard-de-Lans over 24 km. Carved into the cliff face in the 19th century, it hangs above the gorge with vertiginous drops and tunnels bored through solid rock.

The Grotte de Choranche opens at the foot of the Presles cliffs, 500 meters above sea level. Two underground rivers flow through galleries reaching up to 15 meters in height. Thousands of fistulous stalactites—delicate calcite straws of crystalline white hanging over three meters long—create a spectacle unique in Europe.

Inside the cave, the proteus creatures fascinate: these blind, pigment-free cave dwellers from Slovenia can fast for months and live up to 80 years. The tour concludes with a sound-and-light show in the Cathedral Hall, 40 meters in diameter and 25 meters high.

The Palais de l'Île in Annecy, built in the 12th century on a rocky islet in the Thiou River, resembles a stone galley. Over the centuries it served as a prison, mint, courthouse, and old-age home. It's been a Historic Monument since 1900.

Cross the Pont Perrière for the iconic photo of the Palais de l'Île bathed by the turquoise waters of the Thiou. Inside, an exhibit traces the architecture and heritage of Annecy through the centuries.

The Château d'Annecy dominates the old town from its rocky promontory. The imposing Queen's Tower dates from the 13th century. The courtyard façades bear witness to four centuries of evolution, from the Middle Ages to Renaissance style.

Lose yourself in the old town's cobbled streets along canals lined with colorful houses. The ancient fortified city, born in the 12th century when artisans settled along the Thiou to harness its water power, has kept its medieval charm intact.

→

Le Pâquier, a vast 7-hectare esplanade on the lakeshore, offers a panorama of mountains plunging into turquoise waters. An orientation table identifies each peak. The swans have made it their home.

→

Visit the Jardins de l'Europe, an English-style park filled with century-old trees, for a stroll between the lake and old town. Sculptures dot paths shaded by remarkable specimen trees.

→

The Lake Annecy bike loop follows a secure 42-km cycle path. Rental shops offer traditional and electric bikes starting from the old town.

→

In summer, swimming in the lake is essential. Public beaches in the city center provide access to some of the purest water in Europe.
