Côtes-d'Armor Road Trip: Dinan, Pink Granite Coast, Bréhat Island & Guerlédan Lake

Pink granite defies gravity, ramparts tower over the Rance, puffins nest on the Sept-Îles. The Côtes-d'Armor live to the rhythm of tides and legends.

A journey through Breton heritage and untamed nature.

Dinan

Dinan's ramparts stretch 2.7 km around the medieval city—the longest preserved ramparts in Brittany. Built between the 13th and 15th centuries, they still feature 10 towers and 4 monumental gates. The walkway along the ramparts offers sweeping views over the Rance valley below.

Dinan Castle, erected between 1380 and 1384 by Duke Jean IV of Brittany, served both as a residence tower and fortress. The keep houses a museum chronicling the town's history from the Middle Ages to today. Climb to the top for a complete panorama of the rooftops and the Rance river.

Follow rue du Jerzual, the steep cobblestone street that has connected the upper town to the port for ten centuries. Half-timbered houses shelter artist workshops, galleries, and artisan boutiques. Weavers and tanners once worked here—the creative energy remains unchanged.

The Saint-Sauveur Basilica, constructed in the second half of the 12th century, blends Romanesque and Gothic influences across its western facade. It preserves the heart of Bertrand Du Guesclin, Constable of France, in a reliquary in the north chapel. The Romanesque portal displays Oriental-Byzantine motifs rarely seen in Brittany.

Dinan's port, nestled at the foot of the ramparts, has been a trading crossroads since the 11th century. Commerce with England, Flanders, and later Spain built the town's prosperity. Now a marina, its quays lined with terraces maintain a peaceful morning atmosphere that turns lively by evening.

The Clock Tower, a 15th-century belfry, dominates the town from its 46-meter height. Duchess Anne's bell still rings today. Climb the stairs for a 360° panorama over the slate rooftops and surrounding countryside.

→

Guided tour of the medieval city. Expert guides reveal stories behind the 130 half-timbered houses, the murder holes, gun ports, and counter-scarps of the ramparts. A journey from daily medieval life to the present day.

→

Cruise on the Rance from Dinan port. The boat sails upriver toward Léhon or downriver to Saint-Malo, gliding between wooded banks and granite manor houses. The ramparts viewed from the river offer a spectacular perspective.

→

Dinan's weekly market, Thursday mornings. Stalls fill Place du Guesclin and adjacent streets. Local Breton products: galette-sausage, farmhouse cider, salted butter, buckwheat crêpes. A lively and colorful atmosphere.

Cap Fréhel's cliffs plunge 70 meters into emerald seas. Pink sandstone and schist form natural ramparts among the most spectacular in Brittany. On clear days, views extend to the coasts of Jersey and Bréhat Island. The site has been classified since 1967 and holds the Grand Site de France label.

Cap Fréhel lighthouse, rebuilt in 1950 after being dynamited in 1944, rises 103 meters above the sea. Its beam reaches 50 km, sweeping the night with two flashes every ten seconds. Beside it, the old lighthouse built under Louis XIV in 1687 testifies to centuries of maritime signaling.

Fort La Latte, also called Château de la Roche Goyon, has stood on a rocky headland since the 14th century. Two drawbridges, a barbican, a keep, and a cannonball furnace tell seven centuries of coastal defense. Redesigned by Vauban in the 17th century, it offers 360° views over the Emerald Coast from the keep. It's the most visited castle in Brittany.

La Fauconnière bird reserve occupies Cap Fréhel's cliffs. Common guillemots, razorbills, herring gulls, and northern fulmars nest in sandstone crevices. In spring and summer, the colonies reach their peak—bring binoculars.

→

Hike the coastal path from Cap Fréhel to Fort La Latte. The GR34 follows the cliffs between gorse and heather moorlands, each bend revealing a new sea panorama. Allow 1h15 one way, 2h30 round trip with photo stops.

💡 Good hiking shoes required, stay away from cliff edges
→

Visit Fort La Latte. The route passes through stables, guardroom, chapel, a 20,000-liter cistern, and the keep. The cannonball furnace heated projectiles red-hot before firing. Also discover the menhir called 'Gargantua's finger' on the access path.

💡 Avoid 3-5pm in summer (peak crowds)
→

Sunset at Cap Fréhel. The site takes on copper tones as the sun descends to the horizon. The pink sandstone cliffs become incandescent. One of Brittany's finest spots for golden hour.

Saint-Brieuc's Saint-Étienne Cathedral, built from the 13th to 15th centuries, is one of the rare fortified churches in Brittany. Its two massive towers, pierced with arrow slits, resemble a fortress entrance more than a religious edifice—it served as a citadel during several 14th-century sieges. Enter when the sun reaches its zenith: the south transept's rose window projects brilliant colors. A baroque altarpiece from 1745 and a 1540 organ case rank among the interior treasures.

Saint-Brieuc's old town centers on the cathedral. Pedestrian streets, half-timbered houses from the 15th to 17th centuries, and granite mansions tell the story of an episcopal city. The Hôtel des Ducs de Bretagne (1572), with carved lion heads and Renaissance floral motifs, welcomed Mary Stuart in 1548.

The Saint-Brieuc fountain, below the cathedral, marks the site of the monastery founded in 580 by Welsh monk Brieuc. This is where everything began: an oratory near a spring, a land grant from Breton chief Riwal, and a monastery that gave birth to the city.

The Cesson Tower, on a rocky spur overlooking the bay, once guarded the Gouët estuary entrance. This strategic site has been occupied since Viking and Roman times. The ruins offer an unobstructed panorama over the entire Saint-Brieuc bay.

→

Guided tour of the historic center. Between sacred monuments and half-timbered houses, decipher carved facades, shop signs, and ornaments that showcase Breton craftsmanship from the Middle Ages to the Renaissance.

→

Walk in the Saint-Brieuc Bay nature reserve, Brittany's largest. At low tide, mudflats attract thousands of migratory birds. Binoculars recommended for observing brent geese, sandpipers, and oystercatchers.

💡 Check tide times
→

Saint-Brieuc market, Wednesday and Saturday mornings around the historic metal halls classified as a Historic Monument. Fish from Légué, local cider, galettes, and kouign-amann.

Paimpol port, labeled Port of Heritage Interest, was the departure point for the schooners of 'Iceland Fishermen' immortalized by Pierre Loti. Colorful houses of shipowners and privateers line the quays. The memorial to those lost at sea recalls the toll paid by Paimpol families.

Beauport Abbey, founded in 1202 by Premonstratensian canons, stands facing the bay and Bréhat archipelago. A stop on the Way of Saint James—scallop shells carved on the walls bear witness. The 120-hectare estate, owned by the Coastal Conservatory, shelters a heritage orchard of antique apple varieties.

Paimpol's historic center features cobbled streets, art galleries, and handsome granite townhouses. Place du Martray hosts the Tuesday market—Paimpol AOC white beans, scallops, blue lobsters, and farmhouse cider fill the stalls.

→

Visit Beauport Abbey. The restored buildings host contemporary art exhibitions and artist residencies. The natural estate, freely accessible, unfolds between meadows, salt marshes, reed beds, and foreshore. Stunning views over Bréhat Island.

→

Hike the GR34 along the Plouézec and Plouha cliffs. Plouha's cliffs rise to 104 meters—Brittany's highest. The path passes the Shelburn monument, memorial to the escape of 135 Allied airmen during World War II.

→

Oyster tasting at an oyster farm on Pointe de Kérarzic, feet in the sand facing the bay. Paimpol oysters, award-winning several times, have a briny, meaty flavor.

Bréhat Island, 3.5 km long and 1.5 km wide, was France's first classified natural site in 1907. No cars circulate here. The lush southern island resembles the Mediterranean with its agapanthus, mimosas, and fig trees. The wilder northern island spreads with heather and moorland facing the open sea.

Pont ar Prad, built by Vauban in the 18th century, links the two islands that form Bréhat. From this stone bridge, views embrace pink granite rocks, flowering gardens, and turquoise sea. The passage between the two islands is one of Brittany's most photographed spots.

Paon lighthouse, at the island's northern tip, dominates a spectacular granite chaos. Jagged rocks plunge into seas churned by currents. In heavy weather, waves can reach the cliff top. The lighthouse was rebuilt after its 1944 destruction.

Bréhat's citadel, built in the 17th century, now houses the Verreries de Bréhat glassworks. Glassblowers create unique pieces inspired by the island's colors—sea blue, granite pink, garden green. You can watch glassblowing demonstrations.

Port de la Corderie was the departure point for Newfoundland fishermen. In 1508, the Bonaventure was the first known European vessel to return from Newfoundland with holds full of cod—16 years after the 'discovery' of the Americas. Small pink granite houses still line the quays.

→

Walk around Bréhat Island. Paths cross gardens where exotic plants grow—agapanthus, hydrangeas, eucalyptus—in a mild microclimate. Allow 3 to 4 hours for the complete circuit, with contemplative pauses.

💡 Bike rental available on the island
→

Boat or sailing tour of the archipelago. The 86 islets and reefs of the Bréhat archipelago reveal themselves from the water. Cormorants to the east, sea bass and sand eels to the west, seals on isolated rocks. Navigation between buoys and currents is a spectacle in itself.

Saint-Tugdual Cathedral, built from the 11th to 15th centuries, is one of Brittany's finest religious edifices. Three towers rise above the transept—an uncommon silhouette. The 18th-century spire soars over 60 meters, pierced with playing card motifs recalling its funding by Louis XVI's lottery. Inside, Saint Yves' tomb has drawn pilgrims since the 14th century. Patron saint of lawyers and Brittany, he's celebrated around May 19th during the grand Pardon—torchlight procession, traditional costumes, and parish banners. The cathedral is also a stop on the Tro Breiz pilgrimage.

The Flamboyant Gothic cloister, inaugurated in 1470, is the most complete surviving cloister in Brittany. Its 46 arcades, separated by small columns into two trefoil bays, shelter recumbent statues and funerary plaques. Under the wooden vault, silence is striking.

Place du Martray, Tréguier's beating heart, is bordered by 15th-century half-timbered houses. At the center stands the statue of Ernest Renan, philosopher and writer native to the town. His birthplace, transformed into a museum, displays memorabilia of the author of Life of Jesus.

Tréguier port, nestled between the Jaudy and Guindy rivers, is a deep-water harbor accessible 24/7. Its 310 berths welcome sailors steps from the historic center. Cross the Saint-François footbridge for a view over the confluence of the two rivers.

→

Guided tour of Tréguier, a Small Town of Character. The town's 57 half-timbered houses form the highest density in Brittany. Rue Ernest Renan, Saint-Yves, and Colvestre preserve an intact medieval charm.

→

Excursion to the Gouffre de Plougrescant, 10 km from Tréguier. This tiny house wedged between two granite boulders is one of Brittany's most famous images. The coastal path around Pointe du Château offers wild landscapes between moorland and cliffs.

💡 Spectacular in heavy weather
→

Tasting at Tréguier's craft brewery, installed in an early 20th-century brick building on the Jaudy riverbanks. Breton beers brewed on-site, vat visits possible.

Ploumanac'h's granite chaos defies the laws of balance. Enormous blocks of pink granite, sculpted by wind and sea over millennia, compose a landscape unique in the world. The copper color comes from iron oxide impurities in the crystal lattice. Voted France's Favorite Village in 2015.

Mean Ruz lighthouse ('red stone' in Breton), emblem of the Pink Granite Coast, has watched over sailors since 1860. The current lighthouse, in pink granite, replaced one destroyed by bombing in 1944. Depending on the sun's angle, the building and rocks change color—a living masterpiece.

Saint-Guirec beach, nestled among granite chaos, shelters an oratory dedicated to its namesake saint. Tradition held that young women would stick a pin in the granite statue's nose to find a husband within the year. Château de Costaérès stands silhouetted on its islet in the background.

The Maison du Littoral, near the lighthouse, explains the Pink Granite Coast's geology and the Coastal Conservatory's role in protecting the site. Exhibitions on granite formation, coastal flora and fauna.

Trestraou beach, Perros-Guirec's large fine-sand beach, faces the Sept-Îles archipelago. Lined with Belle Époque seaside villas from the late 19th and early 20th centuries, it blends family atmosphere with period architecture.

→

Hike the coastal path from Trestraou to Ploumanac'h. The GR34 winds among pink granite chaos for 4 km, each turn revealing a new tableau. Allow 2 hours for the loop, more if you take time for photos—and you will.

💡 Sunrise or sunset for the finest light
→

Boat excursion to the Sept-Îles archipelago, France's largest bird sanctuary. Protected since 1912, classified as a National Nature Reserve in 1976, the archipelago shelters France's only northern gannet colony, Atlantic puffins, and about forty grey seals. Departures from Trestraou beach.

→

Explore the Traouïéro Valley, between Ploumanac'h and Trégastel. Two steep valleys where the Kérougant stream winds beneath an oak and giant fern canopy. Caves, granite chaos, and Korrigan legends—a shady refuge ideal for summer.

Trestrignel beach, more intimate than Trestraou, is bordered by cliffs and maritime villas. Its clear waters attract kayakers and swimmers. From Pointe du Sphinx, the panorama extends over the Sept-Îles archipelago.

Perros-Guirec port, on Quai de la Douane, is the departure point for excursions to Sept-Îles and Bréhat Island. Fishing boats and sailboats mingle here. On Wednesdays, the La Rade market sets up facing the sea.

The Renote peninsula in Trégastel, linked by a causeway since 1885, ranks among France's most picturesque sites. The path circles the island among pink granite chaos, fine-sand coves, and stunning views over the Sept-Îles archipelago. The loop takes just over an hour.

Trégastel's Marine Aquarium is built beneath a mound of pink granite boulders. Natural caves shelter tanks populated by fish, crustaceans, and anemones from the Breton coast. A setting unique in the world for an aquarium—granite blocks serve as tank walls.

Trégastel's Grève Blanche, a fine-sand beach framed by pink rocks, offers swimming in clear waters with Sept-Îles views. The fantastically shaped rocks bordering it bear nicknames—the Death's Head, the Dice, the Turtle—see if you can spot them.

→

Sea kayaking on the Pink Granite Coast. Paddle among the granite chaos for a water-level immersion. The rocks viewed from the sea take even more surprising shapes. Guided outings accessible to beginners.

→

Walk around the Renote peninsula. The coastal path winds among granite chaos, pebble beaches, and hidden coves. The island is the Pink Granite Coast in miniature—perfect for those wanting to extend the magic of Ploumanac'h.

→

Seafood tasting at Perros-Guirec port. Terraces facing the boats serve oysters, lobsters, and scallops in season. A glass of Muscadet completes the experience.

The Basilica of Notre-Dame-de-Bon-Secours, built from the 12th to 16th centuries, blends three styles across its south facade: Romanesque, Gothic, and Renaissance. It houses a rare Black Virgin, one of the few in western France, a paved labyrinth, and a magnificent 1654 organ case. Every year, the Saturday before the first Sunday of July, the Pardon animates the town—nocturnal procession of the Black Virgin, then lighting of three bonfires on Place du Centre. A tradition documented since the 16th century.

La Plomée, a Renaissance fountain on Place du Centre, is adorned with allegorical figures and dolphins. Vestiges of the medieval castle, residence of the Dukes of Brittany, can still be glimpsed in the urban fabric. Guingamp was the duchy's third fortress after Rennes and Nantes.

The banks of the Trieux, below the old town, reveal themselves via an 80-meter footbridge. Stone bridges, old washhouses, and terraced gardens border the river. A haven of calm steps from the center.

Guingamp's former prison, an unusual and rare heritage site, can be visited during guided tours. Period cells, prisoner graffiti, and 19th-century prison architecture—a little-known facet of the town's history.

→

Guided tour of Guingamp, a Small Town of Character. From the town's origins to today, among cobbled streets, half-timbered houses, former convents, and 18th-century mansions. The guide leads you from castle ruins to the basilica.

→

La Vapeur du Trieux, steam tourist train between Paimpol and Pontrieux. The century-old locomotive descends the Trieux valley at 30 km/h through preserved natural heritage. Castles, manor houses, and grey herons pass along the estuary.

Lac Guerledan

Lake Guerlédan, Brittany's largest freshwater expanse, was born from the construction of a hydroelectric dam between 1923 and 1930. Its 51 million m³ of water stretch through the Blavet valley, between schist ridges and forests. The dam submerged a section of the Nantes-Brest canal.

Bon-Repos Abbey, a Cistercian foundation from 1184 by Alain III de Rohan and Constance de Bretagne, stands on the banks of the Nantes-Brest canal. Legend says the viscount fell asleep at the confluence of the rivers and saw the Virgin in a dream: 'This place shall be for your prayers and eternal rest.' Sold as national property during the Revolution, restored since 1986 by volunteers.

Quénécan forest, over 3,700 hectares of oak-beech woodland south of the lake, rises to 290 meters. For three centuries, charcoal burners produced the charcoal that fed the blast furnaces of the Forges des Salles. Chestnuts, holly, and Scots pines compose a dense canopy.

The Forges des Salles, an intact ironworking village since operations ceased in 1877, nestle in the heart of Quénécan forest. Blast furnace, ironmaster's house, workers' housing, canteen, school, and chapel—time has stood still. Classified as a Historic Monument since 1980.

The Liscuis moors and their Neolithic covered alleyways, dating from 3,000 BCE. These schist megalithic tombs are among the best preserved in Brittany. The panorama from the moors extends over the Blavet valley.

→

Hike the Lake Guerlédan circuit. The 37 km trail can be covered in 2-3 days or by sections. The Trégnanton viewpoint offers the finest panorama—yellow gorse moors, blue waters, and slate schist.

💡 Trégnanton - Bon-Repos section recommended for half-day
→

Water sports on Lake Guerlédan. Beau Rivage beach offers kayaking, paddleboarding, waterskiing, and tubing. Anse de Sordan, on the south shore, provides a wilder setting for canoeing at the foot of Quénécan forest.

→

Visit Bon-Repos Abbey. Self-guided tour, guided tour, or digital tour with augmented reality tablets. In summer, a sound and light show projects the history of Central Brittany onto the facade—two hours of historical fresco on a 2-hectare stage.
