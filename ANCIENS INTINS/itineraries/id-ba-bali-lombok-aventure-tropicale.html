Indonesia: Bali & Lombok — Ubud, Gili Islands and Kuta's Wild Coast

📅 10 days📍 10 stops🚗 345 km⏱ 8h30

Start from Ubud, Bali's spiritual capital, and cross the hidden Sidemen valley with its rice terraces under the gaze of Mount Agung. Reach Amed and its dives on the USAT Liberty wreck torpedoed in 1942, then catch the speedboat to Gili Air and its free-roaming sea turtles. Finish on the virgin beaches of southern Lombok and the Uluwatu cliffs with the Kecak dance at sunset.

✨ Highlights
  • Pura Tirta Empul: purification temple founded in 926 AD, Melukat ritual under 30 sacred water jets
  • USAT Liberty at Tulamben: 120 m cargo ship torpedoed in 1942, now a coral-encrusted artificial reef between 5 and 28 m
  • Gili Air: the only car-free Gili island, with green and hawksbill turtles 30 m from shore year-round
  • Pura Luhur Uluwatu: cliff temple at 70 m, one of Bali's 6 spiritual pillars, nightly Kecak dance since the 1930s
📆 Best period

May, June, July, August, September, October

Day 1

Ubud

Bali's artistic and spiritual heart, where Hindu temples and UNESCO rice terraces merge into the jungle

★★★★★
1

Ubud is best explored on foot: start with Jalan Raya Ubud, the main street, lined with art galleries, batik shops and temples carved from volcanic stone. The Balinese call Ubud the 'navel of Bali' — it has been the island's artistic and spiritual centre for centuries. From early morning, you'll see locals placing banana-leaf offerings in front of every shop and shrine. Pack a sarong in your daypack: you'll need one for every temple today.

⏱ 60 min
2

Pura Taman Saraswati is one of Ubud's most photographed temples, dedicated to the goddess of knowledge. Its lotus-covered pool catches the morning light with rare serenity. Just opposite, Pura Desa is the village's communal temple, dedicated to Brahma, the protector god. Don't stop at the façade: walk into the inner courtyard to see the daily offerings still fresh from dawn. Free admission — enter quietly and keep your shoulders covered.

⏱ 60 min
3

Puri Saren Agung, Ubud's Royal Palace, sits at the heart of town. The Balinese royal family still lives here, but part of the complex is open to visitors. The architecture — paved courtyards, garuda sculptures and centuries-old frangipani trees — is a textbook example of classic Balinese style. Every evening from 7:30 PM, the courtyard becomes a stage for traditional Legong and Barong dances. Reserve your spot in the afternoon: performances often sell out in high season.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

The Subak Juwuk Manis Walk follows rice paddies irrigated by the traditional subak system, a UNESCO Cultural Landscape since 2012. This network of channels is managed collectively by the village farmers — if a channel clogs, everyone pitches in to fix it. The walk takes about an hour on foot from central Ubud. In the morning, low golden light rakes across the stepped terraces: the ideal moment to pull out your camera.

⏱ 75 min

Dinner at a local warung around Jalan Dewisita or Jalan Bisma — these small side streets off the main road concentrate the best traditional Balinese kitchens. Order a nasi campur (rice with an assortment of local condiments) or a lawar, a ceremonial dish of minced meat and grated coconut. Avoid the restaurants on the main drag in high season: prices double and quality often drops.

⏱ 75 min
Day 2

Ubud

Bali's artistic and spiritual heart, where Hindu temples and UNESCO rice terraces merge into the jungle

★★★★★
1

Get up early for the Campuhan Ridge Walk: the trail starts near Gunung Lebah temple, two kilometres from the town centre. The path follows a ridge between the Wos and Cerik rivers, through elephant-grass fields and fluorescent-green paddies. The 2-kilometre walk takes one to two hours depending on your pace. Leave before 8 AM — after 9, organised tour groups arrive in force and the atmosphere shifts completely. The morning light on the valley mist is worth the early start.

⏱ 90 min
2

The Sacred Monkey Forest Sanctuary, known as Mandala Suci Wenara Wana, is home to more than 700 long-tailed macaques roaming freely across 12.5 hectares on the southern edge of Ubud. Three 14th-century temples built from volcanic stone are tucked deep in the forest. The monkeys are used to visitors but can turn aggressive if they spot food — keep bags and sunglasses out of reach. Allow an hour and a half to explore the different trails and shrines.

⏱ 90 min
3

The Pasar Seni, the art market facing the Royal Palace, is the place to pick up textiles, wood carvings and locally made silver jewellery. Posted prices are starting points for negotiation — begin at half the asking price. Watch out for cheap batiks: a genuine hand-drawn batik (batik tulis) takes hours of work and can be recognised by the slight asymmetry of its patterns. Machine-printed imitations sell for a tenth of the price.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Catch a Legong or Kecak dance performance at Puri Saren Agung, Ubud's Royal Palace. The Legong, performed by young dancers in golden costumes, tells episodes from the Ramayana. The Kecak — created in the 1930s by choreographer Walter Spies from a male trance ritual — features a chorus of dozens of men chanting 'cak-cak' with no instruments at all. Shows begin around 7:30 PM; arrive 20 minutes early for good seats.

⏱ 90 min

Late in the evening, stroll the lamp-lit lanes around Jalan Monkey Forest and Jalan Hanoman. These streets are packed with galleries open late, cafés with terraces suspended over the valleys, and warungs where locals dine well past dark. The scent of incense from the evening offerings mingling with the first notes of a gamelan drifting from a nearby temple — that's the real face of Ubud after nightfall.

⏱ 60 min
Day 3

Ubud

Bali's artistic and spiritual heart, where Hindu temples and UNESCO rice terraces merge into the jungle

★★★★★
1

The Tegallalang rice terraces are Bali's postcard landscape — 12 km north of Ubud, a 20-minute scooter ride. Listed as a UNESCO Cultural Landscape under the subak irrigation system, they illustrate perfectly the collective water management practised here for centuries. Go early, before 9 AM: the light is beautiful and the terraces still empty. After 10, guides and souvenir sellers take over. The marked path through the terraces costs a few thousand rupiah.

⏱ 90 min
2

Pura Tirta Empul, 15 km northeast of Ubud (30 to 45 minutes), is one of the most sacred temples in Bali. Its name means 'holy spring' in Balinese. Founded in 926 AD under the Warmadewa dynasty, the temple is built around a natural spring considered blessed. According to legend, the god Indra struck the earth with his spear to create an elixir of life and save his warriors from the poison of the evil king Mayadenawa. Today, Balinese come to purify themselves under the 30 water jets feeding two rectangular pools — this is the Melukat ritual. Visitors can take part by wearing a special sarong available for hire on site. Avoid the 11 AM to 2 PM window, the busiest period.

⏱ 90 min
3

A few minutes from Tirta Empul, Pura Gunung Kawi is an 11th-century rock-cut mausoleum carved into the cliff face of the Pakerisan gorge. Ten shrines sculpted in high relief are dedicated to the kings of the Warmadewa dynasty. To reach them, descend 350 steps through terraced rice paddies — the climb back up will work up a sweat, but the sight is worth it. Arrive before 10 AM to avoid the crowds.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Stop for lunch on the way back near Sari Organik or at a warung perched above the Tegallalang terraces. These small open-terrace restaurants overlook the green tiers as far as the eye can see — look for those with a genuine view over the lower terraces, as some only face the road. Nasi goreng or gado-gado (vegetables in peanut sauce) are the essential local dishes. Allow about an hour on site to soak up the panorama.

⏱ 75 min

On the road between Ubud and Tegallalang, stop at Goa Gajah — the 'Elephant Cave' — a listed 9th-century archaeological site. The entrance to the sanctuary is a demon's mouth sculpted on a grand scale that you walk through to enter the cave. On site, a local Balinese guide can explain the ongoing ritual practices for around 150,000 rupiah — an hour of direct cultural transmission that transforms the experience entirely.

⏱ 60 min

Day 4

Sidemen

A hidden valley in east Bali, where rice terraces glow beneath Mount Agung and ancestral ikat looms still click

★★★☆☆
1

The drive from Ubud to Sidemen (35 km, 1h to 1h15) winds through the hills of eastern Bali with sweeping views of volcanoes and rice paddies. Sidemen sits in the Karangasem regency, in a valley carved by the waters of Gunung Agung, the sacred volcano rising to 3,142 metres just 15 km away. Settle into your accommodation — in this village it almost invariably looks out over green terraces with the volcanic cone as a backdrop, one of the finest views in all of Bali.

⏱ 60 min
2

In the afternoon, explore the Sidemen valley on foot or by scooter. The Sidemen Rice Field Trekking trail is a marked 3-kilometre path (entry: about 25,000 rupiah) that follows terraced rice paddies irrigated by subak channels. You'll pass women harvesting rice by hand — the terraces may be muddy, green or golden depending on the season. Ask the trail attendant to point you to the second, free path on higher ground, which offers a different angle on the lower terraces.

⏱ 90 min
3

In the late afternoon, climb to Pura Bukit Sangkan Gunung, a temple perched on a hill above Sidemen, reachable in 10 minutes on foot from the village via a steep staircase. Not a tourist in sight — just an elevated view over the valley and centuries-old banyans in the silence. At first light or just before sunset, the low-angle light on the paddies is stunning from up here. Free entry — a small offering in the box is appreciated.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Visit an ikat weaving workshop — Sidemen is known as Bali's village of weavers. Ikat (from the Indonesian word meaning 'to tie, to bind') is a cotton cloth with geometric patterns created by dyeing the yarn at precise intervals before weaving. The artisans work on traditional looms, passing the craft from mother to daughter. Songket, the more precious version, is woven with gold or silver thread and reserved for major religious ceremonies. The workshops let you buy quality pieces directly, cutting out the middlemen.

⏱ 60 min

Sunset dinner at one of the warungs perched on the valley hillsides. The tables are often set on open bamboo terraces facing the cascading terraces and Mount Agung. The end-of-day light gilds the paddies in an orange glow that photographers and painters have been trying to capture for decades — Sidemen has attracted artists from around the world since the 1930s. Arrive 30 minutes before sunset to pick your spot.

⏱ 75 min
Day 5

Sidemen

A hidden valley in east Bali, where rice terraces glow beneath Mount Agung and ancestral ikat looms still click

★★★☆☆
1

An essential day trip from Sidemen: Pura Besakih, 30 to 35 minutes by car. Perched at 1,000 metres on the slopes of Mount Agung, it's Bali's largest temple complex — more than 86 individual temples including 18 major ones, arranged on tiered terraces. Founded according to legend in the 8th century by the sage Rsi Markandeya, the site has been supported by the Balinese state since the 15th century and hosts more than 70 religious ceremonies a year. Arrive early in the morning to avoid the coach groups; queues can be long in season. Arrange a driver to wait: there are virtually no taxis available for the return.

⏱ 120 min

Activities

Rafting or trekking on the Telaga Waja river — the main waterway fed by the slopes of Mount Agung, flowing through the valley below Sidemen. Local operators run raft trips suitable for all levels over roughly 16 km of rapids and forested gorges, with some technical passages in the wet season. It's a more physical alternative to the paddy walks, with a perspective on east Bali's dense vegetation from water level.

⏱ 180 min

In the late afternoon, head to Gembleng Waterfall, a cascade reached after a 15- to 20-minute walk from the road. The water tumbles into natural rock pools — perfect for cooling off after a day of sightseeing. The spot is far less crowded than the waterfalls near Ubud. Visit after the rainy season (May to October) for a more generous flow.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 60 km · 2h
Day 6

Amed

A black-sand fishing village where you snorkel a WWII shipwreck at Tulamben without leaving shore

★★★☆☆
1

The drive from Sidemen to Amed (60 km, about 2 hours) follows Bali's east coast with views across the Lombok Strait and the volcanic island of Lombok in the background. Stop at the Karangasem water palaces along the way: Tirta Gangga, built in 1948 by the last raja of Karangasem, is a complex of Balinese pools and fountains set among the rice paddies. Then Taman Ujung, even more impressive, spread out over the water and facing the sea — partly destroyed by the eruptions of Mount Agung in 1963 and since rebuilt. Allow 45 minutes at each site.

⏱ 90 min
2

Arrival in Amed, a fishing village on Bali's northeast coast. The beach is made of pebbles and black volcanic sand — no white tropical sand here, but an authenticity you won't find in the south of the island any more. Every morning, the jukungs (colourful traditional fishing boats) return with the day's catch. Jemeluk Bay, five minutes from Amed, is the best shore-snorkelling spot: strap on your fins and swim 50 metres out to find colourful corals and your first turtles.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Don't miss the trip to Tulamben, 20 minutes west of Amed, to dive or snorkel the USAT Liberty. This 120-metre American military cargo ship was torpedoed by a Japanese submarine on 11 January 1942 in the Lombok Strait, then beached at Tulamben. It sat there for 21 years before being swept into the sea by the eruption of Mount Agung in 1963. The wreck lies between 5 and 28 metres deep, just 30 metres from shore — accessible directly from the beach without a boat. Entirely colonised by corals over 60 years, it now shelters sea fans several metres across, barracuda, turtles and pygmy seahorses. Open to all levels, from beginner to expert.

⏱ 120 min

At sunset, settle at the water's edge in Amed and watch the show: Mount Agung, often capped in cloud, turns red to the west, while the waters of the bay shift to violet. The small beachfront restaurants serve fish grilled to order — order early, the service is slow but the freshness of the catch more than makes up for it.

⏱ 75 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 90 km · 1h30
Day 7

Gili Air

A car-free speck between Bali and Lombok, with sea turtles grazing in the shallows

★★★★☆
1

The speedboat crossing from Amed to Gili Air takes between 45 minutes and an hour and a half depending on the operator. Book in advance — seats fill up fast in high season. On arrival, the contrast with Bali is instant: no motorised vehicles on the island. You get around on foot, by bicycle or by cidomo (horse-drawn cart). Gili Air is the closest of the three Gili islands to Lombok and the quietest — the right balance between the bustle of Gili Trawangan and the near-emptiness of Gili Meno. Drop your bags and walk the island loop in an hour and a half.

⏱ 90 min
2

The 'Turtle Spot' is on the east coast of the island, marked by small wooden signs along the beach. Swim about ten metres from the shore to the seagrass beds where green and hawksbill turtles come to feed. Visibility in the water averages 20 to 30 metres. Don't touch them and stay 2–3 metres away when they surface to breathe. Best time is in the morning at high tide — after 11 AM, small jellyfish may appear on the north coast.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

Free snorkelling from the east and north coast beaches — the coral reefs start just a few dozen metres from shore. The stretch between the Bel Air Resort and the Sunrise Resort (southeast coast) is reputed for the density of turtles. Rent a mask, snorkel and fins from any bar or restaurant on the island for a few euros. If the weather is good and visibility cooperates, you'll probably spot turtles without needing an organised excursion.

⏱ 120 min

Sunset from Gili Air's west beach, facing the open sea and the silhouettes of Bali's volcanoes on the horizon. The beach bars set out cushions and candles on the sand from 5:30 PM — order a coconut cocktail or a fresh juice and watch the sky blaze orange and pink. If you're lucky, the outline of Gunung Agung will appear between the clouds on the western horizon.

⏱ 75 min

Day 8

Kuta Lombok

Wild southern beaches, Mawi's surf break and the traditional Sasak village of Sade

★★☆☆☆
1

Public boat from Gili Air to Bangsal (Lombok's port), then road to Kuta Lombok (roughly 90 km, 2 hours' drive). Kuta Lombok has nothing in common with its overcrowded Balinese namesake. Here, fishermen still land on near-empty beaches and the village lanes keep an end-of-the-world feel. Kuta's main beach is tucked into a crescent bay — its sand has a distinctive grainy texture that earns it the local nickname 'pepper sand.' Order a coconut and watch the boats come and go.

⏱ 60 min
2

Tanjung Aan bay is five minutes east of Kuta: two coves separated by a rocky headland, one with very fine white sand, the other with faintly pink tones from coral fragments mixed into the sand. The turquoise water is remarkably clear. On a weekday outside peak season, you can have the place virtually to yourself. In high season (July–August), arrive when the car parks open. Pantai Seger, just to the west, is even wilder and worth a detour.

⏱ 90 min
3

Mawun Beach is 15 minutes west of Kuta: a perfect crescent bay entirely enclosed by bare hills that cut the wind and flatten the sea. The water is calm, ideal for swimming, and the spot stays uncrowded outside weekends. From the hilltop overlooking the beach to the north, the view down over the bay and surrounding ridges is worth the 10-minute walk up.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Panoramic hike up Merese Hill — the headland overlooking Tanjung Aan east of Kuta. The climb takes 20 minutes from the Tanjung Aan car park on a well-marked trail. The summit delivers a 360° view over both coves of the bay, the green inland hills and the Indian Ocean horizon. It's one of the best sunset spots in southern Lombok — arrive an hour beforehand to pick your rock.

⏱ 90 min

Dinner at a local warung in Kuta Lombok — the street-food options are far better and cheaper than the tourist restaurants along the waterfront. Ayam taliwang (grilled chicken with spices and Lombok sambal, hotter than the Balinese versions) and plecing kangkung (stir-fried water spinach in tomato-chilli sauce) are the island's signature dishes. The Sasak eat spicy — flag your tolerance level before ordering.

⏱ 75 min
Day 9

Kuta Lombok

Wild southern beaches, Mawi's surf break and the traditional Sasak village of Sade

★★☆☆☆
1

Selong Belanak is 20 km west of Kuta (30 minutes on a paved road). It's a long blonde-sand beach curving into an almost enclosed bay — the water is gentle, ideal for family swimming or learning to surf. Operators on the beach hire out foam boards for beginners. The fishing village behind the dunes remains surprisingly untouched by development. Warungs serve freshly grilled fish right by the beach.

⏱ 90 min
2

Mawi Beach is 30 minutes west of Kuta on a sometimes rough road (choose a scooter with decent ground clearance). The beach, framed by steep bare hills, is one of southern Lombok's most renowned surf breaks — powerful waves, for experienced surfers only. But even without a board, the bay is worth the trip: wild, barely developed, with a late-morning light that turns the water jade. Bring water — there's virtually no infrastructure on site.

⏱ 75 min
3

The village of Sade, 15 minutes from Kuta (near Rambitan), is one of the best-preserved Sasak villages on Lombok. The Sasak people, Lombok's indigenous Muslim ethnic group, still live in traditional houses called 'bale,' built from woven bamboo with rumbia-leaf roofs. The villagers carry on the ancestral technique of weaving songket — a colourful cloth with gold or silver thread reserved for ceremonies. A visit with a village resident is far more illuminating than the bus tours.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

In the evening, head to Kuta Lombok's night market — a few streets in the centre come alive from 6 PM with stalls selling grilled skewers, fresh juices and local crafts. Ayam taliwang (grilled chicken with spices and Lombok sambal, hotter than the Balinese version) and plecing kangkung (stir-fried water spinach in tomato-chilli sauce) are the dishes to try. The Sasak eat spicy — flag your tolerance level before ordering.

⏱ 75 min

In the late afternoon, sunset over Tanjung Aan bay from the Merese Hill promontory or from the beach itself. The water shifts from turquoise to copper, water buffalo file home from the fields along the coastal road and fishermen ready their boats for the night. This is the everyday tableau of southern Lombok — simple and powerful. Last evening here: make the most of it before tomorrow's flight.

⏱ 75 min

Day 10

Uluwatu

A sacred cliff temple 70 metres above the Indian Ocean, where the Kecak chant meets the sunset

★★★★☆
1

Morning flight from Lombok (LOP) to Denpasar (DPS), duration approx. 30 minutes. From the airport, head straight to the Bukit peninsula (30 min south) for a half-day of exploring before sunset. Rent a scooter to get around the limestone plateau — the roads are well paved and signposted. Start with Padang Padang, wedged between two cliffs and reached down 150 steps: a natural turquoise pool ringed by boulders. Continue to Bingin Beach, quieter and perfect for a lunch stop facing the waves.

⏱ 120 min
2

Pura Luhur Uluwatu perches 70 metres above the Indian Ocean on a cliff at the southwest tip of the Bukit. Founded in the 11th century by the sage Mpu Kuturan, it is one of Bali's six 'spiritual pillar' temples, dedicated to the spirits of the sea. The walk along the coastal path around the temple is spectacular: you see the building from below against the cliffs, with waves crashing 70 metres beneath you. The temple macaques are notorious pickpockets — stow sunglasses and jewellery before entering. Arrive at least an hour and a half before sunset to explore the site at leisure.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

The Uluwatu Kecak dance takes place every evening at 6 PM on an open-air stage clinging to the cliff, with the ocean as a backdrop. The Kecak was created in the 1930s by Walter Spies, a German painter living in Bali, who drew on the male trance ritual Sanghyang to develop this choral spectacle. Several dozen men seated in a circle chant 'cak-cak-cak' with no instruments at all — the only accompaniment — while costumed dancers perform scenes from the Ramayana as the sun sinks into the ocean. Buy your ticket on arrival; get there 30 minutes early for good seats in the tiers.

⏱ 90 min

Seafood dinner on Jimbaran Beach, 20 minutes north of Uluwatu. Dozens of plastic tables and chairs are set directly on the sand at the water's edge, and vendors offer fish, prawns and squid grilled to order. It's the perfect end-of-trip ritual: feet in the sand, the lights of Jimbaran Bay twinkling in the distance and the skewers still smoking in front of you. Arrive before 7 PM for a good spot.

⏱ 90 min
