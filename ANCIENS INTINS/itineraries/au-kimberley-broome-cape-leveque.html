Broome & Dampier Peninsula: Pearls, Dinosaurs and Red Pindan Cliffs

📅 5 days📍 5 stops🚗 409 km⏱ 6h40

Broome, the world's most isolated pearling town, blends 130-million-year-old dinosaur footprints, Japanese divers' cemeteries, and sunsets over Cable Beach. Two hours north along the dirt track, Cape Leveque offers one of Australia's wildest landscapes: red pindan cliffs plunging into turquoise seas, managed by Bardi Jawi communities whose ancestors have lived on the Dampier Peninsula for tens of thousands of years.

✨ Highlights
  • Gantheaume Point dinosaur footprints: 21 fossilized species across 80 km, National Heritage-listed 2011
  • Cable Beach: 22.5 km of white sand, sunset camel rides along the Indian Ocean
  • Cape Leveque: red pindan cliffs, fringing reef snorkeling, Bardi Jawi wilderness camp
  • Beagle Bay: mother-of-pearl-inlaid church built by Bardi Jawi community in 1918
  • Broome: Pearl Luggers Museum, historic Chinatown, and Staircase to the Moon phenomenon

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Peak Season (December-February): Summer holidays, warmest weather, perfect for beaches, highest prices, most crowded attractions, school holidays.

Shoulder Seasons (March-May, September-November): Excellent weather, fewer crowds, moderate prices, ideal for most regions and outdoor activities.

Winter (June-August): Perfect for tropical north (dry season), ski season in Snowy Mountains, cooler in south, lower prices except ski areas.

Regional Variations: Northern Australia best in dry season (May-October), southern regions pleasant year-round, consider vast distances and climate zones.

Day 1

Broome

Where pearling history meets 130-million-year-old dinosaur tracks in red sandstone

★★★★★
1

Cable Beach stretches 22.5 kilometers northwest of Broome—one of Australia's longest white sand beaches. Named after the undersea telegraph cable laid in 1889 to connect Broome to Java and the wider world, the beach experiences tides reaching up to 9 meters—among the highest anywhere on Earth. At extreme low tide, theropod and sauropod dinosaur footprints emerge from the red Broome Sandstone—the only record of Western Australia's Early Cretaceous dinosaur fauna, preserved 130 million years ago.

⏱ 75 min
2

Gantheaume Point, 6 km south of Broome, is a red sandstone promontory jutting into the Indian Ocean. Its limestone rocks contain fossilized tracks from 21 different dinosaur species—theropods, sauropods, ornithopods—preserved in the Broome Sandstone. The trackways extend along 80 km of coastline and were inscribed on the Australian National Heritage List in 2011. At high tide, plaster casts mounted on the rock let you visualize the prints even when the originals are submerged. In Yawuru tradition, these footprints mark the path of Marala, the Emu Man of the Dreaming.

⏱ 60 min
3

Broome's Chinatown, centered on Dampier Terrace, is the historic heart of the pearling town. At the turn of the 20th century, Broome supplied 80% of the world's mother-of-pearl, and its streets teemed with Japanese, Chinese, Malays, Filipinos, Timorese, and Europeans in a cosmopolitan chaos unique in Australia. Sun Pictures, opened in 1916 by pearl master Ted Pearler, is the world's oldest operating outdoor cinema. Johnny Chi Lane's interpretive panels bring to life this frontier town in the era of opium dens and luggers.

⏱ 75 min
4

Broome's Japanese Cemetery, the largest of its kind in Australia, holds more than 900 headstones carved in Japanese characters—testament to divers who died from decompression accidents, cyclones, or sharks. In 1908, a single cyclone sank 41 luggers and killed 40 men in one night. The central obelisk was erected by the survivors. Half the gravestones bear the names of young men from Wakayama Prefecture—a poor coastal region south of Osaka whose sons made up the majority of divers.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walk the Jetty to Jetty Heritage Trail (2.5 km) from Streeter's Jetty to Town Beach. This self-guided route, narrated by the Yawuru app, traces 13 cultural and historical sites—the colonial jetty, remnants of the pearling town, and Yawuru sacred spaces. Download the app before you go to hear the voices of traditional custodians as you walk.

⏱ 90 min

At sunset, join the iconic Cable Beach camel ride—a Broome institution. Camels walk the beach facing the Indian Ocean in the golden evening light. The experience lasts about an hour; several operators offer late-afternoon departures directly from the beach.

⏱ 60 min
Day 2

Broome

Where pearling history meets 130-million-year-old dinosaur tracks in red sandstone

★★★★★
1

The Pearl Luggers Museum on Dampier Terrace displays two restored early-20th-century luggers complete with copper helmet diving suits, lead boots, and woolen dive gear. The divers' accounts—some made up to 50 dives a day at 40 meters depth for four months straight—reveal the brutal conditions of an industry that shaped the town. In 1900, 400 luggers were anchored in Roebuck Bay.

⏱ 75 min
2

Broome Bird Observatory, on the shores of Roebuck Bay east of town, monitors one of the world's most important shorebird feeding grounds. More than 155,000 waders—including godwits, sandpipers, and plovers—make the annual migration from Siberian Arctic and northeast Asia to Roebuck Bay, a journey of 10,000 km. The bay is a Ramsar-listed wetland of international importance.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Take a guided tour of Willie Creek Pearl Farm or Cygnet Bay Pearl Farm, less than an hour from Broome. These working pearl farms explain the full cycle of culturing Pinctada maxima—the world's largest pearl oyster—from seeding to harvest. Broome produces around 70% of the world's large cultured pearls. Pearl meat tastings and grading sessions are offered.

⏱ 120 min

On full moon nights (March to October), the 'Staircase to the Moon' phenomenon occurs over Roebuck Bay's mudflats: moonlight reflecting off the exposed tidal flats creates the illusion of a golden staircase rising into the sky. Town Beach night markets set up on these evenings.

⏱ 90 min

Day 3

Beagle Bay

Home to a sacred heart church glittering with mother-of-pearl from peninsula reefs

★★★★★
1

Sacred Heart Church at Beagle Bay, completed in 1918, is one of Australia's most extraordinary religious buildings. Built entirely from bricks fired on-site by mission residents—60,000 bricks hand-molded from local clay—its altar is inlaid with mother-of-pearl, cowries, volutes, and olives gathered from peninsula reefs. Traditional Nyulnyul, Nimanborr, and Bardi motifs blend with Stations of the Cross imagery. The Bardi Jawi people have lived on these lands for tens of thousands of years.

⏱ 60 min
2

Middle Lagoon, halfway between Beagle Bay and Cape Leveque, is a lagoon campsite with calm waters protected by a sandstone reef. Red pindan cliffs drop straight into turquoise water—the archetypal Dampier Peninsula landscape. Fishing here is famously productive.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

On the drive from Broome, stop at James Price Point, 60 km north, to see dinosaur footprints on beach sandstone slabs at low tide. Theropods, ornithopods, and sauropods left their tracks in what was then a tropical river delta in the Cretaceous. The site is also known for the successful fight against Woodside Energy's industrial gas project in 2012—a national campaign that preserved this coast.

⏱ 60 min

Stop at Lombadina community for a guided tour with Bardi Jawi traditional custodians: traditional net fishing, medicinal plant gathering, and wood carving demonstrations. The community also offers boat tours into the mangroves.

⏱ 120 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 120 km · 2h10
Day 4

Cape Leveque

Where red pindan cliffs ignite at sunset above water so clear you forget the depth

★★★★☆
1

Cape Leveque is the northernmost tip of the Dampier Peninsula, named in 1803 by French explorer Nicolas Baudin after his hydrographer Pierre Lévêque. In Bardi Jawi language, the place is called Kooljamanan. Pindan cliffs—the red-ochre sandstone characteristic of the Kimberley—plunge straight into turquoise water of exceptional clarity, creating the most striking color contrast on the Australian coast. The lighthouse, automated in 1986, was later purchased by the Aboriginal Development Commission for local Bardi Jawi communities.

⏱ 60 min
2

Kooljaman is a 100% Indigenous-owned wilderness camp, managed by the Bardi Jawi communities of Djarindjin and Ardyaloon (One Arm Point). With no grid electricity or air conditioning—the camp runs on solar power and bore water—it offers safari tents, timber cabins, and beach shelters right on the sand. The camp has won multiple national Indigenous and ecotourism awards. The Dampier Peninsula is a dry zone: no alcohol is permitted.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Snorkel the fringing reef at Eastern Beach, straight off Kooljaman's shore. Crystal-clear waters shelter corals, parrotfish, rays, and marine turtles regularly seen just meters from the beach. Swimming is safe on the eastern side—check with the camp about conditions and any hazards on the western shore.

⏱ 90 min

Visit Ardyaloon (One Arm Point), 16 km south of Cape Leveque. The Bardi Jawi community runs a giant clam and sea turtle hatchery and offers guided tours. From here, spectacular views open across the Buccaneer Archipelago—a labyrinth of islands and islets subject to the continent's highest tides, up to 12 meters.

⏱ 120 min

Watch the sunset from Western Beach at Kooljaman: red pindan cliffs ignite in the low-angled light as the Indian Ocean turns violet. This is the iconic Cape Leveque image—one of the most photographed scenes in the Kimberley. In season (June-November), humpback whales are sometimes visible offshore.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 204 km · 3h
Day 5

Broome

Where pearling history meets 130-million-year-old dinosaur tracks in red sandstone

★★★★★
1

At extreme low tide in Roebuck Bay, the wrecks of flying boats sunk during the Japanese bombing raid of March 3, 1942 rise through the shallows—16 aircraft destroyed in a single day, making this the second-deadliest air raid on Australia after Darwin. Most victims were Dutch refugees fleeing Java. Some wrecks remain visible in optimal conditions from the jetty.

⏱ 60 min
2

Minyirr Park, the coastal reserve adjoining Cable Beach, is co-managed by the Shire of Broome and the Yawuru people. Interpreted trails explain how the Yawuru read the seven seasons of their calendar—Mankala (flowering season), Laja (cold and dry), Barrgana (windy season)—from the behavior of plants and animals. Several species of tree snakes, monitors, and blue-tongued lizards inhabit the dune vegetation.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Browse Broome Courthouse Markets (Saturday morning) or Chinatown markets—local produce, Aboriginal art, cultured pearls, Asian specialties. Last supply stop before heading to Derby and the Gibb River Road.

⏱ 90 min

Return to Derby (220 km, 2.5 hours) to join the Gibb River Road, or fly back to Perth from Broome Airport. Broome is connected to Perth by daily flights (2h10) and to Darwin via connections through Port Hedland or Perth.
