Manado

North Sulawesi's capital, dubbed the City of a Thousand Churches and gateway to the reefs of Bunaken

‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÜ

Welcome to Manado, capital of North Sulawesi and gateway to some of Indonesia's richest underwater treasures. The city's name likely derives from the Portuguese manadas, meaning "cluster of offshore islands" ‚Äî a hint at its seafaring calling since the 15th century. The Ban Hin Kiong Temple, in Manado's Chinatown district, is recognised as the oldest Taoist temple in eastern Indonesia. Founded in 1819 during the Qing dynasty, it was originally built from bamboo before being rebuilt in stone. Its dragon statues, pagodas and monks in orange robes make it a peaceful cultural stop amid the city's bustle. Come early in the morning to catch the surrounding market in full swing and a more contemplative atmosphere.

‚è± 60 min

Depart by 8 AM from Bahowo or Molas pier towards Bunaken Island, a 30-minute boat ride away. Bunaken

A marine park since 1991 where 390 coral species cling to walls plunging 1,800 metres into the deep

‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÜ‚òÜ‚òÜ
1

Spend your second day at Bunaken exploring the less crowded sites. Mandolin Point, named after the "harmony" divers feel here, is an outstanding wall that reveals something different at every depth ‚Äî spectacular at 5 m just as much as at 35 m. Siladen Island, the smallest and least known of the park, lies 8 km east of Bunaken. ‚è± 90 min

‚è± 90 min

Tangkoko

An 8,718-hectare rainforest sheltering the spectral tarsier and the last crested black macaques on Earth

‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÜ‚òÜ‚òÜ
1

The Tangkoko-Batuangus Nature Reserve, at the foot of Mount Dua Saudara (1,361 m), covers 8,718 hectares of lowland rainforest ‚Äî one of the largest intact forests in the Minahasa region. The reserve was established in 1919 to protect its endemic species. Today it shelters at least 127 mammal species, 233 bird species and 104 reptile and amphibian species, the vast majority of which are endemic to Sulawesi. The most endangered include the crested black macaque (Macaca nigra), with only around 5,500 individuals remaining across the entire island, and the spectral tarsier, the world's smallest primate. Between 1978 and 1993, hunting had driven the macaque population down by 75% ‚Äî this reserve is their last viable refuge.

‚è± 60 min
2

The black volcanic beach of Batuputih, at the reserve entrance, is one of the region's most unusual landscapes. The dark sand contrasts sharply with the dense forest and the turquoise waters of the gulf. Flights of knobbed hornbills (Rhyticeros cassidix) ‚Äî the emblem of South Sulawesi province ‚Äî regularly sweep across the canopy above the beach. If you're here at sunrise, macaque troops often descend to the shoreline to forage in the shallows.

‚è± 45 min

Activities

Trek through the reserve with a local guide ‚Äî mandatory for park access. The late-afternoon outing (departing around 3:30 PM) is the best option: you first observe black macaques in full social mode ‚Äî troops can number up to 100 individuals, and some are so accustomed to researchers that they'll let you approach within a metre ‚Äî then search for tarsiers as dusk falls. The spectral tarsier measures 10 to 15 cm and weighs 80 to 100 grams: its eyes, larger than its brain, allow it to see in total darkness. Never shine a torch directly into their eyes. Local guides from Batuputih Bawah village will accompany you for a 2- to 3-hour walk.

‚è± 180 min

If you stay overnight at Tangkoko, the early-morning trek the next day offers the best chances of spotting knobbed hornbills in the canopy and macaques returning from the coast. Some operators also run traditional canoe trips through the mangroves to observe herons, kingfishers and monitor lizards. Ask at your accommodation the evening before.

‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÜ‚òÜ

Gorontalo, capital of the province bearing the same name, is a city often overlooked by travellers rushing to reach the Togean Islands. That's a mistake. Togean

An archipelago of 56 islands hiding a stingless jellyfish lake and a sunken WWII B-24 bomber

‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÜ‚òÜ‚òÜ
1

Arrival at Wakai, on Batudaka Island, in the Togean Islands ‚Äî an archipelago of 56 islands and islets in the middle of the Gulf of Tomini, designated a National Park in 2004. Sailors describe the Gulf of Tomini as one of the calmest deep-water bays in the world, sheltered by the two arms of Sulawesi that flank it. The Togean Islands feature all three types of coral reef within a single area ‚Äî fringing reefs, barrier reefs and atolls ‚Äî a combination found nowhere else on Earth according to marine biologists. The archipelago supports roughly 262 coral species and 596 fish species. Settle into one of the small coastal bungalows on Kadidiri or Togean Island ‚Äî accommodation is basic but the reefs are accessible right from the beach.

‚è± 90 min
2

Lake Mariona (also known as Jellyfish Lake) on Togean Island is one of the most improbable experiences you can have in Indonesia. This ancient marine lake, formed in a limestone sinkhole flooded by seawater, is home to two species of jellyfish that have lost their stinging cells through evolution ‚Äî having no predators left in this enclosed lake. You can swim (without fins, to avoid harming them) among millions of translucent jellyfish drifting slowly around you. It is one of the very few places in the world where this is possible.

‚è± 90 min

Activities

Snorkelling or diving on the Togean reefs. Unlike Bunaken, diving here is still largely wild and uncrowded. The best-known site is the wreck of an American B-24 bomber from World War II, which went down on 3 May 1945 during a mission over Sulawesi. The 17-metre aircraft rests intact at 18 m depth on a sandy bed, its machine guns still visible. Hundreds of bigeye trevally swirl around it constantly. Operators on Kadidiri Island arrange dive trips to this site and other reefs.

Togean

‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÜ‚òÜ‚òÜ

Don't leave the Togean Islands without visiting a Bajo village. The Bajo (or Badjao) are known as the "sea gypsies" ‚Äî a semi-nomadic people, 25,000 of whom live in the Togean Islands in stilt houses connected by wooden walkways above the water. Few operators service Una Una ‚Äî ask around as soon as you arrive at Wakai.

‚è± 90 min

Bring your own provisions as there's nothing on the islets. Ask your accommodation to arrange a custom boat tour to the most remote reefs and beaches. Palu, capital of Central Sulawesi, is a transit city between the Togean Islands and the rest of the island. Makassar

Indonesia's fifth city, where the turtle-shaped Fort Rotterdam guards a harbour of traditional pinisi schooners

‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÜ
1

Makassar (formerly Ujung Pandang) is Indonesia's fifth-largest city and the capital of South Sulawesi ‚Äî a maritime port city that wrote much of the archipelago's seafaring history. Fort Rotterdam is the city's most iconic landmark: this sea-turtle-shaped fort (Benteng Penyu) was originally built in 1545 by King Tunipalangga of the Gowa dynasty ‚Äî the tenth ruler of the kingdom. The current outer walls of 6-metre-high volcanic stone were rebuilt by Dutch Admiral Cornelis Speelman after his conquest in 1667, and named after his hometown in the Netherlands. The fort later served as a colonial prison: this is where Javanese national hero Prince Diponegoro was imprisoned for 25 years until his death in 1855. Inside, the La Galigo Museum displays prehistoric artefacts, Bugis costumes and objects from the great Bugis-Makassar oral epic tradition.

‚è± 90 min
2

Pantai Losari is Makassar's waterfront promenade: 1 km of seafront with no actual sandy beach, but plenty of street-food vendors, the floating Masjid Amirul Mukminin mosque, and crowds of locals gathering every evening to watch the sunset over the Makassar Strait. It's the social heartbeat of the city. Plan to be there around 5:30 PM for the golden hour.

‚è± 60 min
3

Paotere Harbour, 3 km north of Fort Rotterdam, is the traditional port of the pinisi ‚Äî the tall twin-masted sailing ships built for centuries by the Bugis shipwrights of Sulawesi. These wooden schooners still carry goods across the Indonesian archipelago today. Come early in the morning to see crews loading and unloading, brightly painted hulls lined up along the quay.

‚è± 60 min

Activities

Dinner at one of the Losari waterfront restaurants or in the alleys behind the fort. Makassar's specialities are not to be missed: coto makassar (thick, spicy beef and offal soup served with ketupat ‚Äî pressed rice cakes), konro bakar (grilled beef ribs in a dark spicy sauce), and es pisang ijo (green banana ice cream, the city's signature dessert). These are dishes you won't find at the same level outside Makassar.

Tana Toraja

South Sulawesi's highlands where the dead are kept among the living and cliff-side tau-tau watch over the valleys

‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÜ‚òÜ‚òÜ
1

Arrival in Rantepao, the main town of Tana Toraja, after a roughly 6-hour mountain drive from Makassar ‚Äî a magnificent route through terraced rice fields, bamboo forests and the highlands of South Sulawesi. The Toraja are an Austronesian highland people numbering over one million. The word toraja comes from the Bugis to riaja: "people of the uplands". The Dutch colonial government officially gave them this name in 1909. Their ancestral belief system, Aluk To Dolo (the Way of the Ancestors), structures all social life around the cult of the dead. Most Toraja are Christian today, but the ancestral rites continue with undiminished intensity.

‚è± 90 min
2

Visit Kete Kesu village, 4 km from Rantepao ‚Äî one of the best-preserved and most accessible tongkonan villages in the region. Tongkonan are the ancestral houses of the Toraja: built on stilts, their upward-curving roofs ‚Äî shaped like an upside-down boat ‚Äî are instantly recognisable. Tradition holds that this design recalls the vessels on which the Toraja ancestors first arrived in Sulawesi. The walls are covered with pa'ssura ‚Äî geometric motifs carved and painted in black, red, white and yellow, the four sacred Toraja colours, which "read" the family's history and cosmology. Facing the tongkonan stand the rice granaries (alang) of the same shape, forming an architectural ensemble found nowhere else in the world.

‚è± 90 min

Activities

Walk through the terraced rice fields around Rantepao at sunset. The Toraja rice paddies, carved into steep hillsides, are farmed using ancestral methods. This is also when the villages come alive ‚Äî families returning from the fields, children playing in the lanes between the granaries. Tana Toraja

South Sulawesi's highlands where the dead are kept among the living and cliff-side tau-tau watch over the valleys

‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÜ‚òÜ‚òÜ
1

Lemo is the most spectacular and most visited burial site in Tana Toraja. On a vertical limestone cliff face several dozen metres high, scores of burial niches have been carved by hand by to pande batu ‚Äî specialist stone carvers hired by families to suspend their dead in the rock. The work could take months. In front of each tomb, a balcony holds the tau-tau: a wooden statue sculpted and painted in the likeness of the deceased, dressed in fresh clothes and gazing downward ‚Äî toward the living. Toraja tradition holds that the dead are not truly gone until the funeral ceremony has been completed ‚Äî until then, the body is kept in the ancestral house, dressed and fed as if merely ill. These customs can delay the ceremony by months or even years after biological death.

‚è± 90 min

The Londa burial cave, a few kilometres from Lemo, is a complex of natural caverns where generations of Toraja families have been laid to rest. You enter with a kerosene lamp held by a local guide. Inside: ancestral coffins stacked one on top of another, skulls lined up on stone ledges, scattered bones. From the base of the cliff, a group of life-size tau-tau watches over the entrance from a carved wooden balcony. It is at once moving and profoundly unsettling to Western eyes.

Climb to the Buntu Pune or Batutumonga viewpoint for a single sweeping panorama of the entire Tana Toraja landscape: endless terraced rice fields, orange-and-red tongkonan scattered across the valleys, and clouds clinging to the forested peaks. This is the image you'll carry home from this trip.

Activities

If you're here between June and August, you may be able to attend a Rambu Solo (or Rambu Tuka) funeral ceremony. This is the funeral season ‚Äî after the rice harvest and before the new planting. Wakatobi

A UNESCO Biosphere Reserve with 750 coral species and Indonesia's largest barrier reef stretching to the horizon

‚òÖ‚òÜ‚òÜ‚òÜ‚òÜ
1

Welcome to Wakatobi ‚Äî the name is an acronym of the four main islands: Wangi-Wangi, Kaledupa, Tomia and Binongko. This national marine park covering 1.39 million hectares was established in 1996 and designated a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve in 2012. Explorer Jacques Cousteau reportedly called it an "underwater nirvana". The numbers are staggering: over 750 coral species, 942 recorded fish species ‚Äî roughly five times the coral diversity of Hawaii ‚Äî and 50 charted dive sites. Average visibility is 35 metres. With no rivers or streams carrying sediment, the water stays perfectly clear year-round. Wakatobi boasts Indonesia's largest barrier reef ‚Äî the second-largest in Asia after Australia's Great Barrier Reef.

The Bajo stilt villages in Wangi-Wangi Bay are one of the archipelago's most powerful cultural images. The Bajo (or Bajau) people, often called "sea gypsies", have lived in these houses suspended above the water for generations. They are celebrated in the scientific world for their extraordinary breath-holding ability ‚Äî some can hold their breath for over 13 minutes and dive to depths beyond 60 metres. A 2018 study found that their spleens are naturally 50% larger than those of their land-dwelling neighbours, enabling them to store more oxygen.
