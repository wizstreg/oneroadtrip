From Cambridge to London: Eastern England in 10 Days

📅 10 days📍 10 stops🚗 557 km⏱ 10h45

This route begins at the Gothic colleges of Cambridge, passes through Suffolk's timber-framed villages still rich from their wool-trading past, follows the North Sea coast to Britten's seaside town, then heads north to the Norfolk Broads and the palatial coast of Holkham before finishing at Britain's oldest town, Colchester, en route to London. Ten days between medieval history, flat landscapes under vast skies, and a coastline slowly crumbling into the sea.

✨ Highlights
  • Punting on the Cam past Cambridge's colleges
  • Lavenham medieval village, England's best-preserved
  • Cruising the Norfolk Broads, a national park dug by hand in the Middle Ages
  • Suffolk coast: Aldeburgh, drowned Dunwich and Orford Ness
📆 Best period

May, June, July, August, September

⏳ How many days should I plan?

To make the most of it, plan between 17 and 21 days.

☀️ What weather should you expect?

Summer (June-August): Warmest weather, longest daylight, peak tourist season, highest prices, festival season, ideal for all regions.

Spring (April-May): Mild weather, blooming gardens, fewer crowds, moderate prices, excellent for countryside, comfortable temperatures.

Fall (September-October): Pleasant weather, autumn colors, fewer crowds, good value, comfortable for sightseeing, cozy pub atmosphere.

Winter (November-March): Cold temperatures, shortest days, Christmas markets, lowest prices, indoor attractions, cozy cultural experiences.

🌍 What to discover in GB?

Rich history with royal heritage, diverse cultures, and iconic landmarks from London to Edinburgh. Experience pub culture, literary heritage, and beautiful countryside. The country offers excellent transportation with cultural depth and historical significance. Day 1

Cambridge

Where Gothic spires rise above the Cam and punts glide through centuries

★★★☆☆
1

King's College Chapel is the building that made Cambridge world-famous. Henry VI laid the foundation stone on 25 July 1446—he was nineteen and had just evicted dozens of homes, shops and even a church to clear the site. Construction took nearly seventy years, spanning the Wars of the Roses, and it was Henry VIII who finally completed it. Step inside to see the fan vaulting up close: at 89 metres long and 12 metres wide, it's the largest in the world, carved in just three years (1512–1515) by master mason John Wastell. On the wooden screen separating the choir from the ante-chapel, you'll spot the intertwined initials of Henry VIII and Anne Boleyn. If you can attend evensong (every weekday at 5:30pm except Monday), don't miss it—the acoustics amplify each note in a way that raises the hairs on your neck.

⏱ 90 min
2

Trinity College is the largest of Cambridge's 31 colleges—its Great Court, the biggest in Europe, feels more like a palace than a school. Founded by Henry VIII in 1546, it has since produced 32 Nobel laureates, including Isaac Newton. In the gardens, look for the apple tree: it's said to be descended from the one under which Newton formulated his theory of gravitation. Then push on to the Wren Library, designed by Christopher Wren in 1676—it opens on certain weekdays and holds original manuscripts by Shakespeare and Newton's Principia Mathematica. Seeing these pages in the flesh makes the trip worthwhile.

⏱ 60 min
3

Detour through St John's College for its Bridge of Sighs, a covered copy of Venice's famous bridge built in 1831—it's one of Cambridge's most photographed views, and it's even more striking than the pictures suggest. Founded in 1511 by Lady Margaret Beaufort, mother of Henry VII, the college sprawls across three successive courtyards you can walk through freely. If you only have time for one college after King's, this is the one.

⏱ 45 min
4

Climb the tower of Great St Mary's Church for the best elevated view of Cambridge. This fifteenth-century church is the university's official place of worship—the Academic Senate met here until the nineteenth century. From the top of the 123 steps, the entire city spreads before you: college spires, the Cam snaking through the middle, and the meadows of Jesus Green and Midsummer Common to the north.

⏱ 30 min
5

The Fitzwilliam Museum is one of Britain's finest university museums and admission is free. Founded in 1816 through Viscount Fitzwilliam's bequest, it brings together paintings by Titian, Rembrandt, Monet and Picasso, medieval armour, ancient coins and an impressive ceramics collection. Allow at least ninety minutes to see it properly. It's five minutes' walk from the colleges, on Trumpington Street.

⏱ 90 min

Activities

A punt along the Cam past the Backs is essential—it's the best way to see the rear of the colleges, inaccessible on foot (the grounds are private). The stretch known as the Backs runs about a mile and passes eight colleges: you glide under Queens' Mathematical Bridge, beneath St John's Bridge of Sighs, past King's perfect lawn. The tradition of pleasure punting appeared in Cambridge in 1903. Book a guided tour in high season (June–August): boats fill up fast at weekends. Reckon on fifty minutes for the full loop.

⏱ 60 min

Day 2

Lavenham

Half-timbered streets frozen in time since the wool trade crashed

★★★☆☆
1

Lavenham is England's best-preserved medieval village—more than 340 listed buildings for fewer than 2,000 inhabitants. In the sixteenth century, this tiny settlement paid more tax than York or Lincoln, both ten times larger. Its fortune came from a deep-blue woollen cloth called 'Lavenham Blue', exported as far as Russia and Egypt. When the market collapsed in the early seventeenth century, locals couldn't afford to rebuild in a more modern style—and that's what saved the village. Wander down the High Street looking for The Crooked House: built in 1395 for a wool merchant, this leaning timber-framed house is one of Britain's most photographed, and is sometimes cited as a possible inspiration for the nursery rhyme There Was a Crooked Man—a local association whose actual origins remain uncertain. Lavenham's streets served as the film set for Godric's Hollow in the Harry Potter Deathly Hallows films.

⏱ 75 min
2

Arriving at Lavenham's Market Place, you can't miss the Guildhall—this timber-framed building from 1530 dominates the square. It was built by the Guild of Corpus Christi to regulate the wool trade, and today it's one of the most accessible gateways to understanding how this town functioned at the height of its wealth. It belongs to the National Trust; allow forty-five minutes for the indoor exhibition on medieval wool trading. Before you leave, look up at the tower of St Peter and St Paul church opposite: 45 metres high, the tallest in Suffolk, commissioned in 1484 by the Spring and de Vere families so the entire region would know they were rich.

⏱ 60 min
3

Long Melford, eight kilometres south of Lavenham, is another wool town whose High Street stretches for more than three kilometres—one of the longest in England. Stop at Holy Trinity church: with its 45 great windows of fifteenth-century stained glass, it's one of Suffolk's finest Perpendicular churches, and the atmosphere inside is particularly luminous. Next door, Melford Hall, a Tudor mansion from the 1560s, welcomes the public under the National Trust. The novelist Beatrix Potter regularly stayed here with her cousins.

⏱ 75 min
4

If you pass through Sudbury, step into Gainsborough's House—it's the birthplace of Thomas Gainsborough (1727–1788), one of the greatest English portrait painters of the eighteenth century, and the only museum in Britain dedicated to a major artist housed in his birthplace. You'll find the world's largest permanent collection of his works. It's a short, pleasant visit with no queue. Coming out, look for the bronze statue of Gainsborough in the market square, palette in hand—he appears to be watching over the town he spent his life trying to escape for London.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Wander Lavenham's lanes and hidden courtyards in late afternoon when the tour coaches have left. The village is alive—bakery, galleries, antique shops, tea rooms—and it's a pleasure to explore on foot. Several waymarked circuits of 2–4 kilometres start from the car park opposite the church and cross the surrounding fields.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 24 km · 35min
Day 3

Bury St Edmunds

Where medieval barons plotted rebellion beneath Benedictine vaults

★★☆☆☆
1

The ruins of Bury St Edmunds Abbey stand at the heart of the Abbey Gardens, a public park open daily. This was one of the richest and most powerful Benedictine abbeys in England—founded in 1020 by King Cnut to house the relics of the martyr-king Edmund, killed by Vikings in 869. After Canterbury and Little Walsingham, the abbey was England's third most-visited pilgrimage site. In 1214, the English barons gathered here in secret to swear opposition to King John, an episode that led directly to Magna Carta. Henry VIII dissolved it in 1539 and had it systematically dismantled—historians reckon that if the abbey had survived, Bury St Edmunds would today be larger than Durham or Lincoln. Two remarkable medieval gateways remain: the fourteenth-century Great Gate and the twelfth-century Norman Tower, which still serves as the cathedral's bell tower.

⏱ 60 min
2

St Edmundsbury Cathedral, right next to the ruins, deserves a detailed visit. Its nave was rebuilt around 1503 by John Wastell—the same architect who completed the fan vaulting of King's College Chapel in Cambridge. The building only gained cathedral status in 1914, when the diocese was created. The Millennium Tower, finished in 2005, at last gives it the profile of a great cathedral. Don't miss Elisabeth Frink's bronze statue of St Edmund, which stands in the precinct.

⏱ 45 min
3

The archaeological village of West Stow, eight kilometres northwest of Bury St Edmunds, is a full-scale reconstruction of a fifth- to seventh-century Anglo-Saxon village. Excavations from 1965 to 1972 uncovered the foundations of a complete settlement, and houses have since been rebuilt on their original locations using authentic materials and techniques. It's one of the few sites in Europe where you can step inside buildings from that era and understand concretely what daily life looked like 1,500 years ago. The site is surrounded by a fifty-hectare nature reserve on the River Lark.

⏱ 75 min
4

Make the detour to Ickworth House, seven kilometres southwest of Bury St Edmunds: it's one of England's most eccentric country houses, and it truly deserves the description. The 4th Earl of Bristol began this building in 1796 with a fixed idea: a central rotunda 30 metres high, flanked by two curved wings to display the paintings and sculptures brought back from his travels in Italy. The result looks like nothing else. The property belongs to the National Trust—allow time for the Italianate gardens and the grand landscaped park, which are worth as much as the house itself.

⏱ 90 min
5

Newmarket, twenty kilometres west of Bury St Edmunds, is the world capital of horse racing. The town has hosted official races since 1622—Charles II himself used to ride there on his own mount. The National Stud, the training yards and Palace House (the former royal residence converted into a horse racing museum) are open to the public. If you pass through on a weekday, you'll see horses training early in the morning on the Heath, a fairly unique experience.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Stroll through Bury St Edmunds' historic centre in the evening, between Angel Hill and the Victorian Corn Exchange. The town has an active covered market and a collection of Georgian pubs that make you want to linger. Try the local beers from the Greene King brewery, whose warehouses have been based in the town since 1799.

⏱ 60 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 45 km · 55min
Day 4

Ipswich

Where Constable caught the light and Gainsborough learned to paint

★★★☆☆
1

Dedham Vale, on the Suffolk-Essex border, is one of the most famous landscapes in English painting—John Constable was born at East Bergholt in 1776 and painted it all his life. Stop at East Bergholt: St Mary's church has a sixteenth-century wooden bell cage standing on the ground in the churchyard—it's the only example of this type still in use in England. From there, head down to Flatford, where his father owned a mill: Flatford Mill is now managed by the National Trust, and you'll immediately recognise the landscape from The Hay Wain, painted here in 1821.

⏱ 90 min
2

Dedham is the best-preserved village in Dedham Vale, with its great Tudor church and its Georgian houses lined up. It's also an active artists' village: the painter Alfred Munnings (1878–1959) lived here and his former home is now a museum open in summer. Walk the path from Dedham to Flatford Mill along the Stour (thirty minutes)—it's the shortest route into Constable country.

⏱ 60 min
3

Christchurch Mansion in Ipswich is a mid-sixteenth-century Tudor house converted into a museum and art gallery in a large public park. It holds the world's largest collection of paintings by Constable and Gainsborough outside London—admission is free. The interior rooms are furnished in the style of each era, from Tudor to Victorian, making it as much an interior design tour as a painting gallery.

⏱ 75 min
4

Ipswich's Waterfront has been entirely redeveloped around the old Victorian dock. The seventeenth-century Old Custom House, the converted red-brick warehouses and the moored boats create a particular atmosphere. Ipswich claims the title of England's oldest town (founded by the Angles in the seventh century), though Colchester disputes this claim. Head down to the quayside in late afternoon for restaurant tables on the terrace overlooking the basin.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Walk or cycle along the River Stour between Flatford and Dedham: the waymarked path follows the historic towpath for around five kilometres, between meadows and willows, with several viewpoints over scenes almost identical to Constable's paintings. Several companies offer kayaks or rowing boats for hire in Dedham to go upriver to Flatford.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 55 km · 1h05
Day 5

Aldeburgh

Shingle beaches and soaring music under Suffolk's vast sky

★★★☆☆
1

Southwold is Suffolk's most elegant seaside resort: colourful houses, a shingle beach with rows of white and blue painted bathing huts, an immaculate white lighthouse dominating the town since 1890. Don't miss the Pier, built in 1900 and fully restored—it houses at its far end a collection of absurd and poetic mechanical machines invented by artist Tim Hunkin. The town also has a brewery (Adnams) established here since 1872 whose delivery drays still travel the streets by horse to preserve the cobbles.

⏱ 75 min
2

Dunwich, ten kilometres south of Southwold, is the strangest of English villages. In the Middle Ages it was a town of 5,000 inhabitants, one of England's largest, with eight churches, two monasteries and a busy port. The sea swallowed it century by century: today, only a handful of houses remain, a small shingle beach and the ruins of a twelfth-century priory. On calm nights, fishermen off the coast claim to hear the bells of the drowned churches ringing beneath the waves.

⏱ 45 min
3

Aldeburgh is a small fishing town made world-famous by the composer Benjamin Britten, who lived here from 1947 until his death in 1976. Born in Lowestoft in 1913, he founded the Aldeburgh Festival in 1948 with his partner, the tenor Peter Pears—the festival takes place each June and today attracts international artists. On the shingle beach, look for Maggi Hambling's Scallop sculpture, a 4.6-metre stainless steel scallop shell unveiled in 2003, engraved with words from the opera Peter Grimes. The sixteenth-century Moot Hall now stands a few metres from the sea, whereas in the sixteenth century it was in the centre of town—a measure of coastal erosion.

⏱ 75 min
4

Eight kilometres from Aldeburgh, don't miss Snape Maltings—an ensemble of Victorian industrial buildings on the Alde estuary, transformed into one of the world's most renowned music venues. The maltings were built in 1846 by Newson Garrett, then abandoned in the 1960s. It was Britten who had the idea of converting the largest building into a concert hall: Queen Elizabeth II inaugurated it on 2 June 1967. Two years later, a fire destroyed the interior on the very evening of the festival opening. It was rebuilt in 42 weeks, and the queen came back to inaugurate it again. The 832-seat hall is now considered to have one of Europe's finest acoustics. If you don't have a concert ticket, the complex is still worth the visit—craft shops, café with views over the marshes, farmers' market at weekends.

⏱ 60 min
5

Push on to Orford Castle, fifteen kilometres south of Aldeburgh—it's one of England's best-preserved royal castles and it's very little visited. Henry II had it built between 1165 and 1173 to assert his authority over the east of the country. Its 18-sided polygonal keep is unique in Britain. Climb to the top: the view embraces the whole of Orford Ness, a shingle spit that blocks access to the sea for fifteen kilometres—it's the largest such feature in Britain. If you have time, cross by ferry to Orford Ness itself: this secret military site abandoned in 1993 looks like an end-of-the-world scene, with its rusting bunkers lost among the shingle.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Birdwatching at the RSPB Minsmere reserve, ten kilometres north of Aldeburgh. It's one of the UK's most important bird reserves: you regularly observe the bittern (one of Britain's rarest birds), the kingfisher and the avocet. The reserve has several hides and waymarked trails. Arrive early in the morning for the best sightings.

⏱ 120 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 60 km · 1h05
Day 6

Norwich

Norman castle and market stalls where England's finest medieval city still thrives

★★★★☆
1

Norwich Castle dominates the city from arrival: its Norman keep sits on an artificial mound above the centre, visible from everywhere. William the Conqueror ordered its construction as early as 1067—98 Saxon houses were razed to clear the site. The stone keep, built between 1094 and 1121 in limestone imported from Caen, is one of the most decorated of the Norman era: its blind arcading is exceptional. After a five-year restoration, it reopened in 2025 with the interior recreated in its twelfth-century state—it's a good time to visit. The museum inside deserves time: it houses notably the paintings of the Norwich School and, an anecdote not to be missed, Britain's largest collection of ceramic teapots.

⏱ 90 min
2

Norwich Cathedral is a Norman cathedral begun in 1096 at the same time as the castle. Its 96-metre spire is the second-tallest in England after Salisbury. The cloisters are the largest in England and they house more than 400 medieval boss sculptures in the vaults—look up and take time to decipher them: you see scenes from the Apocalypse, fantastic animals and biblical episodes of remarkable quality. The cathedral was built using limestone imported from Caen, carried by barge from the River Wensum to the foot of the construction site via the water gate of Pull's Ferry, whose remains are still visible today.

⏱ 75 min
3

Norwich Market is one of the largest and oldest open-air markets in England, with nearly 200 stalls. It was established here by the Normans in the eleventh century and has never moved since. Right next door, Elm Hill is Norwich's best-preserved medieval street: its fifteenth- and sixteenth-century timber-framed houses now house antique dealers, galleries and cafés. In late afternoon, the oblique light on the cobbles and leaning façades is particularly beautiful.

⏱ 60 min
4

Take twenty minutes to visit the Sainsbury Centre for Visual Arts on the University of East Anglia campus—admission is free and the building alone is worth the trip. Norman Foster designed it in 1978: a great hangar of aluminium and glass that was a revolutionary piece of architecture at the time. Inside, you'll find African sculptures, Etruscan bronzes and works by Giacometti, Francis Bacon and Henry Moore presented in the same space, without partitions—a deliberately unusual arrangement. If you like art or architecture, you won't regret the stop.

⏱ 75 min

Activities

Evening in the Norwich Lanes, the quarter of independent lanes between the market and the cathedral. This is where the historic pubs, restaurants and the town's most original shops are concentrated. If you like craft beers, several pubs serve the local productions of Norfolk's breweries.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 18 km · 30min
Day 7

Wroxham

Where flooded medieval peat pits became Britain's secret waterways

★★★★☆
1

Before you take out a boat, pause to understand what you're about to cross—the Norfolk Broads are nothing natural. What you see is the result of thousands of peat pits dug by hand in the Middle Ages to supply Norwich with fuel, flooded in the fourteenth century when sea levels rose. Nobody understood this until 1952, when botanist Joyce Lambert established the man-made origin of the site after systematic surveys of the banks. The result of this historical accident: 200 kilometres of navigable waterways, 63 lakes and seven rivers linked together, and a national park that shelters a quarter of Britain's rarest species—including the only British habitat of the swallowtail butterfly (Papilio machaon brittanicus).

⏱ 60 min
2

St Helen's Church in Ranworth is nicknamed the 'cathedral of the Broads': perched on a slight rise at the edge of Ranworth Broad, its fifteenth-century tower is visible from the water several kilometres away. Climb to the top of the tower (89 steps) for the most complete view of the Broads. Inside, the church preserves one of England's finest medieval rood screens, dating from 1450, with its polychrome paintings remarkably well preserved. Ranworth Broad Nature Reserve next door has a floating centre on stilts with bird observation facilities.

⏱ 60 min
3

The ruins of St Benet's Abbey, accessible only by boat or on foot from the bank, constitute the only Broads site visited each year by the Bishop of Norwich during a ceremony on a boat in August. Founded in 1020 by King Cnut (the same who founded Bury St Edmunds Abbey), it is the only English abbey that was never formally dissolved by Henry VIII—the Bishop of Norwich remains theoretically its abbot. An eighteenth-century windmill was added within the ruins themselves, creating a singular silhouette in the Broads landscape.

⏱ 45 min
4

Wroxham is your base for taking to the water—it's the 'capital of the Broads' and the first pleasure boat hire company opened here in the 1870s. Today, hundreds of boats are available by the day or by the week, with no locks on the route. If you have children, or even if you don't, pop into Roys of Wroxham: nicknamed 'the world's largest village store', this institution since 1895 occupies several buildings in the centre and sells pretty much everything.

⏱ 30 min

Activities

Hire a motor boat for a half-day on the Broads from Wroxham: no licence is required and a few minutes' instruction is enough. Follow the River Bure towards Horning or head upstream to Barton Broad, the second-largest lake in the Broads. The calm of the waterways—6 km/h speed limit on most stretches—is striking. In summer, book ahead.

⏱ 180 min

Day 8

Wells Next The

★★★★★
1

Holkham Hall is one of England's great Palladian country houses, and it deserves a morning. Thomas Coke, 1st Earl of Leicester, began it in 1734—his inspiration went back to a six-year Grand Tour of Italy (1712–1718) during which he met Lord Burlington and architect William Kent, but he waited sixteen years before launching the project. Construction took 25 years and required demolishing the old village of Holkham to clear the space. Enter the Marble Hall: its ionic columns in alabaster and its 12-metre coffered ceiling are among the most imposing neoclassical interiors in the country. After the visit, walk to Holkham beach behind the estate—three kilometres of pristine sand bordered by pines, almost always deserted outside summer weekends. This is where the final scene of Shakespeare in Love (1998) was filmed.

⏱ 120 min
2

Burnham Thorpe, six kilometres from Holkham, is a village of 144 inhabitants that has the immense merit of being the birthplace of Horatio Nelson, born in 1758 at the parsonage—the house was demolished in 1803, but a plaque marks the spot. Nelson's father, the Reverend Edmund Nelson, was rector of All Saints church for 48 years. Inside the church, the lectern, altar and rood screen were carved from timber from the frigate HMS Victory. Nelson lived here from 1786 to 1793 while waiting to be assigned to a new ship, and organised a final farewell dinner for the villagers before embarking on HMS Agamemnon.

⏱ 45 min
3

Wells-next-the-Sea is the liveliest port on Norfolk's north coast, with its fishing boats and colourful bathing huts lined up on the embankment. Stroll along the quay in the morning when the boats come in, then walk the ten minutes to the beach, which opens onto wide stretches of sand bordered by pines. The town has neat Georgian architecture and several character pubs along the Quay.

⏱ 60 min
4

Cromer, 45 kilometres to the east, is Norfolk's emblematic Victorian seaside resort, with its 1901 pier and its theatre at the end of the jetty still operating—one of the few 'end-of-the-pier shows' that survive in England. St Peter and St Paul church has the tallest church tower in Norfolk (49 metres). Cromer is also famous for its crabs, renowned across the country: stop at the market or in a fisherman's shop to buy some.

⏱ 60 min

Activities

Seal-watching at Blakeney Point: Blakeney Point is home to Britain's largest colony of common and grey seals. Pleasure boats from Morston Quay (five kilometres from Wells) allow you to approach within a few metres. The colony numbers up to 3,000 individuals depending on the season.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 45 km · 55min
Day 9

Kings Lynn

Hanseatic warehouses and royal estates on the edge of The Wash

★★★★★
1

The Sandringham Estate is open to the public for much of the year—it's a rare opportunity to visit a private residence of the Royal Family still inhabited. The house was built in Jacobean Revival style for the Prince of Wales (later Edward VII) between 1870 and 1900. George V died here in 1936, George VI in 1952. Allow at least two hours: the museum of royal cars and the estate's nature reserve justify the trip on their own. Avoid the Christmas and early January period, when the family is in residence and the house is closed.

⏱ 120 min
2

Houghton Hall, fifteen kilometres northeast of King's Lynn, is the Palladian seat of Sir Robert Walpole, Prime Minister from 1721 to 1742—the man who invented the office. The domed hall and State Apartments decorated by William Kent are impressive. What makes the visit really interesting today is that the estate is still inhabited by Walpole's descendants and hosts important contemporary sculpture exhibitions in the gardens each summer—James Turrell, Richard Long and Zaha Hadid have all exhibited here. If you come in summer, check which artist is in residence that year.

⏱ 90 min
3

Set aside an hour to wander King's Lynn's historic centre—it's one of England's best-preserved medieval ports and it's largely ignored by tourists. Head down to the quayside on the Great Ouse to see the seventeenth-century Old Custom House, the town's emblematic image. The Guildhall of the Holy Trinity (1421) is one of the rare civic buildings from Henry V's era still in use. For an anecdote worth the detour: look for the Hanseatic Warehouse, built in 1475, one of the very few surviving Hanseatic League trading posts in England—proof that King's Lynn traded directly with Baltic towns in the Middle Ages.

⏱ 75 min
4

Castle Rising, eight kilometres northeast of King's Lynn, is one of England's best-preserved Norman keeps. Built around 1140, it served as the residence of Isabella of France after her exile from court in 1330—the Queen Mother, nicknamed the 'She-Wolf of France', lived here for nearly 30 years until her death in 1358. The twelfth-century outer ramparts and the keep are open to the public.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Watching migratory birds at The Wash reserve, the shallow estuary between Norfolk and Lincolnshire. It's one of Europe's most important wader sites: up to 350,000 birds winter or stop here during migration. The best observations take place at rising tide, when the birds gather on the few remaining exposed areas. Several access points from the coastal roads between Hunstanton and Snettisham.

⏱ 90 min
🚗Drive to next stop: 160 km · 2h45
Day 10

London

Journey's end via Colchester, where Rome first planted Britain's capital

★★★★★
1

Colchester deserves a stop on the road to London: it's Britain's oldest recorded town. It appears in Roman sources as early as the first century AD—Pliny the Elder mentions it in 77 AD under the name Camulodunum, but Celtic coins struck on site attest to its existence even earlier, in the first century BC, as the capital of King Cunobelin (the character in Shakespeare's Cymbeline). The Romans made it the first capital of the province of Britannia in 43 AD. Boudica, Queen of the Iceni, burned it to the ground in 60 AD, which caused the capital to shift to London.

⏱ 30 min
2

Start with Colchester Castle—don't just photograph it from outside. It was built by the Normans after 1066 directly on the foundations of the largest Roman temple in Britain, a temple dedicated to the Emperor Claudius. If you take a guided tour, you'll descend into these foundations and see the Roman blocks on which the Norman masons simply placed their walls. The keep is the largest in Britain—bigger than the White Tower of the Tower of London. The museum inside is remarkable: look for the Colchester Sphinx, a first-century Roman funerary statue discovered in the town. Allow ninety minutes.

⏱ 90 min
3

After the castle, follow the Roman walls—they're the best-preserved in Britain, with sections still reaching six metres high. A waymarked walkway allows you to circle them in thirty minutes. Don't miss Balkerne Gate: built around 50 AD, it's the largest Roman gateway still standing in England. Finish with the ruins of St Botolph's Priory, five minutes' walk away—founded in the eleventh century, it's the first Augustinian monastery in Britain, and its half-collapsed round arches make a fine photograph.

⏱ 45 min

Activities

Final leg to London by car or direct train from Colchester (under an hour to Liverpool Street). If you arrive in early evening, the Shoreditch quarter or the borough of Southwark are ideal for a first London dinner.

⏱ 60 min
